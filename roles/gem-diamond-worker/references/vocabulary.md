# Vocabulary

### Inclusion mapping
The process of identifying and documenting the location, size, and type of internal flaws within a rough gemstone before finalizing a cutting plan.
**In use:** "Mapped the inclusion at one-point-two millimeters from the projected girdle — that's what's driving the orientation adjustment in this cutting plan."
**Common misuse:** Beginning cutting before completing inclusion mapping — since cutting is irreversible, discovering an inclusion's problematic location mid-process means working around it with already-reduced material rather than having planned around it from the start.

### Crystal structure (grain direction)
The internal atomic arrangement of a gemstone, which determines directions along which the material cleaves or cuts more or less readily, a physical property that must be identified before cutting, not a preference.
**In use:** "Checking grain direction before we make the first cut — cutting against it on this stone risks a fracture, not just a harder cut."
**Common misuse:** Treating cutting direction as a free choice based on desired shape alone, without confirming it aligns with the stone's actual crystal structure — cutting against the grain can cause fracture or produce a significantly more difficult, lower-quality result regardless of the intended shape.

### Yield (carat retention)
The proportion of a rough stone's original weight retained in the finished, cut stone, one of the key variables traded off against clarity/shape quality in a cutting plan.
**In use:** "We're accepting an eleven percent yield reduction here specifically to move the inclusion out of the visible crown area."
**Common misuse:** Treating yield maximization as the automatic, default objective for every cutting plan — yield and clarity/shape quality are often competing objectives, and the actual value-optimal plan depends on the specific stone's inclusion pattern, not a blanket preference for maximum retained weight.

### Clarity grade
A standardized assessment (such as the GIA scale, from Flawless to Included) of a gemstone's internal and surface characteristics, determined against specific technical criteria rather than individual aesthetic impression.
**In use:** "Grading this against the standard criteria — VS1, not just 'looks pretty clean to me.'"
**Common misuse:** Assigning a clarity grade based on general visual impression rather than the standardized scale's specific criteria — grading has real financial consequences, and an inconsistent, subjective approach undermines the entire purpose of having a standardized grading system.

### Girdle (diamond cutting)
The narrow band forming the outer edge of a cut diamond, separating the crown (top) from the pavilion (bottom), a location often used strategically to hide inclusions since it's less visually prominent than the crown.
**In use:** "Positioning the inclusion under the girdle instead of the crown — that's a real technique for managing where a flaw ends up in the finished stone."
**Common misuse:** Assuming inclusion placement in the finished stone is purely a consequence of where the inclusion happened to be in the rough material — deliberate orientation choices during cutting planning can influence where an inclusion ends up relative to the finished stone's more and less visible areas.

### Cutting plan (irreversibility)
The complete strategy for how a rough gemstone will be cut, accounting for crystal structure and inclusion locations together, made before any cut because material removed cannot be replaced.
**In use:** "This plan accounts for both the grain direction and the inclusion map together — we're not making the first cut until both are factored in."
**Common misuse:** Treating early cuts as low-stakes or easily corrected if something doesn't work out — because gemstone material removed during cutting cannot be added back, the cutting plan needs to account for all known factors before the first irreversible cut, not be adjusted reactively after material is already lost.

### Table and crown orientation (diamond cutting)
The specific alignment of a diamond's top facets (table and crown) relative to the rough stone's internal features, adjustable during cutting planning to influence where inclusions end up in the finished stone.
**In use:** "Adjusting table orientation slightly to shift where this inclusion lands relative to the finished crown."
**Common misuse:** Treating table/crown orientation as determined solely by achieving the most symmetric or standard proportions — orientation can be deliberately adjusted (within acceptable proportion tolerances) to manage inclusion visibility, a trade-off worth considering alongside pure symmetry/proportion optimization.

### GIA (Gemological Institute of America) grading standard
A widely recognized, standardized system for assessing diamond quality across the "4 Cs" (cut, clarity, color, carat weight), providing an objective reference against which any specific stone's characteristics are assessed.
**In use:** "Grading against GIA criteria specifically, since that's the standard our buyers and appraisers reference."
**Common misuse:** Using an informal or personally-developed grading approach instead of a recognized standardized system — buyers, appraisers, and the broader market rely on consistent, recognized grading standards, and deviating from them undermines the grade's credibility and value regardless of how carefully the assessment was actually performed.
