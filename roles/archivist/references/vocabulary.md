# Archivist — Vocabulary

### Fonds
The complete body of records, regardless of form, created or accumulated by a single person, family, or organization in the course of its activities. The core unit of archival arrangement — a fonds is kept together and described as a whole, never split apart by subject.
**In use:** "The mayor's fonds includes correspondence, meeting minutes, and photographs — we describe it as one body of records, not three separate collections."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a synonym for "collection" — a collection can be artificially assembled by a repository (e.g., "postcards of the city, from many sources"), while a fonds must share a single organic origin.

### Provenance
The origin and chain of custody of a record — who created it, and through whose hands it passed before reaching the repository. Provenance is documented, not inferred from content.
**In use:** "We can't merge these two accessions — the provenance differs even though the content overlaps."
**Common misuse:** Confused with subject matter ("this is about the same topic, so it's the same provenance") — provenance is about who created/held the record, not what it's about.

### Original order
The sequence and grouping in which the creator maintained the records, preserved during arrangement because it reflects how the records were actually used.
**In use:** "The boxes look jumbled, but the original order tells us these were filed by fiscal year, not alphabetically — we keep it that way."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to mean "however the boxes happened to arrive," including accidental disorder from a move or a basement flood — genuine original order is the creator's functional system, not shipping damage.

### Accession
The formal act (and resulting record) of a repository taking legal and physical custody of material. Distinct from processing, which may happen much later.
**In use:** "The collection was accessioned in March but won't be processed until next fiscal year — it's still legally ours and logged, just not yet arranged or described."
**Common misuse:** Used interchangeably with "processed" or "catalogued" — accession only establishes custody and a legal/tracking record, not discoverability.

### Deed of gift
The legal instrument by which a donor transfers ownership (and typically copyright) of material to a repository.
**In use:** "We can't accept physical custody without a signed deed of gift — otherwise title is ambiguous."
**Common misuse:** Assumed unnecessary for informal or small donations — even a single box needs documented transfer of title to avoid future ownership disputes.

### Finding aid
A descriptive document (traditionally DACS/EAD-structured) that lets a researcher determine a collection's scope, content, and access conditions without physically examining it.
**In use:** "Check the finding aid's scope and content note before requesting the boxes — it'll tell you if Series 3 even covers that period."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a narrative summary or literary description — its job is functional (what's here, how it's organized, what's restricted), not to summarize the intellectual content.

### DACS (Describing Archives: A Content Standard)
The content standard (maintained by the Society of American Archivists) specifying what elements a finding aid must contain — title, extent, biographical/historical note, scope and content, arrangement, restrictions.
**In use:** "The finding aid is missing a DACS-required restriction statement — it can't be published until that's added."
**Common misuse:** Conflated with EAD, which is the XML encoding format for finding aids, not the content standard itself — DACS says *what* to include, EAD says how to encode it.

### MPLP (More Product, Less Process)
A processing methodology (Greene & Meissner, 2005) that describes collections at the series/box level rather than the item level, trading description depth for throughput, to get more of the backlog open to researchers.
**In use:** "This collection gets MPLP treatment — box-level description, no folder-by-folder inventory, unless a series turns out to be high-demand later."
**Common misuse:** Interpreted as "sloppy" or "incomplete" processing — MPLP is a deliberate methodology matching description depth to projected use, not a shortcut taken under duress.

### Series
A grouping of related records within a fonds that share a common function, form, or filing arrangement (e.g., "Correspondence," "Meeting Minutes").
**In use:** "Series 4 (Manuscripts) gets item-level treatment because of active researcher demand; the rest stays at box level."
**Common misuse:** Used loosely to mean any subdivision of a collection, including ones the archivist invents for convenience — a series should reflect a functional grouping that existed in the creator's own use of the records, not an arbitrary split.

### Deaccession
The formal, documented removal of material from a repository's holdings — legally and procedurally the inverse of accession, requiring its own rationale.
**In use:** "Deaccessioning this collection needs board approval and donor notification, same as any accession decision would."
**Common misuse:** Treated as simple disposal or "cleaning house" — deaccession carries the same evidentiary and often legal weight as the original accession decision.

### Preservation master vs. access copy
The preservation master is a high-fidelity, uncompressed digital file (e.g., TIFF, WAV) intended to be the long-term reference; the access copy is a smaller, compressed derivative (e.g., JPEG, MP3) meant for day-to-day researcher use.
**In use:** "We digitized the photographs — TIFF masters go to dark storage with checksums, JPEGs go on the discovery portal."
**Common misuse:** The access copy alone is mistaken for "preservation" — without a validated master format and fixity checks, a convenience scan provides access but no preservation guarantee.

### Fixity
Verification (typically via checksum/hash) that a digital file has not changed since it was captured — the digital equivalent of confirming a physical item hasn't been altered.
**In use:** "The fixity check flagged a mismatch on that TIFF — we need to investigate before we trust it as the preservation master."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to be a one-time check at digitization — fixity needs to be re-verified on a recurring schedule, since storage media and transfers can silently corrupt files over time.

### Restricted/closed records
Material where access is limited or barred for a defined period or condition (privacy law, donor stipulation, ongoing litigation), distinct from material that is simply unprocessed.
**In use:** "That series isn't unavailable because it's unprocessed — it's closed until 2051 under the donor agreement."
**Common misuse:** Conflated with "not yet processed" — a restricted record can be fully processed and described, just not accessible to researchers until the restriction lifts.

### Appraisal
The archival process of determining whether records have sufficient enduring research, evidentiary, or informational value to justify permanent retention — distinct from monetary appraisal.
**In use:** "Appraisal on this accession found about 90% has no lasting research value — we're recommending the rest go to records management for standard retention/disposal."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to mean assessing an item's dollar value (as in "antiques appraisal") — archival appraisal is about research/evidentiary value, not market price.
