# Vocabulary

Terms generalists conflate or misuse. Each: definition, how a practitioner actually uses it, and the common misuse.

### T-CLOCS
The Motorcycle Safety Foundation's pre-ride inspection acronym: Tires & wheels, Controls, Lights & electrics, Oil & fluids, Chassis & suspension, Stands — a standardized category order for a safety check.

**In use:** "Run T-CLOCS on it before you touch the idle complaint — I want to know what else is going on before we chase the thing he actually called about."

**Common misuse:** treating it as a rider-facing pre-ride pamphlet only, rather than an actual shop-side diagnostic intake framework run on every ambiguous complaint.

### Chain slack
The vertical deflection measured at the chain's tightest point of travel (found by rotating the wheel through a full revolution), not wherever the chain happens to sag most while the bike sits still.

**In use:** "Don't measure it wherever it looks loosest — rotate the wheel, find the tightest point, and measure there. That's the number that matters."

**Common misuse:** measuring slack at rest without checking for the tightest point through the wheel's rotation, which can read in-spec while the actual tightest point is well outside it.

### Wear bar (tread wear indicator, TWI)
A raised rubber bar molded into a tire's tread grooves that becomes flush with the tread surface once roughly 1/32 in (0.8 mm) of tread remains — the common legal minimum tread depth.

**In use:** "Wear bar's flush on the rear — that's the legal minimum, and I'm not releasing this bike on it."

**Common misuse:** treating the wear bar as a "replace soon" suggestion rather than a hard legal/safety floor, especially on a bike ridden with any meaningful lean angle.

### DOT date code
A four-digit code molded into a tire's sidewall giving the week and year of manufacture (e.g., "2419" = week 24 of 2019) — the basis for age-based replacement independent of tread depth.

**In use:** "Tread looks fine but the date code says 2016 — that's pushing 10 years, and the sidewall's already showing hairline cracks at the bead."

**Common misuse:** judging a tire only by tread depth and ignoring the date code, missing sidewall hardening/cracking that reduces grip even on a tire with plenty of tread left.

### Shim-under-bucket vs. screw-and-locknut valve train
Two different valve-clearance adjustment architectures: shim-under-bucket requires cam removal and a shim swap to correct clearance; screw-and-locknut allows direct adjustment at the rocker with a wrench and feeler gauge.

**In use:** "This is a shim-bucket engine, so if it's out of spec we're pulling cams — it's not a five-minute locknut turn like the cruiser in the next bay."

**Common misuse:** quoting the same labor time or the same service interval for both architectures, when the correction method and the applicable interval both differ by design.

### Pilot jet / idle circuit
The smallest fuel-metering passage in a carburetor, controlling fuel delivery at idle and just off idle — the first passage to clog with varnish from stale, unstabilized fuel.

**In use:** "Rough idle after storage, no stabilizer added — check the pilot jet before you assume it's a bigger fuel-system problem."

**Common misuse:** assuming a general "old gas" refill will resolve a rough-idle complaint, when varnish already deposited in the pilot jet survives a fuel refill and requires physical cleaning.

### Float level
The fuel level a carburetor's float maintains in the bowl, set by adjusting the float's arm/tang — incorrect float level causes flooding (too high) or lean running/hesitation (too low).

**In use:** "Idle's fine now but it's hesitating off the bottom — check float level before you start re-jetting."

**Common misuse:** re-jetting a carburetor to chase a symptom that's actually caused by an incorrect float level, which no jet size will fix.

### Load index / speed rating
A tire's load index is a coded number (found in a standard table) indicating the maximum weight it can carry per tire at a given inflation pressure; speed rating is a letter code for its maximum sustained speed capability.

**In use:** "That tire fits the rim fine, but check the load index against this bike's GVWR with a passenger and luggage before you mount it."

**Common misuse:** selecting a tire on rim-size and tread-pattern fit alone, without checking whether its load index actually covers the bike's fully loaded weight.

### Countersteering
The technique (and physical reality) of a motorcycle turning by a brief opposite-direction push on the handlebar at speed, which leans the bike into the turn — not a conscious steering input in the car sense.

**In use:** "A worn steering-head bearing changes the feedback he's getting through countersteering input, which is why it reads as 'vague at speed,' not just loose."

**Common misuse:** dismissing a rider's vague-handling complaint as subjective feel, when a chassis defect (bearing play, tire pressure, chain slack) can specifically degrade the countersteering feedback loop a rider depends on for balance.

### Sprocket hook wear
A worn tooth profile on a drive or driven sprocket that develops a hooked, shark-fin shape from prolonged running with excessive chain slack or a stretched chain.

**In use:** "New chain on hooked sprockets won't last a thousand miles — replace the set, not just the chain."

**Common misuse:** replacing a worn chain alone without inspecting sprocket tooth profile, leading to rapid wear of the new chain against sprockets that were never corrected.

### Single-track vehicle
A vehicle (motorcycle, bicycle) that travels on essentially one line of contact with the road and relies on rider balance and countersteering for stability, as opposed to a multi-track vehicle (car) that's inherently stable on four contact patches.

**In use:** "On a single-track vehicle, a bald tire or slack chain isn't a comfort issue — it's a balance-margin issue at lean, which a car's equivalent wear item isn't."

**Common misuse:** applying passenger-vehicle wear-item thresholds (tread depth, chain/belt slack) directly to a motorcycle without accounting for the lack of stability redundancy.

### Fuel stabilizer / stabilized fuel
A chemical additive mixed into gasoline before a storage period to slow oxidation and varnish/gum formation, particularly relevant to ethanol-blended fuel that degrades faster than non-ethanol fuel.

**In use:** "Add stabilizer and run it through the carbs for five minutes before you put it away for the winter — don't just top off the tank and walk away."

**Common misuse:** adding stabilizer to the tank without running the engine to circulate treated fuel through the carburetor bowls and jets, leaving untreated fuel in the circuit most prone to varnishing.

### Float charger / battery tender
A charger designed to maintain a battery at full charge indefinitely without overcharging, by tapering to a low maintenance ("float") voltage once the battery reaches full charge — distinct from a standard charger meant for active charging only.

**In use:** "Put it on the tender for the winter, not just a regular charger left plugged in — the float stage is what prevents sulfation over months of sitting."

**Common misuse:** assuming any charger left connected long-term is equivalent to a float/tender-rated charger, when a non-float charger can overcharge and damage a battery over an extended storage period.
