# Vocabulary — terms generalists misuse

### Remission vs. response
Remission is return to a near-asymptomatic state, operationalized on a validated scale (e.g., PHQ-9 <5); response is a substantial improvement, conventionally ≥50% reduction from baseline, that may still leave clinically significant symptoms.
**In use:** "She's a responder at 55% reduction, but PHQ-9 is still 9 — that's response, not remission, and remission is the actual treatment target."
**Common misuse:** Treating "the patient feels better" or any symptom improvement as equivalent to remission, which stops treatment optimization too early — residual symptoms predict relapse.

### Augmentation vs. combination vs. switch
Augmentation adds a non-antidepressant agent (lithium, an atypical antipsychotic, thyroid hormone) to an existing antidepressant; combination adds a second antidepressant; switch discontinues the first agent for a different one.
**In use:** "Augment with aripiprazole rather than combine with bupropion — fewer interaction and side-effect layers to track in a patient already on two other medications."
**Common misuse:** Using "augmentation" loosely to mean any add-on, including adding a second full antidepressant, which is actually combination and carries a different interaction and side-effect profile.

### Adequate trial
A medication trial that reached a full therapeutic dose and was maintained for the minimum evidence-based duration (typically 6–8 weeks for antidepressants) before judging response.
**In use:** "That's not treatment resistance yet — 50mg of sertraline for three weeks isn't an adequate trial."
**Common misuse:** Labeling any trial that didn't work as "adequate" regardless of dose or duration, which drives premature switching and inflates apparent treatment-resistance rates.

### Treatment-resistant (depression, schizophrenia, etc.)
A specific, defined threshold — for depression, conventionally failure of two adequate trials of different classes; not a synonym for "hasn't gotten better yet" or "difficult patient."
**In use:** "This meets criteria for treatment-resistant depression — two adequate trials of different mechanisms, both non-response — so ECT or esketamine referral is now appropriate to discuss."
**Common misuse:** Applying the label after one inadequate trial, or using it as a description of a patient's perceived difficulty rather than a specific treatment-history threshold.

### Serotonin syndrome
A clinical triad (autonomic instability, neuromuscular abnormality — clonus/hyperreflexia, and altered mental status) from excess serotonergic activity, typically from an interacting drug combination or overdose, distinct from a routine SSRI side effect.
**In use:** "New-onset clonus and hyperthermia after starting a second serotonergic agent — that's serotonin syndrome until proven otherwise, not just SSRI jitteriness."
**Common misuse:** Attributing any agitation or GI upset on an SSRI to "serotonin syndrome," when the diagnosis requires the specific triad, not isolated non-specific symptoms.

### Akathisia
A subjective and objective motor restlessness caused by dopamine-blocking agents (antipsychotics, some antiemetics), experienced as an internal compulsion to move, not simple anxiety.
**In use:** "The 'increased anxiety' on aripiprazole 15mg is akathisia until ruled out — reduce the dose or add a beta-blocker rather than treating it as anxiety with a benzodiazepine."
**Common misuse:** Mistaking akathisia for anxiety or agitation and treating it with an anxiolytic or an antipsychotic dose increase, both of which can worsen it.

### Tardive dyskinesia
Delayed-onset, often irreversible involuntary movements (classically orofacial) from chronic dopamine-receptor blockade, distinct from acute extrapyramidal symptoms that occur early in treatment.
**In use:** "Six months on a first-generation antipsychotic, now with orofacial dyskinesia — screen with the AIMS exam and consider a switch or a VMAT2 inhibitor."
**Common misuse:** Conflating it with acute dystonia or Parkinsonism (which are early-onset and often reversible with dose adjustment or anticholinergics), leading to the wrong management approach.

### Anticholinergic burden
The cumulative sedative/cognitive/constipating/urinary-retention effect of multiple anticholinergic-active medications (some antipsychotics, TCAs, antihistamines, overactive-bladder drugs) taken together, which compounds even when no single agent looks concerning.
**In use:** "Individually low-dose, but three anticholinergic-active drugs stacked in an elderly patient — that's the confusion, not early dementia."
**Common misuse:** Evaluating each medication's anticholinergic risk in isolation rather than as a cumulative burden, especially in older patients where the additive effect drives delirium risk.

### Involuntary hold / civil commitment
A statutory, time-limited detention for evaluation or treatment based on specific legal criteria (danger to self, danger to others, or grave disability, definitions vary by state/country) — not a clinical judgment call made independent of the statute.
**In use:** "Meets the danger-to-self criterion under the statute — initiating the 72-hour hold, documented against each statutory element, not just clinical concern."
**Common misuse:** Treating "hold" as a generic clinical decision based on general worry, rather than a specific legal standard that must be documented element by element.

### Illness script
A clinician's internalized pattern of a disorder's typical presentation, course, and risk factors, built from training and prior cases, used for fast pattern recognition.
**In use:** "The illness script for this presentation doesn't fit — no prior episodes, acute onset over days, not the gradual course expected for primary psychiatric mania."
**Common misuse:** Trusting the pattern match without checking it against atypical or disconfirming findings, which is exactly the mechanism behind premature closure.

### Discontinuation syndrome vs. relapse
Discontinuation syndrome is a time-limited, typically self-resolving symptom cluster from stopping or rapidly tapering a psychotropic (especially short-half-life SSRIs/SNRIs); relapse is the return of the original untreated illness.
**In use:** "Dizziness and 'brain zaps' three days after stopping paroxetine — that's discontinuation syndrome, not a depression relapse, and it resolves faster than relapse would."
**Common misuse:** Interpreting early post-taper symptoms as proof the medication was still needed (relapse), when the timeline and symptom quality point to discontinuation syndrome instead.

### Mixed features (specifier)
A DSM-5-TR specifier for a mood episode (depressive or manic/hypomanic) that also has a threshold number of symptoms of the opposite pole simultaneously, not full criteria for the opposite episode type.
**In use:** "Major depressive episode with mixed features — three manic-spectrum symptoms present at the same time changes the medication choice away from a standard antidepressant alone."
**Common misuse:** Treating any irritability or agitation during a depressive episode as automatically "mixed features" without confirming the specifier's actual symptom-count threshold.
