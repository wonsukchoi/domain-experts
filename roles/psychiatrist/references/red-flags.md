# Red flags — psychiatric evaluation and treatment smell tests

Format per flag: signal with threshold, most likely causes, the first question a veteran asks, and what to pull to check.

### First psychotic episode presenting after age 40 with no prior psychiatric history
- **Usually means:** occult medical cause — structural brain lesion, autoimmune encephalitis, substance-induced state, or delirium — more likely than primary schizophrenia, whose typical onset is late teens to early 30s.
- **First question:** "What does the neuro exam and a basic medical workup show before this gets treated as a primary psychiatric diagnosis?"
- **Data to pull:** head imaging, CBC/CMP, TSH, autoimmune panel if indicated, urine tox, medication list for anticholinergic/steroid exposure.

### "Failed" antidepressant trial documented at less than a therapeutic dose or under 4 weeks of duration
- **Usually means:** an inadequate trial mislabeled as treatment resistance, driving unnecessary switches or polypharmacy instead of simply completing the first trial.
- **First question:** "What was the actual dose and duration, and does it meet the 6–8 week / full-dose threshold?"
- **Data to pull:** pharmacy fill/dispense history (dose and refill gaps), not just the chart note.

### PHQ-9 or other rating scale not repeated at follow-up, only global impression documented
- **Usually means:** treatment response is being judged on recall/impression rather than a comparable measurement, which both over- and under-estimates change and obscures partial response.
- **First question:** "What was the baseline score, and what is it today?"
- **Data to pull:** serial rating scale scores, not narrative-only notes.

### Verbal denial of suicidal ideation with a behavioral inconsistency (giving away possessions, sudden calm after prior agitation, recent access to lethal means)
- **Usually means:** under-reporting, whether from stigma, a genuine wish not to be stopped, or dissociation from intent — the behavioral channel is more reliable than the verbal one here.
- **First question:** "What does collateral (family, staff, recent behavior) say, independent of the patient's verbal answer?"
- **Data to pull:** nursing/staff observation notes, family contact, recent means-access history (firearms, stockpiled medication).

### Lithium level not rechecked within 5 days of a dose change
- **Usually means:** the clinician is either not tracking that steady state takes ~5 half-lives (~5 days) to reach, or assuming linear extrapolation without confirmation — risking either subtherapeutic dosing or missed early toxicity.
- **First question:** "When was the level drawn relative to the last dose change, and is the patient at steady state yet?"
- **Data to pull:** lithium level with draw date/time relative to last dose change, concurrent renal function, and any new NSAID/ACE-inhibitor/diuretic use.

### Clozapine patient with ANC not drawn per the mandated schedule, or ANC in the 500–999/µL band still receiving standard dosing
- **Usually means:** a monitoring lapse that, if ANC continues falling, becomes an agranulocytosis risk that is preventable only by catching it at this band.
- **First question:** "Is this patient current on the REMS-mandated ANC schedule, and if the last ANC was in the moderate-neutropenia band, was dosing interrupted?"
- **Data to pull:** ANC trend (not a single value), REMS monitoring log, current dosing status.

### Antipsychotic started with no baseline metabolic panel on file
- **Usually means:** no comparison point exists to detect the drug-attributable metabolic change the ADA/APA monitoring schedule is designed to catch early.
- **First question:** "Was a baseline weight/BMI, BP, glucose, and lipid panel drawn before or within 3 months of starting this agent?"
- **Data to pull:** baseline metabolic panel date; if absent, order one now and flag the gap.

### Benzodiazepine on a standing (not PRN) schedule for more than 4–6 weeks with no taper plan documented
- **Usually means:** physiologic dependence is likely established, and abrupt discontinuation risks withdrawal seizure, not just symptom rebound.
- **First question:** "Is there a documented taper plan, and does the patient or a covering provider know not to stop this abruptly?"
- **Data to pull:** fill history/duration of continuous use, current dose, any recent abrupt gaps in supply.

### QTc not checked before or during treatment with a known QT-prolonging antipsychotic (e.g., ziprasidone, high-dose IV haloperidol)
- **Usually means:** an avoidable risk of torsades de pointes in a patient with unrecognized additive risk factors (other QT-prolonging drugs, hypokalemia, cardiac history).
- **First question:** "What is the baseline and current QTc, and what else on the med list also prolongs it?"
- **Data to pull:** baseline and follow-up ECG, full medication list cross-checked against a QT-prolongation reference, electrolytes.

### Bipolar spectrum screened positive on a single mood-elevation question without duration/functional-impairment criteria applied
- **Usually means:** over-diagnosis of hypomania/mania from normal mood variability, situational excitement, or substance effect — a documented overcorrection after clinicians get burned by missed bipolarity.
- **First question:** "Does the elevated-mood period meet the DSM-5-TR duration (4+ days hypomania, 7+ days or hospitalization for mania) and functional-impairment criteria, or is it being inferred from one question?"
- **Data to pull:** full mood episode history with duration and functional impact, collateral on baseline personality/mood variability.

### Discharge or "low risk" note with no protective/risk factors itemized, only a conclusion
- **Usually means:** the risk assessment reasoning either wasn't done thoroughly or wasn't documented — indistinguishable from not having occurred on later chart review, including in a malpractice context.
- **First question:** "What specific factors on each side were weighed to reach this conclusion?"
- **Data to pull:** the risk assessment documentation itself; if it's conclusion-only, that absence is the finding.

### Diagnosis unchanged across multiple failed trials with no re-audit of adherence or substance use
- **Usually means:** "treatment-resistant" is being treated as a fixed patient trait rather than re-examined — adherence gaps and undisclosed substance use are the two most common reversible causes of apparent resistance.
- **First question:** "Has adherence (pharmacy fill history) and current substance use been re-checked since the diagnosis was first made, or is the same diagnosis just being carried forward?"
- **Data to pull:** pharmacy fill/refill pattern, recent urine tox, direct re-interview on substance use since last screen.
