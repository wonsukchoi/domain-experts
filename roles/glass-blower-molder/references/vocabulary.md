# Vocabulary

### Coefficient of thermal expansion (COE)
A numeric value describing how much a specific glass expands or contracts per degree of temperature change, critical for determining whether two glasses can be safely joined.
**In use:** "COE 33 on this piece, COE 51 on that one — those aren't going to fuse into a stable joint no matter how clean it looks hot."
**Common misuse:** Assuming any two "glass" materials are compatible for fusing, without checking that their specific COE values are close enough to avoid cooling-stress cracking.

### Annealing point
The specific temperature at which internal stress in glass can be relieved through a controlled soak and slow cooling, distinct from the softening point where glass is workable.
**In use:** "Soak it at the annealing point long enough for the whole piece to equalize before starting the slow cool."
**Common misuse:** Confusing the annealing point with the working or softening temperature, when annealing happens at a specific, lower temperature range designed for stress relief rather than shaping.

### Working range (glass viscosity)
The temperature band within which a specific glass has the right viscosity for forming operations — blowing, drawing, shaping — bounded by the softening point and strain point.
**In use:** "Borosilicate's working range sits well above where soft glass works — can't just eyeball 'hot enough' across both."
**Common misuse:** Treating "hot enough to work" as a single threshold rather than a specific viscosity-defined range that varies by glass type and by the specific forming operation being performed.

### Strain point
The temperature below which glass is rigid enough that internal stress becomes effectively permanent if present at that point.
**In use:** "Cool it slowly through the strain point range — rushing that part is what locks stress in permanently."
**Common misuse:** Assuming stress can be relieved at any point during cooling, when cooling too quickly through the range near the strain point locks in stress that can no longer be relieved without reheating.

### Polariscope
An optical instrument used to detect and visualize residual stress in glass by observing how the material affects polarized light.
**In use:** "Ran it through the polariscope before handing it off — stress pattern's clean, no hot spots left from forming."
**Common misuse:** Skipping a polariscope check on a finished piece and relying on visual or tactile inspection alone, which cannot detect internal stress the way a polariscope can.

### Soak time (annealing)
The duration a glass piece is held at or near its annealing point to allow its entire cross-section to reach a uniform temperature before controlled cooling begins.
**In use:** "Soak time's calculated off the thickest wall section — the thin parts equalize faster, but they're not what sets the schedule."
**Common misuse:** Shortening soak time to speed up the process, leaving the piece's thicker sections still at a different temperature than its thinner sections when cooling begins.

### COE compatibility
The degree to which two different glass types' coefficients of thermal expansion are close enough that a fused joint between them won't develop excessive cooling stress.
**In use:** "These two test close enough on COE to call them compatible for this joint — logged both readings before proceeding."
**Common misuse:** Assuming COE compatibility only matters for dramatically different glass families, when even moderate COE differences between nominally similar glasses can cause joint failure.

### Borosilicate vs. soda-lime glass
Two common glass families with meaningfully different COE values, working temperatures, and thermal shock resistance.
**In use:** "That's soda-lime, not borosilicate — different working temperature and a much higher COE."
**Common misuse:** Treating all clear glass as interchangeable for a given technique, without verifying which family a specific piece of stock actually belongs to.

### Thermal shock (glass)
Fracture or cracking caused by rapid, uneven temperature change that induces stress exceeding the glass's tolerance, distinct from stress accumulated slowly from improper annealing.
**In use:** "That crack showed up after it went straight from a warm car to cold air — that's thermal shock, not an annealing failure."
**Common misuse:** Attributing a delayed crack solely to improper annealing when a later rapid temperature change may be the actual immediate trigger, even on a properly annealed piece with a COE mismatch or other underlying defect.

### Glory hole
A furnace opening used to reheat a piece of glass mid-process to maintain workable viscosity during forming.
**In use:** "Back to the glory hole for a reheat before the next shaping step — it's cooled below working range."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a piece reheated at the glory hole returns to exactly the same working state each time, without accounting for how repeated reheating cycles can affect the glass's condition over an extended working session.
