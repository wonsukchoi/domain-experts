# Vocabulary

Terms generalists get wrong or flatten — the value is in the misuse column, not the definition.

### Titration ceiling
The maximum rate a physician order or protocol permits for a given infusion before a new order or protocol step is required.
**In use:** "We're at the titration ceiling on norepi — I need the vasopressin order now, not a higher rate."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a soft suggestion that can be exceeded briefly "to buy time" while waiting for the physician — it's a scope boundary, not a guideline.

### MAP (mean arterial pressure)
The average pressure driving organ perfusion across the cardiac cycle, calculated roughly as diastolic + 1/3(systolic − diastolic); the usual resuscitation target is ≥65 mmHg.
**In use:** "MAP's holding at 68 on two pressors, but I want to see the lactate before I call that resuscitated."
**Common misuse:** Treated as the resuscitation endpoint by itself — an adequate MAP on climbing doses can still mean an under-perfused patient.

### Lactate clearance
The percentage drop in serial lactate measurements over a defined interval, used as a perfusion trend marker; target is commonly ≥10–20% per 2 hours.
**In use:** "Lactate cleared 26% in 75 minutes — the trend's better than the blood pressure number alone suggests."
**Common misuse:** Reading a single lactate value in isolation instead of as a trend against a prior draw and elapsed time.

### RASS (Richmond Agitation-Sedation Scale)
A −5 to +4 scale scoring sedation depth from unarousable to combative; ICU sedation is typically titrated to a specific ordered target, often −1 to 0.
**In use:** "She's a RASS of −2 against a target of −1 — we're over-sedated, not stable."
**Common misuse:** Charting the score without comparing it to the ordered target, so a deeper-than-intended sedation level goes unaddressed.

### CAM-ICU (Confusion Assessment Method for the ICU)
A validated bedside tool for delirium screening in mechanically ventilated or non-verbal patients, scored positive or negative based on four features (acute onset/fluctuation, inattention, altered consciousness, disorganized thinking).
**In use:** "He's CAM-ICU positive — before we increase sedation, let's rule out hypoxia and a new infection."
**Common misuse:** Using "sedated" and "delirious" interchangeably — a deeply sedated patient may be un-testable, not delirium-negative, and a light, agitated patient can be CAM-ICU positive without being under-sedated.

### SAT/SBT pairing (spontaneous awakening trial / spontaneous breathing trial)
A daily protocol pausing sedation to assess wakefulness (SAT), then, if safe, testing unassisted or minimally supported breathing (SBT) the same day.
**In use:** "SAT passed at 0900, moving straight into the SBT before the sedation gets restarted."
**Common misuse:** Running the SAT alone as a "sedation vacation" without the paired SBT, which forfeits most of the ventilator-day reduction the pairing was shown to produce.

### ABCDEF bundle
The PADIS-guideline-derived sequence — Assess/manage pain, Both SAT and SBT, Choice of sedation, Delirium monitoring, Early mobility, Family engagement — run to reduce ventilator days, delirium, and post-ICU functional decline.
**In use:** "We're behind on the E — he's met the mobility gate for two shifts and PT hasn't been in yet."
**Common misuse:** Treating it as a checklist of separate tasks rather than a sequence where each letter's outcome should inform the next (e.g., untreated pain undermines the SAT).

### Driving pressure
Plateau pressure minus PEEP; a ventilator mechanics number that reflects lung stress independent of absolute tidal volume, increasingly used alongside plateau pressure alone.
**In use:** "Plateau's technically under 30, but the driving pressure crept up — I want to flag that at rounds."
**Common misuse:** Ignored in favor of plateau pressure alone, missing cases where a low PEEP masks a high driving pressure on the same plateau reading.

### Permissive hypercapnia
Deliberately tolerating a higher-than-normal PaCO2/lower pH to keep tidal volume and plateau pressure within lung-protective limits.
**In use:** "We're letting the CO2 run up to protect the plateau pressure — that's permissive hypercapnia, not a ventilation failure."
**Common misuse:** Treated as an emergency needing correction rather than a deliberate tradeoff, prompting an unnecessary tidal-volume increase that undoes the lung-protective strategy.

### ICU-acquired weakness
Measurable neuromuscular weakness attributable to critical illness and its treatment (immobility, sedation, neuromuscular blockade, corticosteroids), distinct from the underlying disease process.
**In use:** "His weakness on the floor traces back to twelve sedated, immobile ICU days, not the pneumonia itself."
**Common misuse:** Attributed entirely to "how sick he was" rather than recognized as partly iatrogenic and partly preventable through the mobility and sedation-minimization elements of the bundle.

### De-resuscitation
The deliberate, protocolized removal of fluid (diuresis or ultrafiltration) once a shock patient stabilizes, reversing the positive balance accumulated during initial resuscitation.
**In use:** "He's 8 liters positive since admission and hemodynamically stable — starting de-resuscitation with a diuretic drip today."
**Common misuse:** Skipped because "fluids helped, so more/neutral is safer" — positive fluid balance carried for days independently worsens oxygenation and mortality risk.

### Vasoactive vs. inotrope
Vasoactive drugs primarily change vascular tone (e.g., norepinephrine raises MAP via vasoconstriction); inotropes primarily change cardiac contractility (e.g., dobutamine); several agents do both to varying degrees.
**In use:** "MAP's the problem here, not cardiac output — that's a vasoactive question before it's an inotrope question."
**Common misuse:** Using "pressor" as a catch-all for any hemodynamic drip, obscuring which physiologic lever a given drug actually pulls.

### Closed vs. open ICU
A closed ICU requires all orders to route through the intensivist/critical-care team; an open ICU lets the admitting physician (often a non-intensivist) retain primary order-writing authority with critical-care consultation.
**In use:** "This is a closed unit — the primary team can recommend, but the intensivist writes the vasopressor order."
**Common misuse:** Assuming escalation pathways are the same everywhere; who the RN calls first changes materially between the two models.

### Rapid shallow breathing index (RSBI)
Respiratory rate divided by tidal volume (in liters), used during an SBT to predict extubation success; a value under 105 is the commonly cited pass threshold.
**In use:** "RSBI's 68 on the T-piece trial — that's a pass, let's bring it to rounds for extubation."
**Common misuse:** Used as the sole extubation criterion, when it's one input alongside mental status, airway protection, and secretion burden.
