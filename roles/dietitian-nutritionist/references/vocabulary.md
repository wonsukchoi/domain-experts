# Dietitian / Nutritionist — Vocabulary

### Refeeding syndrome
A potentially fatal shift in fluids and electrolytes (especially phosphate, potassium, magnesium) that occurs when nutrition is reintroduced too quickly to a chronically malnourished or starved patient, driven by an insulin surge pulling electrolytes intracellularly.
**In use:** "This patient's got two major refeeding-risk criteria — we're starting at 10 kcal/kg and checking phos daily, not going straight to goal."
**Common misuse:** treating it as a concern only for visibly emaciated patients, missing it in obese patients with significant recent unintentional weight loss.

### Mifflin-St Jeor equation
A validated predictive formula for basal metabolic rate (BMR) using weight, height, age, and sex, considered more accurate than the older Harris-Benedict equation for most populations.
**In use:** "Mifflin-St Jeor gives us 1,369 kcal BMR; with the 1.2 stress factor that's about 1,640 kcal total need."
**Common misuse:** treating the output as an exact requirement rather than a ±10-15% estimate that needs clinical reassessment.

### Indirect calorimetry
A measurement technique using a patient's actual oxygen consumption and carbon dioxide production to calculate real energy expenditure, rather than estimating it from a formula.
**In use:** "We got a calorimetry read of 1,850 kcal — that's meaningfully higher than the Penn State equation estimate, so we're adjusting up."
**Common misuse:** assuming it's always available or always more accurate — it requires specific equipment and controlled conditions, and isn't practical for every patient.

### Nutrition-focused physical exam (NFPE)
A structured physical assessment for muscle wasting (temporal, clavicle, quadriceps), subcutaneous fat loss, edema, and functional status, used to confirm or refine a malnutrition diagnosis after a positive screening result.
**In use:** "MST was positive, so I did the NFPE and confirmed temporal wasting and reduced grip strength — that's moderate malnutrition, not just a risk flag."
**Common misuse:** skipping it and diagnosing malnutrition from the screening score alone; screening tools flag risk, they don't diagnose.

### Enteral nutrition
Delivering nutrition directly into the GI tract via a feeding tube (NG, OG, PEG, PEJ) when oral intake is inadequate but the gut is functional.
**In use:** "Gut's working post-op, so we're going enteral via NG rather than starting TPN."
**Common misuse:** conflating "enteral" with "oral" — enteral specifically means tube-delivered, bypassing voluntary swallowing.

### Parenteral nutrition (TPN)
Delivering nutrition intravenously, bypassing the GI tract entirely, used when the gut can't be safely used (obstruction, ischemia, severe malabsorption).
**In use:** "With the bowel obstruction unresolved, we're on TPN until surgery clears the gut for use."
**Common misuse:** using it as a default or convenience option instead of the fallback it's meant to be — it carries higher infection and metabolic-complication risk than enteral feeding.

### Stress/injury factor
A multiplier applied to calculated BMR to account for increased metabolic demand from illness, surgery, trauma, or burns.
**In use:** "Post-surgical recovery, so I'm applying a 1.2 stress factor rather than the 1.0 I'd use for a stable outpatient."
**Common misuse:** applying a flat factor regardless of the specific clinical state, or stacking multiple factors inappropriately (e.g. adding both activity and stress factors when the equation already accounts for bed rest).

### Malnutrition Screening Tool (MST) / Malnutrition Universal Screening Tool (MUST)
Validated, quick screening instruments (recent weight loss + appetite/intake questions for MST; weight loss + BMI + acute-disease effect for MUST) used to flag malnutrition risk for further assessment — not diagnostic on their own.
**In use:** "MST score of 4, that's positive — moving to a full nutrition-focused exam before we finalize anything."
**Common misuse:** treating a positive screen as a malnutrition diagnosis rather than a trigger for the confirming assessment.

### Adjusted body weight
A calculated weight used in energy/protein-needs equations for obese patients (BMI ≥30), correcting for the lower metabolic activity of excess adipose tissue relative to lean mass.
**In use:** "BMI's 34, so I used adjusted body weight instead of actual — otherwise we'd overshoot the calorie target."
**Common misuse:** applying adjusted body weight to non-obese patients, or forgetting to apply it and overestimating needs in an obese patient.

### Medical nutrition therapy (MNT)
The clinical, disease-specific application of nutrition science to manage a diagnosed condition (renal disease, diabetes, oncology, etc.), distinct from general dietary/wellness counseling.
**In use:** "This isn't a general healthy-eating conversation — she needs MNT for stage 4 CKD, which means a very different macronutrient target."
**Common misuse:** substituting generic "eat healthy" advice for a disease-specific protocol when a diagnosis requires one.

### Osmolality (of enteral formula)
A measure of solute concentration in a feeding formula; high-osmolality formulas can cause GI intolerance (cramping, diarrhea) if introduced too quickly or in a sensitive patient.
**In use:** "That formula's osmolality is high for a patient with a sensitive gut post-op — starting at a slower rate to reduce intolerance risk."
**Common misuse:** ignoring osmolality when selecting a formula or troubleshooting GI intolerance, focusing only on the caloric/protein content.

### Carbohydrate counting
A diabetes-management approach where a patient tracks grams of carbohydrate per meal to match insulin dosing or stay within a target range, rather than following a fixed exchange-list diet.
**In use:** "We switched her from the exchange-list approach to carb counting since she's on a flexible insulin regimen now."
**Common misuse:** treating carb counting and general low-carb dieting as the same thing — carb counting is a matching/dosing tool, not inherently a restriction target.
