# Legal Secretary — Vocabulary

### Imputed conflict
A conflict of interest attributed to every lawyer in a firm because one lawyer at the firm has it, under ABA Model Rule 1.10 — the conflict doesn't have to touch the assigned attorney personally.
**In use:** "Nakamura's old client is imputed to the whole firm, so Alvarez has to clear it even though he's never heard of them."
**Common misuse:** treating a conflicts check as complete once it's run against only the assigned attorney's own client list, missing firm-wide imputation.

### Substantially related (Rule 1.9)
The legal test for whether a former-client conflict bars a new, adverse representation — it bars the matter only if the former and new matters share enough factual overlap that confidential information from the first could be used against the former client in the second.
**In use:** "The lease dispute and the supply-contract breach aren't substantially related, so this clears once we document why."
**Common misuse:** assuming any former-client match is automatically disqualifying, rather than a fact-specific determination the attorney has to make.

### Standing order
A judge's individual, ongoing procedural requirements for their courtroom — separate from and sometimes stricter than the court's general local rules.
**In use:** "The local rules allow 25-page briefs, but Judge Park's standing order caps it at 20 — check both."
**Common misuse:** checking only the court's published local rules and assuming they're the complete formatting requirement, missing an individual judge's standing order.

### PDF/A
An archival PDF format standard required by many e-filing systems — distinct from a standard PDF, which most "print to PDF" or "save as PDF" functions produce by default.
**In use:** "The filing bounced because it was a regular PDF, not PDF/A — re-export with the compliant profile."
**Common misuse:** assuming any PDF satisfies an e-filing system's format requirement without checking whether PDF/A specifically is required.

### Event type
The specific category a filer selects when submitting a document through an e-filing system (e.g., "Motion to Dismiss" vs. "Motion — Other") — determines how the clerk's office and the docket process the filing.
**In use:** "It filed fine, but the clerk flagged it — wrong event type, should've been filed under Motion to Compel, not Motion — Miscellaneous."
**Common misuse:** picking the closest-sounding event type without confirming against the local rules' event-type list, risking a docketing error that can affect deadline calculations tied to that motion type.

### Conflicts screen (ethical screen)
A formal, documented set of procedures isolating an attorney or staff member with a conflict from a matter, allowing the firm to proceed under certain conditions rather than declining the representation outright.
**In use:** "We can keep the matter if we screen Nakamura from any involvement and document it — otherwise we have to decline."
**Common misuse:** treating an informal "I just won't work on it" as equivalent to a documented ethical screen, which doesn't satisfy the rule's requirements in most jurisdictions.

### Docketing
The act of entering a deadline, hearing date, or filing event into the firm's system of record (practice-management software) so it's tracked and generates reminders.
**In use:** "It's not really due until it's docketed — get it in the system today, not end of week."
**Common misuse:** treating a deadline as "handled" once it's calculated, without confirming it's actually entered into the tracked system.

### CM/ECF
The federal judiciary's Case Management/Electronic Case Files system — the standard e-filing platform for U.S. federal courts, with its own technical submission requirements distinct from state-court e-filing portals.
**In use:** "This is a federal matter, so it goes through CM/ECF, not the state e-filing portal we usually use."
**Common misuse:** assuming a state-court e-filing procedure (format, size limits, event types) transfers directly to federal CM/ECF filings, when the technical requirements differ by system.

### Statute of limitations vs. filing deadline
The statute of limitations is the outer legal deadline by which a claim must be filed or it's permanently barred; a filing deadline (e.g., a response due date) is a procedural deadline within an already-filed case — missing either is serious, but they carry different consequences and different calculation rules.
**In use:** "The statute runs in six weeks — that's a paralegal-level calculation for case strategy, separate from the response deadline we're tracking on the current motion."
**Common misuse:** conflating the two, or assuming this role calculates the statute-of-limitations date itself rather than routing that substantive legal-deadline judgment to the paralegal/attorney.

### Local rules
A specific court's own procedural and formatting rules, layered on top of the broader rules of civil/criminal procedure — vary by federal district, by state, and sometimes by individual courthouse within a state.
**In use:** "The Rules of Civil Procedure don't specify a font size, but this district's local rules require 13-point minimum."
**Common misuse:** assuming a firm's standard template already reflects every court's local rules, rather than re-verifying against the specific receiving court each time.
