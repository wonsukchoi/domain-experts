# Vocabulary

### CRI (constant rate infusion)
A drug delivered continuously via pump at a calculated mL/hr rate rather than as a bolus, used for analgesia (fentanyl, lidocaine) or sedation maintenance.
**In use:** "Start the fentanyl CRI at 19.2 mL/hr on the diluted solution, not the stock concentration."
**Common misuse:** Treating the CRI rate as fixed once calculated — it needs to be redone whenever the concentration, dilution, or patient weight changes, not just carried over from the last case.

### ETCO2 (end-tidal CO2)
The CO2 concentration measured at the end of exhalation via capnograph, reflecting ventilation adequacy.
**In use:** "ETCO2's climbed to 48 — check the circuit before assuming she needs more gas."
**Common misuse:** Reading a rising ETCO2 as "needs deeper anesthesia" by default, when it more often signals hypoventilation or a circuit problem that adding drug won't fix.

### MAC (minimum alveolar concentration)
The alveolar concentration of an inhalant anesthetic at which 50% of subjects don't move in response to a surgical stimulus — the reference point for how "deep" a given vaporizer percentage actually is.
**In use:** "We're running at about 1x MAC for iso in this patient; that's why she's still responding to the extraction."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a specific vaporizer percentage means the same depth across all patients — MAC shifts with other drugs on board, age, and temperature.

### ASA physical status
The American Society of Anesthesiologists' I–V classification of a patient's pre-anesthetic health, adopted in veterinary anesthesia to set monitoring intensity and drug protocol.
**In use:** "She's ASA III with the kidney values — we're running the higher-monitoring protocol, not the routine one."
**Common misuse:** Treating ASA status as a formality recorded once, rather than a number that changes what monitoring interval and drug choices are actually appropriate.

### Plane (of anesthesia)
The depth of anesthesia, assessed by a composite of reflexes (jaw tone, palpebral, eye position) and response to stimulus, not by vital signs alone.
**In use:** "Plane's appropriate — jaw tone's moderate, no response to the probe at the extraction site."
**Common misuse:** Equating "plane" with vaporizer percentage — the plane is what the patient is actually showing, which can lag or lead the dial setting.

### AVDC periodontal staging
The American Veterinary Dental College's Stage 0–4 classification of periodontal disease by attachment loss, distinct from visible calculus grade.
**In use:** "Visually it looked like a Stage 1 mouth, but probing put two sites at Stage 3 — radiographing before we decide on extraction."
**Common misuse:** Assigning stage from visible tartar/calculus alone rather than actual probing depth and attachment loss.

### PCV/TP (packed cell volume / total protein)
A chairside spun-hematocrit and refractometer reading used as the fastest pre-anesthetic anemia/hydration/protein screen.
**In use:** "PCV/TP's 22 and 4.1 — that goes to the doctor before we draw anything up."
**Common misuse:** Treating PCV/TP as a full bloodwork substitute rather than a quick screen that can still miss things a chemistry panel would catch.

### Six rights
The point-of-administration check (right patient, drug, dose, route, time, documentation) applied to every medication given, whether by a technician or an assistant.
**In use:** "Six rights checked against the chart before this goes in — not from memory."
**Common misuse:** Treating it as a one-time training concept rather than a check repeated at every single administration.

### Technique chart
A radiography reference table mapping measured tissue thickness at the collimated site to kVp/mAs settings, specific to a given generator.
**In use:** "The chart's built for this machine — recalibrate before assuming it transfers to the portable unit."
**Common misuse:** Applying a technique chart by species or scale weight instead of measured thickness at the actual site, or assuming a chart from one generator applies to another.

### Doppler vs. oscillometric (blood pressure)
Two non-invasive BP monitoring methods: Doppler gives an audible pulse signal and a systolic estimate (with a cuff and manual reading); oscillometric gives an automated cuff-cycle readout of systolic/diastolic/mean.
**In use:** "Oscillometric's reading low and erratic on this small patient — switch to Doppler to confirm."
**Common misuse:** Trusting an oscillometric number at face value on very small or hypotensive patients, where cuff-based automated readings are least reliable.

### Attachment loss
Loss of the tooth's supporting periodontal structures, measured by probing depth beyond normal sulcus depth — the actual basis for periodontal staging, distinct from visible calculus.
**In use:** "Calculus grade looks mild, but there's 40% attachment loss on the probe — that's a Stage 3, not a Stage 1."
**Common misuse:** Equating calculus grade (a visual/tactile surface finding) with periodontal stage (an attachment-loss finding) — they often don't match.

### Recovery
The post-anesthetic period from extubation until the patient is sternal, normothermic, and reflexes have returned to baseline — actively monitored, not a waiting period.
**In use:** "She's extubated but still hypothermic — recovery monitoring continues until she's warmed up and sternal."
**Common misuse:** Treating recovery as finished at extubation rather than continuing active monitoring through normothermia and full reflex return.
