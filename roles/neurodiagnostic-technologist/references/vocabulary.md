# Vocabulary

### 10-20 system
The standardized international scheme for placing scalp electrodes at positions 10% or 20% of measured skull landmarks apart, so recordings are comparable across labs and patients.
**In use:** "We're off by half a centimeter on T3 — remeasure before we call this montage standard."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a fixed template applied by eye rather than measured fresh on each patient; skull-size variation (especially pediatric) makes eyeballed placement produce genuinely different electrode locations.

### Montage
The specific arrangement of electrode pairs (bipolar) or electrode-to-reference comparisons (referential) displayed together, chosen to localize activity.
**In use:** "Switch to a transverse bipolar montage to localize that discharge better — the longitudinal chain is smearing it."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to be a display preference rather than an analytic choice; the same discharge can look focal in one montage and diffuse in another, and reading only one montage risks a localization error.

### Ictal-interictal continuum
The spectrum of EEG patterns in critically ill patients that fall between clearly normal/interictal and clearly ictal (seizure), including periodic discharges whose clinical significance is genuinely ambiguous.
**In use:** "This LPD is on the ictal-interictal continuum — it's not a clear seizure, but it's not nothing either; flag it for the epileptologist rather than calling it either way."
**Common misuse:** Collapsed into a binary "seizure or not" call when the entire point of the term is that some patterns are legitimately indeterminate and need clinical correlation, not a forced label.

### GPDs / LPDs (generalized/lateralized periodic discharges)
Standardized ACNS terminology for periodic waveforms occurring diffusely (GPDs) or over one hemisphere/region (LPDs), replacing older terms like "PLEDs."
**In use:** "Left hemisphere LPDs at 1.5 Hz, stable morphology over the last 20 minutes — no evolution, correlates with encephalopathy, not an active seizure right now."
**Common misuse:** Called "PLEDs" or "spikes" informally in ways that blur the standardized terminology the physician needs for a precise read; also often reported without the evolution/stability detail that determines their significance.

### Alarm criteria (IONM)
The pre-defined percentage or qualitative thresholds (e.g., SSEP amplitude down >50% and/or latency up >10% from that case's baseline) that trigger a real-time alert to the surgical team.
**In use:** "We're at both criteria now — amplitude's down 60%, latency's up 12% — that's an alert, not a watch-and-see."
**Common misuse:** Applied as a single universal number across modalities (using an SSEP amplitude rule for MEPs) or measured against a population reference instead of that patient's own case baseline.

### Activation procedure
A provocative maneuver performed during EEG (hyperventilation, photic stimulation, sleep/sleep deprivation) intended to increase the yield of capturing epileptiform activity that a resting record wouldn't show.
**In use:** "Photic drove a photoparoxysmal response at 14 Hz — document the flash rate and stop the sequence there."
**Common misuse:** Treated as optional boilerplate to rush through rather than a diagnostically load-bearing part of the study; skipping or truncating it without documenting the limitation changes what a "normal" result actually means.

### Photoparoxysmal response
An abnormal, generalized epileptiform discharge triggered by photic stimulation, distinct from the normal photic driving response (posterior rhythm entraining to the flash rate).
**In use:** "That's driving, not a photoparoxysmal response — it's following the flash rate exactly and stops the instant we stop flashing."
**Common misuse:** Confused with normal photic driving because both appear time-locked to the flash; the distinguishing feature is discharge morphology and whether it outlasts the stimulus, not just the timing correlation.

### Free-run vs. triggered EMG
Free-run EMG continuously monitors spontaneous muscle activity for neurotonic discharges (nerve irritation); triggered EMG delivers a stimulus and measures an evoked compound muscle action potential to confirm a specific pathway is intact.
**In use:** "Free-run's been quiet — let's get a triggered response off that pedicle screw to confirm it's not breached medially."
**Common misuse:** Treated as interchangeable monitoring modes; free-run tells you about irritation happening now, triggered tells you about the integrity of a specific structure, and neither substitutes for the other.

### Nonconvulsive status epilepticus (NCSE)
Prolonged seizure activity on EEG without the overt convulsive movements of generalized tonic-clonic seizures, often presenting only as altered mental status.
**In use:** "Unresponsive since this morning with no clinical seizure activity — cEEG showed evolving discharges, this is NCSE until proven otherwise."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to require some visible clinical seizure sign; by definition NCSE can present with nothing more dramatic than confusion or unresponsiveness, which is exactly why continuous monitoring exists.

### Impedance
The electrical resistance measured between the scalp electrode and skin, used as a proxy for good electrode contact.
**In use:** "T4's reading 18 kOhm — re-prep before we trust anything on that channel."
**Common misuse:** Checked once at setup and never rechecked; impedance drifts over a long recording (paste drying, patient movement) and a channel that started clean can degrade mid-study.

### Sensitivity (gain)
The amplification setting controlling how many microvolts of signal correspond to one millimeter of vertical deflection on the display.
**In use:** "Bump sensitivity down to 5 uV/mm on this one, the muscle artifact is blowing out the scale at 7."
**Common misuse:** Confused with filter settings; sensitivity changes how big the trace looks, filters change which frequencies are shown — adjusting one to fix a problem caused by the other doesn't work.

### Reformatting
Reviewing an already-recorded EEG in a different montage or with different filter settings after the fact, without re-recording the patient.
**In use:** "Reformat to a referential montage before you call that discharge focal — the bipolar chain can create a false phase reversal."
**Common misuse:** Skipped because the raw acquisition montage "looked fine," missing localization errors (like a false phase reversal) that only resolve when the same data is viewed a second way.

### Breach (pedicle screw, IONM context)
A medial or lateral pedicle wall violation by a surgical screw, checked for using triggered EMG stimulation through the screw.
**In use:** "Threshold's at 8 mA on that screw, well above the 15 mA typical breach cutoff some labs use — but confirm against this program's stated threshold, not a remembered number."
**Common misuse:** A single numeric threshold quoted from memory as if it's universal; breach-detection thresholds are program- and technique-specific and should be confirmed against the local protocol, not treated as a fixed constant.
