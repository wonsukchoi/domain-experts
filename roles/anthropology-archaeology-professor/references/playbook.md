# Playbook

Filled templates a postsecondary anthropology/archaeology professor actually runs. Starting points to adapt, not scripts.

## 1. Tenure/promotion dossier checklist (pre-tenure, year 6 of a 7-year clock)

Built to the format a P&T committee outside the discipline can score, not to how the candidate would narrate their own career.

**Structure, with a filled example (mid-sized state university, R2):**

1. **CV, reverse-chronological**, tagged by category the institution's criteria actually use: peer-reviewed articles (list separately from book chapters), external grants funded (with amount and funder), invited talks, courses taught with enrollment.
2. **Personal statement (2-3 pages)**, organized around the institution's own three headings (research/teaching/service) in the order the written criteria weight them — not the order the candidate finds most compelling.
3. **External review letters: 6-8 solicited**, from full professors at peer or aspirant institutions, at arm's length from the candidate (no co-authors, no dissertation advisors). *Example: candidate proposes 10 names; department chair selects 6, committee adds 2 more the candidate didn't nominate, to avoid a friendly-letter-only file.*
4. **Teaching portfolio**: syllabi for all courses taught, aggregated evaluation scores *disaggregated by course type* (gen-ed vs. major-track, required vs. elective), a 1-page teaching philosophy, and any peer classroom observation reports.
5. **Publication/citation summary**: full-text or accepted-proof copies of all peer-reviewed work, plus a citation count pulled the same month the dossier is assembled (not months earlier) so the number matches what reviewers will find themselves.

**Reconciliation check before submission:** does the self-statement's characterization of the candidate's subfield standing match what the solicited letters actually say? If letters hedge ("solid," "promising") where the self-statement claims "leading," fix the self-statement — the letters are what the committee weighs.

**Cadence:** third-year (mid-tenure) review is a lower-stakes rehearsal of the exact same file — treat it as the dry run, not a formality to survive.

## 2. Program-viability self-study (numbers-first format)

Use when a department is flagged for enrollment-driven review. Lead with the section a provost's office actually reads.

**Section order:**

```
1. HEADLINE NUMBER: cost-per-SCH vs. the institution's policy threshold
   (this is the number that determines whether the review continues)
2. SCH BREAKDOWN by course, with gen-ed share called out explicitly
3. MAJORS/MINORS TREND — presented, not hidden, but framed against SCH
4. PEER-INSTITUTION BENCHMARK (2-3 comparable departments' cost-per-SCH,
   if obtainable — undercuts a "this is uniquely inefficient" framing)
5. PROPOSED ACTION — a concrete curricular change (add/cut a specific
   section), never "we will work to increase enrollment"
```

**Filled cost-per-SCH table (from the worked example in SKILL.md):**

| Item | Value |
|---|---|
| Instructional cost | $518,000 |
| Total SCH | 1,256 |
| Cost/SCH | $412 |
| University policy threshold | $450 |
| Gen-ed share of SCH | 78% |

**Rule:** never submit a self-study whose only response to a declining-majors finding is a defense of the discipline's value. The committee already knows the discipline has value; it is asking whether the arithmetic clears its threshold.

## 3. Grant budget justification (NSF Cultural Anthropology / Archaeology Program, filled example)

For a 2-year, $45,000 doctoral dissertation research improvement-style award. NSF scores on **intellectual merit** and **broader impacts** as separately labeled sections — a narrative that blends them reads as unscored on one criterion.

```
BUDGET JUSTIFICATION

Personnel: $0 (PI unpaid during academic-year fieldwork; advisor
  uncompensated per institutional policy)

Fieldwork travel & subsistence: $18,000
  - International airfare (2 researchers, 1 round trip): $3,200
  - In-country transport (12 months, local rates): $6,800
  - Lodging/subsistence (12 months field season): $8,000

Equipment: $4,500
  - GPS units (2): $1,800
  - Digital recording equipment: $1,200
  - Laptop for field data entry: $1,500

Supplies: $2,500 (translation/transcription services, field notebooks,
  sample collection materials)

Community compensation/collaboration: $6,000
  (research assistants/interpreters from the host community,
  paid at the locally prevailing rate — see broader impacts)

Analysis: $9,000 (radiocarbon dating, 6 samples at $500 avg,
  lab fees, statistical software license)

Dissemination: $5,000 (conference travel x2, open-access
  publication fee for one resulting article)

TOTAL: $45,000

INTELLECTUAL MERIT: [project-specific — the theoretical question
  this fieldwork answers that existing data cannot]

BROADER IMPACTS: local research-assistant employment and training
  (see Community compensation line), open-access publication
  commitment, data deposit in [named repository] on a stated timeline.
```

**Portfolio rule:** submit an equivalent proposal to Wenner-Gren's Dissertation Fieldwork Grants program in the same cycle — NSF and Wenner-Gren funding rates each run roughly 8-15%, and a single-source bet against those odds routinely costs a field season.

## 4. Field school operating plan (break-even check)

Run before committing to offer a field school, not after enrollment closes.

**Fixed-cost side (example, 6-week summer excavation field school):**

| Item | Cost |
|---|---|
| Excavation permit / SHPO compliance fee | $2,500 |
| Faculty summer salary (1 director, 6 weeks) | $9,000 |
| Field vehicle rental + fuel | $3,000 |
| Site insurance rider | $1,200 |
| **Fixed cost total** | **$15,700** |

**Break-even headcount:** at $2,600 tuition-equivalent per student (6 credit hours at the field-course rate), $15,700 / $2,600 ≈ **6.04 students** — the program needs 7 enrolled students to clear fixed costs (6 breaks roughly even; anything below 6 runs at a loss before counting per-student variable costs like lodging).

**Rule:** if enrollment sits below 6 by the registration deadline two summers running, cancel or merge with a partner institution's field school rather than running a third loss-making season, unless an endowment specifically subsidizes the program.

## 5. Syllabus compliance block (filled example)

The four elements a syllabus needs regardless of course content — checked at the start of every term, not assumed carried over from last year's file.

```
ACCOMMODATIONS: Students with a documented accommodation letter from
  [Disability Services office] should contact the instructor within
  the first two weeks of the term to arrange implementation.

ACADEMIC INTEGRITY: Violations (plagiarism, unauthorized collaboration,
  fabricated field/lab data) are referred to [the institution's
  academic integrity office] per policy [citation]; a first offense
  in this course results in a zero on the assignment, not an
  automatic course failure, pending the office's review.

TITLE IX: I am a mandated reporter. If you disclose an incident of
  sexual harassment or misconduct, I am required to report it to
  [Title IX office]; confidential resources are available at
  [counseling center contact].

ARTIFACT/FIELD SAFETY (archaeology courses only): all field and lab
  work follows the RPA code of conduct and site-specific safety
  briefing; students who violate a stated field safety rule may be
  removed from further field sessions without refund, per [policy].
```
