# Vocabulary

Terms generalists flatten together that a postsecondary anthropology/archaeology professor keeps sharply separate — the value is in the misuse, not the definition.

### Student credit hour (SCH)

The unit institutions use to fund departments: one student enrolled in one credit hour of coursework for one term. A course's SCH is enrollment × credit hours, summed across sections.

**In use:** "The intro course alone generates 576 SCH a year — that's the number the provost's office actually funds us on, not our 11 majors."

**Common misuse:** treating "enrollment" or "majors" as interchangeable with SCH; a large low-level gen-ed course can generate more SCH than an entire major track combined.

### Cost per SCH

Total departmental instructional cost divided by total SCH generated, the metric most program-viability policies actually threshold against.

**In use:** "We're at $412 per SCH against their $450 policy line — we clear the bar even with majors down."

**Common misuse:** computing it off major-track courses only, ignoring gen-ed sections, which produces a badly inflated (and misleading) number.

### Carnegie Classification

The institution-type taxonomy (R1/R2 doctoral, master's-granting, baccalaureate, etc.) that determines the actual norms — teaching load, expected publication pace — a given faculty position is judged against.

**In use:** "Don't benchmark her tenure case against an R1 monograph expectation — this is a Carnegie master's-granting institution with a 4-4 load."

**Common misuse:** applying research-institution productivity norms wholesale to a teaching-focused institution's tenure review, or the reverse.

### Probationary period (tenure clock)

The fixed window — commonly up to seven years under AAUP guidance — during which a faculty member must build and submit a tenure case before an up-or-out decision.

**In use:** "Her parental leave stopped the clock for a year, but it didn't extend the NSF proposal deadline that's tied to the calendar year, not the clock."

**Common misuse:** assuming every deadline tied to the career (grants, fellowships, journal special issues) pauses in step with an institutional tenure-clock extension; most external deadlines don't.

### External review letter

A solicited assessment from an arm's-length full professor at a peer or aspirant institution, evaluating a tenure/promotion candidate's standing in the actual field — the mechanism a committee outside the discipline relies on to benchmark the file.

**In use:** "The letters, not the CV, are what convinces a business-school dean that this monograph matters in archaeology."

**Common misuse:** treating letter solicitation as a pro forma step to check off, rather than as the file's actual evidentiary core that the self-statement must match.

### Broader impacts (NSF)

A separately scored NSF review criterion covering a proposal's benefit beyond the specific research question — training, public engagement, community collaboration, data infrastructure.

**In use:** "Community research-assistant employment goes under broader impacts, not personnel — it's evaluated on a different rubric line."

**Common misuse:** writing one generic outreach paragraph reused across every proposal instead of a project-specific plan, which reviewers routinely flag as unscored.

### Informed consent (ethnographic fieldwork)

Ongoing, process-based agreement from a research participant to be studied and to have specific material used in specific ways — not a one-time signature that covers all future uses.

**In use:** "She re-confirmed consent before using that interview clip in the documentary — the original consent form only covered the written dissertation."

**Common misuse:** treating a signed intake form from year one of a multi-year fieldwork relationship as blanket coverage for every later use of the material.

### NAGPRA / repatriation

Legal obligations under the Native American Graves Protection and Repatriation Act attaching to human remains and associated funerary, sacred, or cultural-patrimony objects, regardless of whether they sit in a museum, a lab, or a teaching collection.

**In use:** "The field-school teaching collection has to go through the same NAGPRA inventory process as the museum's — it's not exempt because it's for instruction."

**Common misuse:** assuming NAGPRA applies only to formal museum accessions and not to departmental teaching or reference collections built from prior excavations.

### Field school

A supervised, credit-bearing excavation or fieldwork course that also functions as a credentialing experience toward Register of Professional Archaeologists (RPA) qualification for CRM employment.

**In use:** "Cutting the field school doesn't just lose a course — it removes the fieldwork hours our majors need for RPA eligibility."

**Common misuse:** treating a field school as equivalent to a study-abroad enrichment program rather than a credential-bearing prerequisite for the discipline's main employment path.

### Gen-ed service course

A lower-division course that satisfies a university-wide general education requirement, taken predominantly by non-majors, that generates the bulk of a small department's SCH.

**In use:** "Two of our four intro sections are gen-ed service courses — that's most of our SCH, and it's why the department survives a shrinking major."

**Common misuse:** treating gen-ed teaching as lesser or "not real" teaching for tenure-case purposes, when it is often the course type generating the department's institutional value.

### Contingent/adjunct faculty

Non-tenure-track instructors, typically hired per course or per term, who nationally teach a substantial share of undergraduate instruction.

**In use:** "Three of our six intro sections are adjunct-taught this year — that's up from one section two years ago, and it changes the staffing conversation with the dean."

**Common misuse:** assuming appointment type tracks with teaching quality, or ignoring rising adjunct share as a program-review vulnerability separate from the quality question.

### Course buyout

Using grant funds to pay the department for a reduction in a faculty member's normal teaching load, freeing time for the funded research.

**In use:** "The NSF budget covers a one-course buyout each year of the grant — file the effort-reallocation form with the chair so the department's teaching schedule reflects it."

**Common misuse:** assuming a buyout automatically reduces total workload rather than shifting effort from teaching to the funded research, and forgetting the department-side paperwork that has to match the grant's effort certification.

### Program viability review

An institutional process evaluating whether a department continues, typically triggered by enrollment or budget thresholds and resolved primarily on cost and SCH metrics.

**In use:** "The self-study needs to lead with cost-per-SCH — that's what triggers or clears a program viability review, not a defense of the discipline."

**Common misuse:** treating the review as purely about majors count or purely about the discipline's intellectual merit, missing that it is functionally a cost-accounting decision.

### h-index

A citation-based productivity metric (h papers with at least h citations each) sometimes pulled into tenure dossiers as a shorthand for research impact.

**In use:** "Cite the h-index as one data point in the file, not the headline — anthropology's citation half-life and field size don't compare to a STEM h-index."

**Common misuse:** using an h-index as a cross-field productivity comparator, ignoring that anthropology/archaeology citation norms (slower accrual, book-heavy output) make raw cross-field comparison misleading.
