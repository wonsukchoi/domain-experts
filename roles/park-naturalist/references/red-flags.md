# Red flags

Smell tests a naturalist should catch before a program ships or a season closes out, each with what it usually means, the first question to ask, and the data to pull.

## 1. "Would recommend" score drops >1 point on a 5-point scale while "informative" holds steady

**Usually means:** the content is fine but the format or delivery changed — commonly a shift from participatory to lecture, or a theme that got diluted into a multi-species overview. Content and experience are different survey dimensions; a drop in one without the other points at delivery, not facts.

**First question:** "What changed about *how* this program is delivered between the season it scored well and now — not what changed about the content?"

**Data to pull:** program outline/script from both seasons side by side; staffing turnover dates; open-comment text from the low-scoring season, searched for "lecture," "rushed," "couldn't see/hear."

## 2. Attendance falls >20% over two consecutive comparable seasons

**Usually means:** either a real program-quality issue (see #1) or an external factor masquerading as one — a schedule conflict with a more popular concurrent event, a marketing/calendar-listing lapse, or a access/parking change. Don't redesign a program that's actually a marketing problem.

**First question:** "Did the booking/registration numbers drop, or did no-shows rise? Those point at different fixes."

**Data to pull:** registration vs. actual-attendance counts (not just headcount at the trailhead); calendar-listing accuracy check; any competing event on the same dates; weather log for the affected dates.

## 3. A program outline has more than 5 supporting points under one theme

**Usually means:** either there's no real single theme (it's an overview wearing a theme statement), or the naturalist added content each season without removing anything. Both produce the same symptom: high "informative," lower engagement and recall.

**First question:** "Say the theme in one breath — does it still cover everything on this outline?"

**Data to pull:** the program outline itself, dated version history if one exists, and which points were added in which season.

## 4. A "no change" reading on an invasive-species patch that hasn't actually been re-measured

**Usually means:** the monitoring visit was skipped or rushed, and the previous season's number got carried forward. A skipped survey and a genuinely stable patch produce identical paperwork, which is exactly the trap.

**First question:** "When was this patch's perimeter actually walked and measured this season — show me the date, not the report."

**Data to pull:** GPS/measurement log with dates for the site over the last 3 seasons; staffing/coverage gaps during the monitoring window; weather or access closures that could have prevented the survey.

## 5. A guided-walk group is visibly strung out past voice range

**Usually means:** the group exceeded the interpretive cap (15–20 for a standard walk) without a route or format adjustment, often because a large drop-in crowd showed up unannounced and splitting felt logistically harder than proceeding.

**First question:** "What's the actual headcount right now, and does this route have a second trained adult to help manage spacing?"

**Data to pull:** trailhead headcount vs. the route's stated cap; whether a second staff/volunteer is present; whether this is a recurring pattern (check sign-in sheets for the last several dates) that argues for a standing second date rather than a one-off overflow.

## 6. Enforcement action (citation, program removal from the trail) logged with no interpretive attempt noted first

**Usually means:** either the situation genuinely required an immediate stop (active resource damage, safety risk) or the naturalist defaulted to enforcement under time/crowd pressure rather than explanation — a pattern worth catching before it becomes the norm for that staff member.

**First question:** "Walk me through what was said to the visitor before the citation — what was the explanation, and did they get a chance to respond to it?"

**Data to pull:** incident log narrative; whether this staff member's incident rate is an outlier against peers on comparable shifts; whether the same violation recurs at the same location (which points at a signage/interpretation gap, not a visitor-behavior problem).

## 7. Citizen-science or EDDMapS submissions from one program spike, then vanish

**Usually means:** either a genuine new detection wave (real signal) or a naturalist over-reporting low-confidence sightings, often after a training refresher makes them newly alert to a species — worth distinguishing before regional coordinators mobilize a response crew for noise.

**First question:** "Pull the photos attached to the last 10 submissions — do they show the diagnostic features, or could half of these be a look-alike?"

**Data to pull:** submission photos and confidence notes; verification/rejection rate from the regional coordinator; whether submissions cluster right after a training session (suggests over-alertness) or are geographically spreading (suggests real spread).

## 8. A wildlife buffer distance is being treated as negotiable "if the group is quiet"

**Usually means:** the buffer distance is being justified by visitor behavior (noise, movement) rather than the actual basis for the distance, which for nesting/denning species is about disturbance from *presence*, not noise — a quiet group at half the buffer distance is not equivalent to a normal group at full distance.

**First question:** "What's the actual source for this buffer number — a species-specific agency guideline, or a rule of thumb someone's applying more loosely than it was set?"

**Data to pull:** the state wildlife agency's published buffer guidance for the specific species and season; the route's actual measured distance from the sensitive site; any documented behavioral response (flushing, alarm calls) logged on past dates at that distance.
