# Vocabulary

Terms generalists conflate that practitioners keep sharply separate.

## Interpretation vs. education

**Interpretation** is a communication process that reveals meaning and relationships through firsthand experience with objects, artifacts, or landscapes — its goal is a felt connection, not information transfer. **Education** (in the formal-curriculum sense) is structured instruction against defined learning objectives, typically for a captive audience with an assessment attached.

**In use:** "The school-group version needs to hit the state science standard, so that's education with an interpretive style layered on — the Saturday drop-in version is pure interpretation, no standard to satisfy."

**Common misuse:** using "interpretation" as a fancy synonym for "explaining facts about nature." Reciting accurate information is not interpretation by Tilden's definition even if it's delivered outdoors by a person in a uniform.

## Theme vs. topic

A **topic** is the subject matter ("owls," "wetland ecology"). A **theme** is the one complete-sentence idea the program wants a visitor to walk away holding ("owls hunt by sound, not sight, and that's why the woods have to go quiet"). A topic can support many different themes.

**In use:** "We're not short on topic — we could talk about owls for two hours. We're short on a theme, which is why the outline has no shape."

**Common misuse:** writing "owls" in the theme field of a program plan. A topic isn't testable in a sentence; a theme is, and if you can't say it in one breath, it isn't done yet.

## Carrying capacity vs. group size limit

**Carrying capacity** is the ecological or experiential threshold beyond which repeated visitor presence measurably degrades the resource or the visitor experience for others — a site-level property, often set by trampling studies, soil compaction data, or wildlife disturbance research. A **group size limit** is the operational cap set for a single program or event, which should be derived from carrying capacity but often gets set by staffing convenience instead.

**In use:** "The 18-person cap on the Owl Prowl isn't arbitrary — it's set below the route's estimated carrying capacity so a normal season of programming doesn't push the trail toward it."

**Common misuse:** treating group-size limits as purely a program-logistics number ("that's just how many people fit in earshot") when the harder, slower-moving number underneath it is ecological.

## Phenology

The study of the timing of recurring biological events — bud break, first bloom, migratory arrival, leaf senescence — and how that timing shifts year to year, often tracked against climate variables.

**In use:** "First bloom on the trillium has moved eight days earlier over the eleven seasons we've logged it in Nature's Notebook — that's the kind of shift you only see because someone kept walking the same transect every year."

**Common misuse:** treating a single season's early or late date as meaningful on its own. Phenology only says something as a multi-year trend against a consistent monitoring protocol; one warm March is weather, not a phenology finding.

## Citizen science

Structured data collection performed by volunteers or visitors (often via platforms like iNaturalist or eBird) using a protocol designed so the data is usable by professional researchers, not just informally interesting.

**In use:** "We don't need a grad student to run the phenology transect — we need the volunteers trained to the Nature's Notebook protocol so their observations are actually comparable to last year's."

**Common misuse:** conflating any visitor-submitted sighting with citizen science. An untrained, unprotocoled report is an anecdote; citizen science requires the structure (consistent site, consistent method, ID verification path) that makes the data poolable.

## Early detection vs. established infestation (invasive species)

**Early detection** refers to a new occurrence of an invasive species caught while it's still small enough that eradication is realistic — the entire premise of systems like EDDMapS. An **established infestation** has passed the point where removal is the goal; management shifts to containment or suppression.

**In use:** "This is still early detection — six plants on a quarter acre. Report it now and a crew can pull it in an afternoon. Wait a season and it's a containment project."

**Common misuse:** using "invasive species management" as one undifferentiated bucket. The decision tree, budget, and urgency for a six-plant early detection are completely different from a 40-acre established infestation, and treating them the same wastes the early-detection window that makes eradication possible at all.

## Provocation (Tilden's sense)

In Tilden's framework, **provocation** means prompting the visitor to think, question, or seek meaning on their own — the stated "chief aim" of interpretation, as distinct from instruction, which delivers a predetermined conclusion.

**In use:** "Ending on 'why do you think it goes quiet at dusk?' instead of just stating the answer is the provocation Tilden's talking about — it's a small design choice, not a personality trait."

**Common misuse:** treating "provocation" as license to be vague or withhold information the audience actually wants. Provocation is a specific technique (open questions, unresolved tension, firsthand discovery) layered onto solid content — it isn't a synonym for keeping visitors confused.

## Buffer distance vs. closure

A **buffer distance** is a recommended or required minimum distance from a sensitive resource (nest, den, rare plant population) that visitors and programs are expected to maintain while the area otherwise remains open. A **closure** removes access to an area entirely, typically because a buffer distance can't practically be maintained given the trail layout.

**In use:** "We don't need to close the whole loop — the overlook is well outside the buffer distance for that nest. It's the spur trail that has to close because it cuts inside it."

**Common misuse:** using "closure" and "buffer" interchangeably in visitor communication, which either alarms visitors unnecessarily (implying nothing nearby is accessible) or, worse, understates a genuine closure as a soft suggestion.

## Waysides vs. site bulletins vs. exhibit text

A **wayside** is a permanent outdoor sign, typically weatherproofed and expensive to change, meant to last years. A **site bulletin** is a printed handout, cheap to revise seasonally, that carries more detail or timeliness than a wayside can. **Exhibit text** is indoor interpretive copy in a visitor center, generally revised on a longer cycle than bulletins but shorter than waysides.

**In use:** "Don't put this season's trail-closure date on the wayside — that's a two-year sign. Put it on the site bulletin, which we reprint every spring."

**Common misuse:** putting time-sensitive information (seasonal closures, current bloom status) on a wayside because it seemed like the most visible spot, guaranteeing it goes stale before the sign's replacement cycle comes around.
