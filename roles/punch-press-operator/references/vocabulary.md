# Vocabulary

### Point of operation
The specific area on a press where material is actually positioned, formed, or cut — the location where the punch and die interact, and where contact carries catastrophic injury risk.
**In use:** "Nobody's hands go near the point of operation while the press is in normal cycling mode — that's what the guard and light curtain are for."
**Common misuse:** Treating the point of operation as safe to briefly access "just to check something" while the press remains in normal cycling capability — any access to this area requires the press to be in its designated safe mode (lockout or single-stroke with guard intact), not a judgment call based on how quick the task seems.

### Die clearance
The precisely controlled gap between a punch and its matching die opening, which determines cut quality and must be set specifically for the material type and thickness being run.
**In use:** "Clearance chart says point-oh-oh-six for this gauge steel — that's what we're setting, not whatever the die was left at."
**Common misuse:** Leaving die clearance at a previous job's setting without verifying it against the current material — clearance too tight produces excess burr and premature wear, too loose produces a torn edge, and both symptoms can be mistaken for tool wear if clearance itself isn't checked first.

### Feed pitch
The precise distance material advances between each press cycle on a progressive die, which must remain consistent for each station's tooling to operate on correctly positioned material.
**In use:** "Pitch drifted six thousandths short — that's small enough to miss visually but enough to throw off alignment by the time material reaches station four."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a feed system holds pitch reliably without periodic verification during a run — gradual wear (in feed rollers, for example) can cause a slow drift that's easy to miss until a downstream station's defect appears, at which point the actual cause (feed pitch, not that station's tooling) isn't obvious without direct measurement.

### Progressive die
A die with multiple sequential stations, each performing a different operation on the same strip of material as it advances through the press, with alignment between stations depending on consistent feed pitch.
**In use:** "Four-station progressive — pilot holes go in at station one, final profile cut happens at station four using those same holes for alignment."
**Common misuse:** Diagnosing a progressive die defect by focusing only on the station where the defect appears — a downstream station's misalignment is frequently caused by an upstream feed pitch issue, not a problem at the station showing the visible symptom.

### Tonnage (press tonnage)
The force capacity of a press, which must exceed the actual force required for a given material and operation with adequate margin — undersized tonnage can produce a defective part or accelerate tooling/press wear.
**In use:** "This gauge and this material hardness together push required tonnage higher than the last job — recalculating before we assume the press has enough capacity."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a die's tonnage requirement is fixed because "the press has run this die before" — a material thickness or hardness change can shift required tonnage even on a familiar die and job.

### Die protection sensor
A sensor system detecting fault conditions (a misfeed, a part not ejected, foreign material in the die) that could damage tooling or create a hazard if the press continues cycling.
**In use:** "Die protection tripped — stopping to find out why, not resetting and hoping it clears on the next stroke."
**Common misuse:** Bypassing or repeatedly resetting a die protection alarm to keep production moving — the sensor exists specifically because the fault it detects can destroy expensive tooling or eject hazardous material, and repeated resets without investigation risk exactly that outcome.

### Burr
A rough, raised edge left on a cut or punched part where material didn't shear cleanly, commonly caused by incorrect die clearance or worn tooling.
**In use:** "Burr's showing up along that whole edge — checking clearance against spec before assuming the die needs sharpening."
**Common misuse:** Assuming burr always indicates tool wear requiring die maintenance — incorrect clearance for the current material produces identical-looking symptoms, and clearance should be checked before concluding the die itself is worn.

### Two-hand control
A press safety system requiring an operator to press two separate controls simultaneously to initiate a cycle, ensuring both hands are away from the point of operation when the press activates.
**In use:** "Two-hand control's not optional even for a fast repetitive job — both hands stay on those buttons, full stop."
**Common misuse:** Rigging, taping, or otherwise defeating a two-hand control to allow single-handed or hands-free operation for speed — this defeats the entire safety mechanism's purpose and is a documented root cause of press-related amputations.

### Single-stroke mode
A press operating mode allowing one controlled cycle at a time, typically used for setup, die checks, or troubleshooting, distinct from continuous production cycling.
**In use:** "Switching to single-stroke to check die alignment — not doing this with the press in continuous run mode."
**Common misuse:** Assuming single-stroke mode alone makes hand access near the point of operation safe without also confirming guards remain in place — single-stroke mode reduces but doesn't eliminate risk, and the full designated safe-access procedure (which may include lockout for certain tasks) still applies.

### Lockout/tagout (press-specific)
The procedure isolating a press's power and stored energy sources before any task requiring direct access to its point of operation or moving components, distinct from single-stroke or guarded operation.
**In use:** "Full lockout for the die change — this isn't a single-stroke-and-guard situation, we need the press fully de-energized."
**Common misuse:** Substituting single-stroke mode or guard reliance for full lockout on a task that actually requires it (like a die change or major adjustment) — different tasks require different levels of energy isolation, and using a lesser safeguard than the task actually requires is itself a safety failure.
