# Library Assistant, Clerical — Vocabulary

### Call number
The full classification-plus-author code (e.g., Dewey or LC classification plus a Cutter number) that determines an item's shelf location and order relative to its neighbors.
**In use:** "The call number puts it in 610-619, but check the Cutter before you shelve it — that's what sets the order within the range."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a single opaque string rather than two parts (classification range, then Cutter) that each drive a different part of shelf order — this is exactly the confusion behind digit-transposition misshelving.

### Cutter number
The alphanumeric code appended to a call number, usually derived from the author's or title's name, that orders items within the same classification range.
**In use:** "Nine of the fourteen flagged items had the same Cutter-digit transposition — that's a sort-step problem, not fourteen random mistakes."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to be assigned arbitrarily rather than following a specific alphabetic-to-numeric conversion table, which is why transposition errors cluster in predictable ways.

### Shelf-reading
The scheduled process of walking a call-number range and verifying every item is in correct order, distinct from shelving (putting new returns away) which happens continuously.
**In use:** "Today's shelf-read on 610-619 came back at 7% — well over target, needs a root-cause breakdown before we just reshelve and move on."
**Common misuse:** Conflated with routine reshelving of daily returns — shelf-reading is a systematic accuracy audit of items already believed to be correctly placed, not the act of putting a returned book away.

### Hold-shelf / pickup shelf
The physical shelf where items pulled to fill a patron hold sit until picked up or the pickup window expires.
**In use:** "It's on the hold shelf under her last name, third day of a seven-day window."
**Common misuse:** Treated as interchangeable with general reserve shelving — the hold shelf has a hard expiration policy the general collection doesn't.

### Pull list
The ILS-generated daily report of items to retrieve from the shelves to fill placed holds.
**In use:** "Four items on today's pull list; one came back not-found-on-shelf, flagged for the next shelf-read."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to always be accurate — an item on the pull list showing "available" can still be misshelved, which is why a not-found item gets logged rather than the hold simply cancelled.

### ILS (Integrated Library System)
The software platform managing circulation, holds, cataloging, and patron records (e.g., Sierra, Polaris) — the system of record this role's transactions all pass through.
**In use:** "Check the ILS transaction record before waiving anything — the receipt and the system don't always agree."
**Common misuse:** Assumed infallible; ILS-recorded due dates and item statuses can be wrong (data-entry error, failed sync) and disputes should be checked against corroborating records, not accepted at face value.

### Weeding
The professional decision to permanently remove an item from the collection based on circulation data and written policy — owned by the librarian, not this role.
**In use:** "That's a weeding call, not mine to make — flagging it up to the librarian with the circulation numbers attached."
**Common misuse:** Used loosely by assistants to describe any removal of an item from the shelf, including temporary pulls for repair or transfer — weeding specifically means a permanent discard decision requiring policy authority this role doesn't hold.

### Ready-reference vs. research question
The triage distinction between a question with a quick, factual, locatable answer (ready-reference) and one requiring evaluation of sources or research strategy (research-level, escalated to the librarian).
**In use:** "What year did the library open — that's ready-reference, I can pull that. 'What's the best source on local zoning history' is a research question, that's the librarian's desk."
**Common misuse:** Judged by how long the question takes to answer rather than what kind of judgment it requires — a hard-to-locate known item is still ready-reference; a quick-sounding question needing source evaluation is still research-level.

### In-transit status
The ILS status an item carries while being physically transferred between branches, distinct from "checked out," "available," or "lost."
**In use:** "It's shown in-transit for eight days now — that's past the normal 3-5 day window, worth checking if it's actually moving."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to self-resolve; an item stuck in-transit past the expected window needs an active follow-up, not just patience.

### Holds queue
The ranked list of patrons waiting for a specific title, determining who receives the next available copy.
**In use:** "The hold expired unclaimed, so it goes to the next patron in the holds queue, not back to general circulation."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a fixed list rather than one that reorders as patrons cancel or new copies are added — the "next" patron can change between when a hold expires and when it's processed.

### Grace period
A short window after the due date during which no fine accrues, distinct from the loan period itself.
**In use:** "It's two days overdue but inside the three-day grace period, so no fine yet."
**Common misuse:** Confused with the hold-pickup window, which is a completely separate policy governing how long a filled hold waits on the shelf, not how long a checkout can run late without a fine.

### LSSC (Library Support Staff Certification)
The ALA-APA paraprofessional certification recognizing competencies specific to library-support (non-MLS) roles.
**In use:** "LSSC covers circulation, technology, and communication competencies — it's the recognized credential path for this role, distinct from the MLS librarians hold."
**Common misuse:** Conflated with a full librarian credential (an MLS/MLIS) — LSSC certifies support-role competencies and does not confer the collection-development or reference-service authority an MLS does.

### Class set / bulk checkout
A single transaction checking out a large batch of identical or grouped items (e.g., for a school visit), tracked against a manifest rather than individually reconciled at checkout.
**In use:** "Count the return against the original class-set manifest before closing the transaction — a missing copy shows up here, not at next inventory."
**Common misuse:** Processed like an ordinary multi-item checkout without manifest verification, which is exactly how a missing item goes unnoticed until the next full inventory count.

### Fine-free policy
A library policy eliminating overdue fines (though not necessarily replacement costs for lost items), adopted by many public libraries starting in the late 2010s.
**In use:** "We went fine-free as of last September — if a patron's citing an old fine schedule, check the policy's effective date."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to mean "no consequences for overdue items" — fine-free typically still tracks overdue status and can still bill for a lost or unreturned item; it specifically removes the daily/per-item late fee.

### Barcode / RFID tagging
The physical identification method (scanned barcode or RFID chip) linking a physical item to its ILS record, used for checkout, checkin, and inventory.
**In use:** "The pull-list item's barcode has to match the hold record exactly before it goes on the pickup shelf — a near-match isn't a match."
**Common misuse:** Assumed a visual title match is sufficient verification — two copies of the same title have different barcodes, and matching the wrong copy to a hold causes a downstream discrepancy at return.
