# Vocabulary

### Delegation (task-specific)
The OT or OTA authorizing an aide to perform one exact task, for one exact patient, after observing the aide demonstrate it safely.
**In use:** "Hot pack for Mr. G is delegated, but I've never been signed off on his pulley exercises, so I'm not touching those."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a blanket clearance ("I'm an aide, I can do prep and modalities") rather than a per-task, per-patient, re-verified authorization.

### Service competency
The demonstrated, observed ability to perform a specific delegated task safely and consistently, per AOTA guidance — the prerequisite for delegation, not a credential earned once.
**In use:** "She's got service competency on transfers with the mechanical lift, but not yet on manual two-person transfers."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to transfer automatically from one task or patient to a similar one, instead of being verified per task.

### Direct (line-of-sight) supervision vs. general supervision
Direct supervision means the supervising clinician is on-site and immediately available; general/indirect supervision may allow the clinician to be reachable but off-site, depending on facility policy and state practice act. Aide delegations typically assume the level the facility built the delegation around.
**In use:** "This task's delegation assumes direct supervision — if the OTA leaves the floor, I hold the patient rather than proceed."
**Common misuse:** Treated as interchangeable with "supervision" in general, without checking which level a specific delegated task actually requires.

### Skilled service
A therapy task requiring clinical judgment — assessment, grading, modification based on patient response — that only a qualified therapist or assistant may legally furnish, per payer definitions like CMS's Medicare Benefit Policy Manual.
**In use:** "Grading his resistance band tension is a skilled call — that's not something I can just start for him."
**Common misuse:** Judged by how "easy" or "routine" the task looks rather than by whether it requires in-the-moment clinical judgment.

### Plan of care (POC)
The OT's documented evaluation, goals, precautions, and authorized activities that bound what any team member — including the aide — may do with a given patient.
**In use:** "The POC says standby assist only for transport — I'm not adding a gait belt just because it seems safer to me."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a general guideline the aide can round up or down from based on how the patient seems that day.

### Precaution vs. contraindication
A precaution is a condition requiring caution or modification (e.g., no resisted grip); a contraindication is an activity that must not be performed at all. Both are set by the OT, not inferred by the aide.
**In use:** "His plan lists resisted grip as a precaution until week 8 — I keep the pulley system unweighted regardless of what he asks for."
**Common misuse:** Used interchangeably, or treated as suggestions rather than boundaries the aide has no authority to waive.

### Weight-bearing status (WBAT, PWB, NWB, TTWB)
The documented amount of weight a patient is cleared to bear through a limb — weight-bearing as tolerated, partial, none, or toe-touch — that governs how a transfer or transport must be performed.
**In use:** "He's NWB on the left, so this is a two-person pivot transfer regardless of how strong he looks today."
**Common misuse:** Inferred from the patient's appearance or self-report ("I feel fine to walk on it") instead of checked against the chart.

### Gait belt
A fabric belt fastened around a patient's waist to give a caregiver a secure handhold during a transfer or ambulation, used per a specific technique, not just "worn as a precaution."
**In use:** "Standby assist with a gait belt, hands off unless he loses balance."
**Common misuse:** Applied loosely as a generic safety gesture rather than per the specific hold and positioning technique it requires to actually work.

### Two-person / dependent transfer
A transfer requiring two staff members because the patient cannot meaningfully assist and full manual handling by one person would exceed safe lifting limits.
**In use:** "He's max-assist today, not the usual mod-assist — this is a two-person transfer, get K. before we move him."
**Common misuse:** Downgraded to one-person "just this once" under time pressure, treating the assist level as flexible rather than as a documented safety threshold.

### Recoupment
A payer's retroactive demand to return payment already made for a claim later found non-compliant (e.g., a skilled service actually delivered by unqualified personnel).
**In use:** "If that pattern shows up across a quarter's worth of visits, we're not just correcting a note — that's a recoupment exposure."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a documentation nitpick rather than the direct financial and compliance consequence of an aide performing a skilled task.

### CPT code / billed unit (e.g., 97110)
The procedure code and time-unit structure (commonly 15-minute units) a clinician bills for a therapy service; who is legally permitted to furnish the service the code represents is a separate, binding question from who is in the room.
**In use:** "That's billed as 97110, therapeutic exercise — which is exactly why grading it isn't something I can start."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to be a purely administrative/billing detail with no bearing on what tasks an aide may perform.

### Modality
A physical-agent treatment (hot pack, paraffin, cold pack) applied per a fixed protocol; delegable when it requires no in-session judgment, not delegable the moment it does.
**In use:** "The hot pack protocol is delegated to me; if his skin looks off at the 5-minute check, that's a stop-and-flag, not a judgment call for me to make."
**Common misuse:** Treated as inherently "simple" and therefore always delegable, regardless of whether the specific protocol requires monitoring judgment.

### Infection-control dwell time
The minimum contact time a disinfectant must remain wet on a surface or piece of equipment to actually disinfect it, distinct from how long it takes to look clean.
**In use:** "Two minutes wet contact on the mat before the next patient — wiping it once and moving on doesn't meet dwell time."
**Common misuse:** Conflated with "wiped down," treating a quick pass with a cloth as equivalent to the required contact time.

### Escalation (to the supervising clinician)
Reporting an observation or deviation to the OT/OTA immediately, in objective terms, rather than resolving it independently.
**In use:** "New complaint, no trauma reported — I stopped the modality and I'm escalating before we continue."
**Common misuse:** Reserved only for dramatic events, when routine but unexplained deviations (a small measurement change, a new comment from the patient) are exactly what escalation is meant to catch early.
