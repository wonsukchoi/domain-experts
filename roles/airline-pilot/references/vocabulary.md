# Vocabulary — Airline Pilot

### ETOPS (Extended-range Twin-engine Operations)
The certification and operational framework, under 14 CFR Part 121 Appendix P, that permits twin-engine aircraft to fly routes where part of the track exceeds a fixed one-engine-inoperative diversion time from an adequate airport — expressed as a rating (e.g., 120, 180, 240 minutes).
**In use:** "We're 180-rated, but the ETOPS-MEL note on that hydraulic deferral drops us to 120 for this leg — rerun the ring."
**Common misuse:** Treating ETOPS as a fixed property of the airframe type rather than a rating that can be reduced for a specific tail by an open MEL item.

### Adequate airport (vs. suitable airport)
An adequate airport meets the physical/performance criteria to accept the diversion (runway length, surface, approach capability); a suitable airport adds current considerations — weather at the time, NOTAMs, political/overflight access — layered on top at time of dispatch.
**In use:** "Shemya's adequate for this aircraft, but check whether it's suitable given today's crosswind forecast before we call it our diversion field."
**Common misuse:** Confirming an airport is adequate and stopping there, without separately checking whether it's actually suitable given current conditions.

### Dispatch release (flight release)
The document, jointly produced and jointly owned by dispatcher and captain under Part 121, authorizing a specific flight with its routing, fuel, alternates, and weight limits — the legal instrument that must exist, current and matching conditions, before departure.
**In use:** "The release still shows the original ETOPS rating — we need an amendment before I'll sign for pushback."
**Common misuse:** Treating the release as paperwork the captain signs rather than a document both parties are accountable for having verified.

### Operational control (joint, 14 CFR §121.533)
The legal authority and responsibility for initiating, conducting, and terminating a flight, held jointly by the captain and the dispatcher on domestic/flag Part 121 operations — distinct from Part 135, where operational control typically rests with the certificate holder/PIC without a parallel dispatcher-authority system.
**In use:** "I can refuse this release, but I can't release myself — operational control on this flight is joint, not mine alone."
**Common misuse:** Assuming the captain is the sole authority on go/no-go, the way a Part 91 or Part 135 single-pilot operator typically is.

### Pilot Flying (PF) / Pilot Monitoring (PM)
The task-based (not seat-based) division of flight-deck duties: PF manipulates the controls and flight-path decisions; PM independently verifies PF's inputs, runs checklists, and handles communication — a role that can and does swap between captain and first officer by leg.
**In use:** "FO has PF this leg, I'm PM — I'll be calling out every mode change before you execute it."
**Common misuse:** Treating "monitoring" as a passive, lower-workload backup duty rather than an active verification task with its own failure modes.

### Sterile cockpit (14 CFR §121.542)
The rule prohibiting non-essential conversation or activity during critical phases of flight, generally below 10,000 ft (or the AGL equivalent at high-terrain airports per company OpSpecs).
**In use:** "We're through ten, sterile cockpit's lifted — that conversation can happen now, not before."
**Common misuse:** Applying it only to casual chit-chat while still permitting non-essential radio calls, paperwork, or device use during the same critical phase.

### Flight Duty Period (FDP) — 14 CFR Part 117
The maximum time a crewmember may be assigned flight duty, set by a table keyed to report time and number of flight segments — not a flat duty-day number — reflecting circadian and workload research.
**In use:** "Same 13-hour day, but our report time slipped to 0100 with the delay — the FDP table caps us well under 13 now."
**Common misuse:** Reasoning from a flat "duty day" number (a Part 135 habit) instead of rechecking the FDP table for the actual report-time band and segment count.

### Critical fuel scenario
The ETOPS-specific fuel-planning requirement to carry enough fuel for the worst-case combination — typically decompression plus engine failure — occurring at the route's most adverse (critical) point, diverting at driftdown altitude and speed to the nearest adequate airport.
**In use:** "The track shifted, so rerun critical fuel from the new critical point — don't carry the old number forward."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a shorter diversion distance automatically means lower critical fuel, without rerunning the decompression/driftdown profile for the new point.

### Equal-time point (ETP) / critical point
The point along a route (not necessarily the geographic midpoint) where diversion time to two different adequate airports is equal, or where diversion margin to the nearest adequate airport is most constrained — the location an ETOPS ring check and a critical-fuel calculation are both built around.
**In use:** "Our critical point on this track sits 1,050 miles from Shemya — that's the number the ring check runs against, not the great-circle midpoint."
**Common misuse:** Confusing the ETP with the geographic route midpoint; winds and airport spacing move it, sometimes by hundreds of miles.

### Driftdown
The one-engine-inoperative descent profile and speed schedule an aircraft flies to a lower, sustainable cruise altitude after an engine failure — the performance basis for both the ETOPS ring radius and the critical-fuel scenario.
**In use:** "Driftdown from FL350 on one engine puts us at FL250 by the time we're established toward the alternate — that's the altitude the diversion time is built on."
**Common misuse:** Using a generic OEI cruise speed instead of the aircraft- and weight-specific driftdown profile from the QRH/AFM.

### Type rating
An FAA certificate addition, required under 14 CFR §61.31 for aircraft over 12,500 lb or turbojet-powered, certifying the pilot on a specific aircraft type's systems and handling — earned and maintained through an operator's approved Part 121 training program with recurrent checks, not a general commercial certificate privilege.
**In use:** "I'm type-rated in the -300ER, but not yet in the -8 — different systems, different rating, different training event."
**Common misuse:** Treating type ratings within the same manufacturer family as interchangeable without checking whether the specific variant requires a distinct rating or a differences-training endorsement.

### Authority gradient
The perceived difference in experience, rank, or confidence between two flight-deck crewmembers that can suppress a junior pilot's challenge to a senior pilot's error — a core CRM concept distinct from simple deference or politeness.
**In use:** "The gradient's steep here — captain's a check airman — so PM has to be deliberate about actually voicing the callout, not just noting it internally."
**Common misuse:** Treating authority gradient as only a junior-to-senior problem, when a steep gradient in either direction (an overly deferential captain toward a forceful FO) can suppress the same cross-check.
