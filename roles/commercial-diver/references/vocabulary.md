# Vocabulary

Terms generalists conflate or misuse. Each: definition, a sentence a practitioner would actually say using it, and the common misuse.

## No-decompression limit (NDL) vs. decompression obligation

**NDL** is the maximum bottom time at a given depth after which the diver can ascend directly to the surface (at a controlled rate) without a required stop. Once bottom time exceeds the NDL, the dive incurs a **decompression obligation** — one or more mandatory stops at specified depths for specified durations before surfacing.

*Practitioner usage:* "We're eight minutes past the NDL for this depth — we're not swimming straight up, we owe a stop now."

*Common misuse:* treating "past the no-decompression limit" as a soft guideline that can be skipped if the diver "feels fine" — DCS risk doesn't correlate with how a diver feels in the water.

## Surface-supplied vs. SCUBA mode

**Surface-supplied** diving delivers breathing gas from the surface through an umbilical, giving a primary gas duration limited only by the surface supply itself, plus built-in comms; **SCUBA mode** uses a self-contained cylinder the diver carries, limited by its own volume, and is restricted under OSHA to specific depth, temperature, current, and visibility conditions.

*Practitioner usage:* "Current's running over a knot today — we're surface-supplied, not SCUBA, full stop."

*Common misuse:* choosing SCUBA mode for convenience or mobility without re-checking that every one of its required conditions still holds.

## Bailout bottle vs. reserve breathing gas supply

A **bailout bottle** is the independent cylinder a surface-supplied diver carries for use if the primary umbilical gas supply fails. **Reserve breathing gas supply** is the broader regulatory requirement (OSHA 1910.430) that some adequate backup gas source exist for the diver to reach the surface or a point of safety — the bailout bottle is the typical way that requirement is met, but not the only one.

*Practitioner usage:* "Bailout's sized for the stop plus the ascent, doubled — not just the ascent."

*Common misuse:* sizing the bailout bottle only for a direct ascent to the surface, ignoring that a decompression stop already owed on the dive must also be covered on bailout gas.

## SAC rate (surface air consumption)

The volume of gas a diver breathes per minute, normalized to surface pressure (1 ATA), used to project actual consumption at depth by multiplying by ATA. Working SAC rate is materially higher than resting SAC rate.

*Practitioner usage:* "His resting SAC is 0.6, but he's grinding a weld bevel down there — plan bailout off 1.0, not 0.6."

*Common misuse:* using a resting or "average" SAC rate to size bailout gas for a task that's physically demanding, which understates real consumption and shrinks the safety margin without anyone deciding to shrink it.

## Residual nitrogen time (RNT) / repetitive group

**RNT** is the extra time, expressed as if it had been spent at the next dive's depth, that a diver's tissues still carry from a prior dive after a surface interval. **Repetitive group** is the letter designator a table assigns after a dive, used to look up RNT for a subsequent dive at a given surface interval and depth.

*Practitioner usage:* "Group F down to group C after the ninety-minute interval — that's still 17 minutes of RNT at 60 feet, not zero."

*Common misuse:* assuming a surface interval of any length resets the next dive to the full fresh-dive NDL, rather than looking up the actual carryover.

## Nitrogen narcosis vs. hypoxia vs. CO2 toxicity

**Nitrogen narcosis** is depth-dependent cognitive impairment from breathing nitrogen at increasing partial pressure — reversible on ascent, no lasting injury by itself. **Hypoxia** is insufficient oxygen delivery, from equipment failure or gas-mix error. **CO2 toxicity** (hypercapnia) comes from inadequate ventilation or scrubber failure in enclosed breathing loops, causing headache, rapid breathing, and confusion.

*Practitioner usage:* "That wasn't narcosis, his numbers were off from the start — check the gas panel mix before blaming depth."

*Common misuse:* attributing any underwater confusion to narcosis by default, which can mask an equipment or gas-mix failure that needs a different, faster response.

## Wet weld vs. dry (habitat) weld, and AWS D3.6M class

A **wet weld** is performed directly in the water with waterproofed electrodes; a **dry (habitat) weld** is performed inside a gas-purged enclosure sealing out water at the joint. AWS D3.6M classifies the resulting weld quality as **Class A** (topside-equivalent, for critical/cyclic-load structural use), **Class B** (reduced ductility requirement, non-fracture-critical), or **Class C** (minimal requirement, non-structural).

*Practitioner usage:* "This leg carries cyclic wave load — it needs a Class A weld, which means habitat, not wet."

*Common misuse:* selecting wet welding purely on access/cost grounds without checking whether the joint's load role requires a class wet welding typically can't achieve.

## Standby diver vs. tender

The **standby diver** is a second fully-dressed diver ready to enter the water immediately if the working diver needs assistance. The **tender** manages the working diver's umbilical from topside and relays communications; the tender is not necessarily suited to dive.

*Practitioner usage:* "Standby's dressed and on the line — go ahead and splash."

*Common misuse:* treating "someone else is around" as equivalent to a standby diver who is actually dressed and immediately deployable.

## Dive supervisor / person-in-charge (PIC)

The individual designated with full authority over the dive, including the unilateral authority to abort it, who is accountable for the dive log, gas panel, and overall plan — and who is not the working diver on a task complex enough to require full topside attention.

*Practitioner usage:* "Supervisor called it — we're surfacing, no discussion."

*Common misuse:* assuming the most experienced diver on site is automatically the supervisor, or letting the supervisor also work as the diver on a task that needs dedicated topside attention.

## UWILD (underwater inspection in lieu of dry-docking)

A class-society/USCG-recognized underwater survey of a vessel's hull and appendages, performed in place of pulling the vessel into dry dock, using diver-conducted visual and NDT inspection against the same acceptance criteria a dry-dock survey would use.

*Practitioner usage:* "We're doing a UWILD this cycle, not a dry-dock — the thickness grid still has to meet the same criteria the surveyor would use in dry dock."

*Common misuse:* treating UWILD as a lower-rigor substitute survey rather than an equivalent-standard inspection performed in a harder access condition.

## Pneumofathometer (pneumo)

A surface-read depth gauge connected to the diver via a dedicated hose in the umbilical, giving topside an independent depth reading that doesn't rely on the diver's own gauge or self-report.

*Practitioner usage:* "Pneumo's reading five feet shallower than his gauge — stop and reconcile before continuing."

*Common misuse:* relying solely on the diver's own depth gauge for the official dive log when the pneumo is the independent, topside-verifiable reading meant to catch exactly that kind of discrepancy.
