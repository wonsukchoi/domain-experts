# Red flags

Smell tests a senior strategist catches in the first ten minutes of a review. Format for each: what it usually means, the first question to ask, and the data to pull before anyone makes a decision.

## 1. Blended CAC looks fine (or is drifting slowly)

**Usually means:** one channel is decaying and another — often branded search or email — is quietly absorbing the average. Blended CAC is a lagging, composite number; by the time it moves visibly, the underlying channel has usually been deteriorating for two quarters.

**First question:** "Show me CAC by channel, monthly, for the last six months — not the blended line."

**Data to pull:** channel-level spend and attributed conversions by month; marginal CAC on the last 20% of spend per channel; share of new customers coming from demand-capture vs. demand-creation channels over time. If demand-capture share is rising while total volume is flat, you're harvesting a shrinking pool.

## 2. Branded search is the "most efficient channel"

**Usually means:** last-click attribution is crediting the final navigational step of demand that other activity (or the product itself) created. Geo-holdout benchmarks routinely put branded search around 0.7x incremental ROAS versus its attributed numbers; for strong brands it can be far lower.

**First question:** "If we paused branded search in a quarter of our geos for six weeks, how many of those conversions do we believe would disappear?"

**Data to pull:** branded vs. non-brand split of search spend and conversions; competitor bidding pressure on your brand terms (if nobody bids on your brand, incrementality is lower still); any prior lift-test results. Then actually schedule the geo holdout.

## 3. "Awareness" campaign with no measurement plan

**Usually means:** either a genuinely long-horizon brand bet that nobody bothered to instrument, or — more often — a tactic someone wanted to run, wearing "brand" as an exemption from accountability.

**First question:** "What number, measured how, will look different in six months if this works — and what will we do differently if it doesn't?"

**Data to pull:** baseline aided/unaided awareness (if no tracker exists, that's the tell), share of search vs. competitors, branded search volume trend, direct traffic trend. Any of these can serve as the pre-committed yardstick; refusing to pick one is the red flag confirmed.

## 4. ROAS targets with no margin in them

**Usually means:** the team is optimizing revenue per ad dollar while the business needs contribution per ad dollar. A 3.0 ROAS on a 25%-margin product loses money on every sale before any other cost; the same 3.0 on an 80%-margin product prints. Blanket ROAS targets across a mixed-margin catalog quietly steer spend toward low-margin, high-AOV items.

**First question:** "What's the contribution margin behind that ROAS target, by product line?"

**Data to pull:** margin by product/category; ROAS target vs. breakeven ROAS (breakeven = 1 ÷ contribution margin) per line; whether returns, discounts, and shipping are inside or outside the revenue figure the platform sees.

## 5. Lookalike/prospecting performance eroding on "the same setup that worked"

**Usually means:** audience saturation. The platform has already found the cheap conversions inside that seed; the same targeting now buys the same people more often at higher CPMs. Teams frequently misdiagnose this as creative fatigue and burn a quarter refreshing ads into a saturated pool.

**First question:** "What are frequency, reach, and CPM doing over the last 90 days — and when did we last change the seed audience, not the creative?"

**Data to pull:** frequency and reach trend, CPM trend vs. platform benchmark, audience overlap between ad sets, age of seed lists, marginal CAC on the most recent spend increment vs. six months ago.

## 6. Agency reports only platform-attributed conversions

**Usually means:** each platform is claiming credit for overlapping conversions — sum the platform dashboards and you'll often "acquire" 1.5–2x the customers finance can find. Sometimes it's convenience; sometimes the agency's fee is a percentage of spend and the incentive is exactly where you'd expect.

**First question:** "Reconcile platform-reported conversions against our order/CRM data for last month — what's the over-claim factor?"

**Data to pull:** platform-claimed conversions vs. source-of-truth new customers; the agency contract's fee structure; whether any lift study has ever been run; deduplicated view from your own analytics. Set the norm going forward: reporting against your numbers, not the platforms'.

## 7. Discounting doing the work of strategy

**Usually means:** the offer, not the marketing, is carrying conversion — and it's training customers (and the platform algorithms) to wait for promos. Rising share of discounted orders is deferred margin destruction that shows up later as "our full-price conversion rate collapsed" and an eroded price position that's brutally expensive to rebuild.

**First question:** "What percentage of new customers came in on a discount this quarter versus a year ago, and what do their repeat rates look like at full price?"

**Data to pull:** discounted vs. full-price share of new-customer orders over 8 quarters; cohort LTV and repeat-purchase rate of discount-acquired vs. full-price customers; promo-cadence calendar vs. conversion-rate troughs between promos (the "customers are learning to wait" signature).

## 8. The deck leads with impressions, engagement, and follower growth

**Usually means:** either the campaign's business result was bad, or no causal path from these numbers to revenue was ever defined. Reach metrics are inputs, not outcomes; a report that ends at them is a report that stopped before the answer.

**First question:** "Walk me from these impressions to a revenue or pipeline number — what's the claimed conversion path, and where's the evidence for each step?"

**Data to pull:** the metrics named in the original brief (if the brief named none, that's the upstream failure to fix); funnel data downstream of the campaign window; branded search and direct-traffic movement during flight vs. baseline.
