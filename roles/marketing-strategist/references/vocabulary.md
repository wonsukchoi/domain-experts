# Vocabulary

Terms generalists conflate that practitioners keep sharply separate. Each: definition, how a practitioner actually uses it in a sentence, and the common misuse to avoid.

## Positioning vs. messaging vs. branding

**Positioning** is the strategic decision of what market context you compete in and why you win for a specific segment — a choice, made internally, that customers never read verbatim. **Messaging** is the expression of that choice in words for particular audiences and moments. **Branding** is the accumulated set of associations (visual, verbal, emotional) the market holds about you, built over years of consistent exposure.

*Practitioner usage:* "The messaging isn't the problem — the positioning is wrong, so no rewrite will fix it. We're framed against the wrong alternative."

*Common misuse:* using them interchangeably — "let's fix our positioning" when someone means rewriting homepage copy (messaging), or "rebrand" when someone means a new logo (visual identity, one component of brand).

## CAC vs. CPA vs. blended CAC

**CAC** (customer acquisition cost) is total sales-and-marketing cost to acquire one *new customer*. **CPA** (cost per acquisition/action) is cost per *conversion event*, which may be a lead, trial, or add-to-cart — platform dashboards report CPA, not CAC. **Blended CAC** is total acquisition spend ÷ all new customers, including ones who'd have arrived organically.

*Practitioner usage:* "Meta's reporting a $60 CPA on trials, but trial-to-paid is 22%, so paid CAC is really ~$270 — compare that to LTV, not the $60."

*Common misuse:* quoting a platform CPA as "our CAC," or celebrating a low blended CAC while paid channels deteriorate under a cushion of organic volume.

## Incrementality vs. attribution

**Attribution** assigns credit for observed conversions across touchpoints using a rule or model (last-click, position-based, data-driven). **Incrementality** measures how many conversions would *not have happened* without the spend, via controlled experiments (holdouts, geo tests, lift studies). Attribution describes the paths; incrementality establishes the causation.

*Practitioner usage:* "Attribution says retargeting drove 800 orders; the lift test says ~120 were incremental. We budget on the 120."

*Common misuse:* treating a "data-driven attribution" model as if it measures causation. However sophisticated the model, it's still allocating observed conversions, not comparing against a counterfactual.

## Share of voice vs. share of market

**Share of voice (SOV)** is your share of the category's total advertising/communications presence. **Share of market (SOM)** is your share of category sales. The empirical relationship (IPA/Binet & Field): brands with SOV above their SOM tend to grow share; below it, they tend to shrink — the "excess share of voice" (ESOV) rule of thumb is roughly +10 ESOV points ≈ +0.5–1 SOM point per year, category-dependent.

*Practitioner usage:* "We're at 8% SOM but only 4% SOV — at negative ESOV we should expect share erosion regardless of how clever the creative is."

*Common misuse:* citing social-listening mention counts as SOV (it's a paid+earned presence measure against the whole category, not a mentions tally), or expecting ESOV effects on performance-marketing timescales — it's a multi-year dynamic.

## Category creation vs. category entry

**Category creation** is convincing the market a new problem class deserves its own budget line and evaluation criteria (expensive, slow, usually only worth it with significant funding and no adequate existing frame). **Category entry** is positioning within an existing category customers already understand and shop, differentiating on attributes that matter.

*Practitioner usage:* "We don't have the capital to teach the market a new category — enter 'expense management,' win on close speed, and revisit category creation at $50M."

*Common misuse:* startups declaring a new category to dodge competition, which mostly buys them the cost of explaining what they are before every deal. If prospects have a budget line for it, the category exists — enter it.

## Demand capture vs. demand creation

**Demand capture** harvests intent that already exists — search, marketplaces, retargeting, review sites. **Demand creation** generates intent in people not currently shopping — content, brand advertising, community, events. Capture channels look far more efficient in any attribution system because the buyer arrives pre-motivated.

*Practitioner usage:* "Non-brand search is capped at ~900 conversions a month — the whole intent pool. Above that spend level, every incremental dollar has to go to demand creation."

*Common misuse:* comparing the two on the same CAC yardstick and concluding creation "doesn't work." Capture depletes a pool that creation fills; starving creation shows up 12–18 months later as capture channels saturating at shrinking volume.

## MER / ROAS / payback period

**ROAS** (return on ad spend) is platform-attributed revenue ÷ spend for a channel or campaign. **MER** (marketing efficiency ratio, or blended ROAS) is *total* revenue ÷ *total* marketing spend — immune to attribution games, blind to channel detail. **Payback period** is months until a customer's cumulative contribution margin repays their CAC.

*Practitioner usage:* "Platform ROAS climbed to 4.2 but MER fell from 5.1 to 4.3 — the platforms are claiming credit for revenue they didn't create. And payback stretched from 6 to 10 months, which is what actually constrains growth at our cash position."

*Common misuse:* optimizing platform ROAS as if it were profit (it uses revenue, not margin, and platform-attributed revenue at that), or running high-ROAS/long-payback growth into a cash crunch because nobody was watching payback.

## Marginal CAC

The cost of the *next* customer from additional spend in a channel, as opposed to average CAC across all spend. Because channels saturate, marginal CAC is always at or above average CAC, and the gap widens as spend scales — average CAC can look healthy while the last $20k of spend acquires customers at double the target.

*Practitioner usage:* "Average CAC on Meta is $130, fine. But the last spend increment came in at $210 marginal — the growth budget goes to TikTok, where marginal is still near average."

*Common misuse:* making scale-up decisions on average CAC. "Channel X has our best CAC, pour money in" is precisely how a portfolio's best channel gets driven past its efficient frontier.
