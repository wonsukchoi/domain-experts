# Physics Teacher Playbook

Filled templates for the three recurring artifacts: a Peer Instruction ConcepTest sequence, a split physics/execution rubric, and a lab-safety sign-off checklist.

## 1. Peer Instruction ConcepTest sequence

Target misconception: Newton's third law — students believe the "more active" or "heavier" object in a collision exerts a larger force (common on FCI items 6, 15, 27).

**Question 1 (diagnostic, before any discussion of the topic):**

> A small car collides head-on with a large truck. During the collision:
> A) The truck exerts a larger force on the car than the car exerts on the truck.
> B) The car exerts a larger force on the truck than the truck exerts on the car.
> C) The truck exerts a force on the car, but the car exerts no force on the truck.
> D) The truck and car exert equal-magnitude forces on each other.

Correct answer: D. Typical first-vote distribution in an unprepared intro section: A ~45%, B ~5%, C ~10%, D ~40%.

**Decision on the vote split (40% correct):** falls in the 30–70% band — run 2 minutes of peer discussion (students find a neighbor who voted differently and argue their case), then revote before revealing the answer. If the split had come in under 30% correct, skip straight to a targeted mini-lecture on Newton's third law using this exact scenario, because peer discussion among students who mostly agree on the wrong answer just reinforces it.

**Typical revote after discussion:** D rises to ~68–75%. If revote correct-rate is still under ~55%, do not move on — the misconception is load-bearing for the next three topics (momentum, center of mass, rocket propulsion) and needs a second pass with a different scenario (e.g., a person pushing off a wall) before the lecture continues.

**Question 2 (transfer, later in the same class):**

> A spacecraft with no engine burning drifts in deep space. An astronaut on board pushes off the spacecraft's hull to move toward a docking module. Compare the force the astronaut exerts on the spacecraft to the force the spacecraft exerts on the astronaut.
> A) The astronaut exerts a larger force, since the astronaut is doing the pushing.
> B) The spacecraft exerts a larger force, since it is far more massive.
> C) The forces are equal in magnitude and opposite in direction.
> D) There is no force on the spacecraft since it has no engine running.

Correct answer: C. This question strips away the "heavier object wins" cue (no car/truck size disparity is visually obvious) — if the second-question correct rate matches or exceeds Question 1's revote rate, the concept transferred; if it drops back toward the first-vote level, the fix was scenario-specific pattern matching, not conceptual, and needs a third scenario before moving on.

## 2. Split rubric — physics setup vs. execution

For a standard multi-step problem (example: block on an incline with friction, find acceleration), out of 10 points:

| Component | Points | What earns full credit |
|---|---|---|
| Free-body diagram | 2 | All forces present (gravity, normal, friction, applied if any), correctly directed, no phantom forces |
| Principle/equation selection | 2 | Newton's second law applied along correctly chosen axes (incline-aligned, not x/y) |
| Symbolic setup before substitution | 2 | Equation written in symbols (m, g, θ, μ) before any numbers are plugged in |
| Algebra/calculus execution | 3 | Correct algebraic solution for the symbolic unknown; arithmetic errors here cost at most 1 of these 3 points if the symbolic answer is right |
| Sanity check | 1 | Units correct AND a limiting-case check stated (e.g., "at θ=0, a=0, which is right") |

**Reading the split:** a student who scores 6/6 on setup (diagram + principle + symbolic) but 0/3 on execution has a computational gap, not a conceptual one — route them to more practice problems, not office hours on concepts. A student who scores 1/6 on setup but nails the execution on a wrong equation almost certainly formula-matched from a similar-looking solved example — that student needs the ConcepTest sequence, not more problem sets.

## 3. Lab safety sign-off checklist (laser optics lab example)

Before the lab section runs:

- [ ] Laser class identified and posted at each station (this lab: Class 3R HeNe, <5 mW) — Class 3B/4 sources require a laser safety officer sign-off logged separately, not just an instructor's judgment call.
- [ ] Wavelength-rated eyewear (OD rated for 632.8 nm, this laser's wavelength) present at every station, checked against the laser actually in use that day — eyewear rated for a different wavelength provides false confidence, not protection.
- [ ] Beam path confirmed below eye level when seated, or beam-blocked at table edge — no open beam path across a walkway.
- [ ] TA briefed to stop the experiment immediately (not "note it for later") on any of: eyewear removed, beam path redirected outside the confirmed zone, reflective object (ring, watch, phone) brought into the beam path.
- [ ] Incident log location confirmed and accessible to the TA before students arrive, not looked up after an incident occurs.

**Escalation rule:** two stop-and-corrects for the same violation type within one semester across sections triggers a protocol redesign (station layout, written procedure re-issued), not a third warning to the same TA — the pattern indicates the protocol is ambiguous, not that students are careless.
