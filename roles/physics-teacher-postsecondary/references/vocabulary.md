# Vocabulary

### Normalized gain (⟨g⟩)
Hake's metric for instructional effectiveness on a concept inventory: ⟨g⟩ = (post% − pre%) / (100% − pre%), measuring how much of the possible room for improvement was actually captured.
**In use:** "We're at ⟨g⟩=0.31 on the E&M unit — better than a raw lecture, but nowhere near where Peer Instruction sections usually land."
**Common misuse:** treating a raw point gain (post minus pre, unnormalized) as if it were the normalized gain — a class starting at 70% pretest and ending at 85% has a huge raw gain but a modest ⟨g⟩ of 0.5, because there was less room left to improve.

### Force Concept Inventory (FCI)
A validated 30-item multiple-choice diagnostic covering Newtonian mechanics, with wrong answers designed to map onto specific documented misconceptions rather than being random distractors.
**In use:** "Item 5's wrong-answer cluster is almost all impetus reasoning — that's the thing to fix before we touch kinematics again."
**Common misuse:** treating the FCI as a knowledge test to "study for" — a high score improves scoring on the instrument itself but the instrument is designed to be resistant to surface-level test prep, and coaching to it defeats its diagnostic purpose.

### ConcepTest
A conceptual multiple-choice question, used in the Peer Instruction method, designed so the wrong answers correspond to specific misconceptions and the correct answer requires reasoning rather than calculation.
**In use:** "That ConcepTest came in at 35% correct — right in the discussion band, run the two-minute peer vote before revealing the answer."
**Common misuse:** writing a ConcepTest with implausible distractors that let students eliminate by process of elimination rather than reasoning — that produces a high correct rate that looks like understanding but isn't.

### Peer Instruction
Eric Mazur's classroom method: pose a ConcepTest, individual vote, peer discussion if the vote splits 30–70% correct, then revote before revealing the answer.
**In use:** "We moved to Peer Instruction in Year 2 and the gain nearly doubled — that's the documented effect, not a fluke."
**Common misuse:** running the discussion step regardless of the vote split — discussion below a 30% correct first vote just spreads the dominant wrong answer with more social confidence, since there's no correct-minded student in most groups to anchor the conversation.

### Discrimination index
A per-question statistic (commonly the point-biserial correlation between getting that item right and the overall exam score) indicating whether a question separates stronger from weaker students in the expected direction.
**In use:** "Question 7 has a discrimination index of 0.05 — it's not distinguishing anyone, pull it from the curve calculation."
**Common misuse:** using item difficulty (percent correct) alone to decide a question was "bad" — a hard question with high discrimination is doing its job; a question with a discrimination index near zero or negative is the actual problem, regardless of its difficulty.

### DFW rate
The percentage of enrolled students who receive a D, F, or withdraw from a course — used as an equity and effectiveness metric independent of the grade distribution among students who finish.
**In use:** "Exam averages among completers went up, but DFW also went up — we may be filtering out the same students who would've pulled the completer average down."
**Common misuse:** reading exam-average trends as the whole picture while ignoring that DFW students never entered that average at all — a policy that discourages weak students from finishing will raise the completer average by removing exactly the data that would show the policy is failing them.

### Impetus misconception
The pre-Newtonian intuition that a moving object carries an internal "force" or "impetus" that runs out over time, causing it to slow and stop, rather than requiring an external force (friction, drag) to decelerate it.
**In use:** "The wrong-answer pattern on item 5 is textbook impetus reasoning — that's not a careless mistake, it's a coherent alternative theory we haven't displaced yet."
**Common misuse:** treating a student who states impetus-style reasoning as simply "not paying attention" — the research literature treats it as a stable, internally consistent prior theory that requires targeted confrontation, not repetition of the correct answer.

### Split rubric (setup vs. execution)
A grading scheme that scores the physics reasoning (diagram, principle selection, symbolic setup) separately from the mathematical execution (algebra, calculus, arithmetic) on the same problem.
**In use:** "They lost all 3 execution points to an algebra slip but got all 6 setup points — that's a computation gap, not a conceptual one, route them to more practice, not office hours."
**Common misuse:** collapsing the two into a single overall-impression score ("looks about right, 7/10") — this is exactly the practice that makes the resulting score undiagnostic, since a formula-matched wrong-principle answer and a right-principle arithmetic slip can land on the same number.

### Transfer (conceptual transfer)
Whether a student can apply a learned principle to a physically different scenario than the one it was originally taught in, as opposed to only recognizing the original scenario.
**In use:** "First vote was 68% correct on the car-truck version but only 45% on the astronaut-spacecraft version — that gap is the transfer failure, not noise."
**Common misuse:** inferring transfer from a single scenario's high correct rate — a high score on a scenario nearly identical to the one used in lecture demonstrates recall of that scenario, not that the principle generalized.

### Laser hazard class (ANSI Z136.1)
A standardized classification (1, 2, 3R, 3B, 4) of laser sources by eye/skin hazard level, which determines the required controls — not a subjective judgment of how careful the users seem.
**In use:** "This is a Class 3B source, so it needs the laser safety officer's sign-off logged separately — that's not optional for a 'careful' TA."
**Common misuse:** assuming a low-power-looking laser pointer or demo source is automatically low-hazard-class — visible low output power does not by itself determine the class, and some Class 3B/4 sources look unremarkable to the eye.

### Concept inventory (general term; FCI, CSEM, BEMA, etc.)
A validated diagnostic instrument, specific to a physics subfield, whose distractors are empirically derived from documented student misconceptions rather than written ad hoc by the instructor.
**In use:** "We don't have a validated inventory for this upper-division topic, so we're flying without the diagnostic signal we'd normally rely on."
**Common misuse:** writing an in-house "concept quiz" and calling its results equivalent to a validated inventory's — an ad hoc quiz's wrong answers weren't tested against real student reasoning, so a low score doesn't reliably point at a specific misconception the way a validated instrument's does.
