# Red Flags

### FCI (or CSEM/BEMA) post-course normalized gain below 0.3 with pretest average under 45%
- **Usually means:** instruction never targeted the specific misconception cluster the wrong-answer distractors reveal — lecture covered the correct derivation but not the intuitive belief it needs to displace.
- **First question:** which distractor did students converge on, and does it match a documented misconception (impetus reasoning, "force causes motion" rather than "force causes acceleration")?
- **Data to pull:** item-by-item pre/post response distribution (not just the aggregate percent-correct), cross-tabbed against which ConcepTests (if any) covered that item's concept.

### Exam average rises but concept-inventory gain stays flat across a redesign
- **Usually means:** the redesign or curriculum change improved test-taking mechanics (better-worded questions, more practice with the exact problem format) without improving conceptual transfer.
- **First question:** did the exam questions change in wording or difficulty between the compared terms, independent of the redesign?
- **Data to pull:** side-by-side exam item text for both terms, plus the FCI/CSEM pre/post pair for each term.

### A single grader's scores run consistently 1.5+ points (on a 10-point rubric) above or below the department mean for the same problem
- **Usually means:** rubric line items are being interpreted inconsistently — most often, the grader is scoring by overall impression ("looks about right") instead of by line item.
- **First question:** can the grader point to which rubric line item produced the deviation on three specific papers, chosen at random?
- **Data to pull:** a norming set of 5 previously-graded papers, re-scored blind by the grader in question and compared to the original consensus scores.

### A ConcepTest first-vote correct rate above 85% on a topic never formally taught yet
- **Usually means:** the question is testing prior exposure (a popular textbook example, a viral video) rather than the target reasoning, or the wrong-answer choices are all implausible, letting students eliminate by elimination rather than reasoning.
- **First question:** would a student who has never seen physics notation still get this right by process of elimination?
- **Data to pull:** the distractor-by-distractor vote split, not just percent-correct — flat distribution across wrong answers signals genuine reasoning; near-zero on all but one wrong answer signals guessing.

### DFW (drop/fail/withdraw) rate exceeding ~20% in an intro sequence course
- **Usually means:** either a genuine misconception/preparation gap not being addressed early enough to intervene, or a pacing/prerequisite mismatch (students placed into calculus-based physics without the calculus prerequisite actually completed).
- **First question:** what fraction of the DFW group had a low FCI pretest score (under 30%) combined with a below-median math-placement score?
- **Data to pull:** DFW roster cross-referenced against FCI pretest score and prerequisite-course grade.

### A lab TA reports "no incidents" for an entire semester in a lab with lasers, high voltage, or radioactive sources
- **Usually means:** either the protocol is genuinely well-run, or minor near-misses aren't being logged because the TA doesn't know what counts as reportable.
- **First question:** walk through one specific station's protocol step-by-step with the TA — can they name the exact stop-and-correct triggers without checking a document?
- **Data to pull:** the incident log itself, and a spot-check observation of one live lab section.

### A curve is proposed after an exam where the mean is more than one letter grade below the department's typical mean
- **Usually means:** genuinely miscalibrated exam (one or two questions with low item-discrimination), OR a real instructional gap the curve would paper over.
- **First question:** what is the discrimination index (point-biserial correlation) for each question, and does the low mean concentrate in a small number of items or spread evenly?
- **Data to pull:** item-level statistics (difficulty and discrimination index) for every question, not just the overall mean and standard deviation.

### A student's problem-set score is high but their exam score on the same topic is low
- **Usually means:** collaborative problem-set solving (or copying) masked an individual gap that only shows up under exam conditions.
- **First question:** does the split rubric on their exam attempt show correct setup with failed execution (a computation gap, consistent with genuine but shaky understanding) or a wrong-principle setup entirely (consistent with never having solved the problem type independently)?
- **Data to pull:** the split-rubric breakdown (setup vs. execution points) on the specific exam problem, compared to their problem-set submission for the same topic.
