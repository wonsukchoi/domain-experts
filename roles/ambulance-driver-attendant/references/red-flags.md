# Red flags

Smells a veteran ambulance driver/attendant or their supervisor catches early, with the first question to ask and the data to pull before anyone makes a call.

## 1. Cot locks confirmed "by sound" rather than visually checked

**Usually means:** the crew is rushing a loading sequence under time pressure and skipping the four-point visual check, trusting the click of the mechanism instead of confirming it's actually load-bearing.

**First question:** "Did you visually confirm all four locks — head-end, foot-end, both side rails, and the IV pole bracket if used — before the cot moved?"

**Data to pull:** the pre-trip/loading checklist sign-off for that run, and the cot's maintenance log for any recent lock-mechanism service bulletins or reported partial engagements.

## 2. Crew requests to "run it hot" on a run already dispatched non-emergency

**Usually means:** schedule or family pressure, not a documented change in the patient's condition — the single most common source of unauthorized mode upgrades.

**First question:** "Has the patient's condition changed since dispatch, and did medical control or a supervisor authorize the mode change?"

**Data to pull:** the dispatch classification code and PCS on file, and the medical-control/supervisor authorization log (timestamp and name) if a change is claimed.

## 3. Approach speed doesn't drop to a near-stop at a red light or stop sign under lights-and-siren

**Usually means:** the driver is treating the siren as a right-of-way grant rather than a legal privilege conditioned on due regard — the direct precursor to an intersection T-bone collision, the leading category of ambulance-involved crashes.

**First question:** "Walk me through your last five controlled intersections — did you come to a complete stop and visually clear each lane before proceeding?"

**Data to pull:** telematics/GPS speed trace at the intersection timestamps in question, and dashcam footage if the unit carries one.

## 4. Solo attendant lifting a patient estimated over ~125 lb of dependent weight without a second person or lift-assist device

**Usually means:** an understaffed run or a crew member skipping the two-person threshold to save a few minutes — the leading precursor to career-ending back injury in this role.

**First question:** "Was a second lift point, transfer board, or powered cot used for this transfer?"

**Data to pull:** the injury/incident log for the crew member, the staffing roster for that shift, and whether a manual or powered cot was dispatched for the run.

## 5. Repetitive non-emergency transport running on a stale Physician Certification Statement

**Usually means:** a compliance gap that also signals nobody has re-verified the patient's actual mobility or condition since the PCS was last signed — a clinical-currency problem wearing a paperwork disguise.

**First question:** "When was the PCS last signed, and does it still match this patient's current mobility level?"

**Data to pull:** the PCS signature date and certifying physician, and the most recent facility handoff/nursing note describing mobility status.

## 6. Ambulance returns to base "hot" (lights-and-siren) after an empty, non-emergency drop-off

**Usually means:** personal habit or perceived end-of-shift time pressure with zero patient-care justification — pure liability exposure with no corresponding benefit.

**First question:** "Is there a next assigned call that justifies priority movement, or is this discretionary?"

**Data to pull:** the CAD log for the unit's next assignment, and the agency's written policy on when lights-and-siren use is authorized for non-patient movement.

## 7. Handoff at pickup omits the patient's fall-risk or mobility status before a stair-chair or stretcher transfer

**Usually means:** a rushed facility handoff, leaving the crew to improvise the transfer technique cot-side instead of planning it — where falls and strain injuries happen.

**First question:** "What's this patient's baseline mobility and date of last fall, per the sending facility?"

**Data to pull:** the facility's transfer/handoff sheet and the mobility section of the PCS.

## 8. A cluster of near-misses or collisions at the same intersection or route segment across multiple drivers

**Usually means:** a specific sightline, signal-timing, or geometry hazard at that location — not driver error in isolation, and not something a policy reminder alone will fix.

**First question:** "How many units have logged a near-miss or collision at this exact location in the last 12 months?"

**Data to pull:** the agency's incident log geocoded by location, and, if available, the municipality's signal-timing or sightline data for that intersection.

## 9. Driver's EVOC/CEVO behind-the-wheel certification is lapsed or past the agency's refresher window

**Usually means:** a gap in the agency's training-compliance tracking that, in states with conditioned statutory immunity, can convert an otherwise-defensible collision into an uninsured one for the agency.

**First question:** "When did this driver last complete the behind-the-wheel EVOC/CEVO evaluation, and is it inside the agency's renewal window?"

**Data to pull:** the training/LMS record, the agency's stated renewal cadence, and the state EMS authority's minimum requirement.

## 10. Transport mode changed mid-route with no medical-control contact logged

**Usually means:** the driver or attendant made a clinical judgment call outside their scope and outside the chain of authorization that would otherwise protect the crew if the decision is questioned after the fact.

**First question:** "Who authorized the change to lights-and-siren, and is that logged?"

**Data to pull:** the CAD mode-change timestamp, and the medical-control call log or recording for the corresponding time window.
