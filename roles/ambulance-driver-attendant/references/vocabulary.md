# Vocabulary

Terms generalists flatten together that this role keeps sharply separate — each with a definition, a sentence a practitioner would actually say, and the common misuse.

## Due regard

The legal standard, drawn from the Uniform Vehicle Code and its state adaptations, that an emergency-vehicle driver must operate with "due regard for the safety of all persons" even while exempt from certain traffic laws (speed limits, proceeding against a signal). It obligates *more* caution while the exemption is in effect, not less.

**In use:** "The siren doesn't buy us the intersection — due regard means we still have to confirm every lane is actually yielding before we go through."

**Common misuse:** treating due regard as a technicality that's automatically satisfied by having the lights and siren on. Courts have repeatedly held the exemption void, and the driver liable, exactly when the crew relied on the signal instead of confirming the intersection was clear.

## Lights-and-siren (L&S) vs. routine transport

**L&S** is the driving mode that invokes the statutory traffic-law exemption (speed, signal exceptions) and is authorized only for a classified emergency or an authorized mode upgrade. **Routine transport** obeys normal traffic law and right-of-way. The two are not interchangeable based on how urgent a situation *feels*.

**In use:** "This is routine transport per the PCS — I need medical control on the radio before we go to L&S, not a family member's timeline."

**Common misuse:** treating L&S as a speed setting available on request rather than a classification with its own authorization chain.

## Transport classification (emergency vs. non-emergency, repetitive)

The dispatch- or PCS-driven categorization of a run that determines driving mode, documentation requirements, and (for repetitive non-emergency transport) CMS prior-authorization exposure. Decided before departure, changed only through a formal authorization, not adjusted based on how the run is going.

**In use:** "This is a repetitive non-emergency transport — check the PCS is current before we roll, because that's what makes this a legitimate trip and not a billing problem."

**Common misuse:** conflating "the patient seems anxious" or "the family is in a hurry" with a clinical basis to reclassify the run.

## Physician Certification Statement (PCS)

The physician-signed document certifying that a patient's condition requires ambulance-level (rather than other) transport for a scheduled or repetitive non-emergency run, and stating the patient's mobility/medical basis for it.

**In use:** "The PCS says non-ambulatory, wheelchair-bound — that's what tells us to bring the powered cot, not a stretcher."

**Common misuse:** treating the PCS as pure paperwork rather than the document that actually sets the mobility assumptions the crew loads and drives on.

## Intersection-clearing (complete-stop-and-clear)

The required sequence at every controlled intersection — full stop if the signal calls for it, visual confirmation each cross-traffic lane is stopped or yielding, then proceeding one lane at a time — regardless of transport mode.

**In use:** "We cleared lane one, but the truck in lane two hadn't actually stopped yet — that's why we held instead of rolling through."

**Common misuse:** glancing across the whole intersection at once and inferring all lanes are clear, instead of confirming each lane individually before entering it.

## EVOC / CEVO

**EVOC** (Emergency Vehicle Operator Course) and **CEVO** (Coaching the Emergency Vehicle Operator) are the two common behind-the-wheel training curricula for emergency-vehicle driving, typically including classroom instruction and a scored closed-course evaluation (backing, turning, controlled braking).

**In use:** "His CEVO refresher lapsed two months ago — he's not driving emergency mode until that's current."

**Common misuse:** treating the certification as a one-time box to check at hire, rather than a recurring requirement some states tie directly to the agency's statutory liability protection.

## Statutory immunity (conditioned)

The liability protection some states grant a public ambulance-service agency for emergency-vehicle collisions, conditioned on the agency having adopted and enforced a written driver-training policy (the California Vehicle Code §17004.7 model). The immunity protects the agency, not automatically the individual driver, and only if the policy was actually followed.

**In use:** "If his training record isn't current, the agency's immunity defense on this collision is gone before we even get to the facts of the crash."

**Common misuse:** assuming "we're an ambulance service" alone confers legal protection in a collision, independent of whether the training policy was actually maintained.

## Two-person lift threshold

The safe-patient-handling rule of thumb that a dependent lift above roughly 125 lb of patient weight requires two people or a powered lift-assist device, based on overexertion-injury data showing back injury as the dominant injury category for ambulance crews.

**In use:** "She's a full dependent lift and over the threshold — we're not doing this solo, get the powered cot or wait for the second unit."

**Common misuse:** estimating "I've got this" based on personal strength rather than the patient's actual dependent weight and lift geometry, which is what drives injury risk, not the lifter's fitness.

## Four-point lock check

The visual (not auditory) verification that a cot's four locking points — head-end, foot-end, both side rails, and the IV pole bracket if used — are actually engaged before the cot is moved or loaded, distinct from hearing the mechanism click.

**In use:** "It clicked, sure — now show me you actually looked at all four before we lift it."

**Common misuse:** treating the click as sufficient confirmation; the click means the latch engaged, not that it's carrying load correctly through transit vibration.

## Code 3 / Code 2 (dispatch nomenclature)

Regional shorthand for dispatch priority (roughly, Code 3 = lights-and-siren authorized; Code 2 = urgent but no L&S; conventions vary by agency and state, and some agencies avoid the numeric codes entirely in favor of plain-language classification).

**In use:** "Dispatch called it Code 2 — no lights, no siren, just don't dawdle."

**Common misuse:** assuming the numeric code is universal across agencies/states or using it as a driving-speed instruction rather than a mode classification.

## Repetitive Scheduled Non-Emergent Ambulance Transport (RSNAT) prior authorization

The CMS Medicare rule requiring prior authorization for repetitive non-emergency ambulance transport (in applicable states, generally from the fourth round trip within a rolling window) to control billing for recurring transports like dialysis.

**In use:** "This is trip four this month — check whether prior authorization already came back before we schedule the fifth."

**Common misuse:** treating RSNAT prior authorization as purely a back-office billing issue with no bearing on the crew, when a lapsed authorization can mean the transport shouldn't be scheduled as billed at all.
