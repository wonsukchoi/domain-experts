# Working vocabulary

Terms a personal financial advisor uses precisely, with the misuse a generalist (or a client-facing marketing page) commonly makes.

### Fiduciary duty vs. suitability / best interest
Fiduciary duty (Investment Advisers Act of 1940, §206) requires acting in the client's best interest at all times and eliminating or disclosing conflicts. "Best interest" under SEC Regulation Best Interest applies only at the point of a broker-dealer's recommendation and permits disclosed conflicts a fiduciary would have to avoid.
**In use:** "As an RIA representative I owe her a fiduciary duty on this account; the annuity recommendation from her prior broker only had to meet Reg BI."
**Common misuse:** using "fiduciary" as a general marketing claim rather than a fact about which specific account and role applies — the same advisor can owe different duties on different accounts of the same client.

### Safe withdrawal rate (SWR)
The initial percentage of a portfolio that can be withdrawn annually (adjusted for inflation) with historically low probability of depleting the portfolio over a stated horizon.
**In use:** "We're modeling 3.8% initial withdrawal, not the classic 4%, given where bond yields start this cycle."
**Common misuse:** treating 4% as a universal constant rather than a horizon- and starting-conditions-dependent figure that research revises as market conditions change.

### Sequence-of-returns risk
The risk that the *order* of investment returns, not just their average, determines whether a portfolio survives a withdrawal period — losses early in retirement do more damage than the same losses late, because withdrawals during a decline lock in losses before recovery.
**In use:** "Same 7% average return either way, but the retiree who front-loads two down years fails where the one who back-loads them doesn't."
**Common misuse:** reassuring a client with "the market averages 7-10% long-term" as if that resolves the risk — the average is irrelevant if the sequence is bad in the years withdrawals actually happen.

### Asset location vs. asset allocation
Asset allocation is the stock/bond/cash mix and always trades risk for expected return. Asset location is which *account type* (taxable, tax-deferred, Roth) holds which asset, and can improve after-tax return without changing the underlying risk.
**In use:** "Allocation stays 60/40 either way — we're just moving the bonds into the 401(k) and the index funds into the taxable account for the location benefit."
**Common misuse:** conflating the two, or assuming a location change is a market call rather than a tax-efficiency adjustment.

### Guardrails (Guyton-Klinger)
A dynamic spending-adjustment rule: cut planned spending by a set percentage (commonly 10%) if the trailing withdrawal rate drifts a set distance (commonly 20%) above the initial target; raise spending by the same percentage if it drifts the same distance below.
**In use:** "We hit the lower guardrail this year — withdrawal rate fell to 3.1% against a 4.0% target, so the plan supports a spending increase."
**Common misuse:** applying it as a one-time check instead of an ongoing rule checked at every review, or adjusting spending on a single bad year without checking whether the guardrail band was actually crossed.

### Risk tolerance vs. risk capacity
Risk tolerance is the client's psychological comfort with volatility, usually measured by questionnaire. Risk capacity is their actual financial ability to absorb a loss without harming the plan, driven by time horizon, income stability, and other assets.
**In use:** "His tolerance score says aggressive, but at 61 with no pension and a fixed retirement date, his capacity says otherwise — we split the difference and told him why."
**Common misuse:** setting the allocation from the tolerance questionnaire alone, treating comfort and capacity as the same input.

### Required Minimum Distribution (RMD)
The minimum amount that must be withdrawn annually from most tax-deferred retirement accounts once the account owner reaches the RMD age (73 under SECURE 2.0 for those turning 72 after 2022, rising to 75 in 2033); missed distributions carry an excise tax penalty (25%, reduced to 10% if corrected within two years).
**In use:** "Her first RMD year is this year — we need the distribution confirmed by December 31, not just calculated."
**Common misuse:** confusing the RMD age with the earlier ages that apply to older cohorts, or assuming an inherited IRA follows the same schedule as the original owner's account (inherited-IRA rules differ, including the 10-year rule for most non-spouse beneficiaries under the SECURE Act).

### Roth conversion
Moving funds from a traditional (pre-tax) retirement account to a Roth account, paying ordinary income tax on the converted amount now in exchange for tax-free growth and withdrawals later.
**In use:** "We're converting $60,000 this year to fill up the 22% bracket before it reverts, ahead of his RMDs starting."
**Common misuse:** converting a flat amount every year regardless of the client's current bracket, instead of sizing the conversion to the room left in the current bracket before the next one starts.

### Backdoor Roth and the pro-rata rule
A backdoor Roth is a non-deductible traditional IRA contribution immediately converted to Roth, used by earners above the direct Roth contribution income limit. The pro-rata rule requires the conversion to be taxed proportionally across *all* the taxpayer's traditional IRA balances, not just the new non-deductible contribution.
**In use:** "He has an old rollover IRA with pre-tax money in it, so a backdoor Roth here triggers a mostly-taxable conversion under the pro-rata rule — we need to move that balance into his 401(k) first."
**Common misuse:** recommending a backdoor Roth without checking for other traditional IRA balances that make the pro-rata rule bite.

### Mega backdoor Roth
Making after-tax (not Roth, not pre-tax) contributions to a 401(k) up to the total plan limit, then converting or rolling those after-tax dollars to a Roth 401(k) or Roth IRA — available only in plans that permit after-tax contributions and in-plan conversions or in-service withdrawals.
**In use:** "His plan allows after-tax contributions and in-plan Roth conversions, so we can get another $30,000/yr into Roth beyond the normal deferral limit."
**Common misuse:** assuming every 401(k) plan supports this — it depends entirely on specific plan provisions most plans don't have.

### Bucket strategy
Segmenting a retirement portfolio into time-horizon-based pools (near-term cash/bonds, intermediate, long-term growth), each drawn down and refilled on different rules, so near-term spending never depends on selling growth assets during a downturn.
**In use:** "Bucket one covers the next two years of spending in cash; we only refill it from bucket three's gains, never by selling during a drawdown."
**Common misuse:** describing an allocation as a "bucket strategy" without an actual refill rule — without the rule, it's just a labeled asset allocation, not a sequence-risk mitigation.

### Concentration risk and 10b5-1 plan
Concentration risk is exposure to a single security or issuer large enough to threaten the plan if it declines. A 10b5-1 plan is a pre-scheduled, pre-committed trading plan (commonly used by insiders) that permits selling on a fixed schedule regardless of the seller's later knowledge, avoiding insider-trading timing concerns.
**In use:** "We set up a 10b5-1 plan to sell 5% of the position each quarter regardless of price, so the diversification happens on schedule instead of on conviction."
**Common misuse:** treating "wait for a 10b5-1 plan" as equivalent to "wait for a better price" — the point of the plan is removing discretion and timing risk, not optimizing the sale price.

### Longevity risk
The risk of outliving a portfolio, distinct from market risk — a couple with a healthy 58-year-old has a meaningful chance one spouse lives past 95, which the plan must survive even though it's not the median outcome.
**In use:** "We're planning to age 95 for the survivor, not to life expectancy, because life expectancy is the age half of people outlive."
**Common misuse:** planning to an average life expectancy figure, which by definition leaves roughly half of clients under-provided for.

### Wash sale
IRS rule (IRC §1091) disallowing a claimed capital loss if a "substantially identical" security is purchased within 30 days before or after the sale — the rule applies across all accounts the taxpayer owns, including their own IRA (Rev. Rul. 2008-5), though not a spouse's separately-owned account.
**In use:** "The loss is disallowed — his 401(k) bought the same index fund eight days after we sold it in the taxable account."
**Common misuse:** checking only the account where the loss was realized instead of every account the same taxpayer owns.
