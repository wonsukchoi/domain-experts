# Dental Hygienist — Vocabulary

### Clinical attachment loss (CAL)
The distance from the cementoenamel junction to the base of the periodontal pocket — probing depth plus (or minus) gingival recession — and the actual measurement used to stage periodontal disease.
**In use:** "Probing depth on #3 distobuccal is 7mm, but with 2mm of recession, CAL is 9mm — that's what stages this tooth, not the 7."
**Common misuse:** Treated as interchangeable with probing depth; probing depth alone doesn't account for recession and can under- or overstate actual attachment loss.

### Staging
The 2017 AAP/EFP classification axis that measures how much periodontal damage has already occurred (Stage I–IV), based on CAL, radiographic bone loss, and tooth loss.
**In use:** "This mouth stages at III because of one molar with 9mm CAL and furcation involvement, even though the rest of the mouth is Stage I."
**Common misuse:** Averaged across the mouth instead of set by the worst qualifying site — staging is a worst-case classification, not a mean.

### Grading
The 2017 AAP/EFP classification axis that measures how fast disease is progressing (Grade A–C), based on the bone-loss-to-age ratio and risk factors like smoking or diabetes.
**In use:** "Same Stage II as last patient, but this one's a smoker with bone loss out of proportion to age — that's Grade C, not A, and changes the recall interval."
**Common misuse:** Confused with staging; grading answers "how fast," staging answers "how much damage so far" — they're independent axes, not two names for severity.

### Bleeding on probing (BOP)
Bleeding elicited by gentle probing, indicating active gingival inflammation at that site right now.
**In use:** "BOP is down from 18% to 6% full-mouth since the last SRP — the tissue is responding."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a severity indicator like CAL; BOP tracks present-tense inflammation and trend over time, not how much attachment has already been lost.

### Furcation involvement
Loss of bone/attachment at the point where a multi-rooted tooth's roots divide, graded Class I (early) through Class III (through-and-through).
**In use:** "Class II furcation on the mesial root of #3 — the probe enters but doesn't pass all the way through."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to only matter on molars in general; while true multi-rooted teeth are the ones with furcations, the grading distinction (I vs. II vs. III) meaningfully changes prognosis and is often glossed over as "some furcation involvement."

### Scaling and root planing (SRP)
Non-surgical removal of subgingival calculus and biofilm, plus smoothing of the root surface, typically performed by quadrant under local anesthesia for moderate-to-severe periodontitis.
**In use:** "SRP on quadrant 1 today, re-eval at 4-6 weeks before we decide on quadrant 2's timing."
**Common misuse:** Called a "deep cleaning" without distinguishing it from routine prophylaxis; SRP is a therapeutic procedure for diagnosed periodontal disease, not a more-thorough version of a regular cleaning.

### Prophylaxis
Preventive removal of plaque and calculus above and slightly below the gumline on a patient without active periodontal disease.
**In use:** "Probing depths are all 1-3mm with no bleeding — this patient's due for a straightforward prophy, not SRP."
**Common misuse:** Used interchangeably with SRP; the distinction is clinical (disease present or not), not just how deep the instrumentation reaches.

### Periodontal maintenance
The ongoing recall visit for a patient previously treated for periodontitis, distinct from prophylaxis, typically at a shorter interval (3-4 months) and including re-charting to monitor for recurrence.
**In use:** "She's out of active treatment, but she's on periodontal maintenance, not routine prophy — different coding, different interval."
**Common misuse:** Reverted to a standard 6-month recall once a patient "looks stable," without recognizing that periodontal maintenance interval is set by grade and treatment history, not current appearance alone.

### Local anesthesia (hygiene scope)
Anesthetic administered by a hygienist (where state law permits) for patient comfort during SRP or deep scaling, distinct from a dentist's broader anesthesia scope.
**In use:** "State allows hygienists to administer local, so I'll numb quadrant 1 before starting SRP."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to be uniformly permitted; hygienist scope for local anesthesia administration varies significantly by state and must be verified, not assumed from training alone.

### Biofilm
The structured microbial community (not just loose debris) that forms on tooth surfaces and is the primary driver of gingival and periodontal inflammation.
**In use:** "The disclosing agent shows biofilm concentrated at the gumline on the lower anteriors — that's where the brushing technique is missing."
**Common misuse:** Used as a synonym for "plaque" without the implication that it's an organized, adherent structure requiring mechanical disruption — not something a mouthwash alone reliably removes.

### Attachment apparatus
The combined structures (gingiva, periodontal ligament, cementum, alveolar bone) that anchor a tooth — what periodontal disease progressively destroys.
**In use:** "Once the attachment apparatus is lost, it doesn't regenerate with SRP alone — that's why Stage III/IV cases often need a periodontist."
**Common misuse:** Conflated with just "the gums"; the attachment apparatus includes bone and ligament, which is why radiographic bone loss (not just soft-tissue appearance) is part of staging.
