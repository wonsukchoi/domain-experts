# Red flags

### Blend uniformity judged by visual appearance or standard elapsed mixing time rather than multi-location sampling and statistical calculation
- **Usually means:** an ingredient, especially a low-concentration one, could be unevenly distributed enough to fail specification despite looking fine.
- **First question:** has the blend actually been sampled from multiple distinct locations and a CV calculated against spec?
- **Data to pull:** uniformity sampling/CV result compared against the specification.

### A low-concentration minor component is added directly to a full batch without a premix/intermediate blending step
- **Usually means:** risk of poor dispersion even though the total quantity weighed and added is correct.
- **First question:** what is this component's concentration in the final batch, and was a premix step used per the validated procedure?
- **Data to pull:** component concentration in the final batch compared against whether a premix step was used, per the validated procedure for this formula.

### Mixing continued past the validated standard time on the assumption more mixing always improves uniformity
- **Usually means:** risk of segregation/demixing or particle attrition rather than further uniformity improvement.
- **First question:** what is the actual mixing time used versus the validated standard time for this formula/batch size?
- **Data to pull:** actual mixing time used compared against the validated standard time for this formula/batch size.

### Batch component weights verified only via a completed checklist rather than checking actual scale readings against the formula
- **Usually means:** a missing or incorrect-quantity ingredient could go undetected, since mixing can't distinguish a properly-distributed ingredient from one that was never added.
- **First question:** what do the actual recorded scale readings show for each ingredient versus the formula's required quantities?
- **Data to pull:** actual scale readings recorded per ingredient compared against the formula's required quantities.

### A formula/process scaled to a different batch size using the same proportional mixing time and parameters without re-verifying uniformity
- **Usually means:** mixing dynamics that don't scale linearly could produce different uniformity results than the smaller validated batch.
- **First question:** what do uniformity test results at the new batch size actually show versus the previously validated smaller-scale results?
- **Data to pull:** uniformity test results at the new batch size compared against the previously validated smaller-scale results.

### Components with meaningfully different particle size or density are mixed and then subjected to extended handling/transport without considering segregation risk
- **Usually means:** a uniformly-mixed batch could still demix during subsequent handling before packaging or use.
- **First question:** how large is the particle size/density difference between components, and what handling steps follow mixing?
- **Data to pull:** particle size/density difference between components compared against post-mixing handling/transport steps and their segregation risk.

### A recurring uniformity failure pattern appears for a specific formula/batch size without being traced to a specific cause
- **Usually means:** a systemic process issue — premix step, mixing time, weighing accuracy — rather than isolated one-off batch variation.
- **First question:** does the uniformity failure log correlate with a specific process parameter trend?
- **Data to pull:** uniformity failure log by formula/batch size compared against process parameter logs (premix used, mixing time, weighing verification) over the same period.
