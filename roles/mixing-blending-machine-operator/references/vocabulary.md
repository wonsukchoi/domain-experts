# Vocabulary

### Blend uniformity
The statistical consistency of ingredient concentration across different locations within a mixed batch, typically measured as coefficient of variation (CV) or relative standard deviation (RSD) from multi-location sampling.
**In use:** "Blend uniformity's tested at 10 locations, CV comes in at 3.2% — within the 5% spec for this formula."
**Common misuse:** Judging blend uniformity by visual appearance or standard mixing time alone, rather than actual multi-location sampling and statistical calculation.

### Coefficient of variation (CV) / relative standard deviation (RSD)
A statistical measure — standard deviation divided by the mean — used to quantify how uniformly a component is distributed across multiple samples from a batch.
**In use:** "CV across the ten samples is 4.1% — that's within spec, but close enough to the 5% limit to keep an eye on this formula."
**Common misuse:** Treating a single sample's measured concentration as sufficient evidence of uniformity, when CV specifically requires comparing multiple samples from different batch locations.

### Premix
An intermediate blend created by first combining a low-concentration minor component with a portion of a major component, at a higher and easier-to-verify concentration, before incorporating it into the full batch.
**In use:** "Premix this vitamin into 25 kg of excipient first — don't add the raw 2.5 kg straight into the 500 kg batch."
**Common misuse:** Adding a low-concentration ingredient directly to a full-size batch, skipping the premix step that specifically exists to improve dispersion of components present at very low concentrations.

### Segregation (demixing)
The tendency of a previously well-mixed blend to separate again, often due to differences in particle size or density between components, especially during extended mixing, handling, or transport.
**In use:** "That blend tested uniform right after mixing, but the transport step afterward is a known segregation risk for these particle sizes."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a blend that tested uniform immediately after mixing will remain uniform through subsequent handling, without considering whether particle characteristics create ongoing segregation risk.

### Order of addition
The specific sequence in which batch components are added and blended, which affects dispersion outcomes especially for minor or potent components.
**In use:** "Order of addition on this formula isn't arbitrary — the premix goes in after the major excipients are already blended, not before."
**Common misuse:** Treating order of addition as an arbitrary convenience choice rather than a formulated process parameter that affects final uniformity.

### Scale-up (mixing/blending)
The process of moving a validated formula and mixing process from a smaller batch size to a larger one, which does not automatically preserve the same uniformity performance.
**In use:** "This mixing time was validated at 100 kg — re-verify uniformity before assuming it transfers directly to the new 500 kg batch size."
**Common misuse:** Assuming proportionally scaling ingredient quantities and mixing time at a larger batch size preserves the smaller batch's validated uniformity result, without re-verification.

### Multi-location sampling (sampling thief)
The practice of pulling samples from multiple distinct physical locations within a mixed batch, not just one spot, to assess true uniformity.
**In use:** "Sampling thief pulled from the top, middle, and bottom of the blender — that's what the CV calculation is actually based on."
**Common misuse:** Pulling a single sample, often from an easily accessible location like the top, and treating it as representative of the whole batch's uniformity.

### Attrition (mixing)
Unwanted breakdown or size reduction of particles caused by excessive mixing time or intensity, distinct from the intended blending action.
**In use:** "Extended mixing time on this batch caused attrition — particle size distribution shifted finer than spec."
**Common misuse:** Assuming mixing intensity or time can be increased indefinitely without consequence, when excessive mixing can degrade particle characteristics important to the final product.

### Batch weighing verification
The practice of checking each component's actual measured weight against the formula's required quantity before mixing begins.
**In use:** "Scale reading confirms 2.5 kg of premix actually weighed out — not just a checkbox that it was added."
**Common misuse:** Relying on a completed checklist indicating an ingredient was "added" rather than verifying the actual scale reading recorded for that specific ingredient.

### Fill level / mixer geometry (scale-up factor)
The relationship between a mixer's physical dimensions and blade configuration and the volume of material being processed, which changes mixing dynamics at different batch sizes.
**In use:** "Fill level's different at this batch size — the blade clearance relative to the batch volume isn't the same ratio as the validated small-batch run."
**Common misuse:** Assuming mixing performance is purely a function of ingredient ratios and time, without considering how the physical relationship between mixer geometry and batch fill level changes at a different scale.
