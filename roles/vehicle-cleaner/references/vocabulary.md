# Vocabulary

Terms generalists conflate or use loosely that a fleet cleaner keeps precise, because the precision is what a protocol or a damage claim actually turns on.

### OPIM (Other Potentially Infectious Materials) vs. "bodily fluid"
OPIM is the OSHA 29 CFR 1910.1030 term covering blood and specific body fluids (semen, vaginal secretions, cerebrospinal/synovial/pleural fluid, and any body fluid visibly contaminated with blood, among others) — it's a defined regulatory category, not a synonym for "anything wet that came from a person."

**In use:** "Vomit with visible blood in it is OPIM — full protocol. Plain vomit without blood still gets treated the same way under our site policy, but that's a house rule stacked on top of the regulation, not the regulation itself."

**Common misuse:** treating "bodily fluid" as a vague catch-all and deciding case-by-case whether it "counts," instead of defaulting to the protocol whenever the material is ambiguous.

### Universal Precautions
The principle that all blood and specified body fluids are treated as presumptively infectious for bloodborne pathogens (HIV, HBV, HCV) regardless of the source's apparent health or how minor the spill looks.

**In use:** "Doesn't matter that it's a kid's nosebleed and looks trivial — Universal Precautions means it gets the same PPE and disinfection as anything else."

**Common misuse:** scaling the response to how serious the spill *looks* rather than to what the material *is* — a small, clean-looking spot gets the same protocol as a large one.

### Dwell time (contact time)
The minimum time a disinfectant must remain wet on a surface to achieve the claim on its label (e.g., a tuberculocidal claim). This number comes from the EPA-registered product label, not from OSHA and not from house policy.

**In use:** "Label says 10-minute dwell for the tuberculocidal claim — we don't wipe it early just because the bus needs to roll."

**Common misuse:** treating dwell time as a suggestion that can compress under schedule pressure, or assuming a shorter-dwell product is "just as good" without checking the label claim it's actually rated for.

### Tuberculocidal claim
An EPA registration category indicating a disinfectant has been tested effective against *Mycobacterium tuberculosis*, used as the benchmark for "hospital-grade" efficacy against bloodborne pathogens — a stronger and more specific standard than a general "disinfectant" or "sanitizer" claim.

**In use:** "Grab the tuberculocidal-rated product from the bio-kit, not the everyday all-purpose disinfectant off the general cart."

**Common misuse:** using any product labeled "disinfectant" interchangeably for a bio-event, when only a subset carry the tuberculocidal claim the situation actually calls for.

### Quat (quaternary ammonium compound)
A class of disinfectant/sanitizer active ingredient, typically effective in the ~150–400 ppm range depending on the labeled use, common in fleet cleaning for its broad efficacy on hard surfaces — but formulated for non-porous surfaces, not upholstery.

**In use:** "The quat's fine on the stanchions and floor at 300 ppm — don't reach for it on the leatherette seats, that's a different product."

**Common misuse:** using quat solution as an all-purpose cleaner on upholstery because "it's a disinfectant, disinfectants are good," which degrades vinyl/leatherette coating over repeated exposure.

### Turnaround time vs. ground time vs. layover
**Turnaround time** is the general term for the window between one use of a vehicle and its next; **ground time** is the aviation-specific term for an aircraft's time at the gate between flights; **layover** is the transit/rail term for scheduled recovery time between runs, which is officially there for the driver/crew, not for cleaning.

**In use:** "Don't count on the layover to get the deep clean done — that time belongs to the driver's break, not the cleaning crew."

**Common misuse:** assuming scheduled recovery time (layover) is available cleaning capacity, when it's frequently allocated elsewhere and cleaning has to fit around it, not into it.

### Chain of custody (lost-and-found context)
An unbroken, timestamped record of who had possession of a found item and when, from discovery through release or disposal — the record itself is the thing that resolves a dispute, not anyone's memory of events.

**In use:** "We don't have a dispute here — the chain of custody shows the phone was logged at 11:42 p.m. in seat 14C and released to the passenger with matching ID two days later."

**Common misuse:** treating "lost-and-found" as just a storage location rather than a documentation chain — an item can be physically secure and still have no usable chain of custody if the log entry is missing or late.

### Exposure incident vs. near miss
An **exposure incident** under 29 CFR 1910.1030 is a specific eye, mouth, mucous-membrane, non-intact-skin, or parenteral (needlestick-type) contact with blood or OPIM — it triggers mandatory reporting and post-exposure evaluation. A **near miss** is a situation that could have caused one but didn't result in actual contact.

**In use:** "Glove tore during containment but no contact with skin — that's a near miss, log it for tracking, but it's not a reportable exposure incident."

**Common misuse:** using "exposure" loosely for any bio-event handled, or conversely downplaying an actual contact as "probably fine" instead of triggering the mandatory reporting and evaluation process.

### PSI vs. GPM
**PSI** (pounds per square inch) measures the pressure-washer's force; **GPM** (gallons per minute) measures its flow/volume. Two washers rated at the same PSI can have very different GPM, and it's the combination — not PSI alone — that determines both cleaning power and damage risk to a component.

**In use:** "It's not just the PSI that's too high for that connector — the GPM on this unit means more water volume gets forced into any gap the seal has."

**Common misuse:** citing PSI alone as "how strong" a pressure washer is, ignoring GPM's role in how much water actually reaches (and can intrude into) a component.

### Regulated waste (biohazard waste)
Waste contaminated with blood or OPIM to the point it requires special containment, labeling, and disposal under bloodborne-pathogen and often state/local medical-waste rules — a distinct stream from general trash, not "trash from a bio-event."

**In use:** "Bag it as regulated waste and put it in the labeled container — it doesn't go in the same bin as the regular trash pull."

**Common misuse:** disposing of bio-event materials in general waste because the visible contamination looks minor or has been wiped away, when the regulatory trigger is the material's origin, not its current appearance.

### Ready line (rental fleet)
The staging area and process where a returned rental vehicle is cleaned, inspected, fueled, and re-flagged available before its next reservation — the rental-industry equivalent of a transit turnaround window.

**In use:** "That car's not ready-line yet — maintenance flagged a tire issue, it needs to clear that before it goes back on the lot as available."

**Common misuse:** treating "cleaned" and "ready-line cleared" as the same status — a vehicle can be clean and still blocked from availability by a maintenance or fuel hold.

### Deep clean vs. turn clean (quick-turn clean)
A **turn clean** (or quick-turn clean) is the abbreviated service done inside a fixed turnaround window — trash, visible surfaces, high-touch points. A **deep clean** is the periodic, longer-cycle service — carpet extraction, upholstery treatment, full disinfection — that a turn clean explicitly defers.

**In use:** "That stain's not coming out on a turn clean — flag it for the next deep-clean cycle, don't burn ten minutes on it now."

**Common misuse:** trying to accomplish deep-clean-level results inside a turn-clean window, which either blows the schedule or produces a rushed, incomplete version of the deep-clean task instead of a proper turn clean.
