# Tour Guide — Vocabulary

### Route-timing sheet
A stop-by-stop breakdown of planned narrative duration and transit time between stops, summed against the tour's total sold length.
**In use:** "The route-timing sheet has zero reserve built in, so any overrun at stop two has to come out of a later stop."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a one-time planning document instead of an actively managed budget checked against real-time progress.

### Transition point
Any moment the group moves between locations or re-forms after being dispersed (leaving a stop, re-boarding a bus, exiting a crowded venue) — the designated moment for a headcount.
**In use:** "We do a headcount at every transition point, not just the ones on the schedule."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to mean only the scheduled stop-to-stop moves, missing unscheduled dispersal points like a crowded venue interior.

### Contingency plan / fallback stop
A pre-decided alternate route, shelter point, or early-ending plan for a route's most likely disruptions, prepared before the tour runs, not improvised during it.
**In use:** "The fallback for rain at the viewpoint is the covered arcade below — we don't decide that in the moment."
**Common misuse:** Conflated with general "thinking on your feet" flexibility, when the point is specifically to remove the need for improvisation under pressure.

### Headcount baseline
The confirmed group-size number a guide counts against at each transition point.
**In use:** "Someone joined late at stop two, so the headcount baseline is now nine, not eight."
**Common misuse:** Left un-updated after a group-composition change, causing a correct count to look like a false-positive mismatch.

### Narrative depth / narrative calibration
The amount and level of detail delivered at a given stop, adjusted to the specific group's visible engagement rather than a fixed script.
**In use:** "This group's asking great questions, so I'm going deeper on the narrative here and trimming it at the next stop."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a fixed property of the stop itself ("this stop gets ten minutes") rather than something calibrated per group per run.

### Recency effect
The tendency for a group to disproportionately remember the tour's final stop and closing remarks, used as a reason to protect the last stop's time budget when trimming elsewhere.
**In use:** "We're trimming stop three and five, not the final stop — the recency effect means that's the one people remember."
**Common misuse:** Used to justify cutting every earlier stop to the bone rather than distributing trims proportionally.

### Marquee stop
The route's primary destination or most significant/advertised stop — typically, though not always, the final stop.
**In use:** "The marquee stop is what people bought the tour for; it gets full planned time even under time pressure."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to always be the last stop chronologically, when some routes' marquee stop is in the middle of the sequence.

### Pace deficit
The cumulative gap between planned and actual walking/transit time caused by a group moving slower than the route-timing sheet assumed.
**In use:** "We confirmed an 18-minute pace deficit at the stop-three headcount — that's what we're trimming for, not a single bad stop."
**Common misuse:** Diagnosed from a single late arrival instead of tracked cumulatively across multiple transition points.

### Chokepoint
A narrow or high-density point along a route (a doorway, a crowded market lane) where the group is more likely to separate or where a headcount is harder to take accurately.
**In use:** "The market entrance is a chokepoint — we count right before and right after, not just at the stop's start and end."
**Common misuse:** Not distinguished from ordinary transit segments, so extra vigilance isn't applied where it's actually needed.

### Early-ending route
A pre-planned shortened version of the full route that still delivers a complete, satisfying experience, used when a disruption (severe weather, venue closure) makes finishing the full route unsafe or impossible.
**In use:** "This isn't an improvised cut-short tour — the early-ending route is a planned alternative that still has a real closing stop."
**Common misuse:** Treated as simply stopping wherever the disruption occurred, rather than routing to a pre-selected stop that still functions as a satisfying ending.
