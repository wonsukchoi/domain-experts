# Vocabulary

### IFSP (Individualized Family Service Plan)
The Part C document (birth to age 3) built around family-centered outcomes and natural environments, distinct from the Part B IEP that takes over at age 3 and is built around the child's own goals in a school setting.
**In use:** "We're carrying his IFSP outcomes forward into the IEP present levels, not starting the write-up from scratch."
**Common misuse:** Treating the IFSP and IEP as interchangeable paperwork formats — the IFSP centers the family and natural routines as the intervention context, while the IEP centers the child's goals in an educational setting; converting one into the other requires re-framing, not just re-labeling.

### Developmental delay (eligibility category)
A distinct disability eligibility category under IDEA usable for children up to a state-specified age (commonly through age 9), used when a child's functioning is significantly behind typical development without yet fitting a specific diagnostic category.
**In use:** "His eligibility category is developmental delay, not autism — the evaluation hasn't gotten to a differential diagnosis yet, and at his age it doesn't need to."
**Common misuse:** Treating "developmental delay" as a placeholder that everyone secretly means a specific diagnosis — the category exists precisely because differential diagnosis at this age is often premature or unnecessary for services to begin, and using it as a specific-diagnosis stand-in on paper misrepresents what's actually been determined.

### Embedded intervention (routines-based intervention)
Delivering a targeted skill practice inside an existing daily routine or preferred play activity, rather than as a separate pulled-out drill session.
**In use:** "The vocabulary target is embedded in bath time and mealtime — there's no separate 'language lesson' block on his schedule."
**Common misuse:** Calling a session "embedded" because it happens near play materials, while the actual instruction is still a seated drill sequence conducted alongside the toys rather than inside the play's own structure.

### Present levels (present levels of performance)
The specific, current, functional and developmental description of what a child can and cannot do, forming the baseline every goal is measured against.
**In use:** "Present levels show 22 words expressive vocabulary at intake — that's the number the goal target has to build from."
**Common misuse:** Writing present levels as a general narrative ("communicates well with some support") instead of specific baseline numbers a later data point can be compared against.

### Caregiver coaching
A structured model-observe-feedback cycle where the teacher demonstrates a strategy, watches the caregiver attempt it, and gives specific feedback, so the caregiver becomes the primary strategy-delivery agent in daily routines.
**In use:** "I modeled the expansion strategy at today's visit and watched her try it once before I gave feedback — that's the coaching cycle, not just handing her a strategy sheet."
**Common misuse:** Treating "caregiver coaching" as simply informing the caregiver what to do (a handout or verbal instruction) rather than the full model-observe-feedback cycle that actually predicts whether the caregiver can execute the strategy independently.

### ASQ-3 (Ages & Stages Questionnaires, Third Edition)
A norm-referenced parent-completed screening tool scoring five domains separately against age-banded cutoff scores, used to flag which specific domain (not an overall composite) may need a closer look.
**In use:** "Communication scored 17.5 against a cutoff of 24.16 at the 33-month interval — that's what triggered the referral, not an overall low score."
**Common misuse:** Averaging or summarizing the five domain scores into one overall impression — the tool is designed to separate domains precisely because averaging hides which specific domain needs attention.

### Transition conference (Part C to Part B)
The required meeting, held at least 90 days before a child's third birthday, where the Part C team and the local education agency plan the move from IFSP to IEP services.
**In use:** "We're inside the transition window — the conference needs to be on the calendar in the next two weeks to clear 90 days before his birthday."
**Common misuse:** Treating the transition conference as optional paperwork that can be scheduled whenever convenient — missing the 90-day window is a procedural violation regardless of whether the eventual eligibility decision is substantively correct.

### Joint attention
A child's ability to coordinate attention between an object/event and another person (through gaze, gesture, or vocalization) to share an experience, a core early social-communication skill assessed separately from expressive vocabulary.
**In use:** "His joint attention and gesture use are age-appropriate — that's part of why this reads as an isolated expressive-language delay, not a broader social-communication concern."
**Common misuse:** Folding joint attention into a general "communication" score rather than assessing it specifically — a child can have strong joint attention with low expressive vocabulary, and collapsing the two into one judgment can trigger an unnecessarily broad referral.

### Trajectory (rate of gain)
A child's own observed rate of progress on a specific skill over time, used as the baseline a goal timeline and later progress checks are compared against, instead of a population-average milestone age.
**In use:** "He's gaining vocabulary at about 13 words a month against his own 2-word-a-month baseline — that's the number the 6-month target was built from."
**Common misuse:** Comparing a child's progress to when peers typically reach a milestone rather than to the child's own established rate of gain — normal milestone-attainment ages vary widely, and the relevant comparison for judging a plateau is the child's own trajectory, not a population chart.
