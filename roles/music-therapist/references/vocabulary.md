# Music therapy working vocabulary

Terms a music therapist uses precisely and a generalist blurs together. Format: definition → how a practitioner says it → common misuse.

**Iso principle** — Opening an intervention with music that matches the client's current tempo, energy, and affect, then gradually shifting toward the target state, rather than starting with target-state music.
In use: "He came in agitated, so we opened matched to that energy and brought the tempo down over the session — starting slow would have lost him."
Misuse: assuming the "right" music for a goal (e.g., calm music for an agitated client) should be played from minute one — starting at the target state rather than matching the presenting state first often escalates rather than resolves the presenting state.

**Entrainment** — The involuntary physiological synchronization of a motor or biological rhythm (gait, heart rate, breath) to an external auditory rhythm, distinct from a client consciously trying to keep time with music.
In use: "The cadence gain isn't the patient trying harder — it's entrainment, the motor system synchronizing to the rhythmic cue involuntarily."
Misuse: treating entrainment as equivalent to motivation or effort — it's a documented physiological mechanism that functions even when voluntary motor control is impaired.

**RAS (Rhythmic Auditory Stimulation)** — A Neurologic Music Therapy technique using a rhythmic cue set relative to a client's measured baseline cadence to drive gait improvements, progressed in defined percentage increments with periodic overground re-testing.
In use: "We're at 110% of baseline on the RAS cadence — time to re-test overground before progressing further."
Misuse: using "RAS" loosely for any music played during walking practice — it specifically refers to the baseline-relative, incrementally progressed, re-tested protocol, not background music during ambulation.

**PSE (Patterned Sensory Enhancement)** — A Neurologic Music Therapy technique using musical elements (rhythm, dynamics, melodic contour) to cue and structure the timing and spatial/temporal patterning of a functional movement sequence other than gait (e.g., reaching, sit-to-stand).
In use: "We used PSE to cue the sequencing of the sit-to-stand transfer, not just the gait cadence."
Misuse: conflating PSE with RAS — RAS is specifically for gait/locomotor rhythm; PSE covers a broader range of functional movement sequences.

**TIMP (Therapeutic Instrumental Music Performance)** — A Neurologic Music Therapy technique using instrument playing to exercise and functionally train specific motor patterns (range of motion, finger dexterity, endurance) relevant to a client's rehabilitation goal.
In use: "TIMP with the drum placed at end-range gives him a functional reason to reach further than he would for a stretching exercise alone."
Misuse: treating any instrument-playing activity in a session as TIMP — it specifically means the instrument's placement and playing pattern are engineered to target a documented motor deficit, not general recreational playing.

**MIT (Melodic Intonation Therapy)** — A structured protocol using intoned (sung) speech, hand-tapping, and fading cues to help clients with non-fluent aphasia access speech through relatively preserved melodic/prosodic pathways.
In use: "He's a good MIT candidate — comprehension's intact and he can hum a simple contour, even though propositional speech is very limited."
Misuse: assuming MIT is appropriate for any aphasia diagnosis — it's specifically indicated for non-fluent, expressive-type presentations with a documented intact singing-ability screen, not fluent or receptive aphasia.

**GIM (Bonny Method of Guided Imagery and Music)** — A structured method using classical/orchestral music and guided imagery to access unconscious material for insight-oriented psychotherapeutic work, requiring training at a specific level through the Association for Music and Imagery (AMI) before use at that depth.
In use: "That level of evocative imagery work is beyond what I'm trained for — I'd refer to a Level 2 GIM practitioner for that."
Misuse: treating any music-and-relaxation-imagery activity as "GIM" — the term specifically refers to the trained, structured Bonny Method protocol, not informal guided relaxation with background music.

**Titration (trauma-informed use)** — Deliberately limiting the intensity, openness, or evocative depth of a musical intervention (structured/predictable music, shorter exposure, therapist-directed rather than free improvisation) to keep a trauma-history client within a tolerable range rather than risking overwhelm.
In use: "We're titrating this — structured, familiar material only, until we've established more stability, rather than moving straight to open improvisation."
Misuse: treating titration as simply "going slow" in a generic sense — it specifically means calibrating stimulus intensity against a documented risk profile (dissociation, flooding), not just a cautious pace for its own sake.

**Reminiscence bump** — The well-documented tendency for autobiographical memory, including music memory, to be disproportionately strong for the late-adolescence-to-young-adulthood period of a person's life, relevant to repertoire selection for memory/orientation work.
In use: "For repertoire, we're targeting music from when she was 15 to 25 — that's where the reminiscence bump puts the strongest recall."
Misuse: assuming any music from a client's past will land equally well — the bump effect concentrates around a specific age window, and material outside it (early childhood, later adulthood) is typically less potent for this purpose.

**Active vs. receptive music therapy** — Active involves the client making music (singing, playing, moving); receptive involves the client listening while the therapist or a recording provides the music — a modality choice made against the specific goal, not a stylistic preference.
In use: "The goal here is expressive output, so we're in active mode — receptive listening alone won't touch that goal."
Misuse: treating "active" as inherently more therapeutic or "receptive" as inherently passive/lesser — the correct modality depends entirely on which one the stated goal actually requires.

**Contingent responsiveness** — The capacity of live music delivery to adjust in real time to a client's actual state (tempo, breath, emotional shift), which recorded music cannot provide once started.
In use: "I need contingent responsiveness here — his gait speed varies breath to breath, and a fixed recording can't track that."
Misuse: assuming live music is always superior to recorded — contingent responsiveness matters specifically when the goal requires real-time adjustment; when the goal is fixed predictability itself, recorded music is the correct choice, not a compromise.

**Identified goal area vs. presenting behavior** — The documented, measurable clinical target (gait cadence, word retrieval rate, a specific behavior frequency) versus the surface behavior observed in a session (enjoyment, engagement, mood).
In use: "She seemed to enjoy the session, but the identified goal area is word-retrieval rate — that's what the note needs to track."
Misuse: charting session enjoyment or general engagement as if it were equivalent to progress on the identified goal area — enjoyment is not itself a billable or clinically sufficient outcome.

**Community ambulation threshold** — A functional gait-speed/cadence benchmark used in rehabilitation to indicate a patient can safely navigate real-world walking demands (crossing streets, uneven surfaces) rather than only supervised indoor walking.
In use: "The RAS progression is aimed at getting his cadence up toward the community ambulation threshold before discharge, not just improved indoor gait."
Misuse: treating any cadence improvement as sufficient for discharge planning without checking it against the specific functional threshold the discharge setting requires.
