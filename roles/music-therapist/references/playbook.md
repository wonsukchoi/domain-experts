# Music therapy protocol playbook

Filled worksheets for the four recurring tasks: RAS gait progression, iso-principle session opening, melodic intonation therapy, and trauma/GIM screening.

## 1. RAS (Rhythmic Auditory Stimulation) gait progression worksheet

```
Client baseline overground cadence (average of 3 x 10m trials): <__ steps/min>
Session 1 RAS tempo: 100% of baseline (= baseline bpm)
Session 2 RAS tempo: 105% of baseline
Session 3 RAS tempo: 110% of baseline
  -> Re-measure overground cadence (RAS removed), average of 3 trials: <__ steps/min>
  -> % gain over original baseline: (new overground - original baseline) / original baseline
  -> Carryover check: is the overground gain present without the cue? (yes/no)
If yes: reset 100% mark to new overground baseline; resume 5-10%/session progression.
If no (gain is cue-dependent only): hold current tempo 2-3 more sessions before re-testing;
  do not progress tempo further until overground carryover appears.
```

Rule: never progress RAS tempo past 110% of the last confirmed baseline without an overground re-test — progressing on cued performance alone conflates entrainment-assisted walking with functional gait change.

## 2. Iso-principle session opening worksheet

```
Client presenting state at session start:
  Affect/energy (agitated / low-energy / dysregulated / neutral): <___>
  Approximate tempo the client's current movement/speech/breath suggests: <__ bpm, if observable>

Opening music parameters (match current state):
  Tempo: <match observed tempo or affect-appropriate estimate>
  Dynamics/volume: <match>
  Mode/key: <match, e.g. minor for low affect, avoid immediate major/upbeat>

Shift sequence (gradual, not stepwise-only within a single session):
  Minute 0-3: matched to presenting state
  Minute 3-8: gradual tempo/dynamic shift toward target state (no more than ~10-15% change
    per 2-3 minute increment; faster shifts risk losing the client's engagement or escalating)
  Minute 8+: target-state music, checking client response before continuing

Exception: if acute safety risk is present (e.g. self-harm risk, severe agitation with elopement
  risk), skip iso sequencing and use direct, structured calming intervention instead; document
  the exception and rationale.
```

Rule: an opening that starts at the target state without matching the presenting state first is the single most common junior error with agitated or dysregulated clients — document the presenting-state match explicitly, not just the target.

## 3. Melodic Intonation Therapy (MIT) candidacy and protocol worksheet

```
Candidacy screen (all should be present or MIT is a poor fit):
  - Non-fluent/expressive aphasia (Broca's-type presentation): <yes/no>
  - Relatively preserved auditory comprehension: <yes/no>
  - Intact or near-intact singing ability on simple melodic phrases (screen with a familiar
    tune hummed/sung): <yes/no>
  - Left-hemisphere lesion with right hemisphere available to recruit for melodic processing: <yes/no>

Protocol levels (progress only when the current level is consistently successful):
  Level 1: therapist intones a short, high-frequency phrase (2-3 syllables) on a simple two-note
    melodic contour while tapping the client's left hand in rhythm; client hums along, then
    attempts unison intonation.
  Level 2: therapist fades support (tapping, then intoning) as client repeats phrases with less cueing.
  Level 3: client intones the phrase independently, then transitions toward more natural
    speech-like prosody ("sprechgesang") without the exaggerated melodic contour.

Tracking: phrase list attempted, level reached, independent success rate this session (X/Y phrases).
```

Rule: don't begin MIT without confirming the singing-ability screen — non-fluent aphasia alone doesn't guarantee intact melodic pathways to exploit, and starting the protocol on an unscreened client wastes sessions on a technique mismatched to the actual deficit.

## 4. Trauma-history / GIM screening worksheet

```
Client trauma history flagged: <yes/no, source: referral / intake / disclosed in session>

Dissociation-risk screen (illustrative, not exhaustive):
  - History of dissociative episodes or diagnosis: <___>
  - Reports of "zoning out," time loss, or feeling disconnected from body: <___>
  - Current stability (recent crisis, hospitalization, acute substance use): <___>

If any risk indicators present, or therapist lacks AMI-recognized GIM training level for the
depth of work being considered:
  -> Do NOT proceed with unstructured/evocative improvisation or Bonny Method GIM imagery work.
  -> Use structured, therapist-directed, predictable music (known repertoire, clear start/stop,
     no extended free-associative imagery prompts) instead.
  -> Refer to a GIM-trained clinician at the appropriate level if deeper evocative work is
     clinically indicated.

If no risk indicators and appropriate training level is held:
  -> Proceed with informed consent specific to the evocative/imagery modality, with a
     pre-agreed grounding technique the client can invoke if flooding occurs.
```

Rule: trauma history alone doesn't rule out evocative work, but the combination of trauma history plus an active dissociation-risk indicator plus lack of matched training level is an absolute stop — proceeding anyway risks flooding a client with no safety net in the room.
