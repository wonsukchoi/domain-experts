# Red flags — signals a music therapist notices instantly

Smell tests with thresholds. Any one alone may be a documentation gap; two or more together warrants an immediate reassessment of the treatment plan.

### A session plan lists song titles with no non-musical goal recorded against any of them
- **Usually means:** the session has drifted from a directed intervention toward recreation — there's no way to show a referring physician or payer what the music was chosen to achieve.
- **First question:** "What measurable, non-musical goal was this specific song or technique chosen to address?"
- **Data to pull:** session plan, current treatment goals from the referral/care plan.

### RAS tempo has been progressed for more than 3 sessions with no overground gait re-test
- **Usually means:** the charted gain may be entirely cue-dependent — the patient walks well only while the rhythmic cue is playing, and that's being mistaken for functional improvement.
- **First question:** "When was overground cadence last measured with the cue removed, and did the gain hold?"
- **Data to pull:** RAS progression worksheet (see `references/playbook.md` §1), most recent overground gait trial data.

### A client's agitation increases within the first 2–3 minutes of a session opened directly with high-energy or "upbeat" music
- **Usually means:** the iso principle was skipped — the music started at the target state instead of matching the client's actual presenting state first.
- **First question:** "What was the client's presenting affect and tempo at session start, and did the opening music match it?"
- **Data to pull:** iso-principle session opening worksheet (see `references/playbook.md` §2), session notes on client state at minute 0.

### A trauma-history client is moved into unstructured improvisation or GIM-style imagery work with no documented dissociation-risk screen
- **Usually means:** the session risks flooding the client with no pre-agreed grounding technique or safety net, and the therapist may not hold the training level the depth of work requires.
- **First question:** "Was a dissociation-risk screen completed, and does the therapist hold the AMI-recognized training level this technique requires?"
- **Data to pull:** trauma/GIM screening worksheet (see `references/playbook.md` §4), therapist's current GIM training level.

### Progress notes describe the music rather than the client's functional response
- **Usually means:** documentation is drifting toward a session diary instead of goal-referenced clinical charting, which is unlikely to justify continued treatment to a referring provider or payer.
- **First question:** "Read the last three notes — do they state what the client did in relation to the goal, or only what was played?"
- **Data to pull:** recent progress notes, the stated treatment goal they're supposed to reference.

### A dementia client shows unexpected withdrawal or distress after a "personalized playlist" song plays
- **Usually means:** a familiar song can carry an unexamined negative association (a loss, a difficult period) — familiarity was assumed to be equivalent to safety.
- **First question:** "Is there a known association with this specific song, and who confirmed the playlist content — the client, or only a family member's guess?"
- **Data to pull:** playlist source and rationale, session note describing the reaction and its timing relative to the song.

### A medically fragile or NICU session uses recorded music, or documents no monitoring of vital signs/behavioral state during the session
- **Usually means:** live, infant/patient-directed, contingently responsive delivery is the standard for this population precisely because recorded music can't adjust to real-time physiological signals — using it anyway, unmonitored, risks over- or under-stimulation going unnoticed.
- **First question:** "Was the music live and responsive to this patient's state, and what vital-sign or behavioral monitoring was documented during the session?"
- **Data to pull:** session note, monitoring log, unit protocol for music therapy volume/delivery in medically fragile care.

### A non-fluent aphasia client is started on Melodic Intonation Therapy with no documented singing-ability screen
- **Usually means:** MIT candidacy hasn't been confirmed — the client's melodic pathway may not be intact enough to exploit, and sessions may be spent on a technique mismatched to the actual deficit.
- **First question:** "Was a singing-ability screen completed on a simple familiar tune before starting the MIT protocol?"
- **Data to pull:** MIT candidacy worksheet (see `references/playbook.md` §3).

### A group session mixes clients with widely different cognitive or functional levels under one undifferentiated activity plan
- **Usually means:** the activity is likely serving the highest- or lowest-functioning members well while missing the goal for everyone else, and individual goal progress becomes hard to attribute or measure.
- **First question:** "What does each individual client's documented goal require, and does this single group activity actually address all of them?"
- **Data to pull:** individual treatment goals for each group member, the group session plan.

### A client has been held on the same technique and target with no data showing progress for an extended period, with no documented rationale for maintenance-only care
- **Usually means:** the case may need a technique change, a re-baseline, or a discharge/step-down decision that hasn't been made.
- **First question:** "What does the data show over the last several re-baseline points, and is there a documented reason this is maintenance rather than active treatment?"
- **Data to pull:** goal-tracking data across the relevant re-baseline interval, current treatment plan's stated rationale.
