# Red flags

Smell tests a working theatrical/screen makeup artist catches early. Format for each: what it usually means, the first question to ask, and the data to pull before the next application.

## 1. Design approved only under rehearsal-room work light or a phone photo, never under the actual lighting cue or camera test

**Usually means:** the color and contour choices are unconfirmed hypotheses, not decisions — a tone that reads clean under fluorescent work light can vanish under a colored wash or shift under camera white balance.

**First question:** "Has this been seen under the actual lighting cue (or a camera/lighting test), not work light?"

**Data to pull:** the tech rehearsal schedule and lighting plot (or camera/color-temp plan); whether a swatch test under the real cue has happened yet.

## 2. Quick-change timing was only run once, calmly, in a rehearsal room

**Usually means:** real backstage traffic, adrenaline, sweat, and a moving performer will blow a timing that looked fine walked through slowly and alone.

**First question:** "Has this been timed at full tempo, in full costume, in the actual wing?"

**Data to pull:** stopwatch splits from a full-tempo run in the real space; whether the pre-set/pre-fabrication steps were included in that run or done ahead of time in the test but not in the plan.

## 3. A new adhesive or prosthetic material is being used on a performer for the first time on show day or shoot day itself

**Usually means:** untested allergic-reaction risk with zero recovery time if it goes wrong mid-performance or mid-shoot.

**First question:** "When was this patch-tested on this specific performer, and what was the result?"

**Data to pull:** the performer's product/allergy log; the batch/lot number of the product in question versus what was patch-tested.

## 4. Continuity is being tracked "from memory" rather than photographed and logged, on a shoot spanning more than one day per character state

**Usually means:** a scar placement, bruise shade, or blood level will drift by the time a non-adjacent scene is shot weeks later, and the mismatch surfaces in the edit — expensive to fix, sometimes impossible without a reshoot.

**First question:** "Where's the continuity photo and measurement log for this character state?"

**Data to pull:** the continuity photo/log folder for the character and shoot day in question; whether it was filed before the actor left the chair.

## 5. Application time was never quoted to production or written into the call sheet

**Usually means:** the makeup call will get compressed against a fixed camera-ready or curtain time, and something gets rushed — usually adhesive cure time or a skipped patch test.

**First question:** "What time was quoted for this application, and does the call sheet actually reflect it?"

**Data to pull:** the call sheet's makeup call time versus the department's quoted prep time for that day's design.

## 6. A single hero prosthetic piece is in use across the whole run or shoot with no spare

**Usually means:** one tear, one bad night, or one adhesive failure stops the show or forces an unplanned design downgrade with no recovery option.

**First question:** "How many spares exist, and what's the remake lead time if this one fails tonight?"

**Data to pull:** spare inventory count; life-cast mold availability and turnaround time for a remake.

## 7. The makeup budget was copied as a flat number from a prior, different production

**Usually means:** it doesn't reflect this show's actual prosthetic count, quick-change complexity, or cast size, and will run short mid-run when consumables or a remake are needed.

**First question:** "What's the itemized breakdown for this production's specific design — prosthetics, consumables, spares — versus the number we started with?"

**Data to pull:** itemized budget by line item; consumables burn rate to date versus remaining run length.

## 8. Blood or wound effects are reused night after night without checking costume staining or product breakdown

**Usually means:** accumulated staining on costume fabric changes how the effect reads over the run, and product left on fabric can dry or shift color differently than a fresh application, breaking visual consistency across performances.

**First question:** "When was the costume last checked for stain buildup, and is the product behaving the same on night twelve as it did on night one?"

**Data to pull:** costume stain log; product reorder/replacement cadence for the effect in question.

## 9. A performer reports itching or redness at an application site and it's being waved off as "normal for this adhesive"

**Usually means:** an early contact-dermatitis signal — repeat daily exposure across a run turns a mild reaction into a disabling one within days if the product isn't changed.

**First question:** "Is this the same site every night, and did it start after a product or batch change?"

**Data to pull:** the performer's skin-check log; the batch/lot number in current use; the understudy or alternate-adhesive contingency plan.

## 10. A director or DP's note is "make it look real" with no stated viewing distance or camera format

**Usually means:** nobody has actually defined what "real" reads as at the condition that matters — a note like this, taken literally, optimizes for the wrong distance.

**First question:** "Real at what distance, or on what lens — has the house size or coverage plan for this scene been confirmed?"

**Data to pull:** the seating chart/house size for the scene, or the lens and coverage plan if it's a screen production.
