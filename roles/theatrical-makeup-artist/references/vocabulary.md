# Vocabulary

Terms of the trade generalists conflate or use loosely. Each: definition, how a practitioner actually uses it in a sentence, and the common misuse.

## Blocking (in makeup)

The deliberate exaggeration or simplification of facial shadow and shape so a face reads correctly as a *shape* at a given viewing distance, distinct from realistic rendering. Borrowed from Corson's stage-makeup terminology.

**In use:** "At 80 feet the eye socket has to be blocked in solid, not shaded gradually — a soft gradient just disappears from the back row."

**Common misuse:** confusing it with stage *blocking* (actor movement/positioning) — same word, unrelated craft.

## Life cast

A mold, usually alginate followed by a plaster shell, taken directly from a performer's face or body, used to fabricate a prosthetic appliance custom-fit to that individual.

**In use:** "We need a life cast on him before we can build the forehead piece — nothing off-the-shelf will sit right on his brow structure."

**Common misuse:** calling any pre-made or generic appliance "custom" — without a life cast taken from that specific performer, it's a stock piece adjusted at the edges, not a custom fit.

## Encapsulated silicone vs. foam latex

Two different prosthetic appliance material families. Encapsulated silicone appliances are more expensive, reusable many times, and blend translucently at the edge; foam-latex appliances are cheaper, lighter, but degrade faster and blend more opaquely.

**In use:** "For a nine-week run we're going silicone — foam latex won't survive that many wear cycles without the edges breaking down."

**Common misuse:** treating the material choice as purely an aesthetic preference rather than a budget-versus-reuse-count decision made up front.

## Kit fee / box rental

A separate union payment (IATSE) to the artist for supplying and maintaining their own kit of tools and consumables, paid in addition to — and structured differently from — the day rate or wage.

**In use:** "That's covered under my box rental, not the day rate — the kit fee is what pays for the consumables I'm burning through on this run."

**Common misuse:** treating it as a discretionary tip or bonus rather than a contractual line item tied to the artist supplying their own equipment.

## Quick change (track)

A scripted, rehearsed, cue-timed costume-and-makeup transformation executed live during a performance, with a specific crew member or "track" assigned to run exactly that change every night.

**In use:** "Quick change four is the tight one — 85 seconds, same track every show, no room for improvising the steps."

**Common misuse:** using the term loosely for any fast touch-up backstage — a quick change specifically means a rehearsed, cued sequence with a fixed crew assignment, not an ad hoc fix.

## Patch test

A pre-application skin test, typically 24–48 hours ahead, checking a specific product on a specific performer's skin for an allergic or irritant reaction before it's used on a visible application site.

**In use:** "We patch-tested the new adhesive on her forearm Tuesday — clean at 48 hours, cleared for the face Thursday."

**Common misuse:** skipping it because the product has been used before "on someone else" or "on this brand for years" — sensitivity is specific to the individual performer and can also vary between product batches.

## Continuity photo

A reference photograph taken immediately after a makeup state is applied, used to reproduce the exact same placement, wear pattern, and condition on a later, non-sequential shoot day.

**In use:** "Pull up the continuity photo from day 14 — the scar sits a few millimeters higher than where you've got it right now."

**Common misuse:** treating a single general "beauty" reference photo from the callsheet packet as sufficient — continuity requires a photo of that specific applied state, taken that day, not a generic likeness.

## Key makeup artist vs. makeup department head

On larger productions these are distinct roles: the department head sets creative direction, budget, and sourcing across the whole show; the key artist is the on-set lead who personally executes and maintains continuity for principal cast during shooting, and may report to the department head rather than being the same person.

**In use:** "Run that prosthetic change by the department head before you commit budget — the key artist can approve the day-to-day application, but not a material switch mid-shoot."

**Common misuse:** assuming they're interchangeable titles for the same job, which only holds on small productions where one person does both.

## Cross-lighting

A stage lighting approach using multiple angled sources rather than a single flat front wash, which reduces the deep shadow a single front light throws under the brow, nose, and jaw.

**In use:** "This house is heavily cross-lit — dial the contour back, the rig is already doing half the shadow work for us."

**Common misuse:** designing heavy, old-school greasepaint contour on the assumption of flat single-source front light without checking the actual rig, which can leave the design over-blocked and muddy under a cross-lit plot.

## Straight makeup vs. character makeup

Straight makeup enhances a performer's natural features for a role near their own age and type; character makeup deliberately alters age, features, or physical type to build a distinct persona.

**In use:** "She's straight for act one, then we're into full character work once the transformation scene hits."

**Common misuse:** applying character-level correction time and technique to a straight-makeup call, burning budget and schedule the call doesn't need.

## Wear time

How long an applied prosthetic or makeup design holds up — adhesion, color, edge blending — under actual performance conditions (sweat, movement, run length), as distinct from how long it takes to apply.

**In use:** "Application's only 40 minutes, but wear time under those stage lights tops out around three hours before the edges start lifting — that's the number that matters for a long tech day."

**Common misuse:** quoting only application time on a call sheet or schedule and ignoring wear time, which is the actual constraint on long shoot days or double-show days.
