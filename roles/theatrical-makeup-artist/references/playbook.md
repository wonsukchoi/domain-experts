# Playbook

Filled workflows a working theatrical/screen makeup artist actually runs, with real structures, time budgets, and thresholds — starting points to adapt, not scripts.

## 1. Character design brief (filled example)

Built from the script/character breakdown meeting, before any product touches a face.

```
PRODUCTION: "Hollow Crown" (regional theatre, 650-seat proscienum house)
CHARACTER: Duchess Marguerite, Act II — poisoning scene, visible decline
MEDIUM: Stage. Farthest seat: ~78 ft. Lighting: tungsten front wash + one
  upstage blue special for the death moment.
STORY REQUIREMENT: Skin tone shifts from healthy to visibly grey/ashen
  over 3 consecutive scenes across one act (no costume change to hide it).
VIEWING TEST PLANNED: Tech rehearsal 6/2, cue LX 31 (blue special) —
  base tone must be confirmed under this cue, not work light.
APPLICATION WINDOW: 20 min pre-show for scene 1 state; two on-stage-visible
  progressions built into blocking (no backstage change available for
  scenes 2–3 — must progress via visible business, e.g. actor's own hand
  makeup removal bit written into staging).
CONTINUITY NEED: None (single performance arc within one show, not
  multi-day) — but the 3-state progression must be rehearsed and timed
  identically every performance for the run.
MATERIALS: RCMA base + grey/lavender contour for ashen shift; tested
  swatch options: warm-grey vs. cool-lavender-grey (cool wins under LX 31,
  warm reads muddy-brown under the blue special).
SIGN-OFF: Director + LD present at 6/2 tech test before locking design.
```

## 2. Stage-distance / lighting calibration

**Exaggeration guide by house size** (Corson & Glavan's ratio, adapted):

| Farthest seat | Contour exaggeration vs. natural | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Under 15 ft (film/TV close, black-box front row) | 1x — near-invisible technique | Camera or eye distance reads every edge; blend lines must be perfect. |
| 15–50 ft | 1.25–1.5x | Small proscenium houses, most regional theatre thrust stages. |
| 50–150 ft | 2–3x | Standard Broadway/large proscenium house; this is the default assumption unless told otherwise. |
| 150+ ft (arena, large amphitheater) | 3x+, plus prosthetic build-up for silhouette | Flat paint alone stops working; shape has to change, not just shade. |

**Color-under-gel test procedure** (run at first available tech/lighting session, not skipped for schedule pressure):

1. Apply the proposed base and 2–3 alternate tone options in visible swatches on the performer (or a stand-in with matched skin tone) before the cue is locked.
2. View under every lighting state the character actually appears in — not just the "hero" cue. A tone that reads correctly under a warm front wash can disappear under a cold special seconds later in the same scene.
3. Photograph each swatch under each state for the continuity/reference file, even on single-performance-arc shows — the photo becomes the standard the makeup crew matches to on every performance night.
4. Lock the winning tone in writing (palette name/number) before dress rehearsal. A tone chosen "by feel" backstage on opening night is a tone that will drift over the run.

## 3. Prosthetic and continuity workflow

**Time budgets** [stated heuristics — confirm against the specific material and performer at the fitting]:

| Task | Typical duration |
|---|---|
| Life cast (alginate/plaster impression) | 45–90 min, one-time per performer |
| Full facial prosthetic application (multi-piece) | 2–3 hr |
| Single wound/scar appliance application | 30–45 min |
| Full facial prosthetic removal | 30–45 min |
| Single appliance removal | 10–15 min |
| Daily touch-up (existing appliance, re-set) | 15–20 min |

**Continuity log — per performer, per shoot day, filled example:**

```
CHARACTER: Det. Reyes (aging makeup, present-day timeline)
SHOOT DAY: 14 of 42 (scenes 22, 24, 31 — non-sequential story order)
APPLIED STATE: "Present-day" aging level 2 of 3
  — forehead appliance: batch #A114, applied 6:40am, life-cast mold R-2
  — grey temple streaks: 30% coverage, matches ref photo Day 3
  — wear time so far: 3.5 hrs at last check (10:10am), edges holding
PHOTOS: 3 taken (front, 3/4 L, 3/4 R) at 6:55am, filed
  continuity/reyes/day14/
NEXT MATCHING DAY: Day 31 (scenes 22 continuation) — this log is the
  reference; do not re-derive placement from memory or from the script.
```

**Rule:** any character state that will be revisited on a non-adjacent shoot day gets this log filled before the actor leaves the chair, not at end of day from memory.

## 4. Quick-change track template

```
QUICK CHANGE #__: "<transition name>" — <wing/location> — Track Time: __ sec
  (Cue: <lighting/sound cue in> → <cue out>)

PRE-SET (during Sc. __, __ min prior):
  <what's pre-fabricated, pre-glued, pre-mixed, and staged>

0:00–0:__  <step, action, tool>
0:__–0:__  <step, action, tool>
0:__–0:__  <step, action, tool>
...
GO.
```

**Design rule:** the total of the listed steps must equal the cue window exactly — if it doesn't reconcile on paper, it won't reconcile backstage. Any change requiring more than ~45 seconds of hands-on work should be pre-fabricated (pre-glued, pre-mixed) during an earlier, unhurried scene rather than attempted cold. Rehearse every quick change at full tempo, in full costume, in the actual wing space, at least twice before the first performance it has to run in.

## 5. Patch test and skin-safety protocol

1. **24–48 hours before first use of a new product or adhesive on a specific performer**, apply a small amount (dime-sized) to the inner forearm or behind the ear; observe for redness, itching, or swelling.
2. **Document the result** in the performer's product/allergy log, including product name and batch/lot number — a clean patch test on one batch doesn't guarantee the next batch.
3. **On a run (8 shows/week or a multi-week shoot), check the application site daily** for any change from baseline — redness that wasn't there on show three but appears by show nine is cumulative sensitization, not a one-off irritation, and needs an adhesive change before it worsens.
4. **Escalation path:** any reported itching, burning, or visible redness gets reported to stage management/production same-day, with the specific product and batch flagged, and an alternate adhesive or removal plan ready before the next application — not "monitor and see."

## 6. Kit and budget planning (filled example, mid-size regional production)

```
PRODUCTION: 6-week run, cast of 14, 2 principal aging/prosthetic tracks
BUDGET LINE ITEMS:
  Consumables (base, powder, adhesive, remover, sponges) ... $1,800
  Prosthetic materials (2 life casts + appliance runs)    ... $3,200
  Spare appliance stock (min. 1 spare per hero piece)      ...   included above
  Kit/box rental fee (artist-supplied tools, per union
    contract, separate from day rate)                      ... per-contract rate
  Contingency (reorders, batch-specific patch-test
    failures requiring a product switch)                   ... 10% of above
TOTAL BASE BUDGET: ~$5,500 + contingency + kit fee + day rates
```

**Rule:** never run a single hero prosthetic piece across an entire run without at least one spare in reserve and a known remake lead time — a torn piece with no spare and a multi-day mold lead time stops the show or forces a design downgrade on no notice.
