# Vocabulary

### Diminution (wastage)
The reduction in a structural member's thickness from its as-built (new) value, expressed as a percentage of the as-built thickness, used to compare against a classification society's allowable wastage for that specific member.
**In use:** "Average diminution on this strake is 17.4% against a 22.5% allowable — passes on average, but that doesn't clear the local reading."
**Common misuse:** Applying one flat wastage percentage across every structural member on the hull, when the allowable diminution is set per member based on that member's scantling and loading, not as a single hull-wide figure.

### Rule minimum thickness
The absolute minimum thickness a structural member may be measured at before it requires renewal, per the classification society's rules for that vessel's approved scantlings — distinct from the percentage-based diminution figure.
**In use:** "That point read 11.9 mm against a 12.4 mm rule minimum — it fails on the absolute number regardless of what the plate's average diminution shows."
**Common misuse:** Treating the percentage allowance as the only test and ignoring that a single reading below the absolute rule minimum is itself a finding, even when the plate's average diminution is well within allowance.

### Ultrasonic thickness gauging (UTG)
Non-destructive measurement of a structural member's remaining thickness using an ultrasonic pulse-echo instrument, calibrated against a known-thickness block before use.
**In use:** "We're gauging strake B on the 9-point extended grid before sizing the insert plate, not just re-reading the one low point."
**Common misuse:** Reporting a plate's condition from a sparse spot-check pattern and extrapolating a repair area from it, rather than following the classification society's specified gauging density for the vessel's age and structural category.

### Special Survey
The classification society's most extensive periodical hull and machinery survey, conducted approximately every five years, that re-verifies a vessel's continued compliance with class rules through close-up inspection and thickness gauging.
**In use:** "Special Survey No. 3 is due within the current window but hasn't been completed — there's no independent structural verification on this hull cycle yet."
**Common misuse:** Treating a Special Survey that is "in progress" or under an approved extension as equivalent to one that's completed; an open or extended survey means the vessel's structural condition hasn't been re-verified this cycle, whatever the certificate's face date shows.

### Condition of class / recommendation
A classification society's formal notation that a specific deficiency must be corrected within a stated deadline for the vessel to retain its class notation, issued without immediately suspending the certificate.
**In use:** "There's an open recommendation on the emergency generator with a 90-day compliance window — that's material to the insurability question even though the certificate itself still shows current."
**Common misuse:** Reading "certificate current" as "no open findings"; recommendations and conditions of class exist precisely because a deficiency doesn't warrant immediate certificate suspension, so they don't show on the certificate face at all.

### Enhanced Survey Programme (ESP)
The IACS-mandated survey and gauging regime for bulk carriers and oil tankers that scales inspection extent and thickness-measurement density to the vessel's age and prior survey findings.
**In use:** "At her third Special Survey, ESP calls for a denser gauging pattern across more ballast tanks than she needed at her first."
**Common misuse:** Applying the same fixed gauging pattern regardless of vessel age; ESP density is explicitly age- and history-dependent, not a static checklist.

### Load line (freeboard mark)
The set of statutory marks on a vessel's hull, assigned under the International Convention on Load Lines, indicating the maximum permissible draft for different water types, zones, and seasons.
**In use:** "She's submerged past the Summer mark for this zone and date — that's a Load Line Convention non-conformity, independent of what the hull steel condition shows."
**Common misuse:** Treating freeboard/load-line compliance as a structural-condition question rather than a separate statutory loading limit that applies regardless of the hull's actual physical condition.

### Pitting corrosion
Localized, isolated metal loss forming discrete pits rather than uniform thinning across a surface, assessed by extent and depth relative to remaining thickness rather than by a plate-wide average.
**In use:** "That's pitting, not general wastage — the surrounding plate reads fine, so we grid out the pitted area before sizing the repair, not the whole strake."
**Common misuse:** Averaging pit readings together with sound-plate readings into a single mean thickness, which dilutes the pitted area's severity into a number that looks acceptable.

### Condition survey (P&I)
An inspection commissioned by a Protection & Indemnity club to assess a vessel's insurability and loss-prevention risk before or during entry, distinct in scope from a class survey or a pre-purchase survey.
**In use:** "The P&I condition survey flagged the lifeboat davit corrosion as a loss-prevention item requiring correction before renewal, separate from anything class has open."
**Common misuse:** Scoping a P&I condition survey identically to a class special survey or a pre-purchase structural valuation; the club's question is insurability risk, not exhaustive structural condition or sale price.

### Casualty (damage) survey
An inspection conducted after a specific incident — grounding, collision, cargo damage, machinery failure — to establish the extent and cause of damage for an insurance claim or liability determination.
**In use:** "The casualty survey traced the shell plating deformation to the grounding contact point before the insurer's adjuster would accept the repair estimate."
**Common misuse:** Stating a cause from the damage's visual appearance alone rather than tracing a documented sequence of physical evidence; an unsupported causation finding is the first thing a counterparty's surveyor will contest in a disputed claim.
