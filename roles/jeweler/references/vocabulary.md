# Vocabulary

### Karat (purity)
A measure of gold purity expressed as parts out of 24 — 18k gold is 18/24, or 75% pure gold.
**In use:** "This is 18k — that's 75% gold, 25% alloy metals determining the color and hardness."
**Common misuse:** Treating the karat number as a direct percentage without converting, assuming "18k" and "18%" mean the same thing.

### Casting shrinkage (precious metal)
The dimensional reduction a cast metal piece undergoes as it cools from molten to solid, requiring the wax pattern to be built oversized by the alloy-specific rate.
**In use:** "14k yellow gold shrinks about 1.8% — build the wax to that, not the finished target dimension."
**Common misuse:** Assuming casting doesn't meaningfully change a piece's finished dimensions, or applying a generic shrink rate regardless of the specific karat and alloy.

### Prong / bezel setting
Metal elements holding a gemstone in place, whose actual metal thickness at the retention points — not visual tightness — determines security.
**In use:** "Prongs look tight, but check the thickness at the tip with a loupe before calling it secure."
**Common misuse:** Judging a setting secure by how tight it looks rather than checking actual metal thickness with a loupe and tap test.

### Metal reconciliation
The mass-balance process of weighing all metal inputs (stock, solder, findings) against the finished piece plus recovered scrap to account for the full amount used.
**In use:** "Reconciliation's off by more than the usual tolerance — check the sweeps before assuming it's normal loss."
**Common misuse:** Skipping reconciliation and assuming metal loss is "just normal shop wear" without actually weighing and checking it against a stated tolerance.

### Mohs hardness
A relative scale of mineral hardness used to judge a gemstone's resistance to scratching and abrasion.
**In use:** "Opal's only a 5.5–6.5 on Mohs — handle it very differently than a diamond at the top of the scale."
**Common misuse:** Assuming all gemstones are roughly as durable as diamond (10 on the scale), when many common stones — opal, emerald, pearl — sit much lower and need different handling.

### Cleavage (gemstone)
A plane along which a mineral tends to split cleanly under stress, distinct from general hardness.
**In use:** "It's hard, but it's got a cleavage plane — a sharp knock in the wrong direction will still split it."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a hard stone is automatically impact-resistant, when a stone can be scratch-resistant yet still have a cleavage plane that makes it prone to chipping or splitting from a sharp blow.

### Acid test / electronic karat test
Methods for verifying actual gold purity rather than relying on a stamped hallmark.
**In use:** "Ran the electronic test on this old piece — the solder repairs have dropped the actual purity below the stamp."
**Common misuse:** Trusting a stamped karat mark on an older, resized, or repaired piece without verifying it, since repairs and solder can alter the actual alloy composition at specific points on the piece.

### Tap test
A technique of gently tapping a set stone, often with a specific tool, and observing for movement or sound indicating a loose setting.
**In use:** "Tap test picked up a rattle the visual check missed entirely — that prong needs tightening before it goes back to the customer."
**Common misuse:** Relying on visual inspection alone to judge setting security, skipping the tap test that can reveal looseness invisible to the eye.

### Findings
Small manufactured components — clasps, jump rings, earring posts — used in assembling jewelry, distinct from the main fabricated or cast piece.
**In use:** "Don't forget to weigh the findings separately — they add up in the reconciliation."
**Common misuse:** Assuming findings are negligible in metal reconciliation weight, when their cumulative weight matters for an accurate mass balance.

### Girdle (gemstone)
The widest part of a cut gemstone, where the crown meets the pavilion, and the section a setting's metal must properly retain.
**In use:** "Check metal retention at the girdle specifically, not just how the prongs look at the crown."
**Common misuse:** Focusing setting security checks only on the visible prongs or crown area, without verifying adequate metal retention specifically at the girdle.
