# Vocabulary

### Fit-up
The process of positioning and aligning parts before welding, checked against tolerance for root gap, alignment, and squareness.
**In use:** "Fit-up's not signed off yet — root gap on joint 4 is still outside WPS tolerance."
**Common misuse:** Treating fit-up as simply "holding pieces together," rather than a dimensionally verified step with its own explicit pass/fail criteria before welding proceeds.

### Root gap
The specified gap between two members at a weld joint before welding, called out by the WPS.
**In use:** "WPS calls for a 1/8" root gap here — this is sitting at 3/16", that's outside tolerance."
**Common misuse:** Setting the root gap by eye or feel rather than measuring it against the WPS's actual specified tolerance for that specific joint.

### Tack weld
A small weld used to hold fit-up in position before the final weld pass is made.
**In use:** "That tack cracked overnight — pull it out and redo it before we tack the rest of this joint."
**Common misuse:** Assuming tack welds are inherently lower-quality or exempt from inspection since they'll be "consumed" by the final pass — a defective tack at a high-restraint joint can persist as a crack rather than being fully absorbed.

### Camber / sweep
Mill-induced curvature in a structural member along its length — camber typically vertical, sweep typically horizontal.
**In use:** "This beam's got about 1/4" of camber over its length — orient it up before fit-up, don't fight it flat under the clamps."
**Common misuse:** Assuming incoming structural steel is straight by default, rather than checking for and deliberately orienting any known curvature during layout.

### Racking check (diagonal measurement)
Comparing the two diagonal measurements of a rectangular or square assembly to verify it's actually square.
**In use:** "Diagonals are off by 3/8" on this frame — it's out of square even though every member length checks out."
**Common misuse:** Relying on individual member lengths matching the drawing as proof of squareness, without an actual diagonal comparison to catch racking.

### Cumulative tolerance / tolerance stack-up
The compounding of individually-in-tolerance small errors across multiple pieces into a larger deviation at the far end of an assembly.
**In use:** "Each segment's fine on its own, but stack five of them and the worst case exceeds the overall assembly tolerance."
**Common misuse:** Verifying only piece-to-piece or per-segment tolerance and assuming the whole assembly is therefore within spec, without checking the cumulative worst case.

### Joint preparation
The specific bevel angle, root face, and root gap machined or cut into a joint before welding, as specified by the WPS.
**In use:** "Joint prep on this connection calls for a 30° bevel with a 1/16" root face — check it against the WPS before cutting."
**Common misuse:** Preparing a joint based on general fabrication habit rather than the specific WPS the downstream weld will actually use.

### Shop drawing
A fabricator-produced detailed drawing derived from the engineering design drawings, used to guide actual cutting and fit-up.
**In use:** "Cut list comes from the shop drawing, not the architectural set — those aren't the same document."
**Common misuse:** Confusing shop drawings with the engineering design drawings themselves; shop drawings are a separate, fabrication-specific translation that carries its own revision control.

### High-restraint joint
A connection where the surrounding structure resists the joint's natural contraction during welding, increasing cracking risk.
**In use:** "That's a high-restraint joint — hold tacks to the same quality standard as the final weld there."
**Common misuse:** Applying the same tack-weld quality assumptions to a high-restraint joint as to a low-restraint one, ignoring that restraint level materially changes actual cracking risk.

### Mismatch (weld joint alignment)
The offset between two members' surfaces at a joint, distinct from root gap, which specifically measures the space between them.
**In use:** "Root gap's fine, but there's 1/16" of mismatch across the plate — that needs correcting before we tack."
**Common misuse:** Conflating mismatch and root gap as the same measurement, when one measures surface offset and the other measures the separation between the two members.
