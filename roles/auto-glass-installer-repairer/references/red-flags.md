# Red flags

Smell tests a senior installer catches on the phone quote, at inspection, or in the first minutes of a job. Format for each: what it usually means, the first question to ask, and the data to pull before committing to a repair path.

## 1. Chip repair quoted by phone before on-site inspection

**Usually means:** the quote is based on the customer's verbal description, which routinely understates crack length and can't assess edge proximity or depth at all — a phone quote is a placeholder, not a repair plan.

**First question:** "Has anyone actually measured this crack's length, its distance from the edge, and whether it's inside the driver's direct forward view?"

**Data to pull:** the original phone description against the tech's on-site measurement; whether the damage has grown since the customer first noticed it.

## 2. Crack length approaching or past roughly 6 inches

**Usually means:** the damage has crossed from a resin-repairable break into structural crack territory — temperature swings and road flex propagate cracks faster than customers expect between "I noticed it" and "I called."

**First question:** "What's the measured length right now, not what it looked like when the customer first saw it?"

**Data to pull:** a current length measurement against the shop's repair-disqualifier threshold; whether the crack has visibly grown since intake.

## 3. Damage within roughly 2 inches of the glass edge

**Usually means:** the crack sits in the highest-stress zone of the pane, where resin fill doesn't restore the structural load path — this fails independently of crack length or location.

**First question:** "How far is the nearest point of this damage from any edge, measured, not eyeballed?"

**Data to pull:** edge-distance measurement; whether the crack is actively propagating toward the edge even if not yet touching it.

## 4. Damage inside the driver's direct forward-vision area

**Usually means:** even a technically repairable chip or short crack will leave optical distortion directly in the driver's critical sightline after resin fill — this location alone overrides an otherwise-qualifying size.

**First question:** "Does this sit in the driver's direct forward view with the seat in a normal position, and would a resin fill leave visible distortion there?"

**Data to pull:** damage position relative to the driver's seated eye line; whether the customer has been told repair there is cosmetic-risk even if structurally adequate.

## 5. SDAT quoted from a "typical" or remembered number instead of the product's chart for today's conditions

**Usually means:** the technician is using the warm-shop reference condition instead of pulling the actual temperature/humidity-specific figure — this is the most common way a customer ends up driving on an uncured bond.

**First question:** "What are the actual temperature and humidity for this job, and what does the adhesive's data sheet say the SDAT is at those specific numbers?"

**Data to pull:** the adhesive product's SDAT table; today's on-site temperature and humidity reading, not a shop average.

## 6. Customer told they can drive "as soon as it's done" or "in a few minutes"

**Usually means:** SDAT hasn't been calculated for actual conditions, or is being rounded down to avoid an uncomfortable wait-time conversation with the customer.

**First question:** "What time was the bead completed, and what's the calculated release time from that adhesive's SDAT at today's conditions?"

**Data to pull:** bead-completion timestamp; the SDAT figure used to set the release time.

## 7. Windshield replaced on a vehicle with a forward-facing camera and no calibration line on the work order

**Usually means:** the calibration requirement was missed rather than confirmed unnecessary — a camera's optical reference plane moves with the glass it's mounted to, regardless of whether the bracket itself was touched.

**First question:** "Does this vehicle's OEM procedure require calibration after windshield replacement, and has that been checked for this exact trim, or just assumed from a similar job?"

**Data to pull:** the OEM calibration requirement for the specific model/trim/build; whether a calibration confirmation report exists on file.

## 8. Vehicle released with no calibration confirmation report despite calibration-triggering work

**Usually means:** the calibration was attempted but its result was never verified — some calibration attempts fail or partially complete without an obvious symptom, and the confirmation report is the only artifact that proves success.

**First question:** "Where's the calibration tool's pass/fail printout, not just a note that calibration was performed?"

**Data to pull:** the calibration system's confirmation report; target-placement measurements for static calibration or logged conditions for the dynamic drive.

## 9. Pinch weld shows a scratch or bare metal after cut-out with no primer applied

**Usually means:** the cut-out technique gouged the flange and the step of priming exposed metal was skipped — this becomes a corrosion site sealed under the new bead, invisible until the next replacement.

**First question:** "Was the cut-out surface inspected for e-coat damage before the new bead went down, and was any bare metal primed?"

**Data to pull:** photos of the pinch weld post-cut-out; whether pinch-weld primer was used and its flash time observed.

## 10. Static calibration attempted in a bay that isn't level or under inconsistent lighting

**Usually means:** the calibration target setup doesn't meet the OEM's stated tolerance, which can produce a calibration that "passes" on the tool but leaves the camera mis-aimed in real-world use.

**First question:** "Has the bay's level and the target's distance/height been checked against this specific OEM's spec today, not assumed from the last calibration run in this bay?"

**Data to pull:** bay level reading; target distance and height measurements; lighting conditions at time of calibration.

## 11. Third or later resin repair proposed on the same windshield

**Usually means:** the pane has accumulated enough repair points that another repair concentrates risk rather than genuinely fixing new damage — this is a replacement trigger on its own, independent of the new damage's individual size.

**First question:** "How many prior repairs does this windshield already have, and where are they relative to the new damage?"

**Data to pull:** repair history on this vehicle/glass if available; positions of prior repairs relative to the current damage.

## 12. Aftermarket glass ordered for a vehicle with HUD, acoustic interlayer, or embedded antenna/heating features

**Usually means:** the replacement part was selected on price or availability without confirming feature-code match — a mismatched pane can distort a HUD image, increase cabin noise, or leave an antenna or defroster grid nonfunctional.

**First question:** "Does the replacement glass's feature code match every feature on the original — HUD, acoustic layer, antenna, heating grid — not just windshield-camera compatibility?"

**Data to pull:** the NAGS feature code for the original glass; the feature code of the part actually ordered.
