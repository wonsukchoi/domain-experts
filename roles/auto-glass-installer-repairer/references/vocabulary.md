# Vocabulary

Terms generalists (and front-desk schedulers) conflate that practitioners keep sharply separate. Each: definition, how a practitioner actually uses it in a sentence, and the common misuse.

## Safe drive-away time (SDAT)

The manufacturer-specified minimum time after adhesive application before the vehicle can be safely driven, determined by the specific urethane product and the temperature/humidity at the time of the job — not a fixed number per vehicle or shop.

*Practitioner usage:* "SDAT at today's conditions is 6 hours, not the 1-hour number on the warm-shop chart — the car doesn't leave before 3:15."

*Common misuse:* treating SDAT as a fixed shop policy (e.g., "we always say an hour") instead of looking up the actual product's cure time for the actual conditions on that job.

## AS-1 / AS-2 / AS-3 glazing markings

Federal safety-glazing designations (ANSI Z26.1) etched into the glass: **AS-1** is used in any area requiring maximum light transmission, including the windshield and any area the driver must see through in normal operation; **AS-2** permits reduced light transmission outside the driver's critical vision area; **AS-3** is the most heavily tinted, restricted to areas behind the driver.

*Practitioner usage:* "That upper crack crosses into AS-1 territory — even a technically small repair there leaves distortion exactly where the driver's looking."

*Common misuse:* treating "driver's direct forward-vision area" as a vague or negotiable zone rather than the specific, glass-marked AS-1 region the repair-vs-replace decision is measured against.

## Cut-out / wire-out vs. cold-knife method

**Wire-out** (cheese-wire) draws a wire loop through the adhesive bead to separate glass from the pinch weld; **cold-knife** cuts the bead with a hooked blade. Both are cut-out techniques with different risk profiles for gouging the flange depending on access and bead condition.

*Practitioner usage:* "Tight cowl clearance on this one — wire-out's cleaner here than trying to work a cold knife blind."

*Common misuse:* treating the two methods as interchangeable regardless of vehicle geometry, when one routinely gouges the pinch weld on certain body styles and the other doesn't.

## Pinch weld

The flanged, welded seam of sheet metal around the window opening that the adhesive bead bonds to — its e-coat (corrosion-resistant primer layer applied at the factory) is what the cut-out technique is protecting.

*Practitioner usage:* "Pinch weld's clean on the driver's side, but there's a nick by the A-pillar — that gets primed before we bead over it."

*Common misuse:* assuming a scratched pinch weld is a cosmetic non-issue since it's hidden under the new adhesive; unprimed bare metal under a bond line is where corrosion starts, not where it's prevented.

## Bead height / bead profile

The height and cross-sectional shape of the urethane bead applied to the pinch weld or glass edge before setting the glass — too low risks voids and incomplete bonding; too high risks squeeze-out that contaminates trim or blocks proper glass seating.

*Practitioner usage:* "Bead's running a little flat on this pass — building it back up before we set the glass, not compensating with extra pressure at install."

*Common misuse:* judging bead adequacy by how the finished job looks from outside rather than by verifying height and continuity during application, when voids are only visible before the glass goes on.

## Static calibration vs. dynamic calibration (glass-specific)

**Static calibration** aims the windshield-mounted camera against fixed OEM targets in a controlled, level bay at a specified distance and height. **Dynamic calibration** requires driving at a specified speed with visible lane markings while the system self-calibrates against real road data. Many vehicles require both, in a specific order, after windshield replacement.

*Practitioner usage:* "This RAV4 needs static first to rough-aim the camera against the new glass's mounting plane, then a dynamic drive to confirm it — can't skip to the drive."

*Common misuse:* assuming a test drive alone satisfies a calibration requirement that specifies static first, or treating a passed dynamic calibration as proof the static setup was unnecessary.

## Primer flash time

The manufacturer-specified wait time after applying glass or pinch-weld primer before the adhesive bead can be applied — separate from and typically much shorter than SDAT.

*Practitioner usage:* "Primer's only been on two minutes — give it its full flash time before we bead, that's not the SDAT clock, it's a different one."

*Common misuse:* conflating primer flash time with SDAT, or skipping the flash-time wait because "the bead's going on right after anyway."

## Moisture-cure urethane vs. dual-component/fast-cure urethane

**Moisture-cure** (single-component) urethanes cure by absorbing ambient humidity, making cure time highly sensitive to temperature and humidity. **Dual-component (fast-cure)** systems mix two parts that react chemically regardless of ambient humidity, typically producing a much shorter, more weather-independent SDAT at higher material cost.

*Practitioner usage:* "Cold, dry morning like this — if the schedule can't absorb a 6-hour SDAT, switch to the fast-cure system for this one."

*Common misuse:* assuming all urethane adhesives cure on the same timeline regardless of product type, and applying one product's SDAT chart to a different adhesive system.

## Long crack vs. chip / bullseye / star break / combination break

Distinct damage types with different repair prospects: a **chip** is a small surface impact point; a **bullseye** is a circular impact break with a dark center; a **star break** radiates cracks from the impact point; a **combination break** mixes multiple types from one impact; a **long crack** extends beyond a single impact zone and is size-gated for repair eligibility independent of the others.

*Practitioner usage:* "That's a combination break, not a simple bullseye — resin fill won't reach every leg of it cleanly, and it's already close to my repair-size limit."

*Common misuse:* calling every type of glass damage "a chip" or "a crack" interchangeably, which hides which specific repair-disqualifier criteria actually apply.

## Calibration confirmation report

The pass/fail printout or digital record generated by the calibration tool after a static or dynamic calibration run — the only artifact that proves the camera is correctly aimed, as distinct from a technician's visual or verbal confirmation.

*Practitioner usage:* "Confirmation report's attached to the work order — that's what goes in the file, not just 'ran the calibration.'"

*Common misuse:* treating the calibration process running to completion as equivalent to it passing; some calibration attempts fail or partially complete without an obvious on-screen symptom.

## NAGS number

The part identifier from the National Auto Glass Specifications database (published by Mitchell) used industry-wide to reference a specific piece of glass, including feature codes for options like acoustic interlayer, HUD compatibility, or embedded antenna/heating elements.

*Practitioner usage:* "Pull the NAGS number off the old glass before ordering — feature codes have to match, not just the base windshield part."

*Common misuse:* ordering replacement glass by vehicle year/make/model alone without matching the NAGS feature code, which is how HUD- or acoustic-layer-equipped vehicles end up with a mismatched pane.

## Acoustic interlayer / laminated glass

**Laminated glass** (used for windshields) sandwiches a polyvinyl butyral (PVB) interlayer between two glass layers, which is what allows resin repair (only the outer layer is breached in most chips) and what the airbag deploys against. An **acoustic interlayer** is a modified PVB layer that reduces cabin noise transmission — a feature that must be matched on replacement, not just glass thickness.

*Practitioner usage:* "Damage that reaches the interlayer isn't repairable — that's not outer-layer pitting anymore, that's the layer the whole laminate depends on."

*Common misuse:* treating "laminated" as a single undifferentiated spec, missing that acoustic and non-acoustic interlayers are different parts with different NAGS codes and noticeably different cabin noise if mismatched.

## Structural adhesive vs. non-structural sealant

A **structural adhesive** (crash-rated urethane) is engineered and tested to the retention loads required by FMVSS 212/216/208; a **non-structural sealant** may look similar and seal against water intrusion but isn't rated to hold the glass in a crash or support airbag deployment.

*Practitioner usage:* "This is an airbag-equipped vehicle — structural urethane only, there's no sealant substitute regardless of what's faster to source today."

*Common misuse:* treating any urethane-like sealant as an acceptable substitute when the correct structural product isn't immediately in stock, rather than delaying the job for the right material.
