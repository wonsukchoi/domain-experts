# Vocabulary

Terms generalists flatten together that a guard or flagger keeps sharply separate — because the distinction is exactly what a safety decision hinges on.

## Posted speed vs. operating speed

**Posted speed** is the number on the sign. **Operating speed** is what vehicles actually travel — typically approximated by the 85th-percentile speed of free-flowing traffic, and it is frequently higher than posted, sometimes by 10 mph or more on a rural straightaway.

*In use:* "The sign says 45, but our radar check came back 52 at the 85th percentile — the taper's computed off 52, not 45."

*Common misuse:* using posted speed for the taper or positioning formula because it's the number available, then being surprised when drivers who are technically speeding still blow through a taper that assumed compliant behavior.

## Paddle vs. flag

The **STOP/SLOW paddle** is the standard, MUTCD-specified device: octagonal, minimum 18 in., 6 in. letters, red/white STOP and orange/black SLOW faces. A **flag** is a fallback device used only where a paddle genuinely can't be deployed (e.g., certain pilot-car or emergency situations) — it conveys less precise information and is not the default choice.

*In use:* "We don't flag this closure — paddle only, flag's for the pilot-car handoff at the far end."

*Common misuse:* treating the flag as interchangeable with the paddle because "it's what's in the truck," when the paddle's binary STOP/SLOW face is doing real communication work the flag can't replicate.

## Taper vs. shift vs. closure

A **taper** is the tapered transition that merges traffic out of a closed lane. A **shift** moves traffic laterally without eliminating a lane. A **closure** is the full activity area where a lane or shoulder is unavailable, of which the taper is only the transition into it. The taper length formula (L = WS or WS²/60) applies to the taper specifically, not to the whole closure length.

*In use:* "Taper's 600 feet, but the total closure with the activity area and downstream taper runs closer to a quarter mile — don't quote the taper number when someone asks how long the road's actually blocked."

*Common misuse:* using "taper" and "closure" interchangeably when communicating with drivers or supervisors, which understates how much road is actually affected.

## Advance warning area / transition area / activity area / termination area

The four zones of a temporary traffic control setup, in order of approach: **advance warning** (signs alerting drivers before anything changes), **transition** (the taper itself, where lanes merge or shift), **activity area** (the actual work space, including the buffer and work space), and **termination area** (where traffic returns to normal, marked by an end-of-work-zone sign). Each zone has its own device and spacing requirements.

*In use:* "Flagger goes at the start of the transition area, not inside the activity area — that's too late for the stop decision to matter."

*Common misuse:* treating the whole setup as one undifferentiated "work zone" and placing signs or the flagger station without matching them to the specific zone's function.

## Gap acceptance vs. gap availability

**Gap availability** is how many usable gaps in traffic actually occur per unit time — a property of the traffic stream. **Gap acceptance** is whether pedestrians actually judge and use those gaps successfully — a property of the pedestrians, including how well children in particular judge closing speed and distance. A site can have available gaps that a specific pedestrian population still can't safely use.

*In use:* "There are gaps out there, but this is a K–2 crossing — gap availability isn't the same as this age group's gap acceptance, which is why the guard-assignment threshold is conservative."

*Common misuse:* citing raw traffic-gap counts as proof a crossing is safe without accounting for who's actually crossing.

## High-visibility apparel Class 2 vs. Class 3

Both are ANSI/ISEA 107-2004 performance classes for high-visibility apparel, differing in the amount of background and retroreflective material required. **Class 2** is the daytime minimum for most roadway work below 45 mph. **Class 3** adds more coverage and is required at 45 mph and above, or whenever daytime turns into dusk, dawn, or otherwise reduced-visibility conditions, regardless of speed.

*In use:* "Shift runs past sunset — swap to Class 3 even though the morning assignment was fine in Class 2."

*Common misuse:* treating apparel class as a one-time issuance rather than something re-matched to each site's speed and each shift's actual lighting window.

## Stopping sight distance vs. flagger/guard station distance

**Stopping sight distance** is the distance a vehicle at a given speed needs to see an obstacle and come to a full stop (an AASHTO roadway-design concept). **Station distance** is where the flagger or guard is actually positioned relative to the point vehicles must stop. Station distance is only safe if it's placed with enough margin ahead of the stopping point that stopping sight distance is satisfied given the site's real sightlines — a station can be "correctly" measured off a formula and still be unsafe if a curve or crest eats into the available sightline.

*In use:* "The formula gives us 425 feet, but the curve past the crest only gives drivers 300 feet of actual sightline — move the advance sign back, the flagger position doesn't fix a sightline problem."

*Common misuse:* treating the calculated distance as sufficient on its own without walking the actual sightline to confirm it's achievable at this specific site.

## Positive guidance

The principle that a driver should never have to guess what's expected — every device, sign, and signal should tell them clearly and consistently what to do next, with no ambiguous or conflicting cues. It's the underlying reason the device (not the person) carries the authority.

*In use:* "Two flaggers giving inconsistent signals at the same closure violates positive guidance — a driver seeing 'slow' from one and 'stop' from the other has no correct action."

*Common misuse:* treating positive guidance as a vague aspiration ("communicate clearly") rather than the specific engineering requirement that every device in view of a driver agrees with every other device in view.

## Struck-by vs. caught-between

Two distinct OSHA injury mechanism categories relevant to this work. **Struck-by** is contact with a moving vehicle — the dominant fatal-injury mechanism for flaggers and guards. **Caught-between** is being pinned between a vehicle and a fixed object (a barrier, another vehicle, equipment). The distinction matters because the mitigation is different: struck-by is addressed by positioning, sightline, and escape routes; caught-between is addressed by refuge-area width and equipment placement.

*In use:* "Our escape-route checks address struck-by; we still need to verify refuge width against caught-between risk on the barrier side."

*Common misuse:* treating "stay visible and wear the vest" as sufficient risk mitigation, which addresses struck-by but does nothing for caught-between if the refuge area itself is too narrow.

## Marked crosswalk vs. legal crossing

A **marked crosswalk** is a crossing with painted lines or signage. A **legal crossing** is any point where pedestrians have the legal right to cross, which in most jurisdictions includes unmarked intersection extensions — a marked crosswalk is a subset of legal crossings, not a synonym for them.

*In use:* "There's no paint at that corner, but it's still a legal crossing — the guard assignment covers it the same as the marked one down the block."

*Common misuse:* assuming pedestrian protection or right-of-way only applies where paint is visible on the pavement.
