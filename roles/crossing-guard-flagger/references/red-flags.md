# Red flags

Signals a program supervisor or lead flagger should catch before someone gets hurt, not after an incident report is filed.

### Station positioned inside the sightline gap for the road's operating speed

**Usually means:** the guard/flagger was placed at the "obvious" spot (crosswalk center, curb corner, edge of the work space) rather than the spot the sightline calculation requires — a curve, crest, or parked vehicle is cutting the driver's reaction distance below what the taper or crossing assumes.

**First question:** "Walk both approach directions — where does a driver first actually see the device, and does that match the distance the speed calculation assumes?"

**Data to pull:** measured or radar-confirmed operating speed; sightline distance in both directions; taper length or crossing-assessment distance on file for this site.

### STOP paddle displayed but vehicles aren't visibly decelerating by the calculated point

**Usually means:** either the operating speed used for positioning is understated (drivers are actually going faster than assumed), or the device isn't visible early enough — foliage growth, a new obstruction, or a sun-glare angle that wasn't present when the site was last assessed.

**First question:** "At what distance is the average vehicle starting to brake, and does that match where we assumed compliance would start?"

**Data to pull:** current 85th-percentile speed measurement (re-measured, not the original file value); any recent site changes (foliage, signage, parked vehicles); log of near-misses at this station.

### School crossing logging fewer than 1 safe gap per minute during the peak window, with no guard or signal assigned

**Usually means:** the crossing was assessed once, years earlier, at a different traffic volume, and nobody re-ran the gap count after a nearby development, road change, or school population shift.

**First question:** "When was the gap count last actually measured at this crossing, during the real arrival or dismissal window — not estimated?"

**Data to pull:** current vehicle and pedestrian counts during the actual peak window; posted vs. measured 85th-percentile speed; any recent complaints or near-miss reports tied to this location.

### Two flagger stations on one closure with no radio and no unbroken line of sight

**Usually means:** the closure was staffed to headcount ("we have two flaggers") without checking whether those two flaggers can actually coordinate — a curve, grade, or excessive distance between them means each is guessing when the other releases traffic.

**First question:** "Can each flagger see the other's queue right now, or are they coordinating on assumption?"

**Data to pull:** site sketch showing line of sight between stations; radio equipment status; closure length versus practical hand-signal visibility range (roughly 1,000 ft in daylight).

### High-visibility apparel below the class the speed/visibility table requires

**Usually means:** apparel was issued once at hire and never re-matched to a specific site's speed or the shift's actual lighting conditions (a daytime assignment that runs into dusk, or a Class 2 garment sent to a ≥45 mph rural site).

**First question:** "What's this site's operating speed and this shift's lighting window, and does the apparel on this person match both?"

**Data to pull:** site speed data; shift start/end times against sunset/sunrise; apparel class currently assigned versus the class the table requires.

### Channelizing devices spaced wider than the taper's maximum interval

**Usually means:** the crew placed "however many cones were in the truck" rather than computing the required count from the taper length and the speed-based spacing cap — this is the most common gap between a written control plan and what's actually on the ground.

**First question:** "Count the cones in the taper and the total taper length — does the spacing come out at or under the speed limit in feet?"

**Data to pull:** actual device count and measured spacing on site; taper length and speed used in the control plan; device inventory reconciled against the plan's required count.

### No pre-identified escape route at a live-lane station

**Usually means:** the site was set up around the device and the traffic, with no thought given to where the flagger steps if a vehicle doesn't stop — often because the shoulder or refuge area wasn't checked for width or obstructions before the shift started.

**First question:** "If that next vehicle doesn't stop, where do you go — point to it right now."

**Data to pull:** shoulder/refuge width at the station; obstruction check (barriers, drop-offs, parked equipment) done before shift start; whether this was documented on the pre-shift checklist.

### A guard or flagger negotiating verbally with individual drivers instead of relying on the device

**Usually means:** the person has learned to "manage" impatient drivers by waving them through early or arguing with one who rolled forward, which erodes the predictability every other driver on the road is relying on — the device is supposed to be the only signal, and mixing in ad hoc verbal negotiation reintroduces ambiguity.

**First question:** "Walk me through the last time a driver didn't want to stop — what did you do?"

**Data to pull:** any complaint or incident history tied to that person; whether they've had a refresher on device-only signaling since being hired.

### Three or more near-miss or blow-through reports at one location in a season with no engineering follow-up requested

**Usually means:** incidents are being logged (good) but not escalated (bad) — the site is treated as a staffing problem to keep re-staffing rather than a location that needs a signal, speed-limit change, or redesign.

**First question:** "How many logged incidents does this location have this season, and has anyone requested an engineering review?"

**Data to pull:** the incident log for this specific location over the past two to three seasons; any prior engineering study or signal-warrant analysis on file; whether volume or speed has measurably changed since the last review.

### New or substitute guard/flagger placed at a complex site with only a generic briefing

**Usually means:** onboarding covered general device use and apparel but skipped the site-specific walkthrough — sightline obstructions, the escape route, the multi-leg intersection quirks — that a regular assignee has learned over time and a substitute has no way to know without being told.

**First question:** "Did this person walk the site and get the specific sightline/escape-route briefing, or just the general training?"

**Data to pull:** onboarding checklist completion for this specific location; whether the substitute has covered this site before; any site-specific notes on file that should have been handed over.

### Guard leaves the crossing point to retrieve a pedestrian who left the marked path

**Usually means:** understandable instinct, but it leaves the actual control point — the traffic — unmanaged for however long the retrieval takes, and any vehicle approaching during that window has no one holding the stop.

**First question:** "When that happens, who's watching the vehicles while you're moving toward the child?"

**Data to pull:** whether the site has back-up coverage during dismissal surges; training record on the position-vs-pursue rule; any incident where this sequence occurred.

### Shift length exceeds roughly two hours in high-heat or high-volume conditions without a break

**Usually means:** attention and judgment on the fixed reference-point rule degrade with fatigue and heat exposure faster than a supervisor watching from a distance would assume — this is when the "committed rule, not continuous judgment" principle is most likely to slip.

**First question:** "When was the last break, and what's the heat index or traffic volume been doing since?"

**Data to pull:** shift schedule and break log; local heat advisory status; any near-miss timestamped against the back half of a long shift.
