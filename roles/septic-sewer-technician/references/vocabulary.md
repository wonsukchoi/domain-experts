# Vocabulary

### Sludge judge
A marked, weighted tube lowered through a septic tank's liquid to measure scum and sludge layer depths against the outlet invert.
**In use:** "Ran the sludge judge — scum's at 2 inches off the tee, that's a pump today."
**Common misuse:** treating a visual check ("liquid looks low, plenty of room") as equivalent to a measured reading; scum proximity to the outlet, not overall liquid clearance, is what triggers pumping.

### Outlet tee / baffle
The submerged fitting at a septic tank's outlet that keeps floating scum from passing into the drainfield line while letting clarified liquid through.
**In use:** "Tee's cracked and the baffle's half off — that's why scum was reading close even though the tank wasn't overdue."
**Common misuse:** assuming a tank that measures "clear" on a sludge judge reading has no problem; a broken baffle can pass scum even when the reading looks fine, and it's only checkable with the tank empty.

### Distribution box (D-box)
The junction structure that splits effluent from the tank across multiple drainfield lines.
**In use:** "D-box was sitting a half-inch low on the north side — that line's been carrying more flow than the other three."
**Common misuse:** checking the tank and calling the job done without opening the D-box; an unlevel box fails one field line years before symptoms show up as a general field failure.

### Biomat
The biological clogging layer that forms at the soil interface in a drainfield trench from organic solids in effluent, and that ultimately limits how fast the soil can absorb liquid.
**In use:** "Field's not draining because the biomat's built up under that trench, not because the tank's full."
**Common misuse:** treating drainfield slowness as a tank problem; pumping the tank doesn't reduce biomat, and a field with heavy biomat backs up again regardless of tank condition.

### Effluent filter
An inline filter at the tank outlet that catches solids before they reach the drainfield line, requiring periodic rinsing.
**In use:** "Pulled the filter, it was packed — that's your 'full tank' right there, not the tank itself."
**Common misuse:** quoting a pump-out for a "full tank" complaint on a system with a filter before checking whether the filter itself is just clogged.

### Penetrating nozzle
A jetter nozzle with a forward-facing jet and minimal rear thrust, used to punch through an unopened blockage before switching to a flushing nozzle.
**In use:** "Ran the penetrating head through first — didn't want to build back-pressure jetting straight into a full stoppage."
**Common misuse:** going straight to a flushing nozzle (rear jets only, no forward jet) on an unidentified blockage, which can build hydraulic kickback against the operator and still not clear the line.

### Flushing nozzle
A jetter nozzle with rear-facing jets used to scour the pipe wall clean once the line is open, propelling itself back toward the operator.
**In use:** "Line's open — swapping to the flushing head to get the wall film off before we call it clear."
**Common misuse:** treating "the water's flowing again" (penetrating-nozzle result) as "the pipe is clean" (flushing-nozzle result); a path punched through a blockage still leaves wall buildup that causes the next backup.

### Root nozzle (chain flail / root saw)
A rotating chain- or blade-tipped jetter attachment for cutting root masses out of a pipe.
**In use:** "Camera showed a root ball at the joint — that's a chain-flail job, not a straight flush."
**Common misuse:** running a root nozzle on every recurring stoppage without camera confirmation; flail action on pipe that's actually cracked or offset (not root-choked) can tear the wall.

### CCTV / camera inspection
A push or crawler camera run through a sewer line to visually locate and log defects (root intrusion, fracture, offset joint, sag) at a station/footage number.
**In use:** "Footage shows an offset joint at station 42 feet — that's a repair call, not another snake-out."
**Common misuse:** running a camera as a one-time diagnostic and not logging defect locations in a comparable format; without a station number tied to a defect type, a second inspection can't confirm whether the same defect is recurring or worsening.

### Sag / belly
A section of pipe that has settled below the design grade, holding standing water between the low points instead of draining fully.
**In use:** "That's a belly holding water on every pass — grease and paper are going to hang up there no matter how often we jet it."
**Common misuse:** treating repeat blockages at a bellied section as a grease or root problem to be cleared again; a genuine sag needs a grade fix (spot repair), not repeat cleaning.

### Confined space entry
Entry into a tank, wet well, or manhole meeting OSHA's permit-required confined space criteria (limited entry/exit, not designed for continuous occupancy, potential hazardous atmosphere).
**In use:** "That's a permit-required space — gas meter's reading clear, but we still ventilate and log before anyone goes in."
**Common misuse:** skipping gas monitoring or the permit process for a space "worked in before without issue," which ignores that atmosphere can change call to call.

### ATU (aerated treatment unit)
An onsite wastewater system that mechanically aerates effluent to a higher treatment level than a standard gravity septic tank, typically with its own alarm panel and maintenance contract.
**In use:** "It's an ATU, not a standard tank — check the alarm panel history before assuming this is a pump-out call."
**Common misuse:** servicing an ATU with standard gravity-tank assumptions (pump interval, baffle checks) instead of following the unit's specific maintenance protocol.

### Riser
A vertical extension brought up to grade from a buried tank or D-box lid, installed to allow future access without excavation.
**In use:** "Put a riser on the outlet end while we've got it open — next check won't need a shovel."
**Common misuse:** treating riser installation as optional cosmetic upgrade rather than what determines whether future sludge-judge checks are quick or require re-excavating.

### PACP (Pipeline Assessment and Certification Program)
NASSCO's standardized defect-coding system for CCTV sewer inspection, used to log defects in a format comparable across inspections and companies.
**In use:** "Coded it RFJ at 42 feet — root at the joint, not a generalized root problem the whole run."
**Common misuse:** writing a plain-language note ("roots in the line") instead of a coded, located defect; plain notes can't be compared reliably against a future inspection of the same line.
