# Estimator working vocabulary

Terms a commercial GC estimator uses precisely. Format: definition → how a practitioner says it → common misuse.

**Hard bid vs. negotiated work** — Hard bid: lump-sum competitive bidding against a fixed, complete document set; lowest compliant bid usually wins, no scope negotiation after submission. Negotiated: the GC is selected before the price is final, often through preconstruction involvement, and can shape scope, phasing, or a GMP with the owner.
In use: "This one's hard bid — five GCs, sealed bids Friday at 2pm, no post-bid clarifications allowed."
Misuse: quoting the same overhead & profit percentage for both. Hard bid margins run thin because of price competition; negotiated work supports a fuller markup because relationship and preconstruction value are being paid for, not just a number.

**General conditions vs. general requirements** — General conditions: the GC's own field cost of running the job (superintendent, PM time, temp power/water, trailer, safety, cleanup) — driven by schedule duration. General requirements: the Division 01 contract requirements (bonds, permits, temporary facilities specifications, submittal procedures) that live inside the specifications.
In use: "GCs are running $16,800/week on this one — 14 weeks, so $235,200 before we touch a single trade dollar."
Misuse: treating them as the same line item, or worse, folding the GC's field staffing cost into home-office overhead so the bid recap understates what it actually costs to run the job.

**Overhead & profit (O&P)** — Overhead: recovery of the home office's fixed cost of existing (accounting, estimating department, insurance, office lease), allocated across all active jobs. Profit: the margin the company keeps after every other cost, including overhead, is covered.
In use: "We're at 5 and 4 on this one — thinner than our usual 6-and-6 because there are five bidders and we need to win it."
Misuse: quoting a single blended "9% markup" without knowing the split — hides whether the job is actually profitable or just subsidized by overhead absorbed elsewhere.

**Contingency vs. escalation** — Contingency: a reserve against scope or quantity unknowns still present in an incomplete design; shrinks as design completion increases. Escalation: a reserve against a known, average future cost increase in materials or labor between the estimate date and the midpoint of construction.
In use: "3% contingency because we're at 100% CDs, plus 2% escalation because NTP is three months out and steel's been moving."
Misuse: calling any buffer above direct cost "contingency" — an owner asking "why do we need both" deserves the actual distinction, not a shrug.

**Bid leveling** — Normalizing competing subcontractor bids to an identical scope of work before comparing dollar figures, since bidders routinely include or exclude different items against the same drawings.
In use: "Don't hand me raw numbers — level the bids first, then tell me who's actually low."
Misuse: comparing bottom-line totals straight off the bid tab without checking inclusions/exclusions — the fastest way to carry a bid that's missing $100K+ of required scope.

**Unit price / assembly estimate** — Unit price: cost per measured unit of installed work (material + labor + equipment, e.g., $/SF of drywall). Assembly estimate: a bundled unit price covering a whole system (e.g., $/SF for an exterior wall assembly including studs, sheathing, insulation, and cladding) used for faster, less-precise conceptual pricing.
In use: "At schematic we're using assembly pricing for the envelope; once we've got wall sections we'll break it into unit-price line items."
Misuse: using assembly-level pricing on a bid-grade (Class 1) estimate where a detailed unit-price takeoff is expected — it hides quantity errors a real takeoff would catch.

**AACE Class estimate** — The AACE International classification (Class 5 through Class 1) tying estimate methodology and stated accuracy range to design completion percentage — Class 5 (~0–2% design, roughly −20/+30% to −50/+100%) through Class 1 (~50–100% design, roughly −3/+3% to −10/+15%).
In use: "This is a Class 3 — we're at 30% DDs, so plus-or-minus 20 to 30 is the honest range, not plus-or-minus 5."
Misuse: presenting any estimate as if it's Class 1 accuracy because the client wants certainty — the class is determined by design completion, not by client preference.

**Basis of estimate (BOE)** — The written document accompanying an estimate stating its scope assumptions, exclusions, pricing sources, design completion percentage, and AACE class — the record of what the number does and doesn't include.
In use: "Before you present that number, where's the BOE? I need to know what's assumed before I'll sign off."
Misuse: treating the BOE as optional paperwork attached after the fact rather than the document that makes the number defensible when scope gets challenged mid-project.

**Allowance** — A placeholder dollar amount carried in the estimate for a scope item not yet fully defined (a finish selection, an owner-furnished item's connection cost), to be reconciled against the actual selected cost later, typically via change order.
In use: "Carrying a $40/SF allowance for the lobby flooring until the owner picks a finish — reconcile it once they select."
Misuse: carrying allowances on trades that should be firm-quoted by bid day, which turns a hard-bid estimate into a budget estimate wearing a hard-bid deadline.

**Self-perform** — Trade work the GC executes with its own labor force rather than subcontracting, most commonly demolition, rough carpentry, and general site labor.
In use: "We self-perform demo and rough carpentry — that's where our margin actually lives on a hard bid."
Misuse: assuming self-perform work carries the same markup logic as subcontracted work; self-perform risk (productivity, labor cost overruns) sits directly with the GC, with no sub absorbing it first.

**Schedule of values (SOV)** — The AIA G703-format breakdown of the total contract sum into line items (typically by CSI division), used to track percent-complete and generate monthly payment applications.
In use: "SOV has to reconcile exactly to the estimate recap's direct cost plus GCs before the first pay app goes out."
Misuse: building the SOV as a rough guess disconnected from the actual estimate — any mismatch surfaces later as a payment dispute with the owner.

**Retainage** — A percentage (commonly 5–10%) withheld from each progress payment until substantial completion, protecting the owner against incomplete or defective work.
In use: "10% retainage on this contract — that's $35,425 held back on this month's electrical billing alone."
Misuse: forgetting retainage in cash-flow projections; a GC that doesn't model retainage held across all active jobs can find itself short on cash despite a profitable job.

**Change order (CO) vs. RFI** — Change order: a formal, priced modification to the contract scope, cost, or schedule, requiring owner approval. RFI (request for information): a question to the architect/engineer clarifying an ambiguity in the documents — not itself a cost change, though the answer sometimes triggers one.
In use: "RFI #22 came back requiring an added steel connection — that's a CO, not a contingency draw, since it's an owner-side design gap."
Misuse: processing what should be a change order as a silent contingency draw, which erases the estimator's risk reserve without the owner ever seeing the cost impact.

**Bond (bid bond, performance & payment bond)** — Bid bond: guarantees the bidder will honor its bid price and sign the contract if awarded (typically 5–10% of bid). Performance & payment bond: guarantees the GC completes the work and pays subs/suppliers (typically 0.5–1.5% of contract value, priced into the estimate).
In use: "P&P bond runs 0.85% on this one — call it $45,500 baked into the stack before O&P."
Misuse: treating bonding cost as negligible or forgetting to price it before profit is calculated — it's a real line, not a rounding item, especially on public work where it's mandatory.

**Cost code / CSI division** — A standardized numbering system (MasterFormat, Divisions 00–49) used to organize estimate line items, schedules of values, and job-cost tracking so costs are comparable across projects and trades.
In use: "Pull up Division 09 finishes on the job-cost report — that's where we're tracking over budget."
Misuse: using an internal ad-hoc coding scheme instead of CSI divisions, which breaks comparability against historical data and against the owner's own cost tracking.
