# Red flags — what an estimator notices instantly

Smell tests with thresholds. Any one can be innocent; two or three together on the same bid is a pattern worth escalating before the bid closes or the contingency runs out.

### Sub bid more than ~20–25% below the next-lowest compliant bid
- **Usually means:** missing scope (an exclusion buried in a qualifications paragraph), a misread drawing set, or a subcontractor buying the job to survive a slow backlog quarter.
- **First question:** "Walk me through the three scope lines you'd have to cut to hit this number."
- **Data to pull:** the bid leveling sheet line-by-line against the drawings/specs, the sub's bonding capacity and current backlog, references from their last two GCs.

### Single dollar figure reported with no accuracy range at under ~30% design completion
- **Usually means:** whoever's presenting the number is treating a Class 5/4 conceptual estimate as bid-grade, and the client will anchor on it regardless of caveats said out loud.
- **First question:** "What AACE class is this, and what design completion percentage is it based on?"
- **Data to pull:** the basis of estimate (BOE) document, the design set's completion percentage, the stated +/- range for that class.

### General conditions under ~4% of direct cost on a job with a 6+ month duration
- **Usually means:** field staffing is understaffed in the estimate (one superintendent covering too much scope) or GC costs were miscategorized into home-office overhead to make the bid look leaner.
- **First question:** "Walk me through the staffing plan week by week against the schedule."
- **Data to pull:** the GC line-item buildup, the project schedule, comparable GC% from the firm's last 3 jobs of similar duration.

### Contingency draw-down outpacing construction progress (e.g., 60% of contingency spent at 30% progress)
- **Usually means:** either the unknowns are recurring (bad geotech, undocumented existing conditions) or the PM is charging undisclosed scope creep against the reserve instead of processing owner change orders.
- **First question:** "Break down the draws so far — how many are true unknowns (DSC/design omission) versus owner changes that should've been COs?"
- **Data to pull:** the contingency burn-down log with cause codes, the change-order log for the same period.

### Unit costs sourced entirely from a national-average database with no location factor applied
- **Usually means:** the estimate will mis-price by the gap between the national average and the actual local labor/material market — commonly 15–30% metro to metro.
- **First question:** "What location or city cost index was applied, and to which lines?"
- **Data to pull:** the index source and factor used, at least one live local sub quote per major trade to spot-check the adjusted number.

### Escalation omitted on a bid held or awarded 90+ days from the estimate date in a volatile material market
- **Usually means:** the GC either absorbs the increase silently or fights for a change order later, after the number is already contractually fixed.
- **First question:** "What's the midpoint-of-construction date, and which index tracks the materials most exposed here (steel, lumber, copper)?"
- **Data to pull:** ENR (Engineering News-Record) or producer price index trend for the exposed material basket, the contract's escalation clause (if any).

### Only one bid received in a trade that normally draws 3+ bidders
- **Usually means:** the trade is genuinely capacity-constrained in the current market (real cost pressure, not negotiable away) or the invitation-to-bid list didn't reach enough qualified subs.
- **First question:** "Who else was invited, and do we know why they declined or didn't respond?"
- **Data to pull:** the bid solicitation/invitation list, sub responses or declines, plan-holder list from the reprographics service.

### Self-perform crew productivity priced at manual/database rates with no adjustment for schedule stacking
- **Usually means:** productivity loss from trade congestion, overtime fatigue, or out-of-sequence work isn't priced, which understates labor cost roughly 10–20% on compressed schedules.
- **First question:** "What congestion or stacking factor is applied to this crew, and against which weeks of the schedule?"
- **Data to pull:** the trade-stacking/logic chart from the schedule, crew-loading curve, the productivity factor table used.

### Overhead and profit reported as a single blended markup, not two separate lines
- **Usually means:** home-office overhead absorption is masking whether the job clears real profit on its own, or vice versa.
- **First question:** "Strip overhead out — is this specific job profitable, independent of company-wide absorption?"
- **Data to pull:** historical job-cost reports by job, the firm's overhead allocation method, this job's OH% and profit% as separate figures.

### Bid pursued with less than ~3 weeks from RFP release to bid due date on a full document set
- **Usually means:** insufficient time to get real sub quotes on every trade, forcing budget/allowance numbers onto lines that should be firm bids.
- **First question:** "Which trades are still on allowances, and what's the exposure if the real quotes come in high?"
- **Data to pull:** the outstanding-quote tracker against the bid due date, the allowance list with dollar exposure per line.
