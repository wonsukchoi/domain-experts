# Red flags — what a meeting/event planner notices instantly

Smell tests with thresholds. Any one can be innocent; two or three together on the same event is a pattern worth escalating before contract signature or before final settlement.

### Room block pickup pacing more than 10 points behind the attrition threshold's straight-line pace at the 75-day checkpoint
- **Usually means:** the attendee forecast was set too high, marketing/registration hasn't converted to hotel bookings yet, or attendees are booking outside the block (Airbnb, rate-shopping competitor hotels) because the block rate isn't competitive.
- **First question:** "Is this a total-attendance problem or a block-capture problem?" — check registration numbers against pickup separately before assuming the event itself is undersold.
- **Data to pull:** current registration count vs. forecast, hotel pickup report, block rate vs. publicly bookable rate at the same property for the same dates.

### F&B spend pacing below 80% of the minimum with fewer than 45 days to the event
- **Usually means:** the banquet event orders (BEOs) underscoped compared to what was budgeted against the minimum, a planned function got cut or downsized after the minimum was contracted, or per-person menu selections came in cheaper than modeled.
- **First question:** "Which BEOs are locked, and where's the gap between locked spend and the minimum?"
- **Data to pull:** BEO summary with per-function subtotals, catering change-order log, minimum-vs-spend tracker.

### Attrition clause silent on which nights count, or measured against "peak night" instead of total room nights
- **Usually means:** the contract language is ambiguous by omission (common in a first-draft venue contract) and will be interpreted in the hotel's favor at settlement unless clarified in writing beforehand.
- **First question:** "Does the attrition base include or exclude arrival/departure fringe nights, and is that written down anywhere signed?"
- **Data to pull:** the exact attrition clause text, any side-letter or addendum clarifying the base, comparable past contracts with the same venue or brand.

### Force majeure clause that only lists "acts of God, war, and government seizure of the premises"
- **Usually means:** the clause predates the industry-wide post-2020 push to add epidemic, government travel advisory, and government-mandated-capacity-restriction language — it will not excuse performance for anything short of physical destruction of the venue.
- **First question:** "If a government travel advisory or a venue-city capacity order were issued tomorrow, does this clause actually help us?"
- **Data to pull:** the clause's exact named perils, the venue brand's current standard contract template (ask the sales contact directly — most have updated language available on request), cancellation-fee schedule as the fallback if force majeure doesn't apply.

### Comp room ratio worse than 1:50 (fewer than 1 comp per 50 paid room nights)
- **Usually means:** the group's actual value to the venue is being underpriced in this specific term even if the room rate looks fine — comp ratio is one of the cheapest concessions for a venue to grant and one of the first things given up in a rushed negotiation.
- **First question:** "What's this venue's standard comp ratio for a block this size, and why are we below it?"
- **Data to pull:** comparable contracts (same brand, similar block size) if available, the venue's group sales comp policy.

### Master bill service charge line described internally as "gratuity" in the budget
- **Usually means:** whoever built the budget conflated a mandatory, venue-retained service charge with a discretionary tip, which understates the true cost (service charge is usually taxable, so tax stacks on top of it) and misinforms staff about what they're actually receiving.
- **First question:** "Does this venue's service charge get distributed to staff, and is that disclosed anywhere in writing?"
- **Data to pull:** the catering contract's service-charge clause, the property's or jurisdiction's service-charge disclosure policy, the budget line labeling.

### AV quote from the venue-exclusive provider more than ~15% above the same provider's rate card for a comparable event
- **Usually means:** the quote was built off list price with no volume or exclusivity-relationship discount applied, which happens by default when the AV company doesn't know it's competing (because it isn't, contractually) unless the planner explicitly asks for tiered pricing.
- **First question:** "What does this provider charge a venue-exclusive client at this program's scale, and has that tier actually been applied here?"
- **Data to pull:** the AV provider's published or negotiated rate card, prior events at the same venue with comparable AV scope, the specific line-item breakdown (not a lump sum) in the quote.

### Run-of-show with no named decision-owner for on-site schedule changes
- **Usually means:** the plan assumes nothing will go wrong, or assumes "the team" will sort it out in the moment — both produce the same failure: a 10-minute AV or catering delay turns into a 45-minute one because three people are independently deciding whether and how to compress the schedule.
- **First question:** "If the keynote runs 15 minutes over, who decides what gets cut from the afternoon, right now, without a meeting?"
- **Data to pull:** the run-of-show document itself, checked for a named owner and pre-agreed cut list per session block.

### Final master bill arriving with line items not matched to a signed BEO or contract amendment
- **Usually means:** either a legitimate on-site change order that wasn't documented in real time, or a billing error — venues routinely bill list price on unmatched lines and expect the planner to catch it.
- **First question:** "Show me the signed BEO or change order that authorizes this specific line."
- **Data to pull:** full BEO set, on-site change-order log (verbal approvals should have been converted to signed change orders same-day), original contract exhibit for comparison.
