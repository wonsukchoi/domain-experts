# Working vocabulary

Terms a meeting/event planner uses precisely and generalists misuse. Format: definition → how a practitioner says it → common misuse.

**Attrition (clause)** — The contractual penalty for picking up fewer room nights than a stated percentage (commonly 80-90%) of the contracted block; damages are typically the shortfall below that *threshold*, not below the full block, multiplied by ADR and a rebate factor (often ~80%, reflecting the hotel's saved variable cost).
In use: "We're not short against the block, we're short against the 85% threshold — that's the number that triggers damages."
Misuse: comparing actual pickup to the full contracted block instead of the threshold, which understates the real exposure (or, in the other direction, panicking over a "shortfall" that's actually still above threshold).

**Cutoff date** — The date (commonly 30 days before arrival) after which the hotel may release any unsold rooms in the block to general public inventory; after cutoff, the group has no guaranteed access to the original rate or availability for those rooms.
In use: "We're at cutoff — anything unsold from here is the hotel's to sell at their rate, not ours."
Misuse: treating cutoff as the deadline to *worry* about pickup, when the useful window for corrective action (marketing push, formal block reduction) closes well before cutoff.

**F&B minimum (food and beverage minimum)** — A contracted minimum dollar spend on food and beverage for the event; if actual spend comes in under it, the group is billed the shortfall as if it had been spent, usually plus service charge and tax on the shortfall.
In use: "We're $25,700 under the F&B minimum — that gets billed at contract rate plus service charge and tax, not waived."
Misuse: budgeting F&B to expected spend without checking it against the contracted minimum, and being surprised at settlement that the shortfall itself carries a bill.

**Service charge** — A mandatory percentage (commonly 20-24%) added to F&B and other venue charges, retained and distributed by the venue at its own discretion; it is taxable revenue to the venue, not automatically a tip to staff.
In use: "The 24% service charge is taxable and goes to the house — it's not the same line as gratuity, and we don't budget it as a staff tip."
Misuse: calling it "gratuity" in the budget or assuming it's passed through to service staff; in many jurisdictions and contracts it isn't, and either assumption misstates both the true cost (tax stacks on top of it) and what staff are actually paid.

**Rebate factor** — The percentage of ADR used to calculate attrition damages, reflecting the portion of room revenue that was actually profit to the hotel (the rest being variable costs — housekeeping, utilities — the hotel didn't incur on an unsold room). Commonly negotiated around 80%, sometimes framed the other direction as a 20% deduction.
In use: "Damages are the shortfall times ADR times an 80% rebate factor — the hotel isn't entitled to the full room rate on rooms it never had to service."
Misuse: calculating attrition damages at full ADR with no rebate factor applied, which overstates exposure and gives up a negotiable term without realizing it was ever on the table.

**Force majeure** — The contract clause excusing performance (or scaling cancellation fees) when a named category of event outside either party's control prevents the meeting from happening as planned; only covers what it explicitly names.
In use: "Check whether the force majeure clause actually names government travel advisories, or if we're still on 'acts of God, war, and government seizure of premises' boilerplate."
Misuse: assuming force majeure covers anything that feels catastrophic enough; if the specific trigger isn't named in the clause, it doesn't apply, however severe the situation.

**Comp room ratio** — The number of complimentary room nights the venue provides per some number of paid room nights picked up (commonly 1 comp per 40-50 paid nights), typically applied at the group's discretion (staff, VIPs, speakers).
In use: "At 1,390 picked-up nights and a 1:45 ratio, we're owed roughly 30 comp room nights — confirm the folio actually zeroed them."
Misuse: treating comps as a nice-to-have perk rather than a contracted entitlement that needs to be tracked and verified on the final bill, where it's a common place for a billing error to slip through unnoticed.

**BEO (Banquet Event Order)** — The per-function operational document (one per meal, break, or session) specifying room setup, menu, timing, AV, and headcount that the venue's operations team actually executes from — distinct from the contract, which sets financial terms.
In use: "The BEO for Tuesday's lunch says 620 covers and a 12:15 start — if that number's stale, the kitchen will prep to the wrong count."
Misuse: treating the contract's F&B minimum and the BEO's per-function spend as the same document; the BEO is what gets executed on-site, and it needs updating every time attendance or program design changes, independent of the contract.

**Master account (master bill)** — The consolidated final invoice covering all charges routed through the venue — rooms, F&B, AV (if venue-managed), resort fees, attrition and shortfall settlements — reconciled against every contracted minimum before payment.
In use: "Don't sign off on the master account until every line traces back to a signed BEO or contract exhibit."
Misuse: paying the master bill as presented without matching each line to its authorizing document; venues routinely include unmatched or list-priced lines that a planner is expected to catch.

**Run-of-show** — The minute-by-minute execution schedule for the live program (general sessions, breakouts, meal transitions), distinct from the attendee-facing agenda, used on-site as the single source of truth for timing decisions.
In use: "The run-of-show has the keynote as a protected anchor — if it slips, the cut list kicks in for the afternoon breakouts, not the other way around."
Misuse: confusing the run-of-show with the published attendee agenda; the run-of-show includes buffer times, load-in/load-out, and a pre-agreed cut list that never appears in attendee-facing materials.

**Block reduction (attrition cure)** — A formal, usually one-time contractual right to reduce the room block by a stated percentage if requested by a specified deadline (commonly 90-120 days before arrival), reducing the base against which attrition is later measured.
In use: "Pickup's tracking short at the 75-day mark — this is the last window to file the block reduction before it's off the table."
Misuse: waiting until the cutoff date to react to soft pickup, by which point the block-reduction window (if the contract even has one) has already closed and the original block is the only number that matters at settlement.

**RFP (request for proposal) grid** — The normalized comparison of multiple venue bids across the same set of financial and risk terms (rate, attrition %, F&B minimum, cutoff, comp ratio, AV exclusivity, fees), used to compare total cost and risk exposure rather than headline rate alone.
In use: "Venue B's rate is $20 cheaper, but the grid shows a stricter attrition base and a resort fee that puts their total cost per attendee above Venue A's."
Misuse: comparing venues on room rate alone, which ignores clauses (F&B minimum size, attrition base, fees) that often move total cost more than the rate does.
