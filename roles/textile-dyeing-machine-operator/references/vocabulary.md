# Vocabulary

### Percent owf (percent on weight of fiber)
The standard way dye and chemical quantities are expressed in textile dyeing, as a percentage of the dry fiber weight being processed, independent of liquor ratio.
**In use:** "That's 2.0% owf — 10 kg of dye for a 500 kg batch, regardless of what liquor ratio the machine runs at."
**Common misuse:** Confusing %owf, a fiber-weight-based measure, with bath concentration, a liquor-volume-based measure, which changes with liquor ratio even when %owf stays constant.

### Liquor ratio
The ratio of dye bath liquid weight or volume to the weight of fiber/fabric being processed, which directly affects the dye bath's actual concentration.
**In use:** "Same %owf, but at 6:1 instead of 10:1 the bath concentration is meaningfully higher."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a recipe's %owf dye percentage is independent of the process's actual bath concentration effects, when liquor ratio changes bath concentration — and therefore exhaustion behavior — even at the same %owf.

### Exhaustion (dye)
The percentage of dye that actually transfers from the bath liquor onto the fiber, as opposed to remaining in solution.
**In use:** "Exhaustion's tracking low on this component — check liquor ratio and electrolyte before flagging the dye lot."
**Common misuse:** Assuming exhaustion behavior is a fixed property of a given dye, rather than something that varies with temperature, time, liquor ratio, and auxiliary chemicals.

### Bath concentration
The actual amount of dye present per unit volume of dye liquor, which changes with liquor ratio even when the total %owf dye charge stays the same.
**In use:** "Bath concentration's 66% higher at this ratio than what the recipe was validated at — that's the real variable that changed."
**Common misuse:** Treating %owf and bath concentration as interchangeable, when they diverge whenever liquor ratio changes.

### Level dyeing (levelness)
Achieving uniform color across a batch, as opposed to uneven or blotchy dye uptake.
**In use:** "Shade reads right on average, but levelness is off — check for blotching before calling this batch acceptable."
**Common misuse:** Assuming any shade match to a target standard also guarantees levelness, when a batch can average out to the correct overall shade while still being unevenly dyed across different sections.

### Auxiliary chemicals (dyeing)
Electrolytes, pH adjusters, leveling agents, and similar additives used alongside dye to control exhaustion rate and evenness.
**In use:** "Adjusted the electrolyte level to compensate for the higher bath concentration at this liquor ratio."
**Common misuse:** Treating auxiliary chemical levels as a fixed, secondary detail rather than an active variable that changes exhaustion behavior alongside temperature, time, and liquor ratio.

### Strip testing (exhaustion monitoring)
Periodically sampling and measuring the dye bath liquor's remaining dye concentration during a dyeing run to track exhaustion progress.
**In use:** "Strip test at the 20-minute mark shows exhaustion tracking behind schedule — still time to adjust before the run finishes."
**Common misuse:** Relying only on the finished batch's shade comparison at the end, rather than monitoring exhaustion progress during the run when corrective action is still possible.

### Whiteness/absorbency (bleaching)
Measurable properties verifying that a substrate's bleaching/preparation stage has adequately removed natural pigment and prepared the fiber for even dye uptake.
**In use:** "Reflectance meter confirms whiteness spec is met — clear to proceed to dyeing."
**Common misuse:** Assuming bleaching is "done" based on visual appearance or elapsed process time rather than a measured whiteness/absorbency value against spec.

### Shade standard
A physical swatch or spectrophotometer-recorded color reference that a production batch's dyed result must match within a defined tolerance.
**In use:** "Compare against the shade standard on the spectrophotometer, not just by eye under the shop lights."
**Common misuse:** Comparing a finished batch to the shade standard only by eye, rather than using a spectrophotometer reading for an objective, reproducible comparison.

### Dye class (e.g. reactive, disperse, acid)
The chemical category of dye, each with distinct fiber affinity, application temperature range, and fixation mechanism.
**In use:** "That's a reactive dye process, not disperse — the temperature profile and fixation chemistry are completely different."
**Common misuse:** Assuming process parameters — temperature, auxiliary chemicals, exhaustion behavior — transfer across different dye classes, when each class behaves fundamentally differently even on the same fiber type.
