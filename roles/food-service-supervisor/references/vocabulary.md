# Vocabulary

Terms generalists misuse or flatten together, kept sharply separate by a practitioner.

### Prime cost
Food cost plus total labor cost, expressed as a percentage of net sales — the two line items a shift supervisor and their manager can actually move week to week, unlike rent or utilities.
**In use:** "Prime cost hit 67% this week — I already cut Tuesday's trough shift, so the next move is checking portioning on the top sellers, not scheduling."
**Common misuse:** Treating "labor cost %" alone as the full picture and ignoring that a cheap labor week paired with food-cost creep can still blow prime cost past target.

### SPLH (Sales Per Labor Hour)
Forecasted or actual sales for a period divided by the crew labor-hours scheduled or worked in that period — the primary metric for building and judging a schedule, distinct from labor cost percentage.
**In use:** "SPLH says we're fine at $160/hour, but that's the whole window averaged — Register 1's ticket times are the number that actually matters right now."
**Common misuse:** Using a healthy aggregate SPLH to conclude a shift is adequately staffed, when a single station can be failing in real time while the average still looks good.

### Comp vs. void
A **comp** removes charge for an item already made and typically served (a quality or service recovery); a **void** removes an item from a ticket before or instead of fulfilling it (an order-entry correction). Both are tracked separately because they imply different root causes.
**In use:** "That's a comp, not a void — the burger went out wrong and we remade it. If it was rung in wrong before firing, it's a void."
**Common misuse:** Logging every after-the-fact discount as a "void" because it's the button closest at hand, which hides genuine quality-failure comps inside what should be a clean order-entry-error metric.

### 86
Kitchen-and-counter shorthand for an item that is no longer available to sell, called out so both stations stop taking orders for it immediately.
**In use:** "86 the crispy chicken — we're out of thighs till the truck Thursday."
**Common misuse:** Announcing an 86 only to the kitchen and not the register, so front-of-house keeps selling an item the line can't make.

### Line check
A pre-service verification of every station's setup — par levels, equipment temps, portioning tools in place — done station by station, not a single walk-through glance.
**In use:** "Line check caught the fryer running 15 degrees low before we opened — that's a ten-minute fix now instead of a slow-ticket problem at noon."
**Common misuse:** Treating the opening checklist's temperature entries as the line check, when a line check also covers non-temperature setup (portioning tools, backup product staged, equipment calibration).

### Danger zone
The food-temperature range, roughly 41°F to 135°F, in which bacteria multiply fastest; food safety practice is built around minimizing time in this range, not avoiding it entirely (it's unavoidable during cooking and plating).
**In use:** "That pan's been sitting at 95 degrees for forty minutes — it's in the danger zone, it doesn't matter that it was cooked properly earlier."
**Common misuse:** Treating "was cooked to temp" as sufficient food safety, ignoring how long a holding or cooling item has spent back in the danger zone afterward.

### Tip credit
A wage mechanism (where legal) allowing an employer to count a portion of tipped employees' tips toward the minimum-wage obligation, paying a lower direct cash wage as long as tips make up the difference — largely a front-of-house/server concept, and inapplicable in many QSR settings with no tipped positions.
**In use:** "We don't run a tip credit here — everyone's on a straight hourly wage, so don't apply that scheduling assumption from your last job."
**Common misuse:** Assuming tip-credit rules apply chain-wide when a given concept (counter-service QSR, for instance) may have no tipped positions or tip pool at all.

### Over/short (cash-drawer variance)
The dollar difference between what a register's drawer should contain per the POS z-report and what it actually counts to at close — "over" if there's extra cash, "short" if there's less.
**In use:** "Drawer 2 was $3.25 short tonight — under the write-up line, but that's the second short this week, so it's getting a recount procedure, not a shrug."
**Common misuse:** Treating any single small variance as proof of dishonesty, rather than a pattern-detection signal that only becomes meaningful over multiple shifts.

### Drop safe
A time-delay or slotted safe on the floor where cash above a set drawer limit is deposited during a shift, so no single register or employee holds more than a defined amount at once.
**In use:** "Drawer's over $150, time for a drop — get it into the safe before the next rush, not at close."
**Common misuse:** Confusing the drop-safe count with the register-close count; they're reconciled separately because a drop can be miscounted independent of the drawer itself.

### Shift lead vs. manager-on-duty (MOD)
A **shift lead** is crew-level, first-line authority for routine approvals (small comps, breaks); the **MOD** is the highest-ranking person physically present with authority for anything above the shift lead's ceiling (larger comps, incident calls, disciplinary conversations) — on a given shift these may or may not be the same person.
**In use:** "I'm shift lead tonight but the GM is MOD until 8, so anything over the $25 line goes to her, not me."
**Common misuse:** Assuming the person running the floor day-to-day always has MOD-level approval authority, when a store may schedule a separate, higher-authority MOD for cost or compliance reasons.

### Cross-trained / floater
A crew member certified to work more than one station, held in reserve specifically to cover gaps (a no-show, a rush spike) without calling in extra labor.
**In use:** "She's cross-trained on grill and register — pull her to Register 2 for the rush instead of calling someone in."
**Common misuse:** Treating "cross-trained" as a resume line rather than an active scheduling asset — if the floater isn't actually clocked in and available at the moment of need, the cross-training doesn't help.

### Speed-of-service (SOS) / order-to-window time
The measured interval from when an order is placed (or a car enters the drive-thru line) to when it's delivered — a distinct metric from ticket time, which measures kitchen production time alone.
**In use:** "SOS is up two minutes chain-wide, but our ticket times are flat — check the order-taking or payment step, not the kitchen."
**Common misuse:** Diagnosing a rising SOS number as a kitchen speed problem by default, when the delay is frequently in order-taking, payment, or window handoff instead.

### No-call/no-show
An unscheduled absence with zero advance notice, distinct from a call-out (notice given, even if last-minute) — tracked separately because it changes both the coverage math and the disciplinary response.
**In use:** "That's her second no-call/no-show this quarter — a call-out gets covered and moved on, this one goes to the GM."
**Common misuse:** Lumping a last-minute call-out in with a true no-call/no-show when logging attendance, which understates how serious the no-notice pattern actually is.

### Change fund
The fixed starting cash amount placed in a drawer at the start of a shift, against which the closing count is reconciled — distinct from the day's sales revenue, which is a separate figure entirely.
**In use:** "Drawer starts at the standard $150 change fund — if the open count doesn't match that, it's flagged before a single sale rings in, not blamed on the shift."
**Common misuse:** Folding a change-fund discrepancy into the shift's sales variance instead of catching and correcting it at open, which makes the whole shift's numbers unreliable from the start.
