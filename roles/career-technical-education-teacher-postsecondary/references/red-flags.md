# Red flags

Smell tests a program coordinator catches early. Format for each: what it usually means, the first question to ask, and the data to pull before anyone decides anything.

## 1. Enrollment down 20%+ over two years, no outcome data pulled yet

**Usually means:** someone is about to conflate a recruiting problem with a program-quality problem, because headcount is the easiest number to see and the other three (completion, credential attainment, placement) take a data pull to produce.

**First question:** "What are completion, credential-attainment, and placement rates for the last two cohorts — not enrollment, the outcome indicators?"

**Data to pull:** completer counts against concentrator counts by cohort year; credential pass rate; 3S1 placement rate; regional labor-market openings for the occupation over the same period, to check whether demand moved too.

## 2. Course pass rate is high but credentialing exam pass rate is lower

**Usually means:** the curriculum is teaching to the syllabus, not to the exam blueprint — a gap that shows up the moment an outside credentialing body, not the instructor, is grading.

**First question:** "Which exam domains are completers failing, and how many curriculum hours does each of those domains actually get?"

**Data to pull:** domain-level exam breakdown from the credentialing body (most publish this); current course hours per domain; date of the last curriculum backward-mapping review.

## 3. A student passed the course with a zero on one competency check-off

**Usually means:** averaged grading is hiding a safety-critical or job-critical failure inside an otherwise passing composite score — the exact failure mode competency-based grading exists to prevent.

**First question:** "Which specific competency was zeroed, and is it a safety-critical or equipment-access competency?"

**Data to pull:** the competency checklist (not just the final grade) for the student in question; whether the gradebook averages competencies into a composite score or requires each one individually cleared.

## 4. Advisory committee meeting minutes are the same slide deck twice in a row

**Usually means:** the committee has become a courtesy meeting rather than a curriculum-control mechanism, and the curriculum is quietly drifting from what member employers actually run.

**First question:** "What changed on your shop floor since our last meeting that isn't reflected in this program's equipment or curriculum yet?"

**Data to pull:** prior two meetings' minutes; equipment purchase history against advisory committee equipment requests; whether any committee member has left without replacement (a shrinking committee is itself a signal).

## 5. Work-based learning placement started without a signed training agreement

**Usually means:** the placement was arranged informally (a personal employer relationship, a rush to fill a slot) and is not currently defensible if a Perkins monitoring visit or an on-site injury occurs during the placement.

**First question:** "Where is the signed training agreement and the hours-logging method for this student?"

**Data to pull:** the placement file; state CTE office's minimum WBL hour threshold for 4S1 credit (varies by state — do not assume the program default); institution's liability/insurance policy for off-site placements.

## 6. Cost per completer is above system average and the conversation stops there

**Usually means:** the cost comparison is being read without the wage-premium half of the equation, making an efficient, high-value program look like a budget problem.

**First question:** "What's the regional wage premium for this occupation, and what's the payback period against the cost overrun?"

**Data to pull:** program budget and completer count for cost per completer; regional median wage for the occupation versus the regional median wage overall; system average cost per completer for comparable CTE programs.

## 7. Nontraditional enrollment in a program has been flat under 25% for several years

**Usually means:** recruiting materials, imagery, or outreach channels are implicitly signaling the program to one gender only, or a completer-level barrier (equipment sizing, schedule, childcare) is filtering out the underrepresented gender before enrollment.

**First question:** "Where in the recruiting funnel — inquiry, application, enrollment, or completion — does the underrepresented gender drop off?"

**Data to pull:** funnel data by stage and gender; recruiting material audit; comparable programs at peer institutions with higher nontraditional enrollment, for what they do differently.

## 8. OSHA/shop-safety training was a one-time orientation, never recertified

**Usually means:** the institution is treating a legally recertifiable competency as a one-time checkbox, which is both an audit exposure and a real injury risk as equipment, chemicals, or procedures change.

**First question:** "When was this cohort's last documented safety recertification, and does it cover the equipment currently in use?"

**Data to pull:** safety training records with dates; equipment inventory changes since the last recertification; any near-miss or incident log entries since the last training.

## 9. Placement rate looks strong but "further education" is doing most of the work

**Usually means:** the program is functioning as a transfer pathway rather than a direct-entry occupational pathway, which may be fine — but is a different program design than the one being funded or marketed.

**First question:** "Of the placements counted, how many are direct employment in the trained occupation versus transfer to a further credential?"

**Data to pull:** placement breakdown by destination type; whether the program's stated design (terminal occupational vs. transfer/2+2 pathway) matches what's actually happening; articulation agreement terms if transfer is the dominant outcome.

## 10. Credential attainment rate is high, but it's a low-value entry-level credential

**Usually means:** the program is optimizing for the easiest indicator win rather than the credential employers actually hire against — a common gaming pattern once 4S1 becomes a funding lever.

**First question:** "Does the advisory committee's member companies actually require or prefer this specific credential when hiring?"

**Data to pull:** advisory committee hiring-requirement input; job postings from regional employers in the occupation, checked for named credential requirements; whether a higher-tier credential in the same family exists and is being skipped.
