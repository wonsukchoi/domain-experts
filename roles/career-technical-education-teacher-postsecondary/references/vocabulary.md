# Vocabulary

Terms generalists conflate that CTE practitioners keep sharply separate.

### Concentrator vs. participant vs. completer

A **participant** has taken any CTE course. A **concentrator** (postsecondary Perkins V definition) has earned at least 12 credits in a single CTE program area, or completed the program if it requires fewer than 12. A **completer** has finished the program with a credential, degree, or certificate. All three counts differ, and reporting the wrong denominator flatters or damages a rate depending on which way it's misapplied.

**In use:** "We had 40 participants this year, 24 concentrators, and 18 completers — the completion rate is 18 over 24, not 18 over 40."

**Common misuse:** dividing completers by total participants (or by fall headcount) instead of by concentrators, which understates the true completion rate and invites an unnecessary program-cut conversation.

### Program of Study (POS) vs. course sequence

A **Program of Study** is the Perkins V-required, non-duplicative sequence of secondary and postsecondary coursework aligned to a career pathway, including academic and technical content and the potential for postsecondary credit. A **course sequence** is just the ordered list of classes — it can exist without meeting POS requirements (dual credit, employability standards, labor-market alignment) that make it fundable and portable.

**In use:** "The course sequence is fine, but it's not a POS yet — there's no articulated postsecondary credit and no labor-market data attached."

**Common misuse:** calling any ordered set of CTE courses a "Program of Study" when it lacks the articulation and labor-market-alignment components Perkins V actually requires for that label.

### Credential attainment rate (4S1) vs. course completion

**Credential attainment (4S1)** counts a student toward Perkins V program quality only if they earned a recognized postsecondary credential, at least 12 postsecondary credits toward one, or documented work-based-learning hours. **Course completion** just means passing the class — it carries no federal accountability weight on its own.

**In use:** "She passed the course, but until she sits the NIMS exam she doesn't count toward 4S1."

**Common misuse:** reporting course-completion rates to a dean or board as if they were the credential-attainment rate — the numbers can diverge sharply when the credentialing exam is harder than the course grade.

### Placement rate (3S1) vs. employment rate

**Placement (3S1)** counts completers in employment, the military, or further education within the state's defined window (commonly 6 months to 1 year). **Employment rate** narrowly means paid work in the trained field — a subset of placement. A program can have strong placement and mediocre in-field employment if a large share transferred to further schooling instead.

**In use:** "Placement's at 89%, but strip out the transfer-to-further-education cases and in-field employment is closer to 65%."

**Common misuse:** quoting the broader placement number in a context (an employer recruiting pitch, a labor-market claim) where the narrower in-field employment number is the relevant one.

### Nontraditional enrollment

Perkins V defines **nontraditional** for a given CTE field as a gender representing less than 25% of enrollment in that field — the term describes the enrollment pattern, not the student. A woman in a welding program is a nontraditional enrollee; welding is not itself "a nontraditional program" in the abstract.

**In use:** "We're at 14% female enrollment in welding — that's a nontraditional-enrollment gap the 5S1 indicator is tracking, not just an anecdote."

**Common misuse:** using "nontraditional" loosely to mean "unusual student" (older, career-changer) rather than the specific Perkins gender-representation threshold it refers to.

### Articulation agreement vs. dual credit vs. Tech Prep

An **articulation agreement** is an institution-to-institution agreement recognizing credit or advanced standing for specific coursework. **Dual credit** is a student simultaneously earning secondary and postsecondary credit for the same course, typically under such an agreement. **Tech Prep** is the older federal term (pre-Perkins V) for the 2+2 secondary-to-postsecondary CTE sequencing model that articulation agreements and dual credit now implement.

**In use:** "The articulation agreement covers Welding I and II for dual credit; anything beyond that still has to be reviewed course-by-course."

**Common misuse:** assuming an articulation agreement automatically covers every course in a sequence rather than the specific courses named in the agreement — students routinely lose credit assuming coverage that was never signed.

### Competency-based grading vs. averaged grading

**Competency-based grading** requires each skill standard individually demonstrated (pass/not-yet-pass per competency); a composite grade cannot mask one failed standard. **Averaged grading** blends scores across assignments into one number, which can produce a passing grade despite a zero on a single safety-critical or job-critical competency.

**In use:** "The gradebook is competency-based — she can't get a program completion credential with an open, unmet safety check-off, no matter what her assignment average says."

**Common misuse:** running a shop or lab program on a traditional averaged gradebook and treating the resulting composite grade as equivalent to competency mastery.

### Work-based learning (WBL) vs. internship vs. clinical/practicum

**Work-based learning** is the umbrella term (Perkins V) for any structured, employer-site learning experience tied to program competencies. **Internship** and **clinical/practicum** are specific WBL types — internship generic to most trades, clinical/practicum specific to allied-health programs with licensure-board-defined hour and supervision requirements that differ from a standard WBL agreement.

**In use:** "Nursing's clinical hours follow the state board's supervision ratio, not the general WBL training-agreement template the trades programs use."

**Common misuse:** applying the generic WBL placement checklist to a licensure-board-regulated clinical placement, missing supervision-ratio or hour requirements the board — not the college — sets.

### Industry-recognized credential vs. certificate of completion

An **industry-recognized credential** is issued or validated by a third-party body (NIMS, ASE, ServSafe) whose standard the labor market recognizes independent of the issuing college. A **certificate of completion** is issued by the college itself, attesting the student finished the program — it may or may not correspond to anything an employer recognizes.

**In use:** "The certificate of completion shows she finished the program; the NIMS credential is what tells an employer she can actually run the machine to spec."

**Common misuse:** reporting a college-issued certificate of completion as if it satisfied the Perkins V 4S1 "recognized postsecondary credential" bar, which requires third-party industry recognition, not institutional attestation alone.
