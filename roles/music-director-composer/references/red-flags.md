# Red flags

## Rehearsal running long on the familiar opener with two services left and the premiere still on page one

**Usually means:** rehearsal time got spent on what was rewarding to polish, not on what was actually at risk — the easy piece felt productive, the hard piece felt discouraging, so it got deferred.

**First question:** "How many usable rehearsal minutes does the premiere still need versus how many are left on the calendar?"

**Data to pull:** the rehearsal-minute budget table for the program, the timing log from services run so far, and the net-usable-minutes count for the remaining services.

## A "flat fee, all rights" scoring contract with no mention of writer's or publisher's share

**Usually means:** the agreement was drafted from generic freelancer boilerplate, not a media-scoring template, by someone who doesn't know the writer's share is legally non-assignable.

**First question:** "Does this assign the copyright outright, or does it name my PRO writer's share and specify what happens to the publisher's share?"

**Data to pull:** the actual clause language, a composer-guild or WGA-adjacent sample scoring agreement for comparison, and the production's standard deal memo if one exists.

## Cue sheet where every cue is marked generic background, including on-camera performances or recurring themes

**Usually means:** an assistant or coordinator filled it out from a cue list without checking the actual footage, missing visual-vocal/instrumental and theme categories that pay at different rates.

**First question:** "Were any of these cues performed on camera, or do any of them carry the recurring motif across multiple episodes?"

**Data to pull:** the original spotting notes, the scene-by-scene shot list, and a side-by-side of the cue sheet draft against the final cut.

## Click track locked to one rigid tempo across an emotionally rubato cue

**Usually means:** over-reliance on the tempo map without checking which moments actually require sync precision versus which need to breathe.

**First question:** "Which specific hit points in this cue require the click, and which sections can move off it?"

**Data to pull:** the hit-point list from the spotting session, the mockup rendered strictly to click, and a rubato take for comparison.

## A sample-based mockup gets director/producer sign-off with no note that the final delivery may swap in live players

**Usually means:** approval was given on the mockup's sound, not on the final instrumentation plan, creating a scope mismatch if the live-recording budget later shrinks or disappears.

**First question:** "Does this approval assume live strings on the exposed cues, or is the mockup itself the approved sound?"

**Data to pull:** the session budget worksheet, the approval email/notes, and which cues were explicitly flagged as live-candidate at spotting.

## New commission's parts arrive fewer than 2 weeks before a professional ensemble's first rehearsal (4 weeks for volunteer/community groups)

**Usually means:** the composer or copyist underestimated preparation time; the first rehearsal will burn services correcting copyist errors instead of rehearsing music.

**First question:** "Has anyone proofed these parts against the score, and when?"

**Data to pull:** the delivery date versus the rehearsal-minute schedule, the copyist's proofing checklist (or absence of one), and the ensemble's typical sight-reading tolerance for the idiom.

## A single "music budget" line covers both composer fee and recording costs with no breakdown

**Usually means:** nobody has actually checked whether the live-ensemble ambition fits inside the number, because the two costs were never separated.

**First question:** "What does the session budget worksheet show once composer fee is separated from musician, pension, H&W, cartage, and mix costs?"

**Data to pull:** the itemized AFM scale calculation for the proposed ensemble size and session count, versus the total ceiling.

## A concert program stacks two large-orchestration, rarely performed rental works on one bill

**Usually means:** the program was built for artistic ambition without checking hire-fee stacking, stage-plot logistics, or whether the rehearsal-minute budget can cover both unfamiliar works at once.

**First question:** "Have we requested hire quotes and confirmed the stage plot for both works, and does the rehearsal-minute table show enough time for both?"

**Data to pull:** rental quotes from the publisher's hire desk, the venue's stage-plot capacity, and the rehearsal-minute budgeting table for the full program.

## A vocalist or soloist is added to a program or session with no confirmed key, range, or accompanist reduction

**Usually means:** the addition was agreed verbally before checking whether the existing arrangement fits the voice, risking a same-week transposition scramble.

**First question:** "Has the soloist confirmed the key and range against the existing chart or reduction?"

**Data to pull:** the soloist's stated range/tessitura, the existing arrangement's key, and how much rehearsal time is left if a transposition is needed.

## A composer keeps treating the temp track as a note-for-note reference late into the writing process

**Usually means:** either genuine "temp love" on the production side (the temp got approved internally and now anything different reads as risk) or the composer defaulting to imitation under time pressure.

**First question:** "Which elements of the temp are we matching — tempo and density, or actual melodic and harmonic material?"

**Data to pull:** a side-by-side of the temp cue and the current draft cue, and any notes from the director on what specifically they responded to in the temp.
