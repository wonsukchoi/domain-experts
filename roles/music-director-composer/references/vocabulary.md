# Vocabulary

## Cue vs. cue sheet

A **cue** is a single discrete piece of music tied to a specific moment or scene, identified by an in/out timecode. A **cue sheet** is the reporting document — every cue in a program with composer, duration, and use category — submitted to a PRO so royalties can be calculated.

**In use:** "4M2 is the theme cue over the flashback — make sure it's on the cue sheet as theme, not background."

**Common misuse:** using "cue" and "cue sheet" interchangeably, as if writing the music and reporting the music were the same task; a perfectly written cue with a wrong cue-sheet entry still loses money.

## Spotting vs. scoring

**Spotting** is the process of deciding, with the director/producer, where music happens and what it needs to do — a decision session. **Scoring** is composing and recording the actual music for those decisions. Spotting produces notes; scoring produces cues.

**In use:** "We haven't finished spotting episode 6 yet, so nothing's being scored until that locks."

**Common misuse:** calling the whole compositional process "spotting," or skipping a real spotting session and treating a phone call as equivalent — spotting notes are a specific deliverable, not a vibe check.

## Temp track vs. score

The **temp track** is a placeholder built from existing (usually licensed) music, used during editing to test how the picture feels with music before a score exists. The **score** is the final original composition. Temp is a reference for tempo, density, and emotional shape — not a composition brief.

**In use:** "The temp is Zimmer-adjacent for pacing, but I'm not writing a soundalike — I'm matching the tempo and density, not the material."

**Common misuse:** treating the temp as the deliverable spec, producing a derivative score, or worse, one close enough to create soundalike liability.

## Click track vs. tempo map

A **click track** is the metronome pulse played to musicians during a session for sync accuracy. A **tempo map** is the underlying document of tempo changes and hit points across a cue's duration — the click can follow a tempo map that speeds up, slows down, or holds rubato sections, it isn't inherently one fixed number.

**In use:** "Build the tempo map so the click only locks for the four hit points — let it breathe everywhere else."

**Common misuse:** assuming "click track" always means a single rigid tempo for the whole cue; a well-built tempo map can carry a click through a rubato passage without flattening it.

## Sync license vs. master use license vs. performance royalty

A **sync license** grants the right to pair a specific piece of music with visual media. A **master use license** grants the right to use a specific recording of that music (separate from the composition itself). **Performance royalty** is the ongoing payment for public performance/broadcast, tracked via cue sheets and paid through a PRO — distinct from the one-time sync/master fees paid at licensing.

**In use:** "We cleared the sync and master for the needle-drop, but the ongoing performance royalty is what actually shows up on the cue sheet every time it airs."

**Common misuse:** assuming a sync license fee is the only payment involved; it covers a different right entirely from the recurring performance royalty.

## Writer's share vs. publisher's share

The **writer's share** of PRO performance royalty belongs to the composer and is legally non-assignable — it cannot be signed away in a contract, no matter how the deal is worded. The **publisher's share** is a separate, assignable portion often retained by a studio or production company under a work-for-hire scoring deal.

**In use:** "It's a work-for-hire deal, so they keep the publisher's share — but my writer's share is still mine no matter what the contract says."

**Common misuse:** treating a "buyout" or "all rights" contract as if it eliminates all future royalty; it can only reassign the publisher's share, never the writer's.

## Downbeat vs. ictus

The **downbeat** is the first beat of a measure. The **ictus** is the precise point of rebound in a conducting gesture that marks exact rhythmic placement — it can occur on any beat, not only the downbeat, and is the actual technical unit conductors use to communicate exact timing.

**In use:** "Watch my ictus on beat three, not just the downbeat — that's where the entrance actually lands."

**Common misuse:** using "downbeat" loosely to mean "any beat the conductor gives," collapsing a precise gesture-technique term into a generic one.

## Service vs. rehearsal vs. session

A **service** is the union-contract unit covering any rehearsal or concert appearance, counted against a fixed weekly/seasonal allotment (ICSOM/AFM per-service agreements). A **rehearsal** is the generic activity; not every rehearsal-like activity is a countable service, and not every service is a full rehearsal (some are concerts). A **recording session** is a separate, AFM-scale-governed unit with its own pay structure, distinct from live-performance services.

**In use:** "We've used three of our four services on this program — the dress counts as the fourth, so there's no service left to add a run-through."

**Common misuse:** treating "service" as a synonym for "rehearsal minutes," ignoring that mandated breaks and setup eat into the service's nominal length — the printed call time is not the usable rehearsal time.

## Doubling vs. contractor vs. sub

**Doubling** is one musician playing a second (or third) instrument within the same session, paid an AFM-scale percentage on top of base scale. The **contractor** hires and organizes the ensemble for a date and is typically paid double scale for that role. A **sub** is a substitute player who learns an existing book/part to cover an absence, distinct from doubling (same player, extra instrument) and from the contractor (an organizing role, not a playing one).

**In use:** "Have the reed player double on bass clarinet for that cue instead of booking a separate double — cheaper than a second chair for a part that's only busy eight bars."

**Common misuse:** confusing a "sub" (temporary cover for an absent regular) with "doubling" (one musician covering multiple instruments within a single date) — they solve different staffing problems and are paid under different scale provisions.

## Underscore vs. source music vs. theme

**Underscore** is non-diegetic music the characters can't hear, supporting a scene from outside its world. **Source music** is diegetic — a radio, a band on screen, music the characters can hear. A **theme** is a recurring musical idea tied to a character, place, or the show itself, identified across multiple episodes and cue-sheet-coded separately because it pays at a different rate than a one-off cue.

**In use:** "That's source, not underscore — it's coming from the car radio in-frame, so it needs its own separate license."

**Common misuse:** miscategorizing a diegetic cue as underscore on the cue sheet (missing the source-music licensing implications), or failing to flag a recurring motif as theme every time it appears.

## Orchestration vs. arrangement vs. dance arrangement

**Orchestration** assigns existing musical material to specific instruments (deciding who plays what). **Arrangement** reworks existing music into a new form (a different key, style, or ensemble). A **dance arrangement** is a theater-specific term for an extended instrumental section built around existing thematic material to support choreography, distinct from the song's vocal arrangement.

**In use:** "The dance arranger built sixteen bars off the title-song motif for the act-one break — that's not in the vocal arrangement at all."

**Common misuse:** using "orchestration" and "arrangement" interchangeably outside the field, or assuming a show's dance music is simply the song repeated rather than newly composed material.

## Rental (hire) vs. perusal score

A **perusal score** is a free or low-cost copy provided for evaluation before a work is programmed — not performance-ready. **Rental (hire)** materials are the paid, time-limited performance set (full score and parts) licensed for an actual concert, typically on a fixed window (commonly around ten weeks pre-performance, publisher-dependent).

**In use:** "We're still on the perusal score deciding if this fits the program — don't send parts to the librarian until we've committed and ordered the hire materials."

**Common misuse:** assuming a perusal copy is complete and performance-ready, or ordering hire materials so late that the standard rental window triggers rush fees.

## Book (Broadway) — parts vs. script

In a Broadway pit context, "the book" can mean either the **full set of musical parts and score** a player or sub must learn, or — in a different, non-musical sense — the **show's dialogue/script**. Practitioners disambiguate entirely from context, and confusing the two with an outside collaborator causes real miscommunication.

**In use:** "She's still learning the book" (musical parts, said by a sub) versus "the book needs a rewrite" (the script, said by a writer) — same word, unrelated meaning.

**Common misuse:** assuming "the book" always means the same thing across a production's music and writing departments.
