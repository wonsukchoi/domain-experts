# Red flags

Smell tests a wardrobe crew catches early. Format for each: what it usually means, the first question to ask, and the data to pull before deciding.

## 1. Quick change timed once in early tech and never re-timed since

**Usually means:** the number everyone's relying on is stale — blocking shifted, an actor learned shortcuts (or bad habits), or a costume alteration changed a closure's speed, and nobody re-ran the stopwatch.

**First question:** "When was this change last timed, and has anything — blocking, cast, costume alteration — changed since then?"

**Data to pull:** the dated timing log for that change; the most recent blocking notes for the scenes bracketing the change; any alteration requests logged against the pieces involved.

## 2. Dresser-to-actor ratio below 1:2 on a sub-30-second window

**Usually means:** the change was scheduled on paper without decomposing the actual task list into parallel chains, so one overloaded dresser is quietly absorbing risk every performance.

**First question:** "Walk me through the critical path — which tasks are actually dependent on each other, and which are just assigned to the same person by habit?"

**Data to pull:** the quick-change choreography worksheet (task list, dependencies, chain assignments); the timed critical-path result versus the scripted window.

## 3. Actor reports a costume "feels tight" or "restricts movement" after an alteration

**Usually means:** either the alteration removed too much seam allowance, or the fitting happened standing still and never tested the show's most physical moment.

**First question:** "Show me — can you do [the fight/dance/reach] in it right now?"

**Data to pull:** the alteration record (what was taken in, by how much); whether the fitting included a motion test; the seam-allowance margin remaining on the piece.

## 4. Sweat staining or odor visible on a costume after 3+ performances with no clean logged

**Usually means:** the laundry rotation slipped under schedule pressure — often during a run of two-show days when wear doubles but the rotation didn't adjust.

**First question:** "How many performances has this piece done since its last clean, and does that match the threshold on the rotation log?"

**Data to pull:** the laundry/repair rotation log for the piece; performance count since last clean; whether it's a two-show-day week (wear-rate doubling).

## 5. Rigged closure (velcro, magnet, snap tape) past ~150 cycles or showing visible fatigue

**Usually means:** the piece is past its reliable service life and is now a live failure risk during a quick change, not a hypothetical one.

**First question:** "What's the cycle count on this rigging, and when did we last physically inspect it rather than just glance at it?"

**Data to pull:** the tracked cycle count on the piece (taped/marked, not estimated); date of last physical inspection; whether a spare or backup closure is staged.

## 6. Continuity photo missing or outdated for a scene (film/TV)

**Usually means:** either the photo was skipped under time pressure on a busy shoot day, or the costume state changed (rolled sleeve, undone button, added prop) after the photo was taken and nobody re-shot it.

**First question:** "When was this scene's continuity photo taken — before or after the last costume adjustment on set?"

**Data to pull:** the wardrobe bible entry (photo timestamp) for the scene; the script supervisor's notes for the same scene; any costume notes logged by the set costumer during the take.

## 7. Track sheet timing or assignments exist only in one dresser's memory

**Usually means:** the show has a single point of failure — an absence, illness, or cast swap on that dresser's night breaks a change nobody else can execute correctly.

**First question:** "If you were out sick tonight, could someone else run this change from the posted sheet alone?"

**Data to pull:** the posted track sheet for the change in question; date of its last update; whether a second crew member has actually run it from paper (not been told verbally).

## 8. A costume piece is pulled from stock and put into rotation without a condition check

**Usually means:** the piece's actual wear state (existing repairs, fabric fatigue, prior alterations for a different body) is unknown, so any failure during the run gets misdiagnosed as new damage.

**First question:** "Was this piece condition-checked and logged before it went into this show's rotation, or was it pulled straight from storage?"

**Data to pull:** the stock pull/check-in record; prior show history on the piece if available; visible wear notes from the check.

## 9. Breakaway or rigged stage business (tear-away costume, quick-reveal) rehearsed only in a costume shop mockup

**Usually means:** the rigging was validated on a test garment or in a calm rehearsal room, not the actual performance costume under actual stage lighting, actor adrenaline, and blocking — the two environments don't reliably match.

**First question:** "Has this rigging been run on the actual performance costume, on the actual actor, at full performance speed, more than once?"

**Data to pull:** the rigging test log (mockup vs. actual-garment runs); number of full-speed runs completed before opening; any near-miss or partial-failure notes from tech.

## 10. Laundry rotation threshold set by calendar day instead of performance count

**Usually means:** the rotation will under-clean during high-frequency weeks (two-show days, added performances) and over-clean (wasting shop time) during dark weeks — calendar-based scheduling doesn't track actual wear.

**First question:** "Is this piece's clean threshold set by performance count or by the calendar, and how many performances has it actually done this week?"

**Data to pull:** the performance calendar for the week (single vs. two-show days); the piece's cumulative performance count since last clean; the stated threshold on the rotation log.
