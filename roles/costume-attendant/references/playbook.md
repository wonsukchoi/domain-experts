# Playbook

Filled examples a costume attendant actually runs, with structure and plausible numbers — adapt the specifics, keep the structure.

## 1. Costume plot (filled excerpt)

The master grid every downstream document derives from. Built by the designer/supervisor during prep, maintained by the attendant through the run.

| Scene | Actor | Costume piece | On/off | Change type | Time budget | Dresser assigned |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1.2 | Ella | Rag dress + apron | On (top of show, preset) | Dressing room | n/a | — |
| 1.4 | Ella | Apron off | Off | Onstage, visible | n/a (in scene) | Self |
| 2.1 | Ella | Rag dress → ball gown + heels + wig | Full change | **Quick change, 35 sec blackout** | 23 sec planned / 35 sec window | Dresser A, Dresser B, Wig runner |
| 2.3 | Ella | Ball gown → torn ball gown (midnight) | Costume-to-costume | Onstage, visible (rigged tear) | n/a (scripted stage business) | Self, rigged breakaway |
| 2.5 | Ella | Torn gown → rag dress | Full change | Booth, 90 sec (scene change) | 40 sec planned / 90 sec window | Dresser A |

**Build Out row** (bottom of the plot, added during tech): which pieces are built, which are pulled from stock, which are rentals due back by a hard date — this row is what the supervisor checks against the shop's build schedule weekly.

## 2. Quick-change choreography worksheet

Use for any window under 60 seconds. One worksheet per change; post the finished version as the track sheet.

```
CHANGE: Act 2 rag dress → ball gown          WINDOW: 35 sec (LX 47 blackout)
GARMENT COUNT: 3 (dress, shoes, wig)          CREW: 2 dressers + 1 wig runner

STEP 1 — List every task and its solo duration:
  a. Rag dress off (back zip)         8 sec
  b. Ball gown on (zip, was 14 hooks) 12 sec
  c. Shoe swap (magnetic strap)       6 sec
  d. Wig swap                         10 sec

STEP 2 — Mark dependencies (what MUST happen before what):
  b depends on a (can't zip gown until rag dress is off)
  c depends on: feet clear of rag dress hem (~partway through a)
  d depends on: nothing else — independent from 0:00

STEP 3 — Assign to parallel chains, longest chain = critical path:
  Chain 1 (Dresser A): a → b = 8 + 12 = 20 sec  <- CRITICAL PATH
  Chain 2 (Dresser B): c, starts at 0:08, ends 0:14
  Chain 3 (Wig runner): d, starts at 0:00, ends 0:10

STEP 4 — Apply margin (15% minimum on critical path):
  20 sec x 1.15 = 23 sec planned

STEP 5 — Compare to window:
  35 sec window - 23 sec planned = 12 sec margin. PASS.
  (If margin is negative or under ~10%, re-decompose: split a task further,
  change a closure type, or ask for a longer scripted window before adding crew.)
```

## 4. Laundry / repair rotation log

Track by performance count, not by calendar — a Saturday two-show day doubles wear versus a dark Monday.

| Piece | Performances since last clean | Clean threshold | Performances since last repair check | Status |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Ball gown (bodice, sweat contact) | 3 | Every 4 (hand-wash + air) | 6 | Zipper pull replaced wk 3 |
| Rag dress | 9 | Every 8 (machine-safe) | 9 | Due tonight |
| Ankle-strap magnets (rigging) | — | Inspect every 20 | 38 | **Over threshold — replace before next put-in** |
| Ball gown wig | 12 | Reset/comb every 6, deep clean every 20 | 12 | Reset due |

**Rule of thumb:** any rigged closure (velcro, magnet, snap tape) gets a hard inspection at 20-performance intervals and mandatory replacement by 150 total cycles or first sign of visible fatigue, whichever comes first — track cumulative count on the piece itself with tape and a marker, not from memory.

## 5. Put-in checklist (new actor into an existing track)

Run this in full before an understudy or replacement's first performance — never assume a "quick note session" covers it.

1. Walk the full costume plot with the new actor: what they wear, in what order, with what's assigned to them to self-manage vs. dresser-assisted.
2. Time every quick change on this specific actor's body — a change built for one actor's proportions is not guaranteed to hit the same numbers on another (different zipper length, different reach, different comfort with being touched quickly).
3. Confirm fit on every piece under motion, not just standing — have the actor run the show's most physical moment in costume before the put-in performance, not during it.
4. Walk the actor through every self-managed quick change or onstage costume business (rigged tears, prop pins) at half-speed, then full-speed, minimum twice each.
5. Update the track sheet with the new actor's name and any timing deltas; re-post at the booth.
6. Flag any piece that needs an alteration for this body to the shop before the put-in date — never the morning of.
