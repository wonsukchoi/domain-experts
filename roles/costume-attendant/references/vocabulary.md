# Vocabulary

Terms generalists conflate that a wardrobe crew keeps sharply separate. Each: definition, how a practitioner actually uses it in a sentence, and the common misuse.

### Costume plot vs. track sheet

The **costume plot** is the master actor-by-scene-by-garment grid built during prep — what everyone wears, in what order, and where the changes fall. The **track sheet** is the per-booth, per-dresser execution document derived from it, with cue numbers, timings, and task assignments.

**In use:** "The plot says she changes in scene 2.1 — the track sheet is what tells Dresser A and B who does what in the 35 seconds they've got."

**Common misuse:** treating the plot itself as sufficient backstage documentation. The plot tells you *what* changes where; without a track sheet, nobody knows *how* to execute it under time pressure.

### Quick change

A costume change with a scripted or blocking-imposed time window short enough (generally under 60 seconds, often under 30) that it requires dedicated crew, pre-staged garments, and rehearsal as its own piece of stage business — distinct from an ordinary dressing-room change with no time pressure.

**In use:** "That's not a costume change, that's a quick change — it needs its own choreography worksheet, not just a note on the plot."

**Common misuse:** calling any costume change a "quick change" regardless of the time budget, which dilutes the term and causes ordinary changes to get over-resourced while genuinely tight ones get under-rehearsed.

### Preset / basket list

The pre-show checklist of exactly what garments, in what state (zippers pre-started, straps pre-fastened), go into a specific numbered basket or booth before the house opens — distinct from the costume plot, which is the show-wide map, not a pre-show setup instruction.

**In use:** "Check the basket list before you check the plot — if basket 4 isn't set, the plot doesn't matter."

**Common misuse:** assuming the costume plot doubles as a preset instruction. The plot doesn't specify pre-staged states (a zipper started to first catch, a shoe strap loosened) that make the actual quick change possible.

### Wardrobe bible

The film/TV continuity reference: dated, timestamped photos of every costume state per scene (before and after takes), with notes on what's rolled, tucked, buttoned, or pinned, plus director's notes — the record that lets a scene reshot weeks later match exactly.

**In use:** "Pull the wardrobe bible for scene 14 before we reshoot the pickup — I need to see exactly how far that sleeve was rolled."

**Common misuse:** treating it as optional documentation for a low-budget or single-day shoot. Continuity breaks are just as costly on a two-day shoot with a reshoot as on a six-month schedule; the bible is what makes any reshoot possible at all.

### Dresser vs. stitcher vs. costumer

A **dresser** assists performers into costume, tracks pieces, and runs quick changes during the show — a performance-time role. A **stitcher** builds and repairs garments, typically shop-based, not performance-facing. A **costumer** (film/TV usage) is a broader on-set role covering fitting, tracking, and continuity, closer to a dresser but with wider prep responsibilities.

**In use:** "That's a stitcher fix, not something a dresser can do mid-show — tape it and flag it for tomorrow's shop call."

**Common misuse:** using the titles interchangeably across mediums. Theater's "dresser" and film's "set costumer" cover overlapping but not identical duties, and union job descriptions (IATSE locals) define them separately with real pay and duty implications.

### Breakaway / rigged costume

A garment intentionally modified so it can be torn, opened, or transformed onstage as scripted business (a tear-away jacket, a reveal costume) — engineered fragility, not accidental wear, with its own inspection and replacement cycle independent of the rest of the garment's life.

**In use:** "The breakaway seam is rated for about 150 tears before the velcro backing fails — we're at 140, order the replacement now."

**Common misuse:** treating a breakaway's visible intact appearance as proof it's still reliable. Rigged fragility fails at the rigging point, which is often hidden by design, not at a visibly worn spot.

### Dress shield

A removable, washable underarm liner sewn or pinned into a costume to absorb sweat before it reaches the outer fabric — a maintenance tool, not a fit or design element.

**In use:** "Swap the dress shields before tonight's show, not the whole costume — that's what they're there for."

**Common misuse:** skipping dress shields on "just a background piece" costume, which shortens that piece's life just as fast as a lead's costume; sweat doesn't check the actor's billing.

### Fitting vs. put-in

A **fitting** happens during build, with the costume shop, to construct and adjust a garment to an actor's body before it's finished. A **put-in** happens during the run, when a new actor (understudy, replacement, swing) is walked through an already-existing track and its timings — a performance-readiness process, not a construction one.

**In use:** "She's already been fitted for the gown — what she needs now is a full put-in on the quick change before Thursday."

**Common misuse:** assuming a fitting alone prepares an actor for the show. A well-fitted garment can still fail a performer who's never run the actual quick-change choreography on their own body and timing.

### Distressing vs. damage

**Distressing** is intentional, controlled aging or wear applied to a costume (tears, staining, fading) to match a character or story need, done by the shop with reversible or controlled techniques. **Damage** is unplanned wear or failure from performance use. The two require opposite responses: distressing gets preserved and matched if repaired; damage gets fixed and, if possible, prevented from recurring.

**In use:** "Don't clean that stain out, it's distressing — check the build notes before you touch anything that looks damaged."

**Common misuse:** a new or substitute dresser "fixing" intentional distressing because it looks like damage, erasing design work that then has to be redone by the shop.

### Costume plot Build Out row

The bottom row of a costume plot, added during tech, tracking which pieces are built new, pulled from stock, or rented, along with hard return dates for rentals — the row the supervisor checks against the shop's build schedule and budget.

**In use:** "Check the Build Out row before you promise a rental extension — that jacket's due back Friday regardless of the run schedule."

**Common misuse:** ignoring rental return dates because "the show's still running" — a missed return date can trigger late fees or loss of the piece mid-run, forcing an emergency rebuild.

### Cycle count (rigging)

The running tally of how many times a rigged closure (velcro, magnet, snap tape, breakaway seam) has been used, tracked physically on the piece — the basis for scheduled replacement, distinct from calendar age or visible condition.

**In use:** "It doesn't matter that it looks fine — cycle count says we're past the inspection threshold, pull it and check the stitching underneath."

**Common misuse:** relying on visual inspection alone. Rigged closures typically fail at the mechanism, not at a visible surface, so a piece can look intact right up to the performance it fails.
