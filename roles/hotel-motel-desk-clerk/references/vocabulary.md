# Hotel/Motel Desk Clerk — Vocabulary

### Walk
Relocating an arriving guest with a confirmed reservation to another property because no room is available at check-in.
**In use:** "We're going to have to walk two guests tonight — pull the priority list."
**Common misuse:** Used loosely for any room-change request; a walk specifically means the guest leaves the property entirely, not a room swap within the same hotel.

### PMS (property management system)
The system of record for reservations, rates, room status, and guest profiles.
**In use:** "Check the PMS before you tell the guest their rate is wrong."
**Common misuse:** Treated as just a booking calendar; it's the authoritative record for rate and channel-mapping disputes, not a secondary reference.

### Oversell / overbook
Selling more rooms than physically exist, calculated against an expected no-show/cancellation rate.
**In use:** "Tonight's a 2-room oversell based on this Tuesday's typical no-show rate."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to always be a mistake; it's a deliberate, calculated revenue-management strategy that only becomes a problem when the actual no-show rate runs lower than forecast.

### Walk-priority list
The property's defined order for deciding which guests get walked when an oversell shortfall materializes.
**In use:** "Run the walk-priority list against tonight's arrivals before 6pm, not after."
**Common misuse:** Confused with simple first-come-first-served; the list is based on loyalty tier, length of stay, and reservation timing, applied proactively before the situation is live.

### Compensation authority
The dollar or comp ceiling a front-desk clerk can authorize without escalating to a supervisor.
**In use:** "That request is inside my authority — I can comp the breakfast without checking with the manager."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a soft guideline to stretch when a guest seems upset; it's a hard ceiling, and the discipline is escalating promptly at the limit, not exceeding it.

### Rate code
The specific code attached to a reservation that determines the rate charged (e.g., a corporate code, promo code, or package code).
**In use:** "The rate code shows a promo that didn't apply — that's the source of the discrepancy."
**Common misuse:** Conflated with the rate itself; the code is the underlying reason for the rate, and mismatches at the code level are the actual diagnostic signal.

### Channel-mapping error
A discrepancy between what a guest booked through a third-party channel (an OTA) and what loaded into the property's PMS.
**In use:** "This isn't guest error — it's a channel-mapping error from the OTA feed."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to be rare; channel-mapping errors are a common, systemic source of rate disputes and should be the default hypothesis before assuming guest error.

### Inspected (housekeeping status)
The housekeeping-workflow status confirming a room has been cleaned AND checked by a supervisor or lead, distinct from merely "clean."
**In use:** "Don't assign 412 yet — it's clean but not inspected."
**Common misuse:** Treated as equivalent to "clean"; a room marked only "clean" hasn't passed the final quality check and assigning it early risks a guest finding an issue the inspection would have caught.

### Length of stay (LOS)
The total number of nights a guest is booked for at the property.
**In use:** "She's a 5-night LOS — she outranks the 1-night walk-in on the priority list."
**Common misuse:** Confused with loyalty tier; LOS and loyalty status are separate, independent factors in walk-priority decisions.

### Stayover
A guest who is not checking out today and will occupy their room again tonight.
**In use:** "The shortfall got worse because a stayover extended unexpectedly instead of checking out."
**Common misuse:** Confused with "arrival"; a stayover doesn't need a new room assignment but does hold inventory that affects the arrivals-vs-availability calculation.

### RevPAR (revenue per available room)
ADR (average daily rate) multiplied by occupancy percentage — the property-level metric that oversell and walk decisions ultimately serve.
**In use:** "The oversell strategy exists to protect RevPAR against no-shows, not to inconvenience guests."
**Common misuse:** A desk clerk isn't optimizing RevPAR directly — that's the lodging manager's job — but understanding it explains why the oversell policy exists, which shapes how a clerk narrates a walk to a guest.

### Walk-priority override
A documented exception (medical, family emergency, disability accommodation) that supersedes the standard walk-priority order.
**In use:** "She has a documented medical need — that's an override, skip the standard list for her."
**Common misuse:** Applied too broadly to any guest who seems distressed; a genuine override requires a documented, specific circumstance, not general upset about being walked.
