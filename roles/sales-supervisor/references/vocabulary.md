# Vocabulary

Terms generalists misuse or flatten together, kept sharply separate by a practitioner.

### Lead measure vs. lag measure
A **lead measure** (dials, connects, quotes sent) is a leading indicator a rep can control this week; a **lag measure** (bookings, revenue) is the outcome that shows up weeks later and can't be directly acted on — only diagnosed backward through the lead measures that produced it.
**In use:** "Bookings are the lag measure — don't coach the number itself, pull the lead-measure chain and find which stage actually broke."
**Common misuse:** Treating a missed lag number as something to coach directly ("try harder to close more"), instead of tracing it back to the specific lead measure that collapsed.

### Recoverable draw vs. non-recoverable draw
A **recoverable draw** is an advance against future commissions that must be earned back and reconciled at settlement; a **non-recoverable draw** functions as a guaranteed minimum the rep keeps regardless of commissions earned. Only a recoverable draw creates a balance that can ever offset future pay — and even then, never a departing rep's final paycheck.
**In use:** "His plan says recoverable draw, but we can't take the $2,150 balance out of his last check — that's a wage-deduction problem, not a collections problem."
**Common misuse:** Assuming "recoverable" means the company can deduct the unearned balance from any future payment, including final pay, without checking state wage law.

### Abandonment rate (TSR)
The percentage of outbound calls connected to a live person where no live agent becomes available within the required window, measured per campaign over a rolling 30 days under the FTC's Telemarketing Sales Rule; capped at roughly 3% under the regulatory safe harbor.
**In use:** "Abandonment's at 4.2% on Rep E's queue — that gets fixed today, before we touch his dial-volume target."
**Common misuse:** Treating abandonment rate as an internal productivity metric to optimize for lower agent idle time, rather than a compliance ceiling that overrides any activity target.

### Ramp quota / ramp schedule
A stairstep quota (commonly 33/67/100 over a 60–90 day window) applied to a new hire instead of the flat team quota, reflecting that a rep can't reasonably produce full output before their pipeline, territory knowledge, and account relationships exist.
**In use:** "He's at 62% of his ramp target, not 42% of full quota — that's a monitor-and-support case, not a PIP case."
**Common misuse:** Reporting a ramping rep's attainment against the flat team quota by default, making every new hire look like an underperformer for their first quarter regardless of actual trajectory.

### Book (of business)
The set of accounts currently assigned to a given rep, measured by account count and trailing revenue — the practitioner's shorthand for "what this rep actually has to sell against," distinct from quota, which is a target independent of what the book can support.
**In use:** "Her book's thin — 29 accounts against a 36-account team average — before we talk about her numbers, we talk about whether the book can hit quota at all."
**Common misuse:** Comparing rep performance purely on attainment percentage without checking whether the underlying books are remotely comparable in size or quality.

### MEDDIC / MEDDPICC
A deal-qualification framework (Metrics, Economic buyer, Decision criteria, Decision process, Identify pain, Champion — plus Paper process and Competition in the extended version) used to force explicit answers on complex deals before they're trusted in a forecast.
**In use:** "We don't have an economic buyer identified on this one — it's not qualified yet, no matter what stage the CRM says it's in."
**Common misuse:** Running the full framework on every deal, including small reorders, turning qualification into overhead reps route around by leaving fields blank.

### Connect rate
The percentage of dials that reach a live person (as distinct from a voicemail, disconnect, or wrong number) — the metric that isolates list quality from rep effort, since dial volume can be on target while connect rate collapses entirely on data quality.
**In use:** "Dial volume's fine, connect rate's the problem — get him a fresh list before assuming he's not working hard enough."
**Common misuse:** Using dial volume alone as the activity health check, missing that a rep can hit every dial target on a dead list and produce almost nothing.

### Outside sales exemption (FLSA)
A federal wage-and-hour exemption from minimum wage and overtime for employees whose primary duty is making sales away from the employer's place of business, on a customary and regular basis — a classification test, not a job title.
**In use:** "If he's spending most weeks at the inside desk now, we need to revisit whether the outside-sales exemption still fits, not just keep calling him an outside rep."
**Common misuse:** Assuming the "outside sales rep" title alone preserves the exemption regardless of how the rep's actual weekly time is spent.

### House account
An account explicitly excluded from any individual rep's book and commission, typically handled directly by the supervisor or above — usually a strategic, high-risk, or historically contested account kept off the standard territory map.
**In use:** "That's a house account — nobody gets commission credit on it, and any pricing exception goes through me, not through territory rules."
**Common misuse:** Assuming any large or important account is automatically a house account, when most large accounts are still assigned and commissioned normally.

### Pipeline coverage ratio
Total open pipeline value divided by the remaining quota for the period — a leading indicator of whether a rep or team even has enough opportunity in play to hit the number, independent of how well any single deal is being worked.
**In use:** "Her coverage ratio's 1.8x on a quarter that usually needs 3x to convert at our win rate — the problem isn't her closing skill, it's that she doesn't have enough pipeline yet."
**Common misuse:** Treating any pipeline dollar figure as reassuring without checking it against the win rate needed to convert that coverage into the remaining quota.

### DSO (Days Sales Outstanding)
The average number of days it takes an account to pay an invoice after it's issued — the metric that should be checked before approving any discount or extended-terms request, since a low-margin exception on a slow-paying account compounds two problems at once.
**In use:** "Before I approve net-60 on this account, pull their DSO — if they're already running 75 days on net-30, extending terms just formalizes a problem we already have."
**Common misuse:** Approving a term extension based on the relationship or deal size alone, without checking the account's actual payment history first.

### Account whitespace
The portion of an account's total addressable spend with the distributor or manufacturer that isn't currently being captured — the basis for an upsell/cross-sell target, distinct from the account's current revenue, which only shows what's already being sold.
**In use:** "Current revenue's $40K, but their total category spend across all suppliers is closer to $150K — that whitespace is the actual growth target, not the $40K baseline."
**Common misuse:** Setting growth targets off the account's current revenue alone, without any estimate of the total spend the account could plausibly be capturing.

### Predictive dialer vs. power dialer
A **predictive dialer** places multiple calls per available agent based on statistical pacing, generating higher throughput but real abandonment risk if pacing is set too aggressively; a **power dialer** places one call per agent per cycle, slower but near-zero abandonment risk.
**In use:** "Drop him back to power dialer until the abandonment number's clean again — we can revisit predictive pacing once it's been under 2% for two weeks."
**Common misuse:** Treating dialer type as interchangeable tooling rather than a direct lever on compliance risk, and defaulting to predictive mode purely for the throughput gain.

### PIP (Performance Improvement Plan)
A formal, documented, time-boxed remediation step with specific measurable targets — the last step in the fix-order sequence (process fix, coaching, territory adjustment, then PIP), not a first response to a missed number.
**In use:** "We haven't touched his list quality or his book size yet — a PIP right now would be documenting the wrong problem."
**Common misuse:** Opening a PIP as the default response to any quota miss, skipping the cheaper diagnostic steps that might show the shortfall wasn't a performance problem at all.

### Quota attainment vs. quota credit
**Attainment** is the raw percentage of quota achieved; **quota credit** is what's actually counted toward that percentage after adjustments (split deals, house-account exclusions, returns/chargebacks) — the two numbers can diverge by 10+ points on the same set of closed deals.
**In use:** "His raw bookings hit 105%, but after the split-credit adjustment on the Meridian account, his actual quota credit is 91%."
**Common misuse:** Quoting raw attainment in a performance conversation without checking whether split deals or exclusions change the number that's actually going to be used to evaluate the rep.
