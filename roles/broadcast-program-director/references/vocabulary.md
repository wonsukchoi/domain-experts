# Broadcast Program Director — Vocabulary

### Cume (Cumulative Audience)
The unduplicated number of different people who tuned in for at least one measured interval during a period (usually a week), regardless of how long they stayed.
**In use:** "Cume held at 268,000 — we're still being found, we're just not keeping them."
**Common misuse:** Treating cume as a general "popularity" number interchangeable with AQH or share; a station can have strong cume and weak share if listeners sample it and leave quickly.

### AQH (Average Quarter-Hour)
The average number of persons listening/viewing during a typical 15-minute period within a daypart, computed from cume and time spent.
**In use:** "AQH persons dropped from 13,100 to 7,700 — that's almost entirely a TSL story, not a cume story."
**Common misuse:** Reading AQH movement without decomposing it into the cume term and the TSL term, which produces the wrong fix for the wrong cause.

### TSL (Time Spent Listening/Viewing)
The average amount of time an individual listener/viewer spends with the station or program during a period, expressed in hours per week.
**In use:** "TSL fell 38% — that's the number that explains the whole share drop."
**Common misuse:** Treating TSL as a fixed trait of the audience rather than a lever the clock and spot load directly control.

### Hot clock / format clock
The documented minute-by-minute structure of an hour — where music categories, talk breaks, liners, and stopsets sit — that a live or voice-tracked host executes identically every hour.
**In use:** "Rebuild the hot clock so no stopset touches :00–:05 or :55–:00."
**Common misuse:** Treating the clock as just the music rotation chart, when it's the full replicable structure including breaks, liners, and protected windows — the actual format product.

### PPM (Portable People Meter)
Nielsen Audio's passive electronic metering device that detects inaudible codes embedded in broadcast audio, recording actual exposure rather than relying on listener recall.
**In use:** "This is a PPM market — we win or lose on continuous tuning, not on what people say they like in a survey."
**Common misuse:** Applying diary-era tactics (memorable stunts, recall-driven contests) in a PPM market, where moment-to-moment retention matters more than what a listener would later write down as a favorite.

### Stopset
A block of commercials, promos, and liners aired together, typically at a set position within the clock.
**In use:** "Stopset 2 runs 4:00 max — three spots and one promo, no more."
**Common misuse:** Treating stopset length as purely a sales-department decision; the length and placement are a programming decision with a direct, measurable retention cost.

### Quarter-hour maintenance
The programming practice of structuring content so listeners already tuned in stay through a break and into the next quarter hour, as distinct from attracting new listeners.
**In use:** "This is a quarter-hour maintenance problem — protect the exits, don't launch a new promotion."
**Common misuse:** Conflating quarter-hour maintenance with cume-building; the two require opposite tactics (protecting current listeners' habits vs. reaching people who've never sampled the station).

### Sweeps
Historical Nielsen measurement periods (November, February, May, and in some markets July) used in diary-based and traditional people-meter markets to set local ad rates and evaluate programming.
**In use:** "This market moved to continuous PPM years ago — there's no sweeps event left to plan around."
**Common misuse:** Assuming sweeps still govern local rate-setting in every market; continuous Nielsen Audio PPM and Local People Meter measurement has replaced sweeps-based rate-setting in most metered markets, though the sweeps mental model still applies to some diary markets and network prime-time development cycles.

### Voice-tracking
Pre-recording a host's between-song content (in minutes, not hours) so it can be assembled into a live-sounding shift without the talent present in real time, often across multiple stations in a cluster.
**In use:** "The midday shift is voice-tracked out of the flagship — check TSL before and after the switch."
**Common misuse:** Assuming voice-tracking is audience-neutral compared to a live local show; it removes real-time localism (weather, traffic, timely references) that measurably affects TSL if not compensated for.

### Barter syndication
A programming-supply arrangement where a syndicator provides a show in exchange for a share of the local ad inventory within it, rather than a cash license fee.
**In use:** "This barter deal only clears if those avails would otherwise sell below our local rate card."
**Common misuse:** Evaluating a barter show purely on its national ratings, without pricing it against the local avails it displaces.

### Dayparting / lead-in effect
Scheduling content by time-of-day audience composition (dayparting), and the tendency of a strong preceding program to elevate the rating of the show that follows it (lead-in effect).
**In use:** "Put the untested comedy behind the established hit — it needs the lead-in more than the tentpole does."
**Common misuse:** Crediting a new show's early rating entirely to its own quality when much of it is inherited from a strong lead-in, or blaming a show's weak rating on itself when a poor lead-in or a competitor's stunt explains most of it.

### Sponsorship identification / payola
The FCC requirement (47 CFR §73.1212) that any broadcast content aired in exchange for money, service, or other consideration be disclosed on air; undisclosed compensation for airplay or mentions is payola (or plugola for non-airplay promotional consideration).
**In use:** "That segment needs a sponsorship ID before it airs, not after legal asks about it."
**Common misuse:** Assuming only direct cash payment counts as payola; in-kind consideration (trips, merchandise, cross-promotion value) triggers the same disclosure requirement.

### E/I (Educational and Informational) programming
Children's core programming that satisfies the Children's Television Act of 1990's requirement (47 CFR §73.671) that commercial TV stations air a minimum weekly quota of educational/informational content for children.
**In use:** "We're at 2.5 hours of E/I this quarter — that's short of the requirement before the report's due."
**Common misuse:** Treating any children's-adjacent programming as automatically qualifying, when it must meet the FCC's specific educational-purpose and scheduling criteria to count toward the quota.

### Share vs. rating
Rating is the audience for a station/program as a percentage of the total population in the survey area; share is that same audience as a percentage of only those actually listening/viewing at that time.
**In use:** "Our share is strong in mornings because total listening is low then — the rating tells a different story."
**Common misuse:** Quoting share as if it reflects a station's absolute audience size, when it's relative only to however much of the total population happens to be tuned in at all during that period.
