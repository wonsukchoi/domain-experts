# Vocabulary

Terms generalists use loosely that a foreman keeps sharply separate. Each: definition, how a practitioner actually uses it, and the common misuse.

## Competent person vs. qualified person

**Competent person** (OSHA, 29 CFR 1926.32(f)) is capable of identifying existing and predictable hazards in the specific conditions present and has authority to take prompt corrective measures — a site-specific, task-specific designation, not a certificate. **Qualified person** has a recognized degree, certificate, or professional standing demonstrating the ability to solve problems in a subject — a credential-based standard used for things like crane operation or engineered design review.

*Practitioner usage:* "I'm the competent person on this excavation because I'm the one who can spot the sloughing and stop the work — that's not the same as being a qualified structural engineer if we need a shoring design."

*Common misuse:* treating a safety certificate or a job title as automatically satisfying the competent-person requirement, when OSHA's test is functional (can this person identify the hazard here, today, and act on it) not credential-based.

## Crew-hour vs. man-hour

**Crew-hour** is one hour of output from the assigned crew as a unit (a given headcount and mix), used to track output against a task budget. **Man-hour** (or person-hour) is one hour of one individual's labor, used for total labor-cost accounting.

*Practitioner usage:* "We're budgeted at 225 SF per crew-hour with this 3-person crew — don't confuse that with per-person output when you're comparing to a different crew size."

*Common misuse:* comparing a crew-hour rate from a 3-person crew directly against a 5-person crew's output without normalizing crew size first.

## Float vs. slack

**Float** (or total float) is the amount of time a task can slip without delaying the project finish date; **free float** is how much it can slip without delaying the next task. Both are schedule-network terms with a specific computed value. **Slack** is used loosely in daily speech to mean "extra time," often without anyone having actually computed it.

*Practitioner usage:* "That ceiling grid has four days of float — let the plumber go first. The corridor drywall has none; don't touch its sequence."

*Common misuse:* saying a task "has some slack" as a guess rather than reading the computed float off the schedule, then making a sequencing call on a guess.

## Look-ahead schedule vs. master schedule

**Master schedule** is the full-project schedule showing the critical path and major milestones, owned by the superintendent or PM. **Look-ahead schedule** is a short rolling window (2–3 weeks at the super level, 1–2 days at the crew level) showing near-term task detail the master schedule doesn't carry.

*Practitioner usage:* "The master schedule says corridor drywall finishes Friday — my two-day look-ahead is what tells me the electrician needs that wall open Thursday morning."

*Common misuse:* managing only off the master schedule and missing a same-week sequencing conflict that only shows up at look-ahead granularity.

## Near-miss vs. recordable incident

**Near-miss** is an event that could have caused injury or damage but didn't. **Recordable incident** is an injury or illness meeting a regulator's specific recording criteria (beyond first aid, involving lost time, medical treatment, etc.) — a defined legal category, not a severity judgment call.

*Practitioner usage:* "Log the tipped board stack as a near-miss even though nobody got hurt — that's exactly the kind of event the recordable count won't capture until after someone does."

*Common misuse:* only logging events that already meet the recordable threshold, which throws away the leading-indicator data the near-miss category exists to capture.

## RFI vs. submittal

**RFI** (request for information) is a formal question asked when the drawings or specs are unclear or conflict with field conditions, requiring a documented answer from design/ownership. **Submittal** is a proposed product, shop drawing, or material sample submitted for approval before installation.

*Practitioner usage:* "The wall-height conflict with the duct run is an RFI, not a submittal issue — we need an answer from the engineer, not approval of a product."

*Common misuse:* routing a genuine field conflict through the submittal process (or vice versa), which sends it to the wrong reviewer and adds days to a decision that's blocking today's sequencing.

## Punch list vs. deficiency

**Punch list** is the list of remaining minor items to complete or correct before final acceptance of a scope, typically compiled near substantial completion. **Deficiency** is any specific instance of work not meeting spec or code, which can be flagged at any point in the job, not just at closeout.

*Practitioner usage:* "That's a deficiency today, not a punch-list item — we fix it now, before it gets buried under three more trades' work and becomes a much bigger tear-out."

*Common misuse:* deferring an in-progress deficiency to "clean up on the punch list later," letting subsequent trades build over a problem that gets exponentially more expensive to correct once covered.

## Crew mix / crew ratio

The proportion of skilled trade labor to support labor (laborers, material handlers) assigned to a task, sized to keep skilled trades doing skilled work rather than hauling and staging their own material.

*Practitioner usage:* "We're crewing this at 3 hangers to 1 laborer today specifically because the staging distance is long — drop the laborer and the hangers are back to carrying board."

*Common misuse:* treating crew mix as a fixed ratio applied uniformly across tasks, instead of deriving it from the specific task's staging distance, access, and rate-limiting resource.

## Stop-work authority

The recognized authority — held by the competent person and, on most modern sites, by any worker — to halt work immediately when an unaddressed hazard is present, independent of schedule pressure or chain-of-command approval.

*Practitioner usage:* "I don't need the super's sign-off to stop the pour when the shoring shifted — that's exactly what stop-work authority is for."

*Common misuse:* treating stop-work authority as something that requires escalation and approval before acting, which defeats the purpose of putting the authority at the point of hazard rather than up the chain.
