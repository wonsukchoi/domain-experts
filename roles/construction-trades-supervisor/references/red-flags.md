# Red flags

Smell tests a veteran foreman catches walking the site before lunch. Format for each: what it usually means, the first question to ask, and the data to pull.

## 1. Crew running >20% below budgeted units-per-crew-hour for 2+ consecutive days

**Usually means:** a staging, access, or crew-mix bottleneck — not a skill or effort problem, and rarely fixed by adding headcount.

**First question:** "Walk me through yesterday's hour-by-hour — how much of it was actually hanging/pouring/pulling wire versus getting set up to do it?"

**Data to pull:** the daily labor-unit log for the last 3 days; material staging location vs. point of installation; whether crew mix matches the task (e.g., enough laborers per skilled tradesperson to keep them from hauling their own material).

## 2. Competent person not designated or inspection not logged before today's work started

**Usually means:** the trigger condition (excavation depth, fall-protection height, scaffold work, confined space) was assumed unchanged from yesterday and skipped, or nobody was ever formally assigned the role.

**First question:** "Who signed today's inspection for this condition, and what time?"

**Data to pull:** the competent-person designation log; the specific trigger measurement (excavation depth, fall-protection zone height) as it stands today, not as of the last inspection.

## 3. Near-miss reports drop sharply with no change in task or hazard exposure

**Usually means:** the reporting channel got inconvenient, discouraged, or tied (explicitly or implicitly) to a scorecard that penalizes reporting — not that the site got safer.

**First question:** "What changed about how or where people file a near-miss, and when?"

**Data to pull:** monthly near-miss count against the same period's recordable-incident count and task mix; whether near-miss cards/app entries require a name or route through a supervisor who might discourage them.

## 4. A trade-sequencing conflict sits unresolved past the crew-level look-ahead window

**Usually means:** it surfaced too late to negotiate calmly, or nobody owned the decision and it's drifting toward "whoever shows up first wins."

**First question:** "Whose deadline is actually harder here — which one reschedules easily and which one doesn't?"

**Data to pull:** both trades' controlling deadlines (inspector calendar, delivery window, occupancy date); float remaining on each trade's task in the master schedule.

## 5. "Just get it done" schedule pressure applied against a documented safety hold

**Usually means:** someone upstream is treating a competent-person hold as a negotiable schedule risk rather than a legal stop, usually because the schedule impact hasn't been quantified and communicated yet.

**First question:** "What's the actual number of hours this hold costs, and have we told the superintendent today, in writing?"

**Data to pull:** the hold's documented trigger and duration; whether the schedule impact has been escalated same-day or is being absorbed silently.

## 6. Added crew requested to fix a productivity shortfall before root cause is identified

**Usually means:** headcount is being used as a default lever because it's the fastest visible action, when the actual constraint is staging, access, or an external delivery/placement rate that more people can't speed up.

**First question:** "If we doubled the crew tomorrow, what number would actually improve — and by how much?"

**Data to pull:** the rate-limiting resource for the task (delivery rate, placement rate, access schedule) versus current crew output; whether the requested addition changes that constraint at all.

## 7. Punch-list item on an already-passed inspection gets reopened by another trade without an RFI

**Usually means:** a downstream trade needed access through completed work and took it without documenting the reopening, creating a gap between as-built condition and the inspected record.

**First question:** "Who authorized reopening this, and is there a paper trail an inspector would accept?"

**Data to pull:** the original inspection sign-off; any RFI or change log entry for the reopened item; whether the original trade has been notified to re-close and re-verify.

## 8. Same near-miss type recurs at the same location without a logged corrective action

**Usually means:** the near-miss was reported but treated as an isolated event rather than a pattern, and whatever caused it (a staging choice, a missing barricade, a repeated shortcut) is still in place.

**First question:** "What did we change after the first one — and if the answer is nothing, why did we expect a different result?"

**Data to pull:** the near-miss log filtered by location and type over the job's duration; the corrective-action field for each prior entry at that location.

## 9. Subcontractor crew shows up with a headcount or trade mix that doesn't match today's task plan

**Usually means:** a scheduling miscommunication upstream, or the sub is spreading a crew thin across multiple jobs and this site got the leftover ratio.

**First question:** "Does this crew mix match what's needed for today's task, or are the skilled trades going to spend the day hauling their own material?"

**Data to pull:** today's task plan's required crew ratio (from the crew-sizing calculation); the actual crew that arrived, by trade and headcount.

## 10. Toolbox talk topic is generic or repeats verbatim for several days running

**Usually means:** the talk has become a compliance formality disconnected from what the crew is actually doing that day, which defeats its purpose as the first line of hazard communication.

**First question:** "What's different about today's task versus yesterday's, hazard-wise — and does the talk reflect that?"

**Data to pull:** the toolbox-talk log topics for the past 2 weeks against the actual daily task plan for the same period.
