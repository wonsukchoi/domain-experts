# Working vocabulary

Terms of art practitioners use precisely, that generalists misuse. Load this when writing or reviewing content that needs to sound like an actual AE firm manager, not a generalist describing one.

### Net multiplier
Revenue divided by direct labor cost on a project or firm-wide. The core profitability metric in AE practice.
**In use:** "We came in at a 2.6 multiplier on that job — that's below break-even once you account for our overhead rate."
**Common misuse:** Treated as interchangeable with "profit margin" — a multiplier of 3.0 isn't 300% margin, it's revenue that's 3x direct labor cost, with overhead and profit both coming out of the spread above 1.0x.

### Utilization rate
The percentage of an employee's available hours billed to client projects (vs. non-billable time: admin, marketing, PTO, internal meetings).
**In use:** "Her utilization dropped to 58% last month because she was staffed on the RFP response, which is non-billable."
**Common misuse:** Assumed higher is always better — utilization above ~78-80% sustained is itself a red flag for burnout and compressed review time, not a sign of health.

### Standard of care
The legal benchmark for professional negligence: what a reasonably prudent professional in the same discipline would have done with the same information, at the time, not in hindsight.
**In use:** "The question in a claim isn't whether the design was perfect, it's whether it met the standard of care given what we knew at the time."
**Common misuse:** Confused with "best possible design" or "zero defects" — standard of care is a floor defined by reasonable practice, not a ceiling defined by perfection.

### Additional services
Work outside the originally defined scope of a contract, billed separately (usually hourly) under a change order or supplemental agreement.
**In use:** "That's outside the DD scope we froze — I'll log it as additional services before we start the redesign."
**Common misuse:** Treated as optional paperwork rather than the mechanism that actually protects margin — work done without logging it as additional services is simply unbilled labor, indistinguishable from a scoping error on the books.

### Not-to-exceed (NTE)
An hourly fee structure with a contractual cap — the firm bills actual hours at agreed rates but can't invoice above the stated ceiling without a change order.
**In use:** "We're quoting CD hourly NTE at $156K since the program isn't fully frozen yet."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to fully protect the client the way lump-sum does, or fully protect the firm the way pure hourly does — NTE splits the risk: client is protected from unlimited billing, firm is protected from unbounded lump-sum exposure, but the firm still eats hours beyond the cap unless a change order is executed.

### Peer review (independent)
Technical review of a design element by a qualified professional not on the day-to-day project team, specifically to catch what the project team's familiarity with the design might miss.
**In use:** "The lateral system gets an independent peer review regardless of how the budget's tracking — that's a fixed line item, not a discretionary one."
**Common misuse:** Conflated with routine internal QA/QC by the project team itself — peer review's value comes specifically from the reviewer's independence from the day-to-day design decisions, not from a second look by someone who was already involved.

### Errors and omissions (E&O) insurance
Professional liability insurance covering claims that a design professional's negligent act, error, or omission caused a client (or third party) financial harm.
**In use:** "Check whether this project type is excluded under our current E&O policy before we take it on."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to cover any dissatisfaction with the outcome — E&O responds to alleged negligence (failure to meet standard of care), not to a client simply being unhappy with a design choice that was reasonably made.

### Backlog
Signed or highly probable future work not yet completed, typically expressed in months of revenue or hours at current staffing capacity.
**In use:** "We're sitting at 4.5 months of backlog in structural — that's healthy, but two more signed wins this quarter would push it past what we can staff without hiring."
**Common misuse:** Treated purely as a sales-pipeline health metric — backlog without a specific staffing plan behind it is a liability (overcommitment risk), not simply evidence of a strong sales function.

### Value engineering (VE)
A structured process to reduce construction cost by identifying design changes that maintain function/performance while lowering cost — distinct from simply cutting scope or quality.
**In use:** "The GC's VE list cuts the curtain wall spec — we need to confirm the substitution still meets the energy code before signing off."
**Common misuse:** Used as a euphemism for "cutting quality to hit budget" — genuine VE preserves the functional/performance requirement; a change that doesn't is a scope or quality reduction being mislabeled.
