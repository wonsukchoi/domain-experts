# Red flags

Smell tests a senior repairer catches on the estimate sheet or in the first ten minutes of teardown. Format for each: what it usually means, the first question to ask, and the data to pull before committing to a repair path.

## 1. Estimate written entirely from photos, no teardown scheduled

**Usually means:** the insurer's initial number assumes best-case hidden damage by construction — photo estimates can't see behind a bumper cover or inside a rail. Treating it as the repair budget instead of a placeholder guarantees an adversarial supplement conversation later.

**First question:** "Has this vehicle been torn down to bare structure in the damage zone yet?"

**Data to pull:** the original estimate's line items against what's actually visible with the bumper/trim off; whether the estimate includes any "not-included" operations the platform (Mitchell/CCC/Audatex) typically omits for this repair type.

## 2. Sectioning proposed with no OEM procedure citation

**Usually means:** someone is repeating a technique that worked on a different model or steel grade without checking whether this specific component and location is sectionable at all. On UHSS and boron-alloyed components this is a safety-system failure waiting to happen, not a shortcut.

**First question:** "Show me the OEM position statement or procedure page that authorizes sectioning at this exact location on this VIN's build."

**Data to pull:** the component's steel-strength class from the OEM's body construction chart; the manufacturer's published position statement for that part; whether a sectioning procedure exists at all for that location, or only full-assembly replacement is authorized.

## 3. Frame measurement report shows single-point readings only

**Usually means:** the report is being read one point at a time instead of as a span deviation between point pairs — a common misread that hides real deviation. A +4mm and −3mm pair looks like "both within tolerance" read separately but is 7mm of actual span error.

**First question:** "What's the span deviation between this point and its opposite reference point, not each one alone?"

**Data to pull:** the full measurement printout with both endpoints of each pair; the OEM-published tolerance for that specific point pair, not a generic shop default.

## 4. No ADAS calibration line on a repair that removed a bumper cover, windshield, or structural component near a sensor

**Usually means:** the estimate was written in a platform that doesn't auto-prompt for calibration on that operation code — a known, industry-documented gap (the pattern the Society of Collision Repair Specialists' Database Enhancement Gateway exists to catch), not evidence the calibration isn't needed.

**First question:** "Does the OEM procedure for this specific repair operation require a calibration, and has anyone checked — or did we just go by what the estimate line prompted?"

**Data to pull:** the OEM repair procedure for the exact operation performed; whether the vehicle has a forward camera, radar, or ultrasonic sensor in or referencing the affected zone; any pre-existing calibration fault codes from the pre-repair scan.

## 5. Insurer specifies aftermarket or recycled parts for a component with a "no repair, replace only with OEM" position statement

**Usually means:** the estimate is optimizing for part cost without checking whether the OEM has restricted that specific component to new OEM parts only — common on structural reinforcements, door rings, and safety-cage components.

**First question:** "Does the OEM position statement for this part restrict it to new OEM parts, and does that override the part sourcing on this estimate?"

**Data to pull:** the OEM's parts-sourcing restriction language for that specific component (if any); whether the part is structural/safety-related or cosmetic, since restrictions concentrate on the former.

## 6. Paint and materials calculated as a flat percentage of labor hours

**Usually means:** the shop or estimator is using a shortcut formula instead of the actual panel count, blend requirement, and material type for this specific repair — this under- or over-states the line depending on how many panels actually need refinishing, especially once a supplement adds an adjacent panel.

**First question:** "How many panels are actually being refinished and blended, and does the materials line reflect that count specifically?"

**Data to pull:** the panel-by-panel refinish plan; whether any panels were added to the plan after the original estimate (e.g., from a structural repair reopening an adjacent panel) without a corresponding materials-line update.

## 7. Supplement submitted after paint or reassembly has started

**Usually means:** the shop lost its strongest negotiating position — the ability to show the insurer open, visibly unrepaired damage — and is now asking for money against a repair path that's already partially executed. Adjusters read post-paint supplements as weaker claims regardless of documentation quality.

**First question:** "Was this damage found before or after paint started, and if before, why wasn't the supplement submitted then?"

**Data to pull:** teardown date vs. paint start date vs. supplement submission date; whether the finding was genuinely discoverable only after paint (rare) or simply not submitted promptly.

## 8. Supplement is one lump-sum increase with no line-item sourcing

**Usually means:** the shop is asking the insurer to trust a total instead of proving each addition — this reads as padding to an adjuster and gets partially denied by default, which then forces the itemization that should have shipped in the first submission.

**First question:** "Can you show me the photo, measurement, or OEM citation behind each dollar in this supplement?"

**Data to pull:** teardown photos and measurement printouts by line item; OEM procedure citations for any calibration or non-sectionable-part line.

## 9. No pre-repair or post-repair DTC scan on record

**Usually means:** there's no baseline to distinguish a pre-existing fault from repair-caused damage, and no confirmation that ADAS or stability-control modules came back online correctly after the repair — this becomes a liability dispute the shop can't win with only a verbal "it drove fine."

**First question:** "Where's the pre-scan and post-scan printout for this vehicle?"

**Data to pull:** pre-repair DTC scan report; post-repair DTC scan report; calibration confirmation report if any ADAS work was performed.

## 10. Vehicle released without a calibration confirmation report despite calibration-triggering work

**Usually means:** the shop treated calibration as done because the equipment "ran," without confirming a pass result — some calibration attempts fail silently or partially, and the confirmation report is the only artifact that proves success.

**First question:** "Where's the calibration system's pass/fail printout, not just confirmation the process was run?"

**Data to pull:** the calibration tool's confirmation report; target-placement measurements used for static calibration; road/weather conditions logged for dynamic calibration if the OEM specifies conditions.
