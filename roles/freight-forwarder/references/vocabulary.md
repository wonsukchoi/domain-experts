# Freight Forwarder — Vocabulary

### NVOCC (Non-Vessel Operating Common Carrier)
A forwarder that issues its own bills of lading and assumes carrier-like liability to the shipper, without owning or operating the vessels that actually move the cargo.
**In use:** "We're booking this as NVOCC, so our HBL is what the shipper can claim against — not the ocean carrier's terms."
**Common misuse:** Assuming any forwarder is automatically an NVOCC; a forwarder acting purely as agent has a different, much narrower liability posture even on an identical-looking shipment.

### House Bill of Lading (HBL)
The bill of lading the forwarder (acting as NVOCC) issues directly to the shipper, distinct from the Master Bill of Lading the actual ocean carrier issues to the forwarder.
**In use:** "The HBL particulars have to match the MBL exactly — weight, pieces, marks — or this gets flagged at destination customs."
**Common misuse:** Treating the HBL as an internal reference copy of the MBL rather than the actual contract of carriage between forwarder and shipper.

### Master Bill of Lading (MBL)
The bill of lading the ocean carrier issues covering the full container or vessel space booked, naming the forwarder (or NVOCC) as shipper of record if the forwarder consolidated multiple shippers' cargo.
**In use:** "The MBL shows us as shipper because we're consolidating three LCL clients into this one container."
**Common misuse:** Confusing MBL terms and liability limits with what the forwarder actually owes the underlying shipper under the HBL — they can differ.

### Revenue ton
The billing basis for LCL freight: the greater of a shipment's weight-based tons and volume-based tons (roughly 1 CBM ≈ 1 revenue ton by common industry convention).
**In use:** "At 22 CBM against 11 tons of actual weight, this bills on 22 revenue tons — volume is driving the rate, not weight."
**Common misuse:** Quoting off actual weight alone without computing the volume-based figure, which under-quotes any shipment that's bulky relative to its weight.

### FAK (Freight All Kinds)
A blanket ocean freight rate class applied regardless of the specific commodity, as opposed to a commodity-specific rate.
**In use:** "This lane's FAK rate covers it, but check whether a GRI has been layered on top since we last quoted."
**Common misuse:** Assuming FAK is a fixed, permanent rate rather than a base that surcharges (GRI, PSS, BAF) get added on top of, and that changes week to week.

### GRI (General Rate Increase)
A carrier- or alliance-announced blanket rate increase applied across a trade lane, independent of any individual shipper's negotiated rate.
**In use:** "There's a $500/container GRI effective next week on this lane — reprice anything not yet booked."
**Common misuse:** Treating a GRI as negotiable line-by-line with the carrier rather than a market-wide surcharge that applies unless a contract rate specifically shields against it.

### Demurrage
The charge a terminal or carrier levies for a container that remains at the port/terminal past its free time, before pickup.
**In use:** "Demurrage starts accruing on day 5 if the container isn't picked up from the terminal by then."
**Common misuse:** Using "demurrage" and "detention" interchangeably; they're charged for different things (terminal dwell vs. equipment held outside the terminal) and often have separate free-time clocks.

### Detention
The per diem charge for holding the carrier's container equipment outside the terminal — after pickup, before empty return — past its free time.
**In use:** "We picked up on day 3, so detention free time starts then, separate from the demurrage clock that already stopped."
**Common misuse:** Assuming one combined free-time window covers both demurrage and detention; carriers typically track and bill them separately.

### CFS (Container Freight Station)
A facility where LCL cargo from multiple shippers is consolidated into (or deconsolidated out of) a shared container.
**In use:** "Cargo needs to be at the CFS by Thursday for us to hit this week's consolidation cutoff."
**Common misuse:** Treating CFS dwell time as negligible; consolidation/deconsolidation routinely adds several days to LCL transit that a straight FCL shipment doesn't incur.

### FCR (Forwarder's Certificate of Receipt)
A FIATA document confirming the forwarder has received goods from the shipper, used as proof of receipt — not a transport document and not a document of title.
**In use:** "The FCR confirms we have the cargo in hand, but it doesn't substitute for the bill of lading once it's on board."
**Common misuse:** Treating the FCR as equivalent to a bill of lading for banking/letter-of-credit purposes; most L/C terms require an actual transport document, not an FCR.

### Incoterms
A standardized set of trade terms (published by the ICC) defining where risk and cost transfer between buyer and seller for a given shipment — not a full statement of who arranges transport logistics.
**In use:** "This is FOB, so the seller's responsible through loading, but that doesn't mean the seller's forwarder is booking the main carriage — check who the buyer engaged."
**Common misuse:** Assuming an Incoterm fully specifies who books and pays for freight, insurance, and customs filings; it defines risk transfer points, and the logistics arrangement has to be confirmed separately against the actual shipping instructions.

### VGM (Verified Gross Mass)
The SOLAS-mandated verified weight of a packed container that must be submitted to the carrier before it can be loaded onto a vessel.
**In use:** "VGM has to be in before the doc cutoff, or the container gets rolled regardless of whether cargo is otherwise ready."
**Common misuse:** Treating VGM submission as optional paperwork rather than a hard prerequisite the carrier enforces before loading.

### Blank sailing
A scheduled vessel voyage that the carrier cancels, typically to manage capacity during low demand or network disruption.
**In use:** "This week's sailing was blanked — we need to rebook onto the next available vessel, not just wait it out."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a blanked sailing automatically rolls the existing booking forward; most carriers require active rebooking, and space on the next sailing isn't guaranteed.

### AES (Automated Export System) filing
The U.S. export declaration filed electronically with Customs and Border Protection for shipments above value/licensing thresholds, identifying the U.S. Principal Party in Interest (USPPI) and commodity details.
**In use:** "Confirm who's filing AES on this one — the shipper's broker, or is that on us as the forwarder?"
**Common misuse:** Assuming the forwarder automatically files AES on every export shipment; the filing responsibility depends on who's designated as the USPPI's agent, and it has to be explicitly assigned.

### ISF (Importer Security Filing, "10+2")
A U.S. Customs and Border Protection advance filing required before an ocean shipment loads at the foreign port, providing cargo and party details for security screening.
**In use:** "ISF has to be filed at least 24 hours before vessel loading, or this shipment risks a hold or penalty at U.S. entry."
**Common misuse:** Confusing ISF (a U.S.-specific pre-loading filing) with other jurisdictions' advance cargo security filings (e.g., the EU's ENS), which have different timing and data requirements.
