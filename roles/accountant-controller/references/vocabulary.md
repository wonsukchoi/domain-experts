# Controller working vocabulary

Terms a controller uses daily and precisely. Format: definition → how a practitioner says it → common misuse. US-GAAP context.

**Accrual vs. cash basis** — Accrual basis recognizes revenue/expense when earned/incurred, regardless of when cash moves; cash basis recognizes only when cash actually changes hands. GAAP requires accrual basis for anything beyond the smallest entities.
In use: "We accrued the December utility bill even though it won't be paid until January — it was incurred in December."
Misuse: treating a healthy cash balance as proof the P&L is healthy. A company can be cash-rich and accrual-basis unprofitable, or the reverse, in the same period.

**Materiality** — The threshold above which an omission or misstatement could reasonably influence a financial statement reader's decisions. Commonly anchored around 5% of pretax income, adjusted by qualitative factors.
In use: "It's $18K against a $45K threshold — quantitatively immaterial, but it's related-party, so we're disclosing it anyway."
Misuse: applying a single fixed percentage mechanically. A small dollar amount can be material if it's related-party, changes a trend, or turns a profit into a loss.

**Three-way match** — Verifying a purchase order, receiving confirmation, and vendor invoice agree (item, quantity, price) before releasing payment.
In use: "The invoice quantity doesn't match receiving — hold the payment until warehouse confirms what actually arrived."
Misuse: treating it as a formality to clear quickly rather than an actual control point; overriding it under a payment-run deadline defeats its purpose.

**Segregation of duties (SoD)** — Structuring roles so no single person can both execute and conceal a transaction (e.g., initiate a payment and reconcile the account it clears against).
In use: "We added a compensating control — the owner reviews the bank statement monthly — because we don't have headcount for full SoD on payables."
Misuse: assuming SoD is only about fraud prevention; it catches honest process errors just as often, which is most of what it actually catches in practice.

**Flux (variance) analysis** — A period-over-period or budget-vs-actual comparison at the account level, with variances above a threshold explained in writing.
In use: "COGS flux of +$210K month-over-month — driving factor is a one-time freight surcharge, not a margin trend."
Misuse: producing the variance number without the driving-factor explanation; an unexplained flux is not a completed flux analysis.

**Roll-forward** — A schedule showing an account's beginning balance, additions, reductions, and ending balance for a period (common for reserves, PP&E, deferred revenue).
In use: "The allowance roll-forward shows a $40K beginning balance, $12K of write-offs, $15K of new provision, $43K ending — ties to the GL."
Misuse: presenting only the ending balance without the roll-forward; an ending number without its drivers can't be reviewed or audited.

**Top-side (manual) journal entry** — An entry posted directly to the GL outside the normal sub-ledger flow (AP, AR, inventory), typically for consolidations, reclasses, or corrections.
In use: "That's a top-side entry to reclass the deposit from other assets to prepaid — flag it for extra review since it bypassed the sub-ledger."
Misuse: treating all top-side entries as suspicious by default, or the opposite — treating them as routine and under-scrutinizing them; the right posture is proportionate review based on account and amount.

**Cut-off** — The principle (and the testing procedure) that transactions are recorded in the correct period — not one day early or late relative to when the event actually occurred.
In use: "We held the March 31 shipment invoice for cut-off testing — goods shipped April 1, so revenue belongs in April."
Misuse: treating cut-off as only a year-end audit concern; cut-off errors at any month-end distort trend analysis just as much.

**Reconciling item** — A specific, identified difference between two balances that should agree (e.g., GL cash vs. bank statement), each with a known cause.
In use: "The $84,200 outstanding-check reconciling item will clear next week when the checks are cashed."
Misuse: using "reconciling item" to describe an unexplained variance — a true reconciling item has a known, documented cause; an unexplained difference is a variance requiring investigation, not yet a reconciling item.

**Internal control deficiency vs. material weakness** — A deficiency is any control gap; a significant deficiency is one important enough for those responsible for oversight to know about; a material weakness is severe enough that a material misstatement wouldn't be prevented or detected timely. Distinct severity tiers under SOX/COSO, not synonyms.
In use: "That's a significant deficiency, not a material weakness — it didn't actually cause a misstatement, but the control that should have caught one wasn't operating."
Misuse: using "material weakness" loosely for any control gap; the term has a specific, higher bar and reporting consequence.

**Quality of earnings (QoE)** — An analysis, standard in M&A diligence, of how much reported EBITDA is sustainable and cash-backed versus one-time items or aggressive accounting choices.
In use: "Adjusted EBITDA is $4M, but the QoE review will knock out roughly half the addbacks."
Misuse: taking a company's self-reported "adjusted EBITDA" at face value; the addbacks are exactly what a QoE review interrogates.

**Related-party transaction** — A transaction with a counterparty connected to the company through ownership, control, or family relationship (an officer's business, a major shareholder's affiliate), requiring specific identification and disclosure.
In use: "That vendor is owned by the CFO's spouse — this needs related-party disclosure regardless of the dollar amount."
Misuse: applying a materiality filter before disclosure; related-party status itself, not just the dollar size, triggers the disclosure requirement.

**Going concern** — The auditor's assessment of whether substantial doubt exists about the entity continuing operations for the next 12 months, requiring specific disclosure if doubt exists.
In use: "Cash runway under 6 months with no committed financing is exactly the kind of fact pattern that triggers a going-concern evaluation."
Misuse: treating going-concern language as an automatic death sentence for the company; it's a required disclosure of risk, not a legal conclusion that the company will fail.

**PBC (prepared-by-client) list vs. AJE (audit adjusting entry)** — The PBC list is the set of schedules and documents the controller prepares for the auditors; an AJE is a correcting entry the auditors propose during fieldwork.
In use: "We cleared the PBC list two weeks early this year, and had zero AJEs — the close discipline is paying off."
Misuse: treating a high AJE count as solely an auditor finding rather than a signal about the underlying close process quality; a company with a well-controlled close should see AJEs trend toward zero over time.

**Suspense/clearing account** — A temporary holding account for amounts not yet identified or fully processed, meant to clear to zero (or near it) every period.
In use: "The suspense account has a $6K balance that's been sitting there for three months — that's not a clearing account anymore, that's an unresolved variance."
Misuse: letting a suspense account carry a balance across multiple periods as if that were normal; a persistent balance means the underlying item was never actually investigated.
