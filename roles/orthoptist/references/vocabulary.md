# Vocabulary — terms generalists misuse

### Tropia vs. phoria
A tropia is a manifest deviation present under normal binocular viewing; a phoria is a latent deviation only present when binocular fusion is disrupted (e.g., during cover testing) and controlled by fusion the rest of the time.
**In use:** "Alternate cover shows movement on cover-uncover but no movement is seen with both eyes open — that's a phoria, not a tropia; don't chart it as strabismus."
**Common misuse:** Calling any movement seen during cover testing a "strabismus," when a phoria that's well-controlled under normal viewing is a different clinical entity with a different management threshold.

### Comitant vs. incomitant (noncomitant)
A comitant deviation has the same magnitude in all gaze positions; an incomitant deviation varies by gaze direction, implying a paretic, restrictive, or mechanical cause rather than a typical childhood strabismus.
**In use:** "The angle is 15 PD larger in left gaze than right — that's incomitant, so this needs a Hess chart and a paretic/restrictive workup, not just a standard esotropia surgical plan."
**Common misuse:** Treating any strabismus as comitant by default and skipping versions/ductions grading, which misses the cases that actually need a different diagnostic path.

### Suppression vs. amblyopia
Suppression is an active cortical inhibition of one eye's image during binocular viewing to avoid diplopia/confusion; amblyopia is a monocular reduction in best-corrected visual acuity that persists even with the other eye covered.
**In use:** "Under binocular conditions there's suppression of the left image, but monocular BCVA is 20/20 OS — that's suppression alone, not amblyopia, and the treatment approach differs."
**Common misuse:** Using the terms interchangeably, which leads to prescribing occlusion therapy (an amblyopia treatment) for a purely suppression-based presentation with normal monocular acuity.

### Anomalous retinal correspondence (ARC) vs. normal retinal correspondence (NRC)
NRC means the two foveae share a common visual direction; ARC is an adaptation where a peripheral retinal point in the deviating eye takes on the foveal visual direction of the fixating eye, allowing some binocular function despite a manifest strabismus.
**In use:** "Synoptophore testing shows harmonious ARC at the habitual angle — expect reduced but real binocular function, and know that fully correcting the angle surgically can disrupt the adaptation the patient currently relies on."
**Common misuse:** Assuming any binocular response on synoptophore testing means normal correspondence, without checking whether the angle at which fusion occurs matches the objective deviation (which would indicate ARC, not NRC).

### Prism diopter (PD) vs. degree
A prism diopter is the unit strabismus angles are conventionally measured and prescribed in (1 PD deflects an image 1cm at 1m); degrees are a different angular unit used less often clinically and the two are not numerically interchangeable via a simple ratio at larger angles.
**In use:** "The angle is 25 PD at near — convert carefully if a report gives it in degrees, don't assume 1 degree = 1 PD."
**Common misuse:** Treating prism diopters and degrees as roughly interchangeable at any magnitude, when the relationship is nonlinear and diverges meaningfully above roughly 45 PD.

### Occlusion (dose) vs. occlusion amblyopia
Occlusion (patching) dose is the prescribed and delivered hours of covering the sound eye to treat amblyopia in the weaker eye; occlusion amblyopia is the iatrogenic risk of the previously normal (patched) eye developing reduced acuity from excessive or poorly monitored patching.
**In use:** "VA in the patched eye dropped two lines at this visit — check for occlusion amblyopia before assuming the amblyopic eye's improvement means the regimen is working as intended."
**Common misuse:** Focusing only on the treated eye's acuity gain and not monitoring the patched eye for the opposite iatrogenic risk, especially with high-hour or full-time regimens.

### Convergence insufficiency vs. convergence excess
Convergence insufficiency is an inability to converge/sustain convergence adequately at near, typically with an exophoria/exotropia greater at near than distance; convergence excess is excessive convergence at near, typically with an esophoria/esotropia greater at near than distance — the two get opposite therapy.
**In use:** "Near deviation is more esotropic than distance, with a high AC/A ratio — that's convergence excess, and vergence therapy aimed at reducing near convergence, not the CITT push-up protocol for insufficiency, is the fit."
**Common misuse:** Applying the same "vergence therapy" label and protocol to both conditions, when the direction of the exercises and the underlying deviation pattern are opposite.

### Duction vs. version
A duction is monocular eye movement (one eye tested alone, e.g., adduction, abduction); a version is binocular conjugate eye movement (both eyes moving together in the same direction, e.g., dextroversion).
**In use:** "Abduction on duction testing is full, but the version shows a clear limitation in that direction — that pattern points toward a supranuclear or fusional control issue rather than a primary muscle restriction."
**Common misuse:** Using "duction" and "version" interchangeably in documentation, which obscures whether a limitation is monocular (muscle/nerve) or binocular-only (control/fusional) in origin.

### A-pattern / V-pattern strabismus
A horizontal deviation that changes magnitude systematically between upgaze and downgaze — more esodeviated (or less exodeviated) in upgaze with an "A" pattern, more exodeviated (or less esodeviated) in upgaze with a "V" pattern — implying oblique muscle dysfunction.
**In use:** "There's a V-pattern with inferior oblique overaction bilaterally — the surgical plan needs to address the oblique dysfunction, not just the horizontal angle measured in primary position."
**Common misuse:** Measuring and surgically dosing only the primary-position horizontal angle when an A- or V-pattern is present, missing the vertical/oblique component that drives the pattern.

### Latent nystagmus vs. manifest (or manifest-latent) nystagmus
Latent nystagmus appears only when one eye is occluded (each eye beating toward the open eye); manifest-latent nystagmus is a related form present to some degree with both eyes open but worsening with monocular occlusion — the distinction affects which eye's acuity is more reliable to test.
**In use:** "This is manifest-latent — expect binocular acuity to look better than monocular, since occluding either eye intensifies the nystagmus and depresses the acuity measured that way."
**Common misuse:** Testing and charting monocular visual acuity in a patient with latent/manifest-latent nystagmus without noting that occlusion itself worsens the nystagmus and can understate true binocular visual function.

### Diplopia vs. visual confusion
Diplopia is seeing one object as two images (non-corresponding retinal points stimulated by the same object); visual confusion is seeing two different objects superimposed in the same visual direction (corresponding retinal points stimulated by different objects) — a distinct binocular symptom.
**In use:** "The patient describes two different scenes overlapping, not one object doubled — that's confusion, not diplopia, and it points to a different sensory adaptation status."
**Common misuse:** Using "diplopia" as a catch-all for any binocular visual disturbance, when confusion and diplopia imply different underlying retinal correspondence states.

### Sensory fusion vs. motor fusion
Sensory fusion is the cortical ability to combine the two retinal images into one perception; motor fusion is the vergence eye-movement system's ability to align the eyes precisely enough for sensory fusion to occur and to keep it stable against disparity.
**In use:** "Sensory fusion is present on the synoptophore, but motor fusion range is reduced — the images can combine, but not across enough of a vergence range to stay comfortable at near for long."
**Common misuse:** Treating "fusion" as one property, rather than checking both — a patient can have intact sensory fusion but symptomatic reduced motor fusion range (as in convergence insufficiency), which needs a different intervention than a sensory-status finding alone would suggest.

### Null point (nystagmus)
The gaze position at which a patient's nystagmus amplitude is minimized and visual acuity is best, often adopted as a compensatory head turn/tilt.
**In use:** "Acuity improves from 20/60 in primary gaze to 20/25 at the null point in left gaze — document the head posture and consider whether surgery to shift the null point to primary position is indicated."
**Common misuse:** Measuring visual acuity only in primary gaze and not searching for a null point, which can substantially understate a nystagmus patient's actual best functional acuity.

### Occlusion dose monitor (ODM)
A sensor device attached to or embedded in the amblyopia occlusion patch that objectively records actual wear time, used to distinguish a genuinely under-dosed trial from a true treatment-failure at the prescribed dose.
**In use:** "ODM data for the last two weeks shows 37.5% of prescribed wear — this is an under-dosed trial, not evidence the 2h/day regimen doesn't work for this child."
**Common misuse:** Relying on caregiver-reported wear time as equivalent to objective ODM data when deciding whether a therapy trial was adequate, when self-report is known to systematically overstate actual compliance.
