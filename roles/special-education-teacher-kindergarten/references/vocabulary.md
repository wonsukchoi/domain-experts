# Vocabulary

### IFSP-to-IEP transition
The shift, required by the time a child reaches kindergarten eligibility, from an Individualized Family Service Plan (developmental milestones, family-centered) to an Individualized Education Program (measurable academic/functional goals, standards-referenced).
**In use:** "We're rewriting the fine-motor goal for the transition — it can't stay in milestone language once it's in the IEP."
**Common misuse:** Treating the transition as a paperwork renaming rather than a change in what counts as an acceptable goal — a developmental-language goal carried over unrewritten is not IEP-compliant even if the underlying concern is the same.

### Skill deficit vs. performance deficit
A skill deficit means the child cannot yet perform the task under any condition; a performance deficit means the child can perform it but doesn't in a specific setting (often due to avoidance, group size, or regulation demands).
**In use:** "The 1:1 probe came back at seventy percent — this is a performance deficit layered on a smaller skill gap, not a pure skill deficit."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a classroom refusal is automatically a skill problem and reteaching content the child has already mastered, which wastes instructional time and can reinforce the avoidance.

### Least restrictive environment (LRE)
The IDEA requirement that a child with a disability be educated with nondisabled peers to the maximum extent appropriate, with removal to a more restrictive setting justified only by documented inability to achieve satisfactorily in general education with supplementary aids.
**In use:** "LRE here means general education with pull-out support until we have data showing that's not enough — not defaulting to resource room because it's easier to schedule."
**Common misuse:** Treating LRE as a fixed placement category (a room) rather than a standard that has to be justified fresh, with evidence, at each placement decision.

### Present levels of academic and functional performance (PLAAFP/PLOP)
The IEP section describing the child's current, measured skill levels, which every goal must trace back to.
**In use:** "This goal doesn't map to anything in the present levels — we need a present-levels data point before we can write it."
**Common misuse:** Writing goals off the eligibility category label instead of the child's specific present-levels data, producing a generic, category-typical goal that isn't actually tied to this child.

### Placement inertia
The tendency for a child's first special-education placement to persist through subsequent grades, independent of whether the child's support needs have changed.
**In use:** "Placement inertia is exactly why we're starting in general education with support instead of self-contained — reversing a restrictive first placement is harder than adding restriction later if needed."
**Common misuse:** Ignoring inertia and choosing a more-restrictive placement "to be safe" at kindergarten entry, not accounting for how sticky that first placement tends to be.

### Push-in vs. pull-out support
Push-in delivers special-education services inside the general-education classroom; pull-out removes the child to a separate setting for some or all of a service.
**In use:** "Starting pull-out for the first three weeks in a reduced group, then fading back to push-in once participation improves."
**Common misuse:** Treating push-in as automatically "more inclusive" and therefore always preferable, without checking whether the specific skill or behavior target is actually better served by the reduced stimulation of a pull-out setting, at least temporarily.

### Progress monitoring
The scheduled, repeated measurement of a specific IEP goal using a consistent tool, used to determine whether the current intervention is working.
**In use:** "We're progress-monitoring the phonemic-awareness goal every two weeks — six data points before the next review."
**Common misuse:** Checking progress only at the annual review instead of on the schedule stated in the goal, which means an ineffective intervention runs for months before anyone notices.

### RTI / MTSS (Response to Intervention / Multi-Tiered System of Supports)
A tiered framework of increasingly intensive, general-education-delivered interventions used before or alongside a formal special-education referral, meant to distinguish a lack of instruction from a disability.
**In use:** "The referral needs the Tier 2 fidelity logs attached — without them we can't tell if this is a disability or a delivery gap."
**Common misuse:** Treating RTI tier completion as a formality to clear before referral, rather than as the actual evidence the eligibility team needs to rule out an instructional cause.

### Inclusion model (co-taught/co-planned instruction)
An instructional arrangement where a child with an IEP participates in the general-education classroom for some or all of a subject, with the special-education and general-education teachers co-planning how the child accesses that instruction.
**In use:** "For the phonics block she's accommodated, not modified — same objective, extended time and a visual cue card."
**Common misuse:** Treating inclusion as achieved once the child is physically present in the room, without specifying whether the child is following the same objective (accommodated), a reduced objective (modified), or an unrelated parallel activity — leaving the general-education teacher to improvise.

### Eligibility category
The specific disability classification (e.g., Speech-Language Impairment, Developmental Delay, Autism) under which a child qualifies for special-education services under IDEA.
**In use:** "She qualifies under Developmental Delay, but her actual service needs are driven by the present-levels data, not the category label."
**Common misuse:** Assuming two children with the same eligibility category need the same services or goals — the category establishes eligibility, not the instructional plan.
