# Vocabulary

### Bond line thickness (BLT)
The actual thickness of the adhesive layer between two bonded substrates, a controlled structural variable rather than however much adhesive happens to remain after clamping.
**In use:** "BLT's controlled by the calibrated shims here — not just whatever squeezes out under clamp pressure."
**Common misuse:** Judging BLT by squeeze-out amount or clamping pressure alone rather than controlling it directly with calibrated spacers or fixture stops.

### Open time (working time)
The duration an adhesive can remain applied to a substrate surface before losing enough tack or reactivity to properly bond a second substrate, distinct from pot life.
**In use:** "We're at 24 minutes since application — that's over the 20-minute open time, even though pot life's still fine."
**Common misuse:** Confusing open time with pot life, tracking only how long the adhesive has been mixed rather than how long it's been sitting applied to the substrate awaiting final assembly.

### Pot life
The duration a mixed, typically two-part, adhesive remains usable and dispensable in its mixing container before it gels or becomes too viscous to apply.
**In use:** "Pot life's 45 minutes on this mix — plenty of time to dispense, but that doesn't cover the open-time clock once it's applied."
**Common misuse:** Assuming staying within pot life guarantees the bonding operation is on schedule, when open time — a separate, often shorter constraint — can still be exceeded.

### Surface energy (bonding)
A measurable property of a prepared substrate surface indicating its receptiveness to adhesive wetting and bonding, verifiable via methods like the water break test or dyne pens.
**In use:** "Surface energy checks out with the dyne pen — clear to apply adhesive."
**Common misuse:** Judging surface readiness by visual cleanliness alone, when contamination or insufficient surface energy can be present on a surface that looks perfectly clean.

### Water break test
A surface prep verification method where water is applied to a cleaned surface and observed for whether it sheets evenly, indicating adequate surface energy, or beads and breaks, indicating contamination or insufficient prep.
**In use:** "Water broke into beads on this panel — surface prep isn't done yet, reclean before bonding."
**Common misuse:** Skipping this or an equivalent verification test and relying on visual inspection alone to judge whether a surface is properly prepared for bonding.

### Cure schedule
The specified time-at-temperature profile, or room-temperature cure duration, an adhesive requires to reach full strength.
**In use:** "Cure schedule calls for 60 minutes at 250°F — confirmed the part itself held that temperature, not just the oven."
**Common misuse:** Verifying cure completion by chamber/oven setpoint and elapsed time rather than confirming the actual part temperature reached and held the required cure temperature for the specified duration.

### Squeeze-out
Adhesive pushed out of the bond line during clamping, which serves as a visual indicator of adhesive coverage and bond line thickness control.
**In use:** "Squeeze-out's continuous the whole length of this joint — good sign for coverage, no gaps to investigate."
**Common misuse:** Treating squeeze-out purely as excess mess to be wiped away, rather than as diagnostic information — continuous squeeze-out suggests good coverage, while gaps can indicate a coverage void.

### Substrate preparation (surface prep)
The cleaning, abrading, or priming process applied to a bonding surface before adhesive application, to achieve the surface chemistry and roughness the adhesive needs.
**In use:** "Surface prep here is abrade, solvent wipe, then verify with the water break test — not just wipe it down and call it clean."
**Common misuse:** Treating surface prep as a single generic "clean it" step rather than a specification-driven process with its own verifiable completion criteria.

### Two-part adhesive
An adhesive system requiring mixing of a resin and hardener, or similar components, in a specified ratio before use, as distinct from a one-part pre-mixed or moisture/heat-cure system.
**In use:** "Double-check the mix ratio on this two-part system — off-ratio mixing changes both pot life and final cured strength."
**Common misuse:** Assuming mixing ratio tolerance is loose or forgiving, when an incorrect mix ratio can significantly affect both pot life and final cured strength.

### Proof testing / destructive testing (bonded joint)
Testing a sample joint, often destructively, to verify actual bond strength, used to validate process parameters or investigate a suspected process deviation.
**In use:** "This joint's flagged for open-time overrun — pull a proof sample rather than releasing it on visual inspection alone."
**Common misuse:** Relying solely on visual or dimensional inspection of a finished bonded joint to judge its strength, when only proof or destructive testing on a representative sample can actually verify bond strength.
