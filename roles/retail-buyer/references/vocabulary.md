### Open-to-buy (OTB)
The dollar amount of merchandise a buyer can purchase in a given period, calculated as planned sales plus planned markdowns plus planned end-of-month inventory, minus beginning-of-month inventory and inventory already on order.
**In use:** "Open-to-buy for the month comes to $150,000 at retail after netting out current on-hand and on-order inventory."
**Common misuse:** Treating open-to-buy as a fixed figure set once at the start of a season rather than recalculating it against actual sales and inventory position as the period progresses.

### Initial markup (IMU)
The markup percentage applied to an item at the time it's purchased, calculated from the difference between cost and the initial retail price.
**In use:** "This buy carries a 55% initial markup at the time of purchase."
**Common misuse:** Treating initial markup as the actual realized profitability of a buy, without accounting for the markdowns that will erode it to a lower maintained markup.

### Maintained markup (MMU)
The markup percentage actually realized after accounting for markdowns, shortage, and employee/promotional discounts — the true reflection of a buy's profitability.
**In use:** "Maintained markup came in well below the initial markup once the markdown activity from the slow sell-through was factored in."
**Common misuse:** Reporting a buy's success using initial markup alone, without calculating the maintained markup that reflects what was actually realized.

### GMROI (gross margin return on inventory investment)
A profitability metric calculated as gross margin dollars divided by average inventory cost, measuring how efficiently inventory investment generates margin.
**In use:** "GMROI of 1.85 is below our department benchmark of 2.5, even though the margin percentage looked fine on its own."
**Common misuse:** Evaluating a buy's success on margin percentage alone, missing that a high-margin item tying up capital in slow-turning inventory can produce a weaker GMROI than a faster-turning, lower-margin item.

### Sell-through rate
The percentage of received units that have sold by a given point in time, tracked against a planned sell-through curve at defined checkpoints.
**In use:** "Sell-through at the 4-week checkpoint is 22%, well below the planned 35%."
**Common misuse:** Waiting until end-of-season data to assess sell-through performance, missing the opportunity to act on an early checkpoint shortfall before it compounds.

### Markdown cadence
The planned schedule of price reductions applied to merchandise over its selling life, typically more aggressive for fashion/trend items than for core/replenishable items.
**In use:** "This item's markdown cadence calls for a 20% reduction if sell-through lags at the 4-week mark."
**Common misuse:** Applying the same markdown cadence to core/replenishable items as to fashion/trend items, when the two categories require different management approaches.

### Core/replenishable vs. fashion/trend (assortment classification)
A classification distinguishing items with steady, ongoing demand suited to reorder-based replenishment (core) from items with a limited selling window better managed through markdown cadence without a reorder assumption (fashion/trend).
**In use:** "This is a core item — we'll manage it through reorder based on sell-through, not a markdown schedule."
**Common misuse:** Applying a reorder strategy to fashion/trend items that won't sustain demand long enough to justify replenishment, or applying an aggressive markdown cadence to core items that would otherwise sell through steadily.

### Cash discount (vendor terms)
A percentage reduction in the amount owed to a vendor if payment is made within a specified early period, distinct from the negotiated unit cost.
**In use:** "We negotiated a 2% cash discount for payment within 10 days, on top of the unit cost terms."
**Common misuse:** Negotiating unit cost alone without addressing cash discount and dating terms, leaving additional negotiable margin on the table.

### Dating terms (vendor terms)
The agreed payment timeline for an invoice, which can be extended (e.g., delayed billing to align with a selling season) as part of vendor negotiations.
**In use:** "Dating terms push our payment due date to align with when we expect to have sold through most of this seasonal receipt."
**Common misuse:** Accepting standard dating terms without negotiating them to better align payment timing with the actual expected sell-through period.

### Chargeback (vendor compliance)
A deduction taken from payment to a vendor for a compliance violation, such as late shipment, incorrect labeling, or packaging non-compliance, per the terms of the vendor agreement.
**In use:** "This late shipment triggers the standard chargeback per our vendor compliance agreement."
**Common misuse:** Failing to enforce a chargeback for a documented compliance violation, quietly eroding the margin protection the vendor agreement was meant to provide.
