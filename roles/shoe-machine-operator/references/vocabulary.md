# Vocabulary

### Last (shoe last)
The shoe-shaped form used to shape the upper during manufacturing, whose dimensional accuracy directly determines the fit of every shoe produced on it — a worn last produces a systematic, batch-wide defect, not an isolated variation.
**In use:** "Last's showing point-oh-four-oh deviation at the toe box — that's not a per-shoe issue, every pair made on this last during this run will carry the same fit problem."
**Common misuse:** Treating a last as a fixed, permanently accurate tool that doesn't require periodic inspection — repeated use wears the last's profile, and this wear translates directly into a fit defect affecting every unit produced on it until caught.

### Lasting tension
The controlled pulling/stretching force applied to upper material as it's shaped over the last, which must be set within a range specific to the material's stretch characteristics.
**In use:** "Tension's set for leather, but we're running synthetic today — that needs its own verified range, not the leather setting carried over."
**Common misuse:** Assuming lasting tension is correct because the machine is cycling and producing shoes normally — tension is a specific parameter requiring verification against the current material's characteristics, and a mismatch produces distortion or looseness that a running machine doesn't reveal on its own.

### Sole bond pull strength
A direct measurement of the force required to separate a sole from its attached upper, used to verify actual bonding adequacy beyond what visual inspection of the finished attachment can confirm.
**In use:** "Pull test came back at eighteen pounds-force against a twenty-five minimum — that's a real bond deficiency the visual inspection wouldn't have caught."
**Common misuse:** Relying on visual inspection (does the sole look properly attached, no visible gaps) to confirm bond adequacy — a visually acceptable sole attachment can still have inadequate pull strength if underlying process parameters (temperature, pressure, cure time) have drifted.

### Direct-attach injection molding (footwear)
A sole attachment process where sole material is injection-molded directly onto the assembled upper/last combination, requiring controlled temperature, pressure, and cure time parameters analogous to other molding processes.
**In use:** "Molding temperature's drifted to three-sixty-five against a three-eighty spec — that's why bond strength dropped, not a material defect."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a molding machine's parameters remain constant simply because the machine is running and producing shoes — heater degradation and other equipment issues can cause gradual parameter drift that isn't apparent without direct verification.

### In-process sampling (footwear production)
Testing or inspecting units at intervals throughout a production run, rather than only at batch completion, to catch a developing systematic issue before it affects the entire batch quantity.
**In use:** "Sampling every fifty pairs is what caught this at pair two-fifty instead of finding out at pair five hundred that the whole batch has a bond problem."
**Common misuse:** Relying solely on end-of-batch inspection to catch quality issues — a systematic problem (worn tooling, parameter drift) that develops partway through a run affects every unit from that point forward, and in-process sampling is what limits the affected quantity rather than discovering the full extent only after the entire batch is complete.

### Containment scope (batch quality issue)
The specific range of produced units identified as potentially affected by a quality issue, ideally narrowed using in-process sampling data rather than defaulting to the entire batch.
**In use:** "Sampling data puts this at fifty pairs affected, not the full five hundred — that's the actual containment scope, not a blanket batch-wide hold."
**Common misuse:** Defaulting to a full-batch containment scope whenever a defect is found, without checking whether in-process sampling data can narrow the actual affected range — unnecessarily broad containment wastes inspection/rework effort on units that were produced before the issue began.

### First-article inspection (new/replacement tooling)
Verifying a new or replacement last (or other tooling) produces correct dimensional output before committing to full production, catching a tooling manufacturing defect before it affects an entire run.
**In use:** "New last just came in — running a first-article check before we commit to the full five-hundred-pair order on it."
**Common misuse:** Putting new or replacement tooling directly into full production without a first-article check — a manufacturing defect in the tooling itself would otherwise go undetected until a much larger quantity has already been affected.

### Material-specific parameter verification
Confirming machine settings (tension, temperature, pressure) match the specific requirements of the current material/style combination, rather than assuming settings from a prior run remain valid.
**In use:** "Switching from leather to synthetic upper — re-verifying lasting tension and molding temperature before we start, not just running with yesterday's settings."
**Common misuse:** Carrying over machine parameters from a previous run without re-verification when the material or style changes — different materials have different stretch, bonding, and thermal characteristics that can require meaningfully different process settings.
