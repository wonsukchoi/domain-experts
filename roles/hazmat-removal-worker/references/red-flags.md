# Red flags

Smell tests a senior hazmat supervisor catches reviewing a job file, a containment log, or a clearance report. Format for each: what it usually means, the first question to ask, and the data to pull before signing off.

### Manometer log shows gaps of more than an hour during active removal

**Usually means:** the reading was skipped rather than taken and found compliant — either understaffing on monitoring duty or a reading nobody wanted to log because it was borderline.

**First question:** "Walk me through the manometer log for the shift — where are the gaps, and what was happening on-site during them?"

**Data to pull:** the continuous-monitoring log or alarm printout against the shift's activity log (breaks, material changes, airlock use).

### Containment differential reads between 0 and -0.02 in. w.g. for any sustained period

**Usually means:** the negative air machine is undersized for the enclosure at loaded-filter airflow, or a door/airlock was propped open longer than a single entry/exit cycle.

**First question:** "What CFM is the negative air unit rated at clean-filter, and when was the filter last changed or inspected this shift?"

**Data to pull:** unit's nominal CFM rating and derate assumption used at setup; enclosure volume; filter-change log; door/airlock usage log if available.

### Negative air machine selection wasn't documented against a calculated ACH requirement

**Usually means:** the crew grabbed whatever unit was on the truck rather than sizing to the enclosure volume — often fine by luck on a small room, a real gap on a large one.

**First question:** "What's the enclosure volume, and what ACH does the selected unit's derated CFM actually deliver?"

**Data to pull:** enclosure dimensions; unit's rated CFM; the ACH calculation reconstructed from both.

### Half-face APR assigned on a task with historical or measured exposure at or above ~8x the PEL

**Usually means:** respirator class was picked by how the material looked or felt rather than by the actual exposure multiple — a habit that under-protects at exactly the jobs that feel routine.

**First question:** "What's the exposure data this respirator-class decision was based on, and does the resulting effective exposure (measured ÷ APF) leave any real margin under the PEL?"

**Data to pull:** prior task air-monitoring results or the negative exposure assessment on file; the APF math for the assigned respirator.

### No negative exposure assessment on file but respirator class was stepped down from the regulatory default

**Usually means:** the crew is running a lower-APF respirator than 1926.1101 requires by default for that work class, without the documented monitoring history that's the only thing that legally justifies the step-down.

**First question:** "Where's the negative exposure assessment that supports using a half-face APR instead of the Class default?"

**Data to pull:** the NEA documentation (if any); the work classification (Class I-IV) and its default respirator requirement.

### Containment was struck (removed) before the clearance lab result came back

**Usually means:** schedule pressure overrode the pass/fail gate — someone treated a good visual cleanup as equivalent to a passing clearance sample.

**First question:** "What was the clearance sample result, and what time did it come back relative to when containment came down?"

**Data to pull:** the lab report timestamp against the containment teardown log/photos; who authorized the teardown.

### A failed clearance sample was answered by re-cleaning the entire containment rather than the specific sample location

**Usually means:** the crew didn't map the sample stations to specific containment areas, so the re-clean can't be targeted — this both wastes labor and risks missing the actual failure point if it's re-tested at a different, now-clean station.

**First question:** "Which specific station or surface failed, and where exactly is that relative to what we just re-cleaned?"

**Data to pull:** the station-to-location map from the original sampling plan; the specific failed result.

### Dust-wipe clearance result sits just under the threshold on every sample (e.g., consistently 9-9.5 µg/ft² against a 10 µg/ft² floor standard)

**Usually means:** either genuinely careful work at the margin, or a sampling/lab-selection pattern that's optimized to pass rather than to represent the room honestly — worth a second look either way.

**First question:** "Were these samples taken by the same technician using the same wipe protocol as our other jobs, and is this lab our usual lab?"

**Data to pull:** the lab's chain-of-custody and sampling technician identity; comparable results from other jobs by the same crew/lab pairing.

### Decontamination sequence photos or logs show respirator removal before suit removal

**Usually means:** the crew reversed the fixed decon order under time pressure, defeating the purpose of doffing the respirator last.

**First question:** "Walk me through the exact order gross-decon, suit, gloves/boots, and respirator came off for this exit."

**Data to pull:** decon-step photos/video if the site records them; interview the crew member directly rather than relying on the written log alone.

### Crew member's fit-test or medical clearance is expired or missing for the respirator class assigned

**Usually means:** a scheduling gap where someone was assigned to a job before their annual fit-test or medical evaluation was renewed — a real protection gap even if the respirator itself is correct for the task.

**First question:** "When was this person's last fit-test for this specific respirator make/model, and is their medical evaluation current?"

**Data to pull:** fit-test records (date, respirator model, pass/fail) and OSHA 1910.134 Appendix C medical evaluation status per crew member.

### A "small job" lead-paint or asbestos removal skipped the notification/permit step

**Usually means:** the crew or customer treated the regulatory notification threshold as a matter of scale/judgment rather than a fixed regulatory trigger (e.g., NESHAP's linear/square-footage figures) that applies regardless of how small the job feels.

**First question:** "What's the actual linear or square footage being removed, and does that clear the notification threshold either way?"

**Data to pull:** the measured quantity of material being removed/disturbed; the applicable notification form and threshold for the material and jurisdiction.
