# Vocabulary

Terms generalists conflate that hazmat-removal practitioners keep sharply separate. Each: definition, how a practitioner actually uses it in a sentence, and the common misuse.

### PEL vs. action level vs. excursion limit

The **PEL (Permissible Exposure Limit)** is the legal ceiling for an 8-hour time-weighted average exposure (asbestos: 0.1 f/cc). The **action level** is the threshold that triggers monitoring/medical-surveillance obligations even before the PEL is exceeded (asbestos: also 0.1 f/cc, numerically equal to the PEL under the current standard). The **excursion limit** is a separate short-duration ceiling (asbestos: 1.0 f/cc averaged over any 30-minute period) that can be violated even on a day whose 8-hour average stays under the PEL.

*In use:* "The 8-hour average was fine, but that 20-minute grinding operation blew past the excursion limit — that's still a violation, independent of the daily average."

*Common misuse:* treating the PEL as the only number that matters and ignoring the excursion limit on short, intense tasks that don't move the daily average much.

### Clearance vs. visual inspection

**Visual inspection** is the pre-clearance step confirming no visible debris, dust, or residue remains — a precondition for calling in clearance sampling. **Clearance** is the lab-verified pass/fail result (PCM/TEM air sample or dust-wipe result against a numeric threshold) that legally releases the containment. A room can look clean and still fail clearance, or occasionally pass clearance despite looking dusty if the dust isn't the regulated contaminant.

*In use:* "It passed visual, so we called in the clearance sample — we don't pull containment until that result's back, regardless of how clean it looks."

*Common misuse:* treating a clean visual inspection as sufficient to end the job, skipping or not waiting on the actual clearance sample.

### APF (Assigned Protection Factor) vs. filter rating

**APF** is the respirator-class-level protection factor set by OSHA (half-face APR: 10; full-facepiece APR: 50; tight-fitting full-facepiece PAPR: 1,000) — it describes how much the respirator class reduces exposure, assuming proper fit and seal. **Filter rating** (e.g., P100, N95) describes the filter media's particulate-capture efficiency, a separate spec that doesn't by itself determine the overall protection factor — a P100 filter on a leaking half-face mask still only delivers APF 10 worth of real-world protection.

*In use:* "The filter's P100, that's not the question — the question is whether a half-face APR's APF of 10 is enough margin at this task's exposure level."

*Common misuse:* citing the filter's particulate rating (P100) as if it answers the respirator-class-selection question, when the APF of the respirator class is the number that actually bounds effective exposure.

### Class I-IV asbestos work vs. Condition 1-3 mold

**Class I-IV** (OSHA 1926.1101) categorizes asbestos work by material and disturbance type — Class I (TSI/surfacing material removal, highest exposure potential) down to Class IV (custodial cleanup of asbestos debris). **Condition 1-3** (IICRC S520) categorizes mold situations by fungal ecology status — Condition 1 (normal), Condition 2 (settled spores, no active growth), Condition 3 (actual active growth) — and drives remediation/containment approach independent of the physical size of the area.

*In use:* "This is Class II floor tile work, not Class I — different default respirator and containment expectations even though it's the same building."

*Common misuse:* using square footage alone to set the asbestos work class or the mold containment level, when the material/disturbance type (asbestos) or fungal condition (mold) is what actually sets the category.

### Negative exposure assessment (NEA)

A documented set of prior monitoring data or objective data demonstrating that employee exposure on a specific task will consistently stay below the PEL — the only basis under 1926.1101 for stepping down respiratory protection from the regulatory default for a given work class.

*In use:* "We can run half-face APR on this Class II tile job because the NEA on file shows this exact task type at 0.04 f/cc across the last six sampled jobs — without that NEA, we'd default to the higher class."

*Common misuse:* stepping down respirator class based on general experience or "it's usually fine" rather than the specific documented NEA the standard requires.

### Amended water vs. encapsulant

**Amended water** is water with a surfactant added to reduce surface tension, used to wet asbestos-containing material before disturbance so fibers release into water droplets instead of air. An **encapsulant** is a sealant applied to bind and seal a contaminant in place as a long-term control instead of removing it — a different strategy (in-place management) from removal-with-wetting.

*In use:* "We're removing and disposing here, so it's amended water at the point of cut — encapsulant's for the areas we're leaving in place under the O&M plan."

*Common misuse:* treating encapsulant as a substitute control during active removal, when it's a separate strategy for material that isn't being removed at all.

### Aggressive air sampling

A clearance-sampling technique that deliberately agitates settled dust (leaf blowers, fans directed at surfaces) during air sampling to give a worst-case reading — used specifically because a passive, undisturbed air sample would understate what re-occupancy activity could resuspend.

*In use:* "Clearance sampling here has to be aggressive, not passive — a passive sample after we've swept everything down would just tell us the air was calm, not that the room's actually clean."

*Common misuse:* running a passive (non-agitated) air sample and treating a low passive reading as equivalent to a passing aggressive clearance result.

### NESHAP vs. RRP

**NESHAP** (National Emission Standards for Hazardous Air Pollutants, 40 CFR Part 61 Subpart M) is the EPA asbestos regulation governing notification, wetting, and disposal for renovation/demolition work above specific quantity thresholds. **RRP** (Renovation, Repair, and Painting Rule, 40 CFR Part 745 Subpart E) is the separate EPA lead rule governing certified-renovator requirements, containment, and dust-wipe clearance for pre-1978 housing and child-occupied facilities. They're triggered by different contaminants, different thresholds, and different paperwork — a project can trigger one, both, or neither depending on materials present.

*In use:* "This building's pre-1978, so RRP applies regardless of the asbestos question — check the TSI separately against the NESHAP threshold."

*Common misuse:* treating "hazmat regulation" as one undifferentiated compliance category and citing the wrong rule's threshold or paperwork for the contaminant actually present.

### Regulated area vs. containment/enclosure

The **regulated area** is the legal boundary (per 1926.1101) beyond which only authorized, properly-equipped personnel may enter — a compliance/access-control concept. The **containment/enclosure** is the physical structure (poly sheeting, negative air, airlocks) built to achieve that boundary in practice. The two usually coincide but aren't identical — a regulated area can be marked with barrier tape and signage even before physical containment is fully built, and the legal access restriction applies from the moment the area is designated, not from when the plastic goes up.

*In use:* "The regulated area's been posted since this morning — nobody without the training and PPE crosses that tape, containment or no containment yet."

*Common misuse:* treating the physical plastic enclosure as the only thing that matters, and allowing casual access to a posted-but-not-yet-physically-enclosed regulated area.

### Dust-lead hazard standard vs. clearance level

The **dust-lead hazard standard** is the threshold at which lead dust in existing housing is considered a hazard requiring action, independent of any recent work. The **clearance level** is a separate, generally stricter post-abatement/post-RRP-work threshold that a surface must test at or below before an area is released back for occupancy after remediation. A surface can be below the hazard standard but still fail a clearance test if the applicable clearance level for that surface type is lower.

*In use:* "The hazard standard would call this floor acceptable, but we're doing post-abatement clearance here, and the clearance level for floors is stricter — this sample still needs to come in under that number specifically."

*Common misuse:* citing the (often higher) hazard standard number when the clearance level is the applicable threshold for releasing a just-completed job.
