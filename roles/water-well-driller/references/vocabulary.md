### Specific capacity
Pumping rate divided by the stabilized drawdown it took to produce that rate (gpm/ft).
**In use:** "The well tests at 2.5 gpm per foot — that's what we size the pump against, not the 50 gpm peak we saw during development."
**Common misuse:** treating gross yield ("it makes 50 gpm") as the design number instead of the rate normalized by drawdown; two wells with identical gross yield can have wildly different specific capacities and need very different pumps.

### Drawdown
The drop in water level in a pumping well, measured from the pre-pumping static level to the level under a given pumping rate.
**In use:** "Drawdown stabilized at 20 ft after six hours — static was 48, pumping level is 68."
**Common misuse:** confusing drawdown with total pumping water level; drawdown is the *difference*, not the depth to water while pumping.

### Well development
The process of removing drilling-induced fines and mud cake from the formation and gravel pack around the screen so the well can be pump-tested and produce at its natural capacity.
**In use:** "We're not stopping the surge-and-airlift cycle until discharge runs sand-free for a sustained pumping period, not just a few clear buckets."
**Common misuse:** treating a few minutes of clear-looking discharge as proof development is complete; fines redistribute and a well that looks clear briefly can still pump sand once put into service.

### Gravel pack (filter pack)
Selected, uniformly graded gravel placed in the annulus around the screen to stabilize the formation and filter out fine material while passing water freely.
**In use:** "Formation D50 came back at 0.55 mm, so we're packing with an 8x12 mesh at roughly 2.4 mm — inside the 4-6x ratio."
**Common misuse:** picking pack material by what's on the truck rather than by the sieve analysis of the actual formation; using the same pack on every job regardless of grain size.

### Naturally developed well
A well with no artificial gravel pack, relying on aggressive development to remove fines from the formation immediately around the screen, viable only in coarse, well-graded formations.
**In use:** "This gravel's coarse and well-graded enough — Cu under 3 — we'll develop it naturally instead of packing it."
**Common misuse:** skipping the pack to save cost on a fine or poorly graded formation where it's actually required; "naturally developed" is a formation-driven design choice, not a shortcut available everywhere.

### Annular seal (sanitary seal)
The grout or bentonite placed in the annulus between the casing and the borehole wall, from surface down to a specified depth, to prevent surface and shallow contamination from reaching the aquifer.
**In use:** "State minimum is 50 ft, but the clay runs to 65 — we're sealing to 65."
**Common misuse:** treating the state code minimum as the design depth rather than a floor; the seal should reach the confining layer actually found in the cuttings when that's deeper than the code minimum.

### Artesian (flowing) well
A well completed in a confined aquifer under enough pressure that the water level rises above the top of the aquifer, sometimes above ground surface, without pumping.
**In use:** "Water level's climbing while we're still drilling — stage the valve before we go any deeper, this looks artesian."
**Common misuse:** using "artesian" loosely to mean any high-yield well; it specifically describes confined-aquifer pressure behavior, not production rate.

### Lost circulation
Sudden or gradual loss of drilling fluid into the formation instead of returning to surface, typically from a cavern, solution channel, or highly permeable zone.
**In use:** "We lost full returns at 140 ft — that's a gravel channel, not a slow seep."
**Common misuse:** treating any reduction in return flow as lost circulation; a gradual thinning from fluid loss to a moderately permeable zone is a different problem (and fix) than a sudden total loss.

### Sand pumping
Formation or gravel-pack sand passing through the screen into the pumped water, usually surfacing as a customer complaint about grit or pump wear.
**In use:** "Sand pumping within the first year almost always traces back to slot size versus pack ratio, not the pump."
**Common misuse:** assuming sand pumping is a pump problem to be fixed by changing pump models, when the root cause is nearly always screen/pack sizing at the well.

### Step-drawdown test
A pump test run at several increasing rates in sequence, each held long enough to observe drawdown, used to separate aquifer loss from well loss.
**In use:** "Drawdown roughly doubled between the last two steps — that's well loss climbing, not the aquifer running out."
**Common misuse:** running a single-rate test and calling it a step test; the diagnostic value comes specifically from comparing multiple rates against each other.

### Well loss vs. aquifer loss
Well loss is drawdown caused by inefficiencies in the well itself (undersized screen, incomplete development, pack too fine); aquifer loss is drawdown inherent to the formation's transmissivity, roughly linear with pumping rate. Well loss grows with roughly the square of the rate.
**In use:** "That curve bending upward at higher rates says well loss, not aquifer loss — nothing about the formation changed between steps."
**Common misuse:** attributing all drawdown to "the aquifer" when a step test would show most of it is construction-driven and fixable.

### Tremie
A pipe run to the bottom of the interval being grouted or gravel-packed, used to place material from the bottom up so it doesn't bridge or segregate on the way down.
**In use:** "Pump the grout through the tremie from the bottom — don't just pour it down the annulus from surface."
**Common misuse:** pouring grout or gravel from the top on deep or narrow annuli, which bridges partway down and leaves voids below.

### Driller's log vs. completion report
The driller's log is the real-time, depth-by-depth record of formations and observations made while drilling; the completion report is the formal document filed with the state summarizing final construction (casing, seal, screen, test results).
**In use:** "The completion report says 65 ft of seal — pull the driller's log to confirm that's actually where the confining clay was called."
**Common misuse:** treating the completion report alone as sufficient documentation; it summarizes construction but doesn't carry the depth-by-depth lithology detail needed to diagnose a later problem.

### Slot size (screen aperture)
The width of the openings in a well screen, sized to retain the gravel pack (or formation, if naturally developed) while passing water.
**In use:** "0.020-in slot retains about 90% of the 8x12 pack — that's the target, not the pack's D50."
**Common misuse:** sizing slot to the pack's median grain size (D50) instead of its finer end (D10-D15), which lets roughly half the pack material through.

### Standard Penetration Test (SPT) blow count
A geotechnical sampling method (ASTM D1586) using a 140-lb hammer dropped 30 in to drive a split-spoon sampler, with blows for the final 12 in of a standard drive reported as the N-value.
**In use:** "N-value of 8 in that interval — loose enough the geotech's going to want a different foundation approach."
**Common misuse:** conflating SPT blow counts with drilling penetration rate; they measure soil resistance to a standardized sampler, not how fast the rig is advancing.
