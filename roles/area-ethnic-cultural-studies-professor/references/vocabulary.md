# Vocabulary

### Area studies
A field organized around a world region (Latin American Studies, East Asian Studies, Middle East Studies), typically drawing methods from multiple disciplines to study that region specifically.
**In use:** "Our Title VI eligibility depends on being classified as area studies covering Latin America and the Caribbean, not as an identity-based field."
**Common misuse:** treating "area studies" and "ethnic studies" as interchangeable — area studies is organized by world region, ethnic studies by a group's history and identity (often within one country); the distinction determines different federal funding eligibility (Title VI applies to area studies, not ethnic studies).

### Ethnic studies
A field organized around the history, culture, and social position of a racial or ethnic group, often within a national context (African American Studies, Chicana/o Studies, Asian American Studies).
**In use:** "The HB 2281 case is the field's standing reference point for how exposed ethnic studies curricula are to legislative challenge."
**Common misuse:** assuming it's a synonym for "diversity education" broadly — it's a specific academic field with its own methods, journals, and professional associations (e.g., the National Association for Ethnic Studies), not a compliance category.

### Cultural studies
An interdisciplinary field analyzing culture (media, texts, practices) through frameworks like power, ideology, and identity, methodologically closer to literary/critical theory than to area or ethnic studies' regional/group focus.
**In use:** "That manuscript reads as cultural studies methodology — textual and media analysis — not the ethnographic fieldwork our program's T&P criteria describe."
**Common misuse:** conflating it with "cultural competency" training — cultural studies is a critical-theoretical academic discipline, not a professional-development category.

### Joint appointment (cross-appointment)
A faculty line split across two academic units, each holding a fractional claim on the person's teaching, service, and evaluation.
**In use:** "Without the MOU, the joint appointment defaults to both chairs assuming the other is tracking service load."
**Common misuse:** assuming "joint" means "shared and therefore lighter" — without an explicit split document, it more often becomes additive, not shared.

### FTE (full-time equivalent)
The fractional unit used to express how much of a full appointment a person holds in a given unit (e.g., 0.5 FTE in each of two departments).
**In use:** "The written MOU caps program-side service at what's proportionate to a 0.5 FTE line, not a full department member's load."
**Common misuse:** treating FTE splits as automatically enforced — the number exists in a policy document; someone still has to audit actual committee assignments against it.

### Cross-listing
Offering the same course under two or more department/program codes so it counts toward multiple degree or requirement pipelines simultaneously.
**In use:** "Cross-listing the course with the gen-ed diversity requirement moved projected enrollment from 5 to 12 without changing the syllabus."
**Common misuse:** treating cross-listing as a purely administrative formality — done well, it's the main lever that keeps a small program's low-enrolled courses from being cancelled.

### Tenure home
The specific department or unit that holds the tenure line and whose promotion criteria are binding for the case, as distinct from a program that may also weigh in.
**In use:** "Anthropology is the tenure home here, so its written criteria govern even though the program's criteria are cited as a secondary standard."
**Common misuse:** assuming a joint appointment has two tenure homes — in nearly every institution, only one unit actually holds the line and casts the binding vote.

### Mid-tenure review (third-year review)
A formal, non-binding progress check partway through the tenure clock (commonly year 3 of 6), assessing trajectory rather than issuing a final decision.
**In use:** "The mid-tenure letter flagged the manuscript timeline as the binding constraint, not the article count, which was already on pace."
**Common misuse:** treating it as a pass/fail gate like the final tenure decision — its actual function is to name the risk early enough to still fix it.

### External letters
Evaluative letters solicited from scholars outside the institution, assessing the significance of a tenure or promotion candidate's research contribution.
**In use:** "The letter list needs reviewers who can speak to both the discipline's standards and the field-specific contribution, or half the case goes unevaluated."
**Common misuse:** assuming any senior scholar in a loosely related field can serve — reviewers need direct familiarity with the specific interdisciplinary method being evaluated.

### Community-engaged scholarship (public scholarship)
Research conducted in direct partnership with, or oriented toward direct use by, a non-academic community — oral histories, participatory action research, public curricula.
**In use:** "The oral-history archive counts as community-engaged scholarship under the program's criteria, but it still needs a named anchor before a merit committee will treat it as evidence."
**Common misuse:** assuming the format alone (because a partner organization is involved) makes it self-evidently countable in a merit file — it still requires explicit translation into the binding rubric's terms.

### Cultural taxation
The disproportionate, often uncompensated service burden (mentoring, DEI committees, diversity-representative roles) placed on faculty of color and women, especially in interdisciplinary and ethnic/area-studies fields.
**In use:** "Four committee assignments on a 0.5 FTE joint line is a textbook cultural-taxation pattern, not an individual overcommitment."
**Common misuse:** treating it as an individual time-management failure rather than a structural, measurable pattern that shows up disproportionately by rank, race, and gender.

### Title VI National Resource Center (NRC)
A U.S. Department of Education designation and funding mechanism for area-studies programs (region-focused) meeting curriculum-breadth and language-instruction criteria.
**In use:** "NRC status is what funds the FLAS lines and the outreach budget — it's a specific, competitive federal designation, not a general grant."
**Common misuse:** assuming any interdisciplinary studies program (including ethnic-studies programs) is eligible — NRC/Title VI applies specifically to region-focused area studies, not identity-focused ethnic studies.

### FLAS (Foreign Language and Area Studies) Fellowship
A federally funded fellowship, administered through NRC-designated centers, supporting graduate (and some undergraduate) study of a less-commonly-taught language paired with area-studies coursework.
**In use:** "The renewal narrative needs a language-diversity breakdown of FLAS awardees, not just a total count."
**Common misuse:** reporting only the total number of fellowships awarded — reviewers weigh which languages were funded, especially less-commonly-taught ones, not just raw count.

### Adjunctification
The structural shift of core curriculum teaching from tenure-line to contingent/adjunct faculty, reducing continuity in mentoring and program development.
**In use:** "At 60% adjunct-taught core sections, the program has an adjunctification problem that a strong individual course roster doesn't fix."
**Common misuse:** treating each adjunct hire as an isolated staffing decision rather than tracking the cumulative structural pattern across years.
