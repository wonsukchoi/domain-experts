# Vocabulary

Terms of art in academic English studies that generalists misuse. Format per term: definition, how a practitioner actually uses it, the common misuse.

### Monograph

- **Definition:** a single-authored, book-length scholarly work published by an (typically university) press — the primary form of original research output expected in most English tenure cases.
- **In use:** "The tenure case rests on the monograph being under contract by year four — the articles are supporting evidence, not the spine."
- **Common misuse:** treating a strong article count as an automatic substitute for the book in a department whose actual written or unwritten criteria still center on it.

### Reader report (press)

- **Definition:** an outside scholar's written evaluation of a submitted manuscript, commissioned by a university press's acquisitions editor, that recommends acceptance, revision, or rejection.
- **In use:** "Both reader reports came back asking for a stronger theoretical framing in chapter two — that's an R&R with the press, not a rejection."
- **Common misuse:** conflating a request for revision with a rejection, or treating a single reader's critical comment as the press's final position before the acquisitions editor has weighed both reports.

### Option clause

- **Definition:** a contract provision giving the same press right of first refusal on an author's next book, or specifying delivery-deadline consequences for the current one.
- **In use:** "The option clause gives them first look at the next project, but the delivery deadline on this one is the part that actually needs managing now."
- **Common misuse:** assuming a signed contract removes all further deadline risk; the delivery clause remains binding and can be renegotiated only if raised before it lapses.

### WPA Outcomes Statement

- **Definition:** the Council of Writing Program Administrators' consensus document defining what a first-year composition course should produce, organized around rhetorical knowledge, critical thinking/reading/writing, processes, and knowledge of conventions.
- **In use:** "Every section, whoever's teaching it, gets graded against the Outcomes Statement categories — that's the actual instrument holding the program together."
- **Common misuse:** treating it as a content list to "cover" rather than a set of outcomes each assignment has to produce evidence of.

### Turnitin similarity score / originality report

- **Definition:** a percentage representing how much of a submitted paper's text matches other indexed sources — not a measure of plagiarism by itself.
- **In use:** "34% breaks down as 22% quoted and cited material, 12% common phrasing against the same prompt's paper pool — no unattributed match at all."
- **Common misuse:** treating the headline percentage as a plagiarism verdict without reading the source-by-source breakdown that generated it.

### Contract cheating / essay mill

- **Definition:** the practice of paying a third party (a person or a commercial service) to produce academic work submitted as the student's own — distinct from AI-generated text, though the two are often confused.
- **In use:** "The register mismatch with her in-class diagnostic and the total absence of any draft material points to contract cheating, not AI use."
- **Common misuse:** assuming any suspicious-looking submission is AI-generated by default, when a stylistic register mismatch with no draft history is more classically a contract-cheating signature.

### FYC (first-year composition)

- **Definition:** the required introductory writing course (or sequence) most undergraduates take, typically staffed across many sections by a mix of tenure-line faculty, lecturers, adjuncts, and graduate TAs.
- **In use:** "FYC is where the shared-rubric problem is sharpest — thirty sections, thirty instructors, one outcomes statement."
- **Common misuse:** treating FYC as a lower-stakes or purely service course; it's the program's highest-exposure quality-control problem precisely because of how many different instructors teach it.

### Adjunctification (composition staffing)

- **Definition:** the structural shift of introductory writing instruction toward non-tenure-track faculty (adjuncts, lecturers, graduate TAs) with less job security and typically no scholarly-research expectation.
- **In use:** "Most of our FYC sections are staffed by contingent instructors this year — the norming session isn't optional, it's the only shared quality instrument we have."
- **Common misuse:** treating contingent staffing as a neutral scheduling fact rather than the reason a shared rubric and calibration process are load-bearing rather than optional.

### MLA style vs. Chicago style

- **Definition:** two distinct citation and formatting systems; MLA (Modern Language Association) is the default in most literature and composition scholarship, while Chicago (notes-bibliography or author-date) is used where a specific venue, press, or subfield (often more historical or interdisciplinary work) specifies it.
- **In use:** "The journal wants Chicago notes-bibliography, not MLA in-text — check the venue's style sheet before formatting, not after."
- **Common misuse:** assuming MLA is the universal default across all humanities venues; presses and journals frequently specify Chicago or a house style instead.

### Trigger warning / content note

- **Definition:** an advance notice to students that upcoming material contains potentially distressing content — distinct from academic-freedom protections around the instructor's choice of what and how to teach.
- **In use:** "I add a one-line content note on the syllabus for the assault scene in the assigned novel, but that's my pedagogical choice, not a required accommodation."
- **Common misuse:** treating trigger warnings as either mandatory or as a substitute for actual contextualizing discussion in class — AAUP's position is that they're neither required nor sufficient on their own.

### Portfolio grading / process grading

- **Definition:** an assessment approach that grades a body of work across drafts, revisions, and a reflective memo, rather than a single final draft in isolation.
- **In use:** "The grade reflects the whole portfolio — draft one, the peer-review notes, draft two, and the reflection — not just how the final version reads cold."
- **Common misuse:** grading only the polished final draft while calling it "portfolio grading" because a folder of drafts technically exists, without actually scoring the revision process itself.

### Norming session / calibration

- **Definition:** a pre-grading meeting where multiple instructors independently score the same sample student work against a shared rubric, then reconcile differences in how they're reading the rubric's language.
- **In use:** "We normed on three sample essays this morning before anyone touches a live stack — that's what keeps thirty sections grading the same rubric the same way."
- **Common misuse:** holding a norming session once at the start of the term and treating it as sufficient calibration for every assignment that follows, rather than repeating it before each major graded piece.

### Digital humanities (DH)

- **Definition:** scholarship and pedagogy applying computational methods (text mining, corpus analysis, digital archives, network visualization) to literary or historical texts.
- **In use:** "The project's a DH argument built on topic-modeling across the full run of a 19th-century periodical, not a traditional close reading."
- **Common misuse:** treating any use of digital tools (a searchable database, a course website) as "doing DH," when the term specifically denotes computational methods generating the scholarly argument itself.

### Canon / canon debate

- **Definition:** the set of literary texts conventionally treated as most worth studying within a tradition, and the ongoing scholarly and pedagogical argument over which texts belong in it and why.
- **In use:** "The syllabus redesign isn't abandoning the canon, it's arguing for a different canon — that's a substantive scholarly position, not a lowering of standards."
- **Common misuse:** treating "canon debate" as synonymous with "lowering rigor," when it's a live scholarly argument about inclusion criteria, not a rejection of the idea that some texts merit sustained study.
