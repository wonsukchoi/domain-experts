# Playbook

Filled templates and step sequences, not descriptions of them. Defaults, not laws — deviate consciously and note why in the record you keep.

## 1. First-Year Composition syllabus — WPA Outcomes mapped to assessment

Course: First-Year Composition, 14-week term, shared across multiple sections against one outcomes rubric. Build the assessment column from the WPA Outcomes Statement (3.0) categories before the activities column.

| WPA outcome category | Specific course objective | Assessment producing evidence | Weight |
|---|---|---|---|
| Rhetorical knowledge | Adapt a single argument for two different audiences (op-ed vs. academic memo) | Essay 2 — audience-adaptation pair (2 x 2 pp.) | 20% |
| Critical thinking, reading, writing | Evaluate a source for what it actually supports vs. what it's cited to support | Essay 3 — argumentative essay with annotated source log | 25% |
| Processes | Produce and revise across at least two substantively different drafts | Portfolio: draft 1, peer-review notes, draft 2, reflection memo | 30% |
| Knowledge of conventions | Apply genre conventions and correctly attribute sources in MLA format | Works Cited accuracy check on Essays 2 and 3 (rubric-scored, not pass/fail) | 15% |
| Rhetorical knowledge + processes (combined) | Give and use peer feedback against the shared rubric | Peer-review workshop participation, scored on specificity of comments given | 10% |

Rule: if an outcome category has no row in the assessment column, cut the objective or add the assessment — an outcome that's only "discussed in class" isn't being taught, it's being mentioned.

## 2. Monograph submission pipeline — stage, timeline, action

Confirm the actual press's stated timeline before building a tenure-file narrative around the ranges below — they vary by press and subfield backlog.

| Stage | Typical duration | Action |
|---|---|---|
| Proposal query to acquisitions editor | 2–8 weeks for initial response | Query 1–2 presses in the subfield's top tier first; a non-response past 8 weeks warrants a polite follow-up, not a re-query elsewhere yet |
| Full manuscript sent for outside review | 4–9 months for reader reports | Circulate to 1–2 trusted colleagues informally in parallel — catching a structural problem here is cheaper than catching it in the reader reports |
| Reader reports received (typically 2 outside readers) | Arrives at 4–9 months, all at once | Read for "revise and resubmit to the press" (recoverable, address point-by-point) vs. "reject" (rare at this stage if the press commissioned full review) — most reports request revision, not rejection |
| Editorial board approval | 4–8 weeks after revised manuscript resubmitted | Track as a discrete milestone in the tenure file — "under contract" only starts here, not at proposal stage |
| Contract signed, manuscript delivery deadline set | Contract typically specifies 6–18 months to final delivery | Set the delivery deadline against the tenure review date with a buffer — production adds time after delivery, it isn't the finish line |
| Production (copyediting, typesetting, indexing) | 9–14 months after final manuscript delivery | Budget for this in the tenure timeline explicitly; a "delivered" manuscript is not yet a "published" book, and some tenure clocks require the latter |

## 3. AI-writing / plagiarism integrity review flow

1. Does the flag come from a single tool score alone (Turnitin percentage or AI-detector probability) with no other evidence? Yes → do not initiate contact with the student yet; assemble the fuller evidence package first (steps 2–4).
2. Pull the Turnitin source-breakdown report. Decompose the percentage: how much is quoted/cited material, how much is bibliography, how much is unattributed match to a source not cited anywhere in the paper. Only the last category is a live concern.
3. If an AI-detector was used, cross-check the student's language background (registrar ESL/multilingual flag) and pull any available draft or version history (LMS submission timestamps, Google Docs revision history, or a request for the student's planning notes).
4. Compare the flagged essay's sentence-level register (habitual grammar patterns, vocabulary range) against an earlier proctored or in-class writing sample from the same student.
5. Decision: unattributed-source match found and no matching in-class register → integrity referral, with the specific matched passage identified. No unattributed match, and version history or register evidence supports authorship → decline referral in writing, filed with the writing program administrator per CCCC's 2023 statement discouraging AI-detector scores as standalone evidence. Ambiguous on both counts → conference with the student first, request the planning materials, before any formal referral.

## 4. Content-complaint decision protocol (literary text)

Before responding to any complaint about an assigned text, classify it:

- **Content complaint** (objects to the subject matter itself — violence, sexuality, racial language in the text) → check whether a contextualizing framing is already on record (syllabus content note, first-day discussion of why the text is taught). If yes, respond citing that record; content-only objections rarely succeed against the AAUP academic-freedom standard. If no framing is on record, add one going forward, but the past teaching is still evaluated on what was actually said in class, not assumed.
- **Framing complaint** (objects to how it was taught — no context given, no discussion of the difficult material, ambush assignment) → this is the complaint that actually escalates. Pull lecture notes/slides for that specific session; if no contextualizing discussion is documented, this is a real gap to correct, not a defensible academic-freedom position.
- **Category error** (complainant wants "the other side" represented on a text that is an aesthetic/historical artifact, not a live policy debate) → clarify in the response that the "represent multiple defensible positions" standard applies to genuinely open normative or policy questions, not to whether a canonical work of literature gets taught — misapplying it here is the error, not the absence of "balance."

Documentation to keep contemporaneously for any text likely to draw a complaint: the syllabus content note (if any), the session's lecture materials, and a one-line note on the pedagogical rationale for teaching it — this is the record a grievance process actually reviews.

## 5. Composition-program norming session (multi-section calibration)

1. Select 2–3 sample essays spanning the grade range (strong, middling, weak) for the upcoming graded assignment before any section grades it live.
2. All instructors (adjunct, TA, lecturer, tenure-line) score the samples independently against the shared rubric, without seeing others' scores first.
3. Compare scores; where two instructors differ by more than one rubric band on the same essay, discuss which descriptor language each was applying — the goal is a shared reading of the rubric's language, not forced agreement on the sample's exact grade.
4. Record the calibrated exemplar scores and file them as the reference standard for that assignment across all sections that term.
5. Repeat before each major graded assignment, not once per term — rubric drift across a term is as real as drift across years.
