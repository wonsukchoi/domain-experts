# Red Flags

Smell tests across teaching, scholarship, and program staffing. Format per flag: what it usually means, the first question to ask, the data to pull.

### Turnitin similarity score above ~25%, driven by quoted and bibliography matches

- **Usually means:** the score is a structural artifact of proper citation and Works Cited formatting, not unattributed copying — the raw percentage alone cannot distinguish the two.
- **First question:** "Of that percentage, how much comes from quoted/cited blocks and the bibliography versus an unattributed match to a source not cited anywhere in the paper?"
- **Data to pull:** the source-by-source breakdown in the similarity report, isolating any single contiguous unattributed match over roughly 10–15%.

### AI-detector score above ~80% AI-probability on a multilingual or ESL-flagged student's essay

- **Usually means:** the tool has landed on the exact population Liang et al. (2023) documented as its worst-case false-positive rate — non-native English writing patterns — rather than genuine AI generation.
- **First question:** "Does this student have a registrar language-background flag, and what does their earlier in-class writing sample look like at the sentence level?"
- **Data to pull:** drafting/version history (LMS or Google Docs revision timestamps), an earlier proctored writing sample for register comparison.

### Manuscript under external review by a university press past 9 months with no reader reports back

- **Usually means:** it's stalled with an unresponsive or overcommitted outside reader, not necessarily a negative signal about the manuscript.
- **First question:** "Has it passed the press's own typical review window, and has the acquisitions editor been asked for a status update?"
- **Data to pull:** submission date, press's stated typical timeline (if published), a dated status inquiry sent to the acquisitions editor.

### Multi-section composition course with no norming session held before the term's first major graded assignment

- **Usually means:** grade variance across sections this term is a missing-calibration problem, not an individual-instructor-quality problem, and will show up as inconsistent outcomes data that gets misattributed.
- **First question:** "Did every instructor teaching this course score the same sample essays before grading independently, and how much did their scores vary?"
- **Data to pull:** norming-session sample scores by instructor, or documented absence of a norming session that term.

### A discomfort or bias complaint naming a specific assigned literary text

- **Usually means:** either a genuine framing gap (no contextualizing discussion on record) or a content-only objection mistakenly treated as a framing problem — the response differs sharply between the two.
- **First question:** "Is the complaint about the subject matter itself, or about how the material was actually taught in that session?"
- **Data to pull:** the syllabus content note (if any) for that text, the session's lecture materials, any contemporaneous student questions logged.

### Book proposal or manuscript query sent to only one press with no parallel query

- **Usually means:** a single non-response or slow initial read is being treated as a dead end rather than one data point, wasting calendar time a tenure clock doesn't have.
- **First question:** "Has this proposal gone to more than one press in the subfield's plausible tier, and on what timeline?"
- **Data to pull:** query log with press names, dates, and response status.

### A reader report requesting "major revisions" characterized informally as a rejection

- **Usually means:** the author is reading a normal, recoverable revise-and-resubmit request from the press as a terminal verdict, which can lead to abandoning a viable project.
- **First question:** "Does the report actually reject the project, or does it request specific, addressable changes?"
- **Data to pull:** the full reader report text, itemized into addressable revisions versus any language suggesting the press won't proceed regardless.

### Book contract option-clause deadline within 3 months and manuscript materially incomplete

- **Usually means:** a delivery-deadline miss is close enough to require an explicit renegotiation conversation with the press now, not after the deadline passes.
- **First question:** "Has the acquisitions editor been proactively contacted about an extension, or is this deadline being allowed to simply arrive?"
- **Data to pull:** contract delivery clause language, current manuscript completion status, any prior extension history with this press.

### Publication count cited alone in a book-culture department's tenure or hiring file, without noting book-vs-article mix

- **Usually means:** the count is being used to imply a scholarly-productivity judgment the raw number can't support in a department where the unwritten standard is a monograph, not an article tally.
- **First question:** "Does the department's actual written tenure criteria (not informal norms) specify a monograph requirement, and does this file address it directly?"
- **Data to pull:** the department's written tenure/promotion document, and where the monograph (or its absence) sits in the candidate's file.

### Grad TA or adjunct grading a high-stakes essay with no calibration against the shared rubric beforehand

- **Usually means:** inter-rater inconsistency large enough to generate grade disputes that are really a missing-process problem, not a problem with any specific essay.
- **First question:** "Did this instructor score any sample essays against the rubric with the rest of the teaching team before grading live?"
- **Data to pull:** norming-session participation record, or its absence, for this instructor and this assignment.

### A flagged essay's stylistic register sharply mismatches the same student's other in-class writing

- **Usually means:** either a contract-cheating/essay-mill submission (register mismatch with no plausible drafting explanation) or a legitimate but heavily tutor-edited draft — these require different next steps.
- **First question:** "Can the student produce any planning notes, outline, or draft predating the final submission, and does the in-class sample support or contradict authorship?"
- **Data to pull:** any available draft materials, an in-class writing sample for direct comparison, and (if available) document metadata on the submitted file.
