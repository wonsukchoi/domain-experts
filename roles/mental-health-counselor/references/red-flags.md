# Red Flags

### Passive ideation ("wish to be dead") reported for the first time after 2+ sessions of denial
- **Usually means:** a genuine shift in clinical state (worsening depression, new stressor); second most likely is that the alliance only now feels safe enough for the client to disclose something present all along.
- **First question:** "Has this been on your mind before now, or is it new?" — the answer changes whether you're tracking a new risk trajectory or a delayed disclosure of a stable one.
- **Data to pull:** prior C-SSRS screener results, PHQ-9 item 9 trend across all administrations, session notes for any minimized or deflected risk questions.

### Client cancels or no-shows the session immediately following a session where a rupture or hard disclosure occurred
- **Usually means:** an alliance rupture (withdrawal type) — the client is avoiding the discomfort of the last session, not disengaging from treatment broadly; second most likely is a genuine crisis making attendance impossible.
- **First question:** does this pattern repeat after emotionally intense sessions specifically, or is it random relative to session content?
- **Data to pull:** attendance log cross-referenced with session content flags (rupture, disclosure, confrontation) from the last 90 days.

### PHQ-9 (or equivalent) worsens by ≥5 points across two consecutive administrations despite adherence to the treatment plan
- **Usually means:** the modality doesn't fit the mechanism, or an undiagnosed factor (bipolar spectrum, substance use, unaddressed trauma, medical cause like thyroid) is driving the presentation; second most likely is a psychosocial stressor outside the treatment's reach (job loss, divorce) that needs to be named as the driver rather than treated as a symptom of the original diagnosis.
- **First question:** "What's changed in your life, medication, or substance use since the last measure?"
- **Data to pull:** full severity-measure trend line, medication adherence/change log, substance-use screen re-administration.

### Client discloses a specific, named target and a stated intention to harm them
- **Usually means:** an acute duty-to-warn/protect trigger under Tarasoff-derived case law or the state's statutory equivalent; second most likely is venting/catastrophizing with no actual intent, which still requires the same assessment to rule out.
- **First question:** "Is this a plan you intend to act on, or a thought you're having about them?" — intent and specificity, not the emotional intensity of the statement, drive the legal threshold.
- **Data to pull:** state statute or case law on duty to warn (varies — do not assume Tarasoff's exact rule applies), agency's duty-to-warn protocol, prior history of violence toward this target.

### Client reports a self-harm or suicide plan involving a firearm and the intake means-inventory listed "none in home"
- **Usually means:** access changed since intake (new partner, recent purchase, relapse into an unreported prior access) — this is a high-acuity finding regardless of ideation level, because access to a firearm is the single strongest predictor of attempt lethality; second most likely is that the original intake answer was inaccurate or incomplete.
- **First question:** "Where is it right now, and who else can get to it?"
- **Data to pull:** re-administer the means-restriction section of the safety plan immediately; do not wait for the next scheduled risk screen.

### Client under 18, or a dependent adult, discloses current abuse or neglect
- **Usually means:** a mandated-reporting threshold has been met — "reasonable suspicion" is the common statutory bar, which is lower than "confirmed" or "certain."
- **First question:** does what was disclosed meet this state's statutory definition of abuse/neglect, or is it a boundary/discipline issue that doesn't cross the reporting threshold?
- **Data to pull:** state mandated-reporter statute and definitions, agency's reporting protocol and timeline (often 24–48 hours), prior reports on this client/family if any.

### Client's affect and reported symptoms no longer match the working diagnosis after 6–8 sessions of appropriate treatment with no improvement
- **Usually means:** the working diagnosis was wrong (commonly: unrecognized bipolar spectrum misdiagnosed as unipolar depression, or a personality-disorder pattern underlying what looked like episodic mood symptoms) rather than treatment-resistant depression; second most likely is an unaddressed co-occurring substance use or medical condition.
- **First question:** "Has there ever been a period of unusually high energy, reduced need for sleep, or impulsivity that lasted several days?" — screens for a hypomanic history the intake may have missed.
- **Data to pull:** full psychiatric and family history re-review, treatment-response pattern against the current diagnosis's expected trajectory.

### Client requests session content be kept out of the chart, or asks what would happen "if I told you something and you couldn't write it down"
- **Usually means:** the client is testing the limits of confidentiality before disclosing something significant — often risk-related or legally sensitive; second most likely is a trust issue with documentation itself (prior negative experience with records being subpoenaed or shared).
- **First question:** name the actual limits of confidentiality directly ("here's exactly what I am and am not required to report") before the client discloses, so the disclosure that follows is informed.
- **Data to pull:** none yet — this is a pre-disclosure moment; the "data to pull" step comes after, once the actual content is known.

### LOCUS risk-of-harm dimension scores 3+ while functional status and engagement still score 1–2
- **Usually means:** a client who looks stable on the surface (working, attending sessions, engaged) but is carrying a risk level the surface presentation masks — the single-dimension override applies even though the composite looks mild; second most likely is a scoring error from under-probing the risk dimension specifically.
- **First question:** re-run the full C-SSRS rather than trusting the LOCUS risk score alone — LOCUS risk-of-harm is a summary judgment, not a substitute for the structured risk walk-through.
- **Data to pull:** most recent full C-SSRS results, not just the 6-item screener.

### Two or more consecutive sessions where the client agrees with every suggestion and reports no problems
- **Usually means:** either genuine and rapid improvement (rare at this pace) or a compliance/appeasement pattern common in ruptured alliances and in clients minimizing to avoid a level-of-care escalation they fear (hospitalization, losing custody, disclosure to a partner); second most likely is social desirability in a client who has learned what answers end the risk questions fastest.
- **First question:** "If something wasn't going well, what would that look like — would you tell me, or would you handle it on your own first?"
- **Data to pull:** severity-measure scores compared against session-content notes for the same period — a stated "no problems" against a rising PHQ-9 is the tell.
