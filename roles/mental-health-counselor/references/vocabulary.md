# Vocabulary

### Level of care
The intensity setting of treatment — outpatient, intensive outpatient (IOP), partial hospitalization (PHP), or inpatient — matched to acuity, not to what's convenient or covered by insurance.
**In use:** "Her LOCUS composite still reads outpatient, but I'm watching the risk-of-harm dimension closely — one bad week and we're having the IOP conversation."
**Common misuse:** treated as a permanent placement rather than a fluid decision reassessed at every significant change; also confused with treatment intensity within outpatient (weekly vs. biweekly), which is a frequency change, not a level-of-care change.

### Passive suicidal ideation
Thoughts of wanting to be dead or to not wake up, without active thoughts of self-directed action to bring that about.
**In use:** "He's endorsing passive ideation — wishing he wouldn't wake up — but denies any active thoughts of ending his life, no method, no plan."
**Common misuse:** treated as equivalent to "no risk" (it isn't — it still warrants a full C-SSRS) or, at the other extreme, treated as automatically requiring hospitalization (it doesn't — disposition depends on the full risk picture, not this label alone).

### Rupture (alliance rupture)
A strain or breakdown in the collaborative bond between counselor and client, surfacing as either confrontation (client expresses dissatisfaction directly) or withdrawal (client disengages, minimizes, or avoids).
**In use:** "That was a withdrawal rupture, not disinterest — she went from talking through the whole session to one-word answers right after I pushed on the avoidance pattern."
**Common misuse:** labeled only when a client is visibly upset or angry (the confrontation type); the far more common withdrawal type gets missed and mistaken for "the client is doing better and has less to process."

### Countertransference
The counselor's own emotional reactions to a client, shaped by the counselor's history, that can distort clinical judgment if unexamined.
**In use:** "I noticed I was avoiding pushing on his avoidance pattern the same way I avoid it with my own family — that's worth bringing to supervision before it shapes another session."
**Common misuse:** used as a catch-all excuse for any strong feeling toward a client (frustration at a genuinely difficult behavior isn't automatically countertransference) rather than reserved for reactions traceable to the counselor's own material distorting the work.

### Protective factor
A confirmed, specific circumstance or relationship that measurably lowers a client's near-term risk of self-harm, distinct from a general positive trait.
**In use:** "Her protective factors are concrete — two kids she names unprompted as her reason to keep going, a sister she talks to daily, confirmed no firearm access — not just 'she seems resilient.'"
**Common misuse:** inferred from a client's demeanor or personality ("she's so strong, she'll be fine") rather than confirmed as a specific, named, currently-active circumstance.

### Duty to warn / duty to protect
A legal obligation, triggered when a client discloses a serious and imminent threat to an identifiable third party, to take reasonable steps to protect that person — which may include notifying them, notifying law enforcement, or seeking involuntary hospitalization, depending on the jurisdiction's statute.
**In use:** "This crosses the duty-to-protect threshold under our state's statute — specific target, stated intent — I'm consulting with my supervisor and checking our reporting protocol before end of session."
**Common misuse:** assumed to be a single uniform national rule derived from Tarasoff; the actual trigger, required action, and even whether the duty exists at all varies by state statute and case law.

### Mandated reporting
The legal requirement for licensed clinicians to report suspected abuse or neglect of a child, elder, or dependent adult to the appropriate authority, typically on a "reasonable suspicion" standard rather than certainty.
**In use:** "I don't need to be sure abuse happened — reasonable suspicion is the bar, and what she described meets it, so I'm filing within our state's 24-hour window."
**Common misuse:** treated as requiring near-certainty before reporting, which delays or blocks reports the statute doesn't actually require the clinician to be sure about.

### Level-of-care step-up
Moving a client from their current treatment intensity to a higher one (outpatient → IOP → PHP → inpatient) because acuity has exceeded what the current setting can safely manage.
**In use:** "This is a step-up conversation, not a 'let's try harder in outpatient' conversation — the risk-of-harm dimension alone overrides the rest of the LOCUS composite."
**Common misuse:** delayed past the point the data supports, out of concern for preserving the therapeutic relationship, when the relationship is better served by a well-framed referral than by an outcome the current setting can't prevent.

### Working diagnosis
The current best-supported DSM-5-TR diagnosis, held provisionally and revisited as new history, non-response to treatment, or a new episode changes the clinical picture.
**In use:** "I'm holding this as a working diagnosis of MDD, single episode — if the hypomanic history I just heard about checks out, we're revising to bipolar II and that changes the medication referral entirely."
**Common misuse:** treated as fixed after intake, so a treatment plan keeps running against a diagnosis the ongoing data no longer supports.

### Modality (CBT / DBT / MI)
The structured therapeutic approach chosen to match the mechanism driving the presentation — cognitive-behavioral for distorted cognition-behavior loops, dialectical-behavioral for emotion dysregulation and self-harm, motivational interviewing for ambivalence about change.
**In use:** "She's not resistant to change, she's ambivalent about it — that's an MI problem this month, not a CBT problem; once she's committed, we move to behavioral activation."
**Common misuse:** chosen by clinician familiarity or agency default rather than mechanism fit, and "DBT" specifically misused to describe borrowing a few skills outside the full structured program (skills group, individual therapy, phone coaching, consultation team) that the model actually requires.

### Safety plan
A collaboratively built, written document specifying a client's personal warning signs, coping strategies, support contacts, professional contacts, and means-restriction steps, used proactively — distinct from a "no-harm contract."
**In use:** "We built the safety plan together this session — her actual warning signs, her sister's number, and the specific means-restriction step for the medication in the house — and she's keeping a copy on her phone."
**Common misuse:** conflated with a "no-suicide contract" (asking a client to promise not to harm themselves), which has no evidence of reducing risk and is not the same intervention.

### C-SSRS (Columbia Suicide Severity Rating Scale)
A structured instrument that grades suicidal ideation across five severity levels and separately tracks suicidal behavior, used to standardize risk assessment rather than rely on unstructured clinical impression.
**In use:** "On the C-SSRS she's at ideation level 2 — nonspecific active thoughts — no method, no plan, no behavior history; that's a different disposition than level 4."
**Common misuse:** administered as a single yes/no screener and treated as a complete risk assessment, when a positive screener is supposed to trigger the full graded walk-through, not stand in for it.

### Behavioral activation
A specific CBT technique that increases engagement in reinforcing activities to break the withdrawal-low mood-more withdrawal cycle in depression, distinct from generically "encouraging the client to do more."
**In use:** "We scheduled two specific activities this week — not 'get out more' — a walk with her sister Tuesday and cooking dinner Thursday, both things she rated as previously rewarding."
**Common misuse:** delivered as vague encouragement ("try to stay busy") rather than the scheduled, specific, previously-reinforcing activity the technique actually requires.
