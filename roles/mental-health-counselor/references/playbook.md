# Playbook: Intake, Risk Assessment, and Treatment Planning

Filled process for the three recurring executions of the role: structured intake, a full C-SSRS risk walk-through, and a LOCUS-anchored level-of-care decision. Adapt the specific questions to the client in front of you; keep the sequence and the thresholds.

## 1. Intake (first 2 sessions)

**Session 1 — history and baseline measures**

1. Presenting problem in the client's words, then a functional-impact question: "What has this cost you this month — work, a relationship, sleep?" A generalist stops at symptom checklist; the functional-impact answer is what actually differentiates severity.
2. Administer a validated severity measure matched to presentation: PHQ-9 (depression), GAD-7 (anxiety), or the agency's trauma-specific equivalent. Score and record the raw number, not just "moderate."
3. Psychiatric history: prior diagnoses, prior treatment (what was tried, what worked, what didn't, medication history and adherence), hospitalization history (dates, precipitants, length of stay).
4. Substance use screen (frequency/quantity, not just "do you drink") — co-occurring substance use changes both risk calculus and modality choice.
5. C-SSRS screener (6 yes/no items) regardless of presenting complaint — risk screening is not conditional on the client volunteering it.
6. Safety/means inventory: firearms in home, stockpiled medication, and who else has access to each.

**Session 2 — working diagnosis and plan**

7. State the working diagnosis using DSM-5-TR criteria explicitly matched to reported symptoms (not "depression" — "Major Depressive Disorder, single episode, moderate, per 6 of 9 criteria present ≥2 weeks including anhedonia and reported item-9-equivalent ideation").
8. Name the target mechanism before naming the modality (see SKILL.md's modality-matching heuristic).
9. Draft 2–3 measurable treatment goals tied to the functional-impact answer from step 1 ("return to full-time work attendance" scores against absenteeism, not "feel better").
10. Set the re-measurement cadence: severity measure every 2–4 sessions minimum, more often if trending worse.

## 2. Full C-SSRS Risk Walk-Through

Run this any time the screener flags positive, at intake, and whenever a client discloses a stressor with known elevated risk (job loss, relationship rupture, new diagnosis, discharge from a higher level of care).

**Ideation severity — ask in this order, stop escalating once the client answers "no" twice in a row on intensity items, but always ask the behavior section separately:**

| Level | Question form | If yes, follow-up |
|---|---|---|
| 1. Wish to be dead | "Have you wished you were dead or wished you could go to sleep and not wake up?" | Duration, frequency this week |
| 2. Nonspecific active ideation | "Have you had thoughts of killing yourself?" | How often, how long each episode lasts |
| 3. Active ideation with method, no plan/intent | "Have you thought about how you might do it?" | Which method, has it changed recently |
| 4. Active ideation with some intent | "Have you had these thoughts and had some intention of acting on them?" | What stopped them so far |
| 5. Active ideation with plan and intent | "Have you started to work out or worked out the details of how to kill yourself? Do you intend to carry out this plan?" | Timeline, access to means right now |

**Behavior — always ask independently of ideation level, in the last 3 months and lifetime:**

- Preparatory acts (collecting pills, writing a note, giving away possessions).
- Aborted attempt (started, stopped self before any act).
- Interrupted attempt (started, someone else stopped it).
- Actual attempt (lifetime count, most recent date, lethality, medical severity).

**Protective factors — ask explicitly, don't infer from absence of risk:**

- Reasons for living named unprompted vs. only when asked.
- Reachable support system (name a specific person and confirm they know how to reach them, not "my family").
- Engagement with treatment (attendance pattern, help-seeking behavior).
- Access to lethal means, confirmed directly, not assumed from demographics.

**Classification (map to disposition, not a label alone):**

| Pattern | Acute risk read | Typical disposition |
|---|---|---|
| No ideation, or wish-to-die only, strong protective factors | Low | Continue routine care; re-screen next session |
| Nonspecific/method ideation, no intent, no behavior, protective factors present | Moderate-low | Same-session safety plan; increase frequency; re-screen every visit until resolved 2 consecutive visits |
| Ideation with some intent, or any behavior in past 3 months, or thinning protective factors | Moderate-high | Same-session safety plan; consult supervisor same day; consider level-of-care step-up |
| Plan + intent + access to means, or recent interrupted/aborted attempt | High | Do not let client leave unaccompanied; involve crisis team/ED evaluation same session; document the specific access-to-means finding that drove the decision |

## 3. Safety Planning Intervention (Stanley-Brown, 6 steps — build with the client, don't hand them a form)

1. **Warning signs** — the client's own early-warning pattern (specific thought, image, mood, or behavior), in their words, not a generic list.
2. **Internal coping strategies** — something the client can do alone, tested for whether they can actually execute it in the state warning signs describe (a "10-minute walk" fails for a client whose warning sign is not being able to get out of bed — use a strategy that requires less activation).
3. **Social settings/people for distraction** (not for discussing the crisis) — a specific place or person, not "spend time with friends."
4. **Specific people to ask for help** — name and number, confirmed reachable, confirmed aware they're on this list if appropriate.
5. **Professionals/agencies to contact** — clinician's after-hours protocol, 988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline, nearest ED, confirmed with correct current numbers.
6. **Means restriction** — the single highest-leverage step and the one most often skipped: name the specific lethal means in this client's environment and the specific restriction (removal, lock-box, third-party holds keys/medication) agreed to today, not "be careful."

Give the client a physical or digital copy. A safety plan that lives only in the chart protects the chart, not the client.

## 4. LOCUS-Anchored Level-of-Care Decision

Score each dimension 1 (minimal) to 4 (severe); this is a structured judgment aid, not an arithmetic cutoff — use the composite pattern, not a single sum, to decide.

| Dimension | 1 (Outpatient-appropriate) | 4 (Higher level of care indicated) |
|---|---|---|
| Risk of harm | No ideation or low-acuity per C-SSRS above | Plan + intent + access, or recent attempt |
| Functional status | Maintaining work/school/self-care | Unable to maintain basic self-care or safety |
| Co-occurring conditions | None or stable, managed | Active untreated substance use or medical instability complicating psychiatric presentation |
| Recovery environment | Stable housing, supportive relationships | Unsafe home, no support, active conflict/abuse |
| Treatment/recovery history | Responds to outpatient care, no prior higher-LOC need | Repeated outpatient failures, prior hospitalizations with early relapse |
| Engagement/motivation | Attends, participates, discloses | Refuses care, actively avoidant, or unable to engage safely |

**Decision rule:** any single dimension at "4" plus risk of harm at 3+ is a same-day step-up conversation (IOP/PHP/inpatient), regardless of how the other dimensions score — this mirrors the single-parameter-override logic used in medical early-warning scores: one severe finding overrides an otherwise moderate composite. Two or more dimensions at 3, with risk of harm below 3, is a "increase outpatient intensity first, reassess in 1–2 weeks" call, not an automatic escalation.

## 5. Documentation Standard (what survives an audit)

Every note ties to: the working diagnosis, the specific treatment-plan goal being addressed, what was assessed (with the actual risk-assessment tool named if used), and the plan going forward. Replace narrative summary with quoted or paraphrased client statements plus your structured assessment:

- ❌ "Client seemed down today, we talked about coping skills."
- ✅ "Client reported PHQ-9-equivalent low mood 6/7 days, denied SI on direct screen ('no, I don't think about that'), reviewed distress-tolerance skill (TIPP) from last session — client used it twice this week per self-report, reduced escalation to self-harm urge on one occasion. Next: add opposite-action skill for avoidance pattern."

Referral notes name the specific trigger, not a vague concern: "Referring to IOP intake: GAD-7 stable at 19-21 across 6 weeks of twice-weekly CBT plus exposure homework, LOCUS functional-status dimension scored 3 (missed work 8 of last 10 days), non-response at current level of care" reads differently — and protects differently — than "client isn't improving, may need more support."
