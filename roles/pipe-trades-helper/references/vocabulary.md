# Pipe Trades Helper — Vocabulary

### Invert
The lowest inside point of a pipe's cross-section at a given station — the elevation gravity flow actually follows, as distinct from the pipe's centerline or crown.
**In use:** "Design invert at the manhole is 92.50 — that's what the rod reading has to hit, not the pipe's top."
**Common misuse:** Confusing invert elevation with the pipe's centerline elevation — for an 8 in pipe that's a 0.33 ft difference, enough to blow the grade tolerance if the wrong reference point is used.

### Competent person
An OSHA-defined role: someone capable of identifying existing and predictable hazards in the surroundings and authorized to take prompt corrective measures — the person who classifies soil and inspects the trench, by regulation, not by seniority alone.
**In use:** "Get the competent person to re-classify before anyone goes back in — the rain this morning changes the call."
**Common misuse:** Assuming the foreman or the most senior person on site automatically fills this role — it's a specific designation tied to training and authority, not just rank.

### Haunching
The zone of bedding material placed and compacted from the pipe's springline down to the trench bottom on both sides, providing the pipe's primary side support.
**In use:** "Haunching's not compacted on the left side yet — don't backfill over it until that's tamped."
**Common misuse:** Treating haunching as part of "backfill" generally rather than its own compaction step — uncompacted haunching is the most common cause of pipe deflection that only shows up months later.

### Trench box / shield
A rigid steel or aluminum structure lowered into a trench to protect workers from a wall collapse, rated to a specific depth and soil classification per the manufacturer's tabulated data — distinct from shoring, which braces the walls in place rather than shielding around an open space.
**In use:** "Box is rated for Type B to this depth — check the tab data sheet before we go any deeper."
**Common misuse:** Treating any trench box as interchangeable regardless of the day's actual depth or soil classification — the rating is specific to both, not a general "it's a box, it's safe" assumption.

### Cut sheet
The pipe layer's or engineer's station-by-station table of design inverts (and often cut/fill depths from existing grade), used to check grade as the trench is dug and pipe is set.
**In use:** "Pull the cut sheet before you backfill — don't set grade off yesterday's stakes."
**Common misuse:** Working from remembered numbers or a single spot-check instead of the actual station-by-station sheet — a run can look consistent while drifting off design invert between checked stations.

### Belly (in a pipe run)
A localized low spot below the design grade line, usually from an over-dug trench bottom or a settled bedding zone, that holds standing water or traps solids even though the overall run slopes correctly.
**In use:** "That 0.6 inch deviation at Sta 1+00 becomes a belly if we don't shim it before backfill."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a pipe that "slopes downhill overall" can't have a belly — a single low station relative to its neighbors creates one regardless of the run's average slope.

### NPT (National Pipe Taper)
The standard tapered thread form for pipe fittings in the US, cut at 3/4 in of taper per foot, which seals by metal-to-metal wedging (plus thread compound) rather than a parallel thread's mechanical clamp alone.
**In use:** "Cut it NPT to the spec length — a straight thread won't seal the same way."
**Common misuse:** Treating "thread count" alone as the spec — taper angle and cut length both matter, and a correctly counted but wrongly tapered thread still won't seal.

### Bright metal (copper prep)
The visibly shiny, oxide-free copper surface produced by cleaning with emery or sand cloth immediately before fluxing — the actual standard for "clean enough," not a subjective judgment.
**In use:** "That's still got the mill finish on it, not bright metal — go over it again before you flux."
**Common misuse:** Treating a quick wipe or a slightly dull surface as sufficient — solder needs a genuinely bright, freshly exposed surface to wet and flow into the capillary gap.

### Pipe zone
The backfill region from the trench bottom (bedding) up to roughly 12 in over the pipe crown, where material selection and compaction directly affect the pipe's structural performance — distinct from the general trench backfill above it, which has looser requirements.
**In use:** "Keep that native spoil out of the pipe zone — it's got rock in it, use the screened stock instead."
**Common misuse:** Treating all backfill above the pipe as one undifferentiated task — the pipe zone has its own material and compaction spec that's stricter than the trench backfill above it.

### Locate / markout
The utility owner's or one-call center's field marking (paint, flags, or stakes) showing the approximate horizontal location of buried utilities, valid only for a limited window before it must be refreshed.
**In use:** "This locate's from ten days ago — check if it's still inside the valid window before we dig past it."
**Common misuse:** Treating a locate mark as a precise, permanent location — it's an approximate position valid for a limited time, and mechanical excavation still requires potholing to confirm exact depth and position within the tolerance zone.

### Potholing
Hand-digging or vacuum-excavating a small exploratory hole to physically confirm a marked utility's exact location and depth before mechanical excavation passes near it.
**In use:** "Pothole it first — the mark's close to our alignment and we're not running the excavator blind."
**Common misuse:** Skipping potholing when a marked utility "looks" far enough from the planned cut — the mark's tolerance zone, not visual distance, determines whether confirmation is required.

### Class B bedding
A bedding specification (per ASTM D2321 / common municipal practice) using granular material shaped to the pipe barrel with compacted haunching, distinct from Class A (rigid cradle, used for unusual loading) or Class C (looser, coarser material with less shaping).
**In use:** "Spec calls Class B — shape the bedding to the barrel before you set pipe, don't just dump and level it."
**Common misuse:** Treating "bedding" as a single generic material and depth regardless of class — the classes differ in both shaping requirement and load performance, and using Class C material where Class B is specified undersupports the pipe.
