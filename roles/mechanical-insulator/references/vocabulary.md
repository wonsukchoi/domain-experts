# Mechanical Insulator — vocabulary

### Vapor retarder
A facing or membrane with a tested low permeance rating that resists water-vapor migration into the insulation on a below-ambient system.
**In use:** "The ASJ isn't a vapor retarder here unless the perm rating's under 0.1 and every lap and penetration is sealed — right now it's just a facing."
**Common misuse:** Calling any foil- or paper-faced product a "vapor barrier" regardless of its perm rating or whether the seams are actually sealed; a punctured or unsealed "vapor barrier" isn't functioning as one.

### Dew point
The temperature at which air at a given moisture content becomes saturated and water condenses out.
**In use:** "Don't quote me the RH, tell me the dew point — that's the number the surface temperature has to beat."
**Common misuse:** Treating relative humidity percentage itself as the design target instead of converting it to an actual dew-point temperature for the specific dry-bulb condition, which is what the surface-temperature calculation needs.

### CUI (corrosion under insulation)
Corrosion of the pipe or vessel wall occurring beneath an intact-looking insulation jacket, driven by trapped or cyclical moisture.
**In use:** "That staining at the hanger isn't cosmetic — that's a CUI location, and it's been wet a while."
**Common misuse:** Treating CUI as a coatings/paint problem to be solved by repainting the jacket; it's a moisture-management failure in the insulation system, and repainting without fixing the jacket or vapor retarder just hides it again until the next inspection.

### Thermal system insulation (TSI)
The OSHA 1926.1101 regulatory term for insulation applied to pipes, boilers, tanks, ducts, or other mechanical components to prevent heat loss or gain.
**In use:** "This is TSI, pre-1981, unlabeled — it's presumed ACM until we get a bulk sample back."
**Common misuse:** Using "insulation" and "TSI" interchangeably outside the regulatory context, or assuming TSI rules only apply to obviously old, deteriorated material rather than any pre-1981 insulation regardless of visible condition.

### Perm rating
A tested measure (ASTM E96) of how much water vapor passes through a facing material per unit time, area, and vapor-pressure difference.
**In use:** "Pull the E96 sheet — I need the actual perm number, not just 'foil-faced.'"
**Common misuse:** Assuming any foil-faced product is automatically low-perm and therefore "vapor-tight"; perm rating varies by brand, thickness, and facing construction and has to be checked, not inferred from appearance.

### Insulated pipe shield / support insert
A rigid, load-bearing insulation insert (cellular glass or calcium silicate) paired with a metal saddle, installed at a point of pipe support to carry the pipe's weight without crushing the surrounding insulation.
**In use:** "That's not a shield, that's a bare clevis with foam wrapped around it — it'll condense the first humid day."
**Common misuse:** Calling any wrapped or padded hanger a "shield"; the term specifically means a rigid, non-crushing, load-bearing detail, not a soft-insulation wrap around the hanger hardware.

### Vapor stop
A ring of vapor-retarder mastic or compound applied at a fitting, support, or termination point to block vapor migration along the length of the insulation, independent of the outer jacket.
**In use:** "Vapor stop goes on both sides of every insert before the jacket goes back on — that's not optional on a chilled-water line."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a dab of general-purpose mastic anywhere near a joint counts; a vapor stop is a specific, continuous detail at a defined location, not incidental sealant.

### Economic thickness
The insulation thickness that minimizes total lifecycle cost (energy loss plus installed cost), as calculated by tools like NAIMA's 3E Plus.
**In use:** "3E Plus says 2 in. is the economic thickness here, but the condensation-control number comes out higher — the condensation number wins."
**Common misuse:** Treating economic thickness and condensation-control thickness as the same calculation; they're derived from different governing criteria, and whichever number is larger is the one that has to be specified.

### Presumed ACM (PACM)
Material presumed to contain asbestos under OSHA 1926.1101 based on age and type (e.g., pre-1981 TSI), regulated identically to confirmed ACM until a sample proves otherwise.
**In use:** "It's presumed ACM until the lab says different — we're not opening that pipe chase today."
**Common misuse:** Treating "presumed" as "probably fine" or "likely negative"; the regulatory posture is the opposite — presumed material gets the same exposure controls as confirmed material.

### All-service jacket (ASJ)
A laminated paper/foil/scrim facing used as the standard indoor commercial-insulation jacket, providing vapor retardance and a finished appearance for non-abusive service.
**In use:** "ASJ's fine in the mechanical room, but this run goes outside past the louver — that needs aluminum, not ASJ."
**Common misuse:** Specifying or accepting ASJ for outdoor, wash-down, or mechanically abusive runs; it's rated for indoor, non-abusive service, not weather or physical exposure.

### R-value (insulation)
A measure of resistance to heat flow through a material, independent of vapor performance.
**In use:** "The R-value's fine at 1 in. — the complaint here is condensation, and that's a retarder-continuity problem, not an R-value problem."
**Common misuse:** Assuming more R-value automatically fixes a condensation complaint; once minimum thickness is met, condensation control on a continuous below-ambient system depends on vapor-retarder continuity, not additional bulk thickness.

### Firestop system (UL-listed)
A specific, fire-tested assembly matching a named penetrant, insulation type, and annular-space configuration at a wall or floor penetration, identified by a UL system number.
**In use:** "Give me the UL system number for this exact pipe-and-insulation combination — 'we used firestop caulk' isn't a code-compliant answer."
**Common misuse:** Treating any application of firestop sealant as sufficient; code compliance requires matching the actual installed configuration to a specific tested UL system, not applying a generic product.

### Class I / Class II / Class III asbestos work
OSHA 1926.1101's work classifications: Class I is removal of TSI or surfacing ACM/PACM; Class II is removal of other ACM (roofing, flooring, etc.); Class III is repair/maintenance where ACM/PACM is likely to be disturbed.
**In use:** "This hanger repair on presumed-ACM TSI is Class III — different exposure controls than a full removal."
**Common misuse:** Assuming all TSI disturbance is automatically Class I removal; a repair or maintenance task that disturbs but doesn't remove TSI is Class III, with its own (still real) exposure-control requirements.

### Reflective/radiant facing
A foil facing whose primary function on pipe or duct insulation is low emissivity and vapor retardance, not bulk thermal resistance.
**In use:** "The foil's doing its job as a vapor retarder — don't count on it for meaningful extra R-value on its own."
**Common misuse:** Specifying or marketing foil facing as if it adds significant thermal resistance by itself; on mechanical insulation systems its main contribution is vapor management and surface emissivity, not bulk R-value.
