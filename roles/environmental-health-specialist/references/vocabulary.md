# Vocabulary

Terms generalists flatten together that an environmental health specialist keeps sharply separate — the value is in the misuse, not the definition.

## TCS food vs. "perishable"

**TCS (Time/Temperature Control for Safety) food** is a specific FDA Food Code category of foods that support rapid pathogen growth if not held at proper temperature — a defined list (cut melons, cooked rice, dairy, etc.), not a synonym for "anything that goes bad eventually." **Perishable** is a general commercial/consumer term with no regulatory temperature-control obligations attached.

**In use:** "Cut cantaloupe is TCS the moment it's sliced — it has to go on the 41°F line, not just 'the walk-in eventually.'"

**Common misuse:** treating any food that spoils over time as automatically TCS, when the Food Code's TCS designation depends on the food's specific ability to support pathogen growth, not general spoilage.

## Critical violation vs. Priority / Priority Foundation / Core

Older Food Code editions used "critical" and "non-critical." The current structure is three tiers: **Priority** (direct illness-risk factor, e.g., cold-holding failure), **Priority Foundation** (supports a priority item, e.g., missing thermometer), and **Core** (general sanitation/maintenance). The tiers carry different correction-deadline defaults.

**In use:** "That's a Priority item — same-visit correction if feasible, not the 90-day window you'd get on a Core finding."

**Common misuse:** using "critical" informally to describe anything alarming-looking, when the actual regulatory category depends on the item's relationship to foodborne-illness risk factors, not its visual severity.

## Time as a Public Health Control (TPHC) vs. an undocumented time lapse

**TPHC** is a formal, written alternative to continuous temperature control — the establishment marks the time a TCS food is removed from temperature control and discards it after a defined maximum (commonly 4 hours), with a procedure on file. An **undocumented time lapse** is simply food sitting out with no tracking, which the specialist must treat under the default cumulative-time disposition table, not as a TPHC exception.

**In use:** "They can't claim TPHC on this batch — there's no written procedure on file and nothing time-marked, so it falls under the standard 4-hour discard rule."

**Common misuse:** assuming any food sitting at room temperature for under 4 hours is automatically compliant, when TPHC only applies if the establishment has an actual written procedure and time-marking in place — otherwise the default disposition table governs.

## CT value

The product of disinfectant concentration (C, typically ppm) and contact time (T, in minutes) required to achieve a stated log-reduction of a specific pathogen — the standard unit for comparing disinfection protocols. A higher concentration with a shorter time can, in principle, reach the same CT as a lower concentration held longer, but real-world limits (chlorine demand, stabilizer interference) mean the two aren't always practically interchangeable.

**In use:** "20 ppm for 12.75 hours gets you to a CT of 15,300 — that's the number that matters, not just 'we hit 20 ppm at some point.'"

**Common misuse:** treating peak concentration alone as proof of adequate disinfection, without accounting for how long that concentration was actually sustained.

## Breakpoint chlorination vs. routine shock treatment

**Breakpoint chlorination** is dosing past the point where chlorine is consumed by combined chlorine (chloramines) and organic demand, so free chlorine becomes available for disinfection — a specific chemistry threshold. **Routine shock treatment** is a periodic maintenance dose to control chloramine buildup and isn't necessarily calculated to reach or exceed breakpoint.

**In use:** "The combined chlorine's still high after that shock — we didn't actually clear breakpoint, we just added chlorine."

**Common misuse:** assuming any elevated chlorine dose ("shocking the pool") automatically clears breakpoint and eliminates chloramines, when the dose needed depends on the existing chlorine demand.

## Cyanuric acid (CYA) vs. "chlorine"

**CYA** is a stabilizer that protects free chlorine from UV degradation in outdoor pools but also binds a portion of it into a less reactive form, reducing effective disinfection at a given ppm reading. It is not chlorine itself and isn't measured by a standard FC/pH test.

**In use:** "FC reads fine, but CYA's at 60 — the effective disinfecting chlorine is lower than that number suggests."

**Common misuse:** treating a compliant free-chlorine reading as sufficient evidence of disinfection without checking CYA, especially in a hyperchlorination or closure decision where the CT target depends on real chlorine availability, not the raw ppm number.

## Percolation rate vs. hydraulic/soil loading rate

**Percolation rate** (minutes per inch) is the raw field measurement of how fast water level drops in a test hole. **Soil loading rate** (gallons per square foot per day) is the design value derived from the perc rate (and soil type) used to size the absorption field — the two are related but not the same number, and code tables convert one to the other.

**In use:** "A 20-minute-per-inch perc rate maps to roughly this loading rate on the code table — that's what actually sizes the field, not the raw perc number."

**Common misuse:** citing the percolation rate itself as if it were the design parameter, when the field is actually sized off the derived loading rate from the code's conversion table.

## Imminent health hazard vs. a serious violation

An **imminent health hazard** is a defined regulatory trigger authorizing immediate closure without the standard correction-deadline process. A **serious violation** (e.g., a repeat Priority item) may carry a short correction window and escalated enforcement, but does not by itself authorize same-day closure unless it independently meets the imminent-hazard definition.

**In use:** "This is a serious repeat violation, but it's not an imminent hazard on its own — we cite it with an escalated deadline, we don't close today."

**Common misuse:** using "imminent hazard" as an intensifier for any bad finding, when it's a specific legal trigger with its own defined condition list.

## Vector index vs. trap count

**Vector index** is a calculated risk metric combining mosquito abundance and infection rate (from pooled testing) to estimate transmission risk — a single number meant for threshold comparison. **Trap count** is the raw number of mosquitoes collected, with no infection-rate component.

**In use:** "Trap counts are up, but the vector index is what tells us if that translates into actual transmission risk — pull the pooled infection-rate data before calling this an advisory-level event."

**Common misuse:** treating a rising trap count alone as equivalent to rising transmission risk, without the infection-rate component that the vector index actually incorporates.

## Embargo/hold order vs. voluntary discard

An **embargo or hold order** is a formal regulatory action placing legal control over a food or product pending further action (destruction, testing, or release), issued under specific statutory authority. A **voluntary discard** is the operator agreeing to throw food out without a formal order — legally weaker and reversible if the operator changes their mind before it happens.

**In use:** "Given the repeat pattern, we're not asking for a voluntary discard — we're placing an embargo tag on the product."

**Common misuse:** treating an operator's verbal agreement to discard as equivalent to a formal hold, when only the formal order carries enforceable legal weight if the operator doesn't follow through.

## REHS/RS vs. "sanitarian" (informal usage)

**REHS/RS (Registered Environmental Health Specialist/Registered Sanitarian)** is a specific NEHA credential earned via a defined eligibility track and a 250-question, 4-hour exam with a 650/900 passing score. **Sanitarian** is used informally (and in some state statutes) as a general job title, which may or may not require the NEHA credential depending on the state.

**In use:** "She's REHS/RS-credentialed, which is a different bar than just holding the state's 'sanitarian' job title — check which one the enforcement authority actually requires for this county."

**Common misuse:** assuming "sanitarian" and "REHS/RS" are interchangeable everywhere, when some states license sanitarians independently of the national NEHA credential.

## Cluster investigation vs. isolated complaint

A **cluster investigation** is opened when two or more epidemiologically linked cases (overlapping exposure, matching symptoms/timing) point to a common source — it pulls records systematically (logs, supplier data, employee-illness history). An **isolated complaint** is a single report logged and possibly followed up individually, without triggering the broader systematic pull.

**In use:** "Two unrelated parties, same night, same symptom onset window — that's a cluster, open the full record pull, don't just log two separate complaint tickets."

**Common misuse:** treating multiple complaints about the same establishment as automatically a cluster regardless of exposure-window overlap, or conversely treating clearly linked complaints as separate isolated items because they came in through different intake channels.

## Cross-connection vs. general plumbing violation

A **cross-connection** is a point where potable water supply is physically connected to a non-potable source with no (or failed) backflow prevention — a specific contamination pathway. A **general plumbing violation** may be a code infraction without creating an actual pathway for contamination.

**In use:** "That hose bib submerged in the mop sink is a cross-connection — that's an imminent-hazard-track finding, not a routine plumbing note."

**Common misuse:** treating any plumbing code deviation as a cross-connection, when the defining feature is an actual, uncontrolled pathway between potable and non-potable water.
