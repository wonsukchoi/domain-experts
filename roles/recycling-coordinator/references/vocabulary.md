# Vocabulary

Terms generalists conflate or misuse when discussing recycling-program performance and economics; coordinators keep them sharply separate because the contract and the budget are priced on the distinctions.

### Diversion rate

The percentage of the total waste stream (recycling plus organics plus any other diverted material) kept out of landfill, calculated as diverted tonnage divided by diverted-plus-landfilled tonnage. It is a program-level outcome metric, not a collection-volume metric.

**In use:** "Diversion rate looks flat this quarter, but that's because rejected-load tonnage from contamination is being counted as landfilled — recalculate it net of rejections before you present it."

**Common misuse:** treating diversion rate as synonymous with tonnage collected at the curb, when material collected and later rejected at the MRF for contamination never actually gets diverted — it's landfilled downstream, just later in the chain.

### Contamination band

A tier in a MRF or hauler contract's pricing schedule (e.g., ≤8% clean, 8-18% mid-tier, >18% surcharge/no-rebate) that determines what the program is paid or charged per ton, distinct from the raw contamination percentage itself.

**In use:** "We're at 21% contamination, which puts us in the no-rebate band — the two points from 19% to 21% didn't cost us anything extra, but the two points from 17% to 19% cost us the entire rebate."

**Common misuse:** treating contamination percentage as a smoothly linear cost, when contract bands often make the cost function a step function — a single point crossing a band boundary can change program economics far more than several points moving within a band.

### CBSM (community-based social marketing)

A behavior-change program-design method: identify a specific target behavior, identify the barrier to it, design an intervention mechanism matched to that barrier (prompt, commitment device, social norm), pilot it, measure it, then scale only what worked.

**In use:** "This isn't an awareness problem, it's a habit problem — CBSM says we need a point-of-decision prompt on the cart lid, not another mailer explaining why recycling matters."

**Common misuse:** using "public education" and CBSM interchangeably, when a generic awareness campaign (which CBSM explicitly argues is weak on its own) is a different and usually less effective intervention than a barrier-matched, piloted CBSM mechanism.

### Commodity index (rebate index)

The published market price for a recovered-material stream (blended fiber and containers, or separated by material) that a hauler/MRF contract references to calculate the rebate paid to the program, independent of the contamination rate.

**In use:** "Our rebate isn't down because our contamination got worse — the index itself dropped 40% this year. Two separate variables, and only one of them is ours to fix."

**Common misuse:** attributing a falling rebate entirely to program performance (contamination, participation) without checking whether the referenced commodity index moved independently of anything the program did.

### Price floor / collar

A negotiated contract term that limits how far a program's rebate can fall (floor) or bounds it within a range (collar) regardless of how far the underlying commodity index moves, in exchange for typically giving up some upside if the index rises.

**In use:** "We gave up some rebate upside to get a floor at $35/ton — after watching the index drop from $132 to $58, that trade looks like the right one in hindsight."

**Common misuse:** assuming price protection is standard in recycling contracts; many index-linked contracts carry no floor or collar at all unless the program specifically negotiates for one at renewal.

### Waste composition study

A periodic physical sort and weighing of the general waste stream (not the recycling stream) to determine what recyclable or compostable material is still being landfilled, used to size the maximum addressable diversion-rate opportunity.

**In use:** "The composition study shows 18% of what's going to landfill is still recyclable fiber and containers — that's the ceiling on how much diversion rate can realistically move without a collection-system change."

**Common misuse:** relying only on recycling-stream participation data to gauge diversion potential, missing the resident population that never sets out recycling at all and only shows up in a waste composition study.

### National Sword (as a market-shock reference)

Shorthand in the industry for China's 2017-2018 import restrictions on contaminated recyclables (a near-zero contamination tolerance for imported bales), which triggered a collapse in export demand and domestic commodity prices for mixed paper and plastics. Used as the canonical example of commodity and export-market volatility, not a live current policy.

**In use:** "Model this contract like we're one policy shock away from another National Sword — the index has moved that much before, and it can again."

**Common misuse:** treating it as a one-time historical event with no ongoing relevance, rather than the reference case for how much and how fast recyclables commodity pricing can move for reasons entirely outside a program's control.

### Spot-reject vs. banded rejection

**Spot-reject** is a MRF scale-house decision to reject one specific incoming load, usually above a high single-load contamination threshold (often cited around 25%), redirecting that truck's tonnage to landfill. **Banded rejection** is the contract-wide pricing consequence (no rebate, tipping fee applied) that kicks in once a period's *average* audited contamination crosses a lower threshold, without any single load necessarily being turned away.

**In use:** "That one load got spot-rejected at the scale house today, but our quarterly average is still inside the surcharge band, not the full-rejection band — those are two different thresholds and two different costs."

**Common misuse:** conflating a single rejected load with the program's overall contract-band status, when the two operate on different thresholds and different time horizons (one load vs. a quarterly average).

### Single-stream vs. dual-stream (program-design level)

At the program-design level (as opposed to the operational, per-truck level), this is a structural tradeoff the coordinator owns: single-stream raises participation and tonnage while raising contamination and requiring sustained education/enforcement investment to manage it; dual-stream lowers contamination structurally but caps the participation ceiling and requires more collection infrastructure.

**In use:** "Converting to single-stream is a defensible call, but only if council also funds the education line that has to come with it — otherwise we're choosing the contamination cost without choosing the offsetting spend."

**Common misuse:** presenting single-stream conversion to decision-makers as a pure upgrade (more participation, more tonnage) without naming the contamination and cost tradeoff that comes with it in the same conversation.

### Tipping fee vs. processing rebate (program economics)

At the program level, **tipping fee** is what the program pays per ton when material is landfilled (either because it was never diverted or because a load/period was rejected for contamination). **Processing rebate** is what the program receives per ton for recyclables accepted at or above the contract's clean-tier terms. The two aren't symmetric opposites of the same number — the bands, audit basis, and commodity linkage differ for each.

**In use:** "Don't average the rebate and the fee to get a 'blended cost per ton' — they're triggered by different contract clauses and moving different variables (contamination band for one, commodity index for the other)."

**Common misuse:** treating rebate and tipping fee as a single symmetric swing driven by one variable, when contamination band determines which one applies and commodity index determines the rebate's magnitude — two different levers, not one.

### Cost-per-contamination-point

A benchmarking figure (dollars spent per household per percentage-point reduction in contamination achieved) used to compare the efficiency of different education/enforcement interventions, distinct from total campaign cost alone.

**In use:** "The targeted pilot came in at about $1.10 per household per point — a citywide rollout at the same spend would only reach households that were never contaminating, so the effective cost-per-point citywide would be much higher."

**Common misuse:** comparing two campaigns by total dollars spent without normalizing for households reached and points of contamination actually moved, which makes an untargeted, expensive campaign look comparable to a cheap, targeted one that achieved the same headline reduction.
