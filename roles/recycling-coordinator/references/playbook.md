# Playbook

Filled tables and sequences for the situations a recycling coordinator faces every contract cycle and every audit: contamination-band economics, contract-terms negotiation, education-campaign design, and diversion-rate reporting.

## Contamination-band / contract-terms table

| Contamination band (audited) | Typical contract treatment | Program-economics effect |
|---|---|---|
| ≤8% ("clean") | Full index-linked rebate paid to city/hauler | Program at maximum revenue for the given commodity price |
| 8-18% ("mid-tier") | Rebate reduced, commonly to 40-60% of index [contract-specific] | Meaningful revenue loss even with no change in commodity price |
| 18-25% ("surcharge/no-rebate") | No rebate; flat per-ton tipping/processing fee charged instead | Program flips from revenue to cost purely on contamination, independent of market price |
| >25% single-load spot check | MRF can reject the specific load at the scale house | That truck's tonnage redirected to landfill; cost is the tipping fee plus the lost diversion credit for the whole load, not just the contaminated fraction |

**Contract-terms checklist for renewal (in negotiating-priority order):**
1. Contamination-audit methodology and cadence, specified in the contract text — not left to the MRF's internal schedule alone.
2. A commodity price floor or collar on the index the rebate references, sized to the index's own historical range (a 40-50% swing within 18-24 months is within recent precedent, not a tail event).
3. A dispute/re-weigh process for any single load rejected at the scale house, with a defined window to contest before the load is landfilled.
4. Rebate-band boundaries themselves (the 8%/18%/25%-style thresholds) — negotiate last, since they matter less than having accurate, disputable measurement and price-volatility protection in place first.

## CBSM (community-based social marketing) campaign-design sequence

1. **Name the specific behavior**, not a category. Not "recycle correctly" — "stop bagging recyclables in plastic grocery bags before setting out the cart."
2. **Identify the barrier** for that specific behavior through direct observation or a small resident survey, not assumption. Common barriers: habit carried over from trash routine, belief that bagging keeps the cart cleaner, lack of awareness that bags jam MRF sorting equipment.
3. **Select a mechanism matched to the barrier**, not a generic awareness push:
   - Habit/awareness gap → a prompt at the point of decision (a cart-lid sticker at the exact moment of set-out), not a mailer read days earlier.
   - Belief that bagging is "cleaner" or "helpful" → direct correction naming the mechanical reason (bags jam optical sorters at the MRF), not a general environmental appeal.
   - Low perceived norm ("nobody around here sorts right anyway") → visible normative messaging (door hangers showing area-specific improvement, not citywide averages).
4. **Pilot on a defined subset** — the routes or neighborhoods the audit data shows are actually driving the contamination number, sized large enough for a pre/post measurement to be meaningful (a few thousand households, not a single block).
5. **Measure pre/post contamination on that same subset specifically**, via a targeted route audit or cart-content sample, before scaling. A citywide post-campaign audit average will dilute a real localized effect and can read as "no impact" when the targeted routes actually improved.
6. **Scale only the mechanism that moved the pilot's number**, at the same cost-per-household rate used in the pilot, and re-measure after scale-up rather than assuming the pilot's percentage improvement holds linearly.

**Cost-per-point reference (stated heuristic, program-specific):** targeted route-level outreach (door hangers, cart tags, a mailer) has been benchmarked in Recycling Partnership case studies in the rough range of $0.75-$1.50 per household per contamination-point reduction for a piloted, targeted campaign; citywide undifferentiated campaigns run meaningfully higher per point achieved because most targeted households were never contributing to the contamination number in the first place.

## Diversion-rate / contamination reconciliation template

```
Reporting period: ____
Total tonnage collected (recycling stream): ____ tons
Tonnage rejected at MRF (contamination-driven): ____ tons
Net tonnage actually diverted: collected − rejected = ____ tons
Diversion rate = net tonnage diverted / (diverted + landfilled) = ____%

Contamination rate (latest audit): ____%
Contract band this rate falls in: ____
Rebate or fee applied this period: $____ /ton × ____ tons = $____

Trend vs. prior period:
  Diversion rate: ____ (up/down/flat)
  Contamination rate: ____ (up/down/flat)
  Flag: if diversion rate is flat/up AND contamination rate is up, the
  headline number is masking rising rejected-load cost — report both,
  not diversion rate alone.
```

## Single-stream vs. dual-stream conversion decision table

| Factor | Single-stream | Dual-stream |
|---|---|---|
| Typical participation/tonnage effect | Higher — removes the sorting step from the resident | Lower — sorting friction reduces marginal participation |
| Typical contamination benchmark | Often cited around 16% [heuristic, program-specific] | Often cited around 5-7% [heuristic, program-specific] |
| Collection infrastructure cost | Single cart/bin per household | Two collection streams — more trucks or split-body vehicles, more collection cost |
| Required offsetting investment | Education/enforcement budget must scale with the contamination risk taken on | Lower offsetting-education requirement, but resident compliance friction can suppress diversion-rate ceiling |
| When it's the right call | Participation/diversion-rate growth is the binding constraint and education budget is funded alongside conversion | Contamination cost or a downstream MRF's processing constraints are the binding constraint |
