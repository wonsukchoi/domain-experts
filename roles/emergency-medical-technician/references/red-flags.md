# Red Flags

Decompensation, scope-boundary, and liability signals an EMT checks for on every call. Format per flag: what it usually means, the first question to ask, the data to pull.

### Respiratory rate under 10/min or over 29/min in an adult patient

- **Usually means:** primary respiratory failure (airway obstruction, opioid depression) or a compensatory response to shock/metabolic acidosis; a rate this far outside normal is a BLS-ceiling signal on its own even before other vitals look abnormal.
- **First question:** "Is this a primary airway/breathing problem, or a compensation for something else — what does perfusion and mental status look like?"
- **Data to pull:** SpO2, work-of-breathing observation (retractions, accessory muscle use, tripod positioning), chief complaint and known history (opioid use, asthma/COPD, cardiac).

### Epinephrine auto-injector symptoms recurring within 5–15 minutes of the first dose

- **Usually means:** an early-biphasic anaphylactic reaction, not a failed first dose; second most likely is IM delivery into subcutaneous fat rather than muscle reducing absorption.
- **First question:** "Was the first dose confirmed IM into the anterolateral thigh, and is a second auto-injector on this unit?"
- **Data to pull:** time and site of first dose, current stridor/SpO2/hive-spread status, whether ALS intercept was already requested.

### A patient requesting refusal of transport who is intoxicated, hypoglycemic, or post-ictal

- **Usually means:** impaired capacity masquerading as an oriented refusal — the patient can hold a conversation while still lacking the ability to weigh the actual risk of refusing.
- **First question:** "Can this patient accurately restate, in their own words, what could happen if they don't go — not just agree when asked?"
- **Data to pull:** glucometer reading if available, time since last alcohol/substance use or seizure, full orientation check (person/place/time/event), law-enforcement or family collateral if present.

### Midline spinal tenderness or a neurologic deficit found on exam despite a "minor-looking" mechanism

- **Usually means:** the mechanism looked survivable but the exam is the actual decision input for spinal motion restriction — a low-speed fall can still produce a compression fracture or cord-level injury.
- **First question:** "Has the full spine actually been palpated and a neuro exam done, or was immobilization skipped from mechanism alone?"
- **Data to pull:** exact palpation findings by spinal level, motor/sensory exam bilaterally, mechanism detail (height, surface, point of impact).

### Suspected opioid overdose with respiratory depression not improving 2–3 minutes after naloxone

- **Usually means:** ventilation hasn't actually been established (BVM technique, seal, rate) rather than a naloxone-resistant overdose; second most likely is a very long-acting or high-potency opioid needing a repeat dose.
- **First question:** "Is BVM actually moving air — chest rise, mask seal — or has ventilation been assumed rather than confirmed?"
- **Data to pull:** SpO2 trend, chest-rise confirmation, time and dose of naloxone given, repeat-dose eligibility per protocol.

### Oral glucose given to a patient with an unreliable swallow or declining mentation

- **Usually means:** the airway-protection check was skipped or the patient's status changed between the check and administration — this is an aspiration-risk event, not a dosing error.
- **First question:** "Is the patient actually swallowing this, or has it been sitting in the cheek while mentation keeps dropping?"
- **Data to pull:** mentation/GCS trend before and after administration, airway status, time since administration.

### Scene time exceeding the point where ALS intercept ETA has already been passed by transport time

- **Usually means:** intercept was requested late, or the crew is waiting on scene for an ALS unit that would arrive faster by rendezvousing en route.
- **First question:** "Is it faster to keep moving toward the hospital and meet ALS partway, or to hold position here?"
- **Data to pull:** ALS unit's current location/ETA, remaining transport time to the receiving facility, patient stability trend.

### A "stable vitals" chart note on a patient whose chief complaint is high-risk (chest pain, stroke symptoms, anaphylaxis, overdose)

- **Usually means:** vitals haven't decompensated *yet* — for these complaint categories, the presenting story carries more predictive weight than a single reassuring vital-sign snapshot.
- **First question:** "What's the actual trend since first contact, not just the most recent single reading?"
- **Data to pull:** serial vitals at each reassessment interval, time since symptom onset, any protocol-specific criteria already met (e.g., CDC field-triage physiologic criteria, aspirin/epinephrine administration record).

### An AMA/refusal form missing the medical-control contact line or a capacity-check item

- **Usually means:** the refusal was accepted on the crew's own judgment alone rather than following the full capacity/disclosure/authorization sequence — this is the single highest-liability documentation gap in EMS.
- **First question:** "Was medical control actually called for this refusal, and is that documented with a name and time?"
- **Data to pull:** completed refusal checklist, time of medical-control call, name of physician/designee who authorized the refusal.

### Second epinephrine auto-injector or naloxone dose given with no doses remaining on the unit

- **Usually means:** the call has exceeded what this unit can BLS-manage on its own — this is the explicit signal to have already requested (or immediately request) ALS intercept rather than treating the remaining transport as routine.
- **First question:** "Is ALS intercept already inbound, and if not, why not — has the pattern crossed the BLS ceiling?"
- **Data to pull:** medications remaining on the unit, ALS intercept status/ETA, patient trend since the last dose.
