# Playbook

Filled BLS protocol tables and templates for the EMT scope of practice. Numbers below are the conventional national BLS-tier thresholds (National EMS Scope of Practice Model 2.0, AHA BLS, NAEMT spinal-precautions guidance) — an agency's own protocol always governs where it differs.

## Aspirin decision tree — suspected ACS

1. **Chief complaint consistent with cardiac chest pain?** (pressure/crushing, radiation to arm/jaw/back, diaphoresis, associated dyspnea or nausea) — No → aspirin not indicated on this pathway. Yes → continue.
2. **Contraindication check, all three:**
   - Known true aspirin allergy? Yes → do not give; stop.
   - Active GI bleeding signs (melena, hematemesis) or a recent GI bleed history the patient reports? Yes → do not give; stop.
   - Already took a therapeutic aspirin dose (≥162 mg) in the last 24 hours? Yes → do not give an additional dose; stop.
3. **None of the above present → give 162–324 mg, chewed** (four 81 mg chewable tablets is the common field-supplied form), regardless of anticoagulant/antiplatelet medication use — anticoagulant therapy alone is not a contraindication in the national convention.
4. Document time given, dose, and route on the ePCR before handoff — the receiving team needs this before deciding on additional antiplatelet therapy.

## Epinephrine auto-injector — anaphylaxis, weight-based dosing

| Patient weight | Device/dose | Route | Repeat window |
|---|---|---|---|
| ≥30 kg (≈66 lb), adult/adolescent | 0.3 mg | IM, anterolateral thigh | Every 5–15 min if symptoms persist/recur, second device if carried |
| <30 kg, pediatric | 0.15 mg (junior device) | IM, anterolateral thigh | Same 5–15 min window |

**Trigger to administer immediately** (don't wait for vitals to look "sick enough"): stridor, wheeze, hypotension, or hives spreading beyond the exposure site, following a known/likely allergen.

**Worked example:** 25 kg child → 0.15 mg dose. First dose at minute 0. Reassess at minute 5 (partial response expected, not full resolution). If symptoms recur or worsen between minute 5 and minute 15 *and* a second device is on hand, give it and continue toward the receiving facility; if the unit is out of devices, that recurrence is the BLS ceiling — request ALS intercept for IV epinephrine/fluids rather than waiting for a third relapse.

## Naloxone — suspected opioid overdose

1. **Respiratory rate <10/min or absent, altered mentation, pinpoint pupils, known/suspected opioid use** → open airway, start BVM ventilation *first*.
2. **While ventilating, administer naloxone** per state formulary — commonly 4 mg intranasal (2 mg per nostril via atomizer) or 0.4–2 mg IM/IN per agency protocol.
3. **Reassess respiratory drive within 2–3 minutes.** No improvement → continue BVM, repeat naloxone dose per protocol (commonly every 2–3 minutes) — do not stop ventilating while waiting on the drug to take effect.
4. **Response achieved** → continue reassessment; respiratory depression can recur as naloxone (short half-life, ~30–90 min) outlasts a long-acting opioid — transport regardless of a full initial recovery, and document the recurrence risk in the handoff.

## Oral glucose — suspected hypoglycemia

1. **Confirm suspected hypoglycemia** (known diabetic, altered mentation, diaphoresis) — glucometer reading where scope permits.
2. **Airway/swallow check: can the patient reliably follow commands and swallow on request?**
   - Yes → administer oral glucose gel between cheek and gum, reassess mentation in 5 minutes.
   - No (altered to the point of unreliable swallow) → **do not give oral glucose** — aspiration risk. Request ALS intercept for IV dextrose or IM glucagon instead.
3. Document response — mentation improved, unchanged, or worsened — at the 5-minute mark.

## Spinal-motion-restriction criteria (NAEMT/ACS-COT-style)

Apply full spinal motion restriction (padding/vacuum mattress, not routine rigid backboard for transport) only if the mechanism is significant *and* any one of these fails:

- Reliable exam possible? (not intoxicated, not distracted by a painful injury elsewhere, able to communicate)
- No midline cervical/thoracic/lumbar tenderness on palpation?
- No focal neurologic deficit (motor, sensory, or reported numbness/tingling)?
- No altered mental status?

**All four pass → no spinal immobilization device required**, regardless of a dramatic-sounding mechanism. **Any one fails → apply spinal motion restriction**, preferring padding and straps on the cot or a vacuum mattress over a rigid backboard once the patient is off the ground.

## CPR/AED sequence, worked timing

**Compressions:** rate 100–120/min, depth ≥2 in (5 cm) adult, allow full recoil, minimize pauses to <10 seconds. Ratio 30:2 for a single rescuer or unadvanced airway; continuous compressions with asynchronous ventilation once an advanced airway is placed (ALS-scope step).

**AED sequence:** attach and analyze within the first 1–2 minutes of arrest recognition. Shock advised → clear and shock immediately, resume compressions right away without a pulse check. No shock advised (e.g., asystole/PEA-pattern) → resume CPR for a full 2-minute cycle before the next analysis; asystole on first analysis is not a basis for withholding further CPR or transport — field termination of resuscitation is an ALS/medical-control decision, not a solo-BLS one.

**Worked example:** arrest witnessed at minute 0, AED attached at minute 1, first analysis "no shock advised," 2 minutes of CPR, second analysis at minute 3 "shock advised," shock delivered, CPR resumes immediately — total elapsed time to first shock: 3 minutes, consistent with the every-2-minutes analysis cadence.

                            instructions given verbally and in writing
4. Medical control contact: Time called ____  Physician/designee name ____

5. Signatures:              Patient (or documented refusal to sign +
                            witness signature)     EMT signature

If any Step 1 item is "N" — do not accept the refusal; treat as lacking capacity and proceed per protocol (implied consent, law enforcement assist, or ALS/medical-control escalation).

## BLS-to-ALS intercept fallback ladder

In preference order when a call's pattern exceeds BLS scope: 1) request ALS intercept the moment the pattern is recognized, comparing intercept ETA against transport time to the receiving facility → 2) if intercept ETA exceeds remaining transport time by a wide margin, continue BLS transport to the closest appropriate facility rather than waiting on scene for ALS to arrive → 3) if the patient is unstable and no ALS unit is available within a reasonable window, request air medical if criteria and conditions allow → 4) document the specific point at which BLS scope was exceeded and what was requested, regardless of which option was used — the timeline is what the receiving team and any later review need.
