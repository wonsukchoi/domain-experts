# Vocabulary

Working vocabulary that generalists reliably misuse. Format per term: definition, how an EMT actually uses it, the common misuse.

### BLS vs. ALS

- **Definition:** Basic Life Support (BLS) is the EMT-tier skill/medication set — airway adjuncts, oxygen, AED, aspirin, oral glucose, epinephrine auto-injector, naloxone; Advanced Life Support (ALS) adds IV/IO access, cardiac-rhythm interpretation and drugs, and advanced airways, delivered by a paramedic or above.
- **In use:** "This is a BLS save — oxygen, positioning, and a fast, careful drive — there's no ALS intervention that changes the next ten minutes for this patient."
- **Common misuse:** treating "BLS" as a synonym for "minor" or "not real medicine." Recognizing an ALS-scope pattern early and requesting intercept is itself the highest-value BLS decision on that call.

### Scope of practice

- **Definition:** the specific list of skills and medications a certification level is legally authorized to perform, set by the state EMS office and further narrowed by the local medical director's protocols.
- **In use:** "Naloxone's in our formulary in this county at 4 milligrams intranasal; check the protocol before assuming it's the same next county over."
- **Common misuse:** assuming "EMT scope" is one fixed national list. It varies by state and by medical director — the same certification can carry different allowed skills two counties apart.

### Spinal motion restriction (SMR) vs. spinal immobilization

- **Definition:** spinal motion restriction is the current criteria-based practice of limiting spinal movement only when indicated, using padding/vacuum mattress; "spinal immobilization" (routine rigid backboarding of every trauma patient) is the older, now largely abandoned default.
- **In use:** "He clears all four SMR criteria — no midline tenderness, no deficit, reliable exam, no distracting injury — no board needed, just standard cot precautions."
- **Common misuse:** using the terms interchangeably, or defaulting to routine backboarding "to be safe" when the criteria checklist already argues against it — the current evidence base treats unnecessary rigid immobilization as adding pain and aspiration risk without a shown benefit.
- **Note:** state EMS boards and NREMT terminology have shifted from "immobilization" to "restriction" in updated skill sheets; older textbooks and some field slang still say "board him" out of habit.

### Auto-injector weight threshold (0.15 mg vs. 0.3 mg)

- **Definition:** the pediatric (junior) epinephrine auto-injector delivers 0.15 mg IM and is used under roughly 30 kg (66 lb); the adult device delivers 0.3 mg IM at or above that weight.
- **In use:** "He's 25 kilos — that's the junior device, 0.15 milligrams, not the adult 0.3."
- **Common misuse:** estimating weight by apparent age rather than actual or reported weight, which can put a small-for-age child on the wrong device threshold.

### Biphasic reaction

- **Definition:** a second wave of anaphylactic symptoms recurring after apparent initial improvement, typically within 1–72 hours (most commonly within the first several hours) of the initial reaction, without a new allergen exposure.
- **In use:** "Symptoms came back at minute 12 with no re-exposure — that's an early biphasic pattern, not a failed first dose."
- **Common misuse:** treating any symptom recurrence as proof the first dose was given wrong. Route and site matter, but biphasic recurrence is a recognized pattern independent of technique.
- **Same-term confusion:** distinct from a simple "rebound" reaction from too-short observation before symptoms were ever fully controlled.

### Implied consent

- **Definition:** the legal doctrine that a patient who is unconscious, or otherwise unable to grant or refuse consent, and who has a life-threatening condition, is presumed to consent to emergency treatment a reasonable person would want.
- **In use:** "He's unresponsive with no ID and no family on scene — implied consent covers treatment and transport here."
- **Common misuse:** invoking implied consent for a conscious, oriented patient who is simply being difficult or in pain. It applies specifically to incapacity, not to a patient who is capable of but reluctant to consent.
- **Common misuse:** treating implied consent as a way to override a documented advance directive (DNR/POLST) that's actually present and valid.

### AMA (against medical advice) / refusal of care

- **Definition:** a documented event in which a capacitated patient declines part or all of the recommended assessment, treatment, or transport after risks are explained.
- **In use:** "He's refusing transport AMA — capacity confirmed, risks explained back to me in his own words, med control's on the line for authorization."
- **Common misuse:** treating "AMA" as a form to fill out after the fact rather than a full sequence (capacity check, risk disclosure, alternatives offered, medical-control contact, signatures) completed *before* leaving the patient — this sequence, not the outcome, is what determines whether the refusal is defensible later.

### Rendezvous / ALS intercept

- **Definition:** meeting an ALS unit at a point along the transport route (or having it come to the scene) rather than waiting for it to arrive before departing, chosen based on comparing intercept ETA against remaining transport time.
- **In use:** "Intercept's six minutes out, we're eighteen from the hospital — let's start rolling and meet them at the highway on-ramp instead of holding here."
- **Common misuse:** defaulting to "wait on scene" out of habit even when continuing toward the hospital and meeting ALS partway is strictly faster for the patient.

### SAMPLE / OPQRST history

- **Definition:** SAMPLE (Signs/Symptoms, Allergies, Medications, Pertinent history, Last oral intake, Events leading up) structures the general patient history; OPQRST (Onset, Provocation/Palliation, Quality, Region/Radiation, Severity, Time) structures a specific complaint like pain.
- **In use:** "SAMPLE gave us the med list and the last meal time; OPQRST on the chest pain is what's actually driving the aspirin decision."
- **Common misuse:** running through both as a rote checklist without using the answers to change the plan — the point of "last oral intake" or "time of onset" is that it feeds a specific downstream decision (aspiration risk, LKW-style time window), not that it was asked.

### Termination of resuscitation (TOR)

- **Definition:** the formal decision to stop CPR in the field rather than transport, governed by specific criteria (e.g., unwitnessed arrest, no bystander CPR, no shock advised, asystole after a set number of analysis cycles) and, in most systems, requiring paramedic or medical-control authorization.
- **In use:** "Asystole on first analysis alone isn't a termination criterion — that's a paramedic/med-control call with the full criteria met, not something a BLS crew decides solo."
- **Common misuse:** assuming a "no shock advised" reading by itself means the call is over. Most state protocols require a specific criteria bundle and, at the BLS tier, an ALS or medical-control authorization before stopping resuscitation efforts.
