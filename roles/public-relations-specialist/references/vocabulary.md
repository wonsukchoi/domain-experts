# Public Relations Specialist — Vocabulary

### Embargo
An agreement that a reporter receives information before publication but commits to not publishing until a specific agreed time.
**In use:** "We're giving three reporters an embargo until Monday 6 AM so they have time to write something deeper than a blurb off the release."
**Common misuse:** Treating "embargo" as a synonym for "early access" offered broadly to everyone — a real embargo is a specific trade with a specific reporter for a specific committed publish time, not a mass pre-release.

### Exclusive
An arrangement where one outlet gets the story before anyone else, in exchange for guaranteed coverage.
**In use:** "We gave TechCrunch the exclusive on the funding announcement, so the rest of the list gets the release the next morning."
**Common misuse:** Confusing "exclusive" with "embargo" — an exclusive means only one outlet gets it at all initially; an embargo means multiple outlets get it simultaneously but agree on a shared publish time.

### Boilerplate
The standard "About [Company]" paragraph appended to every press release, reused verbatim until facts change.
**In use:** "Update the boilerplate — we said 300 customers last quarter and we're at 400 now."
**Common misuse:** Treating boilerplate as "set and forget" — stale boilerplate gets quoted as current fact by any outlet that runs the release as-is.

### Media list tiering
Ranking target reporters by beat-fit and likelihood of covering the specific story, not by outlet size or prestige alone.
**In use:** "Tier-one list is five reporters who've covered this exact niche in the last month — that's who gets the personalized pitch first."
**Common misuse:** Building a list ranked purely by outlet circulation/domain authority — a huge generalist outlet with no beat fit is a worse target than a small trade publication that covers this exact topic weekly.

### Pitch angle
The specific framing or hook used to make a story relevant to a particular reporter's beat.
**In use:** "We're testing two angles on this list — the benchmark-data angle and the partnership angle — to see which gets a better response."
**Common misuse:** Treating "angle" as identical to "the news" — the underlying news is fixed, but the angle is the specific lens applied to make that news relevant to a given reporter, and can (should) vary by recipient.

### Holding statement
A short, honest interim statement issued quickly during a live inquiry, acknowledging the question and committing to a specific follow-up time before all facts are confirmed.
**In use:** "Get a holding statement out in the next 30 minutes — we don't need the full picture yet, we need to not go silent."
**Common misuse:** Confusing a holding statement with "no comment" — a holding statement actively acknowledges and commits to a timeline; "no comment" is silence dressed as a response and reads the same as stonewalling.

### AP style
The Associated Press Stylebook's conventions for numerals, titles, dates, and punctuation in press-release and journalistic writing.
**In use:** "Spell out numbers under 10 per AP style, and it's 'percent' not '%' in body copy."
**Common misuse:** Assuming AP style is optional polish rather than a practical filter — a release that ignores basic AP conventions signals unfamiliarity with press norms and can get deprioritized by an editor skimming a crowded inbox.

### Response rate / placement rate
Measurable outcomes tracked per pitch: response rate is the percentage of sent pitches that got any reply; placement rate is the percentage that resulted in actual published coverage.
**In use:** "Angle A had double the placement rate of Angle B — that's the one we lead with next cycle."
**Common misuse:** Treating open rate alone as success — a high open rate with a low placement rate means the subject line worked but the pitch itself didn't convert, which is a different problem requiring a different fix.

### Message house
A shared internal document defining the core facts and key messages a story should stay consistent to, set by the PR manager, that this role's pitches and releases must align with.
**In use:** "Before this pitch goes out, check it against the message house — we can't imply a launch date that hasn't been confirmed by product."
**Common misuse:** Treating the message house as a script to recite verbatim in every pitch — it's a consistency check on facts and key points, not fixed language that has to appear unchanged in every communication.

### Trade press vs. general/business press
Trade press covers a specific industry in depth for an audience already in that industry; general/business press covers a broader audience with less specialized context.
**In use:** "This is too niche for general business press — it's a trade-press story first, and maybe a business-press follow-up once there's broader traction to point to."
**Common misuse:** Defaulting every pitch to general/business press because it "sounds bigger" — a trade outlet with the right beat fit often has a materially higher placement probability than a generalist outlet with none.

### Dateline
The city and date at the start of a press release indicating where and when it originated.
**In use:** "The dateline should be SAN FRANCISCO, not the city where our PR agency is based."
**Common misuse:** Using the agency's or a generic corporate address city instead of where the news is actually happening or where the company is headquartered.

### Follow-up (pitch)
A second message sent to a non-responding reporter, ideally containing new information rather than a repeat of the original ask.
**In use:** "Send a follow-up on Thursday with the new customer stat, not just a 'checking in' bump."
**Common misuse:** Sending a content-free "just following up!" nudge — this reads as pressure, not news, and tends to reduce a reporter's willingness to open future pitches from the same sender.
