# Vocabulary

Terms generalists and junior crew flatten together that a pipelayer keeps sharply separate — each with the misuse that leads to a grade, bedding, or separation defect.

## Invert vs. crown vs. springline

**Invert** is the lowest interior point of the pipe's cross-section — the elevation used for all grade and slope calculations. **Crown** is the highest interior point. **Springline** is the horizontal midpoint of the pipe diameter, the boundary between the haunch zone (below) and the backfill zone (above). Grade is always set and checked at invert, never at crown or the pipe's outside top.

**In use:** "We're shooting invert elevations at each station, not the top of pipe — grade is invert to invert."

**Common misuse:** checking or recording elevation at the top of pipe (crown) and then comparing it to a design invert elevation, which introduces a full pipe-diameter error into the slope calculation.

## Self-cleansing velocity

The minimum flow velocity (commonly cited around 2 fps at design flow) needed to keep solids in suspension rather than settling in a gravity line. It's the reason a minimum slope table exists at all — slope is a means to velocity, not the end goal itself.

**In use:** "This run meets the flat '1/4 in./ft' rule of thumb, but at this diameter it's still under 2 fps at design flow — the slope number alone doesn't confirm the line will stay clean."

**Common misuse:** treating a fixed slope number (like 1/4 in./ft) as sufficient on its own, without checking that it actually produces adequate velocity for the pipe's specific diameter and design flow.

## Manning's n

The roughness coefficient in Manning's equation representing a pipe material's interior friction — smoother materials (PVC, HDPE) carry a lower n and convey more flow at the same slope and diameter than a rougher material (unlined concrete, corrugated metal) at a higher n.

**In use:** "We can use a slightly flatter slope here because it's smooth-wall PVC at n = 0.013, not the rougher concrete this table was originally cut for."

**Common misuse:** applying one material's n value to a velocity or capacity calculation for a different pipe material, understating or overstating the actual conveyance.

## Haunching (haunch zone)

Bedding material placed and compacted from the bottom of the pipe up to the springline, on both sides. For flexible pipe, this is the primary structural support zone — the load path from the pipe into the surrounding soil runs through it, not through the pipe wall.

**In use:** "The mandrel test failed on this section — before blaming the pipe, check whether the haunch was actually compacted or just backfilled and walked past."

**Common misuse:** treating haunch placement as an extension of "backfill" rather than a distinct structural step, resulting in loose or voided material exactly where flexible pipe needs the most support.

## Ring deflection

The percentage a flexible pipe's diameter has flattened (ovalized) under soil and traffic load, measured as the change in diameter divided by the original diameter. Commonly limited to 5% for PVC sewer pipe; exceeding it risks cracking or joint separation even without visible surface distress.

**In use:** "4.1% deflection on the mandrel test — inside the 5% limit, but close enough that I'd want to know the haunch compaction record for that section."

**Common misuse:** treating the mandrel test as pass/fail with no attention to how close the reading sits to the limit, missing an early warning that a section is under-supported even though it technically passed.

## D-load / crush strength vs. pipe stiffness

**D-load** (or crush strength, via the three-edge-bearing test, ASTM C76 for reinforced concrete pipe) rates a rigid pipe's own wall strength against a crushing load. **Pipe stiffness** (ASTM D2412, expressed as PS rating) rates a flexible pipe's resistance to ring deflection under soil load, which depends on both the pipe wall and the surrounding soil. They are different rating systems for different failure modes and aren't interchangeable across pipe types.

**In use:** "Don't compare this PVC's PS46 stiffness rating to the RCP's D-load class — they're measuring different things on different structural systems."

**Common misuse:** assuming a higher-numbered rating always means "stronger" across both systems, when a stiffness rating and a crush-strength rating aren't measuring the same structural behavior.

## Bedding class (ASTM D2321)

A standardized category (Class IA, IB, II, III) describing bedding-material gradation and support level for flexible pipe, tied to the native soil and the pipe's structural needs — not a generic term for "whatever fill goes under the pipe."

**In use:** "Native soil here doesn't meet Class II gradation for this run — we're importing crushed stone to hit Class IB instead."

**Common misuse:** using "bedding" to mean any fill material placed in the trench bottom, without checking whether it actually meets the gradation and compaction requirements of the specified class.

## Proctor density (standard vs. modified)

The reference maximum dry density for a soil at its optimum moisture content, determined by a standardized lab compaction test — **standard Proctor** (ASTM D698) uses lower compactive effort than **modified Proctor** (ASTM D1557). Field compaction targets are expressed as a percentage of whichever reference was specified, and the two aren't interchangeable.

**In use:** "Spec calls for 95% modified Proctor under the roadway — don't run the field density result against the standard Proctor curve, it'll read artificially high."

**Common misuse:** comparing a field density reading to the wrong reference curve (standard vs. modified), which can make an under-compacted lift appear to meet spec or vice versa.

## Separation (horizontal/vertical) vs. clearance

**Separation** in the water/sewer context refers to the specific 10 ft horizontal / 18 in. vertical standard distances between water main and sewer, measured edge-to-edge (outside diameter to outside diameter), not centerline-to-centerline. **Clearance** at a crossing point specifically means the vertical gap between the two pipes at that point, with direction (which pipe is above) mattering as much as the distance.

**In use:** "Ten feet from centerline to centerline isn't ten feet of separation once you subtract both pipes' diameters — measure edge to edge."

**Common misuse:** measuring pipe separation centerline-to-centerline instead of edge-to-edge, which overstates the actual clearance by the combined radii of both pipes.

## Competent person (OSHA term)

A specific OSHA designation for an individual capable of identifying existing and predictable excavation hazards and authorized to take prompt corrective action, including stopping work — not a general term for "the experienced person on site."

**In use:** "The competent person hasn't inspected this trench since yesterday's rain — nobody goes in until that inspection happens."

**Common misuse:** treating any senior crew member's informal opinion on trench safety as equivalent to the competent person's formal daily inspection and sign-off authority, which OSHA specifically requires and documents.

## Mandrel test

A go/no-go deflection test for flexible gravity pipe, pulling a sized mandrel through the completed and backfilled line to confirm ring deflection is within the design limit (commonly 5%) — run only after the specified minimum settlement period (commonly 30 days) has passed, not immediately after backfill.

**In use:** "We can't mandrel-test this section yet — it's only been 12 days since final backfill, and the spec calls for 30."

**Common misuse:** running the mandrel test immediately after backfill to "get it off the schedule," before the backfill has had time to settle into its long-term compacted state, producing a passing result that doesn't reflect the pipe's eventual deflection.

## Trench box vs. shoring vs. sloping/benching

Three distinct OSHA-recognized protective systems: a **trench box** (or shield) protects workers inside it from a collapse without preventing the collapse itself; **shoring** (hydraulic or timber) actively braces the trench walls to prevent collapse; **sloping/benching** cuts the trench walls back to an angle or steps that the classified soil type can hold without support. They are selected by soil classification, depth, and site constraints, not interchangeably by crew preference or equipment availability.

**In use:** "We don't have room to bench back at this depth in this corridor — it's a trench box or hydraulic shoring, not sloping."

**Common misuse:** treating any one of the three as a universal default regardless of soil classification, depth, or available width, rather than selecting per the specific trench condition.
