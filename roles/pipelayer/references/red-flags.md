# Red flags

Field smell tests a pipelayer or lead catches during rough-in or a pre-backfill walkthrough. Format for each: what it usually means, the first question to ask, and the data to pull before backfill closes over the answer.

## 1. Measured slope over a run below the diameter's minimum (e.g., 8 in. line reading under 0.40%)

**Usually means:** grade was set or checked by eye off adjacent ground rather than off stationed invert elevations — the line may still convey clear water today but falls below the self-cleansing velocity solids need to stay in suspension.

**First question:** "What's the measured drop over the actual station distance, off the two known benchmark inverts — not the slope it looks like from the surface?"

**Data to pull:** as-laid invert elevations at each station, design invert elevations, calculated actual slope, Manning's-equation velocity at design flow.

## 2. Trench 5 ft deep or greater with no trench box, shoring, or benching present

**Usually means:** the protective-system requirement was skipped or assumed unnecessary for "quick" work — a cave-in at this depth can bury a worker in seconds, and OSHA's 5 ft trigger is a floor, not a judgment call.

**First question:** "Has a competent person classified this soil today, and what protective system does that classification require at this depth?"

**Data to pull:** soil classification (Type A/B/C) and date/inspector, trench depth, protective system in place, groundwater condition.

## 3. Trench box or shoring rated for shallower depth or a more favorable soil type than actually present

**Usually means:** equipment on hand was substituted for equipment rated to the actual condition — a box rated for Type A soil in an actual Type C, saturated condition can fail under lateral pressure it wasn't designed for.

**First question:** "What's this box's manufacturer depth and soil-type rating, and does today's classification match it?"

**Data to pull:** box/shoring manufacturer rating plate, current soil classification, trench depth, groundwater observations.

## 4. Visible void or soft spot under the pipe haunch when a section is reopened or probed

**Usually means:** haunch compaction was skipped or under-compacted at the time of backfill — the pipe has been carrying load at a single unsupported point rather than along a uniform bearing surface, and cracking or ovalization concentrates there.

**First question:** "What compaction method and lift thickness was used in the haunch zone on this section, and was it density-tested?"

**Data to pull:** compaction method and equipment used, lift thickness, density test results (if any) for that station, pipe material and bedding class specified.

## 5. Compaction test result below the specified percent-of-Proctor for its zone

**Usually means:** the lift won't fail today's test, but it will consolidate under groundwater cycling and surface load over months — producing a settled trench line or exceeding a flexible pipe's deflection limit well after crews have left.

**First question:** "Which zone and lift does this reading belong to, and what's the spec target for that zone?"

**Data to pull:** density-test reading and location (station, depth, zone), spec target for that zone, moisture content at time of test, retest plan if below target.

## 6. New sewer within 10 ft horizontal of a water main with no documented 18 in. vertical clearance

**Usually means:** the separation conflict wasn't checked against actual surveyed elevations, only nominal plan spacing — a cross-connection risk exists if the water main ever sees a negative-pressure event near a sewer defect at this proximity.

**First question:** "What's the surveyed vertical clearance at the closest point, water main relative to sewer, not the plan's assumed corridor spacing?"

**Data to pull:** horizontal separation distance, vertical clearance at closest approach, which line is higher, pipe materials at the conflict point.

## 7. Mandrel or deflection test on flexible pipe reading above 5% (or the project's specified limit)

**Usually means:** haunch or backfill compaction was inadequate, backfill was placed before the design's minimum settlement period, or a point load (rock, debris, over-compaction directly on the crown) ovalized the pipe at one location.

**First question:** "Where along the run does the deflection concentrate, and what's the backfill and compaction record for that station?"

**Data to pull:** mandrel test date relative to backfill completion, deflection reading by station, compaction records for the affected section, any known point-load sources (boulders, debris, tamper contact).

## 8. Backfill placed and compacted before density-test results are returned

**Usually means:** schedule pressure overrode the verification step — an out-of-spec result can no longer be corrected without reopening the trench, and there's no way to confirm today's compaction was adequate after the fact.

**First question:** "Do we have a passing density result for this lift, or are we backfilling on the assumption it'll pass?"

**Data to pull:** density-test schedule and turnaround time, lift/station awaiting results, contingency plan if a result comes back below target after the next lift is placed.

## 9. Pipe laid with less cover than the manufacturer's minimum for its material and the actual traffic loading

**Usually means:** depth was set to match an adjacent trench or a convenient excavation depth rather than the pipe's own load chart for the material and loading class present — ring deflection or crushing risk exists even though the pipe passed its initial test.

**First question:** "What's this pipe's manufacturer minimum cover for this traffic class, and what's the actual measured cover here?"

**Data to pull:** pipe material, class/SDR rating, manufacturer cover chart for the loading class, actual measured cover at the shallowest point.

## 10. Same bedding class and compaction spec applied to both a rigid-pipe and flexible-pipe run in the same trench

**Usually means:** bedding was specified by habit rather than by material — rigid pipe (ductile iron, RCP) needs uniform bearing but not the same haunch support flexible pipe structurally depends on; applying the rigid-pipe spec to flexible pipe under-supports it.

**First question:** "Is this section's bedding class specified for this pipe's material, or copied from the adjacent run?"

**Data to pull:** pipe material by section, bedding class specified per section, ASTM D2321 class requirement for the actual material and native soil.

## 11. Spoil pile, excavated material, or equipment staged within 2 ft of the trench edge

**Usually means:** a surcharge load sits directly above the trench wall, increasing the risk of collapse regardless of the protective system rated for unloaded conditions.

**First question:** "How far is the spoil pile or equipment from the trench edge, measured, not estimated?"

**Data to pull:** measured setback distance, spoil pile height/volume, protective system's rated capacity under surcharge load.

## 12. Joint deflection on a curved alignment approaching or exceeding the pipe manufacturer's maximum angular deflection per joint

**Usually means:** the alignment curve was achieved by over-deflecting individual joints rather than using curved-alignment fittings or shorter radius pipe sections, risking joint pullout or leakage at the gasket.

**First question:** "What's the actual deflection angle at each joint on this curve, measured against the manufacturer's maximum?"

**Data to pull:** joint-by-joint deflection angle, manufacturer's maximum rated deflection for the pipe and joint type, curve radius achieved versus alignment requirement.
