## Household-composition decision table

| Living arrangement | Buys/prepares food together? | Household for SNAP purposes | Household for tax-based programs (e.g. Medicaid) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Spouse, minor children, same residence | Yes (assumed) | One household | One household (tax filing unit) |
| Adult child, same residence, separate groceries | No | Two households | Depends on tax-dependent status, not residence |
| Roommate, separate groceries, shared rent | No | Two households | Two households (unless tax dependent) |
| Unmarried partner, shared groceries | Yes | One household | Two households unless a tax dependent relationship exists |
| Elderly parent, same residence, meals prepared together | Yes | One household (special elderly/disabled provisions may allow separate) | Depends on tax-dependent status |

The SNAP test is behavioral (do they actually buy/prepare food together), not relational — a married couple who genuinely maintain separate food budgets can be two SNAP households even while filing taxes jointly. Confirm the behavioral fact in the interview; don't infer it from the relationship label.

## Deduction checklist by program type

**SNAP-style programs:**
- Standard deduction (household-size-based, fixed amount)
- Earned-income deduction (20% of gross earned income)
- Dependent-care deduction (actual verified cost, no cap in most states)
- Medical expense deduction (elderly/disabled members only, costs over a threshold)
- Excess shelter deduction (rent/mortgage + utilities over a threshold, capped except for elderly/disabled households)

**Medicaid-style (MAGI) programs:**
- No standard deduction — uses tax-based Modified Adjusted Gross Income
- Above-the-line tax deductions only (retirement contributions, student loan interest, etc.)
- No earned-income or dependent-care deduction structure — different eligibility architecture entirely, do not carry SNAP deductions over

**TANF-style cash assistance:**
- Varies by state; typically a flat earned-income disregard plus a percentage disregard, structured to taper benefits as earnings rise rather than cliff-cutting at a threshold

Applying a SNAP deduction to a Medicaid case (or vice versa) is one of the most common processing errors — confirm the program before pulling a deduction list from memory.

## Verification-sufficiency triage

1. **Document present and matches all reported details** → accept, move on.
2. **Document missing, applicant has a plausible reason (new job, hasn't received first pay stub yet)** → request alternative verification (employer statement, offer letter) or hold case open under processing-time clock; do not deny for missing document alone within the allowed processing window.
3. **Document present but shows a detail that contradicts the application (different employer, different pay amount, undisclosed household member appearing in bank statement)** → return to applicant for explanation before proceeding; document both the discrepancy and the explanation given.
4. **Discrepancy remains unexplained after applicant contact, or applicant becomes evasive when asked directly** → escalate per agency fraud-referral procedure; do not make the eligibility determination based on unresolved conflicting information.
