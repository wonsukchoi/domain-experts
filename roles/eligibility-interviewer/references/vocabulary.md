# Eligibility Interviewer — Vocabulary

### Categorical eligibility
Automatic income-test eligibility for a program granted because the household already qualifies for a different program (e.g. SNAP categorical eligibility for households receiving certain TANF-funded benefits), bypassing the standard income test.
**In use:** "This household is categorically eligible off their TANF case, so we don't need to run the gross and net tests separately."
**Common misuse:** Treated as "automatically approved for everything" — categorical eligibility usually still requires confirming the underlying qualifying benefit is active and often still requires the asset test in states that have one.

### Income disregard
An amount or percentage of income excluded from the eligibility calculation before applying deductions — distinct from a deduction, which is subtracted after countable income is established.
**In use:** "The first $65 plus half the remainder of earned income is disregarded before we calculate the TANF grant."
**Common misuse:** Confused with "deduction" — disregards and deductions are applied at different points in some program formulas and mixing up the order changes the result.

### Prospective budgeting
Calculating benefits based on income expected during the upcoming certification period, rather than income actually received in a past period.
**In use:** "Since her hours just increased, we budget prospectively using the new pay rate, not last month's lower-hours pay stub."
**Common misuse:** Applied inconsistently — some programs require prospective budgeting, others require retrospective (based on a prior fixed period); using the wrong method for the program produces a benefit amount that won't reconcile at recertification.

### Retrospective budgeting
Calculating benefits based on income actually received during a defined past period (a "budget month"), used by some programs instead of prospective budgeting.
**In use:** "This program uses retrospective budgeting, so this month's benefit is based on income from two months ago, not current income."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to be the default for all programs — it isn't; mixing prospective and retrospective methods across programs for the same household is a common processing error.

### Countable income
Income that is included in the eligibility calculation, as opposed to excluded income (certain benefits, one-time payments, in-kind support) that doesn't count toward the limit at all.
**In use:** "Child support received is countable income for this program, but the one-time disaster relief payment is excluded."
**Common misuse:** Assumed all income sources are treated the same — exclusion lists are program-specific and change; a generalist assumption that "all income counts" or "all income is treated equally" both cause errors.

### Verification
Documentation or a data match confirming a fact stated on the application (income, household composition, residency), as distinct from self-attestation (the applicant's own statement, unverified).
**In use:** "Income is self-attested this month; we'll require verification at the six-month check-in."
**Common misuse:** Treated as always mandatory at application — many programs allow self-attestation for most facts, with verification required only for specific high-risk data points or at set intervals, not universally at every contact.

### Change in circumstances
A reportable event during a certification period (income change, household-member change, address change) that may require a benefit recalculation before the next scheduled recertification.
**In use:** "She reported a change in circumstances — new job — so we need to recalculate before her next certification date."
**Common misuse:** Assumed every income fluctuation triggers a required report — most programs only require reporting changes above a defined threshold (e.g. a $ amount or a household-composition change), not every paycheck variation.

### Certification period
The fixed span of time (commonly 6 or 12 months) for which an eligibility determination remains valid without a full re-review, subject to interim reporting requirements.
**In use:** "Her certification period runs through October; she'll need to recertify before then to avoid a benefit gap."
**Common misuse:** Confused with "forever until something changes" — a certification period ends on a fixed date regardless of whether circumstances changed, and failing to recertify on time causes a benefit termination even for an unchanged, eligible household.

### Overpayment
Benefits issued in excess of what the household was actually eligible for, discovered retrospectively (often at recertification or through a data match), which typically becomes a debt owed back to the program.
**In use:** "The overpayment traces back to an unreported income increase three months into the certification period."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to always indicate fraud — overpayments are frequently caused by processing delay, a late-reported change, or agency calculation error, not intentional misreporting; the cause determines the recovery process, not just the existence of the overpayment.

### Fraud referral
A formal escalation of a case to an investigative unit when evidence suggests intentional misrepresentation, distinct from a documentation gap or an honest reporting error.
**In use:** "The unexplained employer mismatch after two follow-up contacts is going to a fraud referral."
**Common misuse:** Used as a first response to any inconsistency rather than a last step after the interview process has tried to resolve the discrepancy — premature referrals both waste investigative capacity and can wrongly flag an eligible household.

### Asset test
A separate eligibility test (used by some but not all programs) limiting countable resources (bank balances, non-exempt vehicles, property) below a threshold, independent of the income test.
**In use:** "This state still has a SNAP asset test, so we need his savings-account balance even though his income already qualifies."
**Common misuse:** Assumed universal — many programs and states have eliminated asset tests entirely; applying one where it doesn't exist for that program denies otherwise-eligible households.
