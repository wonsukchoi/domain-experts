# Vocabulary

### Hydrogen embrittlement
A delayed, brittle failure mode in certain steel grades (particularly high-strength steel) caused by hydrogen atoms absorbed during acid processing diffusing into the metal and weakening its structure under load, often manifesting hours or days after processing.
**In use:** "This grade's embrittlement-susceptible — that's why the bake has to happen within the specified window, not just eventually."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a part is safe from embrittlement because it passed dimensional and visual inspection immediately after processing — embrittlement is specifically a delayed failure mode, and immediate inspection doesn't reveal it.

### Embrittlement relief baking
A controlled heat treatment (specific temperature and duration) applied after acid processing to allow absorbed hydrogen to diffuse out of susceptible steel before it can cause delayed cracking, effective only within a specified time window after the acid exposure.
**In use:** "Bake temperature and duration were both correct, but it started two hours past the window — that's still a nonconformance."
**Common misuse:** Treating a correctly executed bake (right temperature, right duration) as sufficient regardless of when it started relative to the acid exposure — the timing between exposure and bake start is itself a critical parameter, since hydrogen diffusion and potential crack initiation can begin during an extended delay.

### Overpickling
A defect where a part remains in an acid pickling bath long enough (or at high enough concentration) that, after scale/oxide removal is complete, the acid begins attacking the base metal itself, causing pitting or dimensional loss.
**In use:** "That surface roughness isn't leftover scale — that's overpickling, the acid kept working after the scale was already gone."
**Common misuse:** Assuming extended pickling time is a safe way to guarantee scale removal — overpickling is a real, distinct failure mode from underpickling, not just "extra insurance" with no downside.

### Titration (bath concentration verification)
A chemical analysis method measuring the actual concentration of an acid bath's active component, used to verify actual bath strength rather than assuming it matches original makeup concentration.
**In use:** "Titration came back lower than expected — iron buildup's diluting the bath's actual pickling strength even though we haven't added water."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a bath's concentration matches its original makeup based on elapsed time or volume of acid originally added — actual concentration changes with use (iron buildup, drag-in dilution) independent of the bath's nominal age.

### Drag-in / drag-out
The unavoidable transfer of solution (acid, rinse water) between process tanks as parts and racks move from one bath to the next, affecting both bath concentration (dilution) and rinse effectiveness (residue carryover).
**In use:** "Drag-in from the rinse tank is part of why bath concentration drifts over time — that's a normal process effect we account for with periodic titration, not something we can eliminate."
**Common misuse:** Treating bath concentration as static because "nothing was added or removed" — drag-in and drag-out are continuous, unavoidable effects of normal processing that shift concentration over time regardless of deliberate additions.

### Rinse verification (pH/conductivity check)
A direct measurement confirming acid or dissolved residue has been adequately removed from a part's surface after pickling, using pH test strips or a conductivity meter rather than visual inspection alone.
**In use:** "Conductivity's still elevated on this rack — rinse isn't done even though it looks clean."
**Common misuse:** Relying on visual appearance (a clean-looking, dry surface) to confirm adequate rinsing — acid or salt residue can be present at a level that affects downstream coating adhesion or causes staining without being visible to the eye.

### Scale (mill scale)
The oxide layer that forms on steel surfaces during hot working or heat treatment, which pickling is designed to remove before subsequent processing (coating, forming).
**In use:** "Scale's not fully off in this corner — that's underpickling, needs more time or a stronger bath, not a rinse fix."
**Common misuse:** Confusing residual scale with a rinse or surface contamination issue — scale removal is the pickling bath's job, and incomplete removal indicates a pickling process issue (time, concentration, temperature), not a downstream rinse problem.

### Sustained-load embrittlement test
A destructive test (per standards such as ASTM F519) applying a sustained load to a sample part for an extended duration to detect delayed cracking from hydrogen embrittlement, used to verify a batch's actual embrittlement status beyond process parameter compliance alone.
**In use:** "Bake timing was out of spec, so we're not just releasing on process records — pulling a sample for sustained-load testing to confirm the parts are actually sound."
**Common misuse:** Treating process parameter compliance (correct pickling and bake conditions) as sufficient proof a batch is embrittlement-free when a known process deviation occurred — when the process itself deviated from spec, direct testing is warranted rather than inferring safety from partial compliance.

### High-strength steel classification (embrittlement susceptibility)
A material property classification (typically based on hardness or tensile strength) determining whether a steel grade requires embrittlement relief baking after acid processing.
**In use:** "This fastener's hardness puts it in the embrittlement-susceptible category — different handling than the mild steel brackets running on the other line."
**Common misuse:** Applying the same pickling/baking process uniformly across all steel grades without checking each material's specific susceptibility classification — susceptibility depends on the material's actual hardness/strength properties, and not every steel grade requires the same post-pickling treatment.
