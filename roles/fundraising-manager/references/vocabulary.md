# Nonprofit development working vocabulary

Terms a fundraiser uses precisely. Format: definition → how a practitioner says it → common misuse.

**Restricted vs. unrestricted funds** — Restricted funds are designated by the donor/funder for a specific program or purpose and can't be used elsewhere; unrestricted funds can be used for any organizational need, including covering gaps restricted funding doesn't address.
In use: "This grant is restricted to the mentoring program specifically — we can't use any of it to cover the overhead gap it creates, that has to come from unrestricted funds."
Misuse: evaluating a grant's value purely by its dollar amount without checking whether its restrictions create a burden (an overhead gap, a scale mismatch) that unrestricted funds will have to absorb.

**Moves management** — A structured approach to major-gift cultivation, planning a specific sequence of deliberate "moves" (meetings, updates, invitations) for each prospect based on their relationship stage, rather than ad hoc outreach.
In use: "This prospect is at move 4 of 6 in the cultivation sequence — not ready for a solicitation conversation yet."
Misuse: soliciting a major gift without a documented cultivation history, treating the ask as a standalone event rather than the culmination of a deliberate relationship sequence.

**Donor pyramid** — A segmentation model organizing donors by giving capacity and engagement level (from broad-based small donors at the base to major and principal gift prospects at the top), used to allocate cultivation effort proportional to leverage.
In use: "We're re-mapping the donor pyramid this quarter — several mid-level donors have crossed into major-gift capacity based on recent wealth-screening data."
Misuse: spreading cultivation time evenly across the donor base regardless of pyramid position, missing that a small number of donors typically provide the large majority of revenue.

**Stewardship** — The ongoing practice of reporting back to donors on the specific impact of their gift, treated as a required part of the giving cycle rather than an optional follow-up.
In use: "Stewardship reports go out within 60 days of the gift's use being completed — that's what makes the next ask credible."
Misuse: treating the ask as the end of the donor relationship and stewardship as an occasional extra, missing that unstewarded donors have no reason to give again.

**Case for support** — The specific, donor-relevant articulation of why a gift matters now, tailored to a particular donor segment's motivations, as distinct from a generic organizational mission statement.
In use: "The case for support for this donor segment leads with the youth-outcomes data, not the org's general mission language — that's what resonates with their stated giving priorities."
Misuse: using the same generic mission-statement pitch across all donor segments, missing the donor-specific "why this, why now, why you" framing that actually motivates a major gift.

**Overhead ratio** — The percentage of an organization's total expenses spent on administrative and fundraising costs versus program costs, a commonly cited (and often over-weighted) donor/watchdog metric for evaluating nonprofit efficiency.
In use: "Our overhead ratio is 18%, and we're prepared to explain why that reflects real capacity-building costs, not inefficiency, when a donor asks."
Misuse: either dismissing overhead-ratio questions as illegitimate or being unprepared to answer them with real program-outcome context — both leave the organization exposed to a common donor objection.

**Donor lifetime value (DLV)** — The projected total giving from a donor over the full duration of their relationship with the organization, as distinct from the size of any single gift, used to guide how much cultivation investment a donor relationship justifies.
In use: "This donor's first gift was modest, but their DLV projection based on age, engagement, and mission alignment justifies more cultivation time than a larger one-time gift from a less-engaged donor."
Misuse: prioritizing cultivation investment based on single-gift size alone, missing that a donor with strong long-term alignment can be worth more total investment than a larger but less durable one-time gift.

**Wealth screening** — A research process (often software-assisted) identifying a donor or prospect's likely giving capacity based on public and proprietary data (real estate, stock holdings, past philanthropic giving), used to inform donor segmentation and cultivation prioritization.
In use: "Wealth screening flagged three mid-level donors with likely major-gift capacity we hadn't previously identified."
Misuse: relying on wealth screening data alone to prioritize solicitation without also weighing actual demonstrated engagement and mission alignment, which matter as much as raw capacity for predicting an actual gift.

**Restricted grant compliance/reporting burden** — The administrative cost (staff time, systems, documentation) required to meet a grant's reporting and compliance obligations, a real cost that should be weighed against the grant's dollar value, not treated as a formality.
In use: "The quarterly reporting requirement on this grant is estimated at 0.4 FTE of compliance work — that's a real cost against the grant's value, not an afterthought."
Misuse: accepting a grant based on its dollar amount without estimating the actual staff time its reporting/compliance requirements will consume, which can materially offset its net value.

**Multi-year pledge** — A donor's commitment to give a specified amount over multiple years, distinct from a single-year gift, requiring its own tracking for cash-flow planning and stewardship timing across the pledge period.
In use: "This is a 3-year, $2.4M pledge — we're planning stewardship touchpoints at year 1 and year 2, not just at the end of the full term."
Misuse: treating a multi-year pledge as equivalent to receiving the full amount immediately, missing the cash-flow timing and the need for stewardship touchpoints throughout the pledge period, not just at its conclusion.
