# Vocabulary

Terms generalists conflate or misuse. Each: definition, how a practitioner actually uses it in a sentence, and the common misuse to avoid.

### Burden vs. spacing

**Burden** is the distance from a blasthole to the nearest free face (the rock surface the charge will break toward) — the dimension that most controls fragmentation and flyrock risk. **Spacing** is the distance between adjacent holes in the same row, perpendicular to burden.

*In use:* "We didn't move spacing, we tightened burden — that's what was letting gas vent toward the highwall."

*Common misuse:* using the two interchangeably, or assuming a fragmentation problem is a spacing issue when burden — the dimension actually facing the free face — is almost always the first thing to check.

### Powder factor

The ratio of explosive weight to rock volume broken, expressed as lb/yd³ or kg/m³. It's an output that describes a design, not an input target to hit for its own sake.

*In use:* "Powder factor came in at 0.92 lb/yd³ on that bench — in range for this limestone, no adjustment needed."

*Common misuse:* treating powder factor as a cost lever to cut in isolation. Cutting it without adjusting burden/spacing to match under-confines the pattern and produces oversize, not savings.

### Scaled distance vs. peak particle velocity (PPV)

**Scaled distance** (Ds = D ÷ √W) is a screening formula relating distance and charge weight per delay to a conservative, regulation-set safe limit — usable without instrumentation. **PPV** is the actual measured ground vibration velocity at a specific structure, read off a seismograph, and is the real physical quantity that causes or doesn't cause damage.

*In use:* "We're inside the scaled-distance table so no seismograph is legally required, but given the complaint history I'm putting a Minimate at the nearest house anyway."

*Common misuse:* treating scaled distance as a prediction of actual PPV. It's a conservative legal ceiling calculated from a regulatory constant, not a site-calibrated model — actual PPV depends on geology (transmission path) that only site-specific monitoring reveals.

### Stemming vs. subdrill

**Stemming** is the inert material (crushed stone, drill cuttings, sand) placed above the explosive column to confine detonation gases. **Subdrill** is the extra hole depth drilled below the intended grade/floor level to ensure full breakage at the toe.

*In use:* "Toe's still standing on that row — check subdrill before you blame the timing."

*Common misuse:* confusing the two, or treating subdrill purely as wasted drilling cost — under-subdrilled holes leave a toe that costs more in secondary breakage and dozer time than the extra few feet of drilling would have.

### Misfire vs. hangfire

A **misfire** is a charge that fails to detonate as planned and remains, or is presumed to remain, live in the ground. A **hangfire** is a charge that detonates after an abnormal (but nonzero) delay rather than failing entirely — dangerous specifically because it can still fire during what should be a safe post-blast approach window.

*In use:* "Treat it as a hangfire risk, not a clean misfire — we heard a delayed report after the main shot, so the wait period restarts from that detonation, not the original firing time."

*Common misuse:* assuming a hangfire "counted" as part of the main shot because it eventually went off — the danger is specifically that it detonated later than expected, which means the standard wait-and-clear timing assumptions were already being violated when it happened.

### Blockholing vs. mudcapping (plaster shooting)

**Blockholing** drills a small hole into an oversize boulder and loads a contained charge inside it. **Mudcapping** places an uncontained charge directly on the boulder's surface, covered with stemming material, when drilling isn't practical.

*In use:* "That one's too close to the property line for mudcapping — we're blockholing it or bringing the hoe ram in."

*Common misuse:* defaulting to mudcapping for convenience (no drilling required) without accounting for its substantially higher flyrock risk versus a contained charge.

### Nonel (shock tube) vs. electric vs. electronic detonators

**Electric** detonators fire via a current pulse through leg wires — vulnerable to stray current and static. **Nonel/shock tube** detonators fire via a shock wave through a plastic tube, immune to stray electricity but with fixed, factory-set delay increments. **Electronic** detonators use a programmable chip, giving millisecond-precise, arbitrarily assignable delays per hole at higher cost.

*In use:* "We're switching this shot to electronic dets specifically so no two holes can end up on the same delay by accident — the det-cord tie-in was the whole problem last time."

*Common misuse:* assuming detonator type is purely a cost decision. Electronic detonators materially reduce duplicate-delay tie-in risk (the leading cause of vibration overruns) precisely because delays are assigned per hole in software, not read off a fixed factory increment chart.

### Blaster-in-charge (BIC)

The individual legally designated on the shot record as holding regulatory authority and personal liability for a specific blast, as distinct from anyone else present (crew, engineer who designed the pattern, site superintendent).

*In use:* "I'm BIC of record on this shot — the pattern was engineered upstream, but the tie-in and the fire decision are mine to sign off."

*Common misuse:* assuming the person who designed the pattern or the most senior person on site automatically carries this liability — it's a specific regulatory designation tied to certification and the shot record, not seniority.

### Flyrock vs. backbreak

**Flyrock** is rock propelled beyond the intended muckpile by blast gas escaping through insufficient confinement — a safety hazard measured in throw distance. **Backbreak** is fracturing that extends behind the last row of holes into rock that wasn't meant to be broken this shot — a design/dilution problem, not primarily a safety one.

*In use:* "That's backbreak into next bench's burden, not flyrock — nothing left the muckpile, but we've compromised next shot's toe."

*Common misuse:* using "flyrock" loosely for any unwanted rock movement. The distinction matters because they have different causes (confinement failure vs. back-row burden/timing) and different urgency (flyrock is an immediate safety review; backbreak is a design adjustment for the next pattern).

### Sympathetic detonation

Unintended detonation of one charge caused by the shock wave of an adjacent detonating charge, rather than by its own initiation system — a risk specifically when charges are placed too close together without adequate delay separation or when explosive is not adequately confined.

*In use:* "Minimum spacing on those decked charges has to account for sympathetic detonation risk between decks, not just the design timing."

*Common misuse:* assuming timing design alone prevents this — physical charge separation and confinement also matter, particularly for decked charges in the same hole or closely spaced holes in sensitive (cap-sensitive) explosive.
