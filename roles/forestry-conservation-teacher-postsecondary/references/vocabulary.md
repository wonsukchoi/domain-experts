# Forestry and Conservation Science Teacher, Postsecondary — Vocabulary

### Basal area factor (BAF)
The multiplier assigned to a wedge prism or angle gauge used in variable-radius plot sampling; multiplying the count of trees counted "in" at a point by the BAF gives that point's basal area per acre.
**In use:** "We're using a BAF 10 prism today — count every tree whose bark image is wider than the prism offset, that's 'in.'"
**Common misuse:** Treating a single point's BAF-derived number as a stand-level estimate instead of averaging multiple points across the stand.

### Variable-radius (prism/point) sampling
A plot method where the "in/out" determination for each tree depends on its diameter and distance from the point, not a fixed plot boundary — faster than fixed-radius plots but requires correct borderline-tree calls.
**In use:** "Point sampling gets you through a 40-acre stand in an afternoon, but every borderline tree call has to be checked with the tape or you're biasing the count."
**Common misuse:** Assuming point sampling is inherently less accurate than fixed-radius plots; the accuracy difference is about borderline-tree technique and point count, not the method itself.

### Red card (Incident Qualification Card)
The National Wildland Coordinating Group's (NWCG) documented proof that an individual has completed the required training (e.g., S-130/S-190/L-180) and fitness test to participate in wildland fire operations, including many prescribed burns.
**In use:** "No red card, no burn line — that's not a departmental preference, it's the standard the cooperating agency will check."
**Common misuse:** Treating prior informal fire experience as equivalent to a current, documented red card.

### McIntire-Stennis (formula funds)
Federal capacity-building funding for forestry research at land-grant and other eligible institutions, authorized by the McIntire-Stennis Cooperative Forestry Research Act of 1962 and administered by USDA NIFA; allocated by formula, not competition, and reported through NIFA's REEport system.
**In use:** "That equipment purchase is capacity-building, so it's a legitimate McIntire-Stennis line — the single-project consulting fee isn't."
**Common misuse:** Spending McIntire-Stennis funds like an ordinary competitive research grant without documenting the capacity-building rationale required in reporting.

### SAF accreditation
Certification by the Society of American Foresters' Committee on Accreditation that a forestry degree program meets defined standards; tied directly to graduates' eligibility for state Registered Forester licensure and the SAF Certified Forester credential in many jurisdictions.
**In use:** "We're mapping every required course against the current SAF standards this spring, a year ahead of the self-study, so any gap gets fixed with time to spare."
**Common misuse:** Treating accreditation as a periodic compliance event handled only in the self-study year, rather than an ongoing curriculum-mapping habit.

### Certified Forester (CF)
A voluntary professional credential from the Society of American Foresters, distinct from state-issued Registered Forester licensure, requiring a degree from an accredited program (or equivalent), experience, and a passing exam score.
**In use:** "The capstone course explicitly maps to CF exam content areas so students aren't seeing forest policy for the first time in the exam room."
**Common misuse:** Conflating CF (a voluntary national credential) with state Registered Forester status (a legal licensure requirement that varies by state).

### Silviculture
The applied science of controlling forest establishment, composition, and growth to meet management objectives — distinct from forest management, which is the broader planning and economic layer above it.
**In use:** "The silvicultural prescription calls for a shelterwood cut, not a clearcut, to favor the shade-tolerant regeneration already established."
**Common misuse:** Using "silviculture" and "forestry" interchangeably; silviculture is the stand-level treatment craft, forestry is the broader profession.

### Site index
A stand-quality measure expressed as the expected height of dominant trees at a reference age (commonly 25, 50, or 100 years depending on species and region), used to predict site productivity independent of current stand age.
**In use:** "This stand has a site index of 70 at base age 50 for loblolly pine — that's a productive site regardless of how young the current stand is."
**Common misuse:** Comparing site index values across species or across different reference (base) ages as if they were on the same scale.

### Increment core
A pencil-diameter wood sample extracted with an increment borer, used to count annual growth rings for age and growth-rate determination without felling the tree.
**In use:** "Pull an increment core at breast height, not at the base — ring counts at the base overestimate age in most species due to suppressed early growth."
**Common misuse:** Treating a ring count from a core taken at ground level as equivalent to one taken at breast height.

### Statement of Regional Research Priorities
The document (updated periodically by the McIntire-Stennis-affiliated regional research coordinating bodies) that defines the applied-research priorities a McIntire-Stennis-funded activity should tie to when justified as capacity-building.
**In use:** "The grad student's remote-sensing project maps directly to the forest-health-monitoring priority in this year's regional statement — that's the capacity-building justification for the assistantship line."
**Common misuse:** Treating any forestry-relevant research as automatically justified McIntire-Stennis spending, without checking it against the current named priorities document.

### Forest Vegetation Simulator (FVS)
A widely used, U.S. Forest Service-maintained individual-tree growth-and-yield model used to project stand development under different management scenarios.
**In use:** "Run the no-treatment and thinning scenarios through FVS and compare the 20-year volume projections before writing the management-plan recommendation."
**Common misuse:** Treating an FVS output as a measured value rather than a model projection sensitive to its input assumptions (site index, regional variant, stocking).

### Escape route (fireline/felling)
A pre-identified, physically walked path allowing personnel to move a safe distance from an active hazard (a falling tree, an advancing fire) before the hazard event begins.
**In use:** "Walk the escape routes with the crew before the saw starts, not after — and re-walk them if wind direction changes."
**Common misuse:** Briefing an escape route verbally without physically walking it, or failing to re-brief it when conditions (wind, terrain visibility) change mid-lab.
