# Vocabulary

Terms of art in academic philosophy and religious studies that generalists misuse. Format per term: definition, how a practitioner actually uses it, the common misuse.

### Principle of charity

- **Definition:** the norm of reconstructing an opposing position in its strongest, most defensible form before objecting to it.
- **In use:** "Before I object, here's the strongest version of the argument — with the missing premise supplied — and now here's where it still fails."
- **Common misuse:** treating disagreement with a conclusion as evidence the argument for it was weak, without ever reconstructing the argument in its best form first.

### Argument reconstruction

- **Definition:** rewriting a position as an explicit premise-conclusion structure, including implicit premises the original author didn't state outright.
- **In use:** "Their stated premises don't get you to the conclusion without an implicit claim about moral status — I'm adding that claim explicitly before I evaluate it."
- **Common misuse:** confusing paraphrase (restating the same words differently) with reconstruction (supplying the logical structure, including what's unstated).

### Validity vs. soundness

- **Definition:** an argument is valid if the conclusion follows necessarily from the premises regardless of whether the premises are true; it's sound if it's valid and the premises are also actually true.
- **In use:** "It's valid — the conclusion follows — but I don't think premise two is true, so it's not sound."
- **Common misuse:** using "valid" to mean "true" or "correct," or treating a valid argument as thereby persuasive regardless of premise truth.

### Descriptive vs. prescriptive (register, religious studies)

- **Definition:** descriptive language reports what a tradition's adherents believe or practice ("adherents hold," "the tradition teaches"); prescriptive language asserts a truth claim about the world ("this is true," "we should believe").
- **In use:** "That slide slipped into prescriptive framing on the resurrection — I need to recast it as what the tradition holds, not what's true."
- **Common misuse:** assuming any factual statement about a tradition's content ("Muslims hold that...") is itself an endorsement or an attack, when the register is descriptive.

### Confessional vs. academic study of religion

- **Definition:** confessional study proceeds from within a tradition's own premises toward forming or deepening faith (a seminary's task); academic study of religion proceeds from an external, disciplinary, historical-critical stance without adjudicating truth claims.
- **In use:** "This is a state-university religious-studies department — we teach the history and practice of the tradition, not its truth claims; that's a seminary's job, not ours."
- **Common misuse:** assuming a public university's religious-studies department teaches theology or faith formation, or that a professor's personal faith (or lack of one) is the operative fact rather than the pedagogical register used in class.

### Big Three (philosophy journals)

- **Definition:** the informal label for the field's most prestigious generalist journals — *Mind*, the *Journal of Philosophy*, and the *Philosophical Review* — known for very high rejection rates and long review cycles.
- **In use:** "It's been under review at the Big Three for fourteen months — that's normal for that tier, not a bad sign about the paper specifically."
- **Common misuse:** treating a Big Three rejection as a verdict on the paper's quality, when the tier's base rejection rate is high enough that most strong papers are rejected there at least once.

### Tenure-track (TT) placement rate

- **Definition:** the share of a given cohort of PhD graduates who land a tenure-track position within a stated window, as tracked by sources like APDA.
- **In use:** "The current subfield placement rate is worth checking before committing to another cycle — it moves year to year."
- **Common misuse:** relying on a single department's self-reported, unaudited placement numbers, or on anecdote from one's own cohort, instead of a tracked, sourced figure.

### Visiting Assistant Professor (VAP)

- **Definition:** a fixed-term (typically one- to three-year) teaching position, usually without a tenure track, sometimes but not always convertible to one.
- **In use:** "It's a two-year VAP with no stated conversion clause — I'm treating it as a strong CV line and a paid market year, not as a probationary tenure-track appointment."
- **Common misuse:** assuming a VAP functions like the early years of a tenure-track probationary period, when most carry no conversion guarantee at all.

### Adjunct / contingent faculty

- **Definition:** faculty employed per course or per term without tenure-track status, job security, or (typically) benefits tied to the position.
- **In use:** "I'm adjuncting four sections across two campuses this term — that's a scheduling and pay-math problem before it's anything else."
- **Common misuse:** treating per-course adjunct pay as scaling linearly with course count in a way that makes a fifth or sixth prep worthwhile, without checking the effective hourly rate.

### Socratic method

- **Definition:** a structured method of questioning that leads an interlocutor to expose or resolve a contradiction in their own stated commitments (elenchus), not merely "asking a lot of questions."
- **In use:** "I'm not asking what they think — I'm building toward the point where their own two premises contradict each other."
- **Common misuse:** conflating any question-heavy discussion format with the Socratic method specifically, when the defining move is the structured contradiction, not the question format.

### Establishment Clause (as applied to teaching religion)

- **Definition:** the First Amendment provision, as interpreted in cases like *Abington School District v. Schempp*, that bars government-sponsored religious exercise but explicitly permits objective academic study of religion in public education.
- **In use:** "This is the *Schempp* line — devotional exercise is out, academic study of the material is squarely in."
- **Common misuse:** assuming any classroom discussion of religious content in a public university violates church-state separation, when the controlling precedent explicitly distinguishes devotional practice from academic study.

### Comparative religion / religious studies

- **Definition:** the academic, cross-tradition, historical-critical study of religious beliefs and practices, distinct from theology, which reasons from within a specific tradition's premises.
- **In use:** "This department teaches religious studies — comparative, historical-critical method — not theology from within any single tradition's premises."
- **Common misuse:** using "religious studies" and "theology" interchangeably, when the two operate from different methodological starting points.

### Analytic vs. continental philosophy

- **Definition:** a rough methodological and stylistic division in the discipline — analytic philosophy emphasizes formal argument structure and close conceptual analysis; continental philosophy draws more on historical and interpretive traditions (phenomenology, critical theory) — not a fixed canon split.
- **In use:** "This search is analytic-leaning in method, which affects which journals and letters read as strong fits — it's not a verdict on the continental candidate's rigor."
- **Common misuse:** treating the division as a rigid, mutually exclusive canon rather than a methodological tendency that affects journal and hiring fit.

### Philosophical Gourmet Report (PGR / Leiter Report)

- **Definition:** a survey-based ranking of PhD programs by faculty reputation among philosophers surveyed, not a measure of placement outcomes or teaching quality.
- **In use:** "The program ranks well on the PGR, but I still want the actual placement numbers before treating that as a hiring or admissions signal."
- **Common misuse:** treating the ranking as a proxy for a program's actual placement rate or teaching quality, which it doesn't measure.
