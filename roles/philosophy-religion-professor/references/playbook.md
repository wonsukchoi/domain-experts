# Playbook

Filled templates and step sequences, not descriptions of them. Defaults, not laws — deviate consciously and log why in the record you keep.

## 1. Syllabus tradition-balance audit (comparative-religion courses)

Run before the term starts, and re-run if a fairness complaint is filed. Count readings per tradition, coded insider/primary (a scripture translation, a practitioner-authored text) versus outsider/secondary (an academic analysis written from outside the tradition).

| Tradition | Readings assigned | Insider/primary | Outsider/secondary | % insider |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Christianity | 4 | 3 | 1 | 75.0% |
| Islam | 3 | 1 | 2 | 33.3% |
| Hinduism | 2 | 0 | 2 | 0.0% |
| Buddhism | 2 | 0 | 2 | 0.0% |

Rule: any tradition sitting at or near 0% insider while another sits above 60% is a real asymmetry to fix before it becomes a complaint, not a stylistic choice — add at least one translated primary text per under-represented tradition.

Slide-register sample (20 slides/unit, coded prescriptive — "this teaches us," "the truth is" — vs. descriptive — "adherents hold," "the tradition teaches"):

| Unit | Prescriptive slides | % prescriptive |
|---|---|---|
| Christianity | 6 / 20 | 30% |
| Islam | 1 / 20 | 5% |
| Hinduism | 1 / 20 | 5% |
| Buddhism | 1 / 20 | 5% |

Rule: any unit running 3x or more above the course's own baseline prescriptive rate gets its slide language revised to match the baseline register before the next offering.

## 2. Argument-reconstruction rubric (philosophy papers, 100 pts)

| Criterion | Points | Descriptor for full credit |
|---|---|---|
| Reconstruction accuracy | 30 | States the target position's strongest form, including implicit premises, in language its proponent would accept |
| Validity of the student's own argument | 25 | Conclusion follows from stated premises; no unstated logical gap |
| Soundness / premise support | 20 | Each premise is either defended or explicitly flagged as an assumption |
| Engagement with the strongest objection | 15 | Names and responds to the most serious counterargument, not the easiest one |
| Prose clarity | 10 | Readable, but never traded against the above four |

Grading rule: a paper that scores full marks on prose clarity but reconstructs a straw man caps at 40/100 (the two argument-relevant rows plus clarity) — clarity cannot offset a failed reconstruction. A paper that reconstructs the opposing position accurately and argues to an unpopular or disagreeable conclusion is graded on the rubric rows only; the grader's agreement with the conclusion isn't a rubric row and doesn't enter the score.

## 3. Bias/fairness complaint response protocol

1. Classify the complaint: register question (religion — descriptive vs. prescriptive) or argument-quality question (philosophy — reconstruction/validity)? Different evidence, different standard.
2. Pull the actual material named in the complaint — reading list, slide deck, or graded paper — before responding; never respond from the aggregate evaluation number or from memory.
3. For a religion-course complaint, run the tradition-balance and slide-register audits above, and check both against Moore's three-part test (academic goals, internal diversity, distinct from faith formation) and the *Schempp* line.
4. For a philosophy-course complaint, separate "the reconstruction is inaccurate" (a real rubric failure, fixable by regrade) from "the grader's or syllabus's conclusion is one I disagree with" (not a rubric failure).
5. Where the audit finds a real asymmetry, name it specifically in the response and commit to a dated, specific fix (add reading X for unit Y next term) — a vague "we take this seriously" response satisfies neither a dean nor a AAUP-standard review.
6. Where the audit finds no asymmetry, respond citing the specific counts (readings per tradition, register sample) rather than asserting fairness in the abstract.

## 4. Job-market decision table (post-PhD)

| Years post-PhD, still market-facing | Default action | Exception |
|---|---|---|
| 0–1 (ABD/first year out) | Apply broadly across TT, VAP, and postdoc listings on PhilJobs; track every application (venue, date, outcome) | — |
| 2–3 | Narrow to subfield-matched TT and named VAP-to-TT-track lines; check APDA's current placement-rate report for the subfield before committing another full cycle | A funded, named VAP-to-TT conversion clause, or a subfield (bioethics, philosophy of medicine, applied ethics) with a documented above-average placement rate |
| 4+ | Default to pivoting the CV toward teaching-track, community-college, or alt-academic framing; treat continued TT-only search as a choice to make explicitly, not a default | A specific, credible signal this cycle (e.g., a funded named line in the exact subfield, an internal hire track already in motion) |

## 5. Philosophy journal-submission triage

| Venue tier | Typical review time | Typical rejection rate | When to use |
|---|---|---|---|
| Generalist flagship ("Big Three" — *Mind*, *Journal of Philosophy*, *Philosophical Review*) | 6–18+ months | Very high (historically cited well above 90%) | Genuinely general-interest contribution across subfields; budget for a long wait and a likely reject |
| Strong generalist (second-tier, still cross-subfield) | 4–10 months | High, lower than the Big Three | Solid general-interest paper not aimed at the absolute top tier |
| Specialist / subfield journal | 2–6 months | Moderate, meaningfully lower than generalist tiers | Most papers — the actual readership for a subfield-specific contribution, and a faster, better-odds placement |
| Edited volume / conference proceedings | Weeks to a few months | Low to moderate, often invited or lightly refereed | Work-in-progress, or building a public record while a journal submission is still in review elsewhere |

Rule: default to the specialist tier unless the paper's actual contribution is legibly cross-subfield; a paper aimed at a narrow debate loses nothing in prestige and gains months of review-time by going to the venue that reads it.

## 6. Adjunct/visiting workload math

| Preps this term | Sections | Approx. hours/week (prep + teaching + grading) | Feasible alongside job search or research? |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 (repeated across sections) | 4 | ~20–25 | Yes — repeated prep frees time |
| 2 | 4 | ~28–34 | Marginal — depends on section size |
| 3 | 5 | ~38–45 | Difficult |
| 4–5 distinct, 2+ campuses | 5–6 | ~50+, plus commute | Rarely — negotiate consolidation before accepting |

Rule: before accepting a fourth or fifth distinct prep, ask whether the department can consolidate into repeated sections of fewer courses at the same total pay — the per-prep effective hourly rate, not the total course count, is the number that determines whether research or market time survives the term.
