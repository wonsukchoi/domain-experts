# Editor — Red Flags

### Manuscript is >20% over target word count with no single identifiable bloat source
- **Usually means:** systemic overwriting (every scene runs long) rather than one cuttable subplot — the fix is harder and slower than a structural cut.
- **First question:** "If I cut the single longest subplot entirely, does that get me within 10% of target?" If no, the problem is prose-density, not structure.
- **Data to pull:** per-chapter word counts sorted longest to shortest; compare against the manuscript's average scene length.

### Style sheet has more than ~15 undecided or inconsistent entries at the copyedit stage
- **Usually means:** the style sheet wasn't started at line-edit, so inconsistencies accumulated silently across passes.
- **First question:** "When did this style sheet actually get started?" If the answer is "this pass," expect another full read-through will be needed after copyedit to catch what a live sheet would have caught in real time.
- **Data to pull:** style sheet creation date vs. manuscript revision history.

### Author rejects every structural note in the first response
- **Usually means:** either the editorial letter opened with critique before demonstrating understanding of the book, or the notes conflict with the author's actual intent for the manuscript (not a craft problem, a communication problem).
- **First question:** "Did the letter's opening accurately describe what this book is trying to do?" A wrong read on intent invalidates everything that follows it.
- **Data to pull:** re-read the letter's opening paragraphs against the manuscript's actual throughline.

### POV slips (head-hopping) more than twice within a single scene
- **Usually means:** the author is still discovering whose scene this is, or drafted it across multiple sessions with different intended POV.
- **First question:** "Whose scene is this — who has the most at stake in this specific moment?"
- **Data to pull:** mark every interiority shift (a line revealing a second character's unstated thoughts/feelings) within the scene.

### Query-comment density exceeds roughly 1 per 100 words at the line-edit stage
- **Usually means:** either the prose has systemic problems beyond what a line edit should fix (developmental issues bleeding into line-level symptoms), or the editor is over-marking stylistic preference rather than functional problems.
- **First question:** "Are these comments fixing something broken, or imposing my voice over the author's?"
- **Data to pull:** sample 3 pages, categorize each comment as mechanical/structural-bleed/voice-preference.

### Timeline or continuity contradiction across chapters (age, date, established fact)
- **Usually means:** revisions happened out of order and earlier chapters weren't reconciled against later changes.
- **First question:** "What's the most recent version of this fact, and does it match everywhere else?"
- **Data to pull:** the style sheet's timeline/fact-tracking section; if none exists, build one before continuing the pass.

### Info-dump exceeding roughly one page within the first three chapters
- **Usually means:** the author is front-loading world-building or backstory the reader doesn't yet have a reason to care about.
- **First question:** "Does the reader need this fact now, or at the moment it becomes relevant to a choice or conflict?"
- **Data to pull:** the paragraph(s) in question, and the nearest point later in the manuscript where the same information would land with more narrative weight.

### Dialogue tags overloaded with adverbs ("she said angrily," "he asked nervously") more than a few times per chapter
- **Usually means:** the dialogue itself isn't carrying the emotional information, so the tag is compensating.
- **First question:** "If I cut the adverb, does the dialogue still read as angry/nervous?" If not, the fix is in the dialogue, not the tag.
- **Data to pull:** count of adverbial dialogue tags per chapter.

### Revision turnaround missed by the author twice consecutively
- **Usually means:** either the scope of requested revisions was underestimated when the deadline was set, or a structural note is bigger than it looked (a "small" throughline change can cascade through every chapter).
- **First question:** "Is the remaining work actually small, or did the letter undersell how much of the manuscript it touches?"
- **Data to pull:** which chapters are affected by the outstanding notes, not just how many notes remain.

### A structural note gets silently implemented by the editor rather than flagged to the author
- **Usually means:** time pressure or a strong editorial conviction crossed a line the author should have gotten to decide.
- **First question:** "Would the author be surprised to see this change if they hadn't asked for it?"
- **Data to pull:** the track-changes/redline diff for any structural-level edit (not line-level) made without an accompanying query comment.
