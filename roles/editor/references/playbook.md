## Before/after line edit (real prose pair)

**Before** (from a submitted draft, permission-style example):
> She walked slowly and carefully into the room, feeling nervous and uncertain about what she might find there, her heart beating quickly in her chest as she tried to calm herself down and prepare for whatever was waiting for her on the other side of the door.

Word count: 53. Diagnosis: stacked adverbs ("slowly and carefully," "quickly") doing the work description should do; redundant interiority (fear stated three ways: "nervous," "uncertain," "heart beating"); passive-feeling construction despite active verbs.

**After:**
> She eased the door open. Her heart hammered. Whatever was behind it, she'd know in three more steps.

Word count: 19 (64% reduction). Same information, same pacing beat, delivered through action and rhythm instead of adverbs stacked on top of stated emotion.

**Query comment attached:** "Cutting the adverb stack here — 'slowly and carefully' and 'quickly' are telling us how to feel about an action that can just show us. Also: three separate statements of fear (nervous/uncertain/heartbeat) — pick the one with the most sensory weight and cut the rest. Sample rewrite attached; not prescriptive, just showing the shape of the cut."

## Editorial letter structure (filled template)

1. **Opening — what the manuscript is doing well and why** (2-3 paragraphs, specific not generic — cite actual scene/chapter numbers).
2. **Top structural issues, ranked by scope of impact** (3-5 items max; each gets: what's happening, why it matters to the throughline, one directional suggestion — not a mandate).
3. **Secondary issues** (pacing dips, underused characters, thin subplot payoffs) — flagged but explicitly marked lower priority so the author doesn't split focus.
4. **What's NOT being addressed in this pass** (line-level prose, typos, style-guide items) — sets expectation for the next pass so the author isn't surprised those aren't in this letter.
