# Editor — Vocabulary

### Developmental edit
The first editing pass, addressing structure, plot/argument logic, pacing, and character arc — not sentence-level prose.
**In use:** "We're not touching line-level anything until the developmental pass is locked — no point polishing a chapter that might get cut."
**Common misuse:** Generalists conflate this with "big-picture feedback" delivered as a vague reaction ("it dragged in the middle") rather than a diagnosed, actionable structural issue with a specific location and cause.

### Line edit
The pass addressing sentence-level prose: rhythm, word choice, clarity, show-vs-tell — done after structure is settled, before copyedit.
**In use:** "The line edit cut this paragraph's word count by 60% without losing any information — it was doing a lot of telling where the scene could just show."
**Common misuse:** Treated as interchangeable with copyediting; line edit is a craft judgment about prose quality, copyedit is mechanical consistency enforcement.

### Copyedit
The pass enforcing grammar, punctuation, and consistency against a style guide (house style, Chicago, AP) — no structural or voice judgment involved.
**In use:** "Copyedit flagged 40 instances of the serial-comma inconsistency the style sheet should have caught at line edit."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to include proofreading; copyedit precedes the final proof pass and is not the last line of defense against typos.

### Style sheet
A living document tracking every consistency judgment call made across a manuscript — spellings, character details, timeline facts, house-style rulings.
**In use:** "Check the style sheet before flagging that as an inconsistency — we ruled on 'Vaelthorn' vs. 'Vaelthorne' in chapter one."
**Common misuse:** Started only at copyedit stage instead of from the first pass, which guarantees a second full read-through is needed to catch what should have been tracked live.

### Query comment
An embedded note to the author raising a question or concern, phrased to preserve the author's authority over the final decision.
**In use:** "That's a query, not a mandate — I flagged it because the timeline seems off, but you may have a reason I'm not seeing."
**Common misuse:** Written as an implicit command ("cut this") rather than a genuine question, which either gets blind compliance or defensive rejection instead of a considered author decision.

### Throughline
The single spine — plot, argument, or emotional arc — that everything else in the manuscript should serve or connect to.
**In use:** "This subplot is well-written, but it doesn't connect to the throughline, which is why it reads as a second, unfinished book."
**Common misuse:** Confused with "theme" or "premise" — throughline is the structural spine that scene-level triage decisions get measured against, not an abstract thematic statement.

### POV slip / head-hopping
An unintentional shift in whose internal thoughts or feelings the narration reveals within a single scene, breaking the established point-of-view discipline.
**In use:** "We head-hopped into the antagonist's interiority for one line in the middle of Mara's scene — cut it or make it an intentional POV break with a scene transition."
**Common misuse:** Treated as always an error; some published multi-POV work does this deliberately with clear scene/section breaks — the flag is for unintentional, unmarked shifts.

### Deep POV
A narrative technique that filters all description and language through a single character's perception without authorial distance or filter phrases ("she felt," "she noticed").
**In use:** "Cut 'she felt a wave of anger' — in deep POV we're already inside her head, so just show the anger."
**Common misuse:** Applied inconsistently within a manuscript, creating a tonal mismatch between deep-POV scenes and more distanced narration elsewhere.

### Show, don't tell
The craft principle of conveying emotion or information through action, sensory detail, or dialogue rather than direct statement.
**In use:** "'She was nervous' is telling; 'her hands wouldn't stop moving' is showing the same fact."
**Common misuse:** Applied as an absolute rule rather than a default — sometimes telling is the right pacing choice (compressing time, summarizing a minor beat), and over-applying "show" bloats word count.

### Killing your darlings
The practice of cutting a passage the author loves for craft reasons, even when it's well-written, because it doesn't serve the throughline.
**In use:** "The Teo subplot is genuinely well-written — it's still a darling that needs to go, because it doesn't connect to the heist arc."
**Common misuse:** Used to justify cutting anything unpopular with the editor personally, rather than specifically material that's well-crafted but structurally unearned.

### Comp title
A published, comparable book used to benchmark a manuscript's genre conventions, length, tone, or market positioning.
**In use:** "Your comp titles run 90-110k words — at 128k you're 20-40% over the norm for this category, which is a real acquisition risk, not just a stylistic note."
**Common misuse:** Chosen for prestige (aspirational bestsellers) rather than genuine comparability in genre, scope, and market tier, which produces useless benchmarks.

### Revision letter
The written document accompanying a developmental edit, laying out prioritized structural feedback.
**In use:** "The revision letter opens with what's working before it gets into the three structural issues — that's not padding, it's what makes the author receptive to the harder notes."
**Common misuse:** Written as an exhaustive line-by-line list rather than a prioritized, scoped document — overwhelms rather than guides.

### Track changes / redline
Markup showing every proposed edit against the original text, allowing the author to accept, reject, or respond to each change individually.
**In use:** "Structural cuts go in as tracked deletions with a query comment explaining the reasoning, never a silent delete."
**Common misuse:** Used for structural-level changes without an accompanying explanation, which removes the author's ability to make an informed decision about their own work.

### House style
The specific style-guide rulings (which manual, plus any exceptions) a publication or press has already adopted, overriding general style-guide defaults.
**In use:** "House style here breaks from Chicago on the em dash — check the style guide doc before flagging that as an error."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to be identical to whatever general style guide (Chicago, AP) is named, when house style is often a modified subset with specific overrides.

### Stet
An editorial mark/instruction meaning "let it stand" — reverting a proposed change and keeping the original text.
**In use:** "Author stetted the passive-voice note in chapter 3 — it's an intentional stylistic choice for that character's distanced narration, not an error."
**Common misuse:** Treated as the author simply refusing feedback, rather than a legitimate editorial tool for the author to exercise final authority over their own text.
