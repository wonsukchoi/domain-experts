# Dragline and Excavating/Loading Machine Operator — Vocabulary

### Bank cubic yard (bcy)
A cubic yard of material measured in its natural, undisturbed (in-place) state before excavation — the unit pit designs, reserve calculations, and stripping ratios are expressed in.
**In use:** "We moved 13,790 bank cubic yards this shift, not 16,000 — that's after converting the bucket's loose rating down."
**Common misuse:** Reporting or comparing production using a bucket's rated loose/heaped capacity directly as if it were bank yardage, overstating actual material moved.

### Loose (heaped) cubic yard
A cubic yard of material after excavation, once it has broken up and expanded from its bank state — the unit bucket capacities are rated in.
**In use:** "The bucket's rated at 60 loose cy — that's the heaped capacity, not what it converts to in the ground."
**Common misuse:** Treating the bucket's rated cubic yardage as directly comparable to bank-yardage pit-plan targets without applying the swell factor.

### Swell factor (load factor)
The ratio between a material's loose (excavated) volume and its bank (in-place) volume; common earth swells to roughly 1.25x its bank volume once excavated, meaning its load factor (the inverse) is about 0.80.
**In use:** "Swell factor's 1.25 for this overburden — divide loose yards by that to get bank yards moved."
**Common misuse:** Using a single swell factor across materially different materials (blasted rock vs. common earth vs. clay) without checking the site's specific reference table.

### Fill factor (bucket fill percentage)
The share of a bucket's rated capacity actually captured per pass, driven by material condition — virgin bank material fills more completely than rehandled or blocky cast spoil.
**In use:** "Fill factor's running 0.85 on this face — that's virgin bank behavior, not the rehandle number we'd expect if this were cast spoil."
**Common misuse:** Applying a virgin-bank fill factor to rehandled or blocky material, overestimating expected production and masking a fragmentation problem.

### Swing angle
The angular distance the bucket travels between the dig face and the dump or spoil point during a cycle; cycle time is minimized near a 90° arc and lengthens as the actual angle departs from it.
**In use:** "We're at 120° now, not the plan's 90° — that's adding real time to every cycle, not just a little."
**Common misuse:** Treating bucket size as the primary lever on production while overlooking that a degraded swing angle from face advance is quietly costing more throughput than any bucket-size difference would.

### Stripping ratio
The ratio of overburden volume (bank cubic yards) that must be removed to expose one unit of the underlying coal or ore, used to gauge a pit or panel's economics.
**In use:** "This panel's running an 8:1 stripping ratio — eight bank yards of overburden per yard of recoverable coal."
**Common misuse:** Quoting a stripping ratio without specifying whether it's bank-to-bank or includes a loose/bank conversion somewhere in the chain, making two quoted ratios not actually comparable.

### Cast blasting
A blast design intended to throw (cast) a significant share of overburden volume directly into the spoil area during the blast itself, reducing the volume the excavating machine has to rehandle afterward.
**In use:** "This pattern's designed for 25% cast — if it underperforms, the dragline inherits more rehandle volume than the plan assumed."
**Common misuse:** Treating blast performance as separate from digging economics, when a blast that casts less than designed directly increases the excavating machine's workload and changes its expected production.

### Rehandle
Material that has already been excavated or cast once (e.g., by a prior blast or a prior digging pass) and must be moved again — distinct from virgin bank material being excavated for the first time.
**In use:** "That's rehandle from last week's cast, not virgin bank — expect a lower fill factor on it."
**Common misuse:** Logging rehandled material's production against virgin-bank fill-factor expectations, producing a systematic overestimate.

### Key cut (box cut)
The initial cut opening a new pit or panel, dug without a prior spoil area available to place overburden into, typically requiring haul-away rather than direct casting until enough space opens up for in-pit spoil placement.
**In use:** "We're still in the key cut on this panel — no spoil room yet, everything's hauling out until the box opens up."
**Common misuse:** Applying standard in-pit spoil-placement swing-angle assumptions to a key-cut phase, when the geometry and volumes are structurally different until the cut advances.

### Ground bearing pressure
The pressure a machine's tub, pontoon, or crawler exerts on the ground beneath it, which must stay under the confirmed bearing capacity of that specific surface for the machine to remain stable while stationary or moving.
**In use:** "Tub pressure's spec'd at the OEM figure for this machine — before we walk onto that new spoil, get engineering to confirm it actually bears that."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a machine's structural rating for its own weight and load is the same question as whether the ground under it can bear that pressure — they're independent checks.

### Berm
A constructed bank or barrier at a dump point or roadway edge sized to stop a vehicle from traveling over the edge; regulatory minimum height is tied to the mid-axle height of the largest unit using the point.
**In use:** "Berm's measuring below mid-axle on the 320-ton trucks now using this point — that's a rebuild before we dump again, not a slow-down."
**Common misuse:** Treating a berm built to an earlier, smaller truck fleet's spec as still adequate once larger units are dumping at the same point.

### Highwall
The vertical or near-vertical excavated rock/soil face left standing on the working side of a surface mine cut, distinct from the spoil side where excavated material is placed.
**In use:** "Highwall's showing a fresh spall near the toe — that gets logged and assessed before we dig any closer to it."
**Common misuse:** Treating highwall stability as purely a geotechnical/engineering concern with no operator role, when the required periodic examination and the first observation of new cracking or movement typically falls to whoever is working the face.

### Digging depth / dump height
The vertical range, from the machine's current position, over which it can dig and then dump material within rated performance — a rated spec, not a variable the operator can extend by attempting a longer reach.
**In use:** "That cut's deeper than our rated digging depth from here — we're benching it down in a lift, not reaching for it in one pass."
**Common misuse:** Attempting a single overreach pass to avoid a benching step, risking a short dump or an overloaded boom instead of taking the extra time to bench the cut.
