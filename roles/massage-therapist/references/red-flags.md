# Massage Therapist — Red Flags

### Unilateral leg swelling with warmth and tenderness, no known injury
- **Usually means:** Possible deep-vein thrombosis (DVT); second most likely is a muscle strain, but the asymmetry (one leg, not both) and absence of an injury story point away from a simple strain.
- **First question:** "Has this come on gradually over the past few days, and is it only on this side?"
- **Data to pull:** Compare both legs side by side for swelling/color/temperature difference; check intake form for any anticoagulant medication or recent long travel/immobility (both raise DVT risk).

### Client reports new, unexplained bruising beyond their normal pattern
- **Usually means:** A blood-thinning medication started or dose-changed since the last visit, or a platelet/clotting issue; ask directly rather than assuming increased fragility from age or prior massage.
- **First question:** "Has anything changed with your medications since your last visit?"
- **Data to pull:** Updated medication list; compare against the last intake form on file.

### Fever, chills, or feeling generally unwell reported at check-in
- **Usually means:** Active systemic illness — a contraindication for the whole session, not just a "go easier" situation, because circulatory work can spread a pathogen and increase symptom severity.
- **First question:** "How are you feeling overall today, not just the area you wanted worked on?"
- **Data to pull:** None needed beyond the verbal report — this is a stop-and-reschedule finding regardless of what else is going on.

### Visible skin condition at a requested work site (rash, open wound, active infection)
- **Usually means:** A local contraindication — work around the area, not necessarily the whole session, unless it looks like it could be systemic (widespread rash, signs of spreading infection).
- **First question:** "How long has this been there, and has a doctor looked at it?"
- **Data to pull:** Visual assessment of whether it's localized or spreading; check whether it's contagious (relevant both for the client's healing and the practitioner's exposure).

### Pregnant client past approximately 20 weeks requesting a supine (face-up) session
- **Usually means:** Risk of supine hypotensive syndrome — the weight of the uterus can compress the vena cava when lying flat on the back for an extended period, reducing blood return to the heart.
- **First question:** "Have you been comfortable lying on your back for extended periods recently, or has anyone advised against it?"
- **Data to pull:** How far along the pregnancy is (trimester, not just "pregnant"); any OB-provided restrictions already given to the client.

### Cancer diagnosis noted, especially active treatment
- **Usually means:** Not an automatic refusal — but requires site-specific modification (avoid direct pressure over tumor site or treatment port, lighter pressure near affected-side lymph nodes).
- **First question:** "Is there anything your care team has told you to avoid, or any area that's currently sensitive or being treated?"
- **Data to pull:** Location of any treatment port or recent surgical site; which side (if any) had lymph nodes removed or irradiated, since that side needs modified lymphatic-drainage-direction handling.

### Client requests pressure well beyond what tissue palpation is showing it can tolerate
- **Usually means:** The client is optimizing for "feels productive" over what's actually safe for the tissue — guarding, held breath, or flinching are the tissue's own signal, and it outranks the verbal request.
- **First question:** (Said to the client, not asked internally) "I can go deeper, but I want to check in — I'm feeling some resistance there, does that match what you're feeling?"
- **Data to pull:** None — this is a felt/palpated signal in the moment, not a data lookup.

### Numbness, tingling, or reported "I can't really feel that" during a technique
- **Usually means:** Either normal light-touch desensitization from sustained pressure, or a nerve-compression issue that needs a pressure/positioning change immediately — the practitioner can't tell which from the report alone.
- **First question:** "Is that a dull, sleepy-numb feeling, or sharp tingling like a nerve being pinched?"
- **Data to pull:** Whether the sensation resolves immediately when pressure/position changes (favors normal desensitization) or persists (favors a positioning problem needing correction, not just noting).

### Client with diagnosed peripheral neuropathy or on medication known to reduce sensation
- **Usually means:** The client's own pain/pressure feedback — the mechanism the pressure-tolerance heuristic relies on — is not reliable for this session.
- **First question:** "Since you may not feel pressure the way you normally would, I'm going to check in more often than usual — is that okay?"
- **Data to pull:** Which areas are affected by the reduced sensation, since it may not be uniform across the body.

### New client with no prior massage history requesting maximum deep-tissue pressure immediately
- **Usually means:** Unknown tissue tolerance and unknown post-session soreness response — going maximally deep on session one risks a client who's too sore to want to return, independent of whether anything went wrong medically.
- **First question:** "Have you had deep-tissue work before, and how did your body respond afterward?"
- **Data to pull:** None beyond the verbal history — this is a session-planning judgment call, not a medical screen.
