# Massage Therapist — Vocabulary

### Local contraindication
A condition that rules out working one specific area but not the rest of the session — a bruise, a healing scar, a local skin infection.
**In use:** "That's a local contraindication on the left forearm — I'll work everything else as planned and just skip that area."
**Common misuse:** Treating any visible mark as automatically requiring the whole session to stop, when most local findings only rule out that one area.

### Systemic contraindication
A condition that rules out the entire session, not just an affected area, because circulatory techniques move fluid and pathogens body-wide.
**In use:** "Fever's a systemic contraindication today — let's reschedule rather than modify around it."
**Common misuse:** Confusing this with a local contraindication and trying to "work around" a fever or active infectious illness by just avoiding one body part.

### Deep vein thrombosis (DVT)
A blood clot in a deep vein, most often the leg, that can become dangerous if dislodged into circulation — the reason unilateral leg swelling/warmth/tenderness is a stop-work finding rather than a "go lighter" finding.
**In use:** "Unilateral calf swelling with warmth and no injury history — that's the classic DVT-risk pattern, no leg work today."
**Common misuse:** Assuming any leg soreness is muscular and working around it with reduced pressure, rather than recognizing the specific unilateral-swelling-plus-warmth pattern as a distinct, higher-stakes finding.

### Guarding
An involuntary tissue or muscular response to pressure that exceeds what the body is ready to release into — visible as tensing, held breath, or flinching.
**In use:** "I backed off there because I felt guarding, not release — pushing through it wouldn't have helped."
**Common misuse:** Reading guarding as "the client just needs to relax more" and continuing at the same pressure, when it's actually the tissue signaling the pressure ceiling has been reached.

### Supine hypotensive syndrome
A drop in blood pressure that can occur when a pregnant person (typically past roughly 20 weeks) lies flat on their back for an extended period, from uterine weight compressing the vena cava.
**In use:** "She's 28 weeks along, so we're doing this session side-lying, not supine, to avoid supine hypotensive syndrome."
**Common misuse:** Applying this precaution uniformly to any pregnant client regardless of trimester, when the risk is specifically a later-pregnancy consideration.

### Myofascial release
A technique targeting the fascia (connective tissue surrounding muscles) using sustained, often lighter pressure to address restriction patterns, distinct from deep-tissue work on the muscle belly itself.
**In use:** "Her limited shoulder rotation looks fascial, not muscular — I'll start with myofascial release rather than deep tissue."
**Common misuse:** Using the term interchangeably with "deep tissue" when they target different structures and use different pressure/pacing.

### Trigger point
A specific, palpable point in a muscle that reproduces a characteristic referred-pain pattern when pressed.
**In use:** "Pressing that point in her rhomboid reproduced the exact pain down her arm she described — that's a genuine trigger point, not just a tender spot."
**Common misuse:** Calling any tender or sore spot a "trigger point" when the defining feature is the reproducible referral pattern, not just tenderness on palpation.

### Oncology massage
Massage practice modified for clients in active cancer treatment or with a cancer history, using site-specific precautions (avoiding direct pressure over a tumor or treatment port, adjusting pressure near affected lymph nodes) rather than a blanket refusal to treat.
**In use:** "He's in active chemo with a port on the right side — this is an oncology-massage modification, not a full refusal."
**Common misuse:** Treating any cancer history as an automatic disqualifier for massage, rather than a set of specific modifications current practice supports.

### Lymphatic drainage
A light-pressure technique intended to encourage lymph fluid movement, requiring particular care around lymph nodes that have been surgically removed or irradiated (commonly relevant in post-cancer-treatment clients).
**In use:** "She's had lymph nodes removed on the left, so I'm avoiding lymphatic-drainage technique on that side and working the right instead."
**Common misuse:** Assuming lymphatic drainage is always safe and beneficial regardless of a client's surgical/treatment history, when it can be contraindicated on an affected side.

### Scope of practice
The set of techniques and judgments a massage therapist is legally and professionally permitted to perform, which stops short of diagnosing a medical condition.
**In use:** "I can tell you what I'm feeling in the tissue, but diagnosing what's causing it is outside my scope of practice — that's for your doctor."
**Common misuse:** A practitioner offering a diagnostic-sounding opinion ("that's definitely just a pulled muscle") instead of describing findings and referring out when something is medically ambiguous.

### Pressure-tolerance ceiling
The point at which client-requested pressure exceeds what the tissue's palpated response indicates is safe to continue, established in-session rather than fixed in advance.
**In use:** "We hit her pressure-tolerance ceiling on the lower back about ten minutes in — I held there instead of pushing deeper."
**Common misuse:** Treating the client's initial verbal pressure request as fixed for the whole session, rather than as a starting point to be checked against tissue response throughout.
