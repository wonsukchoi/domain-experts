# Vocabulary

### Curriculum-Based Measurement (CBM)
A brief, standardized probe (typically 1-3 minutes) administered repeatedly over time to track a student's growth in a specific academic skill, such as oral reading fluency or math computation.
**In use:** "We've got ten weekly CBM points now — enough to calculate a real rate of improvement instead of guessing."
**Common misuse:** Treating a single CBM score as a diagnostic result rather than one data point in a series — CBM's value is in the trend across repeated administrations, not any one number.

### Oral Reading Fluency (ORF)
A CBM measure of words read correctly per minute from grade-level text, used both as a universal screener and as a progress-monitoring metric during intervention.
**In use:** "She's at 54 wcpm now, up from 45 at baseline — that's the number driving the intensify-versus-refer decision."
**Common misuse:** Comparing an ORF score only to the grade-level benchmark without also tracking its rate of change — level alone can't show whether an intervention is working.

### Rate of Improvement (ROI) / slope
The rate at which a student's progress-monitoring score changes per week, calculated from a series of data points, and compared against the rate required to close a gap to benchmark by a target date.
**In use:** "Her ROI is one word per week; she needs closer to two to hit the spring benchmark — that gap is why we're intensifying, not just continuing."
**Common misuse:** Reporting only the most recent score as evidence of progress instead of calculating the slope across the full data series — a single improved data point doesn't establish an adequate rate.

### Dual discrepancy
An SLD identification model requiring both a level discrepancy (student performs meaningfully below grade-level peers) and a rate discrepancy (student's rate of improvement is inadequate to close that gap), rather than either alone.
**In use:** "She's got the level gap, but her rate is holding steady — that's not dual discrepancy yet, so we're not there on the eligibility question."
**Common misuse:** Treating a low level score alone as sufficient for an SLD case — a student can be behind but closing the gap at an adequate rate, which is a response to instruction, not a disability indicator.

### Response to Intervention (RTI) / MTSS tiers
A multi-tiered system of increasingly intensive, data-monitored instruction (commonly Tier 1 core instruction, Tier 2 targeted small-group, Tier 3 intensive individualized) used both to support struggling students and to gather data for SLD eligibility decisions.
**In use:** "She didn't respond adequately to Tier 2, so we're moving to Tier 3 before opening an evaluation."
**Common misuse:** Treating tier movement as an automatic, time-based escalation rather than a data-driven decision — a student should move tiers because the data shows inadequate response with verified fidelity, not because a set number of weeks passed.

### Pattern of Strengths and Weaknesses (PSW)
An alternative or supplementary SLD identification model comparing a student's cognitive processing strengths against weaknesses and academic achievement, used alongside or instead of dual discrepancy depending on state/district policy.
**In use:** "Our state's eligibility criteria want a PSW writeup alongside the RTI data, not instead of it."
**Common misuse:** Assuming PSW and dual-discrepancy/RTI models are interchangeable or that one alone satisfies every jurisdiction's eligibility criteria — the required model is state- and district-specific, and using the wrong one can invalidate the eligibility decision.

### Treatment fidelity
Documented evidence that an intervention was delivered as designed — correct duration, frequency, group size, and materials — used to rule out poor implementation as the explanation for a flat progress-monitoring trend.
**In use:** "Before we call her a non-responder, let's check fidelity — the sessions may not have run at the prescribed dosage."
**Common misuse:** Concluding a student "didn't respond" to an intervention without first checking whether it was actually delivered as designed — an under-dosed or inconsistently delivered intervention proves nothing about the student.

### Least Restrictive Environment (LRE)
The IDEA requirement that a student with a disability be educated with non-disabled peers to the maximum extent appropriate, which directly constrains how much pull-out time a service-minutes schedule can specify.
**In use:** "We can't default to pulling her out for every reading block — LRE means we start from inclusion and add pull-out minutes only where the data supports it."
**Common misuse:** Treating LRE as a one-time compliance checkbox at IEP creation rather than an ongoing constraint that should be reassessed as pull-out minutes are proposed or changed.

### Specific Learning Disability (SLD)
A formal, IDEA-recognized disability category for a disorder in one or more basic psychological processes involved in understanding or using language, manifesting as a significant gap between ability and achievement in a specific academic area not primarily caused by other factors (vision, hearing, motor, intellectual disability, environmental disadvantage).
**In use:** "Before we write SLD on this referral, we need to have ruled out the exposure and attendance factors, not just documented the gap."
**Common misuse:** Using "SLD" as a catch-all label for any student who is behind grade level, without having ruled out instructional-gap, exposure, or attendance explanations first — SLD determination by definition excludes gaps primarily caused by those other factors.

### Pull-out / push-in instruction
Pull-out delivers specialized instruction in a separate setting outside the general-education classroom; push-in (or co-teaching) delivers it within the general-education classroom alongside peers. The choice and dosage of each is a scheduling decision driven by deficit severity and progress data.
**In use:** "She's at 30 minutes of pull-out for reading right now — we'll revisit that dosage at the next progress review, not before."
**Common misuse:** Treating pull-out and push-in as a permanent, one-time placement decision rather than a dosage that should shift as progress-monitoring data accumulates.
