# Vocabulary

Terms of art generalists reach for but frequently misuse. Each entry: definition, a sentence a practitioner would actually say, and the common mistake.

### MAC (minimum alveolar concentration)

The alveolar concentration of a volatile agent at which 50% of patients don't move to a surgical incision — a population statistic, not a patient-specific depth measurement.

**In use:** "She's 75, dose to age-adjusted MAC — table value overshoots her by a meaningful margin."

**Common misuse:** Treating MAC as a real-time "depth of anesthesia" readout for an individual patient, or forgetting it must be age-adjusted (falls ~6%/decade past 40) rather than read off the textbook value.

### BIS (bispectral index)

A processed-EEG number intended to correlate with hypnotic depth, marketed as an awareness-prevention tool.

**In use:** "BIS reads 45, but she's still moving to stimulus — check the analgesia, not just the hypnotic."

**Common misuse:** Treating a BIS number in range as proof awareness is impossible; it correlates with hypnotic level, not analgesia or the other three anesthetic axes, and has known failure modes (electrocautery artifact, certain drug combinations).

### ASA Physical Status classification

A five-tier (plus VI) system correlating comorbidity burden with perioperative mortality risk.

**In use:** "He's an ASA III — the class doesn't tell us the technique, but it tells us we staff a backup provider before induction."

**Common misuse:** Using ASA-PS as a stand-alone surgical risk calculator or billing modifier rather than one input alongside airway exam, OSA screen, and cardiac risk index.

### TIVA (total intravenous anesthesia)

An anesthetic technique using only intravenous agents (typically propofol ± an opioid infusion), no inhaled volatile agent.

**In use:** "High Apfel score and MH-susceptible family history — she's going TIVA."

**Common misuse:** Assuming TIVA is inherently "safer" in all patients rather than a technique chosen for specific indications (PONV risk, MH susceptibility, neuromonitoring) with its own tradeoffs (awareness risk if infusion is interrupted, pump/line dependency).

### TCI (target-controlled infusion)

A pump-driven method of dosing IV anesthetics to a target plasma or effect-site concentration using a pharmacokinetic model (e.g., Marsh, Schnider), rather than a fixed mL/hr rate.

**In use:** "Set the TCI to a 3 mcg/mL effect-site target and titrate from there, not a flat rate."

**Common misuse:** Treating the model's predicted concentration as measured rather than estimated — different models can predict meaningfully different concentrations for the same patient.

### Supraglottic airway (SGA) / LMA

A device sitting above the vocal cords providing a seal for ventilation without tracheal intubation.

**In use:** "SGA is fine for this case — short, supine, low aspiration risk — we don't need a tube."

**Common misuse:** Treating an SGA as equivalent airway protection to an endotracheal tube; it does not reliably protect against aspiration and is the wrong choice for full-stomach or prone/high-aspiration-risk cases.

### Awareness under anesthesia

A rare event where a patient has explicit recall of intraoperative events, usually reflecting inadequate hypnosis (not just inadequate analgesia).

**In use:** "She reported hearing voices in the OR post-op — full awareness workup, don't dismiss it as a dream."

**Common misuse:** Assuming a "normal" BIS or MAC value during the case rules out awareness after the fact — both are correlates, and every reported case gets a real workup regardless of the intraoperative numbers.

### Malignant hyperthermia (MH)

A pharmacogenetic hypermetabolic crisis triggered by volatile anesthetics or succinylcholine in genetically susceptible patients.

**In use:** "Rising ETCO2 and masseter rigidity — treat as MH now, don't wait on the CK."

**Common misuse:** Waiting for lab confirmation (CK, genetic testing) before starting dantrolene — the clinical trigger, not the lab result, starts treatment.

### LAST (local anesthetic systemic toxicity)

Systemic toxicity from local anesthetic reaching the circulation in excess, causing CNS signs (perioral numbness, seizure) progressing to cardiovascular collapse.

**In use:** "She has tinnitus after the block — stop injecting, lipid emulsion to bedside now."

**Common misuse:** Treating LAST as an all-or-nothing event that either happens or doesn't at injection — signs can appear minutes after an apparently uneventful injection as the drug redistributes.

### RSI (rapid sequence induction)

An induction technique minimizing the time between loss of airway reflexes and securing the airway, for patients at elevated aspiration risk.

**In use:** "Full stomach, RSI with cricoid pressure per our protocol, no mask ventilation between induction and intubation."

**Common misuse:** Applying a rigid textbook RSI (including cricoid pressure) uniformly regardless of updated evidence and patient-specific factors — modern practice individualizes cricoid use and allows gentle mask ventilation if desaturation risk is high.

### OIVI (opioid-induced ventilatory impairment)

Respiratory depression from opioid administration, often occurring in the postoperative period with delayed onset as sedation from other agents wears off.

**In use:** "STOP-Bang 4 plus this opioid load — she needs continuous pulse oximetry overnight, not intermittent checks."

**Common misuse:** Relying on a sedation score alone to catch OIVI — patients can be arousable and still hypoventilating; continuous monitoring, not periodic checks, is the actual safeguard in high-risk patients.

### STOP-Bang

An eight-item screening questionnaire for undiagnosed obstructive sleep apnea, scored 0-8.

**In use:** "STOP-Bang is 5 — treat her perioperative plan like she has moderate-to-severe OSA even without a sleep study."

**Common misuse:** Treating a low STOP-Bang score as ruling out OSA entirely — it's a screening tool optimized for sensitivity, not a diagnostic test, and score interpretation depends on which items are positive.

### Apfel score

A four-factor simplified score predicting postoperative nausea and vomiting risk (female sex, non-smoker, prior PONV/motion sickness, planned postop opioids).

**In use:** "Apfel is 3 — she's getting two antiemetic classes and TIVA, not just a single dose of ondansetron."

**Common misuse:** Treating a single antiemetic as adequate prophylaxis regardless of score — risk compounds with each factor and prophylaxis intensity should scale with it.

### ERAS (Enhanced Recovery After Surgery)

A structured, evidence-based perioperative care bundle (multimodal analgesia, early mobilization, minimized fasting, goal-directed fluids) spanning pre-, intra-, and postoperative phases.

**In use:** "This is an ERAS colorectal pathway — no routine NG tube, goal-directed fluids, multimodal analgesia from the start."

**Common misuse:** Using "ERAS" as a synonym for "multimodal analgesia" alone — it's a full care-pathway bundle across the whole perioperative period, and cherry-picking one element without the rest loses most of the outcome benefit.

### Denitrogenation / preoxygenation

Replacing nitrogen in the functional residual capacity with oxygen before induction, to extend the safe apnea window during intubation attempts.

**In use:** "Give her a full three minutes of tidal-volume breathing at high FiO2 before we push anything — she's the patient we can least afford to desaturate fast."

**Common misuse:** Treating preoxygenation as a fixed-duration ritual regardless of patient factors — obesity, pregnancy, and lung disease shrink the achievable apnea window even with adequate preoxygenation technique, and that changes the induction plan, not just the timing.
