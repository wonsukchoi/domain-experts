# Red flags

Intraoperative and PACU smell tests a senior anesthesiologist catches before they become a crisis. Format: what it usually means, the first question to ask, and the data or trend to pull.

## 1. Rising ETCO2 with tachycardia and masseter rigidity after succinylcholine

**Usually means:** malignant hyperthermia — masseter spasm alone is nonspecific but combined with rising ETCO2 and tachycardia is the classic early triad; second possibility is inadequate anesthetic depth (but that doesn't explain the ETCO2 climb).

**First question:** "What's the ETCO2 trend over the last 10 minutes, not the current single reading?"

**Data to pull:** ETCO2 trend, core temperature, arterial blood gas, CK — and activate the MH protocol on clinical suspicion rather than waiting for CK/gas confirmation.

## 2. SpO2 desaturation despite a "normal" capnography waveform

**Usually means:** ventilation is adequate but oxygenation is failing independently — V/Q mismatch, shunt, pulmonary edema, or a pulse oximeter artifact (poor perfusion, motion, nail polish) rather than an airway problem.

**First question:** "Is the capnography waveform actually a normal square shape at a normal rate, or just present?"

**Data to pull:** FiO2 delivered vs. set, auscultation for bilateral breath sounds, peak inspiratory pressure trend, arterial line waveform if present to rule out a plethysmography artifact.

## 3. Sudden hypotension, bronchospasm, and flushing/rash after an antibiotic or latex exposure

**Usually means:** anaphylaxis — the classic triad, though any single element can be masked under anesthesia (rash may be the only sign in a draped patient).

**First question:** "What was given or touched the patient in the last 5-10 minutes — antibiotic, neuromuscular blocker, latex, chlorhexidine?"

**Data to pull:** timeline of all drugs/exposures against the vital-sign drop; tryptase level drawn within 1-2 hours of onset (not immediately) for later confirmation.

## 4. Perioral numbness, metallic taste, or tinnitus during or just after a regional block injection

**Usually means:** early local anesthetic systemic toxicity (LAST) — CNS signs typically precede cardiovascular collapse and are the window to intervene before it does.

**First question:** "Stop injecting — is the lipid emulsion in the room right now?"

**Data to pull:** total local anesthetic dose given vs. weight-based maximum, time since last injection, cardiac rhythm strip; start lipid emulsion on clinical suspicion, don't wait for arrest.

## 5. Rising peak inspiratory pressure with falling tidal volume in a paralyzed, mechanically ventilated patient

**Usually means:** mainstem intubation (tube migrated with position change), pneumothorax, bronchospasm, or a kinked/obstructed circuit — in that rough order of frequency after a position change.

**First question:** "Did anything change position — the tube, the patient, or the table — in the last few minutes?"

**Data to pull:** tube depth at the teeth vs. documented depth at intubation, bilateral breath sounds, ETCO2 waveform shape (upsloping suggests bronchospasm), circuit inspection.

## 6. Failed or patchy neuraxial block with a sensory level rising quickly

**Usually means:** unintentional subdural or subarachnoid spread of an epidural dose (total spinal) rather than simple block failure — the danger is rapid ascent to respiratory and hemodynamic compromise.

**First question:** "What's the sensory level right now, and how fast did it get there?"

**Data to pull:** time course of sensory level, blood pressure and respiratory rate trend, readiness to secure the airway and support hemodynamics if the level keeps ascending.

## 7. Unexplained bradycardia during laparoscopic insufflation

**Usually means:** vagal reflex from peritoneal/mesenteric stretch, not primary cardiac pathology — usually resolves with desufflation or slowing insufflation rate, but can progress to asystole if ignored.

**First question:** "Ask the surgeon to pause insufflation right now — does the heart rate recover?"

**Data to pull:** insufflation pressure and rate of change, heart rate trend correlated to the insufflation timeline, atropine availability at bedside.

## 8. STOP-Bang ≥5 scheduled for high-dose opioid general anesthesia as an outpatient

**Usually means:** undiagnosed severe OSA about to be sent home on an analgesic plan that risks postoperative respiratory depression without overnight monitoring.

**First question:** "Does this patient have a formal sleep study, or is STOP-Bang the only screen we have?"

**Data to pull:** STOP-Bang component breakdown, planned discharge opioid dose, whether the facility can admit for observation if needed — reconsider outpatient disposition before, not after, the case.

## 9. Delayed emergence beyond 30-60 minutes after discontinuing all anesthetic agents

**Usually means:** residual neuromuscular blockade, hypothermia, or a metabolic/electrolyte cause more often than a primary neurologic event — but the differential must be worked systematically, not assumed benign.

**First question:** "What's the train-of-four ratio and core temperature right now?"

**Data to pull:** TOF ratio, core temperature, blood glucose, recent opioid/benzodiazepine total doses, and a rapid neuro exam before assuming "just needs more time."

## 10. Anticoagulation labs more than 24 hours old before a planned neuraxial block on a patient with a new antithrombotic order

**Usually means:** the block-timing decision is being made on stale information — a dose given or held in the interim invalidates the prior hold-time calculation.

**First question:** "Has any anticoagulant been given or held since that lab was drawn?"

**Data to pull:** current medication administration record cross-checked against the ASRA hold window for the specific agent and dose, not just the most recent coagulation panel.
