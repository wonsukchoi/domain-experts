# Operations management working vocabulary

Terms an operations manager uses precisely. Format: definition → how a practitioner says it → common misuse.

**Theory of constraints (bottleneck)** — The principle that a system's overall throughput is limited by its single most constraining step, and improving any other step doesn't increase total output — it just builds up inventory/backlog in front of the real constraint.
In use: "Fulfillment's overall capacity isn't the constraint here — their rush-processing sub-capacity is. That's the bottleneck to fix, not standard-order throughput."
Misuse: investing improvement effort broadly across a process without first identifying the actual bottleneck, producing visible activity that doesn't move overall throughput.

**Gemba (go and see)** — The lean-management practice of directly observing where work actually happens, rather than managing solely from dashboards, reports, or secondhand summaries.
In use: "The dashboard said fulfillment was on track — a gemba walk showed staff manually expediting orders sales had promised without checking capacity first."
Misuse: relying entirely on reported metrics and status updates without periodic direct observation, missing the gap between what's reported and what's actually happening.

**RACI (Responsible, Accountable, Consulted, Informed)** — A framework assigning explicit roles for a cross-functional initiative: who does the work (Responsible), who's ultimately answerable for the outcome (Accountable), who must be consulted before a decision, and who just needs to be kept informed.
In use: "This initiative stalled because no one was marked Accountable — three teams each thought someone else owned the next step."
Misuse: launching a cross-functional initiative without an explicit RACI, leaving ownership implicit and ambiguous, which is the most common reason such initiatives stall.

**Handoff (process interface)** — The specific point where work, information, or a commitment passes from one team or function to another, often the actual location of failure in an otherwise individually-competent process.
In use: "The problem isn't sales or fulfillment individually — it's the handoff where sales promises a delivery timeline without checking fulfillment's actual capacity."
Misuse: diagnosing a cross-functional problem by evaluating each team's individual performance in isolation, missing that the actual failure often lives specifically at the interface between them.

**Standard work** — A documented, agreed baseline process, used not to eliminate all deviation but to make deviations visible and discussable against a known reference point.
In use: "We have standard work for the rush-order check now — any deal that skips it is a visible deviation, not invisible normal practice."
Misuse: treating standard work as a rigid cage that prevents any adaptation, rather than a baseline that makes it possible to notice and discuss when and why a deviation happened.

**5 Whys** — A root-cause analysis technique of asking "why" repeatedly (typically five times) about a problem until reaching the underlying systemic cause, rather than stopping at the first, most visible explanation.
In use: "Five whys on the complaint spike led past 'fulfillment is slow' to the actual root cause: sales has no visibility into rush-capacity limits before promising a timeline."
Misuse: stopping the analysis at the first plausible-sounding cause (often the most visible team involved) rather than continuing to ask why until reaching the actual systemic root.

**Incentive-metric mismatch** — The predictable situation where a team's behavior that looks like "gaming" a metric is actually the rational response to how that metric is designed and rewarded.
In use: "The team isn't gaming the close-rate metric — closing smaller, easier deals is exactly what a metric that only counts deal count, not deal value, would incentivize."
Misuse: treating a team's response to a poorly designed metric as a discipline or character problem, rather than recognizing it as the predictable, rational result of the metric's actual design.

**Small-batch iteration** — Making frequent, small, reversible changes to a process rather than a large, infrequent overhaul, so each change's effect can be isolated and validated (or reversed) quickly.
In use: "We tested the rush-capacity check with one sales team for two weeks before rolling it out company-wide — small batch, quick validation."
Misuse: replacing an entire process at once based on an untested redesign, making it difficult to isolate what specifically worked or failed, and risking a large, hard-to-reverse failure.

**Ground truth** — The actual, current state of operational reality, as distinct from what's reported through status updates or dashboards, which can diverge from it as information passes through layers with incentives to look good.
In use: "The status report says 'on track' — ground truth from the floor shows three separate workarounds keeping it that way."
Misuse: treating a status report or dashboard reading as equivalent to ground truth without periodic direct verification, especially as organizational size increases the natural gap between the two.

**Process mapping (value stream mapping)** — Visually documenting the actual sequence of steps, handoffs, and dependencies in a process, as distinct from the documented or assumed process, to surface where real friction and delay occur.
In use: "The value stream map showed the deal actually passes through four undocumented approval touchpoints, not the two in the official process doc."
Misuse: relying on the documented process description as if it reflects actual practice, without mapping and verifying what's really happening step by step.
