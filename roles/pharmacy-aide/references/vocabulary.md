# Vocabulary

### Scope of practice
The set of tasks a role is legally permitted to perform, defined by the state board of pharmacy, not by training or demonstrated skill.
**In use:** "I know how to key that prescription, but our state's scope of practice only lets clerical staff do intake — I can't enter it."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a skill/experience ceiling ("I've done this so many times, I basically know how") rather than a specific legal line that doesn't move regardless of competence.

### Minimum necessary standard
The HIPAA requirement to limit use or disclosure of protected health information to the amount needed for the purpose at hand — not a zero-disclosure rule.
**In use:** "Turning the label away and spacing out the line satisfies minimum necessary — we don't need a partition for that."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to mean any visibility of PHI is a violation, or conversely assumed not to apply at all to a clerical, non-clinical role.

### Incidental disclosure
An unavoidable, limited byproduct of an otherwise permitted disclosure, not itself a HIPAA violation when reasonable safeguards are in place.
**In use:** "Someone glimpsing a name on the bag while we're spaced out and following our safeguards is an incidental disclosure, not a breach."
**Common misuse:** Invoked to excuse actual carelessness — bags left open, labels facing outward with no spacing — by labeling avoidable exposure as "incidental."

### Perpetual inventory
A continuously updated running log of controlled-substance stock, in versus out.
**In use:** "We keep a perpetual log on the Schedule II safe, but it doesn't replace the biennial physical count — we still have to do that separately."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to satisfy the DEA's biennial physical-inventory requirement on its own; it doesn't — a discrete point-in-time count is still required.

### Biennial inventory
The DEA-required physical count of all controlled-substance stock taken at least once every two years.
**In use:** "Our biennial inventory can land any date within two years of the last one — it doesn't have to hit the same calendar date."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to require a fixed recurring calendar date rather than any date within the two-year window.

### In-transit loss
A DEA-relevant category of controlled-substance loss specifically occurring during shipment between registrants, distinct from loss discovered after receipt.
**In use:** "If the seal was already broken when the driver handed it over, that's an in-transit loss question for the wholesaler, not our internal shrink."
**Common misuse:** Lumped in with all shrinkage or count discrepancies generally, losing the narrower, DEA-reportable distinction of loss that happened before receipt.

### CSOS (Controlled Substance Ordering System)
The DEA's electronic ordering system for Schedule I and II substances, replacing paper DEA Form 222 for enrolled registrants.
**In use:** "This delivery was ordered through CSOS, so the confirmation number should match the invoice."
**Common misuse:** Referred to generically as "the ordering system" without recognizing it specifically governs Schedule I/II electronic orders, not routine stock ordering.

### Cold chain
The unbroken temperature-controlled storage and transport chain required to keep vaccines and certain medications within their manufacturer-specified range.
**In use:** "The cold chain broke overnight — the logger shows the freezer went above range for forty minutes."
**Common misuse:** Assumed satisfied by "the fridge feels cold enough," rather than by continuous, logged, alarmed monitoring against a specific documented range.

### VFC (Vaccines for Children) program
A federally funded program supplying vaccines to eligible children, with its own storage, handling, and monitoring documentation requirements.
**In use:** "This lot is VFC-funded, so it needs the continuous data logger, not just twice-daily readings."
**Common misuse:** Treated as identical to general vaccine storage practice, when VFC enrollment carries additional documentation and continuous-monitoring requirements.

### Shrink
Inventory loss from any cause — theft, error, damage, or administrative miscount — measured against expected stock levels.
**In use:** "Shrink on this SKU is 2%, and about a third of that industry-wide is process error, not theft, so let's check the receiving log before assuming someone's stealing."
**Common misuse:** Used as a synonym for employee theft specifically, when a substantial share is administrative and receiving error.

### Diversion
The unauthorized redirection of a controlled substance from its legitimate medical supply chain, including by pharmacy staff.
**In use:** "Diversion doesn't always mean a break-in — internal diversion by staff is the harder pattern to catch."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to mean only external theft or robbery, overlooking that internal (employee) diversion is a distinct and common category.

### Digital data logger (DDL)
A device that continuously records temperature readings over time and stores a retrievable history, as opposed to a point-in-time thermometer reading.
**In use:** "Pull the DDL's 30-day history, not just today's two readings, before you sign off on the cold-chain log."
**Common misuse:** Treated as interchangeable with a min-max thermometer, when a DDL provides a continuous timestamped record and a min-max unit only shows the extremes since its last reset.

### NDC (National Drug Code)
The unique identifier assigned to a specific drug product, package size, and manufacturer.
**In use:** "Don't restock by drug name alone — check the NDC, the 90-count bottle and the 30-count bottle of the same drug have different codes."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to be one fixed code per drug regardless of package size or manufacturer, when it actually varies by both.

### Direct supervision
The specific proximity and availability a pharmacist must maintain over supervised staff, as defined by state board rule — not a universal standard of physical presence.
**In use:** "Check what direct supervision means in this state before assuming the pharmacist has to be standing right here."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to always mean the pharmacist must be physically on-site and immediately adjacent, when some states permit narrower or broader definitions, including limited telepharmacy arrangements.
