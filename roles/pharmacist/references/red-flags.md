# Pharmacist — Red Flags

### CrCl below 30 mL/min on a renally-cleared drug ordered at standard dose
- **Usually means:** the ordering clinician calculated dose by diagnosis/weight without checking current renal function, or renal function changed since the last dose adjustment.
- **First question:** when was the last serum creatinine drawn, and has the patient's fluid/kidney status changed since (dehydration, contrast dye, new nephrotoxic drug)?
- **Data to pull:** most recent SCr, calculated CrCl or eGFR, drug label's renal-dosing table.

### Two or more QT-prolonging agents on the active medication list
- **Usually means:** drugs were added by different prescribers who each checked their own order against the allergy list but not against each other's QT effect.
- **First question:** is there a baseline or recent ECG, and does the patient have other QT-prolongation risk factors (hypokalemia, hypomagnesemia, bradycardia)?
- **Data to pull:** most recent ECG/QTc, electrolyte panel, full active medication list cross-referenced against a QT-risk drug list.

### High-alert medication (insulin, anticoagulant, opioid, concentrated electrolyte) with any dose ambiguity
- **Usually means:** an order was written quickly (verbal order, handwritten, or copy-forward from a prior admission) without a fresh weight/renal-function check.
- **First question:** is the ambiguity resolvable from the chart, or does it require a direct prescriber callback before dispensing?
- **Data to pull:** current weight, current renal/hepatic labs, the specific order text as written (not as transcribed).

### Patient's home-medication list disagrees across two or more sources
- **Usually means:** the patient fills at multiple pharmacies, a medication was recently changed and not all records updated, or the patient is non-adherent to a listed medication without anyone documenting it.
- **First question:** which source is most recent, and has the patient (or caregiver) been asked directly which medications they're actually taking?
- **Data to pull:** admission note, 30-day fill history, direct patient/caregiver interview.

### Therapeutic substitution proposed with no validated conversion ratio for the specific drug pair
- **Usually means:** the formulary swap was requested for cost/availability reasons without a pharmacist review of whether the two drugs are actually interchangeable at any fixed ratio.
- **First question:** is there a published, drug-specific equianalgesic or equipotency table for this exact pair, or only a general drug-class assumption?
- **Data to pull:** manufacturer or peer-reviewed conversion table for the specific drugs, not a same-class approximation.

### A drug-interaction alert overridden without a documented reason
- **Usually means:** alert fatigue — the pharmacist or prescriber has learned to click through interaction warnings because most are not clinically significant at the actual doses involved.
- **First question:** was the specific mechanism and dose/timing of this pair actually reviewed, or was the alert dismissed on pattern-match to "usually not a big deal" alerts?
- **Data to pull:** the interaction's severity tier and mechanism, the actual doses and administration timing of both drugs.

### New prescription duplicates a therapeutic class already on the active list
- **Usually means:** a new prescriber wasn't aware of an existing prescription from a different provider, or a medication was intentionally added as combination therapy but not documented as such.
- **First question:** is this an intentional combination (e.g. two antihypertensives from different classes for resistant hypertension) or an unintentional duplicate?
- **Data to pull:** full active medication list by therapeutic class, prescriber notes on treatment rationale if available.

### Pediatric or geriatric weight-based dose that doesn't show the calculation
- **Usually means:** the dose was copied from a prior order or a standard adult dose was used without a weight-based recalculation for a patient outside the standard adult range.
- **First question:** what weight was used to calculate this dose, and is it current (not an admission weight from days ago for a patient with fluid shifts)?
- **Data to pull:** current weight, the mg/kg calculation as documented (or its absence), drug's pediatric/geriatric dosing table.

### Discharge medication list doesn't match what was actually being given on the last inpatient day
- **Usually means:** a medication was held or adjusted during the stay (e.g. for a procedure, an interaction, a lab abnormality) and the discharge order set reverted to the original home regimen instead of the current in-hospital regimen.
- **First question:** what was the patient actually receiving in the 24 hours before discharge, and does the discharge list match it or the admission list?
- **Data to pull:** medication administration record (MAR) for the final 24-48 hours, discharge order set as written.
