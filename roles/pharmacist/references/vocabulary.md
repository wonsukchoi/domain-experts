# Pharmacist — Vocabulary

### Therapeutic index
The ratio between a drug's toxic dose and its effective dose. A narrow-therapeutic-index drug (warfarin, digoxin, lithium) has a small margin between effective and toxic, so small dosing or interaction errors matter more.
**In use:** "Warfarin's narrow therapeutic index is why we don't tolerate the same interaction-checking laxity we'd apply to a wide-margin drug like acetaminophen."
**Common misuse:** Treating "therapeutic index" as a synonym for "how dangerous a drug is" generally, rather than specifically the margin between effective and toxic dose.

### Bioequivalence
Two drug products (often brand vs. generic, or two formulations) producing the same rate and extent of drug absorption, within a defined statistical range. Bioequivalent products are treated as interchangeable; therapeutically similar but not bioequivalent products are not.
**In use:** "The generic is bioequivalent to the brand, so the substitution doesn't need a new dose calculation — that's not true of the therapeutic substitution to a different drug in the same class."
**Common misuse:** Assuming "same drug class" means "bioequivalent" — bioequivalence is a specific regulatory finding about the same active ingredient, not a general similarity claim across different drugs.

### Creatinine clearance (CrCl) vs. eGFR
Both estimate kidney filtration function, but CrCl (Cockcroft-Gault) uses actual weight and is the basis for most drug-dosing tables in package inserts, while eGFR (CKD-EPI) is body-surface-area-normalized and used for CKD staging. They can diverge meaningfully in very over- or under-weight patients.
**In use:** "The chart shows an eGFR of 45, but this drug's renal dosing table is calibrated to CrCl — recalculate with Cockcroft-Gault before adjusting the dose."
**Common misuse:** Using eGFR directly against a drug label's CrCl-based dosing table as if the two numbers are interchangeable.

### High-alert medication
A drug category where an error, though not necessarily more frequent, carries a disproportionately severe consequence — insulin, anticoagulants, opioids, concentrated electrolytes, chemotherapy. ISMP maintains the reference list.
**In use:** "It's on the high-alert list, so it gets the independent double-check regardless of how routine the order looks."
**Common misuse:** Assuming "high-alert" means "high error rate" — it means high consequence per error, which is why the double-check applies even to routine-seeming orders.

### Medication reconciliation
The formal process of comparing a patient's actual home-medication regimen against what's ordered at a transition of care (admission, transfer, discharge), resolving every discrepancy rather than defaulting to the most recent list.
**In use:** "Reconciliation isn't done until every discrepancy between the admission note and the fill history has a documented resolution, not just a merged list."
**Common misuse:** Treating reconciliation as a data-merging exercise (combine both lists) rather than a discrepancy-resolution process (figure out which entries are right and why).

### Equianalgesic conversion
A table-based method for converting between opioids or routes at approximately equivalent pain-relief doses, used when switching a patient's opioid or route. Conversions carry wide uncertainty ranges due to incomplete cross-tolerance.
**In use:** "The equianalgesic table gives a range, not a point estimate — start at the low end and titrate, don't convert to the midpoint."
**Common misuse:** Treating the table's midpoint value as a precise, safe starting dose rather than a rough range requiring a downward safety adjustment for incomplete cross-tolerance.

### Pharmacist intervention
A documented instance where a pharmacist identified and acted on a medication-related problem — a dose correction, an interaction hold, a duplicate-therapy catch — distinct from routine dispensing verification that finds no issue.
**In use:** "That's not just a verification note, it's a pharmacist intervention — document the specific finding, the communication with the prescriber, and the resolution."
**Common misuse:** Under-documenting a verbal catch as if the conversation itself were sufficient record, leaving no chartable trail.

### Drug-interaction severity tier
A classification (commonly major/moderate/minor) assigned by clinical decision-support databases to a drug pair, based on the mechanism and the worst-case documented outcome — not a measure of how likely the interaction is to occur at the doses actually prescribed.
**In use:** "It's flagged major, but the mechanism only matters at doses above what's ordered here — that's why we review the mechanism, not just act on the tier."
**Common misuse:** Treating the severity tier alone as sufficient basis for an action decision, without reading the underlying mechanism and whether it's active at the actual doses/timing involved.

### Renal dose adjustment
A modification to a drug's standard dose or frequency (not necessarily both) to account for reduced clearance in a patient with impaired kidney function, specified per-drug in the label's renal-impairment dosing table.
**In use:** "The renal adjustment for this drug changes the frequency, not the per-dose amount — don't halve the mg when the label says extend the interval instead."
**Common misuse:** Assuming all renal adjustments work the same way (always reduce the dose) rather than checking whether the specific drug's adjustment is a dose reduction, frequency extension, or both.

### Formulary substitution
A pharmacy- or insurer-driven swap of a prescribed drug for a different (not generic-equivalent) drug in the same therapeutic class, for cost or availability reasons — distinct from a bioequivalent generic substitution.
**In use:** "This is a formulary substitution to a different molecule, not a generic swap — it needs its own dose conversion and monitoring plan, not a 1:1 mg substitution."
**Common misuse:** Conflating formulary substitution (different drug, needs a real conversion) with generic substitution (same drug, different manufacturer, no conversion needed).

### Duplicate therapy
Two or more active prescriptions from the same or overlapping therapeutic class, which may be an unintentional prescribing error or an intentional combination-therapy strategy.
**In use:** "Before flagging this as duplicate therapy, check whether it's an intentional combination for resistant hypertension — the prescriber's note should say so."
**Common misuse:** Automatically treating any same-class duplication as an error, without checking whether it's a deliberate combination-therapy regimen.
