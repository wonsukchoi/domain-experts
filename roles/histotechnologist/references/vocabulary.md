# Vocabulary — terms of art and their common misuse

### Fixation
Chemically stabilizing tissue proteins (typically in 10% neutral buffered formalin) to halt autolysis and preserve structure before processing.
**In use:** "Fixation start is Friday 3:10 PM per the clinic log, not Monday's accession time — that's what actually counts toward the 72-hour window."
**Common misuse:** treating the lab's receipt/accession timestamp as the fixation-start time, instead of tracing back to when the tissue actually entered formalin.

### Processing
The dehydration (graded alcohols), clearing (typically xylene), and paraffin-infiltration sequence that prepares fixed tissue for embedding and cutting.
**In use:** "This is a fatty resection specimen — extend the processor cycle, don't run it on the routine biopsy program."
**Common misuse:** using "processing" loosely to mean the entire lab workflow, when it refers specifically to the dehydration-clearing-infiltration cycle between fixation and embedding.

### Levels
Additional sections cut deeper into the same paraffin block, either banked unstained or cut on request to reach more diagnostic tissue.
**In use:** "Bank ten levels on this core before running the ordered H&E — there's not much block left if pathology wants a deeper cut later."
**Common misuse:** confusing "levels" (deeper sections of the same block) with "recuts" (re-cutting because of a technical artifact) — they're different requests with different implications for remaining tissue.

### Decalcification (decal)
Chemical removal of calcium from bone or heavily mineralized tissue so it can be sectioned on a microtome; method (acid vs. chelating) trades speed against preservation of antigenicity and DNA.
**In use:** "This bone marrow core is going for lymphoma workup — EDTA decal, not formic acid, even though it's slower."
**Common misuse:** choosing decalcification method purely by turnaround time without considering whether IHC or molecular testing is foreseeable on that tissue.

### Antigen retrieval (HIER)
Heat-induced epitope retrieval — heating fixation-masked tissue in a buffer (commonly citrate pH 6 or EDTA/Tris pH 9) to re-expose antigen sites before immunostaining.
**In use:** "Switch to the high-pH retrieval buffer for this antibody — the vendor's validation data calls for EDTA, not citrate."
**Common misuse:** assuming one retrieval buffer works universally across all antibodies rather than following each antibody's own validated retrieval condition.

### Floater / extraneous tissue
A fragment of tissue on a slide that did not originate from the labeled specimen, transferred during flotation, embedding, or cassette handling.
**In use:** "That glandular fragment doesn't belong on a skin biopsy slide — treat it as a floater until block/slide reconciliation rules it out."
**Common misuse:** reading an inconsistent fragment as an incidental diagnostic finding instead of first investigating it as contamination.

### Chatter (venetian-blind artifact)
Regularly spaced thin lines or bands across a cut section, usually from vibration between the block and blade during microtomy.
**In use:** "That's chatter, not tissue folding — check whether the block was too warm for the water bath before we blame technique."
**Common misuse:** treating chatter as a staining problem and re-running the stain instead of fixing the underlying temperature or blade issue at the cutting step.

### Block face
The exposed cutting surface of a paraffin block containing the embedded tissue; its remaining depth determines how many more sections can ever be cut.
**In use:** "We're down to maybe 200 microns of block face left on this core — bank what we can before the pathologist asks for anything else."
**Common misuse:** treating the block as a practically unlimited resource and cutting to the current order only, without tracking how much diagnostic tissue remains.

### Section thickness (micron)
The physical thickness of a cut tissue section, typically 4-5 microns for routine H&E and thinner (1-4 microns) for IHC or renal biopsies.
**In use:** "Drop to 3 microns for this IHC panel — 5 is too thick and will give uneven antigen exposure."
**Common misuse:** using one default thickness for every application rather than matching thickness to the stain and specimen type.

### Special stain
A non-H&E histochemical stain (e.g., PAS, GMS, trichrome, Congo red) that highlights a specific tissue component — glycogen, fungi, collagen, amyloid — beyond routine morphology.
**In use:** "Run a GMS on this lung biopsy — the H&E shows necrotizing granulomas and the clinical question is fungal versus mycobacterial."
**Common misuse:** ordering a special stain reflexively as a "more thorough" workup rather than because a specific morphologic finding raised a specific question it answers.

### Control tissue
A tissue sample with known, expected staining behavior run alongside case slides on the same IHC or special-stain batch to validate that the run worked.
**In use:** "The control didn't stain — hold every case on this run, this isn't a case-specific problem."
**Common misuse:** interpreting a case slide's unexpected staining as a diagnostic finding without first checking whether the run's control behaved as expected.

### Turnaround time (TAT)
The elapsed time from a defined start point (accession, gross, or receipt) to a defined endpoint (stained slide, sign-out, frozen-section read-out) for a specimen.
**In use:** "Break the TAT down by phase — is this case slow in processing or sitting in the staining queue?"
**Common misuse:** reporting a single undifferentiated TAT number without identifying which phase (gross-to-stain, stain-to-sign-out) is actually the bottleneck.

### Embedding orientation
The deliberate positioning of tissue within the paraffin block (e.g., perpendicular for skin punches, en face for margins) to expose the diagnostically relevant plane when sectioned.
**In use:** "Re-embed this punch on edge — it went in flat and the epidermis-to-dermis relationship won't be visible."
**Common misuse:** embedding by habit (always flat, always the same face up) rather than choosing orientation based on what the pathologist actually needs to see in the section plane.
