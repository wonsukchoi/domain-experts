### Garden classification
A four-grade system for femoral neck (intracapsular hip) fracture displacement, based on trabecular alignment on plain radiograph.
**In use:** "It's a Garden IV — trabeculae are paradoxically realigned, that's arthroplasty regardless of her age."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a linear severity scale where only displacement matters; the trabecular pattern (not just "how displaced it looks") is what actually predicts AVN risk, and grades I–II can still carry meaningful risk if other factors (delay to fixation, patient age) stack on top.

### Gustilo-Anderson classification
A three-tier (with IIIA/B/C subtypes) system grading open long-bone fractures by wound size, contamination, and soft-tissue/vascular injury.
**In use:** "This is a IIIB tib-fib — we need a plastics consult for flap coverage before we're talking about definitive fixation."
**Common misuse:** Assigned based on wound size alone at the bedside before formal debridement; the grade is properly confirmed intraoperatively after debridement, when true soft-tissue and periosteal damage is visible — an early bedside estimate can under- or over-grade the injury.

### Damage control orthopaedics (DCO)
Temporary stabilization (typically external fixation) of major fractures in a physiologically unstable polytrauma patient, deferring definitive fixation until resuscitation is complete.
**In use:** "Lactate's still climbing and he's coagulopathic — we're doing DCO tonight, definitive fixation in 5–7 days."
**Common misuse:** Applied reflexively to every polytrauma patient regardless of actual physiologic stability, when a genuinely stable patient benefits from early total care (definitive fixation in the first 24 hours) instead — DCO is a response to instability, not a default trauma protocol.

### Nonunion, delayed union, and malunion
Nonunion: a fracture that has stopped healing and shows no radiographic progression for an extended period (site-dependent, often ~6–9 months). Delayed union: healing slower than expected but still progressing. Malunion: healed, but in an unacceptable position.
**In use:** "This is an atrophic nonunion — no callus, sclerotic ends — that needs bone grafting, not just more time in the brace."
**Common misuse:** "Nonunion" used for any fracture that hasn't healed by an arbitrary date without checking whether it's still radiographically progressing (delayed union, which just needs time) versus truly stalled (nonunion, which needs intervention).

### Periprosthetic joint infection (PJI)
Infection involving a joint replacement implant, diagnosed per consensus criteria (MSIS/ICM) combining clinical, lab, and intraoperative findings rather than any single test.
**In use:** "Sinus tract plus a synovial WBC over threshold — that meets ICM criteria for PJI, this isn't just a wound-healing issue."
**Common misuse:** Diagnosed off an elevated CRP alone; CRP is sensitive but not specific (elevated after any surgery, in inflammatory arthritis, in unrelated infection) — the diagnosis requires the fuller criteria set, not one lab value.

### Aseptic loosening
Mechanical failure of the bond between implant and bone, absent infection, from wear debris, osteolysis, or inadequate initial fixation.
**In use:** "Radiolucent lines have progressed around the stem since the two-year film — this reads as aseptic loosening, not infection, but we still ruled out infection first."
**Common misuse:** Assumed by default in a painful implant without first ruling out infection — the workup order matters because the treatment (revision alone vs. revision plus infection protocol) is completely different.

### ASA physical status classification
A 1–6 (or 1–5 plus E for emergency) system rating a patient's pre-anesthesia medical status, used across surgery generally, not orthopedic-specific.
**In use:** "She's an ASA III with poorly controlled diabetes — anesthesia wants an A1c and cardiology clearance before we schedule the TKA."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a surgical risk score for the orthopedic procedure itself; it's an anesthesia-focused comorbidity rating, and a low ASA class doesn't mean a technically simple case, nor does a high ASA class necessarily contraindicate a needed procedure.

### Delta pressure (compartment perfusion pressure)
The difference between diastolic blood pressure and measured intracompartmental pressure, used to diagnose acute compartment syndrome.
**In use:** "Delta's 18 — that's below the 20 threshold, we're going to the OR now, not waiting for a repeat measurement."
**Common misuse:** Compared only against an absolute compartment pressure number (e.g., ">30 mmHg") without accounting for the patient's actual blood pressure — a hypotensive trauma patient can have compartment syndrome at a lower absolute pressure than the flat threshold suggests.

### Ligamentotaxis
Indirect fracture reduction achieved by traction on the surrounding soft tissues (ligaments, capsule) rather than direct manipulation of bone fragments.
**In use:** "We got acceptable alignment on the distal radius with ligamentotaxis alone — didn't need to open it."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to work equally well regardless of fracture pattern; it's most effective for fractures with intact soft-tissue attachments and periarticular/metaphyseal patterns, and unreliable for fractures with comminution or soft-tissue stripping.

### Tourniquet time
The duration a pneumatic tourniquet is inflated during limb surgery, tracked because prolonged inflation carries nerve and muscle injury risk.
**In use:** "We're at 90 minutes on the tourniquet — let's release, reperfuse, and reinflate if we need more time."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a hard universal cutoff (commonly cited as ~2 hours) applied identically to every patient; actual tolerance varies with limb size, patient vascular status, and prior tourniquet exposure, and the number is a prompt to reassess, not a fixed budget to spend in full by default.

### Weight-bearing status (NWB, TTWB, WBAT, FWB)
Postoperative instructions specifying how much load a healing fracture or construct may bear: non-weight-bearing, toe-touch weight-bearing, weight-bearing as tolerated, full weight-bearing.
**In use:** "She's TTWB on the left for 6 weeks — the fixation isn't strong enough yet for full load, but toe-touch helps with balance and prevents deconditioning."
**Common misuse:** Given as a single blanket instruction without a stated re-evaluation point; weight-bearing status should be tied to a specific healing milestone or follow-up visit, not left open-ended until the patient asks.

### Revision arthroplasty
A repeat joint replacement surgery replacing some or all components of a prior implant, distinct in complexity, bone-stock consideration, and infection risk from a primary procedure.
**In use:** "This is a revision case with significant acetabular bone loss — we're planning for augments, not just a standard cup."
**Common misuse:** Planned and consented like a primary arthroplasty; revision surgery carries materially higher infection, blood loss, and complication rates, and the bone-stock assessment (not just component removal) drives the actual surgical plan.

### Early total care (ETC)
Definitive surgical fixation of major fractures within the first 24 hours in a polytrauma patient, contrasted with damage-control orthopaedics.
**In use:** "He's stable, lactate's clearing, no coagulopathy — this is an ETC candidate, let's do definitive fixation tonight."
**Common misuse:** Applied as a rigid protocol ("we always do early total care") rather than a decision conditioned on demonstrated physiologic stability — the same surgeon who over-applies DCO to stable patients can also over-apply ETC to unstable ones.

### Five Ps (pain, pallor, pulselessness, paresthesia, paralysis)
The classic taught symptom list for acute compartment syndrome or vascular compromise.
**In use:** "Don't wait for pulselessness — pain out of proportion and pain on passive stretch come first, and by the time pulses are gone the window's mostly closed."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a checklist requiring most items present before acting; in practice, the first two (pain out of proportion, pain with passive stretch) are the early, actionable signs, and waiting for the later ones delays a time-sensitive fasciotomy decision.
