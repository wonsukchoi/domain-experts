### Pain out of proportion to injury, or pain with passive stretch, in a tight cast or high-energy fracture
- **Usually means:** early acute compartment syndrome; second most likely is a cast/dressing that's simply too tight and not yet ischemic.
- **First question:** "Has anything changed about the dressing, or is this new pain despite adequate analgesia?"
- **Data to pull:** loosen/remove circumferential dressing first, then re-exam; if equivocal, needle manometry delta pressure (diastolic BP − compartment pressure).

### Delta pressure ≤30 mmHg (or <20 mmHg) on compartment manometry
- **Usually means:** acute compartment syndrome meeting the surgical threshold; second is a technically flawed pressure reading (wrong compartment, needle position).
- **First question:** "Does the clinical exam agree with the number, and did I measure every compartment at risk?"
- **Data to pull:** repeat measurement in a second compartment location, correlate with exam findings, do not wait for serial trend once threshold is crossed.

### Persistent wound drainage beyond 7 days post-arthroplasty, or a sinus tract
- **Usually means:** periprosthetic joint infection; second most likely is a superficial wound-healing issue in a patient on anticoagulation.
- **First question:** "Is the drainage from the deep space or clearly superficial, and what's the anticoagulation status?"
- **Data to pull:** ESR/CRP trend, synovial fluid aspiration with cell count/differential/culture per MSIS/ICM criteria.

### ESR/CRP elevated and not trending down by 2 weeks post-op
- **Usually means:** evolving infection; second most likely is a normal but slow-resolving inflammatory response in a higher-baseline-CRP patient (e.g., inflammatory arthritis history).
- **First question:** "What was this patient's baseline ESR/CRP before surgery?"
- **Data to pull:** serial ESR/CRP against a documented preop baseline, not just a single elevated value.

### New pain-free interval followed by throbbing pain, or new numbness beyond expected local anesthetic duration
- **Usually means:** for the pain pattern, alveolar/wound-healing complication or early infection; for numbness beyond ~6–8 hours (typical LA duration), possible nerve injury or continued nerve compression.
- **First question:** "Is this on the expected post-op timeline, or does it deviate from the day-3 improvement curve?"
- **Data to pull:** exam documenting sharp/dull and two-point discrimination, comparison against the operative note's nerve-at-risk documentation.

### Fever plus tachycardia plus confusion 24–72 hours after a long-bone fracture (especially femur) or reaming
- **Usually means:** fat embolism syndrome; second most likely is a standard post-op pulmonary complication (atelectasis, pneumonia) or early sepsis from another source.
- **First question:** "Is there a petechial rash on the chest/axilla/conjunctiva, and what's the oxygen saturation trend?"
- **Data to pull:** ABG for hypoxemia, chest imaging, platelet count (thrombocytopenia is common in FES).

### Calf swelling, warmth, or unilateral leg pain post-arthroplasty despite VTE prophylaxis
- **Usually means:** breakthrough DVT; second most likely is a hematoma or superficial thrombophlebitis.
- **First question:** "Was the prophylaxis regimen actually administered and at the correct dose/timing?"
- **Data to pull:** duplex ultrasound, medication administration record to confirm prophylaxis compliance.

### Sudden dyspnea, tachycardia, or hypoxia in the first two weeks post-arthroplasty
- **Usually means:** pulmonary embolism; second most likely is atelectasis or a cardiac event in a patient with underlying cardiac disease.
- **First question:** "Is there a leg symptom preceding this, and what's the sudden-onset timeline?"
- **Data to pull:** CT pulmonary angiogram (or V/Q if contrast contraindicated), D-dimer only if pretest probability is low.

### Fracture with no radiographic progression toward union by 6 months (delayed union threshold varies by anatomic site)
- **Usually means:** nonunion in progress; second most likely is a biologically normal but slower-healing site (e.g., tibia) that hasn't yet crossed the delayed-union threshold for that bone.
- **First question:** "Is this fracture's expected healing timeline actually 6 months, or is this bone/pattern normally slower?"
- **Data to pull:** serial radiographs compared at consistent intervals, CT if plain films are equivocal, assessment of hardware integrity and alignment.

### Absent or diminished distal pulse after fracture reduction or cast application
- **Usually means:** vascular compromise from the reduction/cast itself; second most likely is pre-existing peripheral vascular disease unmasked by the injury.
- **First question:** "What did the pulse exam show immediately before this reduction/cast, and is it symmetric with the contralateral limb?"
- **Data to pull:** immediate re-exam after loosening any circumferential material, ankle-brachial index or vascular surgery consult if pulse doesn't return promptly.

### Progressive deformity or new instability in a joint replacement months to years after surgery, load-dependent pain
- **Usually means:** aseptic loosening or component wear; second most likely is late hematogenous infection (can present similarly).
- **First question:** "Is the pain constant (favors infection) or load-dependent (favors mechanical failure)?"
- **Data to pull:** weight-bearing radiographs comparing to prior films for migration/radiolucent lines, ESR/CRP to rule out infection before attributing to mechanical cause alone.

### Stress fracture pain in a young athletic patient with a history of amenorrhea, disordered eating, or low bone density risk factors
- **Usually means:** insufficiency/stress fracture in the context of relative energy deficiency (female athlete triad / RED-S); second most likely is a purely mechanical overuse injury with no metabolic component.
- **First question:** "What's the training-load change in the weeks before symptom onset, and is there a menstrual or nutritional history worth screening?"
- **Data to pull:** MRI for early stress reaction (more sensitive than plain film), DEXA if metabolic risk factors are present, nutrition/menstrual history.
