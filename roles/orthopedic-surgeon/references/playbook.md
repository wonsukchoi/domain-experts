# Orthopedic Surgeon — Playbook

## Garden classification (femoral neck fracture) and AVN risk

| Grade | Description | Trabecular pattern | AVN risk with fixation |
|---|---|---|---|
| I | Incomplete / valgus-impacted | Trabeculae angulated but continuous | ~10% |
| II | Complete, undisplaced | Trabeculae aligned, normal angle | ~15–20% |
| III | Complete, partially displaced | Trabeculae malaligned | ~30–35% |
| IV | Complete, fully displaced | Trabeculae paradoxically realigned (parallel to femoral shaft) | ~50% |

**Rule:** Garden I–II in a patient under ~60 → attempt internal fixation (screws), preserving the native head. Garden III–IV, or any grade in a patient over ~60 with reduced healing potential → arthroplasty. Within arthroplasty, independent cognitively-intact ambulators default to THA; lower-demand or cognitively impaired patients default to hemiarthroplasty (see SKILL.md worked example for the full reasoning chain).

## Gustilo-Anderson classification (open long-bone fractures) and infection risk

| Type | Wound size / contamination | Soft tissue / periosteal stripping | Reported infection rate |
|---|---|---|---|
| I | <1cm, clean | Minimal | ~2% |
| II | 1–10cm | Moderate, no extensive stripping | ~2–7% |
| IIIA | >10cm, adequate soft-tissue coverage of bone possible | Extensive but coverage achievable | ~4–7% |
| IIIB | >10cm, periosteal stripping, bone exposure | Requires flap coverage | ~30–52% |
| IIIC | Any size, with arterial injury requiring repair | Vascular compromise | ~25–42%, amputation risk up to ~40% |

**Rule:** Type I–II → standard debridement + IV cephalosporin, primary or delayed closure. Type IIIA → aggressive debridement, consider negative-pressure wound therapy as a bridge, still aim for soft-tissue coverage within days. Type IIIB → external fixation + staged debridements + flap coverage plan within 7–10 days; do not delay debridement past 24 hours regardless of OR availability pressure. Type IIIC → immediate vascular consult before any orthopedic fixation decision; fixation exists to stabilize the limb for the vascular repair, not the other way around.

## VTE risk tier and prophylaxis (hip fracture and major arthroplasty)

| Patient population | Risk tier | Default agent | Minimum duration | Extended duration |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Hip fracture surgery (any age) | High (distinct tier — not standard surgical VTE scoring) | LMWH (e.g., enoxaparin 40mg SC daily) | 10–14 days | 35 days total |
| Elective THA/TKA, no added risk factors | Elevated | LMWH or aspirin per shared decision, factoring bleeding risk | 10–14 days | 35 days total (per ACCP/CHEST guidance) |
| Elective THA/TKA + prior VTE or hypercoagulable condition | High | LMWH (not aspirin monotherapy) | 10–14 days | 35 days total, reassess with hematology |

**Rule:** hip fracture surgery is scored as high-risk by injury type alone — do not apply a general surgical VTE calculator (e.g., a generic Caprini-style score built for abdominal surgery) and conclude a lower tier; the orthopedic-specific literature overrides a generic score here.

## Timing-to-surgery decision (hip fracture)

| Time from injury to incision | Associated 30-day mortality effect | Action |
|---|---|---|
| ≤48 hours | Baseline | Proceed once genuinely stable; do not delay for non-essential workup |
| >48 hours | ~41% relative increase (pooled meta-analysis data) | Only acceptable if active medical instability (uncontrolled arrhythmia, decompensated heart failure, correctable coagulopathy) requires it — document the specific reason |

## Compartment syndrome — pressure thresholds and triage

| Measurement | Threshold | Action |
|---|---|---|
| Absolute intracompartmental pressure | >30 mmHg | Supports diagnosis in the right clinical context — not diagnostic alone |
| Delta pressure (diastolic BP − measured compartment pressure) | ≤30 mmHg | Diagnostic threshold in most protocols |
| Delta pressure | <20 mmHg | Definite indication for emergent fasciotomy regardless of exam |

**Triage order when compartment syndrome is suspected:**
1. Remove all circumferential dressings/casts first — this alone resolves a meaningful fraction of borderline cases and costs nothing.
2. Re-examine for pain out of proportion to injury and pain with passive stretch — the earliest reliable signs, present well before pallor, pulselessness, or paralysis.
3. If exam is equivocal or the patient can't reliably report symptoms (intubated, regional block, intoxicated), measure compartment pressure via needle manometry (Whitesides technique) in each compartment at risk.
4. Delta pressure ≤30 mmHg with a consistent clinical picture → emergent fasciotomy; do not wait for serial measurements to "confirm the trend" once the threshold is crossed.

## Post-arthroplasty pain — mechanical vs. infection triage

| Feature | Points toward mechanical (loosening/instability) | Points toward infection (PJI) |
|---|---|---|
| Pain pattern | Load-dependent, worse with activity/weight-bearing | Constant, often present or worse at rest |
| Onset | Gradual, months to years post-op | Can be early (weeks) or late (hematogenous) |
| Wound | Healed, unremarkable | Persistent drainage >7 days, sinus tract, erythema |
| Labs | ESR/CRP normal or mildly elevated | ESR/CRP elevated and not trending down |
| Imaging | Radiolucent lines, component migration | May be normal early; periosteal reaction later |

**Rule:** order ESR/CRP and plain films for any post-arthroplasty pain first — cheap and fast. Escalate to synovial fluid aspiration with cell count, differential, and culture (MSIS/ICM criteria) only when labs or the pain pattern point toward infection; reflexively aspirating every mechanical-pattern pain adds infection risk to a joint that likely just needs an alignment or wear workup.
