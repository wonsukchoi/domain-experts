# Bailiff — Vocabulary

### Essential state interest
The legal standard from *Deck v. Missouri* that must be met, on the record, before a defendant can be visibly restrained during trial — a case-specific finding of escape risk, courtroom safety, or decorum tied to that defendant, not a general transport or facility policy.
**In use:** "We don't have an essential state interest on file for this defendant — the concealed brace, not visible cuffs, is what we requested."
**Common misuse:** treating "he's in custody" or "he's facing a serious charge" as automatically satisfying the standard — those are inputs to the finding, not the finding itself.

### Concealed restraint
A physical restraint (leg brace, under-table cuff) applied in a way the jury cannot see, used to address an escape-risk finding without triggering the due-process concerns of visible shackling.
**In use:** "Judge authorized a concealed leg brace for the duration — jury never sees it, so we didn't need the higher bar for visible restraint."
**Common misuse:** assuming any restraint at all requires the same visible-restraint judicial finding — concealed restraints still need authorization, but the standard and the appellate exposure differ.

### Ex parte communication
Any communication with the judge, or between the judge and a party/juror, that happens outside the presence of, or without the knowledge of, the other party — a jury note answered informally by the bailiff instead of routed through the judge is a textbook example.
**In use:** "That's ex parte if I answer it myself — the note goes to the judge unread, no exceptions for something that seems minor."
**Common misuse:** assuming logistics questions ("what time do we break," "can we see an exhibit again") are exempt from the rule because they're not about the case's merits — the rule is about the channel, not the content.

### Sequestration
Court-ordered isolation of a jury (or occasionally a witness) from outside contact and media for the duration of a trial or deliberation, requiring the bailiff to manage lodging, transport, meals, and monitored outside contact.
**In use:** "Jury's sequestered starting tonight — full transport manifest and no-device rule kicks in at 6pm."
**Common misuse:** treating sequestration as primarily a security posture rather than an information-control one — the point is preventing outside influence on deliberations, not physical protection per se (though the two overlap).

### Chain of custody (courtroom exhibits)
The documented, unbroken record of who physically held a trial exhibit at every point it changed hands, from the clerk's exhibit table through the jury room and back.
**In use:** "Log it before you hand it to the jury room — a verbal hand-off leaves a gap opposing counsel can use."
**Common misuse:** treating chain-of-custody logging as a formality that can be reconstructed later from memory — a gap is a gap regardless of whether anything actually happened to the exhibit.

### Decorum order
The judge's standing rules for conduct in the courtroom (phones, standing for the judge, addressing the court, dress, media presence), which the bailiff enforces on the judge's behalf and does not set independently.
**In use:** "Judge's decorum order bars cameras past the bar — that's not my call to waive even if the request seems reasonable."
**Common misuse:** assuming the bailiff has discretion to relax or tighten decorum rules based on their own read of a situation — enforcement authority, not rule-setting authority.

### Screening perimeter
The controlled boundary (typically the courthouse entrance) past which everyone, including judges, attorneys, and jurors, passes through weapons screening — distinct from courtroom-specific security measures inside that perimeter.
**In use:** "Once you're past the perimeter you don't get re-screened per courtroom — that's what the perimeter is for."
**Common misuse:** treating perimeter screening and courtroom-level security staffing as the same control — a fully staffed perimeter doesn't reduce the case-specific staffing need inside a given courtroom.

### Throughput (screening)
The number of people a single screening lane can process in a given time window, used to calculate whether checkpoint staffing matches expected attendance rather than available officer count.
**In use:** "At 25 seconds a person we clear 72 in the pre-session window — today's expected volume is 70, one lane holds."
**Common misuse:** sizing screening staff to "how many officers we have," which answers a different question than "how many the expected volume requires."

### Removal (Illinois v. Allen)
One of three constitutionally permissible responses to a disruptive defendant — removing them from the courtroom, with counsel continuing to represent them, until they give assurance of proper conduct — distinct from and generally preferred over binding and gagging.
**In use:** "We're at removal, not restraint — he gets one more warning, then he's out until he agrees to behave."
**Common misuse:** treating removal and physical restraint as interchangeable escalation options at the same tier — *Allen* frames bind-and-gag as the last resort specifically because removal is less disruptive to the trial's appearance of fairness.

### Uniformed presence (Holbrook standard)
The case-by-case, not presumptive, standard for whether visible courtroom security staffing is prejudicial — turns on whether the officer count is proportionate to an articulated, case-specific need.
**In use:** "Four officers on a six-defendant case with an overextended detail passed *Holbrook* — the number has to trace back to a stated reason, not just be available headcount."
**Common misuse:** assuming any visible security presence beyond a bare minimum is automatically prejudicial, or conversely that *Holbrook* gives unlimited cover for any staffing level — both misread the case's actual holding.

### Bench warrant vs. courtroom custody
A bench warrant is a judge's order for a person's arrest (often for a failure to appear), distinct from the bailiff's day-to-day custody responsibility for someone already in custody and present in the courtroom.
**In use:** "That's a bench warrant matter for the sheriff's warrants unit — not something I execute mid-session out of this courtroom."
**Common misuse:** treating "bailiff" as a role that personally executes warrants on sight — most agencies route warrant execution through a separate unit, and courtroom bailiff duty is custody and security for the pending session, not active warrant enforcement.
