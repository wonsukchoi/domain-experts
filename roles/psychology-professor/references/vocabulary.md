# Vocabulary

### Effect size (Cohen's d)
A standardized measure of how large a difference is, independent of sample size — unlike a p-value, it doesn't shrink just because n is small.
**In use:** "The p-value clears .05, but d is 0.81 with a CI running from 0.04 to 1.58 — that's not a number I'd plan a follow-up study around."
**Common misuse:** reporting only significance and treating a large observed d from a tiny sample as trustworthy on its face, without checking the confidence interval around it.

### Statistical power
The probability of detecting a true effect of a given size, given the sample size and alpha — computed *before* data collection, from an assumed effect size.
**In use:** "We're powered at 80% to detect d=0.4 with n=98 per group; anything smaller than that and a null result is uninformative, not evidence of no effect."
**Common misuse:** computing "observed power" retroactively from the result itself to justify a finding — circular, and not a valid statistical procedure.

### Preregistration
A time-stamped, public record of hypotheses, design, and analysis plan filed before data collection, distinguishing confirmatory tests from exploratory ones.
**In use:** "That subgroup effect wasn't in the preregistration, so it goes in the paper labeled exploratory, not as a confirmed finding."
**Common misuse:** writing hypotheses vague enough that any pattern of results can be read as confirming them, which defeats the point of preregistering at all.

### IRB exempt / expedited / full board
The three review tracks for human-subjects research, differing by risk level and review speed — exempt for minimal-risk anonymous survey/archival work, expedited for minor-risk procedures, full board for anything with meaningful risk or vulnerable populations.
**In use:** "It's a self-report survey on substance use, so it's not exempt even though there's no physical risk — that topic needs expedited review at minimum."
**Common misuse:** assuming "no physical risk" automatically qualifies a study for exempt status; sensitive topics (trauma, illegal behavior, mental health) usually require expedited or full review regardless of physical risk.

### Construct validity
Whether a measure actually captures the theoretical concept it claims to, as opposed to just producing consistent numbers.
**In use:** "Alpha is .89, but that tells us the items hang together — it doesn't tell us they're measuring anxiety and not just general negative affect."
**Common misuse:** treating high internal-consistency reliability (Cronbach's alpha) as proof of construct validity, when reliability and validity are separate properties.

### Replication (direct vs. conceptual)
A direct replication repeats the original procedure as closely as possible; a conceptual replication tests the same underlying hypothesis with a different manipulation or measure.
**In use:** "That's a conceptual replication with a different priming manipulation — it doesn't 'fail to replicate' the original in the direct-replication sense, it tests whether the effect generalizes."
**Common misuse:** calling any non-significant follow-up study a "failed replication" of the original, even when the design differs enough to be a conceptual test, not a direct one.

### Tenure clock / probationary period
The fixed window (typically 6 years, with a formal 3rd-year review) in which a tenure-track faculty member must build a case for tenure.
**In use:** "That grant resubmission has to go out this cycle — missing it pushes the next possible submission past the 3rd-year review."
**Common misuse:** assuming pre-tenure service work counts toward the case at the same weight as research and teaching; most P&T rubrics weight it well below the other two pillars until after tenure.

### Student evaluations of teaching (SET)
End-of-course numeric and written student ratings, historically used as a primary teaching-quality metric in P&T review.
**In use:** "SET alone isn't going in the dossier as the sole teaching evidence — pairing it with the peer-observation report and the pre/post concept-inventory scores."
**Common misuse:** treating a single semester's SET mean as a definitive measure of teaching quality on its own, when the literature finds it weakly related to actual learning.

### Impact factor
A journal-level metric of average citations per article over a fixed window, often used as a proxy for a paper's importance.
**In use:** "Impact factor norms are different for a quantitative-methods journal than for a clinical outcomes journal — comparing the two numbers directly is comparing different scales."
**Common misuse:** comparing impact factors across psychology subfields as if they were on a single universal quality scale, when publication and citation norms differ widely by subfield.

### Formative vs. summative assessment
Formative assessment checks progress and feeds back into learning without high stakes; summative assessment evaluates achievement, typically for a grade that counts significantly.
**In use:** "Calling it formative doesn't change its function if it's still 15% of the final grade — that's summative with formative branding."
**Common misuse:** labeling a graded weekly quiz "formative" while it still contributes substantially to the course grade, when its actual function is summative regardless of the label.

### Type I / Type II error
Type I: rejecting a true null (a false positive). Type II: failing to reject a false null (a false negative), often from insufficient power.
**In use:** "We can't say the effect isn't there — at n=20 per group we're underpowered for d=0.3, so this is a likely Type II situation, not evidence of no effect."
**Common misuse:** treating a non-significant result as proof the null hypothesis is true, without considering whether the study had enough power to detect the effect if it existed.

### R&R (revise and resubmit)
An editorial decision inviting a revised version of a manuscript for further review, short of acceptance.
**In use:** "It's an R&R, not an accept — plan the tenure-clock timeline assuming it could still be rejected after the second round."
**Common misuse:** treating an R&R as functionally equivalent to acceptance when planning a tenure-case timeline; a substantial R&R can still be rejected on resubmission.

### Retrieval practice / testing effect
The finding that actively recalling information from memory produces more durable learning than re-exposure to the same material (rereading, highlighting).
**In use:** "Swap the review-day rereading for a closed-note retrieval quiz on the same material — the testing effect is one of the most replicated findings in the learning literature."
**Common misuse:** assuming any quiz format counts, including open-book or immediate-repetition recognition tasks, when the effect specifically depends on effortful recall from memory.

### h-index
A researcher-level metric: the number h such that h of their papers have at least h citations each.
**In use:** "h-index isn't comparable here — she's 4 years post-PhD and he's 20, and psych subfields differ a lot in typical co-authorship team size."
**Common misuse:** comparing h-index across career stages or subfields without normalizing for years active or typical team/co-authorship size, which inflates the metric unevenly.
