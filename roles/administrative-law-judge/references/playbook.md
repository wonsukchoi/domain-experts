# Administrative hearing decision playbook

Filled worksheets for the recurring ALJ tasks: contrary-evidence-addressing template, medical/vocational opinion weighing, and record development checklist.

## 1. Contrary-evidence-addressing template (written decision section)

For every significant piece of evidence that cuts against the eventual finding, address it explicitly — never omit it:

```
Contrary evidence: <specific finding/testimony/exhibit, with citation>
Why it doesn't change the outcome (or why it does): <specific reasoning tied to the record,
  not a general statement that it was "considered">
```

Example:
```
Contrary evidence: Consultative examiner's report (Ex. 7F) found only mild concentration
  limitation based on a single 45-minute exam.
Why it doesn't outweigh the treating source's opinion: The consultative exam's findings
  rest on a single data point with limited supportability, while the treating psychiatrist's
  opinion is grounded in 28 documented visits over 30 months showing a consistent PHQ-9
  trend (14-19) and 3 specific redirection incidents (Ex. 4F, 6F, 9F). Supportability and
  consistency favor the treating source's longitudinal record over the one-time snapshot.
```

Rule: a decision silent on significant contrary evidence is vulnerable to remand regardless of whether its ultimate conclusion happens to be correct — the reviewing body can't confirm the evidence was weighed at all.

## 2. Medical/vocational opinion weighing worksheet

| Factor | Opinion A (e.g., treating source) | Opinion B (e.g., consultative examiner) |
|---|---|---|
| Supportability — amount/quality of relevant evidence and explanation | 28 visits over 30 months, tracked PHQ-9 trend, 3 documented incidents | Single 45-min exam, limited notation |
| Consistency — how well the opinion aligns with the rest of the record | Consistent across full longitudinal record, no contradicting evidence | No track record to assess consistency against |
| Relationship with claimant | Treating, 30 months | One-time, non-treating |
| Specialization relevant to the impairment | Psychiatrist (relevant specialty) | General consultative examiner |
| **Weight assigned** | **Greater weight** | **Lesser weight** |

Rule: fill every row with specific record-grounded reasoning, not a general credibility impression — "more objective" or "more credible" alone is not a valid basis for a weight determination under the governing regulatory factors.

## 3. Record development checklist (before closing the hearing)

- [ ] Claimant/party received proper notice of the hearing and its purpose
- [ ] Claimant/party given the opportunity to present evidence and testimony
- [ ] Claimant/party (or representative) given the opportunity to cross-examine any adverse witnesses (e.g., vocational expert)
- [ ] All relevant medical/vocational/documentary evidence identified by either party has been admitted or a ruling made on its admission
- [ ] Any gaps in the record (missing treatment records, unclear vocational history) addressed before closing, not left to the written decision to paper over
- [ ] Claimant/party informed of next steps and any post-hearing submission opportunity

Rule: due process requirements apply regardless of hearing informality or docket pressure — closing a hearing with an incomplete record creates a due-process vulnerability independent of how the eventual decision comes out.

## 4. Mandatory sequential framework discipline (example: SSA 5-step)

```
Step 1: Substantial gainful activity? -> if yes, not disabled, STOP.
Step 2: Severe impairment(s)? -> if no, not disabled, STOP.
Step 3: Meets/equals a listing? -> if yes, disabled, STOP.
Step 4: Can perform past relevant work given RFC? -> if yes, not disabled, STOP.
Step 5: Can perform other work in the national economy given RFC, age, education, experience?
  -> if no, disabled. If yes, not disabled.
```

Rule: address every step in order, even when a later step's answer seems obvious — skipping or reordering a mandatory step is independently reversible error, regardless of whether the ultimate conclusion is substantively correct.
