# Administrative law judge working vocabulary

Terms an administrative law judge uses precisely and a generalist blurs together. Format: definition → how a practitioner says it → common misuse.

**Substantial evidence** — The standard a reviewing court applies to an ALJ's factual findings: enough relevant evidence that a reasonable mind could accept it as adequate to support the conclusion, a lower bar than preponderance of the evidence but one that still requires the decision to engage the whole record, including contrary evidence.
In use: "Substantial evidence is a low bar, but silence on the contrary evidence is what actually gets decisions remanded — not the ultimate weight assigned."
Misuse: treating "low bar" as license to write a thin decision — the standard still requires the decision to show it considered the whole record, not just the evidence supporting its conclusion.

**Supportability and consistency (opinion-weighing factors)** — Under current regulatory frameworks, supportability asks how much relevant evidence and explanation underlies a medical/vocational opinion; consistency asks how well that opinion aligns with the rest of the evidence in the record. These are the specific factors used to weigh conflicting opinions, not rank, credentials, or exam recency alone.
In use: "The treating source's opinion has stronger supportability — 28 visits and a tracked symptom trend — than the consultative exam's single data point."
Misuse: weighing opinions by a general sense of which examiner seems more objective or credentialed, rather than applying supportability and consistency to the specific record evidence behind each opinion.

**Residual functional capacity (RFC)** — In disability adjudication, the most a claimant can still do despite their impairments, expressed in specific functional terms (exertional level, off-task percentage, postural limitations), used to determine whether past or other work is possible.
In use: "The RFC needs to state the off-task percentage specifically — 'moderate limitations' alone doesn't translate into a vocational conclusion."
Misuse: stating an RFC in vague qualitative terms without the specific functional limitations a vocational expert needs to translate into a work-preclusive or non-preclusive conclusion.

**Sequential evaluation (structured mandatory framework)** — A required, ordered set of steps an agency mandates for a specific type of adjudication (e.g., a 5-step disability evaluation), where each step's answer can end the analysis, and skipping or reordering steps is independently reversible error.
In use: "We don't skip to step 5 because the outcome seems obvious — every step gets addressed in order, or the decision is vulnerable regardless of the final conclusion."
Misuse: treating the sequential framework as a general checklist that can be reordered or abbreviated when a later step's answer seems clear from the outset — the mandatory order itself is part of what a reviewing body checks.

**Vocational expert (VE) testimony** — Testimony from a vocational specialist translating a claimant's RFC, age, education, and work history into a conclusion about whether past work or other work in the national economy is available, often including specific thresholds (e.g., work-preclusive off-task percentages).
In use: "The VE testified that 10% off-task is generally the tolerance threshold — the RFC's 20% finding, if credited, is work-preclusive per that testimony."
Misuse: accepting a vocational conclusion without tying it to the VE's actual stated thresholds and reasoning in the decision — the decision needs to cite the specific testimony, not just assert the ultimate vocational conclusion.

**Due process floor (agency adjudication)** — The minimum procedural protections required in agency hearings regardless of their informality: adequate notice, an opportunity to be heard, the right to present and rebut evidence, and a decision based on the record.
In use: "Even in an informal hearing setting, the claimant still gets a real opportunity to cross-examine the vocational expert — that's a due process floor, not a courtesy."
Misuse: assuming informal hearing procedures mean due process protections are relaxed or optional — the floor requirements apply regardless of how casual the proceeding feels.

**Remand (administrative appeal)** — A reviewing body's action sending a case back to the ALJ for further proceedings or a more complete decision, distinct from outright reversal, typically triggered by a procedural or evidentiary gap in the original decision rather than a substantive disagreement with the outcome.
In use: "This isn't a reversal on the merits — it's a remand because the decision never addressed the treating source's contrary findings."
Misuse: assuming a remand means the ALJ's ultimate conclusion was wrong — remands are frequently about a gap in the decision's reasoning or record engagement, not a determination that the outcome itself was incorrect.

**Boilerplate finding** — A generic, templated statement (e.g., "claimant's testimony is not fully credible") reused across cases without record-specific reasoning, a common and predictable ground for remand.
In use: "That credibility line is boilerplate — it needs the specific inconsistency in this claimant's testimony, not a stock sentence."
Misuse: assuming a boilerplate finding is acceptable because it's technically true in a general sense — the requirement is specificity tied to this case's record, not truth in the abstract.

**Treating source vs. consultative examiner** — A treating source has an ongoing treatment relationship with the claimant, typically producing a longitudinal record; a consultative examiner conducts a single, often agency-arranged exam without an ongoing relationship, producing a one-time snapshot.
In use: "The treating source's 30-month record carries more supportability than the one-time consultative exam, all else equal."
Misuse: assuming a consultative examiner's opinion is automatically weighted less (or more) than a treating source's — the actual regulatory factors (supportability, consistency), not the source category alone, govern the specific weight assigned.

**Listing (impairment listing)** — A specific set of medical criteria an agency (e.g., SSA) publishes that, if met or medically equaled, establishes disability at a specific evaluation step without needing to proceed to a functional/vocational analysis.
In use: "The MRI doesn't show nerve root compression, so this doesn't meet Listing 1.15 — we proceed to the RFC analysis instead of stopping here."
Misuse: treating a claimant's diagnosis alone as meeting a listing — listings require specific documented criteria (like particular imaging findings), not just the presence of a qualifying diagnosis.
