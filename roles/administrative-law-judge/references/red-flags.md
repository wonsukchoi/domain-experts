# Red flags — signals an administrative law judge notices instantly

Smell tests with thresholds. Any one alone can be a fixable gap in a draft decision; two or more together is a pattern strongly correlated with remand.

### A credibility or evidentiary-weight finding stated without a specific record citation
- **Usually means:** the finding may be conclusory rather than record-grounded, which is a predictable and common ground for remand regardless of whether the underlying judgment is sound.
- **First question:** "What specific exhibit or testimony supports this exact finding, not the general topic area?"
- **Data to pull:** the draft decision's credibility/weight findings cross-referenced against specific exhibit numbers or transcript citations.

### A draft decision credits one medical/vocational opinion over another using only a general characterization ("more objective," "more credible")
- **Usually means:** the decision hasn't applied the actual governing weighing factors (supportability, consistency) and may not survive review even if the ultimate weighting is defensible.
- **First question:** "Walk through the supportability and consistency factors specifically for each opinion — what does the record actually show for each?"
- **Data to pull:** medical/vocational opinion weighing worksheet (see `references/playbook.md` §2) filled out for the conflicting opinions.

### Significant contrary evidence exists in the record but isn't mentioned anywhere in the draft decision
- **Usually means:** a reviewing body can't confirm the evidence was considered, which is independently remandable regardless of whether the ultimate conclusion is correct.
- **First question:** "What's the strongest piece of evidence against this finding, and where does the decision address it?"
- **Data to pull:** full record review for significant contrary evidence, contrary-evidence-addressing template (see `references/playbook.md` §1) applied to each.

### A mandatory sequential/structured evaluation step is skipped or addressed out of order
- **Usually means:** this is independently reversible error under most agency frameworks, regardless of whether the substantive conclusion happens to be correct.
- **First question:** "Has every required step been addressed in the mandated order, even where a later step's answer seems obvious?"
- **Data to pull:** the agency's mandatory framework checklist (see `references/playbook.md` §4), draft decision's structure against it.

### A hearing was closed without confirming the record was complete or the party had a chance to cross-examine adverse testimony
- **Usually means:** a due-process gap that exists independent of how the eventual decision comes out — informality of the hearing doesn't excuse these floor requirements.
- **First question:** "Was the record development checklist completed before the hearing closed, and is there a record of it?"
- **Data to pull:** record development checklist (see `references/playbook.md` §3), hearing transcript for notice/cross-examination opportunities.

### A written decision reads as a near-identical template to prior decisions on similar fact patterns
- **Usually means:** the decision may not have engaged the specific record facts of this case, which undermines both this decision's defensibility and the broader caseload's reversal-rate exposure if the pattern recurs.
- **First question:** "What in this decision is specific to this claimant's record, versus boilerplate language reused across cases?"
- **Data to pull:** comparison of decision language across recent similar cases, specific record citations unique to this case.

### A treating source's longitudinal opinion is discounted in favor of a one-time examiner's opinion with no supportability/consistency comparison
- **Usually means:** the decision may be defaulting to a general preference (e.g., assuming a one-time exam is inherently more "objective") rather than applying the actual regulatory factors, which is a common and specifically flagged pattern in appellate review.
- **First question:** "What does the longitudinal record actually show, and has it been compared point-by-point against the one-time exam's supportability?"
- **Data to pull:** treating source's full longitudinal record, one-time examiner's report, weighing worksheet applied to both.

### A decision's timeline or drafting shows signs of being rushed to meet a caseload quota
- **Usually means:** caseload pressure is being allowed to substitute for the substantial-evidence floor each individual case still requires, regardless of docket volume.
- **First question:** "Does this specific decision engage the specific record facts, independent of how many other cases are pending?"
- **Data to pull:** decision draft against the contrary-evidence and weighing templates, time spent relative to caseload norms (context, not an excuse).

### Vocational expert testimony is accepted without addressing the specific work-preclusive thresholds it establishes
- **Usually means:** a finding may not follow from the vocational expert's actual testimony if the decision doesn't connect the RFC's specific limitations to what the expert said about work-preclusive thresholds.
- **First question:** "What did the vocational expert specifically say about this exact limitation's effect on available work, and does the decision cite that testimony directly?"
- **Data to pull:** vocational expert hearing testimony transcript, RFC findings, step 4/5 analysis citing the specific testimony.
