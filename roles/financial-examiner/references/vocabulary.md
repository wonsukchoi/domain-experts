### CAMELS rating
The interagency composite rating system for depository institutions, covering six components: Capital adequacy, Asset quality, Management, Earnings, Liquidity, and Sensitivity to market risk, each rated 1 (strongest) to 5 (weakest).
**In use:** "The bank's CAMELS composite is a 3, driven mainly by the asset-quality component."
**Common misuse:** Treating the composite as a simple average of the six component scores rather than a judgment-weighted assessment favoring the most material weakness.

### Risk-weighted assets (RWA)
A bank's total assets adjusted by risk-weight percentages that reflect each asset category's credit risk, used as the denominator for risk-based capital ratios.
**In use:** "Commercial real estate carries a 100% risk weight, while government securities carry 0% — that's why RWA is lower than total assets."
**Common misuse:** Comparing capital to total (unweighted) assets when evaluating regulatory capital adequacy, instead of to risk-weighted assets.

### Leverage ratio
Tier 1 capital divided by total (average) assets, a simple, non-risk-weighted capital measure used alongside risk-based ratios.
**In use:** "Leverage ratio looks fine at 6%, but that doesn't tell us anything about the risk-based ratios."
**Common misuse:** Treating an adequate leverage ratio as sufficient evidence of capital adequacy without also checking the risk-based ratios, which can diverge significantly.

### Tier 1 risk-based capital ratio
Tier 1 capital divided by risk-weighted assets, one of the primary regulatory capital ratios compared against well-capitalized thresholds.
**In use:** "Tier 1 risk-based ratio came in at 7.5%, below the 8% well-capitalized threshold."
**Common misuse:** Confusing this ratio with the leverage ratio (which uses total assets, not risk-weighted assets, as the denominator).

### Prompt Corrective Action (PCA)
The regulatory framework categorizing banks (well-capitalized, adequately capitalized, undercapitalized, etc.) based on capital ratios, with escalating required actions at each lower category.
**In use:** "Falling below 8% Tier 1 risk-based puts this bank in the adequately capitalized PCA category, not well-capitalized."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a bank that looks financially sound in general is automatically in the well-capitalized PCA category without checking the specific ratio thresholds.

### Loan classification (Pass / Special Mention / Substandard / Doubtful / Loss)
The regulatory scale for categorizing loan credit quality based on repayment capacity and collateral, ranging from Pass (no identified weakness) to Loss (uncollectible).
**In use:** "Examiner testing reclassified this loan from Pass to Substandard based on updated DSCR and collateral value."
**Common misuse:** Accepting a bank's internal loan grade as equivalent to the regulatory classification without independent examiner testing.

### Debt service coverage ratio (DSCR)
A measure of a borrower's operating cash flow relative to required debt payments, used to assess primary repayment capacity — a DSCR below 1.0 indicates cash flow insufficient to cover debt service.
**In use:** "Recalculated DSCR using updated financials came in at 0.85 — below the 1.0 threshold."
**Common misuse:** Relying on the bank's original underwriting DSCR calculation without recalculating it against current, updated borrower financials.

### Allowance for credit losses (ALLL/CECL)
The reserve a bank holds against expected credit losses on its loan portfolio — ALLL under the incurred-loss model, CECL (Current Expected Credit Losses) under the newer expected-loss accounting standard.
**In use:** "CECL reserve adequacy needs to be tested at the segment level, not just against the aggregate portfolio."
**Common misuse:** Accepting an aggregate reserve-to-loan ratio as evidence of adequacy without checking whether specific higher-risk segments are individually underreserved.

### Specific reserve
An additional reserve amount held against a specific classified loan's estimated loss, calculated separately from the general portfolio reserve.
**In use:** "The $500,000 uncollateralized exposure at a 40% loss severity assumption requires a $200,000 specific reserve."
**Common misuse:** Applying only a general portfolio reserve rate to a loan already identified as having a specific, well-defined weakness, instead of calculating a loan-specific reserve.

### Matter Requiring Attention (MRA) / Matter Requiring Immediate Attention (MRIA)
Examination finding classifications indicating required corrective action, with MRIA reserved for more severe issues requiring more urgent board-level response.
**In use:** "This capital shortfall is an MRA with a 60-day corrective action deadline; if it recurs uncorrected next exam, it escalates to an MRIA."
**Common misuse:** Treating all examination findings as equally urgent, or failing to escalate a finding's classification when it recurs uncorrected from a prior exam.

### Call Report / UBPR (Uniform Bank Performance Report)
The standardized quarterly regulatory financial report banks file (Call Report) and the peer-comparison analytical report derived from it (UBPR), used as baseline data for examination.
**In use:** "UBPR peer comparison shows this bank's classified-asset ratio well above its peer group average."
**Common misuse:** Treating Call Report figures as examiner-verified data rather than bank-reported data that examination testing may adjust or challenge.
