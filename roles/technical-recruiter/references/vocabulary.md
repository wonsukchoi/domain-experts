# Technical recruiting working vocabulary

Terms a recruiter uses precisely. Format: definition → how a practitioner says it → common misuse.

**Requisition scoping** — The process of defining a role's specific must-haves and success criteria with the hiring manager before sourcing begins, as distinct from sourcing against a generic job description template.
In use: "Requisition scoping caught that 'distributed systems debugging' was the actual must-have — that never appeared in the original template."
Misuse: sourcing candidates against an unscoped, generic requirements list to save time up front, which typically produces a funnel that gets rejected late for reasons that were never made explicit.

**Must-haves vs. nice-to-haves** — The explicit distinction between disqualifying requirements and preferences, defined before candidate review begins, preventing the bar from shifting candidate to candidate based on who happened to apply.
In use: "Prior startup experience is a nice-to-have, not a must-have — don't screen anyone out for lacking it."
Misuse: leaving the must-have/nice-to-have distinction implicit or undefined, letting the bar quietly shift depending on which candidate is currently being evaluated, producing inconsistent and hard-to-defend decisions.

**Structured interviewing** — Using the same core questions and a pre-defined scoring rubric across all candidates for a given role, with each interviewer submitting scores independently before group discussion, to reduce noise and anchoring bias.
In use: "All five interviewers use the same rubric for this role and submit scores before the debrief — that's what makes the loop's signal usable at all."
Misuse: allowing each interviewer to improvise their own questions and evaluation criteria, producing decisions that feel confident in the room but are actually noisy and hard to defend or learn from later.

**Behavioral vs. hypothetical interview questions** — Behavioral questions ("tell me about a time you...") ask about actual past behavior; hypothetical questions ("what would you do if...") ask about an imagined scenario — behavioral questions produce more predictive signal because they test what someone actually did, not how well they can describe an ideal response.
In use: "We replaced the hypothetical system-design prompt with a behavioral question about an actual production incident this candidate handled — much stronger signal."
Misuse: relying primarily on hypothetical questions, which mainly test articulation of an ideal response rather than predicting actual on-the-job behavior.

**Anchoring (interview debrief)** — The bias where the first strong opinion expressed in a group discussion — often the most senior person's — disproportionately shapes the outcome, avoided by requiring independent scoring before group discussion.
In use: "We require every scorecard submitted before the debrief starts specifically to avoid anchoring on whoever speaks first."
Misuse: letting group discussion happen before independent scores are submitted, allowing the loudest or most senior voice in the room to shape everyone else's evaluation before they've formed their own independent view.

**Late-stage rejection** — A candidate rejected after advancing through most or all of the interview loop, often signaling a mismatch between what was screened for early and what interviewers are actually evaluating later.
In use: "A 56% late-stage rejection rate is the signal that our phone screen isn't catching the actual disqualifying criterion — it's costing us interviewer hours for candidates who were never going to pass."
Misuse: treating a high late-stage rejection rate as evidence of a weak candidate pool rather than investigating whether the screening process itself is missing a criterion interviewers are applying later.

**Candidate experience (employer brand)** — How a candidate is treated throughout the hiring process — response time, communication clarity, respect for their time — which shapes whether they become a promoter or detractor of the company regardless of the hiring outcome.
In use: "Even the candidates we didn't hire got specific, honest feedback — that's part of what candidate experience and employer brand actually mean, not just a nice gesture."
Misuse: treating candidate experience as separate from or in tension with rigorous evaluation, when a well-run, transparent, rigorous process is itself part of what makes the candidate experience good.

**Post-hire outcome tracking (interview validity)** — Checking whether interview scores actually correlate with on-the-job performance after hire, used to validate — and periodically recalibrate — whether the interview process is predicting the right thing.
In use: "Post-hire tracking showed our system-design interview score doesn't correlate with actual performance — we're recalibrating what that interview evaluates."
Misuse: assuming an interview process is predictive without ever checking interview scores against actual post-hire performance data, potentially screening for the wrong signal indefinitely.

**Passive candidate sourcing** — Direct outreach to candidates who aren't actively job-searching, often the strongest pool for competitive roles, as distinct from waiting for inbound applicants to a job posting.
In use: "For this senior role, we're doing direct outreach to passive candidates, not just posting and waiting — the best fits for this specific gap aren't actively looking."
Misuse: relying solely on inbound applications for a competitive or specialized role, missing that the strongest candidates for that role are often not actively searching and require direct outreach.
