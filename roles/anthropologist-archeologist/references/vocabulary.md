# Vocabulary

Terms generalists flatten together that a CRM anthropologist/archeologist keeps sharply separate — the value is in the misuse, not the definition.

## Area of Potential Effects (APE) vs. project footprint

The **APE** (36 CFR 800.16(d)) is the full geographic area within which an undertaking may directly *or indirectly* cause changes to historic properties. The **project footprint** is only the physical construction area — a subset of the APE, not a substitute for it.

**In use:** "The turbine pads are the footprint, but the APE has to include the viewshed radius — that's a separate, larger boundary for indirect effects."

**Common misuse:** treating the construction footprint as the whole APE, missing indirect effects like visual or noise impacts on a nearby historic district.

## Phase I vs. Phase II vs. Phase III

**Phase I** identifies historic properties in the APE. **Phase II** evaluates whether an identified site is NRHP-eligible. **Phase III** is data recovery — mitigating an unavoidable adverse effect on an eligible site. Each phase answers a different regulatory question and requires a different scope of work.

**In use:** "We can't call this eligible off Phase I data — that's a Phase II determination."

**Common misuse:** using Phase I shovel-test results to issue a firm eligibility call, or scoping Phase III excavation without a completed Phase II evaluation and MOA to authorize it.

## Shovel test pit (STP) vs. test unit

An **STP** is a small-diameter (commonly 30–35cm) hole used for systematic subsurface survey across a grid, meant to sample, not exhaustively excavate. A **test unit** (commonly 1×1m or 1×2m) is a larger excavation used in Phase II/III to expose features and stratigraphy in horizontal and vertical detail.

**In use:** "The STP grid found the site; we need test units to actually expose that feature."

**Common misuse:** treating an STP's small sample as representative of a feature's full extent or shape, which only a test unit can characterize.

## Site vs. isolated find

A **site** meets the state's site-definition threshold (commonly three or more artifacts within a defined radius, or a feature). An **isolated find** is below that threshold — recorded, but not delineated or evaluated further.

**In use:** "Two flakes, 40 meters apart, no feature — that's two isolated finds, not a site."

**Common misuse:** delineating and evaluating an isolated find as if it were a site, or the reverse — dismissing a low artifact count without checking whether a feature is also present.

## Diagnostic artifact vs. temporally sensitive artifact

A **diagnostic artifact** (a projectile point type, a ceramic type) has a known, typologically established date range from regional comparative research. Not every artifact is diagnostic — undecorated flakes or plain body sherds usually aren't. A **terminus post quem** is the earliest possible date a context could date to, established by its latest-dated diagnostic artifact.

**In use:** "The Savannah River point gives us a terminus post quem of roughly 3000 BC for that level — nothing below it can be later than that."

**Common misuse:** treating every recovered artifact as chronologically informative, or reading a terminus post quem as the actual occupation date rather than an earliest bound.

## Provenience vs. provenance

**Provenience** is the precise three-dimensional findspot of an artifact within the excavation (unit, level, coordinates). **Provenance** is the artifact's ownership/custody history after recovery (collector, dealer, museum accession). Archaeology cares primarily about the former; art history/museums primarily about the latter.

**In use:** "Log the provenience to the centimeter before it's bagged — once it's out of the ground, that data can never be recovered."

**Common misuse:** using "provenance" when "provenience" is meant, which drops the field-context information that gives an artifact its archaeological value.

## Harris matrix

A diagram recording only the direct physical superposition relationships (above/below, cuts/is cut by) between excavated stratigraphic units — a relative sequence, not a dated one.

**In use:** "The matrix shows the pit cuts the sterile subsoil, so the pit postdates it — but the matrix alone doesn't tell us by how many years."

**Common misuse:** reading a stratigraphic sequence as if it directly conveys calendar dates, instead of treating it as the framework absolute dates get attached to.

## Conventional radiocarbon age vs. calibrated age

The **conventional radiocarbon age** (RCYBP) is the lab's direct measurement, corrected for isotopic fractionation but not yet converted to calendar time. The **calibrated age** (cal BC/AD or cal BP) maps that measurement onto the IntCal20 curve to produce a calendar-year range, because atmospheric C-14 concentration hasn't been constant over time.

**In use:** "3,120 ± 30 BP conventional calibrates to roughly 1495–1320 cal BC at 2-sigma — report the calibrated range, not the raw lab number, in the narrative."

**Common misuse:** citing the uncalibrated lab date as if it were a calendar date, which can be off by centuries depending on where it falls on the calibration curve.

## National Register eligible vs. listed

**Eligible** means the property meets NRHP criteria whether or not it has been formally nominated. **Listed** means it has gone through the nomination and been formally entered on the Register. Section 106 review treats eligible and listed properties identically — listing status is a separate, optional administrative step.

**In use:** "It doesn't need to be listed for Section 106 to apply — a documented eligibility determination is enough."

**Common misuse:** assuming an unlisted site has no Section 106 protection, or that eligibility requires the owner's consent (it doesn't — listing does).

## Criteria Consideration vs. Criteria A-D

**Criteria A-D** are the substantive bases for significance (event, person, design/construction, information potential). **Criteria Considerations (A-G)** are exceptions requiring extra justification for certain property types (cemeteries, religious properties, moved properties, reconstructed properties, birthplaces/graves, commemorative properties, properties under 50 years old) that are otherwise presumptively excluded.

**In use:** "It's a cemetery, so we also have to clear Criteria Consideration D before Criterion A significance even matters."

**Common misuse:** evaluating only Criteria A-D and skipping the Considerations check for a property type that requires it, or the reverse.

## Emic vs. etic

**Emic** describes an account expressed in the informant's/culture's own categories and meanings. **Etic** describes the analyst's outside, comparative categories. Both are legitimate data; conflating them collapses "what the informant said it means" with "what the analyst concludes it means."

**In use:** "Record the emic term she used for the practice verbatim, separately from our etic classification of it — don't merge them at the point of collection."

**Common misuse:** presenting an etic analytic category as though it were the informant's own understanding, or treating an emic account as automatically objective.

## Curation (federal collections)

The long-term physical and records management of an archaeological collection after fieldwork ends, governed for federally associated collections by 36 CFR Part 79 — a repository requirement distinct from, and often costlier than, the excavation itself.

**In use:** "Budget for curation fees at the state repository now — a collection with no accepting repository at the end of the project is a compliance failure, not a paperwork detail."

**Common misuse:** treating curation as an afterthought handled informally, rather than a repository-specific requirement that needs to be arranged before fieldwork starts.

## Memorandum of Agreement (MOA) vs. Programmatic Agreement (PA)

An **MOA** resolves an adverse effect for a specific undertaking. A **PA** establishes a standing process for a category of recurring undertakings (e.g., a state DOT's routine road-maintenance program), replacing case-by-case Section 106 review with pre-agreed procedures.

**In use:** "This one-off pipeline gets an MOA; the agency's annual culvert-replacement program runs under its existing PA instead."

**Common misuse:** treating a PA as a blanket exemption from Section 106 rather than a pre-negotiated procedure that still requires compliance with its own terms.
