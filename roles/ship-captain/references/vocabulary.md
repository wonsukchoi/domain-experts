# Vocabulary

Terms generalists conflate that deck officers keep sharply separate. Each: definition, how a practitioner actually uses it in a sentence, and the common misuse to avoid.

### Give-way vessel vs. stand-on vessel

**Give-way vessel** is the vessel required by the applicable Rule to take early and substantial action to keep clear. **Stand-on vessel** is required to hold course and speed — but only until it becomes apparent the give-way vessel isn't acting appropriately, at which point Rule 17 requires the stand-on vessel to maneuver too.

*In use:* "We're stand-on here, but she's shown no course change in three minutes at this range — I'm not waiting for her to confirm she's not moving."

**Common misuse:** treating "stand-on" as a right to hold course no matter what, rather than a conditional obligation that flips to independent action once non-response is apparent.

### CPA vs. TCPA

**CPA** (closest point of approach) is the minimum distance a plotted target is projected to pass from own ship if both vessels hold their current course and speed. **TCPA** (time to closest point of approach) is how long until that point is reached.

*In use:* "CPA's inside our 1.0-mile minimum and TCPA's under twenty minutes — that's an early-action call, not a wait-and-see."

**Common misuse:** citing CPA alone as the risk indicator; a comfortable CPA with a very short TCPA can still leave no time to react if the give-way vessel is late to act.

### CBDR (constant bearing, decreasing range)

A target whose compass bearing does not appreciably change over successive observations while its range is closing — the textbook radar/visual signature of a collision course under Rule 7(d)(i).

*In use:* "Bearing's held at zero-two-one for the last two plots while range dropped over a mile — that's CBDR, treat it as risk of collision."

**Common misuse:** waiting for the bearing to visibly "swing" before acting; a lack of bearing change is itself the danger signal, not the absence of one.

### GM (metacentric height) vs. free-surface correction

**GM** is the distance between the center of gravity (KG) and the metacenter (KM) — roughly, how strongly the vessel wants to right itself when heeled. **Free-surface correction (FSC)** is the reduction to GM caused by liquid free to shift in a slack tank, computed per tank from its free-surface moment.

*In use:* "Solid GM reads point-four-five, but with two ballast tanks slack we're actually at point-three-three fluid — that's the number that matters."

**Common misuse:** quoting solid GM (KM − KG) as the real stability margin without subtracting FSC, which overstates available margin by exactly however much liquid is slack.

### Set vs. drift vs. leeway

**Set** is the direction a current is flowing toward, expressed as a true bearing. **Drift** is the current's speed, usually in knots. **Leeway** is a separate effect — the vessel's own sideways push from wind, not from current — and is corrected for independently of set and drift.

*In use:* "Set's two-zero-zero at a knot and a half — that's on top of whatever leeway we're picking up from this beam wind, they don't cancel each other automatically."

**Common misuse:** treating "current" and "leeway" as the same correction, or applying only one when both are acting on the track.

### DR position vs. EP vs. fix

**DR (dead reckoning) position** is where the vessel would be based purely on course steered and speed through the water, with no current or leeway applied. **EP (estimated position)** is the DR position corrected for known or estimated set, drift, and leeway. **Fix** is a position derived from an independent source (GPS, radar ranges, visual bearings) with no reliance on course/speed assumptions at all.

*In use:* "DR puts us on the zero-nine-zero line, but EP with the current correction puts us about seven degrees south of that — confirm with a fix before altering."

**Common misuse:** treating DR as good enough in a known-current area, or treating an old fix as still valid without re-running the DR/EP forward from it.

### Not under command (NUC) vs. restricted in ability to maneuver (RAM) vs. constrained by draft

**NUC** is a vessel that, through some exceptional circumstance (engine or steering failure), is unable to maneuver as required and so cannot keep out of another vessel's way. **RAM** is a vessel restricted by the nature of her work (dredging, towing, diving operations) from maneuvering as required, even though otherwise capable. **Constrained by draft** is a vessel whose draft in relation to the available depth of water severely restricts her ability to deviate from her course.

*In use:* "She's not NUC, she's RAM — dredge pipe out both sides, she genuinely can't turn inside that arc even if she wanted to."

**Common misuse:** using "NUC" loosely for any vessel that's simply slow to respond, when it specifically means a vessel that legally cannot comply with the Rules due to an exceptional circumstance.

### Overtaking vs. crossing vs. head-on

**Overtaking** (Rule 13) is approaching another vessel from more than 22.5° abaft her beam — the position from which, at night, only her sternlight would be visible. **Head-on** (Rule 14) is a reciprocal or near-reciprocal course encounter. **Crossing** (Rule 15) is risk of collision in any other geometry between two power-driven vessels.

*In use:* "We came up on her from well abaft the beam — that's an overtake, and we stay the give-way vessel even after we draw ahead of her."

**Common misuse:** assuming an overtake situation reclassifies once the overtaking vessel draws ahead; Rule 13 explicitly holds the overtaking vessel's give-way obligation until finally past and clear.

### BRM (Bridge Resource Management) vs. CRM (Crew Resource Management)

**BRM** is the maritime-specific discipline (closed-loop communication, challenge-and-response, shared mental model among the bridge team) built specifically around navigation and watchkeeping decisions. **CRM** is the earlier, aviation-originated version of the same underlying idea, adapted into BRM rather than applied to ships directly.

*In use:* "That's a straight BRM breakdown — the lookout saw it developing and didn't challenge because the mate outranked her."

**Common misuse:** treating BRM as generic "teamwork training" rather than a specific protocol (closed-loop readback, mandatory challenge regardless of rank) tied to documented casualty patterns.

### STCW vs. SOLAS vs. MARPOL

**STCW** (Standards of Training, Certification and Watchkeeping) governs crew competency, certification, and rest hours. **SOLAS** (Safety of Life at Sea) governs vessel construction, equipment, and safety systems. **MARPOL** governs pollution prevention (oil, garbage, emissions, ballast water). All three are separate IMO conventions with separate compliance regimes.

*In use:* "That's an STCW rest-hour finding, not a SOLAS equipment deficiency — different convention, different corrective action."

**Common misuse:** citing the wrong convention when logging a finding or reporting to a flag/port-state inspector, which routes the corrective action to the wrong department or system.

### Watch system: 4-on-8-off vs. 6-on-6-off

**4-on-8-off** is a three-watch rotation (four hours on, eight off) common on larger crewed vessels. **6-on-6-off** is a two-watch rotation more common on smaller crews or certain vessel types, giving fewer but longer rest blocks.

*In use:* "We're 6-and-6 on this boat — that six-hour block has to actually be six hours of rest, not six hours nominally off with calls coming in."

**Common misuse:** assuming any watch system automatically satisfies the STCW rest-hour floor; a 6-on-6-off pattern interrupted by drills or port operations can still fall below the 10h/24h requirement.

### Squat vs. underkeel clearance (UKC)

**Squat** is the additional vessel draft caused by increased speed in shallow or confined water, from the Bernoulli effect on the hull. **UKC** is the actual vertical clearance between the keel and the seabed at a given moment, which must account for squat, not just charted depth minus static draft.

*In use:* "Charted UKC looks fine at rest, but at twelve knots through this channel, squat alone eats most of the margin we thought we had."

**Common misuse:** calculating UKC from static draft only and ignoring squat, which can be over a meter for a large vessel at speed in shallow water.

### Master's authority vs. the conn vs. the pilot

**Master's authority** is the non-delegable overall responsibility and legal authority for the vessel at all times. **The conn** is the specific, delegable authority to give helm and engine orders at a given moment — held by the OOW, master, or pilot depending on who's been given it. **The pilot** provides local navigational expertise and typically holds the conn in pilotage waters, but does not displace the master's overall authority or right to intervene.

*In use:* "Pilot has the conn coming into the channel, but the master's still the master — if something looks wrong, he can and will take it back."

**Common misuse:** treating "the pilot is aboard" as meaning the master's responsibility is suspended; pilotage transfers the conn, not the underlying command authority.
