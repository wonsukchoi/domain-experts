# Masonry Helper — Vocabulary

### Temper / retemper
Adjusting mortar's water content to restore workability as it stiffens on the board. Retempering is adding water and re-mixing; it's allowed once, inside the working-life window.
**In use:** "Batch two's stiffening up — give it one retemper, don't let it sit."
**Common misuse:** Treating "retemper" as a synonym for "add water whenever it looks dry" — past the working-life window, added water doesn't restore strength, it just masks a batch that should be discarded.

### Working life / board time
The window (roughly 2.5 hours from initial gauging under normal conditions) during which mortar can still be placed or retempered before cement hydration makes it unusable.
**In use:** "That batch is at two hours — one more retemper and it's done, not two."
**Common misuse:** Confusing working life with cure time — working life is about whether the mortar can still be spread; cure time is about whether the wall has gained strength. They're unrelated clocks.

### Face-shell bedding vs. full bedding
Face-shell bedding applies mortar only to the outer edges (face shells) of a CMU; full bedding covers the entire top surface including webs. Full bedding is typically required below grade or on structural/reinforced cells.
**In use:** "This is a foundation wall, we're full-bedding it — don't run face-shell mortar quantities on the take-off."
**Common misuse:** Using face-shell bag-yield numbers to order mortar for a full-bed spec (or vice versa) — the yield roughly halves between the two, and getting it backwards means running out mid-wall or over-ordering by a full pallet.

### Buttering
Applying mortar to the end of a unit before setting it against the previous one, forming the head joint.
**In use:** "Don't butter more than two ahead — that lead one's already skinning."
**Common misuse:** Treating buttering-ahead as pure efficiency; buttered mortar that sits too long before the unit is set skins over and produces a head joint that looks full but has poor bond.

### Green wall / green masonry
A wall that has been laid but not yet cured to the point of having structural (especially lateral/wind) capacity on its own.
**In use:** "That lift's green — it doesn't come off bracing until the mason signs off, not just because it looks set."
**Common misuse:** Judging "green" by whether the mortar looks hard on the surface — surface set and structural cure are different timelines, and bracing decisions follow the latter.

### Spot board / mortar board
A small platform (often 2'x2' or 3'x3') positioned near the mason's working edge to hold a workable-size portion of mortar, replenished from the mixer batch.
**In use:** "Board's low — reload it before he's dry, not after."
**Common misuse:** Treating the spot board as just a landing spot rather than a pacing tool — its refill timing is how a helper actually controls the mason's flow without being told each time.

### Story pole
A marked rod showing course heights and openings for a wall, used by the mason to check coursing without measuring every joint.
**In use:** "Check the pole before you cut that closure unit — don't eyeball the last course."
**Common misuse:** Assuming the helper sets or reads the story pole independently — it's the mason's reference; the helper stages material and cuts to marks the mason gives, not to an independent read of the pole.

### Closure unit
The last unit placed in a course, often cut to fit the remaining gap rather than a full-size unit.
**In use:** "Measure twice before you cut the closure — there's no second piece of stock on-site for that course."
**Common misuse:** Pre-cutting closure units before the course is otherwise complete — the actual gap varies with joint thickness accumulated across the run, so a closure cut early is usually wrong by a fraction of an inch.

### Wythe
A single continuous vertical section of masonry wall one unit thick; a wall can be single-wythe or multi-wythe (e.g., a cavity wall with two wythes and an air space).
**In use:** "This is a two-wythe cavity wall — don't count both wythes as one unit run on the take-off."
**Common misuse:** Collapsing a multi-wythe wall's material take-off into a single-wythe calculation, which undercounts units and mortar by close to half.

### Efflorescence
A white, powdery salt deposit on a masonry face caused by water moving through the wall and depositing dissolved salts as it evaporates.
**In use:** "That's fresh efflorescence, not a stain — check whether it's washing off or coming back after every rain."
**Common misuse:** Treating any white residue as efflorescence and scrubbing it off without checking whether it recurs — recurring efflorescence is a water-intrusion symptom that needs tracing to a source, not a cosmetic fix.

### Tuckpointing
Cutting out and replacing deteriorated mortar joints in existing masonry, as distinct from laying new units.
**In use:** "This is a tuckpointing job — grinding out old joints is the silica-exposure task here, not the saw cuts."
**Common misuse:** Assuming tuckpointing dust control is the same as a fresh cut on new unit stock — grinding out old, often contaminated mortar generates different dust volume and duration, and the exposure control plan should treat it as its own task.

### Cold joint
An unintended seam where fresh mortar or grout meets a section that had already begun to set, creating a weaker bond line than a continuous pour or lay.
**In use:** "We stopped grouting mid-cell yesterday — flag that lift for a cold joint before the inspector walks it."
**Common misuse:** Using "cold joint" to describe an intentional control joint or expansion joint — those are designed features, not a bonding defect.

### Duty rating (scaffold)
The load classification (light/medium/heavy duty, in psf) a scaffold platform is rated to carry, set by OSHA 1926.451 and confirmed by the erecting crew's tag.
**In use:** "Tag says medium duty — that's mason plus board stock, not a pallet of block staged on the planks."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a scaffold that "looks sturdy" is rated for whatever's being loaded onto it — the rating is a posted number, not a visual judgment call.
