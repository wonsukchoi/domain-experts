# Vocabulary

### Deliberate indifference
The legal standard from *City of Canton v. Harris* (1989) for holding a municipality liable for a subordinate's constitutional violation via failure to train or supervise — not mere negligence, but a conscious disregard of an obvious, known risk.
**In use:** "If we've had three prior force complaints on this exact scenario and never adjusted training, that's how a plaintiff's attorney builds a deliberate-indifference argument."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a synonym for "the department should have done better," when it's a specific, higher legal bar requiring a known pattern that was ignored — one bad incident, standing alone, almost never meets it.

### Monell claim
A federal civil-rights claim (from *Monell v. Dept. of Social Services*, 1978) against the municipality itself, not just the individual officer — requires proof of an official policy or custom, since there's no automatic employer liability (respondeat superior) for constitutional violations.
**In use:** "The individual excessive-force claim against the officer is separate from the Monell claim against the city — the city is only on the hook if we can show it was a pattern the department knew about."
**Common misuse:** Conflated with ordinary employer liability — a Monell claim specifically requires showing the violation traces to a policy, custom, or documented pattern of indifference, not just that an employee did something wrong on the job.

### Garrity statement
A statement compelled from a public employee under threat of job loss (per *Garrity v. New Jersey*, 1967); inadmissible against the employee in a subsequent criminal proceeding, but usable in the administrative/discipline process.
**In use:** "Read him the Garrity warning before the administrative interview — the statement can be used to discipline him, but it can't be handed to the DA."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to mean the officer can refuse to answer at all — a Garrity-warned employee can be disciplined for refusing to answer duty-related questions; the protection is about the use of the statement, not about a right to silence in the administrative setting.

### Early Intervention System (EIS) / Early Warning System (EWS)
A data-driven flagging system (per Walker, Alpert & Kenney, NIJ 2001) that surfaces officers whose use-of-force, complaint, or pursuit counts cross a statistical threshold within a rolling window, for a non-disciplinary supervisory review.
**In use:** "He crossed the EIS threshold on his third UOF this year — that's a mandatory intervention meeting, not automatically a discipline case."
**Common misuse:** Treated as an accusation or a de facto disciplinary record — an EIS flag is a statistical trigger for review, and treating it as guilt teaches officers to under-report borderline incidents.

### Sustained / not sustained / unfounded / exonerated
The four standard dispositions for a complaint investigation: sustained (evidence supports the allegation), not sustained (insufficient evidence either way), unfounded (the alleged act didn't occur), exonerated (the act occurred but was lawful/justified/within policy).
**In use:** "The complaint was exonerated — the takedown happened exactly as described, but it was within policy given the resistance documented."
**Common misuse:** "Not sustained" and "unfounded" are used interchangeably in casual conversation, but they mean different things on the record — one says the evidence is inconclusive, the other affirmatively clears the officer of the alleged conduct having happened at all.

### Duty to intervene
The obligation of an officer who witnesses another officer using clearly excessive or unlawful force to step in and stop it, articulated in the IACP/PERF National Consensus Policy and now widely codified.
**In use:** "The BWC shows Ortiz saying 'that's enough' and pulling the other officer back — that's a textbook duty-to-intervene action, and it should be documented as a positive, not buried in the file."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to only matter when it's violated (an officer who stood by) — a sergeant reviewing footage should also be identifying and documenting when it was correctly exercised, since it's relevant to both officers' records.

### Span of control
The number of subordinates a single supervisor can oversee without the review quality degrading; in patrol supervision, standard guidance (Cordner) puts the workable range around 6-10 officers per sergeant.
**In use:** "We're running 14 officers under one sergeant on nights — that's past the point where force reviews are more than a signature."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a pure staffing-budget number rather than a supervision-quality constraint — a department can be "fully staffed" on paper while still having a span-of-control problem that degrades every review that sergeant signs.

### Daily Observation Report (DOR)
The Field Training Officer's structured, per-shift scoring instrument for a probationary officer across defined skill categories, typically on a numeric scale (e.g., 1-7), used to track trend, not just a single pass/fail outcome.
**In use:** "Don't just look at his phase-end average — pull the individual DORs, his radio-procedure score has been flat for three weeks."
**Common misuse:** Reduced to a single aggregate number at phase-end review — the diagnostic value is in the category-by-category trend across individual DORs, which a summary score erases.

### Loudermill rights
The due-process protections (from *Cleveland Board of Education v. Loudermill*, 1985) requiring a public employee facing termination receive notice of the charges, an explanation of the evidence, and an opportunity to respond before the termination takes effect.
**In use:** "We can't move straight to termination on this — he's entitled to a Loudermill hearing first, even with the sustained IA finding."
**Common misuse:** Conflated with the full disciplinary appeal/arbitration process — Loudermill specifically covers the pre-termination notice-and-response step, not the entire grievance procedure that may follow.

### Rubber-stamping
Supervisory approval of reports or actions as a routine formality rather than a substantive check — approving without independently verifying the account against available evidence.
**In use:** "Three months of approvals with zero revisions sent back isn't a clean squad, it's rubber-stamping — go pull a sample against the BWC."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to require intent to cover something up — it's often just review fatigue or time pressure, but it carries the same legal exposure (deliberate-indifference pattern evidence) regardless of intent.

### Fact-vs-conclusion writing standard
The requirement that every evaluative word in a report ("combative," "uncooperative," "aggressive") be paired with the specific observable act that supports it, rather than standing alone as an unsupported characterization.
**In use:** "'Subject was uncooperative' gets sent back — tell me what he did. Did he refuse verbal commands, pull his arm away, plant his feet?"
**Common misuse:** Applied only to use-of-force narratives — the same standard applies to EIS intervention memos, discipline recommendations, and DOR write-ups, anywhere a conclusion is being recorded as if it were self-evidently supported.

### Use-of-force review board
A second-level review body above the individual sergeant, convened for force incidents meeting a severity threshold (injury requiring treatment, firearm discharge, in-custody death), that evaluates policy compliance and training implications.
**In use:** "This one goes to the review board regardless of how clean the sergeant's initial read is — hospital treatment was required, that's an automatic referral."
**Common misuse:** Treated as redundant with the sergeant's review rather than as the check on it — the sergeant's review is an input to the board's process, not a substitute that makes the board review optional.
