# Red flags

### Officer with 3+ use-of-force incidents in a rolling 12-month window
- **Usually means:** the officer is drawing a disproportionate share of high-resistance calls (domestic disturbances, mental-health holds), or a genuine escalation-tactics gap — rarely random variance at that frequency.
- **First question:** what's the call-type distribution behind these incidents, and is it different from the rest of the squad's?
- **Data to pull:** EIS individual history, CAD call-type breakdown for the officer's assignments over the same window, squad and department average UOF rate for comparison.

### A sergeant's use-of-force approval log shows zero revisions or rejections across a full quarter
- **Usually means:** rubber-stamping rather than genuine review — either the reports are unusually clean (possible but statistically unlikely across dozens of incidents) or the reviewer isn't reading closely enough to catch the routine documentation gaps every squad produces.
- **First question:** when this sergeant last sent a report back for a missing fact-vs-conclusion fix, and how long ago.
- **Data to pull:** approval log with revision/rejection counts, sample of 5 approved reports checked against BWC for narrative-footage match.

### One officer's sustained-complaint rate is more than double the squad average
- **Usually means:** either a genuine conduct pattern or a specific recurring situational trigger (a particular beat, shift, or partner combination) worth isolating before assuming it's the individual alone.
- **First question:** do the sustained complaints cluster by type (demeanor, force, procedure) or are they scattered?
- **Data to pull:** complaint log by category and date, partner/beat assignment history for the same window.

### Body-worn camera not activated on a documented use-of-force incident
- **Usually means:** a policy-compliance failure (forgot, malfunction) or, less often, a deliberate avoidance — the distinction matters for the discipline track and has to be established, not assumed.
- **First question:** does the officer's activation rate on other calls in the same period show a pattern of gaps, or is this an isolated miss?
- **Data to pull:** BWC activation audit log for the officer's prior 90 days, equipment maintenance/malfunction ticket history.

### Overtime concentrated in a small number of officers (e.g., top 2 of 8 taking >50% of squad OT hours)
- **Usually means:** fatigue and decision-quality risk building in the officers taking the most hours, and/or an inequitable voluntary sign-up process the rest of the squad has stopped bothering to compete for.
- **First question:** what's the trailing-30-day total hours worked (not just OT) for the top OT earners, and is any assignment involving force or pursuit going to a fatigued officer?
- **Data to pull:** payroll OT-by-officer report, hours-worked-in-trailing-24 log for high-liability assignments.

### FTO daily observation reports show a declining or flat trend without a documented remediation plan
- **Usually means:** the probationary officer is heading toward a phase-end failure that a mid-phase intervention could still address, or the FTO isn't scoring consistently and needs recalibration.
- **First question:** when did the trend start, and has anyone had a documented conversation with the trainee about it yet?
- **Data to pull:** full DOR set for the phase (not just the phase-end summary), FTO's scoring history across multiple trainees for calibration comparison.

### One officer generates multiple vehicle pursuits in a short window (e.g., 2+ in 90 days)
- **Usually means:** either an assignment/beat with genuinely elevated pursuit-triggering activity, or a pattern of initiating or continuing pursuits past the point policy or risk tolerance would support.
- **First question:** did each pursuit meet the department's initiation and continuation criteria independently reviewed, or is that review still pending?
- **Data to pull:** pursuit review reports, dash/BWC footage, policy compliance checklist for each incident.

### A discipline case reaches a termination hearing with no contemporaneous documentation of prior counseling on the same conduct
- **Usually means:** verbal-only counseling was treated as sufficient at the time, which now weakens the case in arbitration or a due-process hearing — *Loudermill* rights require the employee had notice, and "notice" is hard to prove without a paper trail.
- **First question:** is there any written record — email, memo, roll-call note — even informal, that predates this incident and references the same conduct?
- **Data to pull:** personnel file, supervisor daily logs, any prior EIS or complaint file referencing the same conduct type.

### Squad's use-of-force rate is significantly above department average with no corresponding difference in call volume or call-type mix
- **Usually means:** either a tactics/training gap shared across the squad, or the elevation is actually concentrated in one or two officers and the squad-wide average is misleading — check individual rates before concluding it's systemic.
- **First question:** does the elevation disappear if the top 1-2 individual contributors are excluded from the squad average?
- **Data to pull:** per-officer UOF rate broken out from the squad aggregate, call-type distribution comparison against a similar squad/beat.

### An officer consistently requests the same single partner or avoids being paired with certain colleagues
- **Usually means:** could be a benign preference, but can also indicate the officer is managing who witnesses their conduct — worth distinguishing before dismissing it.
- **First question:** is there a pattern in which specific officers are being avoided, and do any of them have documented concerns on file?
- **Data to pull:** pairing/assignment history, any informal or formal concerns raised by avoided officers.

### Incident report submitted more than 48 hours after the event with no approved extension
- **Usually means:** memory degradation is already affecting report quality by the time it's written, independent of any intent to obscure anything — specific, defensible detail decays fast.
- **First question:** was there an operational reason for the delay (court, injury, extended scene), or did it just not get prioritized?
- **Data to pull:** report submission timestamp log, comparison to the officer's typical turnaround time.

### A specific squad shows voluntary attrition (transfer requests, resignations) meaningfully above the department average
- **Usually means:** a supervision or culture problem on that squad specifically — attrition rarely clusters by squad for reasons unrelated to how it's being run.
- **First question:** in exit or transfer-request conversations, is a specific name or pattern coming up more than once?
- **Data to pull:** transfer/resignation requests by squad over 12-24 months, exit interview notes if available.
