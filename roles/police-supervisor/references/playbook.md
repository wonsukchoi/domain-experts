# Playbook

Filled procedures a sergeant runs directly. Numbers are stated policy norms drawn from published EIS/FTO models and standard department practice — a specific agency's written policy always controls; adapt thresholds to it.

## 1. Use-of-force report review sequence

Run in this order, every time, regardless of how routine the incident looks:

1. **Pull BWC and any independent video before reading the narrative.** Note timestamp of force application, what preceded it by 10-15 seconds, and what followed.
2. **Read the narrative and flag any sentence that's a conclusion without a paired fact** ("subject was uncombative," "used minimum force necessary") — send back for revision before approving, don't fill in the missing fact yourself.
3. **Check injury documentation**: photos taken, medical clearance/treatment noted, or explicit statement that none was needed and why.
4. **Pull the officer's rolling 12-month UOF and complaint count** from the EIS system — this happens on every review, not only when something looks off.
5. **Classify:** (a) clears at sergeant level — proportionate force, complete documentation, no EIS threshold crossed; (b) needs revision — documentation gap, no policy violation apparent; (c) elevate to use-of-force review board — injury requiring treatment beyond first aid, firearm discharge, any in-custody death, or a policy question the sergeant can't resolve alone.
6. **Sign and date the review with a one-line disposition reason** ("cleared — proportionate to documented active resistance, EIS status: 1 of 3 threshold, no action required"), not a bare signature.

## 2. EIS threshold table and response

| Trigger (rolling 12 months) | Required action | Timeline |
|---|---|---|
| 1st use-of-force incident | Routine sergeant-level review only | Same review cycle |
| 2nd use-of-force incident, or 1 sustained complaint | Sergeant notes pattern in file; no formal meeting required | Same review cycle |
| 3rd use-of-force incident, or 2 sustained/pending complaints | Mandatory non-disciplinary intervention meeting | Within 5 business days of trigger |
| 4th+ use-of-force incident after a documented intervention | Escalate to IA for pattern review; may run parallel to non-disciplinary track | Within 3 business days |
| Any single incident independently warranting IA (dishonesty, excessive force finding, integrity issue) | IA referral immediately, regardless of EIS count | Immediate |

**Intervention meeting structure (non-disciplinary track):**
1. State up front: "This is a non-disciplinary EIS review, not a discipline conversation" — in writing, before the meeting starts.
2. Walk the officer through the pattern data, not just the trigger incident: rate vs. squad/department average, call-type distribution, time-of-shift clustering.
3. Ask the officer's own read on the pattern before offering one.
4. Identify contributing factors: call assignment skew, insufficient de-escalation training, equipment issue, fatigue/scheduling.
5. Agree a concrete remediation (training date, call-assignment rebalance, mentoring) with a re-check date (commonly 60-90 days).
6. File the memo regardless of outcome — an EIS review with no written disposition is functionally undocumented.

## 3. Garrity-compliant administrative interview

**Before the interview:**
- Confirm which track this is: administrative-only, or administrative-with-possible-criminal-referral. If criminal conduct is plausible, coordinate with IA/legal on whether a parallel criminal investigation with a *separate* interviewer and *no* compelled statement is needed before taking a Garrity statement.
- Notify the officer of the specific allegation and the interview date with reasonable advance notice (commonly 48 hours, per department policy or CBA).
- Confirm union/association representative rights apply and are offered.

**Garrity warning (administrative track), read verbatim and documented as read:**
> "I wish to advise you that you are being questioned as part of an official investigation of the [department name]. You will be asked questions specifically directed and narrowly related to the performance of your official duties. You are entitled to all rights and privileges guaranteed by the laws of this state and the Constitution of this state and the United States, including the right not to be compelled to incriminate yourself. I further wish to advise you that if you refuse to testify or to answer questions relating to the performance of your official duties, you will be subject to discipline for that refusal. If you do answer, neither your statements nor any information or evidence which is gained by reason of such statements can be used against you in any subsequent criminal proceeding. However, these statements may be used against you in relation to subsequent departmental charges."

**During:** questions narrowly related to official duties; document verbatim or record; no leading the officer to a predetermined conclusion.

**After:** written summary within 24-48 hours while detail is fresh; disposition (cleared, sustained, not sustained, exonerated, unfounded) documented with the specific facts supporting it.

## 4. FTO / DOR review cycle (Reno/San Jose-model structure)

| Phase | Duration (typical) | Sergeant review cadence | Escalation trigger |
|---|---|---|---|
| Phase 1 — Familiarization | 2-3 weeks | Weekly DOR trend read | Score below 3/7 on any category two shifts running |
| Phase 2 — Increasing responsibility | 4-6 weeks | Weekly | Plateau (no score improvement) over 3 consecutive DORs |
| Phase 3 — Full responsibility, FTO shadows | 4-6 weeks | Weekly + mid-phase sit-down | Any regression from Phase 2 average |
| Phase 4 — Final evaluation, unmarked observation | 2 weeks | Daily | Any single unsatisfactory DOR |

**On an escalation trigger:** sergeant meets with FTO and probationary officer within 3 business days, reviews specific DORs (not just the score), builds a written remediation plan with 2-3 concrete, observable targets and a re-check date before the next phase gate. A phase-end pass/fail decision made without a documented mid-phase remediation attempt is difficult to defend if challenged later.

## 5. Minimum staffing / holdover decision

Standing rule: **minimum staffing for [shift/beat configuration] is 4 patrol units.** When projected staffing falls below minimum:

1. Check voluntary overtime sign-up list first.
2. If unfilled, holdover outgoing shift by seniority-inverse (least senior held first, per most CBAs) — cap at 4 consecutive hours held absent an active emergency.
3. If still short, callback from off-duty list; do not pull a unit from an adjacent beat below its own minimum to fill this one — that converts one coverage gap into two.
4. Track cumulative hours worked in the trailing 24 hours for every held/called-back officer; hard cap at 16 consecutive hours per policy — an officer at or near cap for a high-liability assignment (traffic stops, pursuits) may need reassignment to lower-intensity duty rather than release.
5. Log the staffing decision and reasoning — repeated minimum-staffing shortfalls on the same shift are a scheduling/budget problem to escalate up, not a recurring holdover decision to keep absorbing silently.

## 6. Progressive discipline matrix (illustrative structure)

| Offense category | 1st occurrence | 2nd occurrence (12 mo.) | 3rd occurrence (12 mo.) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Minor procedural (late report, uniform/equipment) | Verbal counseling, documented | Written reprimand | 1-day suspension |
| Moderate (preventable at-fault collision, policy violation without harm) | Written reprimand | 1-3 day suspension | 3-5 day suspension / EIS referral |
| Serious (unjustified force, dishonesty in a report, integrity/*Brady* issue) | Suspension pending IA, up to termination — skip to conduct-appropriate rung regardless of prior history | — | — |

Conduct in the "serious" row does not follow the ladder — a first offense of dishonesty in an official report is evaluated on its own severity, not softened by a clean prior record.
