# Secretary/Administrative Assistant (General) — Playbook

## Shared task queue (filled example)

A visible queue beats a personal notepad because every supported manager can see what's already committed — this is what makes a capacity conflict visible before it becomes a missed deadline.

| Requester | Task | Est. hours | Deadline | Hard/soft | Status |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Sales lead | Client-call deck | 4 | Fri 10am | Hard (external) | In progress |
| Finance lead | Variance report | 5 (full) / 2 (summary) | Fri EOD | Soft (internal) | Summary version accepted |
| Ops lead | Process-update memo | 3 | "By Friday" | Soft (no stated hard date) | Deferred to Monday |
| Sales lead | Follow-up thank-you notes | 1 | Following Monday | Soft | Queued |

Capacity check: 8 hours available this week (Wed–Fri). Committed: Sales deck (4) + Finance summary (2) = 6. Buffer remaining: 2 hours. Ops memo and Finance detail report explicitly deferred, not silently dropped — both appear in the queue with a new target date.

## Capacity-tracking worksheet

Run this check *before* accepting a new request, not after falling behind:

| Week | Available hours | Already committed | Requested (new) | Total if accepted | Over capacity? |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| This week | 8 | 4 (Sales, in progress) | 5 (Finance full) | 9 | Yes — 1 hour over |
| This week | 8 | 4 (Sales) | 2 (Finance summary, revised scope) | 6 | No — 2-hour buffer |

If the total-if-accepted column exceeds available hours, that's the signal to propose a scope change (a shortened deliverable, a later deadline, or explicit deferral) before agreeing to the request as originally asked — not to accept it and hope to make up the time later in the week.

## Collision-resolution sequence

1. **Log the new request** with requester, task, hour estimate, and stated deadline (ask directly whether a deadline is hard or soft if it isn't stated — "by Friday" from one manager can mean a contractual filing date and from another can mean "sometime this week").
2. **Check it against the queue** for the same time window. If nothing collides, accept and move on.
3. **If a collision exists**, identify each request's actual constraint type: external-hard (a client call, a regulatory filing, another party's calendar) beats internal-hard (a manager's own stated deadline) beats internal-soft (a convenience target).
4. **Apply the team's standing tie-break rule** if one exists. If none exists, propose one to whichever party has standing to set it (a shared supervisor, or the group of managers together) rather than inventing a rule silently.
5. **Draft a collision-and-proposal message** to all affected requesters naming the specific conflict, the proposed resolution, and a deadline for objections — not a private decision announced after the fact.
6. **Update the queue** to reflect the resolution, including any deferred items with their new target date, so nothing silently disappears from view.

## Second worked example: recurring meeting-time conflict

Two managers, Ops and Finance, both want a standing weekly 1:1 at 2pm Thursday — the only slot that works for both of their external schedules. No tie-break rule exists yet for recurring commitments (only for one-off task conflicts).

Naive move: alternate weeks between them without telling either why, hoping neither notices the pattern.

Correct move: name the conflict directly — "Both of you want the 2pm Thursday slot for a standing 1:1 and I don't have a rule for recurring conflicts yet. Proposing Ops keeps 2pm (booked first) and Finance moves to 2pm Wednesday, which is also open on both calendars — or if there's a reason Thursday specifically matters for Finance, let me know and I'll bring both options to whoever should set this as a standing rule." This surfaces both the conflict and the absence of a rule, rather than quietly picking a winner and letting the loser wonder why their slot moved.
