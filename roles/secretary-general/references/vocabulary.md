# Secretary/Administrative Assistant (General) — Vocabulary

### Single-principal vs. multi-principal support
The structural distinction between supporting one executive with the standing authority to set priority rules (single-principal, see `executive-administrative-assistant`) versus supporting several roughly-equal managers, none of whom individually has standing to arbitrate between the others' requests (multi-principal).
**In use:** "This role is multi-principal support — I can't just decide Sales beats Finance on my own authority, I have to surface the conflict to both."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a distinction without a difference from single-principal EA work; in practice it changes who gets to make the final call on a conflict.

### Tie-break rule
A standing, documented rule (e.g., "external client-facing work outranks internal work") that resolves a priority conflict without requiring a fresh negotiation each time it occurs.
**In use:** "We don't have a tie-break rule for recurring meeting slots yet, only for one-off task conflicts — that's why this keeps coming up."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to exist by default; in a multi-principal support role, no tie-break rule exists until someone with standing explicitly sets one.

### Hard vs. soft deadline
A hard deadline is fixed by something outside the requester's control (a client's meeting time, a regulatory filing date); a soft deadline is a target the requester set themselves and could, in principle, move.
**In use:** "Is Friday a hard deadline — is something happening Friday that this needs to exist for — or a soft one you picked because it felt like enough time?"
**Common misuse:** Every stated deadline treated as equally hard, which removes the information needed to resolve a genuine conflict by priority.

### Committed hours vs. available capacity
The running total of hours already promised against tasks in the queue, checked against the actual hours remaining in the period — the number that has to be checked *before* accepting a new request, not reconstructed after falling behind.
**In use:** "Committed hours are already at 4 of 8 for this week — accepting the Finance report as scoped would put us at 9, over capacity."
**Common misuse:** Tracked only informally ("I feel busy this week"), which fails to surface an overcommitment early enough to renegotiate scope or timing.

### Collision-and-proposal message
A single communication sent to all parties whose requests conflict, naming the specific collision and a proposed resolution, rather than a private decision announced to each party separately after the fact.
**In use:** "Instead of telling Ops their memo got bumped without context, I sent a collision-and-proposal message to all three leads explaining the capacity math."
**Common misuse:** Skipped in favor of quietly picking a winner, which looks arbitrary to whoever loses and erodes trust that requests are handled consistently.

### Deferred vs. dropped
A deferred task has an explicit new target date and appears in the queue with that date; a dropped task has neither — the same outcome from the requester's point of view unless the deferral is stated out loud.
**In use:** "The Ops memo isn't dropped, it's deferred to Monday — I told them that explicitly and it's in the queue with the new date."
**Common misuse:** A task quietly deprioritized without a stated return date, which functions as a silent cancellation even when no one intended it that way.

### Standing authority (thin form)
The narrower, often-unstated authority this role has to act without asking, distinct from a single-principal EA's explicitly delegated-authority boundary (a defined dollar limit, a defined set of pre-cleared decisions).
**In use:** "I don't have standing authority to decide whose deadline wins between two department heads — that's a decision for whoever supervises both of them."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to be equivalent to an EA's delegated authority, leading to unilateral priority decisions nobody actually authorized.

### Urgency-marking pattern
A requester's track record of how often their "urgent" label has matched an actual hard consequence for delay, used to calibrate how much weight to give the label going forward.
**In use:** "This manager's urgency-marking pattern runs about 30% actually time-critical — worth a quick clarifying question before reshuffling the queue for them."
**Common misuse:** Either always taken at face value (rewarding over-marking) or always discounted (missing the genuinely urgent 30%) instead of checked case by case.

### Queue visibility
The property that a commitment logged in a shared system can be seen by everyone affected by it, as opposed to existing only in the assistant's memory or a private notepad.
**In use:** "Queue visibility is the whole point — if a manager can't see what else is competing for my time, they can't help resolve the conflict."
**Common misuse:** A queue that exists but isn't actually shared or checked by the managers, which recreates the invisibility problem in a different tool.
