# Vocabulary

### Shift handoff
The formal transfer of operational awareness between an outgoing and incoming supervisor, covering open issues, current status, and anything the incoming shift needs to know that isn't otherwise self-evident.
**In use:** "Handoff covers three things: the quality hold on Line 2, the maintenance ticket on the press, and that Dana's out tomorrow — everything else is normal."
**Common misuse:** Treating handoff as a formality satisfied by a brief "everything's fine" when open issues actually exist — an issue not explicitly communicated is effectively invisible to the incoming supervisor, regardless of whether it's technically documented somewhere else.

### Escalation authority
The defined boundary of what a supervisor can resolve directly versus what requires involving another function (maintenance, quality, EHS) or a higher level of management.
**In use:** "This is within my escalation authority to pause the station — but the root-cause fix needs maintenance, so that's who I'm calling."
**Common misuse:** Treating escalation authority as a binary (either "handle everything yourself" or "escalate everything") rather than a calibrated judgment — the skill is correctly sorting which specific issues belong on which side of that line, not defaulting to one extreme.

### Normalization of deviance
The gradual process by which a deviation from standard procedure becomes accepted as normal because it repeatedly didn't cause a visible problem, eroding the safety or quality margin the standard was meant to preserve.
**In use:** "That shortcut's been running for weeks with no issue — that's exactly the pattern where normalization of deviance creeps in, worth correcting now rather than later."
**Common misuse:** Treating a shortcut's track record of no incident as evidence it's actually safe — absence of a problem so far doesn't confirm the original procedure was overly cautious, only that the additional factor needed to turn it into an incident hasn't occurred yet.

### Binding constraint
In a situation where multiple goals (production rate, quality, safety) conflict, the one goal that determines the actual limit on what can be done, which the others have to yield to.
**In use:** "Quality's the binding constraint here — we adjust the production rate around it, not the other way around."
**Common misuse:** Treating all competing priorities as equally flexible and looking for a compromise between them — when one goal (like safety) has a genuinely asymmetric downside, it functions as the actual constraint the others need to accommodate, not one input among equals.

### Near-miss
An event that could have resulted in an incident (injury, quality escape, equipment damage) but didn't, due to circumstance rather than a control that reliably prevents it.
**In use:** "Nobody got hurt, but that's a near-miss worth reporting — the conditions that almost caused an injury are still there."
**Common misuse:** Treating a near-miss as not worth reporting because the actual outcome was fine — the value of near-miss reporting is catching the underlying condition before luck runs out, which requires reporting it regardless of the actual (lucky) outcome.

### Line-level authority
The scope of decisions a first-line supervisor is authorized to make directly, without requiring sign-off from a higher level or specialized function.
**In use:** "Pausing this specific station is within my line-level authority — I don't need to call the plant manager for that."
**Common misuse:** Confusing line-level authority with line-level expertise — a supervisor may have the authority to pause a process but lack the technical expertise to diagnose its root cause, which is exactly the situation that calls for escalating the diagnosis while still exercising the authority to pause.

### Root cause vs. symptom
The underlying reason an issue occurred (root cause) versus its visible, immediate manifestation (symptom) — addressing only the symptom typically allows the issue to recur.
**In use:** "Replacing the worn pin fixed the root cause — just tightening the fixture bolts would've only masked the symptom for a shift or two."
**Common misuse:** Treating a symptom's resolution (the reject rate returning to normal) as confirmation the root cause was found, without verifying what was actually changed — a symptom can temporarily resolve on its own or from an unrelated adjustment without the actual cause being addressed.

### Pro-rata target
A production target adjusted to represent the expected cumulative output at a given point partway through a shift, used to track pace against the full-shift goal.
**In use:** "We're at 280 against a pro-rata target of 300 at hour five — six percent behind pace, not yet a crisis but worth watching."
**Common misuse:** Comparing partial-shift output only to the full-shift target rather than the pro-rata figure — that comparison always looks worse than actual pace and obscures whether the shift is genuinely behind or just not yet complete.

### Corrective coaching
Addressing a specific observed performance or compliance issue directly and promptly with the person involved, distinct from a general reminder or a delayed formal disciplinary process.
**In use:** "That's corrective coaching in the moment — I'm not writing him up, I'm telling him directly what I saw and what the standard is."
**Common misuse:** Substituting a vague general reminder to the whole team ("everybody be careful out there") for direct, specific correction with the individual involved — the general reminder doesn't actually address the specific behavior observed and rarely changes it.

### Quality hold
A formal status placed on product or a process step preventing it from advancing until an identified issue is investigated and resolved.
**In use:** "Sub-assembly station's on quality hold since 17:15 — nothing moves past that point until the fixture issue's confirmed fixed."
**Common misuse:** Treating a quality hold as equivalent to stopping the entire line — a hold can and often should be scoped narrowly to the specific station or process step actually implicated, preserving production continuity elsewhere while still containing the risk.
