# Vocabulary

### Camelot Wheel
Mixed In Key's harmonic-mixing numbering system that maps musical keys to a wheel of 1A–12A (minor) and 1B–12B (major), where adjacent numbers or the same number across letters are considered harmonically compatible.
**In use:** "That transition's an 8A into a 9A — one step around the wheel, it'll blend clean."
**Common misuse:** treating it as a hard rule rather than a tool — a "clash" is sometimes the exact effect a DJ wants for an energy reset, and rigid adherence flattens a set that needs a jolt.

### Phrasing
The 8/16/32-bar structural blocks most dance music is built from; transitions land cleanest on these boundaries.
**In use:** "Cut it on the next 32, not four bars early — you'll chop the drop."
**Common misuse:** assuming every genre phrases in tidy 32-bar blocks — a lot of live-instrumentation or non-four-on-the-floor material doesn't, and forcing the grid onto it produces worse transitions than reading it by ear.

### Open format
A DJ style/booking that moves across genres (hip-hop, house, pop, R&B, Latin) within one set based on the room, rather than staying inside a single genre.
**In use:** "It's an open-format wedding gig, not a house set — bring everything."
**Common misuse:** using it as a synonym for "no plan" — open format still requires genre-specific prep and transition skill in each direction, it's just not committed to one lane in advance.

### Beatmatching
Manually aligning the tempo and beat phase of two tracks so they play in sync, historically by ear and pitch fader, now often assisted by software sync.
**In use:** "Sync's fine for a wedding set, but I still beatmatch by ear when a track's grid is off."
**Common misuse:** treating sync-button use as evidence of lesser skill — the read and track selection are the actual skill being paid for; manual beatmatching is a fallback for when the software's beatgrid is wrong, not the whole job.

### Trainwreck
An intentionally rough, off-phrase or off-beat transition used deliberately for shock value or a hard reset, as distinct from a beginner's accidental bad mix.
**In use:** "That was a trainwreck on purpose — I needed the room to feel the reset."
**Common misuse:** assuming any rough transition is a mistake; a veteran distinguishes an intentional trainwreck from a blown mix by whether it served a specific energy goal.

### Crate-digging
Sourcing and vetting new or deep-catalog tracks ahead of a gig, historically from record crates, now largely digital pools and forums.
**In use:** "I spent Tuesday crate-digging for the opener — needed three more mid-tempo tracks that aren't overplayed."
**Common misuse:** confusing crate-digging with building a fixed set list; the digging stocks the library, it doesn't determine the order the tracks get played in.

### Gain staging
Setting signal levels at each stage of the audio chain (source trim, channel fader, master) so peaks stay clean without relying on a limiter to fix an already-clipped signal.
**In use:** "Master's clipping — check the channel trim before you touch the limiter."
**Common misuse:** riding the master fader to fix distortion that's actually originating upstream at the channel trim or an over-hot source file.

### Rider
A DJ's technical and hospitality requirements document sent to a venue or event planner ahead of a booking — power, sound system specs, table/booth placement, sometimes food/parking.
**In use:** "Send the venue my rider before load-in — they need to know we're bringing two 15-amp circuits' worth of gear."
**Common misuse:** assuming a rider is only for headline club acts; mobile DJs use a lighter version to prevent day-of power and space surprises.

### House DJ vs. mobile DJ
A house DJ plays a resident or guest slot at a fixed venue with its own sound system and clientele; a mobile DJ brings a full rig to one-off private events (weddings, corporate parties) and often takes on MC duties.
**In use:** "I do house gigs on weekends and mobile weddings for the day job — completely different prep."
**Common misuse:** assuming the skill set transfers one-to-one; house work rewards a distinct sound and set-building over a night, mobile work rewards contract execution, MC timing, and universal crowd-pleasing material.

### B2B (back-to-back)
Two DJs alternating tracks or short segments within a single set, as opposed to a solo set or a scheduled handoff between sets.
**In use:** "We're doing a B2B for the last hour — trade every two tracks."
**Common misuse:** confusing a B2B with a simple set handoff; a true B2B requires real-time coordination on key/BPM between two people, not just a clean starting point for the next DJ.

### Needle drop
Cueing and starting a track precisely at a specific point (not necessarily the beginning) — a legacy vinyl term still used with digital cueing.
**In use:** "Needle-drop it at the second verse, skip the slow intro."
**Common misuse:** assuming it only applies to vinyl; the term and the skill (precise cue-point selection) carry over directly to digital controllers and CDJs.

### Must-play / do-not-play list
The client-provided lists that override both the DJ's taste and the live floor read — must-plays are scheduled obligations, do-not-plays are standing prohibitions for the whole event.
**In use:** "Do-not-play list says no country — even if a table's begging for it, that's a hard no."
**Common misuse:** treating a must-play as a suggestion that can be swapped for something "better" for the floor, or treating a do-not-play as negotiable mid-event without the client's written sign-off.

### Certificate of insurance (COI)
A document from a DJ's liability insurer, naming a venue as additional insured, that many venues require before allowing load-in.
**In use:** "Venue wants a COI naming them as additional insured by Thursday or we lose the load-in slot."
**Common misuse:** assuming general liability coverage automatically extends to naming a third party — a COI naming the specific venue has to be requested from the insurer per event or per year, not assumed from a general policy.

### BPM ramp
A deliberate, gradual tempo change across several tracks (rather than one abrupt jump) used to shift a room's energy up or down without losing the floor.
**In use:** "We're ramping 100 to 122 over three tracks to bring the energy back without losing what's left on the floor."
**Common misuse:** confusing a BPM ramp with simply picking faster songs — the increments and the phrase-matched transitions between them are what keep dancers on the floor through the change, not the tempo numbers alone.
