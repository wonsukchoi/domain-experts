# Vocabulary

### Marginal fit
The gap between a dental restoration's edge and the prepared tooth surface it seats against.
**In use:** "Margin's reading 150 microns under the loupe — that's out of tolerance, not a cosmetic issue."
**Common misuse:** Judging margin fit by unaided visual inspection rather than measuring it under magnification against a specific micron-level threshold.

### Casting shrinkage (dental alloy)
The dimensional reduction a cast dental alloy undergoes cooling from molten to solid, requiring wax pattern compensation at tolerances far tighter than general jewelry or industrial casting.
**In use:** "This alloy shrinks 1.5% — build the wax to that, the die's exact dimension will cast undersized."
**Common misuse:** Applying a generic metal-casting shrinkage assumption without using the specific dental alloy's documented compensation data.

### Articulator
A mechanical device that holds upper and lower dental models in a fixed bite relationship, used to replicate a patient's jaw movement and occlusion during lab fabrication.
**In use:** "Mounted on the articulator per the bite registration — build occlusion to that, not to how it looks anatomically."
**Common misuse:** Sculpting a restoration's biting surface by eye for anatomical appearance without verifying it against the actual articulator-mounted bite relationship.

### Occlusion
How the biting surfaces of upper and lower teeth, or restorations, contact each other.
**In use:** "Occlusion's high on the mesial cusp — that'll need adjustment before this goes back to the dentist."
**Common misuse:** Treating occlusal anatomy purely as a cosmetic or shape decision, when the actual contact points and forces have to match the patient's specific recorded bite.

### Value (shade)
The relative lightness or darkness of a tooth shade, distinct from hue (the actual color) and chroma (color saturation).
**In use:** "Hue's close, but value's off — that's what's making this look mismatched from across the room."
**Common misuse:** Matching a shade by hue alone, missing that value mismatches are typically far more visually noticeable to an observer than hue differences.

### Die (dental)
A precise replica of a prepared tooth, poured or milled from an impression or scan, used as the working model for fabricating a restoration.
**In use:** "Die's got a chip at the margin from the pour — repour before we design off it."
**Common misuse:** Assuming any model derived from an impression is an accurate die, without inspecting it for defects introduced during pouring or milling.

### Coping
The underlying substructure of a crown — often metal or zirconia — onto which esthetic porcelain or other veneering material is applied.
**In use:** "Coping fit checks out at the margin — porcelain layering happens after that's confirmed, not before."
**Common misuse:** Confusing the coping's fit requirements with the finished crown's overall appearance requirements, when the coping's dimensional accuracy at the margin is what determines clinical fit regardless of the outer esthetic layer.

### Impression
A physical or digital capture of a patient's oral structures used to create an accurate model for fabrication.
**In use:** "This impression's got a void right at the margin — request a new one before pouring the model."
**Common misuse:** Assuming any submitted impression is inherently accurate, rather than inspecting it for voids, distortion, or pulled margins before building on top of it.

### Try-in
A stage where an in-progress or completed restoration is tested in the patient's mouth before final completion, catching fit and occlusion issues.
**In use:** "Try-in caught the high spot the lab-stage check missed — adjust before final cementation."
**Common misuse:** Treating try-in as a formality rather than as the actual verification step for issues (margin fit, occlusion) that lab-stage inspection alone can't fully guarantee.

### Base metal alloy vs. noble/high-noble alloy
Different classes of dental casting alloys with different shrinkage rates, cost, and biocompatibility properties.
**In use:** "This is a base metal alloy job — its shrinkage compensation is different from the noble alloy case we ran yesterday."
**Common misuse:** Assuming shrinkage compensation and casting parameters are interchangeable across alloy classes, when base metal and noble alloys behave meaningfully differently during casting.
