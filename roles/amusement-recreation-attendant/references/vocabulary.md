# Vocabulary

### Dispatch interval
The minimum time, in seconds, that must elapse between successive vehicle/boat/car releases on a ride, set by the manufacturer's O&M manual based on clearance zones (brake runs, splash zones, block sections) downstream.
**In use:** "We're running a 58-second interval against a 42-second floor — the gap is in loading, not the ride cycle itself."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a throughput dial to shorten when a line is long, rather than a fixed safety parameter that can only be approached, never crossed.

### Go/no-go gauge
A physical device (often a ring, bar, or template) used to test whether a restraint has closed within its certified tolerance — pass or fail, no in-between reading.
**In use:** "The gauge didn't seat flush, so it's a no-go regardless of how it looked from the platform."
**Common misuse:** Treated as advisory ("it's close enough") rather than binary; also skipped in favor of a visual estimate under time pressure.

### Restraint envelope
The full set of size, shape, and positioning conditions under which a restraint is certified to hold a rider safely — not just "does it latch," but latches within the range the engineering was tested for.
**In use:** "He's within the height range but outside the restraint envelope because of how the harness sits on his frame — that's still a no-ride."
**Common misuse:** Collapsed to a single height-chart number, ignoring that weight distribution, torso shape, and clothing all affect whether the certified envelope is actually met.

### Block zone
A section of ride track or path that can only contain one vehicle at a time, enforced by the control system to prevent rear-end contact between vehicles.
**In use:** "The lift hill has three block zones — if a car stops in block two, nothing behind it can advance until it clears."
**Common misuse:** Confused with "the whole ride," when it's a specific, enumerated segment with its own sensor and rule.

### O&M manual
The ride's Operations & Maintenance manual, provided by the manufacturer and required documentation under ASTM F770 — the actual source of dispatch intervals, restraint procedures, wind limits, and maximum hold times for that specific model.
**In use:** "Don't guess the wind limit — it's in the O&M manual for this specific inflatable unit, not a park-wide number."
**Common misuse:** Treated as interchangeable across rides of the "same type" when limits are frequently unit- and manufacturer-specific.

### NAARSO Level I/II/III
The National Association of Amusement Ride Safety Officials' tiered certification: Level I covers ride operations, Level II and III (Advanced/Senior) cover inspection, each requiring continuing education (32 hours per 2-year cycle at Level II/III) to retain.
**In use:** "She's Level I certified to operate, but a structural question like this goes to a Level III inspector or the manufacturer, not to me."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to be a single blanket "safety certified" credential rather than a specific, scoped authority that stops at operations vs. inspection vs. engineering.

### Fixed-site vs. mobile ride
The regulatory distinction that determines federal jurisdiction: fixed-site (permanent) amusement parks are exempt from CPSC oversight under the Consumer Product Safety Act and regulated by state law instead; mobile/traveling rides (carnivals, fairs) remain under CPSC.
**In use:** "This is a fixed-site park, so the inspection cadence is whatever [state]'s ride-safety statute requires, not a federal standard."
**Common misuse:** Assumed that "amusement rides are federally regulated" applies uniformly — it does not, and the gap is a real, frequently cited industry fact, not a technicality.

### 30-30 rule
The NOAA/National Weather Service lightning-safety guideline: hold or evacuate outdoor activity if thunder is heard within 30 seconds of a lightning flash, and don't resume until 30 minutes after the last thunder or strike.
**In use:** "We're at minute 12 of the hold — sky's clear, but the timer resets every time thunder's heard, so we wait it out."
**Common misuse:** Shortened based on visible sky conditions clearing, rather than strictly timed from the last thunder/strike.

### Walk-test
A full, unoccupied test cycle of a ride run after any e-stop activation, mechanical adjustment, or extended downtime, before any paying rider reboards.
**In use:** "E-stop tripped on cycle 340 — full walk-test before we load anyone else, no exceptions."
**Common misuse:** Skipped or abbreviated ("it looked fine on the monitor") after a routine-seeming stop, treating the walk-test as optional rather than the standard restart gate.

### Sizing station
A gate, template, or measuring device used to check a guest's height (and sometimes weight or arm reach) against an attraction's posted minimum/maximum before entry to the queue, not just at the boarding platform.
**In use:** "Catch the height check at the sizing station before they've waited 40 minutes in line, not at the platform."
**Common misuse:** Only enforced at the final boarding point, which wastes guest time and creates pressure to wave through a borderline case rather than turn them away earlier and more gently.

### Flat store / posted-odds statute
State-level regulation governing the payout structure of carnival/arcade games of chance — some states require posted win odds, cap difficulty, or ban certain rigged-difficulty ("flat store") game configurations outright.
**In use:** "That ring toss has to run this season's approved bottle spacing — the posted-odds card on the booth is what we're audited against."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a suggestion an attendant can adjust for a frustrated player, rather than a licensing condition the booth operates under.

### Redemption vs. game of chance
The legal distinction between coin-op games that award tickets/points redeemable for prizes regardless of skill (redemption, often more tightly regulated) and games whose outcome depends on player skill (generally less regulated).
**In use:** "That claw machine is redemption-classified in this state, which is why the payout rate is logged separately from the ring toss."
**Common misuse:** Assumed all coin-operated games fall under one regulatory bucket, when classification (and therefore the rules that apply) depends on the specific mechanic.

### Theoretical vs. measured capacity
Theoretical (rated) capacity is the manufacturer's stated maximum guests/hour at the ride's minimum dispatch interval running with zero friction; measured capacity is what the ride is actually delivering today, including real loading time.
**In use:** "Rated capacity is 800/hr, but we're measuring 620 today because loading is the bottleneck, not the ride cycle."
**Common misuse:** Rated capacity quoted to guests or management as if it were the current, achievable number.

### Incident log
The shift's contemporaneous written record of holds, declines, e-stops, and anomalies — time-stamped, factual, filed the same shift, not reconstructed later from memory.
**In use:** "It's in the log at 2:14 — restraint fail, seat 3, guest declined and rerouted to guest services."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a punitive paper trail to avoid creating, rather than the record that protects the attendant and the park when a decision is later questioned.
