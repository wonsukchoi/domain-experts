# Playbook

Operational sequences an attendant actually runs, filled with plausible example numbers. Adapt the numbers to the specific ride/attraction's O&M manual — never carry these numbers over to a different attraction.

## 1. Pre-shift checkout (ride attraction)

Run before the first dispatch of the day, every day, regardless of yesterday's checkout.

1. **Empty-car test cycle** — run the ride through one full cycle with no riders, watching and listening for anything off-nominal (unusual sound, vibration, hesitation at a block zone, restraint that doesn't fully cycle). Log the cycle count at start of shift.
2. **Restraint function check** — cycle every seat's restraint through full lock and full release at least once; note any seat that doesn't lock cleanly or release with resistance. A seat that fails either direction is taken out of service (block it, don't just "load it last").
3. **Sizing/gauge calibration spot-check** — confirm the go/no-go gauge itself reads correctly against a known reference (many parks keep a marked calibration block for this).
4. **E-stop and communication check** — test the e-stop panel and confirm radio contact with the lead operator/control booth before the first paying rider boards.
5. **Weather briefing** — check the shift's forecast and any active lightning-detection alerts before opening, not after the first hold.
6. **Sign-off** — checkout sheet initialed with time, cycle count, and any seat/component taken out of service, filed before dispatch begins.

**Rule:** any item that fails gets logged and escalated before the ride opens, even if it seems minor and even if it will "probably resolve itself once it's running." Nothing gets carried as a verbal note.

## 2. Restraint/fit go/no-go decision

Applies to any ride with a size, weight, or restraint-fit requirement (drop towers, coasters with lap bars, tall/fast rides).

1. Guest presents at the gauge or the seat.
2. Run the check exactly as specified in the O&M manual — a gauge that must close and latch, or a documented manual pressure/click check. No visual "eyeballing" substitutes for the specified check.
3. **Pass:** board normally.
4. **Fail:** state the rule plainly ("the restraint needs to close fully for this ride, and it's not — I can't let you ride this one"), offer the resolution path (refund for that ride's cost if single-ticket, a reride voucher, or redirect to an unrestricted attraction), and move the line along. Do not re-run the same check hoping for a different result unless the guest has physically repositioned or removed an obstructing item (bag, loose clothing) — in which case one recheck is reasonable, not repeated rechecks.
5. Log the decline in the shift log if the park's policy requires it (most do, for pattern-tracking across shifts) — time, ride, general reason category (not the guest's identifying details beyond what's required).

## 3. Weather hold

1. **Trigger:** thunder heard within 30 seconds of a visible lightning flash (~6 miles), or the park's lightning-detection system alerts — whichever comes first. Do not wait for both.
2. **Immediate action:** halt dispatch/loading at the attraction; if riders are already in a cycle in progress, follow the ride-specific evacuation procedure rather than stranding them mid-cycle for the full storm.
3. **Clear queue to designated shelter** — communicate the reason briefly ("weather hold, safety requirement") rather than a vague "temporarily closed" that invites arguing.
4. **Restart timer:** 30 minutes from the most recent thunder/strike, reset to zero if thunder recurs within that window. A visually clearing sky does not shorten the timer.
5. **Reopen checklist:** re-run the empty-car test cycle before the first paying rider reboards, same as a pre-shift checkout.

## 4. On-ride evacuation (mechanical stop mid-cycle)

1. **Confirm rider status via radio/visual** before doing anything else — injuries change the entire response (medical protocol activates first).
2. **Communicate to riders immediately**, even before the cause is known: what's happening, that it's a precaution, and roughly how long a fix will take if known. Silence is what turns a stopped ride into a panic.
3. **Do not attempt a mechanical fix or restart** outside NAARSO Level I authority — that's maintenance's call. The attendant's job is rider safety and communication during the stop, not diagnosis.
4. **Evacuate per the ride-specific procedure** if the stop will exceed the ride's documented maximum hold time (commonly 15–20 minutes depending on ride type and weather exposure) or if a rider reports distress — don't wait for a fixed universal clock; follow the specific manual.
5. **Full incident report** — time down, time evacuation began/ended, weather conditions, rider count, any injuries, who was notified and when. Filed before end of shift, not "later."

## 5. Queue throughput recalculation (wait-time complaint)

1. **Pull the current measured dispatch interval** (not the manufacturer's theoretical rated capacity — actual measured seconds between dispatches, timed over at least 10 cycles).
2. **Pull the O&M-manual minimum interval** for that ride.
3. **If measured > floor:** the gap is the addressable lever. Identify what's serial in the load process (one attendant checking all seats, one attendant per queue lane, single-file loading) and whether adding staff or reorganizing the load sequence can close the gap toward — never below — the floor.
4. **If measured = floor already:** staffing changes will not move throughput. The only levers left are queue population (shorten the physical line, cap entry) or total ride capacity (a second train/boat, a second unit) — both are decisions above the attendant's authority to make alone, but the attendant's job is to say so with the numbers, not to shave the floor to look responsive.
5. **Report with reconciling numbers**, not a vibe: current interval, floor, current throughput, proposed throughput, and the resulting wait-time change — see the SKILL.md worked example for the exact format.

## 6. Game booth / coin-op attraction shift

1. **Verify posted odds/payout structure matches house procedure** before opening — for any game with state-regulated odds ("flat store" or posted-odds statutes vary by state), confirm the booth is running the current approved structure, not a prior season's.
2. **Track prize payout against expected rate** across the shift (most booths have a target win rate, e.g., roughly 1 in 8 to 1 in 12 plays for a standard midway game) — a rate wildly outside that band in either direction gets flagged to a supervisor, not adjusted unilaterally.
3. **No off-procedure "extra chances"** regardless of how a frustrated or high-spending player pushes — this is a compliance boundary, not a service call.
4. **Cash/ticket reconciliation at shift change** — counted with the incoming attendant present, discrepancies logged immediately, not carried to "sort out later."
