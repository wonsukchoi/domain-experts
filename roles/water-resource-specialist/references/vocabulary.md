# Working vocabulary

Terms of art practitioners use precisely, that generalists misuse. Load this when writing or reviewing content that needs to sound like an actual water resource specialist, not a generalist describing one.

### Prior appropriation
A water-rights doctrine ("first in time, first in right") where the oldest established water right has priority over newer rights during a shortage, common in many western US states (framework varies by jurisdiction).
**In use:** "Their priority date is 1962 — they get their full allocation before the 1994-priority-date users see a drop during a shortage."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to mean water is shared proportionally during a shortage — under strict prior appropriation, senior rights are satisfied in full before junior rights receive anything, which can mean junior users face severe or total curtailment while senior users see no reduction.

### Environmental/ecological flow
A minimum flow requirement maintained in a waterway to sustain aquatic ecosystems, often legally mandated and treated as a binding constraint on total allocatable water, not a discretionary use.
**In use:** "The ecological flow requirement comes off the top before we allocate anything else — it's not competing with municipal and ag demand, it's a prior constraint on the total."
**Common misuse:** Treated as one more use competing for the same pool of water alongside municipal/agricultural/industrial demand — in many regulatory frameworks it's legally binding and applied as a constraint before other allocations, not as a competing claim of equal negotiability.

### Conjunctive use / management
Coordinated management of surface water and groundwater as a connected system, recognizing that use of one affects availability of the other in hydrologically connected basins.
**In use:** "This basin needs conjunctive management — groundwater pumping here measurably reduces baseflow to the river within two seasons."
**Common misuse:** Surface water and groundwater rights/permitting treated as entirely separate administrative systems even in basins with documented physical connectivity, missing that pumping decisions in one affect the other.

### Water balance
An accounting of inflows, outflows, and storage change in a water system over a period, used to check whether allocated/planned uses are sustainable given actual supply.
**In use:** "The water balance closes fine against average yield but shows a 37,000 acre-foot deficit in the drought scenario — that's the number that matters for planning."
**Common misuse:** Calculated only against average conditions — a water balance that "closes" on average tells you little about reliability during the scenario (drought) that actually stresses the system.

### Acre-foot (AF)
A standard unit of water volume in US water resource management — the volume covering one acre to a depth of one foot (~325,851 gallons), used because it scales naturally to irrigation and reservoir volumes.
**In use:** "The reservoir holds 40,000 acre-feet at full capacity — roughly a year's municipal demand for a city this size."
**Common misuse:** Confused with smaller everyday water units (gallons) in casual conversion without checking scale — an error of a factor of 1,000 or more is easy to make when mentally converting between acre-feet and gallons without care.

### Drought contingency plan
A pre-arranged plan (often including voluntary sharing agreements, conservation triggers, and backup supply activation) specifying how a system responds at defined drought severity stages, prepared before a drought occurs.
**In use:** "Stage 3 of the drought contingency plan triggers mandatory conservation targets and activates the groundwater bank withdrawal agreement."
**Common misuse:** Improvised during an actual drought rather than pre-arranged — a plan negotiated and agreed upon in advance (including with senior rights holders) is far more reliable and less contentious than trying to negotiate sharing arrangements in the middle of an active shortage.

### Curtailment
The legally mandated reduction or cessation of water diversion for junior right holders during a shortage, administered according to the priority system.
**In use:** "Curtailment notices go out to all rights junior to the 1988 priority date once the river drops below the compact-required flow."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to be a negotiated or gradual process — curtailment under strict priority administration is typically a binary cutoff by priority date once triggered, not a proportional reduction, which is why advance communication of the priority hierarchy matters so much.

### Yield (basin/system)
The sustainable quantity of water a basin or system can reliably produce, distinct from total precipitation or runoff, and properly qualified by the hydrologic scenario it's calculated against (average, dry-year, drought sequence).
**In use:** "Average yield is 95,000 AF, but drought-scenario yield is 58,000 — planning has to use the second number, not the first."
**Common misuse:** Cited as a single number without specifying which hydrologic scenario it reflects — "yield" without a stated scenario basis is ambiguous and can make a system look more reliable than a drought-scenario analysis would show.
