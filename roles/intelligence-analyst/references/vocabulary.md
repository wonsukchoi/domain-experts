# Intelligence Analyst — Vocabulary

### Analysis of Competing Hypotheses (ACH)
A structured technique that scores every piece of evidence against every plausible hypothesis for consistency, then selects the hypothesis with the fewest disqualifying inconsistencies rather than the most confirming evidence.
**In use:** "Before we brief this up, I want a full ACH matrix — not just the case for the theory we already like."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a formality done after the analyst has already decided, producing a matrix that's reverse-engineered to support the preferred hypothesis rather than genuinely tested against alternatives.

### Admiralty code (source reliability / information credibility)
A two-axis grading system (A–F for source reliability, 1–6 for information credibility) that keeps how much you trust a source separate from how much you believe a specific claim.
**In use:** "That's a B2 — usually reliable source, but this particular claim is only probably true, not confirmed."
**Common misuse:** Collapsing the two axes into one "reliable" or "unreliable" gut call, or letting a source's good track record automatically upgrade an unconfirmed specific claim.

### BLUF (Bottom Line Up Front)
An intelligence-writing convention placing the assessment and confidence level in the first sentence, before supporting reasoning.
**In use:** "Lead with the BLUF — decision-makers reading this at 2am need the conclusion before the methodology."
**Common misuse:** Writing narrative-first, conclusion-last, which buries the actionable finding under paragraphs of context a time-pressured reader won't get to.

### Confidence language
Standardized phrasing (low/moderate/high confidence) describing the quality and corroboration of the analytic process behind an assessment — not the probability that the underlying event occurred.
**In use:** "High confidence here means three independently-sourced B-or-better reports corroborate it, not that I'm 90% certain."
**Common misuse:** Used interchangeably with a numeric probability estimate, or applied uniformly across a report regardless of how well each specific claim is actually corroborated.

### Diagnostic value (of evidence)
How much a specific piece of evidence actually helps discriminate between competing hypotheses — evidence equally consistent with every hypothesis has zero diagnostic value even if it "confirms" the leading one.
**In use:** "That witness statement is consistent with all three hypotheses, so it's not diagnostic — don't count it as support for H1."
**Common misuse:** Counting non-diagnostic evidence as if it strengthens the leading hypothesis, inflating apparent confidence without actually narrowing the field.

### Betweenness centrality
A network-analysis measure of how often a node lies on the shortest path between other node pairs — high betweenness indicates a structural bridge or facilitator role, independent of how many direct connections the node has.
**In use:** "Node F only has three connections, but its betweenness is the highest in the network — that's your facilitator, not the guy with nine contacts."
**Common misuse:** Confused with degree centrality (raw connection count), leading analysts to target the most "popular" node in a chart rather than the structurally critical one.

### Mirror-imaging
The analytic bias of assuming a subject will reason or act the way the analyst would in the same situation, rather than testing that assumption against known evidence about the subject.
**In use:** "We're mirror-imaging here — assuming he'd flee because we would. What does his actual pattern of behavior say?"
**Common misuse:** Rarely named explicitly in the moment; it shows up as an unstated assumption baked into a hypothesis rather than a flagged bias.

### Devil's advocacy / structured dissent
A formal step assigning someone to argue the strongest case against the emerging consensus hypothesis before an assessment is finalized.
**In use:** "Consensus formed fast on this one — I want a formal devil's-advocacy pass before it goes out."
**Common misuse:** Skipped under time pressure, or performed as a token exercise where the assigned dissenter doesn't seriously engage with the counter-case.

### Base rate
How frequently an event or pattern actually occurs in the relevant population, used to calibrate how much weight a pattern-match should carry.
**In use:** "The MO matches, but the base rate for this pattern is high enough that pattern alone gives us maybe 30% precision — we need more."
**Common misuse:** Omitted entirely from pattern-based assessments, letting a compelling-looking match carry more weight than its actual predictive value supports.

### Fusion center
A state or regional facility that aggregates and analyzes intelligence from multiple law-enforcement and public-safety agencies to support a shared operational picture.
**In use:** "Push this assessment to the fusion center so surrounding jurisdictions get the pattern alert."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to be a single unified database rather than a collaborative analysis function — data-sharing agreements and jurisdictional limits still apply.

### RFI (Request for Information)
A formal, specific request to close a named gap in the evidence supporting an assessment, tied to a particular hypothesis or evidence cell.
**In use:** "I've got an open RFI on the device forensics — that's the only thing keeping this at moderate instead of high confidence."
**Common misuse:** Used as a vague "gather more information" request with no specific analytic gap identified, which produces more data without resolving the actual uncertainty.

### Link chart / association chart
A visual diagram showing connections (calls, transactions, addresses) between entities in an investigation.
**In use:** "The link chart shows contact, not conspiracy — we still need to establish what that contact actually involved."
**Common misuse:** Treated as evidence of criminal association by itself, when it only documents that a connection of some kind exists.

### Corroboration
Independent confirmation of a claim from a source or method different from the one that originally reported it.
**In use:** "We've got corroboration now — the phone data confirms what the informant told us independently."
**Common misuse:** Confused with repetition — the same source restating a claim, or two sources who got their information from the same original point, isn't independent corroboration.
