# Operating Engineer — Vocabulary

### Working radius
The horizontal distance from a crane's center of rotation to the load's hook, measured at the point in the lift being evaluated — not a fixed number for the whole lift, since it changes as the boom swings or extends.
**In use:** "Working radius at the pick point is 25 ft, but it's 35 ft once we swing clear of the parapet — chart the 35."
**Common misuse:** Treating the crane-to-target ground distance as the working radius, when the actual radius at the moment the load is heaviest on the chart can be significantly larger once obstructions or set-down geometry are accounted for.

### Net capacity
Chart-listed gross capacity at a given radius and configuration, minus the weight of everything rigged below the hook (block, ball, slings, spreader bar, attachments).
**In use:** "Gross is 24,000 at this radius, but net after the block and spreader is 23,450 — that's the number to compare against the load."
**Common misuse:** Comparing the load weight directly to gross chart capacity, silently eating the margin the chart implies is available.

### Tail swing
The distance the rear of an excavator's or similar machine's house extends beyond its undercarriage/track footprint when the upper structure rotates; "zero tail swing" machines are designed to keep the house within the track width.
**In use:** "Conventional tail swing on this machine — give it clearance on the back side too, not just where the bucket's working."
**Common misuse:** Judging clearance only on the side the bucket or boom is actively working, missing that the rear of the house can swing into a fixed object or person on the opposite side.

### Tolerance zone
The band either side of a marked utility locate line (commonly 18–24 in., varies by jurisdiction) within which the buried facility's true position is uncertain, requiring hand exposure before mechanized excavation.
**In use:** "We're inside the tolerance zone on that gas line mark — pothole it before the trencher goes anywhere near it."
**Common misuse:** Treating the painted mark as the exact centerline of the utility and cutting mechanically right up to it, when the mark only guarantees the line is somewhere within the stated band.

### Locate ticket
The formal 811/one-call request that triggers utility owners to mark or clear their facilities in a work area, valid for a defined window before it needs to be refreshed.
**In use:** "That ticket's past its validity window — get a new one before we open any more ground on this phase."
**Common misuse:** Continuing to dig against an old ticket's marks once the job has run past the stated validity window, when faded paint or since-installed facilities are no longer reliably represented.

### Benchmark (grade control)
A known, verified elevation reference point that a laser or GPS grade-control system is set from; every subsequent grade reading is only as accurate as the last confirmed benchmark check.
**In use:** "Spot-check came back 0.2 ft off — re-verify the benchmark before we cut any more of this run."
**Common misuse:** Trusting a grade-control readout without periodically re-checking it against the benchmark, letting a bumped or shifted reference propagate a consistent error across an entire cut before anyone measures it.

### Rough grade / finish grade
Sequential stages of earthwork precision — rough grade (subgrade prep, commonly ±0.1 ft tolerance) establishes bulk elevation; finish grade (under slab or flatwork, a tighter tolerance, commonly a fraction of an inch) is the final surface other trades build directly on.
**In use:** "We're at rough grade tolerance now — finish grade under that slab needs to be inside a quarter inch, not a tenth of a foot."
**Common misuse:** Applying rough-grade tolerance expectations to a finish-grade pass, producing a surface within earthwork spec but out of tolerance for the trade building on top of it.

### Exclusion zone (swing radius)
The barricaded or otherwise physically/communicated boundary sized to a machine's maximum working radius plus its counterweight swing plus a buffer, inside which ground personnel are excluded during rotation or travel.
**In use:** "Exclusion zone's set to the full swing plus counterweight clearance — nobody crosses that tape without the signal person's go-ahead."
**Common misuse:** Sizing the exclusion zone to the visible working side of the machine only, leaving the counterweight or rear house swing unbarricaded on the side that looks clear.

### Signal person
A person qualified under the applicable hand-signal standard to direct crane or equipment movement when the operator's view of the load or path is obstructed, communicating by confirmed hand signal or radio.
**In use:** "Operator can't see the set point from the cab — signal person's got the sightline and the radio."
**Common misuse:** Treating a signal person's role as advisory or optional once a crew is familiar with each other, skipping the confirmed acknowledgment step before a blind move.

### 85% rule
An industry rule of thumb of working a lift at or under roughly 85% of net chart capacity as a standing margin on top of the chart's own built-in safety factor.
**In use:** "Net capacity's 20,450 and the load's 14,000 — that's 68%, comfortably under our 85% working target."
**Common misuse:** Using the 85% figure as a substitute for reading the chart at the correct working radius, rather than as an added buffer applied after the correct net-capacity number has already been established.

### On outriggers / on rubber
The two distinct load-chart configurations for a mobile crane — outriggers fully extended and the crane leveled (higher capacity, stationary), versus supported on its tires (lower capacity, can travel while carrying a load).
**In use:** "That capacity's an on-outriggers number — we're picking on rubber today, pull the on-rubber chart instead."
**Common misuse:** Reading capacity from the on-outriggers chart while outriggers are only partially extended, or assuming partial extension earns partial credit toward full on-outriggers capacity.

### Pothole (utility)
To expose a buried utility by hand digging or vacuum excavation at a specific point, used to confirm the actual location and depth of a line before mechanized excavation proceeds nearby.
**In use:** "Pothole it at the tolerance-zone edges before the auger goes anywhere close to that line."
**Common misuse:** Skipping the pothole step when a locate mark "looks confident," when the mark's accuracy doesn't depend on how clean or certain the paint line looks.
