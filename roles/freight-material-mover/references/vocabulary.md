# Vocabulary

Terms generalists conflate or oversimplify — practitioners keep them precise because axle-legality, securement, and dock-safety decisions depend on it.

## Bridge Formula (Federal Bridge Gross Weight Formula)

The federal formula (23 CFR 658.17) setting maximum allowable weight on a group of axles based on the number of axles and the distance between them, layered on top of the flat per-axle limits (20,000 lb single, 34,000 lb tandem) and the 80,000 lb gross combination weight limit. It exists to prevent axle or bridge-span overload even when total vehicle weight is legal.

**In use:** "Gross is fine at 61,000, but run the Bridge Formula against the axle spacing before you sign off — that's a separate check."

**Common misuse:** treating the 80,000 lb gross limit as the only number that matters, when a legal gross weight can still fail the per-axle-group or Bridge Formula check.

## Axle weight distribution

How a trailer's total load divides across its steer axle, drive-tandem axle group, and trailer-tandem axle group based on where freight sits along the bed relative to the kingpin and tandem centerline — as opposed to gross combination weight, which is the simple total.

**In use:** "Same 30,000 lb of freight, two different tandem numbers — it's all about where those skids sit on the bed, not how much they weigh."

**Common misuse:** assuming a legal gross weight implies a legal axle distribution, when the two are checked independently and can diverge on the same load.

## Working load limit (WLL)

The maximum load a tiedown, chain, strap, or blocking device is rated to hold in normal service — marked by the manufacturer and distinct from the device's breaking strength, which is a much higher, non-usable number.

**In use:** "That strap's WLL is 3,335 lb — don't quote me its breaking strength, that's not the number the securement rule checks against."

**Common misuse:** citing a tiedown's breaking strength or "rated at" marketing figure instead of its actual working load limit when sizing securement.

## Aggregate WLL / 50% rule

The cargo-securement requirement (49 CFR 393.106) that the sum of the working load limits of all tiedowns securing an article (or group of articles secured as a unit) equal at least 50% of that article's weight. It is one of two independent securement checks, not the whole rule.

**In use:** "Aggregate WLL clears the 50% rule at 9,400 against a 2,500 lb requirement — now check the count rule separately."

**Common misuse:** treating the 50%-of-weight math as sufficient on its own, when the minimum tiedown-count rule can still fail even after the WLL math passes.

## Minimum tiedown count

The separate cargo-securement requirement (49 CFR 393.110) setting a minimum number of tiedowns by article length — roughly one per 10 ft of article length with a floor of two tiedowns per article — regardless of how far a single high-capacity tiedown's WLL exceeds the weight-based requirement.

**In use:** "One chain's WLL covers the weight fine, but this skid still needs a second tiedown to meet the count minimum."

**Common misuse:** assuming one sufficiently rated tiedown satisfies securement because it clears the aggregate-WLL math, without checking the count-by-length rule.

## Blocking and bracing

Physical structures (dunnage, load bars, built-in trailer bulkheads, E-track-mounted braces) placed against freight to prevent movement, distinct from tiedowns, which restrain freight via tension rather than direct physical contact. Irregular freight without a self-interlocking shape typically needs both.

**In use:** "Friction and shrink-wrap held it standing still — it needs actual blocking against that bulkhead before it sees a hard stop."

**Common misuse:** treating tight stacking or wrap as equivalent to blocking and bracing, when neither resists the forward-deceleration force a hard brake actually applies.

## Coupling multiplier (CM)

The NIOSH lifting-equation factor scoring how good a lifter's grip on the load is — near 1.0 for a load with cutout handholds or handles, meaningfully lower (commonly ~0.90 or worse) for a load with no handholds, a slick surface, or an awkward round profile like a drum. It lowers the Recommended Weight Limit independent of the load's actual weight.

**In use:** "Same 40 lb, but this drum's coupling is worse than the cased good we usually handle — the LI comes out higher at the identical weight."

**Common misuse:** assuming coupling quality is roughly the same across freight types, when irregular freight without handholds routinely scores meaningfully worse than uniform cartons at an identical weight.

## Tandem slide

The mechanism letting a trailer's rear (tandem) axle group be repositioned forward or backward along the frame, changing the kingpin-to-tandem span and, with it, the axle-weight split — used to correct an axle-distribution problem without re-loading the freight itself.

**In use:** "Slide the tandem back four inches instead of unloading and re-stacking — that shifts enough weight off the drive axles to clear the limit."

**Common misuse:** treating a slide adjustment as complete once the axle math looks right, without physically confirming the slide pins are fully seated before the trailer moves.

## Dock leveler / dockboard rated capacity

The maximum combined weight (equipment plus load) a dock leveler or portable dockboard is rated to carry while bridging the dock-to-trailer gap, marked per ANSI/MH14.1 — a separate property from whether the leveler's lock is currently engaged.

**In use:** "Rated capacity covers the loaded pallet jack fine — now confirm the lock actually engaged before you cross it."

**Common misuse:** treating "rated capacity is sufficient" and "the lock is engaged" as the same check, when a leveler can be correctly rated and still unlocked.

## Lock engagement (dock leveler)

The state in which a dock leveler's mechanical or hydraulic lock (lip lock, safety leg, or maintenance strut) has actually seated, as opposed to the leveler merely appearing stable because no weight has tested it yet. Verified by a visual indicator or audible click specific to the unit, not inferred from the plate not moving under foot traffic.

**In use:** "It looks steady standing on it, but confirm the lock clicked before the forklift crosses — those aren't the same test."

**Common misuse:** inferring lock engagement from apparent stability under light foot traffic instead of confirming the unit's actual lock indicator.

## LTL vs. FTL

**LTL** (less-than-truckload) consolidates multiple shippers' freight into one trailer, usually across several stops, which makes delivery sequence (last-on-first-off) a real constraint on load layout. **FTL** (full-truckload) moves one shipper's freight to typically one destination, removing the sequencing constraint and leaving axle balance as the primary layout driver.

**In use:** "This is an LTL run — the load plan has to satisfy the stop order and the axle balance at the same time, not just the axle balance."

**Common misuse:** applying an FTL load-planning approach (pure axle-balance optimization) to an LTL run without accounting for the door-access sequencing that multi-stop delivery requires.

## NMFC freight class

The National Motor Freight Classification density- and handling-based rating system (classes roughly 50–500) used to price and route LTL freight, reflecting density, stowability, handling difficulty, and liability — an input to how LTL freight is quoted and grouped, not a substitute for the physical axle or securement plan.

**In use:** "It's rated class 250 for handling difficulty — that tells the pricing desk something, but it doesn't tell you how to block it."

**Common misuse:** treating freight class as a proxy for how a piece should be blocked, braced, or positioned, when those decisions come from the piece's actual weight, shape, and void space, not its rating class.
