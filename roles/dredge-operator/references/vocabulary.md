# Vocabulary

Terms generalists conflate or misuse. Each: definition, a sentence a practitioner would actually say using it, and the common misuse.

## Project depth vs. controlling depth

**Project depth** is the contracted, authorized minimum depth a channel must maintain. **Controlling depth** is the shallowest surveyed point across a given channel cross-section — the number that actually governs what a vessel can safely draft through, regardless of how deep the rest of the cross-section is.

*Practitioner usage:* "Average depth across that cell is fine, but the controlling depth is 44.2 — that's the number that fails us."

*Common misuse:* reporting an average or typical surveyed depth as evidence of compliance, when the binding number is the shallowest point, not the average.

## Paid (allowable) overdepth vs. advance maintenance depth

**Paid overdepth** is a contracted tolerance beyond project depth, compensating for survey and dredge-positioning uncertainty — it's billable but bounded. **Advance maintenance depth** is additional depth dredged on purpose to extend the interval before the next maintenance cycle, a scheduling and economics decision, not an uncertainty margin.

*Practitioner usage:* "The extra two feet on this reach is advance maintenance, not overdepth — we planned for it, it's not a positioning fudge."

*Common misuse:* treating either allowance as free extra yardage to chase for margin, rather than the specific purpose each one serves in the contract.

## Cutterhead vs. hopper vs. mechanical dredge

A **cutterhead** dredge uses a rotating cutter and centrifugal pump to move loose sediment through a pipeline. A **hopper (TSHD)** dredge is self-propelled, loads sediment into an onboard hopper via trailing suction, and transits to discharge it at the disposal site. A **mechanical** dredge (clamshell/bucket or backhoe) lifts material directly with no water added.

*Practitioner usage:* "That reach is debris and old riprap — we're not putting a cutterhead through it, mechanical only."

*Common misuse:* selecting dredge type by whichever plant is already mobilized rather than by the sediment and disposal-path requirements of the specific reach.

## Bulk sediment chemistry vs. bioassay vs. bioaccumulation test

**Bulk sediment chemistry** measures contaminant concentration in the sediment itself against a reference envelope — a screening step, not a disposal decision by itself. A **bioassay** (typically 10-day acute) exposes a test organism to the sediment and measures survival against a reference control. A **bioaccumulation test** (typically 28-day) measures whether contaminants accumulate in tissue, used when a capping or beneficial-use alternative is being evaluated after a bioassay failure.

*Practitioner usage:* "Bulk chemistry alone doesn't fail this unit — it triggers the bioassay, and the bioassay is what actually decides disposal eligibility."

*Common misuse:* treating a bulk chemistry exceedance itself as proof the material must go to confined disposal, skipping the bioassay tier that's the actual determining test.

## ODMDS vs. CDF

An **ODMDS** (ocean dredged material disposal site) is a designated open-water site authorized under MPRSA §103 for material that passes sediment testing. A **CDF** (confined disposal facility) is an engineered containment cell — diked, upland, or nearshore — for material that fails testing and cannot go to open water.

*Practitioner usage:* "Unit B failed bioassay — it's CDF-bound, full stop, ODMDS isn't an option for that material."

*Common misuse:* assuming any disposal site with water nearby functions as an ODMDS, rather than treating ODMDS designation as its own permitted, site-specific authorization.

## Slurry solids content (% by volume)

The proportion of a hydraulic dredge's pumped slurry that is actual sediment solids rather than water, expressed as a percentage by volume; the target band balances production against the risk of settling.

*Practitioner usage:* "We're pumping around 18% solids on this sand — pushing much past that and we're gambling with the critical velocity."

*Common misuse:* assuming higher solids content is always more efficient, without checking whether the pump can still hold the sediment's critical transport velocity at that density.

## Critical (minimum transport) velocity

The minimum pipeline flow velocity, for a given grain size and pipe diameter, below which solids begin to settle out rather than staying suspended in transport — falling below it risks a pipeline plug, especially at bends or during a flow interruption.

*Practitioner usage:* "Velocity's dropped under 4.5 — we're below critical for this sand, back off the density before it settles."

*Common misuse:* judging pipeline performance only by density or production rate, without checking velocity against the critical threshold for the actual grain size in the line.

## Turbidity compliance monitoring point vs. background reading

The **background reading** is a turbidity measurement taken upcurrent of active dredging, representing ambient conditions unaffected by the work. The **compliance monitoring point** is a fixed location (typically downcurrent) where the permit's numeric turbidity limit is measured against that background.

*Practitioner usage:* "Background's running 80 today after the storm — our limit at the monitoring point is 88, not the flat 5-over number we'd use on a calm day."

*Common misuse:* applying a single fixed NTU limit regardless of background conditions, when many permits scale the allowable increase with how turbid the water already is.

## Silt curtain (turbidity curtain)

A floating, weighted barrier deployed around an active dredging or discharge point to contain suspended sediment and limit turbidity migration into surrounding water, moved as the dredge advances rather than left in a single fixed position.

*Practitioner usage:* "Curtain needs to move with us into the next reach — it's still sitting where we started two days ago."

*Common misuse:* treating curtain deployment as a one-time setup step at mobilization instead of a containment measure that has to track the dredge's actual working location.

## Beneficial use (of dredged material)

Placement of dredged material for a productive purpose — habitat restoration, beach nourishment, fill for construction — as an alternative to open-water disposal or confined containment, generally available only to material that meets the placement site's specific suitability criteria.

*Practitioner usage:* "This clean sand is a beneficial-use candidate for the nourishment project down the coast, not just ODMDS filler."

*Common misuse:* assuming any clean-tested material automatically qualifies for beneficial use, without checking the specific grain-size and quality criteria the receiving beneficial-use site requires.

## Testing unit

A discrete sediment volume within a dredging project, defined by physical or contamination-source boundaries, that is sampled and classified separately from the rest of the project — classification and disposal eligibility apply per testing unit, not to the project as a whole.

*Practitioner usage:* "Unit B failing doesn't touch Unit A's classification — they were sampled and will be disposed of separately."

*Common misuse:* treating one testing unit's failed result as reason to reclassify or second-guess an adjacent unit's passing result, when the boundary was drawn specifically to isolate them.
