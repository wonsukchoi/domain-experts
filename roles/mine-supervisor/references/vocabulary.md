# Vocabulary

### Drummy roof
A hollow, dull-thud sounding-bar response indicating separation between rock layers above the opening — a strong precursor to a roof fall, distinct from the sharp ring of solid, bonded strata.
**In use:** "Tapped it twice from the last row of bolts out to the face — it's drummy the whole way, pull the crew back before anybody goes further."
**Common misuse:** treating any unusual tap sound as "drummy." The term is specific to a dull, hollow response from layer separation, not a catch-all for anything that sounds off — a generalist will flag solid-but-noisy roof the same way and either over-alarm or, worse, learn to discount real drummy responses as false positives.

### Pre-shift examination
A statutory examination (30 CFR §75.303 in US underground coal; state-equivalent forms elsewhere) of specified areas for hazards, conducted and certified by a qualified person within a defined window (typically 3 hours) before any miner is allowed to enter or work in that area.
**In use:** "Pre-shift's not signed off at the No. 3 heading yet — nobody past the last open crosscut until it is."
**Common misuse:** confusing it with the on-shift examination, which happens during the working shift itself (at least once per shift) rather than before it, or treating it as a paperwork formality instead of a legal precondition that gates physical entry.

### Roof control plan
An MSHA district-approved document (30 CFR §75.220) specifying a mine's bolt pattern, spacing, and support requirements, engineered to that mine's specific geology and legally enforceable exactly as written.
**In use:** "We can't move to 5×5 spacing to save time — that's not what's approved for this seam, that needs a plan revision first."
**Common misuse:** treating it as generic industry guidance a supervisor can adjust for efficiency, rather than a mine-specific, legally binding design that requires a formal MSHA-approved amendment to change.

### Action level (gas)
The specific numeric gas concentration (e.g., 1.0% CH4 at working-face equipment under 30 CFR §75.323) that triggers a mandatory, predefined response, distinguished from a general elevated reading that hasn't crossed a threshold yet.
**In use:** "We're not at the withdrawal level, 1.2%, but de-energize the miner now — that threshold already passed."
**Common misuse:** treating "elevated gas" as one undifferentiated concern rather than tracking which specific numeric threshold has been crossed, since different thresholds attach to different mandatory actions.

### Permissibility (electrical)
An MSHA-approved explosion-proof design standard for underground electrical equipment in gassy mines (30 CFR Part 18 and the §75.500 series); a "permissibility defect" is physical damage that breaks that ignition-proof integrity specifically.
**In use:** "That junction box has a cracked gasket — that's a permissibility defect, tag it out before it goes back underground."
**Common misuse:** conflating it with general equipment condition or mechanical guarding. Permissibility is specifically about preventing an ignition source; a piece of equipment can be mechanically sound and still permissibility-defective, or vice versa.

### Cutoff grade
The minimum ore grade at which material is economically worth processing rather than sent to waste, set relative to whichever constraint (mill capacity, mining cost, market price) is currently binding — not a fixed geological property of the deposit.
**In use:** "Cutoff's moving to 0.28% Cu next quarter because the mill's the bottleneck now, not the pit — reclassify the block model accordingly."
**Common misuse:** treating it as a fixed number tied to the ore body itself. It's an economic decision set by resource planning/engineering that a supervisor executes against in loading and grade-control decisions, not one the supervisor sets.

### Dilution (ore control)
Waste rock unintentionally mixed into an ore load during blasting or loading, expressed as a percentage of planned tonnage, which drops the effective mill-feed grade below the block-model estimate.
**In use:** "Muck pile's running 14% dilution against a 10% plan design — hold it for grade control before it goes to the stockpile."
**Common misuse:** confusing it with total waste tonnage mined (development or stripping waste), rather than specifically the waste mixed into what should have been a clean ore load.

### Unwarrantable failure (§104(d) order)
An MSHA finding that an operator knew or should have known of a violation and failed to correct it — aggravated conduct beyond ordinary negligence — which carries a mandatory withdrawal order, distinct from a routine citation.
**In use:** "That's not a routine citation — inspector's writing it up as a (d) order because we'd already logged the same hazard twice without fixing it."
**Common misuse:** treating it as synonymous with "a serious citation." It specifically requires the aggravating element of prior knowledge plus failure to correct, not just the severity of the underlying hazard.

### Withdrawal order (§103(k)/§107(a))
An MSHA order removing miners from an area due to imminent danger or an unresolved hazard, distinct from a citation, which documents a violation while typically allowing continued work under a correction deadline.
**In use:** "Inspector issued a 107(a) — that's everybody out of the section now, not a citation we fix on our own schedule."
**Common misuse:** treating a citation and a withdrawal order as equivalent in consequence; a citation permits continued work with a deadline, a withdrawal order does not.

### Fire boss / gas examiner
The certified person who conducts pre-shift gas and hazard examinations — a certification that may be separate from or combined with the shift supervisor's own certification depending on state law.
**In use:** "Fire boss cleared No. 2 heading at 5:40 — we're good to send the crew in."
**Common misuse:** assuming a mine-supervisor certification automatically includes examiner authority. Several states require a separate exam/papers for the examiner function specifically.

### Berm
A mounded barrier along a bench edge or haul road, sized in common surface-mining practice to roughly the mid-axle height of the largest vehicle using that road, intended to stop a vehicle from going over the edge.
**In use:** "That berm's only knee-high against a truck with 5-foot axles — get it built back up before the next haul cycle."
**Common misuse:** treating berm height as one fixed universal number rather than a spec sized to the largest vehicle actually in service on that specific road.

### Permissible advance / cut-depth limit
The maximum unsupported distance a continuous miner may cut before roof bolting must catch up, set by the roof control plan and tied to the specific roof rock's self-supporting time, not a machine capability limit.
**In use:** "We're at the cut-depth limit for this heading — miner stops here until the bolter catches up, doesn't matter if the seam looks solid."
**Common misuse:** treating the limit as conservative padding that can be stretched when the roof "looks fine" — the limit is set by unsupported-span behavior specific to that roof rock, which isn't reliably visible before it fails.
