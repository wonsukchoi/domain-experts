# Vocabulary

### Cut/fill balance
The relationship between soil volume excavated (cut) and soil volume needed to build up other areas (fill) on the same site.
**In use:** "We're at an 8.6% surplus on cut/fill — that regrades into the perimeter berm, no off-haul needed."
**Common misuse:** Generalists treat grading as purely a shape/contour exercise and don't realize an unbalanced cut/fill plan is a direct line item in the construction budget.

### Caliper
The trunk diameter of a nursery tree, measured 6 inches above grade for stock up to 4 inches, used to specify tree size at planting.
**In use:** "Spec'd at 2.5-inch caliper instead of 4-inch — same eventual canopy, much lower transplant shock."
**Common misuse:** Clients conflate caliper with final mature size; a larger caliper at install doesn't mean faster growth to maturity, and often means higher transplant mortality.

### Hardiness zone
A geographic classification (USDA system in the US) indicating the average annual minimum temperature range a location experiences, used to predict whether a plant species survives there.
**In use:** "That palette was pulled from a Zone 8 reference project — this site is Zone 6b, half those species won't overwinter."
**Common misuse:** Treated as the only survivability factor; soil drainage and pH often matter as much as temperature zone and get skipped.

### Impervious surface
Any hard surface (pavement, roofing, compacted soil) that prevents water infiltration and generates stormwater runoff.
**In use:** "That extra parking bay pushes net new impervious past the review trigger — we need to re-run the detention sizing."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to only mean pavement; compacted gravel and some "permeable" pavers still count as partially or fully impervious depending on the jurisdiction's definition.

### Design storm
A statistically defined rainfall event (e.g., the 10-year, 24-hour storm) that a stormwater system is engineered to handle without exceeding its capacity.
**In use:** "Code requires sizing to the 10-yr storm here, not the 2-yr — that changes the detention volume by almost double."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to be a fixed national standard; design storm return periods and durations are set by local or state code and vary by jurisdiction.

### Detention vs. retention
Detention temporarily holds stormwater and releases it at a controlled rate; retention holds water permanently (a pond) or until it infiltrates/evaporates.
**In use:** "This is a detention basin, not retention — it's designed to empty within 72 hours, not hold a permanent pool."
**Common misuse:** Used interchangeably; the design intent, maintenance requirements, and permitting are different for each.

### Rational method
A simplified formula (Q = CiA) for estimating peak stormwater runoff rate from a site, using a runoff coefficient, rainfall intensity, and drainage area.
**In use:** "Rational method gives us 2.05 cfs peak on the impervious area — that sets the minimum detention outlet sizing."
**Common misuse:** Applied to drainage areas or site complexities beyond its valid range (generally small, homogeneous catchments); larger or mixed-use sites need a more detailed hydrologic model.

### Percolation (perc) test
A field test measuring how quickly water infiltrates into a specific soil, used to size infiltration-based stormwater features.
**In use:** "We can't size that bioswale off a soil-survey default — this site needs an actual perc test."
**Common misuse:** A perc test result from one part of a large site is assumed to apply site-wide; infiltration rates can vary significantly across a single parcel.

### Setback
The minimum required distance between a structure or site feature and a property line, easement, or other reference line, set by zoning code.
**In use:** "That retaining wall is 3 feet inside the required 10-foot rear setback — it has to move or get a variance."
**Common misuse:** Confused with easements; a setback is a zoning restriction on where you can build, an easement is a right granted to someone else to use part of your land.

### Easement
A legal right for a specific party (utility company, adjacent owner) to use a defined portion of a property for a stated purpose, regardless of who owns the land.
**In use:** "We can't plant large-canopy trees over that sewer easement — the utility can remove anything blocking access."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to only restrict building structures; most utility easements also restrict deep-rooted planting and permanent hardscape.

### Bioswale
A vegetated, shallow channel designed to slow, filter, and infiltrate stormwater runoff as it moves across a site.
**In use:** "Route the parking-lot runoff through a bioswale before it hits the detention inlet — cuts the sediment load."
**Common misuse:** Treated as purely decorative landscaping; its function depends on correctly sized soil media and slope, not just planting choice.

### Site-review overlay
A jurisdiction- or HOA-imposed additional layer of design requirements (architectural review, historic district, environmental overlay) beyond base zoning.
**In use:** "This parcel sits inside the river-corridor overlay — that adds a buffer-planting requirement on top of the base setback."
**Common misuse:** Assumed base zoning compliance is sufficient; overlays can impose stricter requirements that supersede the base code.

### Plant schedule
A tabular construction-document deliverable listing every specified plant by quantity, size, spacing, and botanical/common name.
**In use:** "The schedule says 45 canopy trees but the plan only shows 38 symbols — that has to reconcile before bid."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a formality; contractors bid directly off it, so a mismatch with the drawing becomes a change-order dispute.
