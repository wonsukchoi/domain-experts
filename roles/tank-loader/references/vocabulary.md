# Vocabulary

Terms generalists blur together that a tank car, truck, and ship loader keeps sharply separate. Each: definition, how a practitioner actually uses it in a sentence, and the common misuse.

## Bonding vs. grounding

**Bonding** connects the vehicle/vessel and the loading equipment to the same electrical potential so no spark can jump between them. **Grounding** connects that same system to earth potential, which matters for dissipating any residual charge but doesn't by itself equalize potential between the tank and the fill pipe.

*Practitioner usage:* "The bonding cable is what stops the spark between the truck and the rack — the ground rod is a separate connection, and having one without the other isn't the same protection."

*Common misuse:* treating "it's grounded" as equivalent to "it's bonded," when a vehicle can be earthed and still be at a different potential than the fill equipment it's connected to.

## Switch loading

Loading a compartment with a product different from the one it carried on its previous run, which mixes residual vapor from the old product with the new one — this atmosphere is more likely to sit inside the flammable range and more prone to static accumulation than either product's vapor space alone.

*Practitioner usage:* "This compartment ran diesel last trip and we're putting gasoline in it now — that's a switch load, so we're running the restricted rate regardless of what the bottom-loading line normally allows."

*Common misuse:* assuming a bottom-loading (submerged) fill design removes the switch-load risk, when the mixed-vapor-atmosphere problem is independent of whether the fill is submerged or splash.

## Splash loading vs. submerged (bottom) loading

**Splash loading** introduces liquid from above the surface, generating turbulence and static charge as it falls and mixes with air. **Submerged/bottom loading** introduces liquid through a fill pipe that reaches near the tank bottom, reducing splash — but the restricted initial-rate rule still applies until the outlet is confirmed submerged, and switch-load risk isn't eliminated by the design alone.

*Practitioner usage:* "Bottom loading cuts down splash once the drop tube's covered, but for the first couple hundred gallons before it's submerged, we're still running the restricted rate."

*Common misuse:* treating "this is a bottom-loading rack" as a blanket exemption from restricted initial-rate procedure, rather than a design that only removes the splash-loading risk once the outlet is actually submerged.

## Outage

The deliberately unfilled volume left in a compartment (expressed as a percentage of rated capacity) to allow for thermal expansion of the product during transit — not spare capacity that's wasted, but a required safety margin.

*Practitioner usage:* "We load to 98%, not 100% — that 2% outage is what keeps this compartment from becoming overpressured if it sits in the sun for a few hours."

*Common misuse:* treating outage as flexible operational slack that can be shaved to fit more product in when a shipment is slightly oversized, rather than a fixed requirement tied to the specific product's thermal-expansion behavior.

## Vapor recovery (balance vs. vacuum-assist)

Capturing vapor displaced by incoming liquid and returning it through a closed line instead of venting it to atmosphere. **Balance systems** rely on the incoming liquid's own displacement pressure to push vapor back; **vacuum-assist systems** actively draw the vapor out with a blower or pump, needed where balance-system pressure alone isn't sufficient.

*Practitioner usage:* "This rack's vacuum-assist, not balance — if the vacuum reading isn't showing up at the coupler, the liquid valve interlock won't let us start, full stop."

*Common misuse:* treating vapor recovery as purely an emissions/environmental requirement, missing that a leaking or bypassed vapor path also means displaced flammable vapor is loose near the rack's own ignition sources.

## Overfill-prevention system vs. high-level alarm

An **overfill-prevention system** is an automated shutoff (optic or float sensor wired to an interlock) that stops the fill at a set trip point without requiring a human action. A **high-level alarm** merely notifies a human, who must then act — it's a different, weaker layer of protection unless the alarm is itself wired into an automatic shutoff.

*Practitioner usage:* "We confirmed the overfill system actually tripped and stopped the pump — an alarm that just makes noise while someone runs to the valve isn't the same control."

*Common misuse:* citing "we have a high-level alarm" as equivalent to having overfill prevention, when an alarm without an automatic shutoff still depends on a human reacting in time.

## Proper shipping name vs. trade/brand name

The **proper shipping name** is the specific regulatory-defined name (e.g., "Gasoline," UN1203) that must appear on the shipping paper and match the applicable hazmat table entry. A **trade or brand name** (e.g., a specific retailer's branded fuel) is not a substitute and doesn't by itself satisfy the shipping-paper description requirement.

*Practitioner usage:* "The paper needs to read the proper shipping name and UN number, not just the customer's brand name for the product — those aren't interchangeable for the hazmat description."

*Common misuse:* assuming a familiar trade name on a purchase order is sufficient documentation for the shipping paper, when the regulatory proper shipping name is the field that's actually checked.

## Placard vs. shipping paper

A **placard** is the physical hazard-class diamond displayed on the outside of the vehicle/vessel. The **shipping paper** is the accompanying document listing the proper shipping name, hazard class, UN number, packing group, and quantity. They must agree, but they're two independent checks — a correct placard doesn't confirm a correct shipping paper, and vice versa.

*Practitioner usage:* "The placard's right, but I still need to check the shipping paper's quantity field against what actually went into the tank — one doesn't verify the other."

*Common misuse:* treating a visually correct placard as proof the shipping paper is also correct, skipping the independent document cross-check.

## Dry-break (dry-disconnect) coupler

A coupling designed to seal both sides automatically on disconnection, preventing product spillage or vapor release when the hose or arm is removed — distinct from a standard coupling that requires the line to be drained or the valve closed first to avoid a spill.

*Practitioner usage:* "Disconnect the dry-break after the valve's confirmed closed — it'll seal on its own, but that's not a substitute for confirming the valve position first."

*Common misuse:* relying on the dry-break coupler's automatic seal as a reason to skip confirming the upstream valve is closed before disconnecting.

## Declaration of Inspection (DOI)

A joint vessel/facility document (marine transfers) recording the agreed communication method, emergency shutdown signal, transfer rate, and other safety terms for a specific bulk-liquid transfer — required before hose connection and not automatically valid across a crew or watch change without re-confirmation.

*Practitioner usage:* "We re-signed the DOI when the dock supervisor changed at 1000 — the transfer terms don't carry over to a new watch without that."

*Common misuse:* treating a DOI signed at the start of a long transfer as covering the entire operation regardless of watch changes, rather than a document that needs re-confirmation when the responsible parties change.

## Cargo tank/tank car periodic test types (K/V/P/UT)

Under 49 CFR Part 180, cargo tanks and tank cars carry distinct periodic test requirements identified by letter: leakage test (K), external visual inspection (V), pressure test (P), and thickness/ultrasonic test (UT), each on its own interval — passing one doesn't imply the others are current.

*Practitioner usage:* "The tank's current on its V and K, but the P test is what's actually due this month — check the specific test type, not just 'is it certified.'"

*Common misuse:* treating "the tank is certified" as a single blanket status, rather than checking which specific test type and interval is current versus due.
