# Vocabulary

### Performance Validity Test (PVT)
A measure designed to detect whether a patient gave adequate effort on cognitive testing, independent of whether they actually have a deficit.
**In use:** "We can't interpret the CVLT scores until we check whether he passed his PVTs."
**Common misuse:** Treated as interchangeable with a "malingering test" that proves intentional deception — a PVT failure indicates invalid performance, not motive; the report should state the finding, not diagnose malingering, which requires additional evidence of intent.

### Symptom Validity Test (SVT)
A measure (often a self-report checklist) that detects over-reported or implausible symptom endorsement, distinct from PVTs which test cognitive performance rather than self-reported symptoms.
**In use:** "His symptom checklist elevated on the SVT even though his PVTs were clean — the exaggeration is on the self-report side, not the performance side."
**Common misuse:** Collapsed into "PVT" as if they measure the same construct; a patient can pass every PVT and still over-report symptoms, or vice versa.

### Multiple-failure model
The interpretive rule that a single PVT failure is indeterminate, but two or more independent PVT failures meet the threshold for probable invalid performance.
**In use:** "One borderline TOMM score isn't enough to call this invalid — we need the multiple-failure model, so add the WMT before drawing a conclusion."
**Common misuse:** Applied to failures of subscales within the *same* instrument (e.g., two WMT indices) as if they were independent — they aren't fully independent and count with less weight than failures across different instruments.

### Base rate of low scores
The expected proportion of abnormal scores that occur by chance alone in a healthy population when many measures are given in one battery.
**In use:** "In a 25-test battery, having three scores below the 5th percentile is within the healthy base rate — it's not automatically a finding."
**Common misuse:** Ignored entirely, so any single low score in a large battery gets reported as a deficit without checking whether that many low scores is expected by chance.

### Reliable Change Index (RCI)
A statistic that determines whether a change between two testing occasions exceeds what measurement error and practice effects would produce on their own.
**In use:** "The 5-point drop isn't a reliable change once you subtract the expected practice effect — it's inside the RCI's error band."
**Common misuse:** Computed from raw score difference alone, skipping the practice-effect correction, which inflates apparent "improvement" at retest.

### Practice effect
The score gain a patient gets purely from having taken the same or a similar test before, unrelated to any real change in ability.
**In use:** "He didn't get better cognitively — the WMI gain is right in line with the published 12-month practice effect."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to be negligible or ignored for short retest intervals; practice effects are often largest on the first retest and can persist for a year or more on some measures.

### Demographically corrected norms
Normative comparison data adjusted for factors like age, education, and sometimes sex or race, rather than a single population-wide norm.
**In use:** "His raw Trail Making B time looks slow, but against demographically corrected norms for his age and education it's average."
**Common misuse:** Skipped in favor of raw or age-only norms, which can make an elderly, less-educated patient look impaired or a young, highly-educated patient look artificially normal.

### Ecological validity
How well a test's performance predicts a patient's actual real-world functioning (driving, managing finances, working), as opposed to just predicting other test scores.
**In use:** "His executive scores are borderline, but the ecological validity question is whether that shows up in his actual bill-paying and medication management."
**Common misuse:** Assumed automatic — a below-average score on an office-based executive-function test does not by itself prove real-world functional impairment without corroborating collateral or occupational data.

### Lateralization
Localizing a cognitive function (most often language or memory) to the dominant hemisphere, typically established via Wada testing or fMRI before epilepsy or tumor surgery.
**In use:** "Wada confirmed left-hemisphere language dominance, so the pre-op verbal memory drop is the one that matters for surgical risk."
**Common misuse:** Assumed from handedness alone; a right-handed patient is not automatically left-hemisphere dominant for language, especially with early-life or congenital brain abnormality.

### Double dissociation
Evidence that two functions are separately impaired by damage to two different regions — function A is impaired with lesion 1 but spared with lesion 2, and function B shows the reverse pattern.
**In use:** "The double dissociation between his intact recognition and impaired free recall points to a retrieval, not storage, deficit."
**Common misuse:** Claimed from a single patient's single-session profile without corroborating group or lesion data — a single case's score pattern is suggestive, not proof, of a double dissociation.

### Flexible battery vs. fixed battery
Two approaches to test selection: a fixed battery (e.g., the original Halstead-Reitan) gives the same full set of tests to every patient; a flexible battery selects tests based on the referral question and hypothesis.
**In use:** "We're not running the full Halstead-Reitan — a flexible battery targeted at his frontal-lobe hypothesis answers the referral question faster."
**Common misuse:** Flexible battery assumed to mean "whatever the clinician feels like giving" — it still requires a stated hypothesis and adequate domain coverage, not an ad hoc selection.

### Embedded validity indicator
A performance validity measure derived from a standard cognitive test's existing data (e.g., Reliable Digit Span from WAIS-IV Digit Span) rather than a stand-alone PVT.
**In use:** "His Reliable Digit Span of 6 is an embedded indicator that adds to, not replaces, the stand-alone TOMM finding."
**Common misuse:** Treated as sufficient on its own for the multiple-failure model when only one embedded and zero stand-alone PVTs were given — embedded indicators generally carry less weight than dedicated stand-alone PVTs.

### Mild neurocognitive disorder (DSM-5-TR)
A diagnostic category for a modest cognitive decline from a previous level in one or more domains that does not interfere with independence in everyday activities, distinct from major neurocognitive disorder (dementia).
**In use:** "His scores support mild, not major, neurocognitive disorder — he still manages his own medications and finances independently."
**Common misuse:** Used as a synonym for "early dementia" as if progression is guaranteed; mild neurocognitive disorder has multiple possible courses, including stable or reversible presentations (e.g., depression, medication effects).
