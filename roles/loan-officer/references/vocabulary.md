# Loan officer working vocabulary

Terms of art a loan officer uses precisely. Format: definition → a sentence a practitioner would actually say → the common misuse.

**DTI (front-end vs. back-end)** — Front-end (housing) DTI = proposed PITIA ÷ gross monthly income. Back-end DTI = PITIA plus all other qualifying debt ÷ gross monthly income.
In use: "Front-end's fine at 28%, but back-end is 46% once you count the student loans — that's the number underwriting cares about."
Misuse: quoting only front-end DTI to a borrower as "the" ratio; programs qualify (and decline) off back-end.

**LTV / CLTV** — LTV = loan amount ÷ lesser of purchase price or appraised value. CLTV (combined) = all liens against the property ÷ same value, used when there's a second mortgage or HELOC.
In use: "First is at 80% LTV, but with the HELOC it's 95% CLTV, which changes the mortgage insurance and pricing entirely."
Misuse: computing LTV off the contract price after the appraisal comes back lower — the lesser-of rule applies regardless of which number the borrower prefers.

**QM / Ability-to-Repay (ATR)** — Qualified Mortgage: a loan meeting CFPB safe-harbor criteria under the Ability-to-Repay rule (post-2021, largely a price-based test — APR within 2.25 points of APOR for most first liens — rather than a hard 43% DTI cap). Non-QM loans exist but carry different investor and disclosure requirements.
In use: "This prices out as general QM at 6.75%, comfortably inside APOR+2.25 — we don't need the DTI exception path."
Misuse: telling a borrower "43% DTI is the legal cap" — that was the old general QM test; agency and program overlays, not the QM rule itself, are what's actually capping this file today.

**PMI vs. MIP vs. funding fee** — PMI (private mortgage insurance) applies to conventional loans above 80% LTV. MIP (mortgage insurance premium) is FHA's version (upfront + annual). VA uses a one-time funding fee instead of ongoing mortgage insurance.
In use: "FHA MIP doesn't cancel on this one since we're above 90% LTV — factor that into the conventional-vs-FHA comparison."
Misuse: assuming all "mortgage insurance" behaves like conventional PMI (cancels at 78–80% LTV) — FHA MIP at high LTV is a life-of-loan cost without a refinance.

**BPMI vs. LPMI** — Borrower-paid PMI is a separate monthly line item that cancels automatically at 78% LTV (original value, normal amortization) or on request at 80%. Lender-paid PMI is baked into a higher note rate and does not cancel without a refinance.
In use: "LPMI drops the monthly PMI line, but it's baked into the rate for the life of the loan unless she refinances — with a 5-year hold, BPMI wins."
Misuse: presenting LPMI as strictly "no PMI" — it's PMI paid through rate instead of a line item, and the borrower needs to understand it doesn't go away on its own.

**Loan Estimate (LE) / Closing Disclosure (CD)** — The two TRID-mandated disclosures: LE within 3 business days of a complete application; CD at least 3 business days before consummation, with tolerance rules governing how much each fee can move between them.
In use: "We re-disclosed the LE after the rate lock, but the CD's 3-day clock only resets if the APR move crosses the threshold."
Misuse: treating LE and CD as interchangeable "the paperwork" — they're legally distinct documents with different timing rules and different consequences for missing them.

**Changed circumstance** — A defined TRID term: new information after the LE that justifies a fee increase outside normal tolerance (e.g., appraisal reveals needed repairs, borrower changes loan program, title discovers a lien).
In use: "The lower appraisal is a valid changed circumstance for the appraisal fee tolerance, but it doesn't touch the origination fee tolerance."
Misuse: labeling any fee increase a "changed circumstance" to justify it — it must meet the regulatory definition and be documented, or the fee increase breaches tolerance and the lender eats the cure.

**Rate lock / float-down** — A rate lock guarantees a rate for a set period (typically 15–60 days) for a fee built into pricing; a float-down option lets the borrower capture a market rate improvement before closing, usually for an added cost.
In use: "We're locked 45 days with a float-down — if the 10-year keeps dropping, we can capture it up to 10 days before closing."
Misuse: adding a float-down the week of closing "just in case" — there's no time left to exercise it, so it was a wasted cost from day one.

**Points (discount vs. origination)** — Discount points are prepaid interest that buys down the rate (deductible, priced against a breakeven period). Origination points are a lender/broker fee for originating the loan (zero-tolerance category under TRID) and don't affect the rate.
In use: "One discount point gets her to 6.625% with a 4-year breakeven; she's planning to hold 7+ years, so it pays for itself."
Misuse: calling any point charged at closing a "discount point" — if it doesn't move the rate, it's an origination or lender fee, and it's held to a stricter TRID tolerance.

**APR vs. note rate** — The note rate is the interest rate on the promissory note, used to compute P&I. APR incorporates the note rate plus certain finance charges (points, some fees) expressed as an annualized cost, used for QM pricing tests and comparison shopping.
In use: "Note rate's 6.75%, but the APR's 6.91% once you fold in the origination points — that's the number that matters for the QM price test."
Misuse: quoting APR as "the payment rate" to a borrower — P&I is always computed off the note rate, not the APR.

**Seasoning** — How long funds or an account have been in place, used to judge whether a deposit or debt is legitimately the borrower's own money/obligation versus a recent, undocumented event.
In use: "That $18K deposit isn't seasoned — it showed up nine days ago, so we need a paper trail before it counts as reserves."
Misuse: assuming any deposit sitting in the account at application is automatically "seasoned" — most guidelines look at a specific lookback window (commonly 60 days) and require source documentation for large, recent deposits regardless of balance.

**AUS (Desktop Underwriter / Loan Product Advisor)** — The agency automated underwriting systems that issue an initial approve/refer recommendation with conditions, based on the data submitted.
In use: "DU approved it, but the findings still require the last-30-day large-deposit letter before it's truly clear to close."
Misuse: treating an AUS "Approve/Eligible" as the final decision — it's a data-driven first pass; a human underwriter still verifies documentation and can require additional conditions.

**Compensating factors** — Documented strengths (large reserves, high credit score, low LTV, long job tenure) that justify approving a loan above a program's standard DTI ceiling.
In use: "We're at 47% back-end, above the 45% standard, but 12 months' reserves and a 780 score are the compensating factors DU is crediting."
Misuse: asserting compensating factors verbally without documenting them in the file — underwriting needs the reserves statement and score pulled and attached, not a sentence in an email.

**Non-arm's-length transaction** — A sale between parties with an existing relationship (family, employer-employee, landlord-tenant, business affiliates), which triggers stricter appraisal, down-payment-source, and sometimes LTV rules under most agency and FHA guidelines.
In use: "It's a non-arm's-length sale to his brother-in-law — FHA identity-of-interest rules cap this at 85% LTV instead of the standard max."
Misuse: only checking arm's-length status for family sales — employer-employee and business-affiliate relationships trigger the same overlays and are easy to miss on a 1003.

**Temporary buydown (2-1, 3-2-1)** — A seller- or lender-funded subsidy that lowers the borrower's effective payment for the first 1–3 years, stepping up to the full note rate; qualification is based on the note rate, not the reduced early-year payment.
In use: "The seller's funding a 2-1 buydown — great for her cash flow in years one and two, but it doesn't change the DTI we qualify her at."
Misuse: presenting a temporary buydown as a way to help a marginal DTI borrower qualify — agency guidelines require qualifying at the note rate specifically to prevent this use.
