# Compensation & benefits working vocabulary

Terms a comp manager uses precisely. Format: definition → how a practitioner says it → common misuse.

**Compa-ratio** — An individual's pay divided by the midpoint of their assigned band, expressed as a ratio (e.g., 1.05 means 5% above midpoint) — the standard tool for checking where someone sits within their intended pay structure.
In use: "She's at a 1.05 compa-ratio after six years and a top-performer rating — that's a defensible position, not an outlier."
Misuse: comparing raw salary numbers across employees without normalizing by band/midpoint, which ignores that different roles and levels have entirely different appropriate pay ranges.

**Range penetration** — The percentage position of an individual's pay within their band's full range (from minimum to maximum), a related but distinct measure from compa-ratio (which is specifically relative to midpoint).
In use: "At 78% range penetration, there's limited room left to grow this person's pay within the current band without a promotion or a band update."
Misuse: treating range penetration and compa-ratio as interchangeable; they answer slightly different questions (position within the full range vs. position relative to the single midpoint anchor).

**Total compensation (total rewards)** — The complete value of an employee's pay package: base salary, bonus/variable pay, equity, and benefits — the correct unit for comparing competitiveness or equity, as opposed to any single component in isolation.
In use: "Base looks 8% below market, but once equity and the richer benefits package are included, total comp is actually at the 60th percentile."
Misuse: comparing offers or internal equity using base salary alone, which can make a package look uncompetitive or overly generous when the full total tells a different story.

**Pay band (salary range)** — The defined minimum-to-maximum pay range assigned to a specific role/level, the structural backbone that individual pay decisions are checked against for internal consistency.
In use: "The L4 band is $155,000-$185,000 — any offer outside that range needs an explicit, documented exception rationale."
Misuse: treating the band as a soft suggestion that gets routinely overridden for specific hires, which erodes the entire point of having a band-based structure.

**Pay equity audit** — A statistical analysis (typically regression) checking whether pay differences correlate with protected characteristics after controlling for legitimate factors (role, level, tenure, performance) — run to surface unexplained gaps proactively, not just reactively.
In use: "The equity audit controls for role, level, tenure, and last two performance ratings — anything left over after that is what we investigate."
Misuse: running a raw pay comparison (e.g., average pay by gender with no controls) and treating any gap as proof of discrimination, or conversely dismissing a real controlled-gap finding as coincidence without investigation.

**Market benchmarking** — Comparing an organization's pay for a specific role, level, and geography against external market data from the actual competitive talent pool for that role — not a generic industry-wide or company-wide average.
In use: "We benchmark this role against the specific set of companies we actually lose candidates to, not a broad tech-industry average that includes companies twice our size."
Misuse: using a generic, broad benchmark survey without checking whether its peer group actually reflects the organization's real competitive talent market for the specific role.

**Job leveling / career ladder** — A framework defining distinct levels within a role family (e.g., L1-L6 for engineering) with associated scope, expectations, and pay bands, providing the structural basis pay decisions are checked against.
In use: "This promotion moves her from L3 to L4 — the band and the scope expectations both change together, not just the number."
Misuse: adjusting pay significantly without a corresponding leveling change, which breaks the connection between the leveling framework and actual pay that makes the system defensible.

**Off-cycle adjustment** — A pay change made outside the organization's normal review cycle (e.g., mid-year, in response to a retention risk or market shift), as distinct from the standard annual/cyclical review process.
In use: "This is an off-cycle adjustment tied to a documented competing offer, not a routine raise — it's being logged and checked against the band like any other change."
Misuse: treating off-cycle adjustments as routine, undocumented exceptions rather than deliberate, tracked decisions checked against the same structure as cyclical pay decisions.

**Pay transparency** — The practice of disclosing pay ranges, pay-setting criteria, or actual pay figures to employees, candidates, or the public, distinct from pay equity (whether the underlying pay is actually fair) though the two are often conflated.
In use: "We're not publishing bands company-wide until the equity audit findings are addressed — transparency without underlying equity just makes the gaps more visible."
Misuse: assuming a transparency initiative alone resolves equity concerns, when transparency about an internally inconsistent structure can increase distrust rather than reduce it.

**Total rewards statement** — A document or tool presenting the full value of an employee's compensation package (base, bonus, equity, benefits, retirement match, etc.) in one place, used to communicate total compensation clearly rather than leaving employees to infer it from a base-salary number alone.
In use: "The total rewards statement shows this offer is actually above median once the equity grant and benefits are valued in — the base number alone undersold it."
Misuse: relying on verbal mentions of "great benefits" instead of a documented total rewards statement with actual dollar values, which makes the full package harder for the employee or candidate to evaluate and compare.
