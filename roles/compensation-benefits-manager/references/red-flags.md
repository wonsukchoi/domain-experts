# Red flags — what a comp manager notices instantly

Smell tests with thresholds. Any single one can be innocent; two or three together are a pattern.

### A new-hire offer priced above the band ceiling or above the highest-paid tenured employee in the same role/level
- **Usually means:** either the band is genuinely stale and needs a real market-driven update, or an individual exception is about to be approved that will show up as an unexplained gap in the next equity audit.
- **First question:** "Is there independent market data justifying a band-wide update, or is this exception being justified only by this one candidate/negotiation?"
- **Data to pull:** current band range, compa-ratios of existing incumbents in the role/level, most recent market survey data for the role.

### Compa-ratio dispersion within a single band exceeding roughly 0.85-1.15 with no tenure/performance pattern explaining it
- **Usually means:** individually-reasonable exceptions have accumulated into real internal inconsistency — the classic "death by a thousand exceptions" pattern.
- **First question:** "What specific, legitimate factors (tenure, performance rating, market timing) explain the employees at each end of this range?"
- **Data to pull:** compa-ratio by employee within the band, cross-tabulated against tenure and most recent performance rating.

### A pay equity regression showing an unexplained gap correlated with a protected characteristic, even a small one (commonly flagged above ~2-3%)
- **Usually means:** a real, potentially legally significant issue, regardless of whether it resulted from intentional decisions.
- **First question:** "Does this gap persist after controlling for role, level, tenure, and performance, or does it wash out once those factors are included?"
- **Data to pull:** the full regression output with controls, sample size behind the flagged group (small samples can produce misleading point estimates).

### A market benchmark drawn from a generic industry-wide survey rather than the actual competitive talent set for the specific role, level, and geography
- **Usually means:** pay decisions are being calibrated to the wrong comparison set, producing numbers that look "data-driven" but don't reflect real competitive exposure.
- **First question:** "Who do we actually lose candidates to for this specific role, and does the benchmark data reflect that set?"
- **Data to pull:** benchmark survey's peer group composition, actual candidate/offer-decline data for the role if available.

### A total-compensation comparison built on base salary alone for a role where bonus, equity, or benefits are a material part of typical pay
- **Usually means:** the competitiveness or equity read is misleading — a role can look under- or over-paid based on base alone while the full package tells a different story.
- **First question:** "What does the total compensation comparison look like once bonus, equity, and benefits are included?"
- **Data to pull:** full total-compensation statement for the roles being compared, not base salary in isolation.

### A publicized pay-transparency initiative launched before an internal consistency/equity audit has been completed
- **Usually means:** transparency will make existing inequity more visible rather than resolve it, generating distrust instead of building it.
- **First question:** "Has a pay equity audit been run and any findings addressed before this goes public/internal-wide?"
- **Data to pull:** most recent equity audit results and remediation status.

### Negotiated pay outcomes for a role/level showing wide variance that correlates with hire date or negotiation intensity rather than role/market/performance factors
- **Usually means:** negotiation assertiveness, not designed structure, is the primary determinant of pay — a system in name only.
- **First question:** "If we control for role, level, and performance, does negotiation history still predict pay — and by how much?"
- **Data to pull:** pay by hire cohort/negotiation notes if tracked, controlling for the same legitimate factors used in the equity audit.

### A benefits-package change evaluated only on direct cost, with no employee-value or competitive-positioning data attached
- **Usually means:** a decision that looks like pure cost-cutting may be a false economy if it damages retention or offer-acceptance rates in ways the cost analysis didn't capture.
- **First question:** "What does this change do to our competitive positioning and to employees who value this benefit disproportionately?"
- **Data to pull:** benefit utilization data, competitive benchmark for the specific benefit, any employee survey data on valued benefits.
