# Physical therapist aide — working vocabulary

Terms an aide needs to use precisely, since the precision is what keeps a task inside scope. Format: definition → a sentence a practitioner would actually say → the common misuse.

### Direct personal supervision

The supervision standard applied to aides: the supervising PT (or, where state law allows, PTA) must be physically present and immediately available for any patient-related task.
**In use:** "I can't start the transfer yet — she's still in Room 2, that's not direct personal supervision."
**Common misuse:** confusing it with the looser "general supervision" standard PTAs work under, where the PT only needs to be reachable, not on-site — that standard is for the PTA's independent treatment, not for aide-performed patient tasks.

### Patient-related task vs. non-patient-related task

The core scope split: non-patient-related tasks (cleaning, scheduling, laundry, equipment setup) can be done unsupervised; patient-related tasks (transport, transfer assistance, applying a modality) require the supervising clinician on-site.
**In use:** "Restocking the hydrocollator is non-patient-related, I'll do it now — the hot pack application on Mr. Lee is patient-related, that waits for the PT."
**Common misuse:** treating "I'm just physically near the patient" as automatically non-patient-related — the definition turns on what's being done, not on room location.

### Line-of-sight supervision

A stricter supervision standard than general on-site availability, requiring the supervising clinician to be able to see the task being performed; applies specifically to aide-performed services in some settings (e.g., Medicare skilled nursing facility billing).
**In use:** "This is a SNF patient, so it's line-of-sight, not just 'somewhere in the building' — she needs to actually see this."
**Common misuse:** assuming the outpatient-clinic supervision standard (PT on-site and available) automatically satisfies a line-of-sight requirement elsewhere — the two aren't interchangeable across settings.

### Modality

A physical agent (heat, cold, ultrasound, electrical stimulation) applied as part of treatment.
**In use:** "The modality today is moist heat, 20 minutes, per the PT's note from this morning."
**Common misuse:** treating "applying a modality" as a single undifferentiated skill level — setting the parameters (clinical judgment) and executing a pack someone else already parameterized (delegable, supervised) are different tasks with different scope rules.

### Scope of practice vs. job description

Scope of practice is the legal ceiling set by state practice acts and board rules; a job description is the employer's internal list of duties, which can be narrower than the legal scope but can never expand it.
**In use:** "Our job description says I can do gait training assist, but I checked the practice act and this state classifies that as patient-related requiring direct supervision — the job description doesn't override that."
**Common misuse:** treating the employer's job description as the authoritative boundary, especially when it's more permissive than the actual state law.

### Delegation

The PT's or PTA's act of assigning a specific, patient-related task to the aide, with the supervising clinician retaining full responsibility for the outcome.
**In use:** "The PT delegated the cold-pack application for this visit — she's still accountable for it, but I'm executing it."
**Common misuse:** treating delegation as a one-time blanket permission ("she said I could do hot packs") rather than a per-task, per-visit act that requires supervision each time.

### Skilled task vs. unskilled task

Skilled tasks require clinical training and judgment to perform safely and appropriately (progressing an exercise, interpreting a response to treatment); unskilled tasks can be performed by someone without that training, given supervision and clear parameters.
**In use:** "Applying the pack the PT already set up is unskilled; deciding whether to increase the temperature next visit is skilled — that's hers, not mine."
**Common misuse:** assuming "unskilled" means "low-stakes" — an unskilled task like hot-pack monitoring still has a real injury failure point; unskilled describes who can legally perform it, not how much care it requires.

### Erythema

Redness of the skin from increased blood flow, often an early visible sign of thermal irritation.
**In use:** "I saw erythema at the pack border at the 2:09 check, so I pulled it and paged the PT."
**Common misuse:** using it as a diagnosis ("the erythema means a burn") rather than an observation to report — the aide names what's seen, the PT determines what it means.

### Contraindication

A condition or circumstance under which a specific modality or technique should not be applied at all, as opposed to applied with extra caution.
**In use:** "Open wound on that leg is a contraindication for paraffin — we skip it, we don't just wrap it more carefully."
**Common misuse:** treating a contraindication as a "monitor more closely" flag rather than a stop — contraindications are a hold, not a caution level.

### Gait belt / transfer belt

A belt fastened around a patient's waist used to provide balance support and a secure hand-hold during ambulation or transfer.
**In use:** "Gait belt on before we start walking — it's for balance support, it's not rated to catch a full fall."
**Common misuse:** treating the belt as a lifting or fall-arrest device; it stabilizes an ambulatory patient, it doesn't substitute for a two-person lift or transfer when one is needed.

### Timed CPT unit ("8-minute rule")

The billing convention where physical therapy services are charged in 15-minute increments, with a minimum of roughly 8 minutes of a service required to bill a full unit under Medicare's timed-code rules.
**In use:** "If the room isn't reset by 2:15, that whole unit slides to the next slot."
**Common misuse:** thinking of this as purely a billing-office concern — the aide's turnover speed is a direct input to whether a unit is billable at all, not just to patient convenience.

### Hydrocollator

The heating unit (and, by extension, the moist-heat pack itself) that keeps silica-gel packs submerged in water at a controlled temperature, typically 160–166°F, for hot-pack therapy.
**In use:** "Hydrocollator's reading 163 — that's in range, packs are ready."
**Common misuse:** using "hydrocollator" and "hot pack" interchangeably in documentation when the unit temperature and the pack-on-skin duration are two separate parameters that both need checking.

### Universal precautions / standard precautions

The OSHA-driven infection-control practice of treating all blood and certain body fluids as potentially infectious, regardless of the patient's known status.
**In use:** "Standard precautions on every table wipe-down — we don't skip it because the last patient 'looked healthy.'"
**Common misuse:** applying precautions selectively based on how a patient presents, which defeats the entire point of a universal standard.

### Fall-risk tier

A PT-assigned classification (e.g., high/moderate/low) of a patient's fall risk, used to set staffing and technique requirements for transfers and ambulation.
**In use:** "She's tiered high-risk as of this week's re-assessment — two-person transfer only, no exceptions today."
**Common misuse:** treating the tier as static once assigned; a same-day change in the patient's condition (new dizziness, medication change) means the aide upgrades the working assumption until the PT re-assesses, rather than trusting the last-recorded tier.
