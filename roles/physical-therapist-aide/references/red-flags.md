# Red flags — signals a physical therapist aide should catch instantly

Smell tests with thresholds. Format per flag: the signal → what it usually means → the first question → the data to pull. Any one of these can be innocent; two together on the same patient or the same shift is a pattern.

### Hot-pack skin check delayed past the first 5-minute mark on a sensory-impaired patient

**Usually means:** the aide is running the standard 20-minute-endpoint monitoring cadence on a patient who needs the shortened 3-minute cadence — a protocol mismatch, not a one-off oversight.
**First question:** "Does this patient have neuropathy, PVD, or a prior burn — and does today's monitoring interval reflect that?"
**Data to pull:** the patient's sensory/circulatory status from the chart, the modality log's check timestamps for this visit.

### Patient reports numbness, tingling, or "just warm" at any point under a hot or cold modality

**Usually means:** early thermal injury masked by an unreliable subjective report — treat as an objective abort signal, not a comfort-level comment to note and continue.
**First question:** "What does the skin actually look like right now, independent of what the patient said?"
**Data to pull:** direct visual skin check, elapsed time since application, toweling/barrier layer count used.

### Supervising PT/PTA not in the building during a scheduled patient-related task

**Usually means:** either the schedule double-booked the supervising clinician, or the aide is being asked (explicitly or by default) to proceed without the required supervision level.
**First question:** "Is the PT physically on-site and reachable right now, or just scheduled to be back soon?"
**Data to pull:** the clinician's real-time schedule/location, the state practice act's supervision requirement for this specific task.

### Aide asked to progress exercise resistance, reps, or range without a PT signoff

**Usually means:** someone — the patient, a rushed PT, or the aide's own initiative — is treating a plan change as routine execution.
**First question:** "Did the PT write or verbally set this specific progression today, or is this being inferred from how the patient looked?"
**Data to pull:** today's chart note or a same-day verbal order, prior visit's documented parameters for comparison.

### Shared equipment used on a patient with no matching entry in the between-patient cleaning log

**Usually means:** the turnover checklist was skipped under time pressure — often the first casualty when the schedule runs long.
**First question:** "Was this actually cleaned, or assumed clean because the prior patient looked fine?"
**Data to pull:** the equipment/room cleaning log for the relevant time window, disinfectant contact-time spec for the product in use.

### Aide answering a patient's clinical question directly ("is this normal for my knee?")

**Usually means:** scope creep into clinical-judgment territory, usually from a well-meaning instinct to reassure rather than redirect.
**First question:** "Was that answered with an observation, or with an opinion about the patient's condition?"
**Data to pull:** none from a chart — this is a coaching conversation, not a documentation issue, unless it recurs.

### Solo gait-belt transfer attempted on a patient flagged high fall-risk

**Usually means:** either the fall-risk tier wasn't checked before the transfer, or a second person wasn't available and the transfer proceeded anyway instead of waiting.
**First question:** "What tier was this patient assigned, and why did the transfer go solo?"
**Data to pull:** the PT's fall-risk assessment note, staffing on the floor at the time of the transfer.

### Two-person transfer done solo because the second person is "five minutes out"

**Usually means:** schedule pressure overriding a safety requirement that exists specifically for moments like this one.
**First question:** "What was the actual wait time, and was the patient or the aide at risk in that window?"
**Data to pull:** staffing schedule for the shift, incident/near-miss log if one exists.

### Modality applied from memory of the patient's last visit rather than a same-day-verified order

**Usually means:** a verbal handoff several patients old has decayed, or the chart wasn't checked before setup.
**First question:** "When was this parameter last confirmed by the PT — today, or assumed from last time?"
**Data to pull:** today's chart entry, timestamp of the PT's last direct instruction for this patient.

### Room turnover consistently running more than ~5 minutes over the clinic's scheduled unit length across several consecutive patients

**Usually means:** either the turnover checklist has grown past what fits the slot, or steps are being rushed in a way that will surface later as a missed clean or a missed parameter check.
**First question:** "Which step in the turnover checklist is actually taking the extra time?"
**Data to pull:** turnover timestamps for the affected patients, the checklist steps completed vs. skipped.

### Aide's chart entry contains an assessment or interpretation rather than an observation

**Usually means:** the documentation habit has drifted into clinical territory — a scope violation on paper even if the actual task was done correctly.
**First question:** "Does this sentence describe what was seen and done, or what it means?"
**Data to pull:** the specific note, compared against the observed-fact format the clinic expects from aide documentation.

### Paraffin or hot pack applied over an open wound, active rash, or scar under 6 months old

**Usually means:** the contraindication check was skipped, either from time pressure or because the aide wasn't trained on the specific list.
**First question:** "Was the skin checked against the contraindication list before application, or after a complaint?"
**Data to pull:** the modality's documented contraindication checklist, the patient's skin condition as noted at intake.

### Clinic trains aides to a supervision standard looser than the state's actual requirement

**Usually means:** the clinic's internal training was copied from a different state's practice, or hasn't been updated since a state's rules tightened.
**First question:** "Does our training match this state's practice act, or a more permissive one?"
**Data to pull:** the current state practice act and board rules on aide supervision, the clinic's aide training/onboarding materials.
