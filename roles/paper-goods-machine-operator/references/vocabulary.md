# Vocabulary

### Moisture content (paper)
The percentage of water content in paper or paperboard stock, a critical process variable affecting flexibility, brittleness, and machinability during converting.
**In use:** "Checking moisture before we blame the creasing tool — 5.5% is well below our 7-9% spec, and that alone explains the cracking."
**Common misuse:** Treating moisture as a storage/receiving concern only, not an active converting-process variable — moisture at the point of converting (which can differ from moisture at receiving, especially in dry ambient conditions) directly determines mechanical behavior during folding and creasing.

### Web tension
The controlled pulling force applied to a continuous paper web as it moves through a converting line, which must stay within a band to avoid tearing (too high) or wrinkling/misfeeding (too low).
**In use:** "Tension's fine for a full roll, but we need to taper it down as this roll runs out — same setting, different actual stress at a smaller diameter."
**Common misuse:** Applying a single fixed tension setting throughout an entire roll's run — the same tension value produces different actual stress on the web at different roll diameters, and most systems need adjustment (manual or automated) as the roll runs down.

### Bond strength (peel/pull test)
A direct physical test measuring the actual force required to separate an adhesive-bonded joint, used to verify bonding quality beyond visual confirmation that adhesive was applied.
**In use:** "Glue looks fine on this sample, but the peel test is what actually tells us if the bond will hold under handling."
**Common misuse:** Treating visible adhesive presence as equivalent to confirmed bond strength — a bond can look properly applied while failing a strength test due to wrong quantity, temperature, or set time, none of which are reliably visible.

### Set time (adhesive)
The time required for an applied adhesive to reach adequate bond strength after application, which must be accounted for in line speed and downstream handling.
**In use:** "This adhesive needs a few seconds of set time before the fold gets handled downstream — running too fast doesn't give it that."
**Common misuse:** Assuming an adhesive is fully bonded the instant it's applied — set time is a real, material-specific requirement, and downstream handling before it completes can disrupt a bond that would otherwise have formed correctly.

### Roll diameter (and tension profile)
The changing physical size of a paper roll as material is consumed during a converting run, which affects the relationship between a given tension setting and the actual stress experienced by the web.
**In use:** "We're running an automated taper on tension as roll diameter decreases — manual fixed tension doesn't account for that relationship."
**Common misuse:** Treating roll diameter as irrelevant to process settings once the roll is loaded and running — the diameter-to-tension relationship changes continuously throughout a roll's use, and settings appropriate for a full roll can become inappropriate near empty.

### Converting speed
The rate at which paper stock moves through a converting machine's folding, cutting, or forming stations, which interacts with moisture and tension conditions rather than functioning as an independent throughput variable.
**In use:** "We're not just bumping speed for output — checking fold and bond quality at the new speed first, since moisture's already borderline today."
**Common misuse:** Treating converting speed changes as dimensionally/quality-neutral throughput adjustments — a speed that's safe under one moisture/tension condition can produce defects at the identical setting if those conditions have shifted.

### Score line / fold line
The pre-weakened line on paperboard where a fold is intended to occur cleanly, created by a creasing or scoring tool, whose integrity depends on both tooling condition and paper moisture.
**In use:** "Score line's cracking, not just folding — that's either a dull crease tool or paper that's too dry, and we're checking moisture first."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a cracked score line always indicates a tooling problem — brittleness from low paper moisture produces a visually similar defect, and moisture should be checked before assuming the crease tool needs replacement.

### Dancer roll / load cell (tension control)
Mechanical or sensor-based systems used to measure and automatically adjust web tension in real time as conditions (roll diameter, line speed) change during a converting run.
**In use:** "Dancer roll's keeping tension consistent automatically as this roll runs down — that's what we'd have to do manually otherwise."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a tension control system eliminates the need to monitor tension at all — automated systems still require periodic verification that they're functioning correctly and holding tension within the actual required band.

### Consistent vs. intermittent defect pattern
A distinction in defect timing used to diagnose root cause: a consistent, from-the-start defect typically indicates a setup error; an intermittent or worsening defect typically indicates a drifting process variable.
**In use:** "This crack rate's climbing as the run goes on — that points to something drifting, like moisture or tension, not a setup mistake we'd have seen from part one."
**Common misuse:** Treating every defect the same way regardless of its timing pattern — the distinction between consistent and intermittent/worsening defects is itself diagnostic information pointing toward different root cause categories (fixed setup vs. drifting variable).

### Kraft linerboard
A specific type of paperboard commonly used in corrugated box construction, with its own typical moisture specification range and mechanical properties relevant to converting.
**In use:** "Standard kraft linerboard spec here is seven to nine percent moisture — that's the band we're checking against."
**Common misuse:** Applying a generic "paper" moisture spec rather than the specific material's actual specification — different paper/paperboard grades have different appropriate moisture ranges, and using the wrong reference range can mask or misdiagnose an actual out-of-spec condition.
