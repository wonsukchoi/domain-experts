# General Dentist — Vocabulary

### CAMBRA
Caries Management by Risk Assessment — a framework categorizing a patient's caries risk (low/moderate/high/extreme) from bacterial-challenge and protective-factor inputs, not just current decay count.
**In use:** "She's CAMBRA high-risk, so we're doing 3-month recalls even though there's nothing active today."
**Common misuse:** Treating it as a one-time label instead of something reassessed at each recall as home care and diet change.

### DMFT
Decayed, Missing, and Filled Teeth — a count-based index of a patient's caries history.
**In use:** "His DMFT is low, but that doesn't mean his risk is low going forward."
**Common misuse:** Using DMFT alone as a risk predictor when it only measures past disease, not future trajectory.

### ICDAS
International Caries Detection and Assessment System — a standardized visual scoring scale for lesion severity, from sound tooth surface to extensive cavitation.
**In use:** "That's an ICDAS 3 — into the dentin, not yet cavitated, worth restoring."
**Common misuse:** Scoring posterior proximal surfaces by visual exam alone when ICDAS assumes radiographic or transillumination confirmation on those surfaces.

### Remineralization protocol
A non-surgical treatment approach using high-concentration fluoride and other agents to arrest and reverse an early, non-cavitated lesion instead of restoring it.
**In use:** "That lesion's only into outer enamel — put her on the remin protocol and recheck in three months instead of drilling today."
**Common misuse:** Applying it to lesions already into dentin, where arrest rates drop substantially and delay risks progression.

### Onlay
An indirect restoration covering one or more cusps but not the entire occlusal surface, used when structure loss is too much for a direct filling but not enough to need a full crown.
**In use:** "At 45% structure loss she's borderline — an onlay is more conservative than a full crown here."
**Common misuse:** Treating "onlay vs. crown" as interchangeable terms rather than distinct coverage decisions based on measured structure loss.

### Pulp vitality testing
Diagnostic testing (cold, electric pulp test, sometimes heat) to determine whether a tooth's nerve is alive, inflamed, or necrotic.
**In use:** "Cold test lingered past 15 seconds on #30 — that's irreversible pulpitis, she needs a root canal or extraction, not a filling."
**Common misuse:** Skipping vitality testing when a radiograph looks unremarkable, missing early irreversible pulpitis that hasn't yet shown a radiographic periapical change.

### Scaling and root planing (SRP)
A non-surgical periodontal procedure removing plaque and calculus from below the gumline and smoothing root surfaces, typically the first-line treatment for periodontitis before considering surgery or specialist referral.
**In use:** "First documented 5mm pockets — SRP and reevaluate in six weeks before we talk about a perio referral."
**Common misuse:** Referring to periodontics immediately on first documentation of moderate pocket depths without attempting SRP first, when SRP is the appropriate first-line step.

### Bleeding on probing (BOP)
A clinical sign, recorded during periodontal charting, indicating active gingival inflammation at that specific site.
**In use:** "Pocket depth alone doesn't tell the whole story — she's got BOP at twelve sites, that's active disease, not just anatomy."
**Common misuse:** Treating a deep pocket without bleeding as equally urgent as a shallower pocket with bleeding — BOP is a stronger indicator of active, progressing disease than depth alone.

### Marginal ridge
The raised enamel border at the mesial and distal edges of a posterior tooth's occlusal surface; its loss is a key marker of how much structural support remains.
**In use:** "Both marginal ridges are gone on that molar — we're past the point where a simple filling holds up."
**Common misuse:** Assessing structure loss only by "how big the cavity looks" instead of specifically noting marginal ridge involvement, which more directly predicts fracture risk.

### Occlusal overload
Excess biting force on a tooth or restoration beyond what it was designed to bear, often from bruxism or a high spot in the bite.
**In use:** "That composite's third failure in two years on the same tooth — check for occlusal overload before you just re-place the same material again."
**Common misuse:** Attributing repeat restoration failure solely to material quality or technique when an unaddressed bite problem is the actual cause.

### Informed consent (per-phase)
Documented patient understanding and agreement to a specific phase of treatment, its cost, risks, and alternatives — distinct from a blanket signature on a full treatment plan.
**In use:** "Get consent on the SRP and composite today; we'll get separate consent on the crown once we're at that phase."
**Common misuse:** Treating one signature on the initial treatment-plan estimate as sufficient consent for procedures that happen months later, after findings or the plan may have changed.

### Interim stabilization
A lower-cost, temporary restorative approach (e.g., glass ionomer sealant over an active lesion) used to arrest disease progression when the patient can't yet afford the definitive restoration.
**In use:** "She can't do the crown right now — let's stabilize with a glass ionomer so it doesn't get worse while she saves up."
**Common misuse:** Presenting only the full-cost definitive plan and letting a patient defer treatment entirely, rather than offering the interim option that keeps disease from progressing in the meantime.
