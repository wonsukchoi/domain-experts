# General Dentist — Red Flags

### Radiolucency penetrating past the outer third of dentin on bitewing
- **Usually means:** Active caries progression that has moved past the point where remineralization alone reliably arrests it; second most likely is a radiographic artifact from cone angulation — recheck angle before committing to operative treatment.
- **First question:** Is the patient currently on a documented remineralization protocol with a recent (within 6 months) risk-reassessment data point?
- **Data to pull:** Prior bitewing series for lesion-progression comparison, most recent CAMBRA risk category.

### Post-excavation cuspal structure loss exceeding ~50% of the crown
- **Usually means:** The remaining tooth structure is at meaningfully elevated fracture risk under a direct composite restoration; second most likely is that the caries extended further than the pre-op radiograph suggested.
- **First question:** What percentage of original cuspal structure remains after excavation, measured chairside, not estimated from the pre-op film?
- **Data to pull:** Intraoperative photo or direct measurement post-excavation, opposing occlusion/bruxism history.

### Probing depth >5mm with bleeding on probing, first time documented
- **Usually means:** Active periodontal disease that hasn't yet been treated non-surgically; second most likely is a false reading from probe angulation or recent trauma (e.g., popcorn hull).
- **First question:** Has a non-surgical protocol (SRP) been attempted and re-evaluated, or is this the first documentation of the depth?
- **Data to pull:** Full periodontal chart with bleeding-on-probing map, prior chart's pocket depths at the same site.

### Patient reports pain but radiograph and clinical exam are unremarkable
- **Usually means:** Early irreversible pulpitis not yet visible radiographically, or referred pain from an adjacent structure (TMJ, sinus, adjacent tooth); second most likely is atypical facial pain requiring a different specialist entirely.
- **First question:** Does cold testing reproduce the pain and does it linger past 10 seconds after stimulus removal?
- **Data to pull:** Cold and electric pulp test results on the suspected tooth and its neighbors, recent sinus/TMJ history.

### Repeat restoration failure at the same tooth within 2 years
- **Usually means:** An underlying etiology wasn't addressed — active caries risk factor, occlusal overload, or a marginal-seal problem with the material choice; second most likely is inadequate isolation during the original placement.
- **First question:** Was the patient's caries-risk category re-assessed since the original restoration, or was the same material simply re-placed?
- **Data to pull:** Restoration date and material history for that tooth, current CAMBRA risk category, occlusal analysis for overload signs.

### Impacted third molar with radiographic root proximity to the inferior alveolar nerve canal
- **Usually means:** Elevated risk of nerve injury (paresthesia) if extraction is attempted without further imaging or specialist evaluation; second most likely is a normal anatomic variant that merely appears close on a 2D panoramic.
- **First question:** Does the panoramic show darkening of the root, interruption of the canal's white line, or root deflection — the classic proximity signs?
- **Data to pull:** Panoramic radiograph, CBCT if any proximity signs are present, patient's age (root proximity risk changes with root maturity).

### Patient declining recommended treatment repeatedly across visits
- **Usually means:** Cost is the actual barrier, not disagreement with the diagnosis; second most likely is a trust or communication gap in how the finding and urgency were explained.
- **First question:** Has a lower-cost interim option (e.g., stabilization instead of definitive restoration) ever been offered, or has the same full-cost plan been re-presented each time?
- **Data to pull:** Treatment-plan history and what was actually offered/discussed at each prior visit.

### Bruxism wear facets with no protective appliance in place
- **Usually means:** Ongoing occlusal overload that will shorten the lifespan of any restoration placed, especially composite and porcelain; second most likely is a temporary stress-related habit that may resolve.
- **First question:** Is the wear pattern progressing across recall visits (compare serial photos/models), or has it been stable?
- **Data to pull:** Serial occlusal photos or study models across recall visits, patient-reported stress/clenching history.
