# Vocabulary

Terms generalists conflate that deck and engine-room ratings keep sharply separate. Each: definition, how a practitioner actually uses it in a sentence, and the common misuse to avoid.

### Snap-back zone

The area along and around a mooring line's path under tension where the line can recoil at lethal speed if it parts — a mapped, predictable geometry (OCIMF MEG4), not a vague "stay back from the line" instinct.

*In use:* "Tension's at 60% of MBL, clear the snap-back zone before we heave any more in — that's 15 meters past the bollard and off the centerline, not just standing behind the winch."

**Common misuse:** treating a bitt, bollard, or winch housing as cover from a parting line — those fittings are often exactly what the line whips around, not a barrier that stops it.

### Spring line vs. breast line

**Spring line** runs fore-and-aft from the vessel to a shoreside fitting, resisting movement along the berth (forward/aft surge). **Breast line** runs roughly perpendicular to the vessel's length, holding the vessel against or away from the berth.

*In use:* "Put out the after spring first to stop us surging forward before we run the breast lines."

**Common misuse:** using "mooring line" generically when the specific line's function (surge control vs. off-berth control) determines which one takes the load first and where the snap-back risk actually concentrates.

### Single up

The order to reduce mooring lines from the full mooring configuration down to one line per direction (typically one head line and one stern line, or one spring), in preparation for letting go entirely.

*In use:* "Single up to one head line and one stern spring — stand by to let go on the captain's word."

**Common misuse:** treating "single up" as interchangeable with "let go" — singling up is a controlled intermediate step, not the final release, and the remaining lines still carry real load.

### Sounding vs. ullage

**Sounding** measures the depth of liquid from the tank bottom up. **Ullage** measures the empty space from the top of the tank down to the liquid surface. Both describe the same tank but from opposite reference points, and mixing them up inverts the reading.

*In use:* "Give me the sounding on number two, not the ullage — I need it from the bottom up to match the transfer log."

**Common misuse:** reporting an ullage figure as if it were a sounding (or vice versa) without converting against the tank's known depth, producing a reading that looks plausible but is actually inverted.

### Gauge round vs. watch round

**Gauge round** specifically means reading and logging instrumented values (temperatures, pressures, levels) at scheduled intervals. **Watch round** is the broader patrol that includes gauge reading plus visual/physical checks (leaks, unusual noise, security of gear) not captured by any instrument.

*In use:* "Gauge round's clean, but on the watch round I noticed the port generator's making a new knock — logging both separately."

**Common misuse:** treating the gauge round as the complete round and skipping the physical walk-through, missing exactly the kind of anomaly (smell, sound, vibration) no gauge reports.

### Holiday (coating defect)

A gap or thin spot in an applied coating system exposing bare or under-protected substrate — a specific, named defect term, not informal shorthand for "needs paint eventually."

*In use:* "Found a holiday on the port bulwark, about a hand's width, right down to bare metal — that's on this rotation's work order, not next quarter's."

**Common misuse:** using "holiday" loosely for any area due for repainting, rather than the specific finding of exposed substrate that changes the urgency of the repair.

### Dry film thickness (DFT) vs. wet film thickness (WFT)

**DFT** is the coating thickness after full cure, the figure specified against the coating system and checked with a magnetic or ultrasonic gauge. **WFT** is the thickness of the coating immediately after application, before solvent flash-off and cure, used to predict whether the cured DFT will land in spec.

*In use:* "WFT reads right for the specified 75-micron DFT once it flashes off — we're not under-applying."

**Common misuse:** checking only WFT at application and assuming it guarantees the specified DFT, without accounting for the solids content and shrinkage the data sheet specifies.

### Sacrificial anode vs. cathodic protection (impressed current)

**Sacrificial anode** is a reactive metal (commonly zinc or aluminum) bolted to the hull that corrodes preferentially, protecting the surrounding steel by giving up its own material. **Impressed-current cathodic protection (ICCP)** achieves the same protective effect electrically, using an external power source rather than a consumable anode.

*In use:* "Anodes are down to maybe 40% material left at this survey — schedule replacement before the next drydock, don't wait for them to waste away completely."

**Common misuse:** treating anode wastage as a cosmetic or low-priority finding rather than a corrosion-protection budget running down, which accelerates hull corrosion once anodes are depleted.

### Riding turn

An overlapping wrap on a winch or capstan drum that jams the line and prevents it from paying out or surging smoothly, occurring specifically because the line crossed over itself while under load.

*In use:* "There's a riding turn on the spring winch — ease it from the slack side, do not reach for that wrap while it's still under tension."

**Common misuse:** attempting to clear a riding turn by hand while the line is still loaded, rather than easing tension first — the jam itself is not the hazard, reaching into a loaded drum to fix it is.

### Rating forming part of a watch

The STCW-defined status (Sections A-II/4 deck, A-III/4 engine) of an unlicensed crew member certificated to stand a navigational or engine-room watch under an officer's or engineer's charge, distinct from a licensed watchkeeping officer who holds independent charge of the watch.

*In use:* "I'm rated to stand part of the engine-room watch, not to hold it independently — anything past a reported reading goes to the engineer of the watch."

**Common misuse:** treating "standing a watch" as equivalent regardless of rating vs. license, blurring who has independent charge and diagnostic authority versus who is executing and reporting under it.

### Bitt vs. bollard vs. cleat

**Bitt** is a paired, usually vertical mooring fitting on deck. **Bollard** is typically the shoreside (or larger shipboard) post a line is made fast around. **Cleat** is a smaller horn-shaped fitting for lighter lines (heaving lines, fenders), not rated for a ship's mooring load.

*In use:* "Run the spring to the bollard, not the cleat — that cleat's for the fender line only."

**Common misuse:** making a mooring line fast to a cleat rated for light-duty lines, which fails under real mooring tension rather than holding the load the bitt or bollard is built for.

### Muster station vs. abandon-ship station

**Muster station** is the assembly point where crew gather for a headcount during any drill or emergency. **Abandon-ship station** is the specific location (typically at the lifeboat/liferaft embarkation point) crew go to only when abandonment is actually ordered, reached after mustering.

*In use:* "Muster first, headcount confirmed, then we move to abandon-ship stations if the order's given — don't skip straight to the boats."

**Common misuse:** conflating the two and heading straight for the lifeboats at the first alarm, before the muster headcount has confirmed who's actually accounted for.

### Standing by the engine / bell book entry

"Standing by" means the engine room is manned and ready to answer telegraph/order changes immediately, logged with a specific timestamp in the bell book — distinct from the engine simply being running unattended in a periodically-unmanned-machinery-space mode.

*In use:* "Log 'standing by' at 0545 for the pilot boarding — telegraph orders start getting logged the moment that entry goes in."

**Common misuse:** treating "standing by" as a general description of readiness rather than a specific, timestamped log entry that changes the manning and response-time expectation for the watch.
