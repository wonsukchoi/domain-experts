# Postsecondary Educator — Vocabulary

### Backward design
Course design that starts from the desired learning outcomes and their assessments, then builds instruction to reach them, rather than starting from lecture content.
**In use:** "We're doing backward design this redesign — I wrote the final exam before I touched the slides."
**Common misuse:** Treated as just "write objectives first" while the lectures and assessments are still built independently and drift apart from each other anyway.

### Norm-referenced vs. criterion-referenced grading
Norm-referenced grades a student relative to the rest of the class (a curve); criterion-referenced grades against a fixed standard regardless of how the class performed.
**In use:** "This course is criterion-referenced — if everyone hits the rubric bar, everyone gets an A."
**Common misuse:** Assuming "curve" always raises grades; a curve applied to correct an easy exam can lower them.

### Item analysis (difficulty and discrimination)
Post-exam statistics computed per question: difficulty (percent of students who answered correctly) and discrimination (whether stronger students outperformed weaker students on that specific item).
**In use:** "Question 7 has a negative discrimination index — don't count it against anyone."
**Common misuse:** Treating a hard question (low difficulty index) as automatically a bad one; a hard question with high discrimination is functioning correctly.

### Rubric anchor
The specific, observable description tied to each score band on a criterion, distinct from the bare point value.
**In use:** "The anchor for a 'developing' thesis says it states a position but doesn't address a counterargument."
**Common misuse:** Writing rubrics with only numeric point ranges and no anchor text, which is exactly where grader-to-grader disagreement comes from.

### Formative vs. summative assessment
Formative assessment checks understanding during learning, typically low- or no-stakes, and is meant to inform teaching; summative assessment certifies learning at the end and informs the grade.
**In use:** "The weekly quizzes are formative — they don't count toward the grade, they tell me who needs a follow-up."
**Common misuse:** Grading formative work heavily, which pushes students to game it instead of using it to honestly surface confusion.

### Grade inflation vs. grade compression
Inflation is grades rising over time without matching attainment; compression is a narrowing range of grades — most students clustering near the top — which makes the grade less informative even without literal inflation.
**In use:** "It's not inflation, it's compression — the mean moved but so did the spread, we can't distinguish strong from adequate anymore."
**Common misuse:** Using the two terms interchangeably; they describe different statistical patterns and call for different fixes.

### Academic freedom (AAUP sense)
The protection of a faculty member's right to teach, research, and publish within their discipline without institutional retaliation — distinct from an unlimited right to say anything in any context.
**In use:** "Academic freedom protects my right to assign controversial primary sources in my discipline — it doesn't cover conduct unrelated to teaching or research."
**Common misuse:** Invoked as a blanket defense for conduct outside one's disciplinary competence or unrelated to teaching/research/publication.

### Course release
A reduction in teaching load, granted in exchange for research funding, an administrative role, or as negotiated compensation, tracked in course-equivalent units.
**In use:** "The grant buys out one course release this fall."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to be permanent or open-ended rather than a specific, term-bound trade recorded in the workload agreement.

### Syllabus addendum
A written, dated amendment to the syllabus changing a policy mid-term — the form a policy change needs to be enforceable or defensible in an appeal.
**In use:** "I posted a syllabus addendum extending the deadline — a verbal announcement alone won't hold up if this gets appealed."
**Common misuse:** Believing an in-class verbal announcement is equivalent to a written addendum.

### Halo effect (grading)
The tendency for an overall impression of a student's ability or effort to bias the score given on an individual, unrelated criterion.
**In use:** "Don't let the halo effect from her strong first two papers bump this criterion — grade what's in front of you."
**Common misuse:** Believing familiarity with a student's usual quality makes criterion-by-criterion grading unnecessary.

### Blind regrade
Re-scoring an assessment without seeing the original grade or grader identity, used to resolve a dispute without anchoring on the first number given.
**In use:** "Send it back for a blind regrade before we tell the student anything."
**Common misuse:** Having the same grader "double-check" their own work without concealing the original score — that isn't blind and rarely changes the outcome.

### Contact hours vs. credit hours
Contact hours are scheduled instructional time; credit hours are the institutional unit of academic credit, which by common accreditation convention assumes roughly two to three hours of outside work per contact hour.
**In use:** "This is a 3-credit course, so about 6–9 hours of outside work per week is the expected load on top of the 3 contact hours."
**Common misuse:** Treating contact hours and credit hours as the same number, leading to under- or over-assigned outside workload.

### Text-similarity score
An automated percentage of text-matching against a reference database — a lead requiring human review of the actual matched sources, not a plagiarism verdict on its own.
**In use:** "The similarity score is 45%, but half of that is a properly quoted block — pull the source report before filing anything."
**Common misuse:** Treating the raw percentage as proof of misconduct without reading what was actually matched.
