# Red flags

Smell tests a landscaping supervisor catches in the first look at a route sheet, a job-cost report, or a crew's day. Format for each: what it usually means, the first question to ask, and the data to pull before deciding anything.

## 1. Billable % under 65% on a route with a "full" stop count

**Usually means:** the route looks efficient on paper (lots of stops scheduled) but is losing the day to drive time between properties, not to slow crew work.

**First question:** "Pull the GPS breadcrumb — how much of today was driving versus parked at a stop?"

**Data to pull:** service-time vs. paid-time log per crew per day; stop-to-stop drive distances and times; whether stops cluster geographically or scatter across the service area.

## 2. Labor cost >5 points above the 30–35% target band for the route type

**Usually means:** either a drive-time/density problem (see #1) or the route was priced before it absorbed added stops/geography and needs re-quoting at renewal, not mid-contract.

**First question:** "Is this a billable-hours problem or a per-stop pricing problem — what does the route's billable % say?"

**Data to pull:** billable % for the same route/crew; original bid sheet and stop count vs. current stop count; wage/burden rate changes since the route was last priced.

## 3. A crew consistently running >20% over budgeted production time on a specific property

**Usually means:** the property's conditions (bed density, obstacles, slope, turf type) changed since the original estimate, or growth-stage timing (post-rain flush) is outpacing the mow cycle — rarely that the crew got slower.

**First question:** "When was this property last walked by someone other than the crew mowing it?"

**Data to pull:** original estimate assumptions (equipment, expected time); most recent site photos or notes; mow-cycle frequency versus growth rate for the season.

## 4. A spray application logged with no wind speed or temperature recorded

**Usually means:** the application happened but the record that would defend it in a drift complaint or regulatory audit doesn't exist — often a sign the applicator skipped the pre-application check entirely, not just the paperwork.

**First question:** "Walk me through what was recorded at the time of application — do we have wind and temp, or just product and rate?"

**Data to pull:** the application log for that stop; the certified applicator of record; whether a hand-held wind meter or weather app reading is standard crew equipment and was used.

## 5. A drift or off-target damage complaint on a day with forecast wind above 10 mph

**Usually means:** the go/no-go call was skipped or overridden under schedule pressure — check whether the crew lead had authority to reschedule without approval, because if not, the incentive was to spray anyway.

**First question:** "Was this stop rescheduled or pushed through — and who made that call?"

**Data to pull:** wind forecast and actual reading at time of application; crew lead's authority to reschedule spray stops unilaterally; frequency of similar overrides in the past season.

## 6. An uncertified technician applying product with the certified applicator "somewhere in the area"

**Usually means:** supervision coverage is being assumed rather than confirmed — the certified applicator may be outside the state's required response radius or unreachable, which becomes the branch's liability the moment an incident occurs.

**First question:** "Which certified applicator is assigned to this crew today, and can they reach this site within the required window right now?"

**Data to pull:** daily applicator-assignment sheet; certified applicators' actual locations/schedules that day; state lead-agency's specific supervision-response requirement for the product class in use.

## 7. Mowing continuing on visibly saturated turf or after >0.25" recent rain

**Usually means:** schedule pressure is overriding the turf-condition call, trading a day's slip for soil compaction, ruts, or disease spread that costs more in callbacks than the missed day.

**First question:** "What's driving the push to mow today — a hard customer deadline, or just staying on the weekly cycle?"

**Data to pull:** rainfall in the prior 24–48 hours; any rutting/compaction complaints on this property in the past; whether the stop can shift a day without breaching a contracted service-frequency term.

## 8. A route's per-stop price hasn't changed in 2+ seasons while wage/burden costs have risen

**Usually means:** the contract is quietly eroding margin every renewal cycle even if the crew executes it perfectly — job costing will show a route that "used to work" sliding out of the target labor-cost band with no change in crew performance.

**First question:** "When was this route last re-quoted, and what were wage and burden rates at the time versus now?"

**Data to pull:** route pricing history; wage/burden multiplier history; current labor-cost % versus target band.

## 9. A seasonal crew's start date is treated as the same date as the job offer

**Usually means:** spring route commitments were built against budgeted headcount instead of confirmed onboarding dates (background checks, H-2B processing, equipment certification) — the gap shows up in week one as missed or late routes.

**First question:** "Which seasonal hires are confirmed to start on the date the route plan assumes, and which are still pending?"

**Data to pull:** offer date vs. confirmed start date per seasonal hire; visa/processing status if applicable; route plan's headcount assumption per week for the ramp period.

## 10. A near-miss or minor injury involving a mower/trimmer with no incident report filed

**Usually means:** the crew treated it as routine (a kickback, a near-contact with a bystander, a guard removed to clear a jam) rather than a signal — the same conditions will produce a reportable injury eventually if the pattern isn't caught.

**First question:** "Walk me through exactly what happened, including anything that got fixed on the spot without being written down."

**Data to pull:** equipment maintenance log (guard/blade condition); PPE issued and worn at the time; any prior near-misses on the same equipment or crew in recent months.
