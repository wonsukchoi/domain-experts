# Vocabulary

Terms generalists conflate or use loosely that a landscaping supervisor keeps sharply separate, because the distinction changes what gets scheduled, staffed, or reported.

## Billable % vs. utilization

**Billable %** is service time actually performed at a customer's property divided by the crew's total paid time for the day — it captures drive time, breaks, and equipment downtime as loss. **Utilization** in some shop-management software instead measures scheduled hours against available crew-hours regardless of whether the scheduled time was actually productive on-site.

*In use:* "Utilization on the schedule board says we're at 95% booked, but billable % off the GPS logs is 62% — the board doesn't see the drive time."

*Common misuse:* reporting utilization to ownership as if it answers the profitability question; a fully booked schedule with poor route density still bleeds margin.

## Production rate vs. sold hours

**Production rate** is the estimator's or industry table's expected time to complete a task under assumed conditions (equipment, turf type, obstacle density). **Sold hours** is what the customer's contract price was built on, which may or may not match the production rate if the bid was aggressive or the property's conditions have changed.

*In use:* "We're running 18 minutes against a sold 12 — either the estimate was wrong for this property or conditions changed since it was bid."

*Common misuse:* treating any gap between actual and sold hours as a crew-performance problem before checking whether the original estimate matched the property.

## Direct supervision vs. general supervision (pesticide application)

**Direct supervision** means the certified applicator is physically present or in direct visual/voice contact while an uncertified person applies product — typically required for restricted-use pesticides. **General (or indirect) supervision** allows the certified applicator to be off-site but reachable and able to provide instructions, with a state-defined response time to reach the site if needed — typically the standard for general-use product.

*In use:* "This is a restricted-use product on that account, so the certified applicator rides with the crew — general supervision doesn't cover it."

*Common misuse:* assuming any certified applicator "on staff that day" satisfies supervision requirements regardless of product classification or actual reachability.

## Drift vs. runoff

**Drift** is pesticide/herbicide movement off the target site through the air at the time of application, driven primarily by wind and droplet size. **Runoff** is product movement off-site after application via water, driven by rainfall timing, slope, and soil saturation, independent of wind conditions at application time.

*In use:* "The complaint isn't drift — there was no wind that day — it's runoff, because we sprayed six hours before a storm the forecast had at 80%."

*Common misuse:* diagnosing every off-target complaint as a wind/drift problem, missing runoff cases where the application-time weather was fine but the post-application forecast wasn't checked.

## Route density vs. stop count

**Route density** measures how geographically clustered a day's stops are, expressed practically as drive-time share of the paid day. **Stop count** is simply how many properties are on the list — a schedule metric that says nothing about whether those stops are next door to each other or spread across town.

*In use:* "Don't judge the route by 24 stops — judge it by drive-time share. Twenty-four tight stops and twenty-four scattered stops are different jobs."

*Common misuse:* building or evaluating routes by stop count alone, which rewards packing the schedule and punishes nothing about geography.

## Labor burden vs. base wage

**Base wage** is the hourly rate paid to the worker. **Labor burden** is base wage multiplied by a loading factor that captures payroll taxes, workers' compensation (a landscaping-specific class-code rate, often higher than average due to injury frequency), and benefits — commonly 1.30–1.45x base wage in this industry.

*In use:* "$22 an hour looks fine until you burden it — fully loaded that crew costs closer to $30 an hour, and that's the number that has to clear the job-cost target."

*Common misuse:* job-costing or quoting off base wage alone, which understates true labor cost by roughly a third and quietly erodes margin on every job priced that way.

## Applicator of record

The certified/licensed individual whose name and license number attach to a specific pesticide application for recordkeeping and liability purposes — not necessarily the person who physically operated the sprayer, but the person accountable for the supervision and the record.

*In use:* "Put my license number as applicator of record on that job even though Marco ran the sprayer — I was the certified supervisor on-site."

*Common misuse:* leaving the applicator-of-record field blank or defaulting it to "whoever was on the crew that day" without confirming that person was actually certified or acting under proper supervision.

## Scalping vs. mowing height

**Mowing height** is the deck height set for a given cut. **Scalping** is cutting below a turf species' minimum stress height for the season, which removes too much leaf tissue at once and stresses or browns the turf — a distinct failure mode from simply "cutting it short."

*In use:* "That's not a low cut, that's a scalp — cool-season fescue shouldn't go below about 3 inches in summer heat, and we just took it to an inch and a half."

*Common misuse:* treating any customer complaint about a "too short" cut as a subjective preference issue rather than checking whether the height crossed the species' actual stress threshold.

## Restricted-use vs. general-use pesticide

**Restricted-use pesticides (RUP)** can only be purchased and applied by or under the direct supervision of a certified applicator, due to higher toxicity or environmental risk, and carry stricter recordkeeping and supervision rules. **General-use product** can be applied by uncertified technicians under looser, "reachable" supervision standards.

*In use:* "Check the label classification before you staff this job — if it's an RUP account, we need the certified applicator physically there, not just on call."

*Common misuse:* assuming supervision requirements are uniform across all pesticide work, when the classification on the product label is what actually sets the bar.

## Windshield time vs. service time

**Windshield time** is time spent driving between stops. **Service time** is time spent actually performing the contracted work at a stop. The ratio between them, not either number alone, is what determines whether a route is structurally profitable.

*In use:* "We didn't lose the day to slow mowing — windshield time was nearly half the shift. That's a route-design problem."

*Common misuse:* reporting only total hours worked, which hides whether the day was lost to driving or to service, and points the fix at the wrong lever.
