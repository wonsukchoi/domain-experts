# Vocabulary

Terms generalists flatten into one meaning that extension practitioners keep sharply separate.

## Bennett's Hierarchy

A seven-rung evaluation ladder (inputs, activities, participation, reactions, KASA change, practice change, end results/SEE) used to classify what a piece of evaluation data actually proves, from "we spent money" at the bottom to "the county's social/economic/environmental condition changed" at the top.

**In use:** "Our data sits at rung 4 — reactions. We need rung 6 before this goes in front of the commissioners."

**Common misuse:** treating any post-program survey as "impact" data regardless of which rung it actually measures — a satisfaction score and an adoption rate are both "evaluation," but only one answers a funder's real question.

## KASA change

Knowledge, Attitude, Skill, and Aspiration change — the rung-5 outcome measuring whether participants learned and internalized something, as distinct from whether they later acted on it (practice change, rung 6).

**In use:** "KASA scores moved, but we won't know if it's practice change until the follow-up survey next spring."

**Common misuse:** reporting a strong post-test knowledge score as evidence of behavior change — KASA change is a precondition for practice change, not proof of it.

## Fidelity vs. adaptation

**Fidelity** is delivering a curriculum's developer-designated core components (dosage, sequence, key techniques) as validated by its effectiveness research. **Adaptation** is adjusting delivery language, examples, or format to fit local context. Core components are non-negotiable if the program is to claim the original research's outcomes; everything else is legitimately adaptable.

**In use:** "We translated the materials and swapped the food examples — that's adaptation. We didn't cut the self-monitoring module — that's fidelity."

**Common misuse:** calling any content cut "adaptation for our audience" when it removes a core, research-validated component — that's a different, unvalidated program wearing the original's name.

## Logic model vs. work plan

A **logic model** states inputs, outputs, and outcomes (short/medium/long-term) working backward from the intended change. A **work plan** is the operational schedule of what gets delivered when. The logic model justifies why the work plan's activities should produce the claimed result.

**In use:** "The work plan says 8 sessions in March — the logic model is what tells us why 8 sessions should move the needle we're claiming."

**Common misuse:** writing a work plan (a calendar of sessions) and calling it a logic model — a calendar has no stated outcome chain to be held accountable to.

## Formula funds vs. county appropriation vs. grant funds

**Formula funds** (Smith-Lever Act) are federal money allocated to land-grant universities by statutory formula, largely stable year to year. **County appropriation** is discretionary local government funding, renewed (or not) annually by elected commissioners. **Grant funds** are competitive, categorical, and time-limited, usually tied to a specific population or outcome (EFNEP, SNAP-Ed).

**In use:** "Formula funds cover my base salary; the county appropriation covers this specific series; the SNAP-Ed grant only lets us serve income-eligible households."

**Common misuse:** treating all three as one undifferentiated "extension budget" — each has different accountability requirements, renewal risk, and allowed clientele, and conflating them leads to spending grant-restricted dollars on ineligible participants.

## Self-selection bias vs. targeted outreach

**Self-selection** is the pattern where program attendees are disproportionately people already engaged enough to show up — better-resourced, already-trusting of extension. **Targeted outreach** is a deliberate, often federally mandated effort (EFNEP, SNAP-Ed, USDA 2501) to reach a specific underserved population who would not self-select in.

**In use:** "Our open-enrollment list is self-selected toward larger operations — the 2501 outreach line item is how we reach the limited-resource farmers who won't show up to a flyer."

**Common misuse:** treating a well-attended open-enrollment program as evidence the program is reaching "the community," when the attendee demographics may be nothing like the county's actual target population.

## Diffusion of innovation / adopter categories

Rogers' model of how new practices spread through a population over time: innovators (~2.5%), early adopters (~13.5%), early majority (~34%), late majority (~34%), laggards (~16%). Adoption is a curve across time, not a single decision point.

**In use:** "We're one season in — expecting more than the innovators and early adopters to have already adopted is expecting the curve to skip stages."

**Common misuse:** judging a program a failure after one low-adoption season, when the diffusion curve predicts low early adoption as the expected shape, not a warning sign — the actual warning sign is near-zero attendance, not slow early adoption.

## EFNEP vs. SNAP-Ed

**EFNEP** (Expanded Food and Nutrition Education Program) is a federally funded, paraprofessional-delivered nutrition education program for low-income families and youth, direct-education focused. **SNAP-Ed** (SNAP Education) funds nutrition education and broader policy/systems/environmental change work for the SNAP-eligible population, often at larger scale with looser direct-contact requirements.

**In use:** "EFNEP gets us sustained one-on-one contact with a caseload; SNAP-Ed lets us also fund the policy work around school food access."

**Common misuse:** using the two funding streams interchangeably in reporting — each has distinct eligibility rules, reporting systems, and allowed activities, and a compliance review will separate them even if the office doesn't.

## Dosage (program fidelity)

The amount of a curriculum actually delivered — number of sessions, contact hours, or repetitions — as validated by the original effectiveness research. Distinct from "coverage" (how many people received it).

**In use:** "The research validated 6 sessions; we delivered 4 to fit the grant's timeline, so we should report this as a reduced-dosage adaptation, not the full validated program."

**Common misuse:** reporting reach (how many people attended) as if it were equivalent to dosage (how much of the program each person actually received) — 200 people who got 2 of 6 sessions is not the same claim as 200 people who got all 6.

## Master volunteer program

A structured volunteer certification model (Master Gardener, Master Food Preserver) requiring a fixed initial training-hour block, background screening, and an ongoing annual service-hour requirement to retain active/certified status — distinct from an informal, uncertified volunteer.

**In use:** "She trained as a Master Gardener two years ago but hasn't logged hours since — she's lapsed, not currently certified to represent the program."

**Common misuse:** treating anyone who once completed the training as permanently certified — certification in these programs is a maintained status, not a one-time credential.

## Impact statement vs. program report

An **impact statement** is a short, funder-facing document translating results into the funder's currency (dollars, ROI, avoided cost) at the highest available Bennett's Hierarchy rung. A **program report** is the fuller internal/university document with fidelity notes, KASA data, and methodology.

**In use:** "The commissioners get the one-page impact statement with the ROI number; the full program report with the survey instrument goes to the state office."

**Common misuse:** sending a funder the full program report instead of a translated impact statement — technically complete data that never answers the funder's actual question in their terms.
