# Red flags

Smell tests a veteran extension educator catches before a budget review or program audit goes sideways.

## 1. Only satisfaction data on file when a funding renewal is coming up

**Usually means:** the evaluation plan was built around what's easy to collect (a same-day form), not what the funder actually needs (practice change, end results). By the time the question "what did we get for this" is asked, there's no time left to collect the rungs that would answer it.

**First question:** "What Bennett's Hierarchy rung does our current data actually sit at, and when did we last collect anything above rung 4?"

**Data to pull:** post-session forms (rung 4) vs. any follow-up survey (rung 6) vs. any aggregate economic estimate (rung 7) — if only the first exists, the program has an evaluation gap, not necessarily a performance gap.

## 2. 90%+ satisfaction alongside flat or shrinking unique-attendee counts over 3+ years

**Usually means:** the program is serving the same repeat audience rather than reaching new clientele — satisfaction is real but reach has stalled, which reads as stagnation to a funder even when the content quality is fine.

**First question:** "Of this year's attendees, how many are new versus repeat from the last two years?"

**Data to pull:** sign-in sheets cross-referenced by name/household across years; demographic comparison of the attendee list against the county's target population (Census of Agriculture demographics, SNAP-Ed eligibility maps).

## 3. Same names on the sign-in sheet for three-plus consecutive years

**Usually means:** self-selection has locked in — the program has become a standing benefit for an engaged subgroup rather than an outreach effort, and any federally mandated targeted population (limited-resource farmers, EFNEP-eligible households) is very likely underreached.

**First question:** "If a USDA 2501 or EFNEP reviewer asked who we're *not* reaching, what would we show them?"

**Data to pull:** attendee list demographics vs. county population demographics; any documented non-participant outreach attempt in the last cycle.

## 4. A curriculum's core components no longer match the fidelity checklist

**Usually means:** time pressure or a shorter grant-funded session slot led to cutting content the developer's effectiveness studies say is load-bearing — the program is still branded with the validated curriculum's name but no longer entitled to its outcome claims.

**First question:** "Which core-component box on the fidelity checklist did we skip, and why?"

**Data to pull:** the curriculum developer's published fidelity checklist against the actual session plan delivered; session length and sequence as taught vs. as designed.

## 5. Volunteer service-hour logs with lapses or missing background-check dates

**Usually means:** a certification-dependent volunteer (Master Gardener, 4-H leader) is operating past their liability coverage window without anyone noticing until an incident forces the question.

**First question:** "Is every volunteer currently delivering programming inside their certified hour/screening window?"

**Data to pull:** volunteer roster with last-training-date, last-screening-date, and logged service hours year-to-date; program MOU terms on what "active" status requires.

## 6. A grant's compliance reporting consumes more staff time than the FTE it funds

**Usually means:** the grant was accepted for the funding, not evaluated for net capacity — a categorical grant that requires more hours of reporting than it buys in delivery capacity is quietly cannibalizing the core program it was meant to protect.

**First question:** "How many staff hours per month does this grant's reporting take, versus how many delivery hours does it fund?"

**Data to pull:** timesheet allocation by funding source; grant reporting requirements and deadlines; comparison against the FTE or dollar amount the grant provides.

## 7. No advisory committee meeting or listening session in the past 12 months

**Usually means:** the program's priorities are running on inertia from whenever the plan was last written, not on a current read of the county's needs — this is invisible until a new problem (a crop disease, a demographic shift) emerges that the program isn't positioned to address.

**First question:** "When did we last hear from someone who isn't already a repeat attendee?"

**Data to pull:** advisory committee meeting minutes and attendance roster; date and format of the last non-participant listening session.

## 8. An "impact report" evaluated the same week as the final session

**Usually means:** what's being reported is reaction (rung 4) relabeled as impact — for any program whose target behavior takes a season or budget cycle to show up, this data cannot yet contain practice change or end results, regardless of the label on the report.

**First question:** "What's the earliest point at which the taught behavior could plausibly have been adopted and observed?"

**Data to pull:** the program's own logic model outcome timeline; the actual collection date of the "impact" data against that timeline.

## 9. A commissioner or dean asks "what did we get for the money" and the honest answer is attendance and satisfaction only

**Usually means:** the evaluation gap has become a funding-risk event — this is red flag #1 arriving at the worst possible moment, in a public meeting instead of a planning session.

**First question:** "What's the fastest legitimate follow-up data we can collect before the next budget vote, and what conservative assumption will we state for non-responders?"

**Data to pull:** the full attendee list for follow-up outreach; any secondary data (county economic indicators) that can support an end-result estimate on short notice.
