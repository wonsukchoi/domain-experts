# Vocabulary

### ALARA (As Low As Reasonably Achievable)
The regulatory principle that dose must be kept below the legal limit and further reduced to the extent reasonable given cost, schedule, and benefit — not driven to an absolute minimum regardless of cost.
**In use:** "We could shield that another 10%, but the job would run two extra hours for a dose reduction that's inside our margin already — that's not an ALARA call, that's just spending dose budget on paperwork."
**Common misuse:** Treating ALARA as "zero dose or bust," which either burns resources on immaterial reductions or stalls an already-adequate plan in endless review.

### TEDE (Total Effective Dose Equivalent)
The whole-body dose metric that sums external exposure and committed internal dose from any intake, used to compare against the 5 rem/year regulatory limit.
**In use:** "His TEDE for the year is 1.8 rem — that's fine against the 5 rem limit, but it's already past our 1.5 rem administrative trigger for this quarter."
**Common misuse:** Confusing TEDE with a single-organ or skin (shallow dose equivalent) reading, which have their own separate limits and don't roll into TEDE the same way.

### DAC (Derived Air Concentration)
The airborne concentration of a specific radionuclide that, if breathed continuously for a working year (2,000 hours), would deliver the annual intake dose limit; used to set air-sampling action levels.
**In use:** "That grinding job's air sample came back at 30% of DAC for Co-60 — we're upgrading to supplied-air before the next cut."
**Common misuse:** Treating "below DAC" as automatically safe regardless of duration — DAC is a concentration, and cumulative exposure (DAC-hours) depends on both concentration and time.

### RWP (Radiation Work Permit)
The site's binding authorization document for work in a radiologically controlled area, specifying dose limits, required PPE, calculated stay time, and monitoring requirements for that specific job.
**In use:** "The RWP caps this at 100 mrem individual — if your EPD hits that, you're out, no exceptions written into the permit."
**Common misuse:** Treating the RWP as a paperwork formality to get signed rather than a binding stop-work authority that overrides schedule pressure.

### Contamination vs. exposure
Two distinct hazards: contamination is loose radioactive material that can spread, be inhaled, or be ingested; exposure (dose) is energy deposited by a radiation field, either directly from outside the body or from material already inside it.
**In use:** "That's a contamination problem, not an exposure problem — dose rate at the boundary is background, but the smear came back hot."
**Common misuse:** Calling anything radiologically elevated "hot" without distinguishing which hazard is present, which leads to reaching for the wrong instrument and the wrong control (decon vs. shielding/distance).

### Frisking
Surveying personnel, clothing, or equipment with a hand-held or portal detector for removable contamination, typically on exit from a controlled area.
**In use:** "Re-frisk your left hand at the same spot before you call it a false alarm — the portal doesn't lie twice in a row."
**Common misuse:** Scanning faster than the detector's specified response time or recommended scan rate, which produces false negatives that look like a clean frisk.

### Hot spot
A localized area of elevated dose rate distinct from the surrounding general area, often not reflected in a general-area survey taken before the condition that created it.
**In use:** "General area's 45 mrem/hr, but there's a hot spot at the valve body reading 220 — don't plan the job off the posting."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a general-area posting covers every point within it, missing point sources that a broader survey averages out.

### Stay time
The calculated maximum duration a worker can remain in a radiation field and stay within a dose limit or job budget, given the actual dose rate at the work point.
**In use:** "Stay time at 220 mrem/hr for a 100 mrem alarm is 27 minutes — that's what goes on the briefing, not 'try to be quick.'"
**Common misuse:** Calculating stay time from the general-area average dose rate instead of the highest dose rate the worker's body will actually occupy during the task.

### Source check (instrument check)
Verifying a survey instrument reads within an established range against a known check source immediately before use, distinct from a full periodic calibration.
**In use:** "Source check's within range, but it's still due for cal next week — usable today, flag it for the cal group."
**Common misuse:** Treating a passed source check as proof the instrument is fully in calibration, when it only confirms gross function at one energy/geometry, not the full calibrated response curve.

### Administrative control level (ACL)
An internally set dose, dose-rate, or contamination trigger that is stricter than the regulatory limit, meant to prompt review well before conditions approach the legal limit.
**In use:** "We're over the ACL for this job, not over the reg limit — that still means it stops for an ALARA review before it continues."
**Common misuse:** Conflating an ACL exceedance with a regulatory violation; it's a self-imposed early-warning trigger, and exceeding it is a program success (it caught the trend), not a license event.

### Removable contamination (smear/swipe survey)
Activity measured by wiping a defined surface area with filter paper and counting the paper, distinct from fixed or total contamination measured by direct instrument scan.
**In use:** "Smear came back at 420 dpm/100 cm² removable — under the release limit, item's clear to leave."
**Common misuse:** Reporting a direct-scan (fixed + removable combined) reading as if it were a removable-only result, which overstates what could actually spread.

### Whole-body counter (WBC)
An instrument that measures internal radionuclide burden by detecting gamma emissions from inside the body, used to confirm or bound a suspected intake.
**In use:** "WBC came back clean, but for I-131 that doesn't rule out an intake yet — it needs to redistribute to the thyroid first, recheck in 24 hours."
**Common misuse:** Treating an immediate post-event WBC result as conclusive when some radionuclides need time to redistribute in the body before they're detectable.

### DAC-hour
A cumulative airborne exposure metric — concentration (in units of DAC) multiplied by exposure time — used to track intake risk the way dose-rate times stay time tracks external dose.
**In use:** "He's logged 0.4 DAC-hours on this job already; two more hours at this level and we're pulling him for the shift."
**Common misuse:** Skipping DAC-hour tracking because a single sample read below 100% DAC, ignoring that sustained lower-level exposure accumulates the same way.

### Committed dose
The dose projected to accrue over the following 50 years from radioactive material taken into the body in a single intake, attributed entirely to the year the intake occurred for dose-of-record purposes.
**In use:** "That intake's committed dose gets booked against this year's TEDE, even though most of it won't actually deposit for years."
**Common misuse:** Assuming an intake's dose only "counts" in the years the radionuclide is physically still in the body, rather than being front-loaded onto the year of intake by regulation.

### Free-release limit
The site- and radionuclide-specific contamination threshold below which material or equipment can leave a radiologically controlled area without being controlled as radioactive material.
**In use:** "It reads background on the quick check, but it doesn't leave the RCA without a documented smear against the actual free-release limit."
**Common misuse:** Treating "reads background" on an informal frisk as equivalent to a documented release survey with instrument ID, calibration date, and a smear count on record.
