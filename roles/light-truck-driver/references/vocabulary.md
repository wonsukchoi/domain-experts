# Light Truck Driver — Vocabulary

### GVWR (Gross Vehicle Weight Rating)
The manufacturer-certified maximum loaded weight for a single vehicle, stamped on the vehicle's data plate — a fixed rating, not the truck's actual weight on a given day.
**In use:** "This box truck's GVWR is 19,500 — we're under 26,001, so it's still non-CDL, but we're over the 10,001 mark for the medical card requirement."
**Common misuse:** Checking today's loaded weight on a scale instead of the certified rating, and concluding a truck is "fine" because it's lightly loaded when its rating still crosses a threshold.

### GCWR (Gross Combination Weight Rating)
The manufacturer-certified maximum combined weight of a power unit plus a towed unit — relevant when a trailer is added to a route vehicle.
**In use:** "Add the pup trailer and our GCWR is 28,000 — that crosses the 26,001 line even though the truck alone is under it."
**Common misuse:** Evaluating only the truck's GVWR when a trailer is attached, missing that the combination rating is the one that governs CDL requirement.

### 26,001 lb threshold
The GVWR or GCWR (with a towed unit over 10,000 lb) at or above which a CDL is federally required to operate the vehicle.
**In use:** "That reassignment puts us at 27,000 combined — nobody on this route has a CDL, so we can't take that truck out."
**Common misuse:** Treating the threshold as approximate or negotiable based on how the vehicle handles, rather than a fixed rating check.

### Non-CDL commercial motor vehicle (CMV)
A vehicle rated 10,001–26,000 lb GVWR that does not require a CDL but is still a regulated commercial motor vehicle under FMCSA's broader CMV definition.
**In use:** "We don't need a CDL for this truck, but it's still a CMV over 10,001 — the medical card and DQ file requirements still apply."
**Common misuse:** Assuming "no CDL required" means no federal driver-qualification or medical-certification requirements apply at all.

### DOT medical card (medical certificate)
A physical-qualification certification required for CMV drivers, including non-CDL drivers of vehicles over 10,001 lb GVWR operating in interstate commerce.
**In use:** "My medical card's still current through next spring — that's on file before I take the 19,500-pound truck out."
**Common misuse:** Treating the medical card as a CDL-only requirement and skipping it for non-CDL trucks that still meet the CMV weight threshold.

### Driver-qualification (DQ) file
The federally required file of a CMV driver's qualifications — medical certificate, road test or equivalent, employment history, violation record — maintained by the employing carrier.
**In use:** "The DQ file needs the current medical card in it before this driver is dispatched on the 19,500-pound truck."
**Common misuse:** Maintaining the file only for CDL drivers and omitting non-CDL drivers who are operating CMVs above the 10,001 lb threshold.

### GOAL (Get Out And Look)
The backing protocol of exiting the vehicle to walk and check the intended reverse path before backing, used at any stop requiring a reverse maneuver.
**In use:** "Tight cul-de-sac, doing a full GOAL before I back in — not skipping it just because I'm running late."
**Common misuse:** Treating GOAL as optional on "familiar" stops or when short on time, which is precisely where the incident-frequency data says not to skip it.

### Proof of delivery (POD)
The record — signature, ID-verified signature, or geotagged photo — captured at the moment of delivery that establishes the package was placed, and that shifts the burden of proving non-delivery onto the recipient once disputed.
**In use:** "Photo shows it on the porch, timestamped and geotagged — that's the POD if this delivery gets disputed."
**Common misuse:** Scanning a package as delivered before physically placing it, which creates a POD record that doesn't match what actually happened and becomes evidence against the driver if disputed.

### Adult signature required
A delivery classification (age-restricted or high-value items) requiring an ID-verified signature from someone of the required minimum age before release.
**In use:** "Can't leave this on the porch — it's adult-signature, need ID and a signature or it goes back to the facility."
**Common misuse:** Substituting a release photo for the required ID-verified signature to avoid a redelivery attempt.

### DCR (Delivery Completion Rate)
A last-mile carrier scorecard metric measuring the percentage of assigned stops completed successfully within a service period, commonly used to evaluate route or driver performance.
**In use:** "DCR dropped this week because of the reroute — that's a route-assignment issue, not a pace issue."
**Common misuse:** Reading a DCR dip as automatically pace- or effort-related without checking whether stops were reassigned, added, or blocked by a hazard.

### Route density / stops-per-hour benchmark
The expected pace of stop completion for a given route type (dense urban, suburban, rural), used to plan a shift's stop count against its on-duty time budget.
**In use:** "This is a suburban route, so 15 to 20 stops an hour is the planning number, not the 25 we'd expect downtown."
**Common misuse:** Applying one route type's benchmark to a different density of route, producing an unrealistic time budget in either direction.

### Telematics safety score
An AI-dashcam or fleet-telematics-derived score (Netradyne/Mentor-style) aggregating flagged driving events — hard braking, following distance, sign/signal violations, distraction — into a single band used for coaching and scorecards.
**In use:** "Score dropped into the Fair band this month — pulling the day-by-day event log to see which days are driving it."
**Common misuse:** Treating the aggregate score as the diagnosis instead of a starting point, without pulling the underlying event log to find the specific days or stop types responsible.

### Short-haul (150-air-mile) exception
An FMCSA provision letting CMV drivers, CDL or non-CDL, use time records instead of full duty logs when operating within 150 air miles of their reporting location and returning the same day.
**In use:** "We're inside the 150 air-mile radius and back at the depot tonight, so it's a time record, not a full log."
**Common misuse:** Assuming the exception removes Hours of Service limits entirely rather than just the detailed-logging requirement.

### Carmack Amendment / released value
The federal framework (49 U.S.C. §14706) governing carrier liability for lost or damaged freight, including the concept of a released or limited value that caps a carrier's liability absent a higher declared value.
**In use:** "Without a declared value on file, the claim is capped under the released-value terms, not the item's replacement cost."
**Common misuse:** Assuming full replacement-value liability applies automatically regardless of what value was declared or released at shipment.
