# Tutor — Vocabulary

### Diagnostic assessment
A pre-instruction assessment used to identify the specific skills a student has and hasn't mastered, tagged by skill cluster before scoring.
**In use:** "The diagnostic showed her integer operations are solid — the gap is entirely in distributive property."
**Common misuse:** Treated as interchangeable with a regular quiz or the current unit's homework. A diagnostic deliberately mixes current-unit and prerequisite items; a quiz on this week's material can't isolate an upstream gap because it never tests the prerequisite skill directly.

### Formative check
A low-stakes assessment used to inform the next session's plan, not to grade the student.
**In use:** "That exit check was formative — it tells me whether to move on or spend session 4 on the same skill."
**Common misuse:** Scored and reported to the parent with the same weight as a real retest, which pressures the tutor to make every check look good rather than honestly diagnostic.

### Automaticity
The ability to perform a skill quickly and without conscious effort, distinct from conceptual understanding.
**In use:** "He understands why 7×8 is 56, he just can't retrieve it fast enough — that's an automaticity gap, not a conceptual one."
**Common misuse:** Conflated with "doesn't understand it," leading to re-teaching the concept when the actual fix is timed drilling for speed.

### Prerequisite skill
An earlier-taught skill that a current skill depends on; a break here causes downstream errors that look like a different problem.
**In use:** "The two-step-equation errors trace back to a prerequisite gap in distributive property from last year."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to be one grade level back by default. Prerequisite chains often go back two or more years, and the diagnostic — not an assumption about grade level — is what should locate it.

### Score band
A range of scores on a standardized test, used because gains within a band behave differently (steeper at the low end, flatter near the ceiling) than a single point estimate suggests.
**In use:** "She's in the 500-600 band, where a 40-point jump in a month is realistic — that won't repeat once she's in the 650-700 band."
**Common misuse:** Extrapolated linearly — assuming the rate of the first gain will repeat every month, rather than accounting for the diminishing-returns shape of the curve.

### Spaced review / spaced retrieval
Re-checking a previously taught skill after a delay (not the same session), to test whether it was actually retained rather than just recalled short-term.
**In use:** "It scored 100% on the session-3 exit check, but I want a spaced-review check in session 5 before I call it retained."
**Common misuse:** Skipped because the same-day exit check already looked good, treating short-term recall as proof of retention.

### Leverage (gap ranking)
How many current-unit problems a specific skill gap is blocking, used to sequence limited session time toward the highest-impact fix first.
**In use:** "Distributive property is the highest-leverage gap — it's blocking 9 of the 12 homework problems we sampled."
**Common misuse:** Sequenced by which gap the client mentioned most recently or found most frustrating, rather than by how many downstream problems it actually blocks.

### Effect size
A standardized measure (e.g., standard deviations) of how much an intervention like tutoring moves an outcome, used to set realistic expectations instead of anecdote.
**In use:** "Human tutoring's effect size research puts typical gains around 0.79 SD — that's real, but it's not the old '2 sigma' number people still quote."
**Common misuse:** Cited as a guarantee for any individual student, when it's a population-level average across a meta-analysis, not a promise for one case.

### Backslide
An apparent drop in performance on a previously "mastered" skill, revealed by a later spaced-review check.
**In use:** "The fraction-ops backslide at the session-5 spot check is exactly why we don't call something retained after one clean retest."
**Common misuse:** Treated as evidence the tutoring "didn't work," when it's the expected signal that a skill needs one more review cycle — the alternative (not checking) just hides the same problem until the real test.

### Isolating the variable
Asking a follow-up diagnostic question that changes only one element of a problem (e.g., the numbers, not the structure) to determine exactly which step is causing an error.
**In use:** "I swapped the numbers but kept the same equation structure to isolate whether the error was the distributive step or the combining-like-terms step."
**Common misuse:** Skipped in favor of guessing which step is broken from a single wrong answer, when more than one step could plausibly explain the same mistake.

### Session budget
The fixed, paid number of sessions available, which determines whether the tutoring plan can afford to cover every visible weakness or must prioritize by leverage.
**In use:** "With an 8-session budget, we're targeting the two highest-leverage gaps, not all five weaknesses the diagnostic surfaced."
**Common misuse:** Ignored when planning, leading to a plan that would be ideal with unlimited sessions but doesn't fit the number actually purchased.

### Referral (outside tutoring's scope)
Recommending the family pursue an outside evaluation (educational, medical, or psychological) when a gap isn't closing despite targeted, repeated tutoring effort.
**In use:** "Four sessions of direct work on this and no diagnostic movement — I want to flag a referral conversation with the parent before we keep billing sessions against it."
**Common misuse:** Avoided out of a sense that recommending outside help looks like admitting tutoring failed, when it's the correct call once the pattern shows the cause is likely outside tutoring's reach.
