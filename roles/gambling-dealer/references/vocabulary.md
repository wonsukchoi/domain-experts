# Vocabulary — Gambling Dealer

### Past posting
Adding chips to a bet, or increasing its value, after the outcome is already determined but before the dealer pays or collects.
**In use:** "Seat 4's stack went from two green to two green and a black right after the flop hit — that's a past-post, freeze it and call the floor."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a synonym for any chip movement near a resolved bet; the defining fact is timing relative to the outcome, not the movement itself — a player squaring their own unresolved bet isn't a past-post.

### Pinching
Removing a chip from a losing bet after the outcome is known but before the dealer collects it, so the loss is smaller than it should be.
**In use:** "That was a pinch, not a drop — the stack was three chips before the card flipped and two after."
**Common misuse:** Confused with a player simply picking up chips from a bet that already lost cleanly and was already collected — pinching specifically means the removal happens in the gap before collection.

### Penetration
The fraction of the shoe dealt before the cut card is reached and a reshuffle occurs, e.g. 234 of 312 cards (75%) in a six-deck game with the cut card at 1.5 decks from the back.
**In use:** "This property runs 75% penetration on six-deck — deeper than that and card counters get too much information before the reshuffle."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to be a fixed industry standard; it's a property-specific setting that trades game speed against advantage-play exposure, and it varies by casino and even by pit.

### Toke
A tip given directly to the dealer, typically placed as a bet on the player's behalf or handed over after a win, usually pooled and split among dealers per shift under house policy.
**In use:** "Player tipped a toke bet on my behalf — if it wins it goes in the pool same as a cash toke."
**Common misuse:** Treated as personal income handled at the dealer's discretion; most properties require tokes to go into a shared pool split per a fixed formula, not kept individually.

### Fill / credit
A fill moves chips from the cage to a table's tray (replenishing the bank); a credit moves chips from the table back to the cage (removing excess). Both require a signed three-way slip (dealer, floor, cage).
**In use:** "Tray's under threshold — that's a fill, not a credit; we need chips coming to the table, not leaving it."
**Common misuse:** The two terms get swapped colloquially; fill and credit are directionally opposite and the slip paperwork differs accordingly.

### Marker
A signed credit instrument that lets an approved patron draw chips against a pre-set credit line, collectible like a check.
**In use:** "Marker request at seat 2 — that's a floor and credit-desk call, not something I extend at the table."
**Common misuse:** Treated as an on-the-spot dealer decision or a casual IOU; it's a formal, pre-approved credit instrument that requires floor/credit-desk verification of remaining headroom before issuance.

### Hole card
The dealer's face-down card in games like blackjack, revealed only after all player decisions are complete.
**In use:** "Hole card stays down until every seat has acted — flashing it early changes the whole hand's information."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to be revealed whenever convenient for pacing; the timing (after all player decisions, before the dealer draws) is a fixed procedural point, not a judgment call.

### Hole-carding
An advantage-play technique where a player catches a glimpse of the dealer's hole card through a dealing angle or tell, then plays with that information.
**In use:** "That player's win rate on hands where they're seated at third base is way above expected — worth a hole-carding review with surveillance."
**Common misuse:** Confused with card counting; hole-carding exploits a procedural/mechanical leak (dealing angle, card handling), not probability tracking, and the fix is a procedure or equipment change, not a betting-pattern policy.

### Cut card
A solid, blank card used to cut the deck/shoe and to mark the point at which the shoe is exhausted and a reshuffle is triggered — never a card with a face value that could be mistaken for a playing card.
**In use:** "Offer the cut card to the player at first base before you start the new shoe."
**Common misuse:** Treated as interchangeable with the burn card; the cut card marks penetration and is offered to a player to cut, the burn card is discarded by the dealer after the cut.

### Burn card
The top card of a freshly shuffled and cut shoe, discarded face-down before dealing begins, as a control against a marked or tracked top card.
**In use:** "Burn one before the first hand of every new shoe — no exceptions even if the count looks fine."
**Common misuse:** Skipped as a formality on a slow shift; it's a control against a specific exploit (a known or marked top card), not a ritual pause.

### No more bets
The dealer's explicit verbal/gesture call marking the exact moment after which no chip may be added to or removed from any active wager.
**In use:** "Called 'no more bets' the second the ball dropped — anything that moves after that is a procedural violation, full stop."
**Common misuse:** Treated as advisory or approximate timing; it's the bright-line moment the payout-window control depends on, and its exact timing (not "around when the outcome happens") is what makes past-posting detectable.

### Eye in the sky
The overhead surveillance camera system monitoring tables, staffed by security/surveillance separate from the pit.
**In use:** "Flag it to the floor and let the eye in the sky pull the footage — don't try to settle it from what you saw."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to replace the dealer's own procedural discipline ("the camera will catch it anyway"); the camera is a verification layer for disputes, not a substitute for correct real-time procedure.

### Continuous shuffle machine (CSM)
A device that shuffles discards back into play continuously rather than dealing down a fixed shoe to a cut card, eliminating a fixed-penetration cycle.
**In use:** "This table runs a CSM, so there's no cut-card penetration to track — every hand comes from a freshly randomized set."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to always favor the house identically to a hand-shuffled shoe; it changes the game's actual dynamics (no penetration to exploit or track) rather than just speeding up dealing.

### Structuring
Splitting cash transactions into amounts under the $10,000 CTR threshold specifically to avoid triggering Title 31 reporting.
**In use:** "Patron asked twice how much they could cash out without paperwork — that's a structuring flag for compliance, not a dealer-level call."
**Common misuse:** Treated as the dealer's problem to police at the table; the dealer's role is to notice and flag the pattern, the determination and filing sit with the cage/compliance function.
