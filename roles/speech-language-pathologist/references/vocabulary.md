# Speech-Language Pathologist — Vocabulary

### Silent aspiration
Food or liquid entering the airway below the vocal folds without triggering a cough or any other observable external sign.
**In use:** "The risk profile here is high enough that I don't trust a clean bedside exam to rule out silent aspiration."
**Common misuse:** Assuming "no coughing during the meal" means the patient isn't aspirating — silent aspiration is defined by the absence of that exact sign.

### Clinical (bedside) swallow evaluation
A non-instrumental swallow assessment using trial swallows, oral-motor exam, and observation of signs like cough and vocal quality change.
**In use:** "Bedside cleared her for nectar-thick, but with this risk profile I still want an instrumental study before advancing further."
**Common misuse:** Treating a clean bedside exam as conclusive proof of swallow safety rather than a screening tool with a known false-negative rate.

### VFSS (videofluoroscopic swallow study)
An instrumental, radiographic swallow study (modified barium swallow) that directly visualizes the swallow in real time, including any silent aspiration.
**In use:** "We need a VFSS to see what's actually happening with the thin liquids — bedside can't tell us if it's silent."
**Common misuse:** Ordering a VFSS reflexively for every dysphagia referral, exposing low-risk patients to unnecessary radiation and cost when a clear bedside exam is sufficient.

### FEES (fiberoptic endoscopic evaluation of swallowing)
An instrumental swallow study using a flexible endoscope to directly visualize the pharynx and larynx during swallowing, without radiation exposure.
**In use:** "FEES is a better fit here since she can't be transported to radiology, and we avoid the radiation dose."
**Common misuse:** Assuming FEES and VFSS are interchangeable — FEES can't visualize the oral phase or the moment of swallow itself (the "white-out" period), which VFSS captures.

### Dysphagia
A disorder of swallowing that can occur at the oral, pharyngeal, or esophageal phase, with distinct causes and management at each phase.
**In use:** "This is pharyngeal-phase dysphagia — the oral phase is intact, so a straw or utensil modification won't fix it."
**Common misuse:** Treating "dysphagia" as a single undifferentiated problem rather than phase-specific, leading to a mismatched intervention (e.g., oral-phase strategies for a pharyngeal-phase deficit).

### Aphasia
An acquired language disorder, typically from stroke or brain injury, affecting production and/or comprehension of language — distinct from a motor-speech disorder like dysarthria or apraxia.
**In use:** "This is expressive aphasia, not dysarthria — her articulators work fine, she can't retrieve or sequence the words."
**Common misuse:** Using "aphasia" and "dysarthria" interchangeably — aphasia is a language problem, dysarthria is a motor-execution problem, and they require different treatment approaches.

### Apraxia of speech
A motor-planning disorder where the patient knows what they want to say but has inconsistent difficulty programming the articulatory movements to say it, often producing inconsistent errors on the same word across attempts.
**In use:** "The inconsistency on repeated trials of the same word is the tell for apraxia, not a language-retrieval problem."
**Common misuse:** Conflating apraxia with aphasia because both affect "talking" — apraxia is a planning/execution deficit with intact language knowledge, requiring motor-based treatment, not language-retrieval treatment.

### AAC (augmentative and alternative communication)
Any communication method or device that supplements or replaces natural speech, ranging from low-tech picture boards to high-tech eye-gaze devices.
**In use:** "Let's start her on a low-tech core-vocabulary board while the high-tech device request goes through insurance."
**Common misuse:** Treating AAC as a last resort only after verbal speech has definitively failed, rather than a parallel-track option introduced early when functional communication is insufficient.

### Feature matching
The process of matching a patient's motor, cognitive, sensory, and linguistic profile to the specific features of an AAC system (access method, symbol set, vocabulary organization) rather than choosing a device by popularity or availability.
**In use:** "Feature matching pointed us to switch-scanning access, not direct selection — his fine-motor control isn't there yet."
**Common misuse:** Selecting an AAC device based on what's in stock or what a similar patient used, skipping the individualized access and vocabulary assessment.

### Fluency (in aphasia classification)
The rate, effort, and grammatical complexity of spontaneous speech output — a key axis (alongside comprehension and repetition) for classifying aphasia type, distinct from stuttering-related fluency.
**In use:** "His output is fluent but empty — lots of words, low information content, which points toward Wernicke's."
**Common misuse:** Confusing aphasia-classification "fluency" with the fluency-disorder sense of the word (stuttering) — they're unrelated uses of the same term.

### Language sample analysis
Collecting and analyzing a naturalistic speech/language sample (conversation, narrative retell) to assess grammar, vocabulary diversity, and narrative structure, as a complement to standardized test scores.
**In use:** "The standard score looked borderline, but the language sample showed clear narrative-sequencing gaps that the test didn't capture."
**Common misuse:** Relying on standardized test scores alone without a language sample, missing functional deficits that don't show up on a structured test format.

### Cervical auscultation
Listening with a stethoscope over the larynx/pharynx during swallowing to detect abnormal sounds suggestive of dysphagia, used as an adjunct to (not a replacement for) visual/behavioral observation during a bedside exam.
**In use:** "Cervical auscultation picked up a gurgle after the thin-liquid trial that lines up with the wet vocal quality we heard."
**Common misuse:** Treating cervical auscultation findings as diagnostic on their own — evidence on its accuracy is mixed, and it's used to support, not replace, the rest of the clinical exam.
