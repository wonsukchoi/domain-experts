# Speech-Language Pathologist — Red Flags

### Wet or gurgly vocal quality after any swallow trial, on a consistency intended for the diet order
- **Usually means:** Residue is pooling at or above the vocal folds — a strong bedside predictor of aspiration risk on that consistency.
- **First question:** Did the wet quality clear with a cough/reswallow, or persist across multiple trials?
- **Data to pull:** Trial-by-trial cervical auscultation notes, cough-response strength on the same trials.

### 2 or more silent-aspiration risk factors present with no instrumental study yet ordered
- **Usually means:** Bedside clearance alone is not reliable enough for this patient — silent aspiration has no external bedside sign in this risk band.
- **First question:** Which specific risk factors are present, and has an instrumental study (VFSS/FEES) been discussed with the team?
- **Data to pull:** Risk-factor checklist, recent pneumonia/respiratory history, stroke-territory or neurologic-diagnosis documentation.

### Child's language assessment limited to an articulation/sound-production test only
- **Usually means:** A broader language disorder (vocabulary, grammar, narrative) may be present and undiagnosed — sound errors are often the visible surface of a larger deficit.
- **First question:** Was a standardized receptive/expressive language measure administered, or only an articulation inventory?
- **Data to pull:** Full assessment battery list, referral question as originally written.

### Aphasia type classified without a repetition-testing component
- **Usually means:** Conduction aphasia cannot be distinguished from a milder fluent/non-fluent presentation without repetition data — the type label may be wrong.
- **First question:** What was the patient's performance on a standardized repetition subtest?
- **Data to pull:** Full aphasia battery subscores (fluency, comprehension, repetition, naming).

### AAC introduced only after verbal therapy has been tried and "failed" for months
- **Usually means:** A parallel-track opportunity was missed — early AAC introduction generally supports rather than suppresses natural speech development/recovery.
- **First question:** What was the reasoning for delaying an AAC evaluation, and is there evidence (not assumption) that it would slow verbal progress?
- **Data to pull:** Timeline of verbal-therapy progress notes, any documented AAC discussion or referral.

### Plan-of-care justification uses only a severity label ("moderate aphasia," "severe dysphagia") with no dated functional-impact example
- **Usually means:** The justification is unlikely to satisfy payer/IEP-team documentation requirements and risks denial or discontinuation.
- **First question:** What specific, dated incident or measurement shows the functional impact on daily activity?
- **Data to pull:** Recent care-team communication logs, incident reports, or a specific failed-communication example with a date.

### Diet texture restricted to a level well below what the instrumental study actually confirmed as unsafe
- **Usually means:** Over-restriction out of excess caution after an equivocal bedside finding, unnecessarily reducing quality of life/nutritional intake.
- **First question:** Does the current diet order match the least-restrictive consistency the instrumental study confirmed as safe, or is it more restrictive than the data supports?
- **Data to pull:** Instrumental study report's specific consistency-by-consistency findings, current diet order.

### Family/patient resistance to AAC framed by the clinician as a data problem ("they just need to see the evidence")
- **Usually means:** The resistance is more likely emotional (fear of "giving up") than informational, and more data won't resolve it.
- **First question:** Has the AAC discussion been reframed as a parallel bridge alongside continued speech therapy, with a concrete example?
- **Data to pull:** Prior conversation notes with family, specific concerns they've raised.
