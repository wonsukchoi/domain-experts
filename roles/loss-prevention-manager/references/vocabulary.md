# Loss prevention working vocabulary

Terms a loss prevention manager uses precisely and a generalist blurs together. Format: definition → how a practitioner says it → common misuse.

**Shrink** — The difference between recorded inventory (what the books say should be on hand) and actual physical inventory, expressed as a percentage of sales. Decomposes into external theft, internal theft, administrative error, and vendor shortage.
In use: "1.8% shrink region-wide, but that number is meaningless until we know how much is refund fraud versus receiving errors."
Misuse: treating shrink as a single problem with a single fix (usually "more security") without decomposing which category is actually driving the number.

**Exception-based reporting (EBR)** — Software that flags transactions or employee behavior falling outside normal statistical variance (unusual void rates, refund patterns, discount overrides) for investigator review.
In use: "EBR flagged this cashier's void rate at 3.8x the store average — that's a lead to open a case on, not a conclusion."
Misuse: treating an EBR flag itself as proof of wrongdoing rather than a statistically unusual pattern that still requires investigation to confirm cause.

**Sweethearting** — An employee (cashier) intentionally under-ringing, voiding, or not scanning items for a friend, family member, or accomplice at checkout.
In use: "The void happened after the customer left with the item — that's sweethearting, not a scanning error."
Misuse: assuming sweethearting requires an external accomplice colluding in advance; it's equally common as a unilateral favor with no prior arrangement.

**Organized retail crime (ORC)** — Coordinated theft, typically across multiple stores or regions, involving groups that steal merchandise for resale rather than personal use, often through secondary/online marketplaces.
In use: "Same SKU category elevated at four stores in the same three-week window — that's an ORC escalation, not four isolated shoplifting cases."
Misuse: treating every multi-store theft pattern as automatically ORC without checking for the actual coordination signals (same individuals/vehicles across locations, resale-marketplace matches) — the label changes the appropriate response (task force/law enforcement) and shouldn't be applied loosely.

**Six-element evidentiary bar (shoplifting detention)** — The elements most U.S. shopkeeper's-privilege statutes require before a legal stop: observed selection/concealment, continuous observation through all points of sale, confirmation of non-payment, item still in possession, policy-compliant manner of stop, and documentation.
In use: "We lost continuous observation for 40 seconds near the fitting room — that breaks the chain, so this is documentation-only, no stop."
Misuse: treating "we're pretty sure they took it" as sufficient without the continuous-observation element, which is the single most common way a wrongful-detention claim survives in court.

**Wrongful detention (liability exposure)** — Legal exposure created when a stop, search, or accusation doesn't meet the jurisdiction's evidentiary bar, regardless of whether the underlying suspicion was factually correct.
In use: "Even if he did take it, we didn't have continuous observation — that's a wrongful-detention exposure regardless of the outcome."
Misuse: assuming being factually right about the theft is a defense to a procedurally deficient stop — the bar is about the process followed, not just the ultimate truth of the suspicion.

**Return fraud / wardrobing** — Return fraud broadly covers any scheme abusing the refund policy (receipt reuse, price-tag switching, returning stolen merchandise). Wardrobing specifically is buying an item, using it, and returning it as unused.
In use: "The return timestamps cluster right after a known local event — that pattern reads like wardrobing on formalwear, not a defective-product return."
Misuse: treating all high-return customers as fraud suspects without checking whether the return pattern matches a specific fraud signature versus a legitimate high-return category (e.g., clothing sized without trying on).

**Manager-PIN override** — A control requiring a supervisor's authentication code before a transaction (refund, discount, void) above a set dollar threshold can be completed, creating an audit trail and a second-person check.
In use: "Lowering the no-receipt refund override threshold from $20 to $10 closes the gap these two employees were exploiting."
Misuse: setting the threshold and assuming compliance — the control only works if override completion is actually audited, not just technically required by the POS system.

**Collusion (internal-external)** — A theft scheme where an employee coordinates with someone outside the organization (a "customer" accomplice, a delivery driver, a vendor rep) to defeat controls that assume the two parties are acting independently.
In use: "This isn't just an employee discount abuse — the same 'customer' shows up every time this cashier is on shift, that's collusion."
Misuse: investigating the employee and the external party as separate incidents when the pattern only makes sense as coordinated — missing the connection understates both the dollar exposure and the appropriate response (joint case, not two unrelated ones).

**Cycle count vs. physical inventory** — A cycle count is a partial, rotating inventory check (by category or location) done frequently to catch variance early; a physical inventory is a full count, typically annual, used as the authoritative reconciliation baseline.
In use: "Cycle counts on this category have been trending high variance for two quarters — we don't need to wait for the annual physical to open an investigation."
Misuse: waiting for the annual physical inventory to notice a shrink pattern that cycle-count data already surfaced months earlier.

**Chargeback vs. shrink** — A chargeback is a payment reversal initiated by a customer's card issuer (fraud dispute, unauthorized transaction); it's a payments/fraud category, distinct from inventory shrink even though both show up as a loss on the P&L.
In use: "That's a chargeback loss from a stolen-card transaction, not shrink — don't fold it into the shrink percentage or the decomposition gets distorted."
Misuse: lumping chargeback losses into the shrink number, which conflates a payments-fraud problem with an inventory-loss problem and misdirects the intervention.
