# Kindergarten Teacher — Vocabulary

### Phonemic awareness
The ability to identify and manipulate individual sounds (phonemes) in spoken words, entirely orally, with no print involved.
**In use:** "He can blend /c/-/a/-/t/ into 'cat' with no letters in front of him — that's the phonemic-awareness piece, separate from knowing what the letters look like."
**Common misuse:** Used interchangeably with "phonics" or "letter-sound knowledge," which are print-based skills — phonemic awareness is specifically the oral, pre-print skill, and conflating the two leads to teaching letter-sounds to a child who hasn't yet built the oral foundation they depend on.

### Phonological awareness
The broader umbrella skill of noticing sound structure in spoken language — rhyme, syllables, and onset-rime — of which phonemic awareness (individual sounds) is the most granular, most predictive layer.
**In use:** "Rhyming and clapping syllables are phonological awareness; segmenting a word into its individual sounds is the phonemic-awareness layer underneath that, and it's the one that predicts reading."
**Common misuse:** Treated as synonymous with phonemic awareness specifically, when it's the umbrella term — a child can be strong at rhyming (phonological) and still weak at phoneme segmentation (phonemic), which is the layer that actually matters for decoding.

### Letter-sound fluency
How quickly and accurately a child produces the sound associated with a printed letter, typically measured as sounds-per-minute.
**In use:** "His letter-sound fluency is strong — 24 correct sounds a minute — but that's a print-knowledge measure, not a phonemic-awareness one."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a stand-in for overall reading readiness, when a child can score well here while still lacking the oral segmentation skill that predicts actual decoding success.

### Tier 2 intervention
A targeted, time-limited small-group instructional response to a specific, screening-identified skill gap, delivered in addition to (not instead of) core instruction, within a multi-tiered system of support.
**In use:** "He's getting a 15-minute Tier 2 phonemic-awareness group four days a week, pulled from choice time, not from the core literacy block."
**Common misuse:** Used as a vague synonym for "extra help" without a named construct, a specific time allocation, or a recheck date — a Tier 2 plan without those three elements isn't actually a Tier 2 plan, it's an intention.

### Child Find
The IDEA-mandated obligation for schools to identify, locate, and evaluate children who may have a disability and need special education services, triggered by a documented, persistent concern rather than a single data point.
**In use:** "Two full Tier 2 cycles with no movement and fidelity confirmed — that's enough to open a Child Find conversation with the team."
**Common misuse:** Treated as something a teacher can either trigger unilaterally off one low score or, conversely, avoid indefinitely by continuing to informally reteach — the actual trigger is a documented pattern that has survived a real intervention attempt, discussed with the family and team.

### Grade retention
Having a student repeat the same grade the following year, rather than being promoted with the rest of their cohort.
**In use:** "The data doesn't support retention here — it's an isolated phonemic-awareness gap, which responds to a targeted intervention, not another year in the same content."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a general-purpose "buy more time" solution for any combination of academic and social immaturity, when meta-analytic research finds retained students underperform matched, promoted peers over the following years rather than catching up.

### Redshirting
Delaying a child's kindergarten entry by a year, usually for a child close to the age cutoff, distinct from retention (which happens after a child has already started).
**In use:** "He's one of the youngest in the class by the cutoff date, and the family's asking about redshirting before he even starts — that's a different conversation from a retention decision mid-year."
**Common misuse:** Discussed as functionally identical to retention, when redshirting is a pre-entry timing decision made on limited information (chronological age, general impression), while retention is a post-entry decision that can be grounded in a full year of actual construct-specific data.

### Kindergarten entry assessment (KEA)
A whole-child screening administered in the first weeks of kindergarten, covering literacy, number sense, self-regulation, fine motor, and social-skill domains, used to set a baseline rather than to diagnose.
**In use:** "His KEA baseline showed strong number sense and a self-regulation flag — that self-regulation piece is what shaped his first six weeks, not the numeracy score."
**Common misuse:** Read as a pass/fail readiness gate rather than a baseline-setting tool — a domain flagged at entry is a starting point for observation and instruction, not evidence the child shouldn't have started kindergarten.

### Zone of proximal development (ZPD)
The gap between what a child can do independently and what they can do with scaffolding from a more skilled adult or peer — the productive target for instruction.
**In use:** "He can't segment a three-sound word alone yet, but he can with me tapping out each sound with him — that's exactly where the daily routine should be working."
**Common misuse:** Applied to any task that's simply hard for the child, including ones far beyond current reach where scaffolding won't yet help — the ZPD is specifically the scaffoldable gap, not "whatever's difficult."

### Fidelity (of intervention delivery)
Whether an instructional intervention was actually delivered as designed — correct frequency, group size, and activity — which has to be confirmed before its results are trusted.
**In use:** "Before we call this intervention a failure, I checked the log — it was delivered three of five scheduled days most weeks, not five, so fidelity's actually the first thing to fix."
**Common misuse:** Skipped entirely when judging whether an intervention "worked" — a low-fidelity intervention that shows no progress is evidence about the delivery, not about whether the approach itself was wrong.

### Self-regulation (executive function)
The developing capacity to manage attention, impulses, and emotional response — inhibitory control, working memory, and cognitive flexibility — which follows its own maturational timeline distinct from academic skill.
**In use:** "His transitions flag isn't a discipline issue — it's a self-regulation skill that's still forming, and the visual-timer routine is the scaffold, not a consequence."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a character trait ("he just doesn't listen") rather than a developing skill, which leads to escalating consequences instead of the practiced-routine scaffolding that actually builds the skill.

### Responsive Classroom (morning meeting)
A specific structured daily routine (greeting, sharing, group activity, message) used to build predictable social-emotional scaffolding into the start of the day.
**In use:** "Morning meeting runs the same four steps every day — that predictability is doing the regulation work, not the content of any single day's activity."
**Common misuse:** Run inconsistently or shortened under time pressure, treating it as filler rather than the intentional routine-building block it's designed to be — its value comes specifically from repetition, not from any one day's content.
