# Working Vocabulary — Terms Generalists Misuse

Format per term: definition, a sentence the way a practitioner actually uses it, and the common misuse to avoid.

---

**Simultaneous submission**
Sending the same piece to multiple markets at once, with the understanding that the author will withdraw it from all others immediately upon acceptance anywhere.
*Usage:* "It's out simultaneously at five markets; the moment one says yes, I withdraw from the other four same-day."
*Misuse:* treating "they haven't responded yet" as a reason to delay withdrawal after an acceptance — the obligation starts at acceptance, not at the other venues' response time.

**FNASR (First North American Serial Rights)**
The standard rights a magazine licenses: the right to be first to publish the piece in North America, after which all other rights revert to the author.
*Usage:* "The contract only asks for FNASR, so I can reprint it in a collection once it's out."
*Misuse:* assuming any publication contract is FNASR by default and signing without reading the actual clause — some ask for "all rights" or exclusive terms far broader than the norm.

**Volta**
The turn — a shift in argument, tone, or perspective, classically at line 9 of a sonnet but present in many short poems regardless of form.
*Usage:* "The volta doesn't land until the final couplet here, which is why the middle feels flat — it's carrying too much before the turn."
*Misuse:* assuming only sonnets have (or need) a volta, or forcing an artificial turn into a poem that earns its ending through accumulation instead.

**Persona**
The speaker constructed by a poem, distinct from the poet who wrote it, even when written in first person.
*Usage:* "The persona in this poem is unreliable on purpose — don't read the speaker's certainty as the poet's."
*Misuse:* assuming every first-person poem is autobiographical, in critique or in the poet's own defense of a piece — both collapse a craft question into a biographical one.

**Prosody**
The sound-pattern of language in verse: stress, rhythm, and pace, not just rhyme.
*Usage:* "The rhyme scheme is fine; the prosody is the problem — the stresses fight the line breaks."
*Misuse:* treating prosody as a synonym for rhyme scheme and ignoring stress patterns, which is where most "unsingable" lyrics actually fail.

**Enjambment**
A line break that cuts across a syntactic unit, carrying the sentence into the next line rather than ending at a natural pause.
*Usage:* "The enjambment on 'the door / opened' is doing real work — it makes the reader hang on 'door' for a beat before the reveal."
*Misuse:* enjambing every line as a default style choice with no attention to what tension or emphasis each specific break creates.

**Topline**
The melody line written over a chord progression or track, often the first compositional element in pop/song co-writes.
*Usage:* "She wrote the topline first; I fit the lyric to the melody's stress pattern after."
*Misuse:* assuming the topline writer and the lyricist are always the same person, or that topline always comes before lyric — the order varies by collaboration and changes what's negotiable later.

**Split sheet**
A signed document recording each co-writer's percentage share of a composition, based on contribution, filed at or near the writing session.
*Usage:* "We signed the split sheet before we left the studio — 55/45, lyrics and structure versus melody and hook."
*Misuse:* treating a verbal understanding as equivalent to a signed split sheet; disputes almost always surface only after a song has commercial value, when it's hardest to resolve fairly.

**Mechanical royalty**
Royalty paid to songwriters/publishers for the reproduction of a composition (physical copies, downloads, and — under a separate statutory framework — streams).
*Usage:* "Mechanical royalties come from reproduction of the song itself, separate from what gets paid for a live performance or radio play."
*Misuse:* confusing mechanical royalties with performance royalties — they're collected by different bodies, for different uses of the song, and often paid to different parties.

**Performance royalty**
Royalty paid to songwriters/publishers when a composition is performed publicly (radio, streaming platforms' performance component, live venues), collected through a PRO (ASCAP, BMI, SESAC).
*Usage:* "Register the song with your PRO before release, or the performance royalties have nowhere to route to."
*Misuse:* assuming PRO registration happens automatically on release, or that it substitutes for mechanical-royalty collection through a publisher.

**Chapbook**
A short, bound poetry collection, typically 20-40 pages, often published via a press's vetted contest or open reading period.
*Usage:* "The chapbook won [Press]'s prize — juried, not self-funded — which is what makes it a real publishing credit."
*Misuse:* treating a self-published or pay-to-publish chapbook as equivalent, for credentialing purposes, to one selected through a vetted contest or editorial process.

**Slush pile**
The general, unsolicited submission queue at a magazine or press, read (often by volunteer or junior readers first) separately from solicited work.
*Usage:* "That poem came out of the slush pile — no connection to the editor, just a strong submission."
*Misuse:* assuming every market reads exclusively from the slush pile; some issues or sections are solicited-only, and submitting there wastes the piece's shot in the actual open queue.

**Workshop model**
The Iowa-style critique structure: the piece is read or discussed while the author stays silent, speaking only afterward to answer direct craft questions.
*Usage:* "Stay silent through both rounds — you'll get to respond to questions after, not to defend the poem in real time."
*Misuse:* the author jumping in to explain intent mid-critique, which shifts the room from evaluating the poem on the page to litigating what the poet meant.

**Contributor copies**
Physical or digital copies of the issue/anthology given to the author in place of, or in addition to, cash payment.
*Usage:* "It's a copies-only market — good platform, not part of the income plan."
*Misuse:* budgeting contributor copies as if they carry the same value as a cash payment when planning a year's income.
