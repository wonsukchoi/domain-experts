# Poet/Lyricist Artifacts — Templates With Example Content

Real work products, filled in with plausible examples. Copy the structure, replace the content.

---

## 1. Submission cover letter

Kept short on purpose — editors read hundreds of these; the poem, not the letter, does the persuading.

```
Dear [Editor Name],

Please consider the enclosed poem, "Threadbare," for [Journal Name]. It is
not under simultaneous consideration elsewhere. [Or, if it is: "This poem
is under simultaneous submission; I will notify you immediately if it is
accepted elsewhere."]

I'm a poet based in [City]. My work has appeared in [Journal], [Journal],
and [Journal]; my chapbook [Title] was published by [Press] in [Year].

Thank you for your time and consideration.

Sincerely,
[Name]
[Contact info]
```

What makes this work: no synopsis, no explanation of the poem's meaning, bio in third person kept to two sentences, and the simultaneous-submission status is stated plainly rather than omitted — most editors expect it and only a minority of markets prohibit it, but the ones that do check.

## 2. Split sheet (songwriting collaboration)

Signed at or immediately after the writing session — before mixing, mastering, or release. Percentages must total 100 and reflect compositional contribution (lyric, melody, harmony/chord structure), not attendance or session time.

```
SONGWRITER SPLIT SHEET

Song Title: "Low Tide"
Date of Session: 2026-03-14
Studio/Location: [Location]

Writers and Contributions:
| Name          | Role                     | Contribution %  | PRO / IPI #   | Signature |
|---------------|--------------------------|------------------|----------------|-----------|
| J. Alvarez    | Lyrics, song structure   | 55%              | ASCAP #XXXXXX  | ______    |
| M. Reyes      | Melody, topline hook     | 45%              | BMI #XXXXXXX   | ______    |

Notes: Producer T. Nakamura contributed track/instrumental production only,
no melodic or lyrical material — compensated separately via production fee
and master points, not a songwriting split.

Total: 100%
All parties confirm this reflects the finished composition and supersedes
any prior verbal understanding.
```

The note on the producer is the point: a production credit and a songwriting split are different instruments, and conflating them is the single most common source of later disputes.

## 3. Workshop critique format (author-silent model)

Structure for running or participating in a poem workshop so notes stay craft-anchored.

```
Poem under discussion: "Threadbare" (author: J. Kim)

Round 1 — First impressions (30 sec each, no rebuttal)
Reader A: "The final image lands, but I lost the thread in stanza 2."
Reader B: "Stanza 2, line 4 — the metaphor shifts from weather to fabric
           and I couldn't tell if that was intentional."
Reader C: "Same note as B, stanza 2 line 4."

Round 2 — Line-level (readers point to specific lines/words only)
- Stanza 1, line 3: "waiting" repeated from title — intentional echo or slip?
- Stanza 2, line 4: metaphor mix flagged by 2 of 3 readers — likely a real
  craft issue, not a taste split (see decision framework: 2+ independent
  readers on the same line = treat as signal).
- Stanza 3: no notes — read as working.

Author response (after both rounds, craft questions only):
"On stanza 2 line 4 — that's not intentional, I'll fix it. The 'waiting'
echo in stanza 1 is intentional, keeping it."

Action items:
[ ] Revise stanza 2, line 4 metaphor consistency
[ ] Leave stanza 1 and 3 as-is
```

The discipline: the author does not explain intent during Round 1 or 2, and single-reader notes without a second independent flag get logged but not automatically actioned.

## 4. Chapbook contest submission checklist

```
Before submitting to [Press] Chapbook Prize (entry fee $25, prize $1,000 +
publication + 25 author copies):

[ ] Verify fee-to-prize ratio and sponsor legitimacy (CLMP member? prior
    winners publicly listed? judge named in advance?)
[ ] Manuscript formatted per guidelines EXACTLY (font, margin, page count,
    no author-identifying info in the file itself — most are blind-judged)
[ ] Manuscript order: strongest poem first and near-last, not buried middle
[ ] Simultaneous submission policy checked — most chapbook contests
    DISALLOW simultaneous submission unless stated; verify before sending
    the same manuscript to two contests at once
[ ] Track: contest name, fee paid, submission date, response date expected,
    manuscript version number submitted
```

The simultaneous-submission flag matters more here than for single poems — chapbook and full-manuscript contests disallow it far more often than magazines do, and it's the opposite default from single-poem submission.

## 5. Grant/fellowship application — artist statement structure

```
# Artist Statement — [State Arts Council] Individual Artist Grant

## The specific problem this body of work solves
Not "I write poems about memory" — instead: "These poems test whether a
sonnet's fixed volta can hold a memory that resists a single turning point
— most of the manuscript works against the form's assumption that
insight arrives once."

## Why this project, why now
[1-2 sentences: what stage the manuscript is at, what the grant enables
that unfunded time doesn't — e.g., "the manuscript is two-thirds drafted;
this grant funds the revision pass, not first drafts."]

## Writing sample selection
Submit the most-revised, most externally-read piece in the batch, not the
newest — panelists are reading for control and finish, not novelty.

## Budget line (if required)
Example: $2,500 grant — 40 hrs dedicated revision time valued at $50/hr
opportunity cost ($2,000) + $500 toward a paid manuscript consultation
with an outside editor.
```

The reframe from "what I write about" to "the specific formal problem the work solves" is what separates a fundable statement from a generic artist bio.
