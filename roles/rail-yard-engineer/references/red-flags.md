# Red flags

Smell tests a yard engineer, hostler, or reviewer catches before a kick, a move past a blue signal, or a terminal servicing move. Each entry: what it usually means, the first question a veteran asks, and the data to pull before proceeding.

### Coupling speed on a tank/hazmat or refrigerated car running at or above the yard's general 4 mph limit

**Usually means:** the general coupling-speed limit was applied instead of the commodity's tighter carrier-specified limit, or the release speed wasn't computed against the actual rollout distance.
**First question:** "Does this specific car type carry a coupling-speed limit tighter than the yard's standard kick, and was today's release speed computed against it?"
**Data to pull:** the carrier's commodity/car-type coupling-speed instructions, today's switch list car type, and the rollout distance to the standing cut.

### A move proceeds onto a track after a verbal "we're clear" with no direct visual confirmation of blue-flag/light status

**Usually means:** a radioed relay is being treated as equivalent to personally verifying the flag or light is down.
**First question:** "Who placed this protection, and has the engineer personally seen it removed, or only been told it's clear?"
**Data to pull:** the blue-signal placement log, and the identity of whoever gave the "clear" call versus who placed the protection.

### A car is kicked despite an unverified or suspect rollability condition (dragging rigging, hand brake status unknown)

**Usually means:** kick vs. couple-and-shove was decided by habit or time pressure rather than a rollability check.
**First question:** "Was this car's hand brake and running gear checked before it was released to roll free?"
**Data to pull:** the car's last inspection or bad-order tag, and the hand-brake set/release record for that car.

### A hostler move continues past the terminal track-limits boundary without additional authority

**Usually means:** the boundary is being treated as informal because the physical rail continues past it, rather than as the hard limit of the hostler's qualification.
**First question:** "What does this terminal's track-limits diagram show as the boundary here, and was authority obtained to go past it?"
**Data to pull:** the terminal track-limits diagram/special instructions, and any dispatcher or yardmaster authority granted for the move.

### An RCO/beltpack move is executed with the operator out of direct line of sight of the point of movement

**Usually means:** positive point protection has been replaced with an assumption about what's ahead, rather than continuous visual verification.
**First question:** "Could the operator actually see the leading end of this move at the moment in question?"
**Data to pull:** the RCO system's position/zone log if available, and the crew's statement on sightline during the move.

### Cars crest a hump closer together than the tower's called spacing

**Usually means:** the shove pace is running ahead of the tower's call, risking a downstream switch not lining in time for the next car.
**First question:** "What pace did the tower call for this cut, and did the shove actually match it?"
**Data to pull:** the hump control system's car-speed/spacing log at the crest, and the radio traffic between the tower and the engine crew.

### A car is left resting past the clearance point on a classification track after a kick or shove

**Usually means:** the move was checked against "is the switch lined" rather than against the actual clearance point, so the adjacent track is fouled even though the switch itself is correct.
**First question:** "Where does this car actually sit relative to the clearance point, not just relative to the switch?"
**Data to pull:** the track's clearance-point marker or diagram, and the car's resting position after the move.

### A cut is secured by hand-brake count alone with no effectiveness test (air released, equipment observed stationary)

**Usually means:** securement is being verified by the number of brakes applied rather than by the test the rule actually requires.
**First question:** "Was this cut tested with the air brakes released to confirm it actually holds, or just counted against the table?"
**Data to pull:** the carrier's securement table for that track's grade and tonnage, and the effectiveness-test record (or absence of one) for this specific move.

### A switch-list change mid-shift isn't followed by a re-check of blue-signal status or track occupancy before the next move

**Usually means:** the crew is acting on the prior lineup instead of the yardmaster's current one.
**First question:** "Has the switch list changed since the last briefing, and was blue-signal/occupancy status re-confirmed before this move?"
**Data to pull:** the current switch list with timestamp, and the last job briefing record for this crew.

### A yard-limit movement is run at a speed assuming the track ahead is clear rather than at restricted speed

**Usually means:** yard limits are being treated as "go slow" rather than as the specific stop-within-half-the-sight-distance requirement they impose absent block signals.
**First question:** "At the speed actually run, could this move have stopped within half of what was visible at every point on the track?"
**Data to pull:** the recorded or estimated speed through the movement, and the sight-distance conditions (curvature, standing equipment, structures) along that track.
