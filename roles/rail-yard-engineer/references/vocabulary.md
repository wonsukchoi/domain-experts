# Vocabulary

Terms generalists conflate that a rail yard engineer, dinkey operator, or hostler keeps sharply separate, because the difference changes what was actually checked, authorized, or physically at risk during a switching move.

## Kick vs. couple-and-shove

**Kick** is uncoupling a car (or short cut) while it's still moving and letting it roll free toward a classification track under its own momentum, with the engine continuing on or reversing. **Couple-and-shove** keeps the engine physically coupled to the car through the entire move, maintaining positive control of its speed and stopping point throughout.

**In use:** "That one's dragging a brake beam — we're not kicking it, couple up and shove it in, I want a hand on it the whole way."

**Common misuse:** choosing kick as the default for speed, without first confirming the car will actually roll free (no dragging equipment, hand brake released, ordinary resistance) — a car that doesn't roll as expected turns a kick into an uncontrolled stall or overrun.

## Hump yard vs. flat-switching yard

**Hump yard** classifies cars by pushing a cut over a graded crest, uncoupling each car at the top, and letting gravity roll it into the correct classification track under retarder speed control. **Flat-switching yard** classifies cars entirely by engine moves (kicks and shoves) on level or near-level track with no crest or retarders.

**In use:** "This isn't a hump — we're flat-switching this cut, so every car in it gets moved by the engine, nothing's rolling in on its own."

**Common misuse:** applying hump-style "let it roll and trust the grade" thinking to a flat-switching move, where there is no retarder system managing speed and the engine is the only speed control there is.

## Master retarder vs. group retarder

**Master retarder** is the first, heavier speed-control point at a hump yard, slowing a car from crest speed to a controlled intermediate speed regardless of which classification track it's headed to. **Group retarder** is a second, finer speed-control point further downstream, tuned per track group to hit the specific coupling speed needed for that car's destination track.

**In use:** "Master retarder's holding everything to a common speed off the hump — it's the group retarder down at the bowl that's actually dialing in the coupling speed for each track."

**Common misuse:** treating "the retarder" as one system, when the master retarder's job (bring everything to a common controlled speed) is distinct from the group retarder's job (tune to the specific track's coupling-speed target).

## Terminal track limits vs. yard limits

**Terminal track limits** are the specific boundary a carrier defines for an engine terminal's servicing trackage (fuel rack, service track, ready track), inside which a hostler is authorized to move locomotives. **Yard limits** are the broader classification-yard boundary within which movements operate at restricted speed absent block signals, a wider scope than a hostler's terminal authority.

**In use:** "I can move this unit anywhere inside the terminal limits for fueling, but the second it needs to go out to the yard proper, that's outside what I'm qualified to run."

**Common misuse:** treating a hostler's terminal-track qualification as covering any track that's physically connected to it, rather than the specific bounded area the carrier defines.

## Blue signal (operator's-side compliance)

A blue flag or blue light displayed on a track or car under 49 CFR 218 Subpart B means no equipment may be moved onto, coupled to, or removed from it by anyone except the employee(s) who placed it. From the engine operator's seat, compliance means personally confirming the flag or light is down before any move — not accepting a relay from anyone else that it's clear.

**In use:** "Radio says we're clear, but I haven't seen that blue light come down myself — we hold until I see it."

**Common misuse:** accepting a conductor's, trainmaster's, or dispatcher's verbal "it's clear" as sufficient, when the rule requires the crew that placed the protection to be the one that removes it, and the operator to verify that fact directly.

## Rollability

Whether a car will actually roll freely under its own momentum once uncoupled — a function of hand brake status, running-gear condition, and rolling resistance, not of how the move was planned. A car assumed free-rolling that has a set hand brake or a dragging component will not travel the distance or reach the speed a kick was computed for.

**In use:** "Check the hand brake on that one before we kick it — if it's still set, it's not going anywhere near the cut and we'll be re-switching it."

**Common misuse:** assuming rollability from a car's type or general appearance rather than checking the specific car's hand brake and running-gear condition before choosing to kick it.

## Cut vs. drill

**Cut** is the group of cars being handled together in a single switching move. **Drill** is a specific scheduled switching session or work order (e.g., "the 2 p.m. drill") assigned to classify a set of inbound or outbound traffic, which may include multiple cuts moved in sequence.

**In use:** "This drill's got four cuts to build — first cut's the eight cars for Track 6, don't mix them with the next one."

**Common misuse:** using "cut" and "drill" interchangeably, which loses the distinction between a single move and the whole session of moves it's part of.

## Clearance point / fouling a track

**Clearance point** is the fixed location past which a car or equipment on one track physically obstructs movement on an adjacent track through the connecting switch. A car resting beyond it is **fouling** that adjacent track, regardless of whether the switch itself is correctly lined.

**In use:** "That car's a car length past the clearance point — Track 9's fouled even though the switch is lined for Track 8, nothing moves on 9 until it's pulled back."

**Common misuse:** checking only whether the switch is lined correctly and concluding the adjacent track is clear, without checking the car's actual position against the clearance point.

## RCO/beltpack vs. cab-controlled move

**RCO (Remote Control Operator)** moves are made with the operator on the ground running the locomotive from a trackside beltpack, common in flat switching. **Cab-controlled** moves have the operator physically in the locomotive cab. RCO requires the operator to maintain direct line of sight of the point of movement at all times; a cab-controlled move has an operator whose position is fixed relative to the equipment.

**In use:** "I lost sight of the point going around that string of cars — stopping the move until I've got eyes on it again, not finishing on the last position I had."

**Common misuse:** treating radio/beltpack contact with the equipment as equivalent to visual contact with the point of movement — RCO's safety case depends specifically on the operator seeing what's ahead, not just controlling it.

## Dinkey

A small, low-horsepower locomotive used for yard switching or short industrial hauls, as distinct from a road/mainline locomotive built for sustained tonnage over distance. The term names the equipment class this SOC code's operators run.

**In use:** "We're pulling this cut with the dinkey, not calling out a road unit for a three-car spot."

**Common misuse:** using "dinkey" loosely for any small locomotive, when the term specifically implies yard/industrial duty cycle (frequent start-stop, low sustained horsepower need) rather than just physical size.

## Hostler vs. yard engineer (scope)

**Hostler** moves locomotives specifically for servicing purposes (fueling, sanding, inspection, positioning) within an engine terminal's defined track limits, typically without switching revenue cars. **Yard engineer** performs the broader classification-yard switching work (kicks, shoves, building cuts) that a conductor's or yardmaster's switch list calls for.

**In use:** "I'm qualified as a hostler on this terminal, but building cuts out in the yard bowl is a different qualification I don't hold."

**Common misuse:** assuming hostler and yard-engineer qualifications are interchangeable, when a hostler's authority is bounded to terminal servicing moves and doesn't extend to yard classification switching by default.

## Bad order vs. set-out

**Bad order** is a car flagged with a mechanical defect that must be repaired before it can continue in service, typically marked with a physical tag. **Set-out** is the act of physically removing a car from a train or cut and spotting it on a specific track, which may be done for a bad order or for an unrelated reason (destination, hazmat placement, brake percentage).

**In use:** "That car's bad-ordered for a flat wheel — set it out on the rip track, not just anywhere it fits."

**Common misuse:** treating "bad order" and "set-out" as synonyms, when a set-out can happen for reasons that have nothing to do with a mechanical defect.
