# Red flags — what a tax preparer catches before it becomes an exam or a penalty

Signals worth a second look during intake or review, before the return is filed. Any one alone can be innocent; more than one on the same return raises the exam-risk conversation with the client up front.

### Schedule C expenses exceed 80–90% of gross receipts with no supporting records
- **Usually means:** legitimate high-overhead business (fine, if documented) or expenses estimated/rounded to shrink a profit figure — both look identical to DIF scoring until backup is checked.
- **First question:** "Can you show me receipts or a mileage/expense log for the three largest categories?"
- **Data to pull:** bank/credit-card statements for the business account, mileage log, any 1099-K/1099-NEC received, prior-year Schedule C for ratio comparison.

### Schedule C shows a loss in 3 or more of the last 5 years
- **Usually means:** either a real business in a genuine rough patch, or an activity closer to a hobby that IRC §183 presumes isn't run for profit absent contrary evidence.
- **First question:** "What changed this year, and what's the plan to get to profitability — in writing anywhere?"
- **Data to pull:** prior 5 years of Schedule C net profit/loss, any business plan or marketing records, time-tracking showing material participation.

### EITC, CTC/ACTC/ODC, AOTC, or HOH claimed with no Form 8867 basis documented
- **Usually means:** the preparer is relying on the client's say-so instead of the required due-diligence inquiry — the single most penalized gap in individual return prep.
- **First question:** "What did I actually ask and verify for each of these, and is it written down?"
- **Data to pull:** completed Form 8867 worksheet, custody/support documentation for dependents, school 1098-T for AOTC, proof of household-maintenance cost (>50%) for HOH.

### Dependent's SSN was already used on another return (e-file reject code)
- **Usually means:** a co-parent or family member claimed the same child, sometimes in good faith (both believe they qualify) and sometimes not.
- **First question:** "Who has physical custody the majority of nights this year, and is there a custody agreement or Form 8332 release?"
- **Data to pull:** custody agreement, school/medical records showing residency, any signed Form 8332 (noncustodial parent release).

### 100% business-use vehicle claimed with no personal-use miles logged
- **Usually means:** an honest but incomplete log, or an inflated business-use percentage — both draw the same scrutiny since near-zero personal use is statistically rare.
- **First question:** "Do you have another vehicle for personal use, and where's the mileage log?"
- **Data to pull:** contemporaneous mileage log (date, destination, business purpose), odometer readings at year start/end, registration for any other household vehicle.

### Round, even-dollar amounts across multiple expense categories
- **Usually means:** estimated rather than actual figures — clients round in both directions, but examiners read round numbers as unsubstantiated.
- **First question:** "Are these actual totals from records, or your best estimate?"
- **Data to pull:** underlying statements or receipts for any category over $1,000 quoted in round hundreds/thousands.

### Cash-intensive business with reported income below local/industry norms
- **Usually means:** underreported gross receipts — sometimes unintentional (informal cash not tracked), sometimes not.
- **First question:** "Walk me through how you total up cash receipts day to day."
- **Data to pull:** point-of-sale or register tapes if any, bank deposits vs. reported income reconciliation, comparable-business benchmarks by NAICS code.

### IRS transcript shows a 1099/W-2 the client didn't bring in
- **Usually means:** the client forgot, changed jobs mid-year and lost the form, or is a gig-platform worker who doesn't recognize a 1099-K as reportable income.
- **First question:** "Does this payer/platform match any work you did this year?"
- **Data to pull:** IRS Wage & Income transcript, client's own bank statements for the payer's deposits.

### Large noncash charitable deduction (>$5,000) without a qualified appraisal
- **Usually means:** the deduction will be disallowed on exam regardless of the donation's legitimacy — Form 8283 Section B and a qualified appraisal are mandatory above that threshold, not optional paperwork.
- **First question:** "Do you have a qualified written appraisal, not just a valuation estimate from a donation app?"
- **Data to pull:** Form 8283 with donee acknowledgment, appraisal report, photos/receipts for the donated property.

### CP2000 or audit notice where the IRS's proposed number doesn't match your records
- **Usually means:** duplicate third-party reporting, a timing mismatch (e.g., installment sale, wash sale), or income already reported elsewhere on the return that the matching program didn't credit.
- **First question:** "Does our copy of the return already include this item somewhere the IRS's letter didn't check?"
- **Data to pull:** the filed return as submitted, all 1099s/W-2s for the year in question, the IRS's own transcript for that year to see exactly what they're matching against.

### Client had material 1099/Schedule C income and made no estimated payments
- **Usually means:** a §6654 underpayment penalty is coming regardless of the final balance, unless a safe-harbor exception applies (annualized income, first year self-employed with no prior-year liability).
- **First question:** "Did you make any quarterly payments, or is all your payment coming from withholding?"
- **Data to pull:** Form 2210 calculation, prior-year total tax (for the 100%/110% safe harbor), current-year withholding total.
