# Tax preparer working vocabulary

Terms preparers use precisely and generalists blur. Format: definition → how a practitioner says it → common misuse. Figures are 2024 tax year unless noted.

**PTIN (Preparer Tax Identification Number)** — The IRS-issued ID every paid preparer must have and put on every return prepared for compensation; renewed annually.
In use: "I can't sign this return without a current PTIN on file — check the renewal date before tax season, not during it."
Misuse: assuming a PTIN implies credentials or IRS endorsement of competence. It's a registration number, not a license — EA/CPA/attorney status is separate.

**Circular 230** — The Treasury regulations (31 CFR Subtitle A, Part 10) governing conduct of anyone practicing before the IRS: due diligence, conflicts of interest, and standards for the positions taken on a return.
In use: "Signing this without asking about the missing 1099 is a Circular 230 problem, not just a customer-service one."
Misuse: treating it as only relevant to audits/representation. It governs return preparation conduct too, every day, on every return.

**Due diligence (Form 8867)** — The documented inquiry and record-keeping required before claiming EITC, CTC/ACTC/ODC, AOTC, or HOH filing status; a separate requirement from simply computing the credit correctly.
In use: "The math on the CTC is right, but there's no 8867 basis in the file for the qualifying-child test — that's the exposure."
Misuse: treating Form 8867 as a formality checkbox rather than evidence the required questions were actually asked and answered.

**Understatement penalty (§6694)** — The preparer-level penalty for a return position that understates tax without reasonable basis (§6694(a): greater of $1,000 or 50% of the fee) or with willful/reckless disregard (§6694(b): greater of $5,000 or 75% of the fee).
In use: "There's no substantial authority for this one — I'm either dropping the position or disclosing it on 8275 before I'll sign."
Misuse: assuming the penalty only bites on aggressive tax-shelter positions. It applies to ordinary individual returns just as readily.

**Substantial authority / reasonable basis / more-likely-than-not** — A hierarchy of confidence standards for a return position, each unlocking different protections: reasonable basis (~20% chance of success) needs disclosure to avoid penalty; substantial authority (~40%) doesn't need disclosure; more-likely-than-not (>50%) is required for certain tax-shelter-adjacent items.
In use: "I've got reasonable basis, not substantial authority — we disclose it on 8275 and we're covered."
Misuse: using "I think it's fine" as if it maps to one of these standards. Each has a specific evidentiary bar, not a gut-feel equivalent.

**CP2000** — An IRS automated underreporter notice proposing additional tax because third-party reporting (1099s, W-2s, brokerage 1099-Bs) doesn't match the filed return.
In use: "This CP2000 is proposing tax on a 1099-B sale we already reported net of basis on Schedule D — it's not a real discrepancy, just a matching-program gap."
Misuse: treating a CP2000 as a final bill. It's a proposal; the taxpayer can respond with documentation before it becomes an assessment.

**DIF score (Discriminant Information Function)** — The IRS's proprietary scoring algorithm ranking returns by audit-selection likelihood, based on how far line items deviate from statistical norms for similar returns.
In use: "A Schedule C at this expense ratio is going to score high on DIF regardless of whether every dollar is legitimate — get the backup together now."
Misuse: treating a high-DIF return as automatically wrong. It flags statistical deviation, not fraud; a legitimately unusual business still scores high.

**Above-the-line vs. below-the-line deduction** — Above-the-line (adjustments to income, Schedule 1 Part II) reduces AGI and is available whether or not you itemize; below-the-line (itemized or standard) only helps past the standard-deduction threshold.
In use: "Her self-employed health insurance is above the line — it helps her even though she's taking the standard deduction."
Misuse: assuming all deductions require itemizing. The above-the-line category is exactly the set that doesn't.

**Self-employment (SE) tax** — The 15.3% combined Social Security (12.4%, up to the annual wage base — $168,600 for 2024) and Medicare (2.9%, uncapped) tax on net self-employment earnings, computed on 92.35% of Schedule C net profit.
In use: "His income tax bracket is low, but SE tax on $49K of Schedule C profit is still almost $7,000 — that's the number that surprises people."
Misuse: forgetting it exists when eyeballing a self-employed client's liability, or forgetting the wage-base coordination with any W-2 income earned the same year.

**Safe harbor (estimated tax, §6654)** — Paying, through withholding plus estimates, the lesser of 90% of current-year tax or 100% of prior-year tax (110% if prior-year AGI exceeded $150,000) to avoid the underpayment penalty.
In use: "We're not going to hit 90% of this year, but 110% of last year's liability is achievable — that's the safe harbor to target."
Misuse: assuming any estimated payment avoids the penalty. It's calculated quarter-by-quarter against a specific threshold, not just a year-end total.

**AMT (Alternative Minimum Tax)** — A parallel tax calculation that disallows certain deductions/preference items and applies its own rate; the taxpayer owes the higher of regular tax or AMT.
In use: "State tax deduction add-back plus the ISO exercise pushes him into AMT territory — run the 6251 before you promise a refund number."
Misuse: assuming AMT is a legacy concern post-TCJA. It's less common at typical income levels now, but still triggered by ISO exercises and certain state-heavy itemizers.

**NIIT (Net Investment Income Tax)** — An additional 3.8% tax on the lesser of net investment income or the excess of MAGI over $200,000 (single) / $250,000 (MFJ).
In use: "The rental income alone doesn't trigger it, but add the capital gain and she's over the MAGI threshold — NIIT applies to both."
Misuse: treating it as only relevant to "high earners with stock portfolios." It applies to rental income, capital gains, and passive business income at these thresholds regardless of source.

**Amended return (Form 1040-X)** — The mechanism to correct a previously filed return; generally must be filed within 3 years of the original filing date (or 2 years from tax paid, if later) to claim a refund.
In use: "We missed the home office deduction on last year's return — a 1040-X inside the 3-year window gets that refund back."
Misuse: assuming any error can be fixed anytime. The refund-claim window is a hard statutory deadline, not a courtesy period.

**Injured spouse vs. innocent spouse relief** — Injured spouse (Form 8379) reclaims one spouse's share of a joint refund seized for the other spouse's separate debt; innocent spouse relief (Form 8857) relieves one spouse of liability for the other's understatement/errors on a joint return.
In use: "Her refund got taken for his old student loan default — that's an injured spouse claim, not innocent spouse."
Misuse: using the terms interchangeably. One is about splitting a refund already filed jointly; the other is about escaping liability for the other spouse's reporting errors.
