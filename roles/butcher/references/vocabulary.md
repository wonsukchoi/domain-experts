# Vocabulary

### Yield percentage
The ratio of sellable product weight to the primal's purchase weight after cutting and trimming.
**In use:** "Yield's running 60% on this primal — that's what actually goes into the true cost-per-pound calculation."
**Common misuse:** Assuming purchase weight and sellable product weight are the same thing when calculating cost per pound, which understates the real cost of the finished cut.

### Value allocation
Distributing a primal's total cost across its resulting cut categories based on their relative market value rather than an equal weight share.
**In use:** "Value allocation puts most of the $900 cost on the steak, since it's 89% of the total potential revenue even though it's only 60% of the weight."
**Common misuse:** Splitting cost evenly by weight across cuts of very different market value, which systematically underprices premium cuts and overprices cheap ones.

### Primal / sub-primal
The large sections a carcass is first divided into (e.g. ribeye, chuck), further broken down into sub-primals before final retail cuts.
**In use:** "That's a ribeye subprimal — still needs to be broken down into individual steaks and trim."
**Common misuse:** Using "primal" loosely to mean any large piece of meat, rather than the specific standardized wholesale cut it actually refers to.

### Dry-aging
Controlled refrigerated aging of meat that develops flavor and tenderness while the product loses significant moisture weight over time.
**In use:** "This ribeye's been dry-aging 28 days — expect noticeable weight loss by the time it's ready to trim and sell."
**Common misuse:** Pricing dry-aged product at its starting (green) weight, ignoring that the weight actually sold after aging is meaningfully less.

### Trim
The smaller pieces of meat separated from a primal during cutting, typically destined for ground product.
**In use:** "Trim's running heavier than usual on this batch — check if the steaks are being cut too lean."
**Common misuse:** Treating trim as waste with no recoverable value, rather than a byproduct with its own real market price.

### Fabrication
The overall process of breaking down a primal or carcass into smaller, sellable cuts.
**In use:** "Fabrication on this order took longer than usual — more portion sizes than the standard breakdown."
**Common misuse:** Using "fabrication" and "trimming" interchangeably, when fabrication is the broader cutting process and trimming is just one specific step within it.

### Danger zone (food safety)
The temperature range — commonly 40–140°F (4–60°C) — in which bacteria multiply most rapidly, with a defined cumulative time limit for product to safely spend within it.
**In use:** "Track cumulative time in the danger zone across receiving, cutting, and holding — not just one exposure at a time."
**Common misuse:** Treating the danger zone as a single-exposure event rather than tracking cumulative time across multiple separate exposures throughout the process.

### Bone-in vs. boneless yield
The difference in sellable meat weight depending on whether bone is left in or removed during cutting.
**In use:** "Can't compare cost per pound between the bone-in and boneless product without adjusting for their very different yields off the same primal."
**Common misuse:** Comparing cost per pound between a bone-in and a boneless product without first adjusting for the different yield percentages each spec produces from the same starting primal.

### Grade (USDA quality grade)
A standardized classification (e.g. Prime, Choice, Select) reflecting marbling and other quality factors that affects both price and expected yield characteristics.
**In use:** "Grade's dropped from Choice to Select on this delivery — expect a different fat trim yield than usual."
**Common misuse:** Assuming grade only affects flavor and price, when it also correlates with different expected fat-trim percentages and yield outcomes.

### Case-ready
Meat that arrives already cut and packaged for retail display, requiring no further in-house fabrication.
**In use:** "That case-ready tray doesn't need a yield calculation — the cutting cost's already built into what we paid for it."
**Common misuse:** Assuming all product in a display case is case-ready, missing that in-house cut product needs its own separate yield and cost calculation that case-ready product doesn't.
