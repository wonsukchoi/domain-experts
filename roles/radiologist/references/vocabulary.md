# Radiologist — vocabulary

### Satisfaction of search
The perceptual phenomenon where finding one abnormality reduces the likelihood of detecting a second, unrelated one, because the visual/cognitive search stops once a positive finding is registered.
**In use:** "The rib fracture was reported but the apical pneumothorax was missed — classic satisfaction of search, needs a documented second pass before sign-off."
**Common misuse:** Used loosely as a synonym for "distracted" or "rushed," rather than the specific, well-documented bias it names — which is why the fix (a deliberate second pass) is specific, not just "slow down."

### Prevalence effect
The tendency for detection sensitivity to drop when the true prevalence of an abnormal finding in a batch of studies is very low (e.g., a screening population), because the reader's threshold calibrates to what's typically there.
**In use:** "Our screening-mammography miss rate ticks up in low-prevalence months — that's a prevalence effect, not a competence problem."
**Common misuse:** Conflated with satisfaction of search; they're distinct biases — prevalence effect is about the reader's calibrated threshold across a batch, satisfaction of search is about stopping within a single study.

### BI-RADS
The ACR's Breast Imaging Reporting and Data System — a lexicon assigning a numbered category (0-6) to a breast imaging finding, each tied to a defined malignancy-likelihood range and management pathway.
**In use:** "It's a BI-RADS 4A — 2 to under 10 percent malignancy likelihood, biopsy indicated, not a 6-month follow-up."
**Common misuse:** Reporting a plain-language impression ("probably benign") without the category number — the number, not the adjective, is what carries the management instruction and gets tracked in outcomes audits.

### Fleischner criteria
The Fleischner Society's guideline table for managing an incidental pulmonary nodule found outside a screening program, stratified by nodule size and patient risk factors.
**In use:** "6-8mm, high-risk given the smoking history — Fleischner puts this at CT in 6-12 months, not routine screening."
**Common misuse:** Applied inside an annual low-dose CT screening program, where Lung-RADS is the correct lexicon instead — the two tables assume different pretest populations and aren't interchangeable.

### Lung-RADS
The ACR's structured reporting and management lexicon specifically for nodules found within an organized low-dose CT lung-cancer screening program.
**In use:** "This is her third annual screening LDCT — score it Lung-RADS, not Fleischner, since she's in the screening cohort with priors."
**Common misuse:** Used as a generic synonym for "any lung nodule scoring system," including for incidental nodules found on a diagnostic (non-screening) CT, where it doesn't apply.

### LI-RADS
The ACR's Liver Imaging Reporting and Data System, categorizing a liver observation's likelihood of being hepatocellular carcinoma (LR-1 to LR-5) based on major imaging features like arterial phase hyperenhancement and washout — restricted to patients at risk (cirrhosis, chronic hepatitis B).
**In use:** "In a cirrhotic patient, this arterially enhancing lesion with washout and a capsule is LR-5 — treat as HCC without biopsy."
**Common misuse:** Applied to any incidental liver lesion regardless of risk status — LI-RADS's category thresholds are validated specifically for an at-risk population, not the general public.

### Critical finding
An imaging finding that could reasonably result in death or serious harm if not acted on immediately — the highest urgency tier under the ACR communication framework, requiring direct, immediate, closed-loop notification.
**In use:** "Tension pneumothorax is a critical finding — call the ED attending now, don't wait for the report to route."
**Common misuse:** Used interchangeably with "urgent" or "actionable" findings, which carry a different (less immediate, but still direct) communication obligation — collapsing the tiers into one blurs which findings need a phone call in the next few minutes versus by end of day.

### Closed-loop communication
Confirmation that the intended recipient actually received and acknowledged a communicated finding — not just that an attempt to communicate was made.
**In use:** "Called Dr. Alvarez directly, she repeated the finding and the plan back to me at 03:14 — that's the closed loop, documented in the addendum."
**Common misuse:** Treating a voicemail, an unread page, or "I told the nurse" as sufficient — none of those confirm the recipient with clinical authority actually got and understood the finding.

### RADPEER
The ACR's peer-review program for scoring interpretive agreement between an original reader and a subsequent reader on the same or a similar prior study, using a 1-4 discrepancy scale.
**In use:** "That prior read scored a RADPEER 3 — a discrepancy most radiologists would agree with in retrospect, worth a section-level pattern check, not a one-off."
**Common misuse:** Treating any non-1 (non-perfect-agreement) score as equivalent to negligence or a standard-of-care breach — RADPEER measures interobserver variation for quality improvement, not a legal determination.

### ALARA
"As Low As Reasonably Achievable" — the radiation dose-optimization principle: use the lowest dose that still produces a diagnostic-quality study for the clinical question being asked.
**In use:** "We adjusted the pediatric CT protocol per ALARA — lower dose, still diagnostic for the appendicitis question being asked."
**Common misuse:** Treated as "always use the least dose possible" without the "still diagnostic" qualifier — a too-low dose that produces a non-diagnostic study and forces a repeat scan increases the patient's total radiation exposure, which is the opposite of what ALARA is for.

### Diagnostic reference level (DRL)
An institutional or national benchmark dose value for a given protocol, used to flag protocols that may need dose optimization — not a regulatory ceiling on any individual patient's dose.
**In use:** "This protocol's CTDIvol is running above our DRL for three months straight — worth a size-specific dose review, not an automatic violation."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a hard per-patient dose limit that can't be exceeded — a DRL is a population-level optimization benchmark; an individual patient's clinically necessary dose can legitimately sit above it.

### Overread
In teleradiology and trainee workflows, the final, official interpretation that supersedes a preliminary read (e.g., an attending's overread of a resident's or a teleradiology night-read preliminary).
**In use:** "The night-float preliminary called it negative; the morning overread caught the nondisplaced fracture — discordance logged for review."
**Common misuse:** Used colloquially to mean "over-calling" or exaggerating findings, rather than its actual meaning as the superseding final interpretation in a preliminary/final read workflow.

### Discrepancy vs. error
A discrepancy is a difference in interpretation between two readers of the same study; an error implies a departure from the applicable standard of care. Not every discrepancy is an error, and not every miss discovered on peer review rises to a standard-of-care breach.
**In use:** "It's a discrepancy on peer review, not an error — a reasonable radiologist could have read it either way given the image quality."
**Common misuse:** Using the terms interchangeably in quality-assurance discussions, which turns a calibration exercise (discrepancy review) into something that reads like a fault-finding process and discourages honest self-reporting.
