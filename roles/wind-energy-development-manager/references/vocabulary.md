# Wind project development working vocabulary

Terms a wind energy development manager uses precisely and a generalist blurs together. Format: definition → how a practitioner says it → common misuse.

**Interconnection queue** — The ordered list of generation projects requesting to connect to the transmission grid at a given point, managed by the regional transmission organization (RTO) or utility, where queue position affects study timing and cost-allocation exposure.
In use: "Our queue position puts us in this cluster study with three other projects — if one withdraws, our cost allocation could shift materially."
Misuse: treating queue position as simply "first come, first served" for capacity, without accounting for how cluster composition and study methodology actually determine cost allocation.

**System impact study (SIS) vs. facilities study** — The SIS is the interconnection study identifying necessary network upgrades and providing a preliminary cost estimate; the facilities study follows with a more detailed, binding cost and design for the specific interconnection facilities.
In use: "The SIS gave us a $52M preliminary estimate — the facilities study will refine that number, but it's not going to move in our favor without a restudy or cost-share appeal."
Misuse: treating the SIS cost estimate as final and binding, when it's a preliminary study that a facilities study or restudy can still revise.

**Cost allocation (interconnection network upgrades)** — The methodology (often "cost causation") an RTO/ISO uses to assign the cost of required network upgrades among the interconnecting projects that triggered the need, sometimes shared across a queue cluster.
In use: "If a competing project in our cluster withdraws, the shared upgrade cost could reallocate more onto us — or less, if the withdrawal reduces the total upgrade scope."
Misuse: assuming a project bears 100% of an upgrade cost in isolation without checking whether the RTO/ISO's methodology shares it across a cluster of triggering projects.

**Beginning-of-construction safe harbor (PTC/ITC)** — IRS rules establishing when a project is treated as having "begun construction" for tax-credit eligibility purposes, satisfied by either incurring at least 5% of total project cost (the cost test) or starting physical work of a significant nature (the physical-work test), followed by a continuity requirement through completion.
In use: "We're safe-harboring under the 5% cost test — need binding equipment contracts and paid invoices totaling 5% of total cost before the deadline, documented and dated."
Misuse: treating "we started construction" informally as sufficient, without the specific documentation (binding contracts, invoices, proof of payment, or physical-work evidence) the IRS test actually requires.

**PTC vs. ITC (Production Tax Credit vs. Investment Tax Credit)** — The PTC pays per kWh of electricity produced over the credit period; the ITC is a one-time credit based on a percentage of qualified project cost at commercial operation. Wind projects typically choose one or the other, not both, and the choice affects financing structure.
In use: "We modeled both PTC and ITC scenarios — the PTC nets out higher given this site's strong capacity factor, but it changes the tax-equity structure we need."
Misuse: assuming the credit choice is purely a tax-rate question without considering how it changes the financing structure (production-based PTC favors a partnership-flip structure differently than a one-time ITC).

**Tax equity flip structure** — A financing arrangement where a tax-equity investor receives most project cash flow and tax benefits initially, with allocation "flipping" to the sponsor/developer after the investor reaches a target return, common for monetizing PTC/ITC value.
In use: "The flip date is modeled at year 9 based on the tax-equity investor's target IRR — that's the point our cash flow share increases."
Misuse: treating the flip structure as a simple loan, when it's an equity partnership with tax-benefit allocation rules distinct from debt repayment mechanics.

**Land option vs. lease vs. easement** — An option secures the right to lease the land later (at agreed terms) without immediate payment obligations beyond the option fee; a lease is the actual land-use agreement once exercised; an easement grants a specific limited right (e.g., transmission line crossing) without full site control.
In use: "We're still in the option period on 95% of parcels — the lease terms convert once we exercise, which we won't do until interconnection economics are confirmed."
Misuse: conflating option and lease terms, especially around payment timing — options typically defer most payment obligations until exercise, and treating them as already-binding leases misstates the project's actual capital commitment.

**Levelized cost of electricity (LCOE)** — The total lifetime cost of a project (capex, opex, financing cost) divided by total lifetime expected energy production, expressed per MWh, used to compare against an offtake price to assess project viability.
In use: "LCOE moved from $24.00 to $28.46/MWh once we added the interconnection upgrade allocation — that's now above our anchor PPA price."
Misuse: comparing LCOE against a spot or short-term price rather than the actual contracted offtake price and term the project will realize revenue under.

**Curtailment commitment (environmental mitigation)** — A binding agreement to reduce or stop turbine operation during specific conditions (e.g., bat activity season, low-wind nighttime hours) as a condition of an environmental permit, which reduces expected production and must be reflected in the project's economics model.
In use: "The permit requires seasonal nighttime curtailment during bat migration — that's built into the resource-adjusted production estimate, not treated as a footnote."
Misuse: modeling project economics off the turbine's theoretical full-operation output without incorporating a known or likely curtailment commitment from the permitting process.

**Queue restudy** — A re-run of the interconnection cost-allocation study, typically triggered by a change in queue cluster composition (a project withdrawing or a new project joining), which can materially change a remaining project's assigned upgrade costs.
In use: "We're requesting a restudy given the withdrawal — the shared upgrade need should drop, and our allocation with it."
Misuse: assuming a restudy is a routine formality with a predictable outcome, rather than an active process the developer should track and, where beneficial, request rather than wait passively for the RTO/ISO to initiate.
