# Vocabulary

### Wet film thickness (WFT)
The thickness of a coating as measured immediately after application, while still wet, used to predict the resulting dry film thickness and confirm application meets specification.
**In use:** "Checking wet film thickness with the gauge at three points on this panel — visual coverage alone doesn't tell us if we hit the 3-4 mil spec."
**Common misuse:** Judging coating adequacy by visual coverage or appearance alone — a surface can look fully and evenly covered while wet film thickness is below spec, since color opacity and film thickness aren't the same thing.

### Flash time (recoat window)
The specified interval a coating must be allowed to partially cure/release solvent before the next coat is applied, with both a minimum (to avoid solvent entrapment) and often a maximum (to avoid intercoat adhesion failure).
**In use:** "Flash time's 20 to 30 minutes on this system — recoating at 15 risks trapping solvent, waiting past 45 risks poor intercoat bond."
**Common misuse:** Treating flash time as a flexible pause that can be shortened under schedule pressure or extended indefinitely without consequence — it's a chemistry-driven window with real risk on both the early and late side.

### Intercoat adhesion
The bond strength between two coats of material applied in sequence, which depends on the timing and surface condition of the underlying coat when the next coat is applied.
**In use:** "That peeling between coats is an intercoat adhesion failure — likely from recoating well past the specified window."
**Common misuse:** Assuming any two coats of the same material will bond adequately regardless of timing — intercoat adhesion is time-and-condition-dependent, and either recoating too soon or too late relative to the specified window can compromise it.

### Solvent entrapment
A coating defect where solvent from an underlying coat is sealed in by a subsequent coat applied too soon, leading to bubbling, blistering, or impaired cure as the trapped solvent later tries to escape.
**In use:** "Bubbling showed up a day after application — classic solvent entrapment from recoating before flash time was up."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a coating that looks fine immediately after application is free of solvent entrapment — the defect often doesn't become visible until later, as trapped solvent gradually works its way out.

### Orange peel
A coating surface texture defect resembling the dimpled skin of an orange, typically caused by improper spray technique, viscosity, or environmental conditions during application.
**In use:** "Getting some orange peel on this panel — checking gun distance and current humidity before assuming it's a material issue."
**Common misuse:** Assuming orange peel always traces to the coating material itself — application technique (distance, speed) and environmental conditions (temperature, humidity) are equally or more common causes.

### Fixed reference sample (master sample)
A physical sample or documented specification used as the unchanging standard for comparing a decorative or cosmetic finish across an entire production run, as opposed to comparing units to each other sequentially.
**In use:** "We're checking every tenth unit against the master sample, not just against the one we just finished — that's how we catch drift before it adds up."
**Common misuse:** Comparing each new unit only to the immediately preceding one in the run — small, individually unnoticeable deviations can accumulate into a significant drift from the true standard over the course of a run if there's no fixed reference check.

### Dry film thickness (DFT)
The thickness of a coating after it has fully cured, the actual value that determines coating performance (corrosion protection, durability), distinct from wet film thickness measured at application.
**In use:** "Wet film thickness at application predicts DFT, but we're confirming actual dry film thickness on the cured part before final release."
**Common misuse:** Treating wet film thickness as interchangeable with dry film thickness — WFT and DFT relate via the material's solids content (a coating that's mostly solvent will lose significant thickness on cure), and confirming DFT (where required) is a separate verification step.

### HVLP (High Volume, Low Pressure) spray gun
A type of spray gun using a high volume of air at relatively low pressure to atomize and apply coating material, generally producing less overspray and higher transfer efficiency than conventional spray guns.
**In use:** "Switched to the HVLP setup for this job — better transfer efficiency and less overspray for this material."
**Common misuse:** Assuming spray gun type alone determines film thickness or quality without regard to actual technique (distance, speed, overlap) — the gun type affects atomization and efficiency, but consistent technique still determines whether film thickness ends up uniform and within spec.

### Cure conditions (environmental)
The temperature, humidity, and airflow conditions present during a coating's application and curing period, which affect both application behavior and final coating properties.
**In use:** "We're outside the validated cure conditions today — extending flash time to compensate for slower solvent release."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a coating process validated under one set of environmental conditions will behave identically under different conditions without adjustment — temperature and humidity meaningfully affect evaporation rate, cure speed, and application behavior.
