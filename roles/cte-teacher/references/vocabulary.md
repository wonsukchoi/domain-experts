# Working vocabulary — terms a CTE teacher uses precisely

Format: definition → how a practitioner says it → common misuse.

**Concentrator** — A student who has completed at least two courses (or one advanced-level equivalent) in a single, CIP-coded program of study, per Perkins V's definition — the population most Perkins performance indicators are actually computed on.
In use: "She's a concentrator after this term — two courses in the same sequence — so she counts toward our 5S1 data next year."
Misuse: applying "concentrator" to any student who's ever taken one intro elective in the department, which inflates the population the indicators are supposed to be measured against.

**Exiter** — A concentrator who leaves the program in the reporting year, whether by graduating, completing the sequence, or otherwise not re-enrolling — the actual denominator for most Perkins V outcome indicators (5S1, 5S2, 5S3), not "everyone currently enrolled."
In use: "Our attainment rate is 15 out of 24 exiters, not 15 out of the 32 students in the program total."
Misuse: computing a credential-attainment or placement rate against total program enrollment instead of the exiter subset, which produces a number that doesn't match what the state actually reports.

**Explorer** — A student taking an introductory CTE course without yet meeting the concentrator threshold; relevant for recruitment and pipeline tracking, not for outcome-indicator reporting.
In use: "We've got strong explorer numbers this year, but that's a pipeline metric — it doesn't move our credential-attainment rate."
Misuse: citing explorer/enrollment growth as evidence the program is meeting its Perkins targets, when those targets are computed on a different population entirely.

**CIP code** — The federal Classification of Instructional Programs code identifying a specific program of study; used to define which courses count toward the same concentrator sequence.
In use: "These two courses share a CIP code, so together they make a student a concentrator."
Misuse: assuming any two technical-sounding courses count as the same sequence without checking they share a CIP code.

**Perkins V performance indicators (1S1–5S3)** — The federally required outcome measures under the Strengthening Career and Technical Education for the 21st Century Act: academic proficiency, graduation rate, postsecondary placement, nontraditional-program enrollment, and program-quality measures including credential attainment (5S1), postsecondary/dual credits earned (5S2), and work-based learning participation (5S3).
In use: "Our 5S1 is credential attainment, 5S3 is work-based learning participation — they're tracked separately, not blended into one 'program success' number."
Misuse: treating "Perkins performance" as a single undifferentiated score instead of naming which specific indicator is under discussion.

**Industry-recognized credential** — A certification or license issued by an industry or licensing body (not the school) verifying a specific, externally validated competency — e.g., a NOCTI, ASE, or ServSafe credential.
In use: "A course completion certificate from us isn't the same as an industry-recognized credential — the 5S1 count is credentials from the outside body."
Misuse: reporting an internal end-of-course certificate as an "industry-recognized credential" when no external body issued or validated it.

**Work-based learning (WBL) continuum** — The progression of employer-connected experiences from lower-intensity (career awareness, guest speakers, job shadowing) to higher-intensity (internship, cooperative education, registered apprenticeship), each with different documentation and liability requirements.
In use: "A job shadow doesn't need the same training-agreement paperwork an internship placement does — they sit at different points on the WBL continuum."
Misuse: treating every employer interaction as equally regulated, either over-papering a one-day job shadow or under-papering an actual internship placement.

**Training agreement** — The signed document scoping a work-based learning placement: specific tasks authorized, supervision plan, and liability/insurance coverage — the placement isn't valid without it, regardless of employer goodwill.
In use: "The agreement authorizes MIG tack-welding under supervision; it doesn't cover the frame straightener, so that task isn't happening."
Misuse: treating a verbal or informal understanding with the employer as sufficient, or assuming a signed agreement automatically covers any task the employer later assigns.

**Career and Technical Student Organization (CTSO)** — Nationally chartered co-curricular organizations (e.g., SkillsUSA, DECA, FCCLA, HOSA, FFA) whose competitive events are built on the same technical standards taught in the CTE course, intended to reinforce classroom content, not replace it.
In use: "The competition rubric is just our course standards in event form — team practice reinforces what the whole class needs anyway."
Misuse: running CTSO prep as a separate, parallel curriculum that only benefits a select competition roster at the expense of the broader cohort's lab time.

**Articulation agreement** — A formal agreement between a secondary program and a postsecondary institution specifying the exact grade, assessment, or skills-verification threshold a student must meet to receive transcripted college credit for a high school CTE course.
In use: "The agreement requires an 80% posted grade and a signed skills form, submitted by May 1 — that's the actual bar, not a general sense the student is 'college-ready.'"
Misuse: assuming any strong-performing student "deserves" the credit regardless of whether the specific documented threshold in the agreement was met.

**Nontraditional enrollment (Indicator 4S1)** — Perkins V's measure of enrollment by students whose gender is underrepresented in a given program's typical labor-market demographics (e.g., women in a welding program), tracked as a program-quality indicator.
In use: "4S1 is about closing the enrollment gap through recruitment, not through moving individual students' schedules around near the count date."
Misuse: treating 4S1 as solvable by a short-term roster adjustment instead of a sustained recruitment-pipeline effort.

**Advisory committee** — A standing group of local employers and industry representatives who review a CTE program's curriculum, equipment, and labor-market alignment, typically meeting at least once or twice per year.
In use: "The advisory committee flagged that we're still teaching on equipment two generations behind what the local shops actually run."
Misuse: treating advisory committee meetings as a formality to check off rather than a substantive input into curriculum and equipment decisions.

**Lockout/tagout (LOTO)** — The OSHA-defined procedure (29 CFR 1910.147) for de-energizing and tagging equipment found unsafe, so it can't be operated until formally cleared.
In use: "The band saw's guard didn't seat properly, so it's tagged out — nobody runs it until maintenance clears it and I re-inspect."
Misuse: verbally telling students not to use a machine without physically tagging it out and logging the defect, which leaves no record if someone uses it anyway.

**Program of study** — The full, sequenced set of courses in a single CIP-coded pathway from an introductory course through a capstone, aligned to both academic and technical standards and, where applicable, a postsecondary or credentialing endpoint.
In use: "Our welding program of study runs three courses across two years, ending in the NOCTI credential exam."
Misuse: using "program of study" to mean any single course, rather than the full multi-course sequence with a defined endpoint.
