# Red flags — what an experienced CTE teacher notices instantly

Smell tests with thresholds. Any one can be innocent; two or three together is a pattern worth acting on.

### Credential-attainment rate calculated against total enrollment rather than concentrator-exiters

Usually means: the denominator is wrong — Perkins V's 5S1 indicator applies to concentrators who exited the program this reporting year, not everyone currently enrolled, and using the larger denominator can make a genuinely on-target program look like it's failing (or the reverse).
First question: "How many of these students are exiters this year versus continuing into next year?"
Data to pull: the SIS concentrator-exit report by CIP code for the reporting year.

### A machine or tool fails a daily inspection but stays in rotation "until this group finishes"

Usually means: a known defect is being used with awareness, which is the exact fact pattern that converts a near-miss into instructor liability the moment there's an injury.
First question: "Is this machine tagged out right now, or is it still running?"
Data to pull: the daily equipment-inspection log and lockout/tagout log for the machine.

### A student authorized on equipment because they "seem ready," with no signed competency checklist entry

Usually means: certification-by-impression has replaced certification-by-checklist, and the gap only surfaces after an incident, when there's no record the step was ever completed.
First question: "Where's the signed checklist entry for this specific student on this specific machine?"
Data to pull: the competency/certification log for that student and machine.

### A work-based-learning placement task list that doesn't match the student's certified skill list

Usually means: the employer's open task list, not the student's actual certified scope, is driving the placement — any incident on an uncertified task shifts liability onto the program.
First question: "Which of these specific tasks does the student have a signed certification for?"
Data to pull: the student's competency checklist against the employer's task list in the training agreement.

### A work-based-learning start date arrives with an unsigned training agreement or unconfirmed insurance coverage

Usually means: scheduling urgency has outpaced the paperwork chain, leaving the student on an employer floor with no confirmed liability coverage.
First question: "Is the agreement signed and is district liability coverage confirmed, right now, today?"
Data to pull: the signed training agreement and insurance-confirmation record.

### CTSO competition-team practice is scheduled during core lab time for the broader concentrator cohort

Usually means: instructional time budgeted for baseline technical-standard mastery across the whole cohort is being redirected to polish a small competition roster.
First question: "Is this practice happening on the whole cohort's shop time, or on separate time?"
Data to pull: the weekly lab schedule and CTSO practice calendar.

### The same exam module fails across two or more consecutive testing cohorts

Usually means: a curriculum-to-blueprint mismatch on that specific module, not a motivation or effort problem with two different groups of students.
First question: "Does this module map to a specific unit in the course sequence, and how much instructional time does that unit actually get?"
Data to pull: the exam blueprint crosswalk against the course scope-and-sequence, two years of module-level results.

### An exam vendor updates its credentialing blueprint and the curriculum crosswalk hasn't been redone

Usually means: the course is still teaching to the old blueprint, and any attainment-rate dip after the change will misleadingly read as an instructional failure rather than a blueprint-lag issue.
First question: "When did the blueprint last change, and when was the crosswalk last updated?"
Data to pull: the vendor's current blueprint version and the date of the last curriculum crosswalk revision.

### Nontraditional-enrollment target (Perkins Indicator 4S1) addressed through a one-time roster adjustment near the reporting deadline

Usually means: a compliance number is being managed for the report rather than through an actual recruitment-pipeline change, which surfaces as an inconsistency the next time the indicator is reviewed.
First question: "Did this enrollment change happen through recruitment outreach, or through a schedule change right before the count date?"
Data to pull: the recruitment/outreach log and the enrollment count-date history for the program.

### Advisory committee hasn't reviewed equipment or curriculum in over a year

Usually means: the program's technical content may be drifting from what local employers actually hire for, since the committee is the mechanism that catches that drift.
First question: "When did the advisory committee last review the equipment list or course scope-and-sequence?"
Data to pull: advisory committee meeting minutes and action items.

### An articulation credit submission goes in after the partner institution's stated deadline

Usually means: the credit won't transfer this cycle regardless of the student's actual grade or skill level, because most articulation agreements treat the deadline as a hard cutoff, not a formality.
First question: "What is the partner institution's exact submission deadline, and has it already passed?"
Data to pull: the articulation agreement's deadline clause and the submission date.

### A borderline articulation grade dispute is argued from the underlying unrounded score instead of the agreement's stated threshold

Usually means: the dispute is being framed around what feels fair rather than what the signed agreement actually specifies (posted grade vs. underlying score, rounding rules).
First question: "Does the agreement specify the posted grade or some other calculation, and what does it actually say about rounding?"
Data to pull: the exact articulation agreement clause and the gradebook's posted (not underlying) value.
