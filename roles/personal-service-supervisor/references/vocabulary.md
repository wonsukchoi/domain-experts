# Vocabulary

Terms generalists use loosely that a personal-service supervisor keeps sharply separate, because the distinction changes who's legally in control, who gets paid what, and what gets logged.

## Booth rent vs. commission vs. employee

**Booth rent** is a flat fee a provider pays to operate as an independent business inside the shop, keeping 100% of their own revenue. **Commission** is a percentage split of service (and often retail) revenue paid to a provider who is a shop employee. The two aren't interchangeable pay-scale options — they're different legal relationships with different control rules.

**In use:** "She's not commission, she's booth rent — I can't dictate her schedule the way I do with the rest of the team."

**Common misuse:** treating booth rent as just a higher-percentage commission tier, rather than a distinct classification that removes the shop's authority to control schedule, pricing, and technique.

## Rebooking rate

The percentage of clients who book their next appointment before leaving their current one, tracked per provider. It's the leading indicator of retention — a client who leaves without a booked next visit is measurably less likely to return.

**In use:** "His rebooking rate dropped to 38% this month — that's a checkout-conversation problem, not a marketing problem."

**Common misuse:** treating overall client-count or revenue trends as the retention signal instead of the rebooking rate specifically, which shows the problem weeks before revenue does.

## Retail attach rate

The percentage of service revenue (or service tickets) that also includes a retail product sale, tracked per provider. It's the standard measure of add-on selling performance, distinct from total retail revenue, which is skewed by shop traffic.

**In use:** "Attach rate is 9% shop-wide, but that's two providers at 20% covering for four at basically zero — it's not a shop-wide coaching issue."

**Common misuse:** reporting a shop-wide blended attach rate as the health metric instead of breaking it down per provider, which hides exactly who needs coaching or a comp-plan fix.

## Column

A provider's individual appointment schedule for the day, including block rules for services that need processing or buffer time (color, chemical treatments). "Filling the column" means booking it; "double-booking a column" intentionally overlaps two clients during a processing wait, distinct from accidentally overbooking it.

**In use:** "That's not overbooked — it's a planned double-book, her color's processing while she does the walk-in's dry-cut."

**Common misuse:** treating any two overlapping appointments as an overbooking error, without checking whether the overlap was a planned buffer-time double-book.

## Patch test

A required preliminary test applying a small amount of a chemical product to a client's skin, typically 24–48 hours before a color or chemical service, to check for an allergic reaction before the full service is performed.

**In use:** "No patch test on file means we don't do the color today, full stop, regardless of how long she's been a repeat client."

**Common misuse:** skipping the patch test for repeat clients on the assumption that a prior clean history rules out a future reaction, which it doesn't.

## IC misclassification

Treating a worker as an independent contractor (booth renter) for tax and liability purposes when their actual working relationship — schedule control, pricing control, tool ownership, mandatory meetings — meets the legal test for employee status. The exposure is on the shop, via back payroll tax, penalties, and interest, not on the worker.

**In use:** "We can call her a contractor on the lease, but if we're still setting her hours and requiring her at every staff meeting, that's misclassification exposure sitting on our books."

**Common misuse:** assuming a signed booth-rent agreement alone settles the classification question, when the actual day-to-day control is what a state or IRS audit examines.

## License reciprocity

The extent to which a cosmetology, barbering, or esthetics license issued in one state is recognized (fully, partially, or not at all) in another, governed state by state and coordinated loosely through bodies like the National Interstate Council of State Boards of Cosmetology (NIC).

**In use:** "She's licensed in Texas, but we're in a non-reciprocal state — she needs to sit for the state exam here before she's on the schedule."

**Common misuse:** assuming any active out-of-state license transfers automatically, without checking the specific receiving state's reciprocity rule.

## Backbar vs. retail product

**Backbar** product is inventory the shop supplies for use during services (the color, the shampoo used at the bowl) and is a cost of goods, not a sale. **Retail** product is what's sold to the client to take home, tracked separately because it's the revenue line behind the attach rate.

**In use:** "That's backbar toner, not a retail sale — don't count it toward her attach number."

**Common misuse:** conflating product used during a service with product sold to the client, which inflates the apparent retail-attach number without any actual retail revenue behind it.

## No-show / late-cancellation fee

A charge applied when a client misses a booked appointment without adequate notice (commonly 24–48 hours), designed to protect a provider's column revenue from unrecoverable lost time, distinct from a standard cancellation made with notice.

**In use:** "That's inside the 24-hour window — it's a late cancellation, the fee applies; if she'd called yesterday morning it wouldn't."

**Common misuse:** applying or waiving the fee inconsistently by provider preference rather than the shop's stated notice-window policy, which creates client-fairness complaints and inconsistent revenue protection.

## Bloodborne pathogen exposure control

The set of protocols (PPE, sharps handling, disinfection) required under OSHA's Bloodborne Pathogens Standard (29 CFR 1910.1030) for any service with a reasonable chance of blood or skin-breaking exposure — waxing, electrolysis, some nail and tattoo procedures.

**In use:** "Waxing counts under our exposure control plan — gloves and the sharps-disposal protocol apply the same as any skin-breaking service."

**Common misuse:** assuming bloodborne-pathogen protocols only apply to services with visible blood, when the standard covers reasonably anticipated exposure, not just confirmed incidents.

## Chair time vs. service time

**Service time** is the actual hands-on duration of a treatment. **Chair time** is the total slot held on the column, including processing, setup, and buffer — the number the schedule is actually built around.

**In use:** "The cut is 30 minutes of service time, but we block 45 minutes of chair time to leave buffer before the next client."

**Common misuse:** scheduling back-to-back appointments using service time instead of chair time, which produces a column that looks efficient on paper and runs late by midday.
