# Playbook

Filled worksheets and step sequences for the recurring personal-service-supervisor tasks: comparing compensation models, building the daily appointment column, and running a license/sanitation compliance check.

## 1. Booth-rent vs. commission breakeven calculator

Run this before proposing or approving any classification change — production relative to breakeven, not preference, drives the recommendation.

| Provider | Monthly service production | Commission % | Commission net to shop | Retail margin retained (commission model) | Employer payroll/WC load (~12% of provider pay) | **Shop net — commission model** | Weekly booth rent quoted | **Shop net — booth rent model** | Recommendation |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Dana (top producer) | $19,000 | 45% | $10,450 | $1,000 | $1,026 | **$10,424** | $325/wk ($1,408/mo) | **$1,408** | Keep on commission — booth rent gives up $9,016/mo |
| Marcus (mid producer) | $7,200 | 45% | $3,960 | $360 | $389 | **$3,931** | $325/wk ($1,408/mo) | **$1,408** | Keep on commission — still favors the shop by ~$2,500/mo |
| Priya (lower producer, wants flexibility) | $3,400 | 45% | $1,870 | $170 | $184 | **$1,856** | $325/wk ($1,408/mo) | **$1,408** | Near breakeven — booth rent is a reasonable offer here, and it's the volume level where flexibility trades don't cost the shop materially |

**Reading the table:** the shop's breakeven production level (where commission net ≈ booth rent) at a 45% commission rate and $325/week rent is roughly $3,400–3,600/month. Above that, commission retains more value for the shop as production rises; below it, booth rent is close to neutral or favorable. **Never quote the same rent conversation to a top producer and a near-breakeven provider as if it's the same decision** — it isn't the same trade at different volumes.

**Classification checklist (apply independently of the economics, per IRS Rev. Rul. 87-41 behavioral/financial-control factors):**

| Factor | Employee indicator | Independent-contractor indicator |
|---|---|---|
| Schedule | Shop assigns days/hours | Provider sets own hours |
| Pricing | Shop sets service prices | Provider sets own prices |
| Tools/station | Shop-owned chair, backbar product | Provider supplies own tools/product |
| Meetings | Mandatory staff meetings | No attendance requirement |
| Client ownership | Client belongs to the shop's system/schedule | Provider brings and controls own client list |
| Training | Shop dictates technique/brand standards | Provider trained/operates independently |

A rent agreement doesn't override this table — if four or more factors point "employee," the classification conversation needs to change the actual working relationship before the paperwork changes, or the misclassification exposure exists regardless of what the contract says.

## 2. Daily appointment column build

Convert today's confirmed bookings and known call-outs into a column plan before opening — never let online self-booking auto-fill without buffer rules applied first.

| Provider | Service-time buffer rule | Confirmed bookings | Walk-in slots held | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Full-service colorist | Color/chemical services block 90–120 min including processing time; no back-to-back color starts inside 30 min of each other | 5 | 1 (11:30am) | Processing-time overlap allows a second client's cut/dry during the wait — column shows "double-booked" but isn't overbooked |
| Cut/style specialist | 30–45 min per service, 10 min buffer | 9 | 2 | Standard column; no chemical buffer needed |
| Nail technician | 45 min mani/pedi combo, 15 min buffer for polish cure | 8 | 1 | Buffer prevents late cascade if a client requests nail art mid-service |

**Call-out protocol (provider calls out same-day):**
1. Pull that provider's confirmed column for the day and separate by service type — chemical/color services can't be reassigned to a provider not trained or licensed for that specific service.
2. Offer same-day rebooking first to clients whose service *can't* transfer (specialized color formula, ongoing treatment plan); offer transfer to another qualified provider second.
3. Never silently double-book a remaining provider's column to absorb the gap — recompute that provider's buffer time before adding anyone in, the same way a housekeeping shortfall gets recalculated per lever, not estimated.
4. Log the call-out and the resolution (rebooked, transferred, cancelled) — a pattern of call-outs on a specific provider or day is a scheduling or retention signal worth a separate review.

## 3. License and sanitation compliance sweep

Run monthly, and immediately on any new hire or state-board renewal cycle change.

| Check | Frequency | Data source | Action if failed |
|---|---|---|---|
| License current (not expired, correct service scope) | Monthly + 30-day-out alert | State board public license-lookup portal | Pull provider from schedule for that service the day it lapses; no exceptions |
| Sanitation log (implement disinfection between clients) | Daily spot-check | Station sanitation log | Same-day correction and log entry; repeat failure escalates to written warning |
| Patch-test record on file for chemical services requiring one | Per chemical service | Client intake/patch-test log | Service does not proceed without a documented patch test on file per manufacturer instructions |
| Bloodborne-pathogen exposure control (waxing, electrolysis, any skin-breaking service) | Quarterly review | Exposure control plan, PPE log | Escalate to owner/EHS same day if PPE or sharps-handling protocol gap found |

**Escalation rule:** any compliance gap gets corrected the same day it's found and logged with date, provider, and corrective action — never deferred to the next scheduled staff meeting. This is the record a state board inspector or an insurer asks for first.
