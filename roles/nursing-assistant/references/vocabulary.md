# Nursing Assistant — Vocabulary

### ADL (Activities of Daily Living)
The basic self-care tasks — bathing, dressing, toileting, transferring, feeding, continence — a CNA assists with and tracks independence-level changes on.
**In use:** "Her ADLs dropped from assist-x1 to assist-x2 on transfers overnight — that's a functional-change finding, not just a busier morning."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a static label ("she needs assist with ADLs") instead of a tracked trend where the *change* in level is the reportable signal.

### Assist-x1 / Assist-x2
Documentation shorthand for how many staff members a task requires — one-person assist vs. two-person assist.
**In use:** "She's assist-x2 for transfers now; yesterday she was assist-x1. I flagged that."
**Common misuse:** Recorded without comparison to the prior shift's level, which erases the trend that makes the number useful.

### Baseline
A specific patient's own normal range for a vital sign or functional level, as distinct from the general population-normal range.
**In use:** "His resting pulse runs in the 90s at baseline — 96 isn't abnormal for him even though it's high-normal on the general chart."
**Common misuse:** Skipped entirely — checking only against textbook normal ranges instead of the patient's documented recent history.

### Blanchable vs. non-blanchable (skin)
Whether a reddened skin area turns white under light finger pressure (blanchable, usually normal) or stays red (non-blanchable, an early pressure-injury sign).
**In use:** "The area on his heel didn't blanch when I pressed it — that's why I flagged it instead of just noting 'redness.'"
**Common misuse:** Conflated with simple redness — a CNA who doesn't test for blanching can't distinguish a normal finding from an early pressure injury.

### Care plan
The individualized, nurse-authored document specifying a patient's assistance levels, fall-risk interventions, and monitoring frequency — a CNA executes it, doesn't write it.
**In use:** "Her care plan calls for vitals q4h and assist-x2 on all transfers — I don't deviate from that without the nurse changing the order."
**Common misuse:** Treated as optional guidance rather than the actual scope-defining document for what the CNA is authorized to do.

### CNA (Certified Nursing Assistant)
The certified, state-registered role — training and competency requirements set at the state level, typically under federal OBRA-87 minimums.
**In use:** "I'm a CNA, not an LPN — I can't assess a wound, but I can describe exactly what I see and report it."
**Common misuse:** Scope confused with LPN/RN — a CNA reports and executes tasks; assessment and care-plan decisions are outside certification.

### Delegation
The nurse's act of assigning a task (like a vital-signs check) to a CNA, while retaining responsibility for interpreting the result and acting on it.
**In use:** "The RN delegated the 2 PM vitals round to me, but she's still the one who decides what the trend means."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to transfer clinical responsibility along with the task — it doesn't; the CNA's job ends at accurate measurement and reporting.

### Early-warning sign
An observation that precedes a measurable, threshold-crossing vital-sign change — often behavioral or functional rather than numeric.
**In use:** "New confusion and reduced appetite showed up a day before her fever spiked — that's the early-warning-sign pattern this role is trained to catch."
**Common misuse:** Dismissed as "just an off day" because it doesn't show up on the vitals sheet — the whole point of the category is that it precedes the numeric change.

### Functional decline
A drop in a patient's independence level on a previously-mastered task, tracked day-over-day on the ADL flow sheet.
**In use:** "He fed himself independently yesterday and needed full assist today — that functional decline is the reportable finding, separate from any vital-sign change."
**Common misuse:** Logged as a single day's data point without the day-over-day comparison that reveals it's a change, not a baseline.

### Intake/output (I&O)
Tracked fluid intake (oral, IV) and output (urine, drainage) — used by the nursing team to monitor hydration and organ function trends.
**In use:** "His output's been under 30cc/hr for the last two checks — I logged it and flagged it to the nurse."
**Common misuse:** Recorded but not totaled/trended across the shift, losing the signal that a single low reading doesn't show.

### Redirection (fall-prevention context)
The act of guiding a patient back to a safe position/activity after observing an unsafe attempt (e.g., trying to stand unassisted).
**In use:** "Third redirection this shift for the same behavior — I'm reporting the pattern, not just logging today's count."
**Common misuse:** Logged as isolated incidents without noticing that repeated redirections in one shift are themselves a reportable pattern.

### Reportable finding
Any observation that meets a hard threshold or represents a meaningful change from baseline/trend — the CNA's core output.
**In use:** "Not everything I see is a reportable finding, but a three-reading trend in the same direction is, even without a single abnormal number."
**Common misuse:** Narrowed to only mean "a number outside the normal range" — pattern-based and functional-change findings are equally reportable.

### SBAR (Situation-Background-Assessment-Recommendation)
A structured nurse-to-physician (or nurse-to-nurse) communication format; CNAs typically report using a simpler factual structure that feeds into the nurse's SBAR, not a full SBAR themselves.
**In use:** "I gave the nurse the numbers and the trend — she's the one who'll SBAR it to the physician if it needs to go further."
**Common misuse:** CNA attempts a full SBAR including an "Assessment" — that section is a clinical judgment outside CNA scope; the CNA's contribution stops at Situation/Background-level facts.

### Scope of practice
The legally defined boundary of tasks a CNA is certified and permitted to perform, set by the state nurse-aide registry and the employing facility.
**In use:** "Staging that pressure area is outside my scope of practice — I document what I see and let the nurse make the staging call."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a soft guideline rather than a legal boundary — operating outside scope (e.g., making a diagnostic statement) is a real compliance issue, not just an overreach in wording.

### Trend
A pattern across three or more sequential readings, as distinct from a single data point.
**In use:** "One elevated pulse after a transfer is normal exertion; a trend of three rising readings across the shift is what I reported."
**Common misuse:** Ignored in favor of evaluating each reading only against the general normal range, discarding the comparison that makes a trend visible.
