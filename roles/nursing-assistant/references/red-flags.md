# Nursing Assistant — Red Flags

### Pulse <50 or >120 bpm
- **Usually means:** Dehydration, pain, medication effect, or (at the extremes) a cardiac or respiratory event in progress.
- **First question:** Is this a change from the patient's own baseline, or is this their normal resting rate?
- **Data to pull:** Last three vital-sign readings for this patient; whether a new medication was given in the last hour.

### SpO2 <92% on room air
- **Usually means:** Genuine desaturation, or a probe-placement/perfusion artifact (cold extremity, nail polish, patient movement).
- **First question:** Does the patient look symptomatic (labored breathing, dusky color, confusion) or does the number look wrong for how they appear?
- **Data to pull:** Re-check on a different finger/site; note whether the patient is on supplemental oxygen and at what rate.

### Three consecutive vitals trending the same direction, even if none cross a hard threshold
- **Usually means:** An evolving process (infection, post-surgical complication, medication effect) that hasn't crossed a single-reading threshold yet.
- **First question:** How many readings, over what span, and in which direction — is this a real trend or normal shift-to-shift variability?
- **Data to pull:** The full run of readings for the shift (or 24 hours), not just the most recent one.

### Sudden drop in independence level for a task the patient did unassisted yesterday
- **Usually means:** Infection, medication side effect, or a new neurological event — functional decline often precedes a measurable vital-sign change.
- **First question:** Is this the first instance today, or has it been building across multiple tasks?
- **Data to pull:** Yesterday's and today's ADL flow-sheet entries side by side.

### New coughing, choking, or wet-sounding voice during feeding/drinking
- **Usually means:** Aspiration risk — a new or worsening swallowing problem.
- **First question:** Did this happen with liquids, solids, or both?
- **Data to pull:** Stop the task immediately (this one doesn't wait for the end of the report cycle); note texture/consistency of what was being given.

### Patient repeatedly attempting to self-transfer despite a documented fall-risk care plan
- **Usually means:** Increased confusion, unaddressed toileting need, pain, or a care-plan that no longer matches the patient's actual risk level.
- **First question:** How many attempts this shift, and is there a common trigger (e.g., always after being left alone, always around a specific time)?
- **Data to pull:** Bed-alarm log/rounding log for the shift; last toileting time.

### Reddened or discolored skin area that doesn't blanch (turn white) under light finger pressure
- **Usually means:** Early pressure injury — non-blanching is the clinical distinguishing sign from a normal blanchable redness.
- **First question:** Where on the body, and is it over a bony prominence (heel, sacrum, hip)?
- **Data to pull:** Size, exact location, and time first noted; last repositioning/turning time.

### Temperature >100.4°F (38°C), or a drop below 96.8°F
- **Usually means:** Infection (fever) or, at the low end, hypothermia/sepsis-adjacent physiologic compensation — both are reportable, not just the high end.
- **First question:** Is this an isolated reading or part of a rising trend across the shift?
- **Data to pull:** Route used for this reading (must match care-plan-specified route for a valid trend comparison) and the last two readings.

### Automated vital-sign reading that doesn't match how the patient visibly looks
- **Usually means:** Equipment artifact (poor perfusion, motion, wrong cuff size) more often than a genuinely contradictory physiologic state, but can't be assumed without checking.
- **First question:** Does a manual re-check (palpated pulse, visual respiratory count) agree with the automated number?
- **Data to pull:** Manual recheck result; equipment/probe placement.

### Patient reports new or worsening pain not explained by their known condition
- **Usually means:** A new problem (infection, DVT, injury) rather than progression of the known issue — new-onset pain in a different location than the primary complaint is more concerning than expected post-op soreness.
- **First question:** Where exactly, and is it new or a change in the pain they already had?
- **Data to pull:** Pain-scale rating if the facility uses one, location, and time of onset.
