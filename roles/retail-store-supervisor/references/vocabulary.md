# Vocabulary

Terms generalists flatten or conflate that a shift supervisor keeps sharply separate.

## Conversion rate vs. traffic

**Traffic** is the count of people who walk in — a marketing/location/season outcome the supervisor doesn't control. **Conversion rate** is the share of that traffic that buys — the floor-execution outcome the supervisor does control (staffing, wait times, product findability).

*In use:* "Traffic's flat year over year, so don't touch the marketing conversation — this is a conversion problem, and that's ours to fix on the floor."

*Common misuse:* saying "sales are down" as if it's one number's problem, when a sales miss decomposes cleanly into a traffic component (not the supervisor's lever) and a conversion component (the supervisor's lever) — conflating them sends the fix to the wrong owner.

## UPT vs. ATV vs. basket size

**UPT** (units per transaction) is the average number of items in a sale. **ATV** (average transaction value, also "average sale") is the average dollar amount per sale. **Basket size** is used loosely for either in casual conversation but should mean UPT when precision matters, since "basket value" already covers the dollar figure.

*In use:* "UPT's down but ATV's flat — that's an add-on execution gap, not a pricing problem; if it were pricing, ATV would've moved too."

*Common misuse:* treating a UPT and an ATV drop as the same signal calling for the same fix — one is a coaching issue, the other is usually a merchandising or pricing issue.

## SPLH (sales per labor hour)

Total sales divided by total scheduled labor hours for the same period — the reconciling metric between "the schedule feels tight" and "sales feel soft." Tracked at the week level it hides staffing misallocation; tracked by daypart it exposes it.

*In use:* "Weekly SPLH looks fine at $199/hour, but Saturday peak is running $95 — that block is what's actually underwater."

*Common misuse:* citing a single store-wide weekly SPLH figure as evidence a schedule is efficient — an average can be healthy while a specific block is badly misallocated.

## Shrink vs. shortage vs. waste

**Shrink** is the broad category: inventory recorded in the system that isn't physically present, from any cause. **Shortage** is sometimes used interchangeably with shrink, but in tighter usage refers specifically to the cash-drawer or till version (money that should be there and isn't). **Waste** is inventory loss from spoilage, damage, or markdown-to-zero — a subset of shrink with a different, usually non-theft, root cause.

*In use:* "Don't lump the damaged-freight write-offs into the same 'shrink is up' conversation as the register shortages — different causes, different fixes."

*Common misuse:* using "shrink" and "theft" as synonyms — theft is one cause among several (administrative error, vendor fraud, and waste are frequently comparable in size).

## No-sale vs. void vs. RTV (return-to-vendor)

**No-sale** opens the register drawer without a transaction (making change, checking a price). **Void** cancels a transaction after it's been entered but before completion. **RTV** is inventory shipped back to a vendor, logged separately from a customer return. All three are legitimate, routine actions — and all three are also the specific mechanisms process error or fraud hide inside, which is why they're the first audit target on a shrink spike.

*In use:* "Pull no-sales and voids by register before you pull camera footage — it's faster and it's usually where the answer is."

*Common misuse:* treating a high no-sale or void count as inherently suspicious; most stores have a handful of registers that run high on these for entirely legitimate reasons (fitting-room price checks, frequent price-match corrections) — the signal is a register or associate that's an outlier against its own baseline, not a raw count.

## Key holder vs. manager on duty (MOD)

**Key holder** is whoever has alarm codes and safe/register access — an operational/security designation, not a rank. **Manager on duty** is whoever holds decision authority for the shift (approving a return exception, handling an escalated complaint) — can be the same person as the key holder or not, depending on the store's structure.

*In use:* "She's not MOD tonight, but she is the key holder — she can open the safe, but the discount-override call still goes to whoever's MOD."

*Common misuse:* assuming key-holder status implies decision authority, or vice versa — the two are assigned independently and a store can have a key holder on shift with no MOD authority.

## Zone coverage vs. headcount

**Headcount** is how many people are scheduled. **Zone coverage** is whether those people are positioned against where the traffic and tasks actually are. A store can be fully headcounted and badly zoned.

*In use:* "We're not short-staffed today, we're mis-zoned — move one off folding and onto the fitting-room line."

*Common misuse:* requesting "more hours" when the actual problem is allocation of the hours already scheduled — a headcount increase without a zone plan often just adds people to the same already-covered spots.

## Predictability pay / Fair Workweek

**Predictability pay** is the statutory premium owed to an hourly worker when their employer changes a posted schedule inside the required advance-notice window, under a **Fair Workweek** (predictive-scheduling) ordinance. Amount and notice window are set by the specific jurisdiction's statute, not a company-wide default.

*In use:* "That shift add is inside our 14-day window and wasn't voluntary, so it owes predictability pay — log it before you forget."

*Common misuse:* assuming these rules are a single national standard; they are jurisdiction-specific (Oregon, NYC, San Francisco, Seattle, Chicago, Philadelphia each have their own notice windows and premium schedules) and a multi-store supervisor needs the local text, not a memorized company-wide rule.

## Clopening

Scheduling the same associate to close one night and open the next morning, leaving a short rest window between shifts. Several Fair Workweek ordinances set a minimum rest period (commonly 9–11 hours) and require a premium if it's violated.

*In use:* "That's a clopening under the ordinance's 10-hour rest rule — either move the open to someone else or pay the premium."

*Common misuse:* treating clopening as only a fatigue/morale issue when the jurisdiction has made it a specific, logged compliance violation with a dollar consequence.

## Comp sales (comparable-store sales)

Sales growth measured only against stores open in both periods being compared (typically 12+ months), stripping out the effect of new-store openings or closures. The metric leadership actually tracks; a store's raw sales growth number is not the same figure.

*In use:* "New store openings are dragging total company sales up, but comp is only 1.2% — that's the number we're actually held to."

*Common misuse:* citing raw total sales growth as if it reflects existing-store performance — it conflates new-unit growth with same-store execution, which is exactly what comp sales is designed to separate out.

## Sell-through

The percentage of received inventory that has sold within a given period, distinct from the buyer's open-to-buy planning — a supervisor tracks it at the store/category level mainly to flag a markdown or reorder conversation upward, not to make the buy decision itself.

*In use:* "Sell-through on the fall jackets is 35% at week six — flag it to the buyer now, before we're sitting on it at week twelve."

*Common misuse:* treating sell-through as a store-level decision lever (marking down or reordering unilaterally) rather than a signal to escalate — assortment and pricing calls generally sit with buying/merchandising, not the store supervisor.
