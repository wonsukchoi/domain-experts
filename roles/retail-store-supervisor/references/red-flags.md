# Red flags

Smell tests a supervisor catches before a shift report gets forwarded up. Format: what it usually means, the first question to ask, and the data to pull.

## 1. Weekly sales down but weekly traffic flat

**Usually means:** conversion has fallen in one specific daypart or zone, masked by an unchanged weekly average — not a store-wide selling slump.

**First question:** "Break conversion out by daypart for the last four weeks — where exactly did it fall?"

**Data to pull:** transaction counts and door counts by 2-hour block; the posted schedule for those blocks; any mystery-shop notes covering the same window.

## 2. Shrink rate up 2+ consecutive quarters

**Usually means:** roughly half the time it's a paperwork/POS-exception problem (unlogged markdowns, refund abuse, receiving miscounts) rather than shoplifting — the "unknown loss" bucket in most shrink reports absorbs both causes indistinguishably until audited.

**First question:** "Before we call loss prevention — have voids, no-sales, and refund-without-receipt counts been pulled by register and associate for this period?"

**Data to pull:** POS exception report by register/associate; receiving and RTV logs for the same window; cycle-count variance by SKU/category to see if the loss is concentrated or spread evenly (concentrated points to a specific process or person; spread points to a systemic scan/receiving issue).

## 3. SPLH looks fine at the weekly level

**Usually means:** an underwater peak block is being subsidized by an overstaffed quiet block — the weekly average is the one number built to hide exactly this.

**First question:** "What's SPLH broken out by the same dayparts we schedule against, not the week total?"

**Data to pull:** WFM report sliced by 2-hour block; schedule for the same blocks; conversion by block to confirm whether the low SPLH block is a staffing or a demand problem.

## 4. UPT falling while ATV holds steady

**Usually means:** an add-on/cross-sell execution gap on the floor — associates ringing the primary item and not asking the add-on question — rather than a pricing or assortment issue, which would typically move ATV or conversion instead.

**First question:** "Is this UPT drop showing up across the whole team or concentrated on a few registers/associates?"

**Data to pull:** UPT by associate/register over the period; whether a new POS prompt or bagging change removed a natural cross-sell touchpoint; mystery-shop transcript for the add-on ask.

## 5. One register or associate producing a disproportionate share of voids or no-sales

**Usually means:** most often a training gap (not knowing the correct override path) or a process workaround; less often, but seriously, an early theft or refund-fraud signal — the two look identical in the raw count and only the pattern over time separates them.

**First question:** "Pull that register's void/no-sale count against the store average for the same shift hours over the last month — is this one bad week or a trend?"

**Data to pull:** void/no-sale counts by register and associate, trended weekly; manager-override log for the same transactions; whether the associate was trained on the correct override procedure at all.

## 6. Discrepancy over $10 at close, same register or associate, more than once in a month

**Usually means:** counting error or till-handling process gap the first time; a pattern narrows to either a specific process failure at that register or, less often, misappropriation — investigate the process first since it is the more common and far cheaper cause to fix.

**First question:** "Has this register or this associate had an over/short before this month, and was it ever resolved?"

**Data to pull:** over/short log by register and associate for the trailing 90 days; whether the count was independently verified per the closing checklist; whether the same till/drawer is involved regardless of associate (points to a hardware or float-setup issue, not a person).

## 7. Chronic lateness or call-outs concentrated around one shift type

**Usually means:** most often a scheduling-fit problem (the shift conflicts with school, a second job, or transit) rather than a motivation problem — the fix is often a schedule swap, not a write-up.

**First question:** "Is this happening on shifts with a specific start time or day, or across the board?"

**Data to pull:** attendance log by shift type; whether the associate has requested a schedule change that wasn't accommodated; whether other associates on the same shift type show the same pattern (points to the shift, not the person).

## 8. A schedule change gets made inside the statutory advance-notice window without a logged reason

**Usually means:** either a genuine emergency that wasn't documented in the moment, or a habit of treating "we're busy" as an emergency exception — the second exposes the store to a predictability-pay claim and erodes the exception's credibility with the compliance team.

**First question:** "What's the documented trigger for this change, and does it match the ordinance's emergency-exception language?"

**Data to pull:** the schedule-change log entry and its stated reason; the jurisdiction's specific emergency-exception text; how many changes in the trailing quarter were logged under "emergency" versus a specific named cause.

## 9. New associate's performance is fine on the floor but their numbers (UPT, conversion) lag the team

**Usually means:** more often a POS/attribution issue (transactions logging to the wrong associate ID at a shared register) than an actual selling gap — check the mechanical explanation before assuming a skill gap.

**First question:** "Are they working shared registers where sales get logged under a house or shift-lead ID?"

**Data to pull:** register assignment log against transaction-associate-ID data; a direct floor observation to sanity-check the numbers against what's actually happening.
