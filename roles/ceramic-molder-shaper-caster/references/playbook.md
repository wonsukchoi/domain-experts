# Playbook

## Shrinkage-compensation worksheet

Fill for any new mold or material formulation change.

| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| Target final (fired/cured) dimension | 20.00" |
| Current material's characterized shrinkage rate | 12% (verified for CURRENT formulation, not assumed from prior) |
| Calculated green (mold) dimension | 20.00 / (1 - 0.12) = 22.73" |
| Mold cut to | 22.73" |
| Test-fire/cure sample performed? | Y — required before full production commitment |
| Test-fire result | 19.98" (within ±0.02" of 20.00" target) |
| Cleared for full production? | Y |

**Rule:** never assume a previous material formulation's shrinkage rate applies to a new or modified formulation — always verify current material's actual rate, and always test-fire/cure a sample before committing a new mold to full production.

## Demold-timing readiness table

| Signal | Too early (don't demold) | Ready to demold | Too late (risk) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Firmness (tactile/visual) | Soft, deforms under light touch | Firm, holds shape, slight give | Very hard, may grip mold tightly |
| Moisture content (if measurable) | High — still plastic/wet | At specified target range | Very low — over-hardened |
| Visual surface | Wet sheen, no shrinkage gap visible | Slight shrinkage gap from mold wall visible | Excessive shrinkage, potential sticking |

**Rule:** use the actual readiness signal appropriate to the material, not a fixed elapsed time — conditions (ambient temperature/humidity, batch moisture) shift the correct timing from one production run to the next.

## Drying-rate control guide for uneven-thickness parts

1. Identify the part's thickest or otherwise slowest-drying section before setting a drying/curing schedule.
2. Set the overall drying rate based on what that slowest section can tolerate, not the fastest section's capability.
3. For parts with significant thickness variation, consider controlled/humid environment drying (slower, more uniform) over rapid open-air drying.
4. Monitor for early crack signs at thickness transition points during drying — these are the highest-risk locations for differential shrinkage stress.
5. If cracking occurs, review whether the schedule was set for the slowest section or for overall convenience/speed before adjusting the mix/material rather than assuming a material defect.
