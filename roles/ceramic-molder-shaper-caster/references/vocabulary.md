# Vocabulary

### Shrinkage rate (linear shrinkage)
The percentage by which a material's dimension decreases from its wet/green state through drying, curing, or firing to its final state, used to calculate mold dimensions that will produce the correct final part size.
**In use:** "This body shrinks twelve percent — mold needs to be cut to that compensation, not the finished dimension."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a shrinkage rate from a previous material formulation still applies to a current or modified formulation — shrinkage rate is specific to the actual material composition and process, and changes when the material changes, even subtly.

### Green state (green part)
The unfired, uncured, or still-wet state of a molded part, before drying/curing/firing has caused shrinkage and hardening to reach the final material state.
**In use:** "Green dimension's 22.73 inches — that shrinks down to our 20-inch target once it's fired."
**Common misuse:** Measuring or verifying a part's dimension only in its green state and treating that as confirmation of final spec compliance — green dimension is only meaningful in combination with the material's actual shrinkage behavior; final verification requires measuring after firing/curing.

### Demold window
The specific timeframe during which a cast or molded part has firmed enough to hold its shape unsupported but hasn't yet hardened or shrunk enough to stick to or damage the mold on removal.
**In use:** "We're checking firmness, not the clock, to find the demold window on this batch — humidity's different today."
**Common misuse:** Treating demold timing as a fixed elapsed-time rule applicable regardless of material batch or ambient conditions — the correct window shifts with temperature, humidity, and material-specific factors, and a fixed time that worked previously may not apply today.

### Differential shrinkage stress
Internal stress within a part caused by uneven shrinkage rates between different sections (a fast-drying thin section versus a slow-drying thick section), which can cause cracking if not managed.
**In use:** "That crack's right where the wall thickness changes — classic differential shrinkage stress from uneven drying."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a uniform drying/curing schedule is inherently safe for any part geometry — differential shrinkage stress specifically arises from geometric unevenness, and a schedule that's fine for a uniform-thickness part can crack an uneven one.

### Slip casting
A ceramic forming process where a liquid clay suspension (slip) is poured into a porous (typically plaster) mold, which absorbs water from the slip to build up a solid layer against the mold wall.
**In use:** "Slip's been in the mold the right amount of time to build the wall thickness we need before we drain the excess."
**Common misuses:** Treating slip casting wall-build time and demold time as the same parameter — wall-build time determines part thickness, while demold time determines when the formed part can be safely removed, and they're governed by different aspects of the process.

### Air entrapment (casting void)
Air pockets trapped within a cast or poured material during filling, forming a weak point or surface defect that's often invisible until later inspection, firing, or structural failure.
**In use:** "We vent and vibrate every pour specifically to avoid air entrapment — you can't see a trapped void from the outside until it's a problem."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a mold that appears fully filled during pouring is free of trapped air — visual fill assessment doesn't reveal internal voids, which require deliberate technique (venting, vibration) to prevent, not just visual confirmation after the fact.

### Mold wear (parting line wear)
Gradual degradation of a mold's dimensional accuracy from repeated use — plaster molds absorbing moisture and softening, rigid molds wearing at the seams where mold sections meet.
**In use:** "This mold's done a lot of cycles — checking parts against spec before assuming it's still producing to the original dimension."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a mold's output stays constant indefinitely based on its original qualification — molds degrade with use in ways that shift actual output dimension, requiring periodic re-verification rather than one-time trust.

### Vibration/venting (casting technique)
Deliberate mechanical agitation (vibration) or provision of escape paths for trapped air (venting) during a pour, used to minimize air entrapment in the finished cast material.
**In use:** "Vibrating the form as we pour to work air bubbles up and out before this sets."
**Common misuse:** Treating vibration/venting as optional extra effort for a "simple" pour — air entrapment risk exists on any pour regardless of perceived simplicity, and skipping the technique trades a small time savings for a real, often-undetected defect risk.

### Firing schedule / curing schedule
The specified time-temperature (firing) or time-condition (curing) profile a material must follow to reach its final hardened state correctly, distinct from the mold or casting process itself.
**In use:** "Firing schedule calls for a slow ramp through this specific temperature range — that's where most cracking risk lives if we rush it."
**Common misuse:** Treating the firing/curing schedule as a fixed total time that can be compressed for efficiency — certain temperature/time ranges within the schedule carry specific risk (like water loss or phase transitions in ceramics) that require controlled passage, not just an overall faster total time.
