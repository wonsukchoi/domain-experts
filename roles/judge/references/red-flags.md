# Red flags — signals a judge notices instantly

Smell tests with thresholds. Any one alone can be a fixable gap in a ruling; two or more together on the same case is a pattern worth pausing to address before proceeding.

### A sentence is set at a guideline range's endpoint with no stated reasoning tied to specific case facts
- **Usually means:** the sentence may have been applied mechanically rather than individualized against the statutory factors, which is vulnerable on appeal regardless of whether the number itself is reasonable.
- **First question:** "What specific facts about this defendant and this offense justify this exact point in (or outside) the range, stated on the record?"
- **Data to pull:** sentencing statutory-factor worksheet (see `references/playbook.md` §1), victim impact statements, any aggravating/mitigating facts not captured by the guideline calculation itself.

### A potential conflict exists (financial interest, personal relationship, prior involvement) and the judge's own confidence in personal fairness is the basis for not recusing
- **Usually means:** the appearance-of-impartiality standard, not personal certainty, is the operative test — a reasonable observer's perception controls regardless of actual bias.
- **First question:** "Would a reasonable, informed observer question impartiality here, independent of whether actual bias exists?"
- **Data to pull:** recusal determination checklist (see `references/playbook.md` §2), nature and timing of the relationship or interest in question.

### A dispositive motion or evidence exclusion is granted as a case-management sanction with no on-record due process finding
- **Usually means:** docket discipline may be overriding a party's right to a fair opportunity to be heard, creating reversible error regardless of whether the sanctioned party's conduct was genuinely dilatory.
- **First question:** "Is there an explicit, on-record finding that the sanctioned party had fair notice and an opportunity to respond or cure, before this sanction was imposed?"
- **Data to pull:** case management order balancing worksheet (see `references/playbook.md` §3), procedural history showing notice and opportunity given.

### A jury instruction on a contested legal point is drafted from scratch rather than adapted from the jurisdiction's pattern instructions
- **Usually means:** instructional error is reviewed independently as a question of law — improvised language on a novel point carries meaningfully higher reversal risk than a vetted pattern instruction, even if the underlying substantive ruling is sound.
- **First question:** "Does a pattern instruction exist for this point, and if this deviates from it, what's the specific legal justification stated on the record?"
- **Data to pull:** applicable pattern jury instructions for the jurisdiction, draft instruction language, on-record justification for any deviation.

### A ruling on a novel or case-dispositive issue is made immediately from the bench without a recess or structured argument
- **Usually means:** the ruling may not reflect adequate consideration or a record sufficient for appellate review, even if the snap judgment happens to be correct.
- **First question:** "Was this issue routine and well-settled, or novel/dispositive enough to have warranted a brief recess for structured argument first?"
- **Data to pull:** real-time ruling triage framework (see `references/playbook.md` §4), nature of the legal question and its likely case impact.

### Ex parte communication with one party (or their counsel) about a pending matter occurred outside a recognized exception
- **Usually means:** a structural impartiality violation that undermines the proceeding's legitimacy regardless of whether the communication actually influenced the outcome.
- **First question:** "Was this communication disclosed to all parties, and does it fall within a recognized exception (e.g., scheduling, emergency relief with proper notice attempted)?"
- **Data to pull:** nature, timing, and content of the communication, applicable exceptions under the jurisdiction's code of judicial conduct.

### A written opinion addresses only the prevailing party's arguments, not the losing party's strongest points
- **Usually means:** the opinion may not survive appellate scrutiny even if the ultimate ruling is correct, since a reviewing court can't confirm the losing party's arguments were actually considered.
- **First question:** "What was the losing party's strongest argument, and where does the opinion address it directly?"
- **Data to pull:** both parties' briefing, draft opinion's structure and whether it engages the losing party's key arguments specifically.

### A sentencing or ruling pattern shows the same outcome regardless of case-specific facts across similar case types
- **Usually means:** decisions may be defaulting to a habitual pattern (e.g., always sentencing at the guideline low end, or always denying a particular type of motion) rather than being individualized to each case's actual record.
- **First question:** "What in this specific case's record drove this outcome, distinct from the pattern observed across similar cases?"
- **Data to pull:** comparison of rulings/sentences across recent similar cases, case-specific facts distinguishing (or not distinguishing) this one.
