# Judge working vocabulary

Terms a judge uses precisely and a generalist blurs together. Format: definition → how a practitioner says it → common misuse.

**Harmless error vs. reversible error** — Harmless error is a mistake that didn't affect a substantial right or the outcome of the proceeding, and doesn't warrant reversal on appeal; reversible error is one that did, requiring the appellate court to overturn or remand.
In use: "Even if that evidentiary ruling was technically wrong, it's harmless error given the overwhelming weight of the other evidence on that point."
Misuse: assuming any technical mistake is automatically reversible — the harmless error doctrine specifically asks whether the mistake affected the outcome, not merely whether a rule was violated.

**Appearance of impartiality vs. actual bias** — The recusal standard under most judicial conduct codes asks whether a reasonable, informed observer would question the judge's impartiality, a broader and more protective standard than requiring proof of actual bias.
In use: "There's no evidence of actual bias here, but a reasonable observer would still question impartiality given the financial interest — that's enough to recuse."
Misuse: treating personal confidence in one's own fairness as sufficient to avoid recusal — the operative test is the reasonable observer's perception, not the judge's self-assessment.

**Advisory guideline range vs. statutory factors** — Since United States v. Booker, federal sentencing guidelines are advisory rather than mandatory; the guideline range is a starting point, but the final sentence must be independently justified against the statutory sentencing factors (18 U.S.C. § 3553(a)).
In use: "The guideline range gives us 27 to 33 months as a starting point, but the sentence itself has to be justified against the statutory factors, not just picked from within that range."
Misuse: treating the guideline range as binding or self-justifying — a sentence within the range still needs individualized statutory-factor reasoning, and a sentence outside the range (a variance) needs even more explicit justification.

**Upward/downward variance vs. departure** — A variance is a sentence outside the guideline range justified by the § 3553(a) statutory factors directly; a departure is a sentence outside the range justified by a specific guideline provision authorizing deviation for a stated reason (e.g., substantial assistance to authorities).
In use: "This isn't a departure under a specific guideline provision — it's a variance based on the § 3553(a) victim-vulnerability analysis."
Misuse: using the terms interchangeably — they rest on different legal bases (guideline-provision-specific vs. statutory-factor-based) and require different reasoning to support on the record.

**Ex parte communication** — Communication between a judge and one party (or that party's counsel) about a pending or impending matter, outside the presence of the other party, generally prohibited except within narrow recognized exceptions.
In use: "That scheduling call needs the other side looped in or documented — anything substantive ex parte on a pending motion is off-limits."
Misuse: assuming any informal or brief contact with one side is automatically fine as long as it's not about the "merits" — even seemingly minor substantive contact about a pending matter can constitute a prohibited ex parte communication depending on content.

**Pattern (model) jury instructions** — Standardized instruction language developed and vetted by a jurisdiction's courts or judicial committees, used as the default over improvised instruction language on the same legal point.
In use: "We're using the pattern instruction on this element — no need to draft new language when a vetted version already exists."
Misuse: assuming any legally accurate instruction is equally safe regardless of whether it's pattern or improvised — instructional error is reviewed independently as a question of law, and deviating from vetted pattern language carries its own distinct reversal risk.

**Case management order** — A judicial order setting deadlines, discovery scope, and procedural requirements for a case's progression, balancing docket efficiency against each party's due process rights.
In use: "The case management order sets a firm discovery cutoff, but any sanction for missing it still needs its own due-process finding before it's enforced."
Misuse: treating a case management order's deadlines as self-enforcing sanctions — enforcing them against a party (e.g., excluding evidence, entering default) still requires its own on-record due process finding at the time of enforcement.

**Position of trust (sentencing aggravator)** — A recognized aggravating consideration in sentencing where the defendant used a position of authority, expertise, or trust (financial advisor, caregiver, fiduciary) to facilitate the offense, distinct from and potentially additional to the offense's dollar-loss or severity calculation.
In use: "The guideline loss table captures the dollar amount, but the position-of-trust factor is a separate consideration the court is weighing on top of that."
Misuse: assuming the guideline calculation automatically accounts for every aggravating circumstance — some factors (like the disproportionate impact on vulnerable victims relative to the same dollar loss elsewhere) may not be captured by the loss table and need separate, explicit consideration.

**Recusal vs. disqualification** — Recusal is the judge's voluntary withdrawal from a case; disqualification is often used interchangeably but sometimes refers specifically to a formal, statutorily-governed removal process (e.g., under 28 U.S.C. § 455), sometimes initiated by a party's motion rather than the judge's own initiative.
In use: "This is a self-recusal on my own initiative, not a disqualification motion — but the underlying standard I'm applying is the same appearance test § 455 sets."
Misuse: assuming the two terms always describe procedurally identical events — depending on jurisdiction and context, one may be voluntary and the other may involve a formal motion and ruling process.
