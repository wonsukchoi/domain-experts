# Sentencing, recusal, and case management playbook

Filled worksheets for the three recurring judge decisions: sentencing statutory-factor analysis, recusal determination, and case management order balancing.

## 1. Sentencing statutory-factor worksheet (18 U.S.C. § 3553(a) framework)

```
Advisory guideline range: <offense level, criminal history category, resulting range>

Factor 1 - Nature and seriousness of the offense:
  <specific facts, not a general characterization>

Factor 2 - Defendant's history and characteristics:
  <criminal history, personal circumstances, position of trust if applicable>

Factor 3 - Need for the sentence to reflect seriousness, promote respect for law,
  provide just punishment:
  <specific reasoning>

Factor 4 - Adequate deterrence (general and specific):
  <specific reasoning, e.g. targeting a vulnerable population increases deterrence need>

Factor 5 - Protection of the public:
  <specific reasoning>

Factor 6 - Rehabilitation needs:
  <specific reasoning>

Factor 7 - Avoiding unwarranted sentencing disparities:
  <comparison to similarly situated defendants if relevant>

Factor 8 - Restitution:
  <amount and allocation>

Aggravating/mitigating facts NOT captured by the guideline calculation itself:
  <e.g. abuse of position of trust, victim vulnerability, extraordinary acceptance
   of responsibility beyond the standard reduction>

Final sentence and reasoning tying it to the above:
  <explicit statement, not just the number>
```

Rule: every factor gets an explicit entry, even a brief one — a sentencing decision silent on a required factor is vulnerable on appeal regardless of whether the ultimate sentence is reasonable.

## 2. Recusal determination checklist

- [ ] Any financial interest (personal or immediate family) in the outcome or a party?
- [ ] Any personal relationship (family, close friendship, prior close professional relationship) with a party, attorney, or witness?
- [ ] Any prior involvement in the matter (as counsel, material witness, or in a different judicial capacity)?
- [ ] Any public statement made about the specific matter or parties that could suggest prejudgment?
- [ ] Would a reasonable, informed observer question impartiality based on any of the above, even absent actual bias?

Decision rule: if the last box is checked "yes" or is a close call, recuse. The standard is the reasonable-observer's perception of appearance, not the judge's own confidence in personal fairness.

## 3. Case management order balancing worksheet

| Consideration | Docket-discipline side | Due-process side |
|---|---|---|
| Discovery deadline | Firm deadline prevents indefinite delay | Extension warranted if a party shows genuine, non-dilatory need for more time |
| Motion in limine / evidence exclusion | Prevents late, disruptive evidentiary surprises at trial | Exclusion as a sanction requires on-record findings that the excluded party had fair notice and opportunity to cure |
| Sanctions for delay | Deters strategic stalling | Sanction proportional to the actual prejudice caused, not punitive beyond that |
| Summary disposition on a procedural default | Keeps docket moving, discourages non-compliance | Requires confirming the defaulting party actually had notice and opportunity to respond before default is entered |

Rule: any case-management ruling that resolves a case (or a significant issue) as a sanction needs an explicit on-record finding that due process was still afforded — docket efficiency alone doesn't justify skipping that finding.

## 4. Real-time ruling triage (during trial)

```
Is this objection/motion:
  (a) Governed by well-settled, clear rule? -> Rule promptly, state the rule briefly on record.
  (b) Novel, close, or likely case-dispositive? -> Take a brief recess/sidebar; hear structured
      argument; state reasoning explicitly once ruling.
  (c) Involves a jury instruction on a contested legal point? -> Default to the jurisdiction's
      pattern instruction; only deviate with a specific, on-record legal justification.
```

Rule: the fast path (a) is appropriate for the bulk of trial rulings — reserving the slower path (b) for genuinely close or dispositive calls is what keeps the trial moving without sacrificing the record a reviewing court will need on the calls that actually matter.
