# Red flags

Smell tests a senior PCO catches reviewing a job file or a service ticket. Format for each: what it usually means, the first question to ask, and the data to pull before signing off.

### A treated perimeter's linear-foot total doesn't match the site survey

**Usually means:** a slab, patio, or plumbing penetration was skipped rather than switched to an alternate delivery method — the technician trenched what was easy and left the rest as "inaccessible."

**First question:** "Walk me through every linear foot of this perimeter — which sections were trenched, which were rodded, and which weren't treated at all?"

**Data to pull:** the site survey/sketch against the service ticket's method-per-section entries; total finished-dilution gallons used against the calculated requirement for the full perimeter.

### Finished dilution used is noticeably below the label-rate calculation for the job size

**Usually means:** the technician stretched one container to avoid a second trip or a partial-container order, silently under-dosing the barrier below the labeled minimum.

**First question:** "How many gallons of finished dilution did the calculated job require, and how many were actually mixed and applied?"

**Data to pull:** concentrate purchase/usage log for the job; the dilution math (target %, concentrate % AI, total gallons) reconstructed from the ticket.

### Technician asks to mix above the label's stated maximum percentage

**Usually means:** either a genuine high-pressure site the label's own range doesn't seem to cover (rare — the range usually does), or a belief that "stronger is safer" that ignores the label is a legal ceiling, not a technical suggestion.

**First question:** "What in the site conditions makes you think the labeled maximum isn't enough — and have we ruled out a barrier gap or missed conducive condition first?"

**Data to pull:** the specific label's stated range; prior treatment history at the site; inspection notes on conducive conditions that a rate increase wouldn't actually fix.

### Wind speed field is blank on an exterior application ticket

**Usually means:** the technician didn't check, or checked and didn't want to document a borderline reading — either way, there's no record to defend against a future drift complaint.

**First question:** "What was the wind reading at the time of application, and where's it logged?"

**Data to pull:** the label's stated wind-speed maximum for that application type; any weather-station or handheld anemometer log the crew carries; neighboring-property complaint history for the area.

### Reinfestation callback within the warranty period at the same structure

**Usually means:** either a barrier gap from the original treatment, a conducive condition that was never corrected (moisture, wood-to-soil contact), or — less often — a colony that was never the one originally treated (secondary infestation).

**First question:** "Pull the original treatment record — where was the barrier, what method was used section by section, and has the conducive condition list changed since?"

**Data to pull:** original service ticket and site survey; current inspection findings mapped against the original barrier line; moisture-meter readings then vs. now.

### A repair guarantee was sold at the same visit as a warranty retreatment claim

**Usually means:** a sales conversation got layered onto a claim visit, creating both a conflict-of-interest appearance and a pricing risk — repair guarantees need their own inspection standard, not a bundled add-on during a claim.

**First question:** "Was this repair guarantee underwritten with its own pre-existing-damage inspection, or was it added onto today's retreatment visit?"

**Data to pull:** the contract date and inspection report tied to the repair guarantee specifically; whether a separate pre-existing-damage baseline was documented.

### WDO report calls a section "no visible evidence" but access notes say the area was blocked

**Usually means:** a rushed inspection (often real-estate-closing time pressure) reported a finding for an area that was never actually seen — a fact-misrepresentation risk on a document a lender or buyer relies on.

**First question:** "Was this area physically accessed, or is 'no visible evidence' standing in for 'couldn't get to it'?"

**Data to pull:** the inspector's access notes per structural area; photos or a walkthrough log; the specific NPMA-33 (or state-form) checkbox used for blocked-access areas versus a clean finding.

### PCO's WDO report includes a repair-cost estimate for damage found

**Usually means:** the inspector stepped past the license scope — structural repair scoping is a structural evaluator's call, not a pest control operator's, and a cost estimate on the WDO report can be read as a professional opinion the PCO isn't licensed to give.

**First question:** "Is this a referral to a structural evaluator, or did we put a number on it ourselves?"

**Data to pull:** the referral documentation (if any); the exact wording used on the report for the damage finding; company policy on repair-scope language.

### Bed bug or German cockroach job closed after a single visit

**Usually means:** a scheduling shortcut rather than a treatment decision — bed bug egg incubation and German cockroach resistance/avoidance both commonly outlast one application's residual.

**First question:** "What did the follow-up monitors show, and when's visit two scheduled?"

**Data to pull:** monitor/sticky-trap placement and read dates; whether a follow-up cadence was ever put on the calendar or just implied.

### Applicator's license category doesn't cover the work performed

**Usually means:** either a scheduling error (wrong tech dispatched) or, more seriously, a company routinely running termiticide or fumigation jobs under a general structural license without the specific endorsement — a state lead agency enforcement exposure that surfaces at audit or after a complaint.

**First question:** "What license category is this technician certified under, and does it match termiticide/fumigation work specifically, or only general structural pest control?"

**Data to pull:** technician's license category and renewal/CEU status; the job type performed; state requirements for that specific category.
