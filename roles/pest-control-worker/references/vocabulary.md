# Vocabulary

Terms generalists conflate that practitioners keep sharply separate. Each: definition, how a practitioner actually uses it in a sentence, and the common misuse.

### Misuse vs. off-label

**Misuse** is the legal term: any pesticide use inconsistent with the label (wrong site, wrong pest, wrong rate, skipped PPE), unlawful under FIFRA §12(a)(2)(G). **Off-label** is a colloquial phrase people use as if it describes a gray zone — there isn't one; any use the label doesn't authorize is misuse.

*In use:* "There's no such thing as 'off-label but fine' here — if the label doesn't say this site and this pest, it's misuse, full stop."

*Common misuse:* treating "off-label" as a softer, more defensible category than "misuse" when they describe the same violation.

### REI vs. structural re-entry statement vs. fumigation clearance

**REI (Restricted Entry Interval)** is an agricultural Worker Protection Standard concept (40 CFR Part 170), expressed in hours for crop re-entry. Most **structural/residential labels don't use REI at all** — they carry a re-entry statement instead ("keep people and pets off treated surfaces until dry," sometimes a stated hour window). **Fumigation clearance** is a distinct, stricter standard: for sulfuryl fluoride structural fumigation, re-entry requires a certified fumigator's logged air-concentration reading, commonly at or below 1 ppm, before anyone goes back in.

*In use:* "This isn't an ag job, there's no REI number to quote — check the re-entry statement on this label, and if it's fumigation, we're waiting on a clearance reading, not a clock."

*Common misuse:* quoting a generic "REI" hour figure for a structural job when the applicable label doesn't use that framework at all.

### Retreatment guarantee vs. repair guarantee

A **retreatment guarantee** commits the company to re-treat chemically if reinfestation occurs, with no cap on labor/material for the retreat but no coverage for damage found. A **repair guarantee** commits the company to pay for new structural damage caused by termite activity after treatment — a real cost exposure that has to be underwritten and priced separately, not bundled as a paperwork upsell.

*In use:* "The contract on file is retreatment-only — we can re-treat that pier today, but any joist repair is a separate conversation the customer never bought into."

*Common misuse:* selling or documenting a repair guarantee as if it's included by default, or as a same-visit add-on without its own pre-existing-damage inspection.

### WDO vs. WDI vs. conducive conditions

**WDO (wood-destroying organism)** is the umbrella term covering insects and organisms that damage wood, including fungi. **WDI (wood-destroying insect)** is the narrower insect-only subset (termites, carpenter ants, powderpost beetles) — some state forms use WDI specifically, others use WDO more broadly; check which the jurisdiction's form actually covers. **Conducive conditions** are the environmental factors that invite infestation (wood-to-soil contact, moisture intrusion, cellulose debris) — not damage or activity themselves, but the risk factors for future activity.

*In use:* "The report needs a conducive-conditions line even where there's no active WDI finding — wood-to-soil contact at that porch post is a future-risk disclosure, not a current-damage one."

*Common misuse:* treating "no WDI found" as "no findings to report" and omitting conducive conditions, which many state forms require disclosed regardless of current infestation status.

### Trench-and-treat vs. rod injection vs. bait system

**Trench-and-treat** applies liquid termiticide into an open soil trench along the foundation. **Rod injection** delivers the same finished-rate chemical through drilled holes where a slab or hardscape blocks trenching — same barrier concept, different delivery. A **bait system** (in-ground stations monitored and refilled with a slow-acting toxicant) doesn't build a chemical barrier at all; it works by colony elimination through worker foraging and sharing.

*In use:* "We can't trench the patio side, so that section's rod-injected at the same rate — it's still one continuous barrier, just two delivery methods."

*Common misuse:* assuming bait systems and liquid barriers are interchangeable "termite treatments" with the same failure mode and timeline — bait is colony-elimination over weeks to months; liquid barrier is an immediate physical/chemical block.

### IPM action threshold vs. zero-tolerance

An **action threshold** is the pest-presence level at which control action is economically/practically justified — IPM's inspect-identify-monitor-threshold sequence exists specifically to avoid treating below it. **Zero-tolerance** is a policy choice (common in food-handling or healthcare facilities) that sets the threshold at the first sighting, overriding the normal cost-benefit threshold logic.

*In use:* "This account is zero-tolerance by contract — one German cockroach sighting triggers service, we don't wait for a monitor trend like we would on a standard residential threshold."

*Common misuse:* applying a standard IPM threshold mindset to a zero-tolerance account (under-responding to a single sighting), or applying zero-tolerance urgency to every residential job (over-treating and running up chemical cost where a threshold approach would do).

### ACE vs. BCE credential

**ACE (Associate Certified Entomologist)** is an NPMA-affiliated, experience-plus-exam credential aimed at career pest control operators. **BCE (Board Certified Entomologist)** is the Entomological Society of America's academic/professional credential, requiring an entomology or related science degree plus exam — more common among consulting entomologists than field PCOs.

*In use:* "Bring in a BCE for the species-identification dispute on this commercial account — this isn't a field-tech call anymore."

*Common misuse:* treating the two as interchangeable seniority markers; ACE signals field/operational experience, BCE signals academic entomology credentials, and they answer different kinds of questions.

### PCO (pest control operator)

The traditional industry term for a licensed pest control technician/company, still used in contracts, insurance documents, and state statutes even where day-to-day job titles say "technician" or "specialist."

*In use:* "The insurance policy names the PCO of record, not the individual technician who happened to be on the ticket that day."

*Common misuse:* assuming "PCO" is an outdated synonym safe to ignore in favor of newer job titles — statutes and contracts still use it as the operative legal term.

### Drift

The off-target movement of a pesticide (spray particles or vapor) beyond the intended treatment area during or shortly after application, most often driven by wind, particle size, or volatility — distinct from a label violation, since drift can occur even at a fully label-compliant rate under the wrong weather conditions.

*In use:* "The rate was compliant and logged — the neighbor's drift complaint is about wind conditions at time of application, not the dose."

*Common misuse:* assuming a drift complaint implies misuse; label compliance and drift liability are separate questions, which is exactly why labels carry wind-speed restrictions independent of rate limits.
