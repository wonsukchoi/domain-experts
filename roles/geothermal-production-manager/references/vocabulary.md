# Geothermal production working vocabulary

Terms a production manager uses precisely. Format: definition → how a practitioner says it → common misuse.

**Decline curve** — The modeled or observed trend of a well or field's output decreasing over time, used as the baseline against which actual production is monitored for early signs of accelerating decline.
In use: "Actual decline is running 7%/year against a modeled 3% — that gap is the real early-warning signal, not the absolute output number."
Misuse: monitoring absolute output against a target without comparing the decline rate itself to the modeled curve, missing an accelerating-decline signal that a raw output number alone wouldn't show until much later.

**Sustainable yield** — The extraction rate at or below which a reservoir can maintain output over a long planning horizon without accelerating decline, analogous to sustainable yield concepts in groundwater or fisheries management.
In use: "We're likely above sustainable yield on this field right now — that's the most plausible explanation for the decline-rate gap."
Misuse: treating maximum current output as the right target without checking whether it's above the field's actual sustainable yield, which can produce short-term gains at the cost of accelerated long-term decline.

**Reinjection** — Returning spent geothermal fluid to the reservoir, which helps maintain reservoir pressure and can extend field life when done at the right location, rate, and temperature — a first-order reservoir-management lever, not a disposal formality.
In use: "We're adjusting reinjection location to better support pressure near the production wells showing the fastest decline."
Misuse: treating reinjection as a fixed, one-time-approved compliance parameter rather than an active variable that should be reviewed and adjusted as reservoir understanding evolves.

**Thermal breakthrough** — A reservoir management failure mode where reinjected (cooled) fluid reaches production wells prematurely, cooling the produced fluid and reducing power output — a specific risk of poorly designed reinjection rate, location, or temperature.
In use: "The reinjection well is too close to production well 7 — thermal breakthrough risk is why we're relocating it further away."
Misuse: assuming reinjection is purely beneficial regardless of its specific design; poorly sited or rated reinjection can actively cause thermal breakthrough, undermining the production it was meant to help sustain.

**Induced seismicity** — Seismic activity caused by fluid injection (or extraction) altering subsurface stress, a real operational, safety, and community-relations risk associated with reinjection that requires active monitoring and a defined response protocol.
In use: "Seismic monitoring near the reinjection zone triggered our traffic-light protocol this week — injection rate is being reduced pending review."
Misuse: treating induced seismicity monitoring as a one-time permitting checkbox rather than an ongoing operational responsibility requiring continuous monitoring and a real, activatable response protocol.

**Reservoir-wide pressure management** — Evaluating any single well's production or workover decision against its effect on the shared reservoir pressure system, as opposed to optimizing each well as if it were an independent production unit.
In use: "Increasing draw on well 3 looks good in isolation, but reservoir-wide pressure modeling shows it would reduce output from wells 4 and 5 more than it gains here."
Misuse: making well-by-well production decisions based only on that well's own output potential, missing the shared-resource interaction that can make a locally optimal decision reservoir-wide negative.

**Well workover** — A capital investment to restore or improve an underperforming well's output (re-drilling, re-completion, stimulation), evaluated against its cost per unit of recovered output and against the reservoir model's confidence.
In use: "The workover on wells 8 and 9 is projected to recover about 65% of the gap to field-average output, for $2.4M per well."
Misuse: evaluating a workover purely on its absolute output gain without normalizing to cost-per-unit-output, which can make a smaller, cheaper workover look inferior to a larger one that's actually less capital-efficient.

**Model uncertainty (reservoir model)** — The degree of confidence in a reservoir's projected behavior, generally higher for fields with longer production history and better-characterized geology, and lower for newer or more geologically complex fields — a factor that should directly scale how much capital risk is acceptable.
In use: "This field only has 4 years of production history — that's why we're treating this new-well decision more conservatively than we would on our 20-year field."
Misuse: applying the same capital confidence to a newer, less-characterized field as to a long-producing, well-understood one, understating the real risk of a reservoir model being wrong.

**Traffic-light protocol (seismic monitoring)** — A tiered response system linking observed seismic activity levels to specific, predefined operational actions (e.g., green: continue normal operations; yellow: reduce injection rate; red: suspend injection), used to make induced-seismicity response concrete rather than discretionary in the moment.
In use: "We hit the yellow threshold this week — injection rate is being reduced per protocol, not based on a judgment call made under pressure."
Misuse: having a stated seismic monitoring commitment with no actual predefined thresholds or response actions, leaving the real response to be improvised in the moment rather than following an agreed protocol.

**Field life projection** — An updated estimate of how long a field can sustain economically viable output, revised as new production and decline data accumulate, rather than fixed at the initial assessment made before significant production history existed.
In use: "The updated field life projection, incorporating the last 18 months of faster-than-modeled decline, is meaningfully shorter than the original assessment."
Misuse: relying on the original, pre-production field life estimate indefinitely without updating it as actual decline data accumulates, especially when that data diverges from the original model.
