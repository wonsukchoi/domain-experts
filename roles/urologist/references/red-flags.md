# Red flags — what a urologist notices instantly

Smell tests during a urology clinic visit, consult, or on-call page. Format per flag: signal, what it usually means, first question, what to pull.

**Acute scrotal pain with a TWIST score ≥5, and the plan is "get an ultrasound first."**
Usually means: imaging turnaround is being allowed to consume the salvage window — torsion salvage drops from >90% at <6h to <10% past 24h.
First question: "What time did the pain start, and can I get this patient to the OR faster than I can get a Doppler read?"
Pull: exact symptom-onset time and cremasteric reflex/high-riding testis exam findings — not the ultrasound order status.

**PSA elevated and the plan jumps straight to biopsy without density, velocity, or MRI.**
Usually means: reflexive pattern-matching on a single number instead of separating anatomy (large gland) from disease.
First question: "What's the PSA density, and has this patient had an mpMRI?"
Pull: prior PSA values for velocity, most recent prostate volume (TRUS or MRI), and PI-RADS score if MRI was done.

**Any renal cyst described as "complex" being referred straight to surgery.**
Usually means: Bosniak category is being skipped — "complex" is not a category, and IIF lesions are surveillance, not resection.
First question: "What's the actual Bosniak category, not just the word 'complex' on the read?"
Pull: the CT/MRI report's septation, wall thickness, and enhancement description against the Bosniak criteria table.

**Gross hematuria or microhematuria workup skipped in a patient over 60 with a smoking history.**
Usually means: the risk tier was never calculated — age and smoking alone push a patient into the high-risk tier requiring cystoscopy plus CT urogram.
First question: "What's this patient's actual AUA microhematuria risk tier, not just 'they had blood in their urine once'?"
Pull: age, RBC/HPF count, smoking pack-years, and any prior gross hematuria episodes.

**LUTS patient scheduled for surgery with an AUA-SI never documented and no absolute indication charted.**
Usually means: surgery is being offered before medical therapy was tried, absent one of the actual absolute indications (retention, recurrent UTI, bladder stones, renal insufficiency, recurrent hematuria).
First question: "Has this patient had an adequate trial of alpha-blocker ± 5-ARI, or is there an absolute indication I'm missing?"
Pull: current AUA-SI score, current medication list and duration, and creatinine/renal ultrasound if obstruction is suspected.

**A distal ureteral stone >10mm placed on medical expulsive therapy.**
Usually means: the size threshold for MET was not checked — passage rates above 10mm are low enough that a MET trial mostly just delays definitive treatment.
First question: "What's the actual stone size and location on the CT, not the patient's description of it?"
Pull: stone measurements (axial and craniocaudal) and location from the CT report, plus renal function and any signs of infection.

**Fever plus hydronephrosis on imaging, and the plan is definitive stone treatment (ureteroscopy) same visit.**
Usually means: an infected obstructing stone is being treated as a routine stone case — this is a decompress-first emergency, not a same-setting definitive-treatment case.
First question: "Has this system been decompressed yet, and are cultures pending?"
Pull: temperature curve, WBC, and whether a stent or nephrostomy tube has already been placed.

**Bosniak or RENAL score never calculated before a renal mass goes to the OR.**
Usually means: partial-nephrectomy candidacy was never formally assessed, risking an unnecessary radical nephrectomy on a low-complexity mass.
First question: "What's the RENAL nephrometry score, and has partial nephrectomy actually been ruled out or just not considered?"
Pull: cross-sectional imaging measurements feeding each RENAL component (radius, exophytic %, nearness to collecting system, anterior/posterior, polar location).

**High-grade non-muscle-invasive bladder cancer recurs within 6 months of completed BCG, and the plan is another induction course.**
Usually means: this meets the BCG-unresponsive definition, and the guideline-default next step is a cystectomy discussion, not a repeat of a therapy that already failed.
First question: "Does this actually meet BCG-unresponsive criteria, and has cystectomy been discussed?"
Pull: the BCG administration record (induction + maintenance cycles actually completed) against the timing of recurrent high-grade disease.

**Active surveillance patient for prostate cancer skips the 12-month confirmatory biopsy/MRI.**
Usually means: surveillance is being run as "just check PSA," which is not the protocol — PSA alone misses grade progression that a confirmatory biopsy catches.
First question: "When is the confirmatory MRI/biopsy scheduled, and has it already been missed?"
Pull: date of original biopsy, most recent PSA trend, and whether a 12-month confirmatory study has been completed or scheduled.

**A GDMT-style "meds first" mantra applied to a BPH patient who already had an episode of urinary retention.**
Usually means: the general LUTS heuristic (medical therapy first) is being applied past its exception — retention is an absolute surgical indication regardless of symptom score.
First question: "Was there an actual retention episode, and if so, why is this patient still on watchful waiting?"
Pull: ED/clinic notes documenting the retention event and post-void residual at the time.

**Stone patient with a second episode and no metabolic workup ordered.**
Usually means: each stone is being treated as an isolated event instead of triggering the guideline-indicated recurrence workup.
First question: "Is this actually stone number two, and has a 24-hour urine ever been ordered?"
Pull: prior stone history, stone composition if ever analyzed, and whether dietary counseling was given after the first episode.
