# Vocabulary

Terms of art generalists misuse, with practitioner usage and the common mistake.

### PSA density
PSA divided by prostate volume (ng/mL ÷ cc), used to separate elevation from gland size versus elevation from disease.
**In use:** "His PSA is 5.5 but his gland is 45cc, so density is only 0.12 — that's reassuring, not alarming."
**Common misuse:** Treated as interchangeable with raw PSA, or ignored entirely in favor of the absolute number, when density is what actually changes the biopsy decision.

### Grade Group
The 2014 ISUP simplification of Gleason score into five prognostic groups (GG1 = Gleason ≤6, GG5 = Gleason 9-10), designed to stop patients hearing "Gleason 6 out of 10" and assuming near-maximal severity.
**In use:** "This is Grade Group 2, Gleason 3+4 — the primary pattern is still low-grade, which matters more than the sum."
**Common misuse:** Quoting only the Gleason sum (e.g., "Gleason 7") without specifying 3+4 versus 4+3, which are prognostically different despite the same sum.

### Active surveillance
A structured monitoring protocol (scheduled PSA, DRE, MRI, and repeat biopsy) for low-risk cancer, intended as an active management strategy, not a deferral of care.
**In use:** "He's on active surveillance with a confirmatory biopsy at 12 months, not just 'watching and waiting.'"
**Common misuse:** Conflated with watchful waiting (a passive, symptom-triggered approach used mainly in men with limited life expectancy) — the two have different intent, monitoring intensity, and patient population.

### Watchful waiting
A passive management approach for men with limited life expectancy or significant comorbidity, treating symptoms only if/when they occur, without a structured surveillance protocol.
**In use:** "Given his life expectancy, watchful waiting is more appropriate here than active surveillance's biopsy schedule."
**Common misuse:** Used synonymously with active surveillance in casual conversation, which erases the clinically important distinction in intent and monitoring intensity.

### Medical expulsive therapy (MET)
Use of an alpha-blocker (typically tamsulosin) to relax ureteral smooth muscle and facilitate spontaneous stone passage, appropriate for a defined size/location range.
**In use:** "It's a 7mm distal stone, no infection — let's trial MET for four to six weeks before we talk ureteroscopy."
**Common misuse:** Offered for stones outside the size range where it has a meaningful effect (e.g., >10mm or proximal ureter), which mainly delays definitive treatment rather than avoiding it.

### Bosniak classification
A five-tier system (I, II, IIF, III, IV) for renal cystic lesions on cross-sectional imaging, correlating imaging features with malignancy probability.
**In use:** "It's Bosniak IIF — re-image at six months, not straight to biopsy or resection."
**Common misuse:** Collapsing every non-simple cyst into an undifferentiated "complex cyst" label, skipping the category that actually determines management.

### RENAL nephrometry score
A standardized scoring system (Radius, Exophytic/endophytic, Nearness to collecting system, Anterior/posterior, Location) quantifying renal tumor complexity for surgical planning.
**In use:** "RENAL score is 5 — low complexity, favorable for partial nephrectomy even though it's a 4cm mass."
**Common misuse:** Assuming tumor size alone dictates partial versus radical nephrectomy, when a small but centrally located, high-complexity mass can be harder to preserve than a larger peripheral one.

### BCG-unresponsive
A specific FDA/AUA-defined category: persistent or recurrent high-grade non-muscle-invasive bladder cancer despite adequate BCG therapy (induction plus at least one maintenance cycle, or two induction courses).
**In use:** "This meets BCG-unresponsive criteria — we're having the cystectomy conversation, not repeating induction."
**Common misuse:** Applied loosely to any recurrence after BCG, including recurrences after inadequate BCG dosing, which is a different (and differently managed) problem.

### AUA-SI / IPSS
The American Urological Association Symptom Index (identical instrument to the International Prostate Symptom Score), a 7-question patient-reported severity scale for LUTS.
**In use:** "AUA-SI is 22 — that's severe, and it's up from 14 at his last visit six months ago."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a one-time diagnostic cutoff rather than a longitudinal trend; a stable 15 and a rapidly rising 15 call for different responses.

### TWIST score
A clinical scoring system (testicular swelling, hard testicle, absent cremasteric reflex, nausea/vomiting, high-riding testis) stratifying acute scrotal pain by torsion probability.
**In use:** "TWIST is 6 — we're not waiting on ultrasound, this goes straight to the OR."
**Common misuse:** Used as a reason to defer to imaging at any score, when a high score is specifically meant to override the reflex to image first.

### PI-RADS
Prostate Imaging Reporting and Data System — a 1-5 scale scoring the likelihood a lesion on mpMRI represents clinically significant cancer.
**In use:** "PI-RADS 4 lesion in the right peripheral zone — that's what we're targeting on the fusion biopsy."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a cancer diagnosis itself rather than a pretest-probability tool that determines whether and where to biopsy.

### Detrusor overactivity
A urodynamic finding — involuntary detrusor contractions during bladder filling — distinct from the symptom complex of overactive bladder, which is a clinical diagnosis based on symptoms alone.
**In use:** "Urodynamics confirmed detrusor overactivity, which supports but isn't required to treat his overactive bladder symptoms."
**Common misuse:** Using "overactive bladder" and "detrusor overactivity" interchangeably, when one is a symptom diagnosis and the other a urodynamic finding that doesn't always coexist with it.

### Retrograde ejaculation
Ejaculate entering the bladder instead of exiting the urethra, a common and expected consequence of TURP and similar bladder-outlet procedures, not a complication in the adverse-event sense.
**In use:** "Counsel him up front that retrograde ejaculation is expected after TURP, not a rare side effect to mention only if it happens."
**Common misuse:** Framed to patients as an unlikely complication rather than the near-universal, foreseeable functional tradeoff it actually is for outlet-reducing procedures.

### Stone-free rate
The percentage of patients rendered free of clinically significant residual fragments after a stone procedure, typically reported at a defined post-op interval and residual-fragment size cutoff.
**In use:** "PCNL stone-free rate for a 2.5cm lower-pole stone runs lower than for an equivalent renal-pelvis stone — that affects whether we stage the case."
**Common misuse:** Compared across studies or modalities without checking the residual-fragment size cutoff each one used, which can make outcomes look more or less favorable than they are.
