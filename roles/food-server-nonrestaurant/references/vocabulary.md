# Vocabulary

Terms generalists conflate that nonrestaurant food-service practitioners keep sharply separate. Each: definition, a sentence a practitioner would actually say, and the common misuse.

## Banquet Event Order (BEO) vs. a restaurant ticket

A **BEO** is the event office's controlling document for an entire function — guaranteed headcount, room setup, course sequence, timing, and any dietary flags — issued days in advance. A **restaurant ticket** is generated per-table, per-order, in real time as a guest orders. The BEO fixes the meal before service starts; there's no equivalent real-time order-taking step in banquet or tray-line service.

*Practitioner usage:* "The BEO says entrée service starts at 7:10 — that's not a target, that's what the room was staffed and prepped against."

*Common misuse:* treating the BEO as a rough outline that service can improvise around, the way a server might handle a loosely-worded verbal request at a table.

## Guarantee vs. actual attendance vs. overset

The **guarantee** is the headcount the client submits by a contractual deadline (commonly 72 hours prior), which the kitchen preps against. **Overset** is the small buffer (commonly 3–5%) prepped above the guarantee. **Actual attendance** is who actually shows — it can land below, within, or above the overset, and each case has a different correct response.

*Practitioner usage:* "We're prepped to 315 on a 300 guarantee — if the door count comes in at 310 we're fine, if it comes in at 320 that's a kitchen call, not something we absorb."

*Common misuse:* assuming actual attendance above the guarantee is automatically a problem the service team should quietly solve, rather than an escalation with its own billing and prep consequences.

## Course drop / simultaneous service vs. individual table pacing

A **course drop** is the coordinated, room-wide delivery of a course to every table within a fixed window, called by a captain. **Individual table pacing** — appropriate to à la carte service — times each table independently against that table's own conversation and readiness. Banquet service is judged on the room-wide window, not on any single table's experience.

*Practitioner usage:* "Every station fires on my call — I don't want table 6 finishing their app course five minutes before table 20 even sees theirs."

*Common misuse:* a server with à la carte experience pacing their own station comfortably, producing a wide spread across the room even though no individual plate was late.

## Diet ticket vs. allergen flag vs. escort card

A **diet ticket** (hospital/LTC) carries the full nutrient/texture-modified order for a specific patient's tray. An **allergen flag** marks a specific restricted ingredient, which may sit inside a diet ticket or stand alone on an otherwise standard order. An **escort/place card** is the banquet-room equivalent — a seat-level marker tying a modified plate to a specific guest, without the clinical detail a hospital ticket carries.

*Practitioner usage:* "Seat 17's escort card is flagged gluten — that's the banquet version of a diet ticket, same rule: verify before it leaves the pass."

*Common misuse:* treating an escort-card flag as lower-stakes than a hospital diet ticket because it carries less clinical detail — the accuracy requirement is the same, only the paperwork format differs.

## Two-identifier verification vs. single-glance check

**Two-identifier verification** confirms a tray or plate against two independent identifiers (e.g., name plus room number, or name plus date of birth) at the point of delivery, separate from whatever check happened at assembly. A **single-glance check** — eyeballing a tray number against a cart position — catches far fewer transposition errors and is not a substitute.

*Practitioner usage:* "I confirm name and room number out loud at the door before I uncover the tray — cart position alone isn't a check."

*Common misuse:* treating the assembly-stage ticket match as sufficient and skipping the independent delivery-point check, collapsing two checkpoints into one.

## Service ratio (covers per server) vs. total headcount

The **service ratio** is covers per server, which varies by service style (plated, buffet, reception) — it determines staffing, not the raw headcount alone. Two events with the same headcount can need very different staffing if one is plated and the other is a buffet.

*Practitioner usage:* "Same 300 guests as last week's buffet, but this one's plated — we need double the servers, not the same crew."

*Common misuse:* staffing off total headcount alone without adjusting for service style, producing an understaffed plated room or an overstaffed buffet.

## Retherm cart vs. steam table

A **retherm (mobile heated) cart** holds individually assembled trays at temperature for transport to a patient unit or dining room — each tray is a discrete, already-plated unit. A **steam table** holds bulk, undivided product for on-demand portioning at a line. Tray-line service moves completed trays; a cafeteria line portions from bulk holding.

*Practitioner usage:* "These trays are already built — get them on the retherm cart, don't try to portion off a steam table for this round."

*Common misuse:* assuming any hot-holding equipment is interchangeable, when a retherm cart's job (holding pre-assembled units) is structurally different from a steam table's (holding bulk product for portioning).

## Uplift vs. headcount

**Uplift** (airline/rail catering term) is the number of meals actually loaded onto a specific flight or train service, reconciled against the manifest. **Headcount** is the broader, setting-agnostic term for people expected or served. Uplift carries its own reconciliation process tied to manifest and catering-order deadlines, distinct from a banquet guarantee process.

*Practitioner usage:* "Uplift for tonight's flight is 142 meals against a 148-seat manifest — six passengers pre-selected the alternate service."

*Common misuse:* using "headcount" and "uplift" interchangeably, which obscures that uplift reconciliation follows the caterer's manifest deadlines, not a banquet-style guarantee window.

## Never event vs. routine service error

A **never event** (patient-safety term) is an error category considered preventable and unacceptable regardless of circumstance — a wrong-allergen tray reaching and being consumed by a patient falls in this category in most facilities. A **routine service error** (a cold plate, a late course) is a quality issue, not a safety category, and is handled through normal service recovery rather than an incident report.

*Practitioner usage:* "A wrong-diet tray that actually reaches the patient gets reported as a never event, not logged as a routine service miss."

*Common misuse:* treating all tray or ticket errors as equivalent in severity, when an allergen or therapeutic-diet mismatch that reaches the patient triggers a different reporting and escalation process than a cosmetic or timing miss.

## Action station vs. plated service vs. buffet

An **action station** is a live-cooked or live-finished item (carving, omelet, pasta) staffed separately from the main buffet or plated line, usually with its own short queue and its own staffing ratio. **Plated service** delivers a fully composed plate to each seat. A **buffet** is self-service from set-up stations, replenished rather than delivered. Each has a distinct staffing and timing model.

*Practitioner usage:* "The carving station needs its own attendant on top of the buffet-replenishment count — don't fold it into the general buffet ratio."

*Common misuse:* budgeting an action station's staffing inside the general buffet ratio, understaffing the station that actually has a queue and a live-cook time cost.

## Meal-delivery window vs. cook time

The **meal-delivery window** (hospital/LTC) is the elapsed time a facility allows between tray assembly and patient delivery (commonly 30–45 minutes), tracked separately from how long the item took to cook. A tray can be well within safe holding temperature and still violate the facility's stated delivery window if the delivery round runs long.

*Practitioner usage:* "Temp's fine on this tray, but we're at 38 minutes since assembly — that's past our 30-minute window, log it."

*Common misuse:* treating temperature compliance alone as sufficient, missing that the facility's delivery-window policy is a separate, often stricter, clock.
