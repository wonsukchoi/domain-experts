# Red flags

Smell tests a banquet captain, dietary services supervisor, or dining services manager should catch during the shift, not at the next debrief. Format: what it usually means, the first question to ask, the data to pull.

## 1. Course drop spread wider than 20 minutes across a room sized for a 12–15 minute window

**Usually means:** the room was staffed at too low a server-to-guest ratio for the service style, or the captain staggered the "fire" call by table instead of calling all stations at once.

**First question:** "Was every station fired simultaneously, and what was the actual server-to-cover ratio tonight?"

**Data to pull:** the staffing sheet for the event, the captain's fire-call timestamp, per-station clear times.

## 2. Actual attendance exceeds the guarantee's overset with no kitchen escalation logged

**Usually means:** the door count came in above what the kitchen prepped and it was absorbed silently — by thinning portions, serving cold backups, or making a guest wait — instead of being flagged the moment the count was known.

**First question:** "What time did the door count come in, and what time was the kitchen notified?"

**Data to pull:** the guarantee/overset reconciliation sheet, door-count timestamp, kitchen escalation log (or its absence).

## 3. A tray or plate ticket mismatch caught only at delivery, not at assembly

**Usually means:** the assembly-stage verification step was skipped or rushed, pushing the entire burden of catching diet/allergen errors onto the last checkpoint before the item reaches the patient or guest.

**First question:** "Was this tray's ticket checked against its contents at assembly, or only when it got to the room?"

**Data to pull:** the tray-line verification log, assembly-stage sign-off (or its absence), the specific ticket in question.

## 4. Identity verified once instead of twice on a hospital or long-term-care tray round

**Usually means:** staff are treating the assembly-stage ticket match as sufficient and skipping the independent bedside/room check, which is the checkpoint that catches carts mixed up in transit.

**First question:** "Was this patient's name and room/DOB confirmed against the ticket a second time at delivery, separate from the assembly check?"

**Data to pull:** the tray-line log's two checkpoint columns, delivery-round timestamps, which server ran that round.

## 5. An allergen or diet flag "handled" by removing a garnish or component rather than serving the substituted item

**Usually means:** whoever plated or delivered the item treated the flag as a presentation issue instead of recognizing the allergen or restricted nutrient is in the base, sauce, or cooking medium, not the visible topping.

**First question:** "Is the base item itself the substituted version, or was something just picked off the standard plate?"

**Data to pull:** the substitution batch record for that flag, the standard recipe's allergen/nutrient composition, the specific plate or tray in question.

## 6. Server-to-guest ratio set below the service-style standard (e.g., one server covering 35+ covers on a plated event)

**Usually means:** staffing was booked off a headcount without adjusting for service style, or last-minute call-offs weren't backfilled before doors opened.

**First question:** "What ratio was this room staffed at, and does it match the plated/buffet/reception standard for this size room?"

**Data to pull:** the staffing sheet, the event's service style from the BEO, any last-minute staffing call-offs.

## 7. Hot component at tray assembly or banquet hot-hold reads below 140°F with no corrective action noted

**Usually means:** the hold equipment is undersized or overloaded for the batch, or a temperature check was logged as a formality rather than a gate on release.

**First question:** "What was done when this item read below hold temperature — reworked, or sent anyway?"

**Data to pull:** the tray-line or banquet temperature log, the corrective-action note (or its absence), the hold equipment's rated capacity versus batch volume.

## 8. Meal count reconciliation (banquet, airline, or rail) shows served/uplifted count diverging from the manifest or guarantee with no line-item explanation

**Usually means:** either a legitimate last-minute count change that wasn't logged, or a systemic over/under-catering pattern with a recurring cause (booking process, no-show rate) that's being absorbed shift to shift instead of escalated.

**First question:** "Break the variance down — is this a one-time count change, or has this happened on the last several events/flights?"

**Data to pull:** the reconciliation sheet, the original guarantee or manifest, prior-period variance history for the same route or client.

## 9. The same diet, allergen, or ticket-mismatch error recurs across multiple shifts at the same station

**Usually means:** a training gap or a station-specific process failure (e.g., that station's assembly cart is out of ticket-printer range, or that server hasn't been walked through the two-checkpoint rule) rather than isolated individual mistakes.

**First question:** "How many times has this specific error type happened at this station in the last few weeks?"

**Data to pull:** the incident/error log by station and shift, training records for the staff assigned to that station.

## 10. Escort cards or place-card allergy flags don't match the kitchen's substitution-batch count for the event

**Usually means:** the event office's flagged-guest list and the kitchen's prep count were never reconciled before service — either more flagged guests exist than plates prepared, or the kitchen over-prepped against a stale list.

**First question:** "Does the number of flagged escort cards match the number of substitution plates the kitchen actually built?"

**Data to pull:** the event office's allergy/dietary-flag list, the kitchen's substitution-batch production count, the BEO's stated modified-meal count.
