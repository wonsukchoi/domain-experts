# Vocabulary

Terms generalists conflate that a wholesale/manufacturing sales rep keeps sharply separate. Each: definition, how a practitioner actually uses it in a sentence, and the common misuse to avoid.

## GMROI (Gross Margin Return on Investment)

Gross margin dollars generated per dollar of average inventory investment — margin $ ÷ average inventory cost. It's the buyer's own hurdle metric for whether a SKU earns its shelf/warehouse space, independent of how good the relationship with the vendor is.

**In use:** "This SKU's GMROI is 2.1, and their category hurdle is 3.0 — no amount of relationship saves it from the line review unless we fix margin or turns."

**Common misuse:** treating margin percentage alone as the health signal. A 45%-margin item that turns twice a year can have worse GMROI than a 25%-margin item that turns eight times.

## Chargeback vs. deduction

A **chargeback** is a specific, itemized fee a buyer assesses for a compliance failure (late ASN, mislabeled pallet, missed delivery window). A **deduction** is the broader category — any amount a buyer withholds from remittance, including chargebacks, but also co-op fund true-ups, short-ship claims, and promotional allowances.

**In use:** "The remittance is $1,200 short — $400 of that is a legitimate short-ship deduction, the other $800 is a chargeback we're disputing because the ASN went out on time."

**Common misuse:** using the terms interchangeably when disputing them, which makes it harder to route the dispute to the right internal owner (logistics vs. compliance vs. marketing).

## Dating terms (2/10 net 30)

Shorthand for payment terms: a 2% discount if paid within 10 days, full amount due within 30 days regardless. "Extended dating" pushes the due date further out (e.g., net 60 or net 90), often for seasonal goods ordered ahead of the selling season.

**In use:** "They're asking for extended dating to net 60 on the fall reset order — that's a real ask given the seasonality, not a stall tactic."

**Common misuse:** quoting "net 30" as if it's the same as "2/10 net 30" — the early-pay discount materially changes the effective price and cash-flow timing, and dropping it from the conversation understates what the buyer is actually asking for.

## Slotting fee vs. MDF (Marketing Development Funds) vs. co-op advertising

A **slotting fee** is a one-time or per-listing charge a retailer/distributor assesses to place a new SKU on the shelf or in the catalog. **MDF** is money a vendor provides for the buyer to spend on agreed marketing activities, often with reporting requirements. **Co-op advertising** is a specific, often-contractual subset of MDF tied to advertising placements, usually reimbursed against submitted proof of performance.

**In use:** "The listing fee is a slotting cost, one-time — separate from the annual MDF budget, which they're supposed to report spend against and haven't for two cycles."

**Common misuse:** lumping all three into "marketing budget" in negotiations, which makes it impossible to tell whether a request is for shelf access, a promotional campaign, or an ongoing fund renewal.

## Line review

A periodic (commonly annual or biennial), formal process where a retailer or distributor re-evaluates every vendor and SKU in a category against performance data (GMROI, fill rate, deduction rate) and competitive alternatives, deciding what stays, what's cut, and what's added.

**In use:** "Their line review is in Q3 — we need our GMROI story and forward commitments ready 60 days out, not the week before."

**Common misuse:** treating a line review as a routine check-in rather than the specific, data-driven event it is — showing up with a relationship pitch instead of a scorecard defense is the single most common way a vendor gets cut.

## Manufacturer's rep vs. distributor sales rep vs. broker

A **manufacturer's rep** (or "manufacturer's agent") is an independent contractor selling one or more non-competing lines on commission, typically not taking title to goods. A **distributor sales rep** is an employee of a wholesale distributor, selling that distributor's stocked inventory (often multiple manufacturers' lines) directly to retailers. A **broker** typically works in food/consumer packaged goods, representing manufacturers to retailers/distributors without taking title, often on a smaller commission spread across many accounts.

**In use:** "We use manufacturer's reps in the smaller territories where a dedicated headcount doesn't pencil, and direct distributor reps in the top five markets."

**Common misuse:** calling any outside salesperson a "rep" without specifying which structure — commission rate, who holds inventory risk, and who owns the account relationship differ substantially across the three.

## OTIF (On-Time-In-Full)

The percentage of orders delivered both on the promised date and with the complete quantity ordered — the primary fulfillment scorecard metric most large distributors and retailers grade vendors on, frequently tied directly to chargeback schedules.

**In use:** "OTIF dropped to 91% last quarter against their 95% requirement — that's almost certainly what's behind the sudden pricing pressure, not the price itself."

**Common misuse:** treating "on time" and "in full" as one number when they should be tracked separately — a vendor can be 98% on-time but only 88% in-full, and the two have different root causes and different fixes.

## House account vs. commissioned account

A **house account** is managed directly by the company (often a sales manager or the company itself) rather than by an individual rep, typically because it's large, strategic, or predates the current rep's tenure — the rep may service it but doesn't earn full commission. A **commissioned account** is one the rep owns for compensation purposes.

**In use:** "That national chain is a house account — I handle day-to-day service, but the commission split reflects that corporate owns the relationship."

**Common misuse:** assuming "house account" means the rep has no responsibility for it; service expectations and reporting are usually identical, only the commission structure differs.

## MOQ (Minimum Order Quantity)

The smallest quantity a manufacturer or distributor will accept or ship per order or per SKU, usually set to keep production runs or freight economics viable.

**In use:** "We can't do a one-case order on that SKU — MOQ is a full pallet, 48 cases, because anything smaller than a pallet load blows up the freight cost per unit."

**Common misuse:** treating MOQ as a negotiable courtesy rather than a real cost threshold — waiving it repeatedly for one account without adjusting price quietly erodes margin on every order below the line.

## EDI / ASN

**EDI** (Electronic Data Interchange) is the standardized electronic format for exchanging purchase orders, invoices, and other transactional documents between trading partners. An **ASN** (Advance Shipping Notice) is the EDI document sent before a shipment arrives, detailing contents and expected delivery — many large buyers' chargeback systems trigger automatically on ASN timing or accuracy errors.

**In use:** "Check whether the chargeback traces to a late ASN or an actual short-ship — those go to completely different fixes internally."

**Common misuse:** treating EDI compliance as purely an IT/operations problem the sales rep has no stake in — a pattern of EDI errors on an account shows up in the rep's own margin numbers as chargebacks.

## Rebate vs. discount

A **discount** reduces price at the point of invoice, immediately and unconditionally. A **rebate** is paid after the fact, contingent on meeting a stated condition (usually a volume tier over a period), and is typically structured so it doesn't change the invoiced or list price.

**In use:** "We're not cutting list — we're offering a rebate at the 300,000-unit tier, so the price book stays intact for every other account."

**Common misuse:** treating the two as interchangeable ways to "give a better price." A discount is unconditional and immediately visible to every account that compares notes; a rebate is earned, reversible if the condition isn't met, and doesn't reset the list-price baseline.

## Drop-ship vs. warehouse stock

**Drop-ship** means the manufacturer ships directly to the end customer or retail location, bypassing the distributor's own warehouse. **Warehouse stock** means the distributor holds inventory and fulfills from it. The choice changes who carries inventory risk, who controls delivery timing, and how freight is typically allocated.

**In use:** "They want to move the slow-moving SKUs to drop-ship so it's not tying up their warehouse space — that shifts the freight allowance conversation too."

**Common misuse:** assuming a shift to drop-ship is purely logistical; it usually carries pricing and terms implications (freight allowance, minimum order size, delivery lead time) that need to be renegotiated, not just operationally executed.
