# Red flags

Smell tests a territory rep should catch before a pricing or renewal conversation goes badly. Format for each: what it usually means, the first question to ask, and the data to pull before agreeing to anything.

## 1. Buyer cites a competitor's price without producing the quote

**Usually means:** a negotiating opener, not a documented fact — or a real but non-comparable quote (different MOQ, freight terms, or dating) being compared to your all-in price.

**First question:** "Can you send me that quote so I can compare it line for line — unit price, MOQ, freight terms, and dating?"

**Data to pull:** your own landed cost and freight terms on the account; history of whether this buyer has produced competitive quotes before or just referenced them verbally.

## 2. Deductions/chargebacks exceed ~3% of invoiced revenue in a rolling quarter

**Usually means:** either a genuine compliance gap (EDI errors, mislabeled pallets, late ASNs) or the account using chargebacks as an informal, undocumented price reduction.

**First question:** "Walk me through the last five deductions line by line — which were confirmed errors on our side, and which weren't?"

**Data to pull:** deduction log by reason code and dollar amount over the trailing two quarters; remittance vs. invoice reconciliation; whether the deduction rate correlates with a specific new buyer or category manager.

## 3. New category manager or buyer takes over the account

**Usually means:** a line review is more likely in the next 6–12 months — new buyers are frequently mandated (or simply motivated) to re-justify the existing vendor lineup.

**First question:** "What are your priorities for this category over the next year, and how do you evaluate current vendors against them?"

**Data to pull:** the account's current scorecard on your lines (OTIF, fill rate, GMROI); competitive SKUs the new buyer has recently added elsewhere in the category.

## 4. Order frequency drops while total volume holds

**Usually means:** the account is consolidating orders (often to reduce their own freight/handling cost) rather than reducing demand — but it can also mean they're building a bridge inventory before dropping the line at the next line review.

**First question:** "Has anything changed on your ordering or inventory strategy for this category?"

**Data to pull:** order-frequency trend over 12 months against total unit volume; sell-through data if available, to rule out the account simply sitting on excess stock.

## 5. Payment terms drifting past agreed dating

**Usually means:** either a cash-flow problem on the buyer's side worth flagging to credit before it becomes a write-off risk, or an informal test of how much slack the rep will tolerate before it becomes the new normal.

**First question:** "I noticed the last three payments landed past our agreed 30 days — is something changing on your end I should know about?"

**Data to pull:** days-sales-outstanding trend for the account over 6–12 months; credit department's current risk flag status; whether other vendors have reported the same drift.

## 6. Unsolicited request for extended dating or consignment terms

**Usually means:** a liquidity or inventory-risk concern on the buyer's side they haven't stated directly — consignment in particular shifts inventory risk entirely onto the vendor.

**First question:** "What's driving the request — is this about cash flow, or about not wanting the inventory risk on a new item?"

**Data to pull:** the account's current payment history and credit terms; whether the request is account-wide or tied to a specific new or slow-moving SKU.

## 7. SKU's GMROI falls below the account's stated hurdle rate

**Usually means:** the SKU is a live candidate for cut at the next line review regardless of relationship history — buyers increasingly run this as a mechanical scorecard cut, not a discussion.

**First question:** "What's the GMROI hurdle you're using for this category, and where does this SKU currently sit against it?"

**Data to pull:** margin per unit, inventory turns, and average inventory cost for the SKU on that account specifically (not blended across the whole territory).

## 8. Co-op ad fund / MDF spend requested with no performance reporting

**Usually means:** the fund is being treated as a rebate rather than a marketing investment — money goes out, nothing comes back to justify renewing it next cycle.

**First question:** "What did the last co-op spend actually produce — lift, sell-through, redemption — and can you show me?"

**Data to pull:** prior co-op/MDF spend by initiative with any available performance data; whether the fund renews automatically or requires a report each cycle.

## 9. One account exceeds ~15% of territory revenue

**Usually means:** structural concentration risk — the account has real leverage precisely because losing it would be disproportionately damaging, and sophisticated buyers know it and periodically test it.

**First question:** "If this account left tomorrow, what's the actual revenue and margin gap, and how long would it take to backfill?"

**Data to pull:** territory revenue breakdown by account; pipeline of prospective accounts that could offset a loss; historical pattern of how often this account has tested pricing or threatened alternatives.

## 10. Freight or handling allowances rising without a corresponding price conversation

**Usually means:** the manufacturer is quietly absorbing a cost increase because nobody flagged it as a pricing event — allowances tend to drift upward and never get renegotiated down even after the underlying cost pressure eases.

**First question:** "When did we last review the freight allowance on this account against current carrier rates?"

**Data to pull:** freight allowance history vs. actual freight cost trend over 12–24 months; whether the gap is growing.

## 11. Slotting or new-item listing fees increasing cycle over cycle

**Usually means:** the retailer/distributor is tightening shelf economics generally, or specifically pricing out weaker vendors ahead of a category reset — not necessarily anything specific to your line.

**First question:** "Is this fee increase category-wide or specific to our items?"

**Data to pull:** slotting fee history for this account across the last several new-item launches; whether competitors in the category are reporting the same increase.

## 12. Sales-manager overrides on discount authority becoming routine

**Usually means:** either the rep's pre-cleared authority band is set too low for current market conditions, or the rep has developed a pattern of over-promising in the room and asking forgiveness after.

**First question:** "Looking at the last quarter of override requests — is the authority band wrong, or is the pattern the room-first, ask-later habit?"

**Data to pull:** override request log by account and dollar amount over the last two quarters; whether the requests cluster on Tier A/B accounts (band may genuinely be too tight) or are scattered (habit problem).
