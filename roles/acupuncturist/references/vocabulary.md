# Vocabulary

Working vocabulary that generalists reliably misuse. Format per term: definition, how a practitioner actually uses it, the common misuse.

### Deqi (needle sensation)

- **Definition:** the composite sensation — soreness, numbness/tingling, distention/fullness, heaviness — signaling a needle has engaged the point correctly, distinct from sharp pain.
- **In use:** "No deqi at ST36 after three adjustments — I'll reposition before assuming the point's just non-responsive today."
- **Common misuse:** treating deqi as synonymous with pain, or assuming its absence always means a failed treatment. Some points, and some deficiency-pattern presentations, deliberately use a gentle, non-deqi technique instead.

### Bian zheng vs. bian bing (pattern differentiation vs. disease diagnosis)

- **Definition:** bian zheng is diagnosing the pattern (the functional imbalance); bian bing is diagnosing the disease (the named biomedical condition). TCM treatment follows the pattern.
- **In use:** "Same biomedical diagnosis — migraine — is three different patterns in three different patients; we treat the zheng, not the bing."
- **Common misuse:** treating a named condition ("migraine," "insomnia") as a fixed protocol rather than re-deriving the pattern for each patient.

### Ben/biao (root/branch)

- **Definition:** ben is the underlying imbalance driving a presentation; biao is the acute or presenting symptom sitting on top of it.
- **In use:** "The cough is biao, the Kidney Qi deficiency underneath it is ben — treat the branch acutely, then return to the root."
- **Common misuse:** chasing symptom relief indefinitely without ever addressing the root, or the opposite overcorrection — refusing to touch an acute, disabling branch symptom because "the root comes first."

### Qi stagnation vs. Qi deficiency

- **Definition:** stagnation is qi that isn't moving freely (distention, irritability, pain that moves); deficiency is qi that is depleted (fatigue, weak voice, spontaneous sweating).
- **In use:** "This reads as stagnation, not deficiency — moving points, not tonifying points."
- **Common misuse:** prescribing draining/moving points (LR3, LI4) for a fundamentally deficient patient, which worsens fatigue instead of relieving it.

### Excess (shi) vs. deficiency (xu)

- **Definition:** excess describes a pathogen or blockage that's present and active; deficiency describes a lack of the qi, blood, yin, or yang needed for normal function.
- **In use:** "Don't read 'excess' as 'severe' and 'deficiency' as 'mild' — a deep-seated Yang deficiency can be the more serious, harder-to-shift pattern of the two."
- **Common misuse:** equating excess with severity and deficiency with mildness; some of the most chronic, treatment-resistant presentations are deficiency patterns.

### Cun (proportional unit)

- **Definition:** a body-relative unit of measurement scaled to the patient's own anatomy (e.g., the width of the patient's own thumb), not a fixed ruler distance.
- **In use:** "Three cun proximal to the wrist crease" locates correctly whether the forearm belongs to a petite or a tall patient, because the measurement scales with them."
- **Common misuse:** applying a fixed centimeter value across all patients — a cun on a small-framed patient is physically shorter than a cun on a large-framed one, and point location shifts accordingly.

### Ashi point

- **Definition:** a tender or reactive point located by palpation in the moment, not a fixed-location channel point with a formal name.
- **In use:** "No named point sits here, but there's a clear Ashi point at the muscle belly — needle it directly."
- **Common misuse:** treating "Ashi" as a formal channel-point category, or conflating it with a trigger point without acknowledging that it's defined by palpation in that specific patient, not by anatomical location on a chart.

### Moxibustion

- **Definition:** burning prepared mugwort (Artemisia vulgaris), either directly, on a needle handle, or over a point, to add a specific warming and tonifying therapeutic action.
- **In use:** "Add moxa at Ren4 for this deficient-cold pattern — needling alone doesn't supply the warming tonification this presentation needs."
- **Common misuse:** assuming a heating pad or infrared lamp is functionally interchangeable with moxa's herb-smoke warming action, or using moxa on an excess-heat pattern where it's contraindicated.

### Eight Principles (Ba Gang)

- **Definition:** the Yin/Yang, Interior/Exterior, Cold/Heat, Deficiency/Excess triage scaffold used to make a first-pass read of a presentation.
- **In use:** "Ba Gang gets us to 'deficient, cold, interior' fast — now we go one layer deeper into which organ system."
- **Common misuse:** stopping the differentiation at the Ba Gang level and writing a formula from it directly, which produces a generic result; it's a triage step, not a finished diagnosis.

### Five Elements / Five Phases (Wu Xing)

- **Definition:** a correspondence system (Wood/Fire/Earth/Metal/Water) mapping organs, emotions, seasons, and constitutional types — used as the primary diagnostic lens in some lineages (Five-Element acupuncture) and as a secondary reference in TCM/zang-fu practice.
- **In use:** "In a Five-Element frame this reads as a Metal-constitutional grief presentation; a straight TCM read would call the same picture Lung Qi deficiency."
- **Common misuse:** treating Five-Element and TCM zang-fu terminology as interchangeable — they're related systems with genuinely different point-selection logic, not two names for the same method.

### Dry needling

- **Definition:** insertion of a solid filiform needle directly into a muscle trigger point for musculoskeletal pain relief — technically indistinguishable from trigger-point acupuncture.
- **In use:** "Dry needling is trigger-point acupuncture under newer terminology — the 2003 NCCAOM job-task analysis found about 82% of acupuncturists already use it as standard practice."
- **Common misuse:** both sides of the scope-of-practice dispute misuse the term — one claims it's an entirely separate, non-acupuncture technique to justify a shorter training pathway for other professions, the other dismisses it as "not real acupuncture" — when the underlying technique is the same either way.

### Auricular acupuncture

- **Definition:** treatment via ear-based points (the NADA protocol is a common standardized form, often used in addiction/detox settings) that maps the body onto the ear as its own microsystem, with different selection logic than body-point acupuncture.
- **In use:** "Add the auricular protocol for the detox component — that's a separate microsystem, not just extra body points."
- **Common misuse:** treating ear points as incidental add-ons to a body-point formula rather than a distinct system with its own diagnostic mapping.

### Course of care

- **Definition:** the pre-committed block of visits (commonly 6–8 weekly sessions for a chronic complaint) over which a formula is given a fair trial before being judged as ineffective.
- **In use:** "We're committing to 8 weekly visits before deciding this pattern read was wrong — three sessions isn't enough signal either way."
- **Common misuse:** judging non-response after 1–2 visits (too early to see a slow-responding correct pattern take effect), or the opposite failure — running 15+ unchanged visits without a documented reassessment.
