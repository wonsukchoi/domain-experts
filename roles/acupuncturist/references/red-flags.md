# Red Flags

Smell tests for in-session events, post-treatment reports, and documentation gaps. Format per flag: what it usually means, the first question to ask, the data to pull.

### Sharp, pleuritic chest pain or shortness of breath starting minutes to hours after upper-back/shoulder/supraclavicular needling

- **Usually means:** pneumothorax — GB21 alone accounts for roughly 30% of acupuncture-related pneumothorax cases; ST12 and the upper back Shu points (BL13–BL15) are the other recurring sites.
- **First question:** "Is the pain worse when you breathe in, and where exactly was I needling right before it started?"
- **Data to pull:** oxygen saturation and respiratory rate now; refer immediately for a stat chest X-ray or emergency evaluation — do not "wait and see" on any respiratory symptom following thoracic-region needling.

### New bruising, prolonged bleeding, or oozing at a needle site

- **Usually means:** undisclosed anticoagulant/antiplatelet use, or a herb-drug interaction — danshen combined with warfarin has a documented case of INR climbing above 8.4 with resulting hemothorax.
- **First question:** "What blood thinners, high-dose fish oil, or herbal supplements are you taking that weren't on the intake form?"
- **Data to pull:** full current medication and supplement list; most recent INR or platelet count if the patient is on warfarin or another anticoagulant.

### Dizziness, sweating, or pre-syncope in the first 2–3 minutes of a first-ever treatment

- **Usually means:** vasovagal response — estimated at 0.02%–7% of treatments and concentrated heavily in first-time, needle-anxious, or fasted patients, especially when seated.
- **First question:** "Have you eaten today, and is this your first acupuncture session?"
- **Data to pull:** patient position at symptom onset (seated vs. supine) and time since last meal; move to supine immediately regardless of the answer.

### A presentation that doesn't resolve into any coherent pattern after a full four-examination workup

- **Usually means:** biomedical pathology outside TCM's frame — a mass, autoimmune flare, or structural lesion — rather than an unusually subtle pattern.
- **First question:** "Is there anything here — unexplained weight loss, night sweats, a progressive neurological change — that pattern differentiation genuinely can't account for?"
- **Data to pull:** full symptom timeline, existing referral or imaging history; refer out before continuing to treat empirically.

### Same point prescription unchanged for 4+ consecutive visits with no reassessment note

- **Usually means:** "protocol acupuncture" — a formula that worked once running on autopilot rather than tracking the pattern.
- **First question:** "What changed in the tongue, pulse, or complaint since last visit that justifies keeping this exact formula?"
- **Data to pull:** the Assessment section of the last 4 SOAP notes — if tongue/pulse findings are copy-pasted, the reassessment isn't happening.

### Electroacupuncture requested for a patient with a pacemaker or ICD

- **Usually means:** the request needs a hard stop or a manual-only substitution — electrical current can interfere with device sensing regardless of stated "low" intensity.
- **First question:** "Confirm the device type, and has cardiology cleared any electrical modality specifically?"
- **Data to pull:** device card/model number and a documented cardiology clearance note before any e-stim is used.

### Pregnant patient requesting treatment for a non-obstetric complaint

- **Usually means:** a fork between two failure modes — either the practitioner defaults to a blanket point-exclusion list and withholds an otherwise helpful treatment, or dismisses the exclusions entirely on "thin evidence" grounds.
- **First question:** "What trimester, and for this specific point/technique, is the exclusion an actual known-risk mechanism or a defensive-practice convention?"
- **Data to pull:** gestational age, OB clearance for any high-risk pregnancy, and documented informed consent naming the uncertainty either way.

### Insurance claim denied for "lack of medical necessity" on a case with 12+ prior visits

- **Usually means:** the documentation described sensations and pattern language instead of a trending, quantified functional outcome.
- **First question:** "Does every note in this chart show a quantified outcome measure — pain scale, ROM, disability index — moving toward a stated goal?"
- **Data to pull:** the outcome-measure score from every visit in the denied date range, plotted as a trend line.

### Scope-of-practice dispute framed as "dry needling isn't real acupuncture"

- **Usually means:** a turf argument, not an outcomes argument — a 2003 NCCAOM job-task analysis found roughly 82% of acupuncturists already use trigger-point needling as standard technique.
- **First question:** "Has anyone asked what technique gets this specific patient better, versus who's licensed to bill for which name?"
- **Data to pull:** the state's actual scope-of-practice statute language distinguishing (or not distinguishing) dry needling from acupuncture.

### Redness, warmth, or discharge at a needle site days after treatment

- **Usually means:** a skin-prep or immunocompromise issue far more often than a sterilization failure, given single-use pre-sterilized needles are standard.
- **First question:** "Was the skin at that site already compromised — eczema, a healing wound, a skin condition — before insertion?"
- **Data to pull:** needle lot/sterilization documentation, site photos, and a wound culture if there's active drainage.
