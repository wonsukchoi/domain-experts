### Statute of limitations (assessment)
The time period within which the government must assess additional tax, generally 3 years from filing, extended to 6 years for a substantial (over 25% of gross income) unreported-income understatement, and unlimited for fraud or a non-filed return.
**In use:** "Unreported income here is 16% of gross income — under the 25% threshold, so we're on the standard 3-year clock, not the extended 6-year period."
**Common misuse:** Assuming the 3-year period always applies without checking the 25%-of-gross-income threshold, or assuming an extended period applies without actually calculating the ratio.

### Badges of fraud
Recognized indicators — concealment, implausible or shifting explanations, destroyed or absent records, a pattern of substantial underreporting — used to distinguish civil fraud from ordinary negligence.
**In use:** "No badges of fraud here — records were incomplete but the taxpayer was cooperative and consistent in their explanation."
**Common misuse:** Treating incomplete or messy recordkeeping alone as a badge of fraud, without evidence of concealment or deliberate misrepresentation.

### Civil fraud penalty
A 75% penalty imposed when the government proves, by clear and convincing evidence, that an underpayment was due to fraud.
**In use:** "The evidence here doesn't clear the clear-and-convincing bar for fraud — this stays an accuracy-related matter."
**Common misuse:** Applying the fraud penalty and its proof standard interchangeably with the lower preponderance standard used for an ordinary deficiency.

### Accuracy-related penalty
A 20% penalty applying to negligence or a substantial understatement of tax, using the standard preponderance-of-evidence standard, distinct from and less severe than the civil fraud penalty.
**In use:** "This is a substantial understatement — $36,000 additional income exceeds the 10%-of-correct-tax threshold — so the accuracy-related penalty applies."
**Common misuse:** Defaulting to the accuracy-related penalty (or the fraud penalty) based on the size of the understatement alone, rather than the actual evidence supporting negligence versus fraud.

### Cohan rule
The doctrine permitting an examiner (or court) to estimate a deductible expense when the taxpayer lacks full documentation but credible evidence shows the expense plausibly occurred, rather than disallowing it entirely.
**In use:** "No mileage log exists, but fuel receipts and the taxpayer's occupation support a reasonable estimate under Cohan."
**Common misuse:** Either disallowing an undocumented but plausible expense entirely, or accepting the taxpayer's proposed estimate without independently deriving or bounding it.

### Indirect income reconstruction (bank deposit method, net worth method)
Methods for determining actual income when direct records are unreliable or incomplete, by analyzing bank deposits, asset changes, or other indirect financial evidence.
**In use:** "Bank deposit analysis shows $210,000 in business-related deposits versus $180,000 reported gross receipts."
**Common misuse:** Treating all bank deposits as taxable income without excluding identified non-taxable sources (loans, gifts, transfers between the taxpayer's own accounts).

### Reasonable collection potential (RCP)
The examiner's independent calculation of what the government could realistically collect from a taxpayer, combining asset equity and future income capacity, used to evaluate installment agreements and offers in compromise.
**In use:** "RCP came out higher than the taxpayer's offer — the proposal needs to be revised upward."
**Common misuse:** Accepting a taxpayer's proposed settlement or payment amount without independently calculating RCP to check whether it's actually adequate.

### Collection Due Process (CDP)
The taxpayer's right to notice and a hearing before certain collection actions (liens, levies) proceed, with specific required notices and waiting periods.
**In use:** "The levy can't proceed until the CDP notice's 30-day response window has elapsed."
**Common misuse:** Proceeding with a lien or levy without confirming every required CDP notice was issued and its waiting period fully elapsed, which can invalidate the action procedurally.

### Deficiency
The additional tax the government determines is owed after an examination, calculated from the additional taxable income identified (unreported income plus disallowed deductions) at the applicable tax rate.
**In use:** "The deficiency here is $8,640 before penalties, based on $36,000 in additional taxable income at a 24% marginal rate."
**Common misuse:** Calculating additional tax owed without separating and clearly presenting the components (unreported income, disallowed deductions) that produced the deficiency figure.

### Substantial understatement
A specific threshold (understated tax exceeding the greater of $5,000 or 10% of the correct tax) that triggers the accuracy-related penalty independent of any negligence finding.
**In use:** "This $36,000 additional income produces a substantial understatement on its own, separate from any negligence question."
**Common misuse:** Assuming the substantial-understatement penalty requires proof of negligent behavior, when it can apply based on the size of the understatement alone.
