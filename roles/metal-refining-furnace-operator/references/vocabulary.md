# Vocabulary

### Recovery (alloy addition)
The percentage of an alloying element added to a melt that actually ends up dissolved in the final chemistry, as opposed to being lost to oxidation or slag.
**In use:** "Mn recovery on this furnace runs about 90% — the addition calculation has to account for that, not assume all of it sticks."
**Common misuse:** Assuming 100% recovery — element added equals element retained — rather than accounting for the element- and furnace-specific recovery rate, which is typically well under 100%.

### Melt chemistry / assay
The measured elemental composition of a melt at a given point in the process, typically via spectrometer sample.
**In use:** "Latest assay shows Mn at 0.40% — that's the basis for the addition calc, not last heat's numbers."
**Common misuse:** Treating a chemistry sample as permanently representative of the melt's current state, without accounting for further changes from continued melting or reaction time since the sample was pulled.

### Slag basicity
A measure of a slag's chemical character — basic versus acidic — which governs which refining reactions, like desulfurization, the slag can actually support.
**In use:** "Slag basicity's too low for the desulfurization target here — adjusting lime addition before proceeding."
**Common misuse:** Treating slag as a passive byproduct to be skimmed off, rather than an actively controlled process tool whose chemistry must match the specific refining reaction required.

### Tap temperature / superheat
The melt's temperature above its liquidus point at the time of tapping, which must be controlled within a specific range for the alloy and downstream casting process.
**In use:** "Tap temp's confirmed at target superheat for this alloy — clear to tap, not just 'looks hot enough.'"
**Common misuse:** Judging tap readiness by time-in-furnace or visual appearance rather than a verified actual temperature measurement against the specific target.

### Ferroalloy (e.g. ferromanganese, ferrosilicon)
An alloy of iron with another element, used as the delivery form for adding that element to a melt, distinct from the pure elemental content it delivers.
**In use:** "That's 444 kg of elemental Mn needed — figure the actual ferromanganese tonnage from its own Mn content percentage."
**Common misuse:** Confusing the total ferroalloy tonnage added with the elemental content actually delivered, when a ferroalloy's own composition percentage determines how much of the target element it actually contributes.

### Desulfurization / dephosphorization
Refining reactions that remove sulfur or phosphorus from a melt, each requiring specific slag chemistry and process conditions to proceed effectively.
**In use:** "Dephosphorization needs an oxidizing, basic slag — this slag practice won't support that reaction as-is."
**Common misuse:** Assuming these reactions will occur simply because slag is present and time passes, without verifying the specific slag conditions each reaction actually requires.

### Heat (metallurgy)
A single batch or campaign of metal melted and processed together in a furnace, from charging through tapping.
**In use:** "This heat's charge mix is different from last week's — recalculate the additions, don't reuse the prior heat's numbers."
**Common misuse:** Assuming process parameters — additions, recovery, timing — from one heat automatically transfer to the next heat without accounting for that heat's own actual charge composition and conditions.

### Charge weight
The actual measured weight of metal in the furnace at a given point, used as the basis for calculating percentage-based chemistry adjustments.
**In use:** "Addition calc is based on the actual 50,000 kg charge weight, not a nominal furnace capacity figure."
**Common misuse:** Using an assumed or nominal charge weight rather than the actual measured weight, which produces systematically incorrect addition calculations since percentage targets are relative to actual mass.

### Liquidus
The temperature above which an alloy is fully liquid, distinct from the solidus (below which it's fully solid) — the reference point for defining superheat.
**In use:** "We're above liquidus, but check superheat against the target before tapping — being liquid isn't the same as having enough margin."
**Common misuse:** Treating "hot enough to be liquid" and "hot enough to have adequate superheat for the process" as the same thing, when adequate processing or pouring requires superheat above the liquidus, not just reaching it.

### Furnace practice
The specific, furnace- and process-line-specific operating parameters — recovery rates, addition sequences, slag practices — that may differ from generic published values for a given alloy system.
**In use:** "That recovery figure's from a textbook — check this furnace's own established practice before using it in the calc."
**Common misuse:** Applying generic textbook recovery rates or addition practices rather than this specific furnace's established, calibrated practice, which can differ meaningfully due to furnace design, refractory type, and operating habits.
