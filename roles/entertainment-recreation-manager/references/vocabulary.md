# Working vocabulary

Terms of art practitioners use precisely, that generalists misuse. Load this when writing or reviewing content that needs to sound like an actual entertainment/recreation manager, not a generalist describing one.

### Certified operating limit
The manufacturer- and inspection-established conditions (wind, temperature, rider restrictions) under which an attraction is approved to operate — a compliance/safety boundary, not an operational guideline.
**In use:** "We're at 32 mph sustained, limit is 35 — still operating, but monitoring interval tightens to every 15 minutes per protocol."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a soft guideline that can flex under demand pressure — it's a hard compliance boundary tied to inspection and manufacturer documentation, not a judgment call.

### Virtual queue / timed entry
A capacity management system letting guests reserve a return time for a specific attraction instead of physically waiting in line, or capping total daily entry to manage overall density.
**In use:** "Put the flagship coaster on virtual queue today — that's where wait time is actually driving satisfaction scores down, not the water rides."
**Common misuse:** Applied uniformly across all attractions or not at all — the value concentrates on the specific high-demand attractions where wait time is the actual binding constraint on guest experience.

### Per-cap (per capita spend)
Average secondary spend (food, retail, games) per paying guest, distinct from admission revenue — a key driver of total revenue per visit.
**In use:** "Per-cap is down $3 this month even though attendance is flat — check whether it's a mix shift or a pricing issue in food service."
**Common misuse:** Ignored in favor of attendance-only reporting — per-cap changes can materially affect total revenue independent of attendance trend, and hide or reveal problems attendance numbers alone don't show.

### Theoretical vs. actual throughput
The maximum riders/participants an attraction can process per hour under ideal conditions (theoretical) vs. what it actually processes accounting for loading inefficiencies, ride-operator pace, and downtime (actual).
**In use:** "Theoretical throughput on this coaster is 1,800/hour, but we're actually running 1,400 — that gap is where the queue backs up, not overall demand."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to be the same number — the gap between theoretical and actual throughput is often the actual lever to pull (loading procedure, dispatch interval) rather than needing more capacity or a queue-management system.

### Rain-day / contingency programming
Pre-planned alternative activities or capacity (indoor attractions, extended hours elsewhere, alternate schedule) activated when weather or another disruption affects planned operations.
**In use:** "Contingency programming is only worth building out heavily for our top 15 revenue days — a rained-out Tuesday in the shoulder season isn't worth the same investment."
**Common misuse:** Built as a uniform, one-size plan applied regardless of the specific day's revenue stakes — contingency investment should scale with what's actually at risk that day.

### Supervision ratio
The required or policy-set ratio of trained supervisors/lifeguards/operators to participants or equipment for a given activity, often tied to certification requirements.
**In use:** "Aquatics supervision ratio is fixed at 1:25 regardless of how busy the pool gets — that doesn't flex for a demand surge."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a target to hit on average rather than a floor that applies at every moment — a ratio that's correct on average across a shift but breached during a specific busy hour is still a violation during that hour.

### Demand smoothing (season pass pricing)
Pricing structures (season passes, off-peak discounts) designed to shift demand from peak periods to shoulder/off-peak periods, improving both capacity utilization and revenue predictability.
**In use:** "The pass's blackout-free weekday pricing tier is there specifically for demand smoothing — it's priced against the value of moving visits off our most congested days, not just against the single-day gate price."
**Common misuse:** Evaluated purely on "does this pass pay for itself vs. buying single-day tickets" from the guest's perspective — the operator's side of the value (smoothing peak congestion, predictable revenue) is a separate and often larger part of the pricing rationale.

### Blackout date
A date on which a discounted pass or ticket product is not valid, used to protect peak-demand days from being sold at a discounted rate.
**In use:** "This holiday weekend is a blackout date for the value pass — we're not diluting our highest-demand days with a discounted product."
**Common misuse:** Set once and left static — blackout dates should be reviewed against actual demand data each season, since a date that wasn't historically high-demand can become one (or vice versa) as attendance patterns shift.
