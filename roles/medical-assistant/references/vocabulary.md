# Medical Assistant — Vocabulary

### Standing order
A pre-authorized, physician-signed protocol permitting an MA to perform a specific clinical task under defined conditions without a fresh instruction each time.
**In use:** "The flu-shot standing order covers healthy adults 18+ with no egg allergy — anything outside that needs a direct order."
**Common misuse:** Treated as blanket permission for anything "similar" to what's written, rather than exactly what's written.

### Scope of practice
The legally and organizationally defined boundary of tasks a role is authorized to perform, set by state regulation and the supervising practice's policies.
**In use:** "Drawing blood is in my scope here; interpreting the CBC results isn't."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to be uniform across states or practices, when it varies significantly by both.

### Delegation
The physician's act of authorizing a specific clinical task to be performed by someone else under their supervision and legal responsibility.
**In use:** "The physician delegated EKG administration to MAs via the standing order, but interpretation stays with them."
**Common misuse:** Confused with independent authority — delegation doesn't transfer the underlying decision-making responsibility, only the task execution.

### Escalation threshold
A specific, pre-defined numeric or clinical trigger point at which a finding must be reported immediately rather than noted for later review.
**In use:** "BP crossed the escalation threshold on the first reading, so I notified before finishing the rest of vitals."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a suggestion to use judgment about, rather than a fixed trigger requiring the specified action regardless of how the MA feels about the situation.

### Z-track technique
An intramuscular injection method displacing the skin laterally before needle insertion and releasing it after withdrawal, sealing the injection track to prevent medication leakage/tracking into subcutaneous tissue.
**In use:** "Iron dextran gets Z-tracked — it stains tissue if it leaks back along the needle path."
**Common misuse:** Skipped as an unnecessary extra step for medications where it isn't strictly required, when the habit of using it consistently prevents the error of forgetting it when it matters.

### Objective vs. subjective documentation
The distinction between directly observed/measured findings (objective — "BP 168/104") and patient-reported statements (subjective — "patient states chest tightness").
**In use:** "Chart it as subjective — that's what the patient said, not something I measured."
**Common misuse:** Blended into one undifferentiated narrative, losing the epistemic distinction between "I measured this" and "the patient told me this."

### CLIA-waived test
A point-of-care laboratory test simple enough in method and low enough in risk of erroneous result to be performed without a full clinical-laboratory license, under the Clinical Laboratory Improvement Amendments.
**In use:** "Rapid strep is CLIA-waived, so I can run it here — the throat culture that confirms a negative isn't."
**Common misuse:** Assumed all point-of-care tests are automatically within an MA's authorized scope just because the equipment is present in the office.

### Precordial leads
The six chest-placed EKG electrodes (V1–V6) positioned at specific anatomical landmarks to capture the heart's electrical activity from the horizontal plane.
**In use:** "V1 and V2 go at the 4th intercostal space — get that wrong and the whole precordial series shifts."
**Common misuse:** Placed by approximate visual estimate rather than counting intercostal spaces from a known landmark, producing consistent but silently wrong placement.

### Rooming
The process of bringing a patient back from the waiting area, taking vitals/history, and preparing them for the physician's exam.
**In use:** "Rooming took longer than usual — the BP reading triggered the escalation protocol."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a purely administrative/logistical step rather than the primary clinical-screening opportunity it actually is.

### Chief complaint vs. reason for visit
The chief complaint is the patient's own stated primary concern in their words; the reason for visit is the scheduling-system's category, which may not match if something has changed since booking.
**In use:** "Reason for visit says 'annual physical,' but chief complaint today is chest tightness since this morning — that's a mismatch worth flagging."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to always be the same thing, so a mismatch goes unnoticed rather than documented and escalated.

### Verbal order
A physician's spoken instruction for a specific task, given in real time rather than pre-written into a standing order, typically requiring documentation and often co-signature per practice policy.
**In use:** "That was a verbal order for the extra dose — I documented it with the time and who gave it."
**Common misuse:** Acted on without documentation, leaving no record of the authorization if later questioned.
