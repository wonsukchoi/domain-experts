# Vocabulary

### Ultimate bar passage rate
The percentage of a graduating cohort's bar-takers who pass a bar exam within two years of graduation — the metric ABA Standard 316 actually measures for accreditation.
**In use:** "The class of 2022's ultimate rate closed at 82.0%; that's the compliance figure, not the first-time number from July."
**Common misuse:** Generalists (and panicked committee members) treat the first-time pass rate as if it were the accreditation metric — it's a leading indicator, not the test itself.

### Cold call
Calling on a student without prior notice to answer questions about an assigned case, the hallmark technique of the Socratic/case method.
**In use:** "This week's session is unannounced by design — it's simulating the pressure of an actual client meeting."
**Common misuse:** Treated as synonymous with "the Socratic method" broadly, and defended or attacked as a monolith rather than evaluated session-by-session against a specific learning objective.

### On-call panel
A pre-announced, rotating list of students expected to be prepared to answer questions in a given class session, the common alternative to unannounced cold-calling.
**In use:** "The panel for Thursday is posted — five names, cases 12 through 15."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to be a lesser or "watered-down" version of Socratic teaching rather than a deliberate substitute that preserves the accountability function while removing the unpredictability cost.

### Curve (mandatory grading curve)
A school-set required or strongly recommended grade distribution (commonly a median in the B/B+ range) that individual course grades must be compressed or stretched to fit.
**In use:** "The section's median lands at 3.1 under our curve policy — I adjusted the raw scores to fit it before releasing grades."
**Common misuse:** Discussed as if it were the professor's personal grading philosophy rather than an institutional policy the professor is required to apply.

### Expedite request
A message to higher-ranked law reviews asking them to review a submission faster because an offer already exists from another journal, with a stated decision deadline.
**In use:** "I filed expedites to the top 15 on the list — the deadline in the request is Friday at 5pm."
**Common misuse:** Sent without an actual offer in hand, which most student editors read as unsupported and ignore.

### Exploding offer
A journal acceptance offer with a short, firm deadline (often 24–72 hours) after which it's withdrawn.
**In use:** "It's an exploding offer — I have until Wednesday to decide before it's gone."
**Common misuse:** Confused with an expedite deadline, which is a request the author sets, not an offer's own condition.

### Standard 405(c) faculty (clinical faculty)
Faculty who supervise students on real client matters in a law school clinic, given "a form of security of position reasonably similar to tenure" under ABA Standard 405(c) rather than traditional tenure itself.
**In use:** "Her 405(c) review is due — pull the supervision logs and client outcomes, not a publication list."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to be tenure-track by title, or conversely dismissed as untenured adjunct staff; it's a distinct third category with its own review criteria.

### Standard 405(d) faculty (legal writing faculty)
Faculty who teach legal research, writing, and analysis, historically on shorter renewable contracts with comparatively less security of position than clinical or tenure-track faculty.
**In use:** "The legal writing program's contract-renewal cycle runs every three years under our 405(d) policy."
**Common misuse:** Treated as interchangeable with clinical faculty; the two tracks have different governing subsections and different typical job security.

### 1L
First-year law student, or the first year of law school itself, when the standardized doctrinal curriculum (Contracts, Torts, Civil Procedure, Property, Criminal Law, Constitutional Law, Legal Research and Writing) is taught.
**In use:** "The 1L fall GPA percentile is the strongest single predictor we have for later bar-passage risk."
**Common misuse:** Used loosely to mean "beginner," obscuring that it refers to a specific standardized curricular year with its own accreditation-linked data (entering credentials, first-year attrition, bar-passage correlation).

### IRAC
Issue, Rule, Application, Conclusion — the standard structure for legal analysis taught in first-year courses and used as a grading rubric for exam answers.
**In use:** "The exam rubric awards points issue-by-issue under an IRAC structure, not for overall writing quality."
**Common misuse:** Taught as a rigid template to fill in mechanically rather than as a structure for organizing analysis that still needs actual issue-spotting underneath it.

### NextGen Bar Exam
The National Conference of Bar Examiners' redesigned licensing exam, rolling out starting July 2026 in early-adopter jurisdictions, expanding tested content to include lawyering skills like client counseling alongside doctrinal knowledge.
**In use:** "We're crosswalking the upper-level electives against the NextGen content scope before the first affected cohort hits 3L year."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a future problem to defer, when curriculum-alignment work needs to start with the cohort entering roughly two to three years before the jurisdiction's adoption date.

### SSRN download rank
A ranking of legal scholarship by download count on the Social Science Research Network, used in legal academia as an alternate visibility metric alongside (not instead of) journal placement tier.
**In use:** "It placed in a second-tier journal, but it's top-10 in its SSRN subject category — that's real visibility even without the top-tier placement."
**Common misuse:** Treated as equivalent to peer-reviewed citation counts in other disciplines; it measures downloads, not scholarly vetting, and student-edited law reviews don't run blind peer review at all.

### Expedited review vs. blind peer review
Law review articles are selected by student editors on a rolling basis, often influenced by author placement and expedite offers — not blind expert peer review as used in most other academic disciplines.
**In use:** "Placement here runs on editor judgment and timing, not blind review — that's why simultaneous submission is standard practice, unlike in a peer-reviewed field."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to work like peer review in other disciplines, leading to sequential single-journal submission strategies that cost months for no benefit.
