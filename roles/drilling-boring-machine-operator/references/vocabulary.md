# Vocabulary

### Peck drilling
A drilling cycle that periodically retracts the drill fully or partially out of the hole during the operation, clearing accumulated chips and allowing coolant to reach the cutting tip, used for deep holes where continuous feed would pack chips in the flute.
**In use:** "Six-to-one depth-to-diameter ratio on this hole — that's a peck drilling job, not continuous feed."
**Common misuse:** Skipping peck cycles on a deep hole to save cycle time — the time saved is small relative to the risk of chip packing, heat buildup, and drill breakage, which costs far more in scrapped material and extraction effort than the pecks would have taken.

### Drill wander
The tendency of a drill to deviate from a straight path during drilling, following the path of least resistance rather than the intended centerline, especially in deep or unsupported holes.
**In use:** "Wander's worse at the bottom of this hole than the top — that traces back to how the start was established, not something that develops independently at depth."
**Common misuse:** Treating wander as a problem to fix once it's discovered partway through a hole — wander compounds progressively from its starting point, and correcting it after it's underway is far harder than establishing a straight, well-supported start in the first place.

### Reach-to-diameter ratio (boring)
The ratio between a boring bar's unsupported length (reach) and its diameter, a key factor in how much the bar deflects under cutting load.
**In use:** "This is an eight-to-one reach setup — we're not running the same parameters we'd use on a three-to-one job."
**Common misuse:** Assuming cutting parameters that work on a short-reach boring setup transfer directly to a longer-reach one for the same material — bar deflection increases with reach-to-diameter ratio, and identical parameters on a longer-reach setup risk chatter or taper that wouldn't occur on a shorter, stiffer setup.

### Boring bar deflection
The bending or flexing of a boring bar under cutting load, which increases with reach-to-diameter ratio and can produce a tapered or chattered bore if not accounted for in cutting parameters.
**In use:** "That taper's classic bar deflection — the bar's flexing more at full reach than it does closer to the tool holder."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a bore's dimensional accuracy depends only on the boring bar's own precision, not on how much it flexes under the specific cutting load and reach being used — deflection is a real, calculable factor distinct from the tool's inherent accuracy.

### Through-tool coolant
A coolant delivery system that channels coolant through the drill or boring tool itself to the cutting point, as opposed to external flood coolant sprayed from outside the hole.
**In use:** "Switching to through-tool coolant for this deep hole — flood coolant from outside just isn't reaching the tip anymore at this depth."
**Common misuse:** Relying on general flood coolant for a deep-hole operation without checking whether it's actually reaching the cutting point — external coolant delivery becomes progressively less effective as drilling depth increases, even though the coolant is clearly flowing at the surface.

### Chip evacuation
The removal of material chips from the cutting zone during drilling or boring, critical for heat management and preventing tool binding or breakage.
**In use:** "That drill broke from poor chip evacuation, not a material hardness issue — chips were packing in the flute instead of clearing."
**Common misuse:** Assuming chip evacuation is automatically adequate as long as the tool is cutting — in deep holes especially, chip evacuation requires deliberate process design (peck cycles, coolant delivery), not just correct tool selection.

### Hole start technique
The method used to establish the initial engagement of a drill into the workpiece, aimed at achieving a straight, well-supported beginning to the hole before full-depth drilling proceeds.
**In use:** "Taking extra care on the start here — a good pilot engagement now saves us from wander compounding over the full three inches."
**Common misuse:** Rushing through hole initiation to get to the "real" drilling faster — the start is disproportionately important because any wander introduced there compounds over the entire subsequent depth.

### Bore gauge
A precision measurement tool used to verify actual bore diameter at one or more depths within a hole, used to catch dimensional drift that visual tool inspection wouldn't reveal.
**In use:** "Bore gauge at three depths shows we're tapering — half a thousandth tighter at the bottom than the top."
**Common misuse:** Relying on a single diameter check (often just at the hole opening) to represent the entire bore — taper and other depth-dependent defects require measurement at multiple depths to detect.

### Depth-to-diameter ratio
The relationship between how deep a hole is being drilled and the drill's diameter, used as the primary factor determining whether peck drilling or continuous feed is appropriate.
**In use:** "Six-to-one ratio here — that's well past the three-to-five range where peck drilling becomes necessary."
**Common misuse:** Judging whether peck drilling is needed by absolute hole depth alone rather than the ratio to diameter — a deep hole with a large diameter may not need pecking, while a shallow hole with a very small diameter might, depending on the actual ratio.

### Tool wear (dimensional, non-visual)
Gradual degradation of a cutting tool's actual cutting geometry that affects hole diameter or surface finish without necessarily being visible to the naked eye.
**In use:** "Tool looks sharp, but the last few holes are running undersized — that's wear affecting actual cutting geometry, not something you'd catch just looking at the edge."
**Common misuse:** Relying on visual tool inspection alone to judge whether a drill or boring tool needs replacement — dimensional drift from wear can occur before visible dulling or damage is apparent, which is why periodic hole measurement is a separate, necessary check.
