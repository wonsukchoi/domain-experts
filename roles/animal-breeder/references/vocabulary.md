# Vocabulary

Terms generalists conflate that a genetics-literate breeder keeps sharply separate. Each: definition, a sentence a practitioner would actually say, and the common misuse.

### EPD vs. GE-EPD vs. genomic prediction (GEBV)
**EPD** (Expected Progeny Difference) is a prediction of how an animal's future offspring will differ from another animal's offspring for a given trait, built from pedigree and (when available) progeny records. **GE-EPD** (genomically-enhanced EPD) blends a genomic test result into that same EPD framework, raising accuracy especially for young animals with no progeny yet. A raw **genomic prediction (GEBV)** from the lab alone, before it's blended into the breed association's evaluation, is not the same published number a buyer sees in the catalog.

*Practitioner usage:* "His EPD went from a 0.20-accuracy parent average to a 0.58-accuracy GE-EPD the day we ran the genomic panel — the trait value barely moved, but I trust it a lot more now."

*Common misuse:* calling any DNA-tested animal's number a "genomic EPD" without checking whether the breed association has actually blended the test into the evaluation yet, versus quoting a raw lab genomic score as if it were the published EPD.

### Accuracy vs. reliability
**Accuracy** (the term used by most US beef breed associations, 0 to ~0.99) describes how confident an EPD is, driven by the amount and type of data (pedigree, genomic, progeny) behind it. Some evaluation systems (and other species/countries) report **reliability**, a related but not numerically identical concept, or "possible change" bands instead of an accuracy score. They are not interchangeable units across systems.

*Practitioner usage:* "Don't compare his 0.75 accuracy straight across to a BREEDPLAN possible-change figure — different systems, different math."

*Common misuse:* treating accuracy as if it measures how "good" the trait value is, rather than how confident the estimate is — a high-accuracy EPD can still be a mediocre trait value, correctly and reliably reported.

### Inbreeding coefficient (F) vs. relationship coefficient (a)
The **relationship coefficient** (a) measures how genetically related two animals already are (e.g., full siblings share a = 0.50). The **inbreeding coefficient** (F) is a property of a specific offspring, predicting the probability that offspring inherits two copies of the same ancestral allele: **F = 0.5 × a(sire, dam)** for the proposed mating.

*Practitioner usage:* "The sire and dam have a relationship of 0.25 through their common grandsire, so this calf would come in at F = 12.5% — that's our line."

*Common misuse:* using "relationship" and "inbreeding coefficient" interchangeably, or quoting a herd's average relationship level as if it were the inbreeding coefficient of a specific proposed calf.

### $Value index (e.g., $W, $B, $C) vs. single-trait EPD
A **$Value index** combines multiple EPDs (growth, carcass, maternal, or all three) into one economically-weighted dollar figure representing expected profitability under a defined production scenario. A **single-trait EPD** (marbling, weaning weight, milk) isolates one trait with no weighting against the others.

*Practitioner usage:* "His marbling EPD is the highest in the pen, but his $B index is only middle-of-the-pack once you weight in his carcass weight and yield grade — that's the number I sell on."

*Common misuse:* ranking sires by a single flashy trait EPD when the actual revenue stream (seedstock sale vs. terminal commercial) is better served by the composite index that matches that scenario.

### Fixed-time AI (FTAI) vs. conventional AI (heat detection)
**FTAI** breeds every synchronized female at a predetermined time regardless of observed heat, driven by a hormone protocol (e.g., CO-Synch + CIDR). **Conventional AI** breeds each female individually, timed to her observed standing heat, typically requiring daily heat-detection labor over an extended window.

*Practitioner usage:* "We're running FTAI on the whole group Thursday morning — nobody needs to be out here watching for standing heat all week."

*Common misuse:* assuming FTAI eliminates the need for any heat observation — some FTAI protocols still benefit from heat-detection aids to identify early-cycling cows for a cleanup pass, and conception rates are commonly quoted separately for detected-heat vs. fixed-time animals within the same protocol.

### CIDR vs. GnRH vs. PGF2α
**CIDR** (controlled internal drug release) is a progesterone-releasing intravaginal device that suppresses heat/ovulation while inserted, part of a synchronization protocol, not a hormone injection itself. **GnRH** (gonadotropin-releasing hormone) is injected to trigger ovulation or a new follicular wave at specific protocol days. **PGF2α** (a prostaglandin) is injected to regress the corpus luteum and bring the animal into heat, typically given at CIDR removal.

*Practitioner usage:* "Day 0 is GnRH plus the CIDR insert, day 7 is CIDR pull with the prostaglandin shot, then AI at hour 66 to 72 with the second GnRH."

*Common misuse:* referring to "giving her the CIDR shot" — the CIDR is a physical device inserted and removed, not an injection; conflating it with the GnRH or PGF2α injections given on the same protocol days.

### Effective population size (Ne) vs. census population
**Effective population size (Ne)** is a genetics measure of how many animals are actually contributing genes evenly to the next generation — always smaller than the **census population** (the literal head count) when a small number of sires dominate breeding. FAO livestock-conservation guidance treats Ne below roughly 50 as a short-term inbreeding-depression risk for a breed or population.

*Practitioner usage:* "We've got 40,000 registered cows in the breed, but Ne is a fraction of that because three AI sires cover half the calf crop — that's the number that actually predicts inbreeding drift."

*Common misuse:* citing a large registered-population headcount as evidence a breed's genetic diversity is safe, when heavy popular-sire use can leave the effective population size far smaller.

### Parent average vs. progeny-tested EPD
A **parent average** EPD is calculated purely from the sire's and dam's own EPDs before the animal in question has any genomic test or progeny of its own — the lowest-accuracy starting point. A **progeny-tested EPD** is built from that animal's own recorded offspring performance across multiple herds, the highest-accuracy end state.

*Practitioner usage:* "He's parent-average only right now at 0.15 accuracy — wait for either the genomic panel or his first calf crop before betting heavy semen inventory on him."

*Common misuse:* quoting a bull's parent-average EPD with the same confidence as a progeny-tested herd sire's, especially when marketing a young bull's catalog page.

### Correlated response / genetic antagonism
A **correlated response** is a change in one trait that results from selecting on a genetically linked trait, whether or not anyone selected for it directly. A **genetic antagonism** is a correlated response that works against the breeding goal — the two most cited in cattle are birth weight rising with growth-trait selection (calving-ease cost), and milk EPD rising with mature cow size and maintenance-energy cost.

*Practitioner usage:* "We didn't select for bigger cows, but ten years of chasing milk EPD dragged mature size up right along with it — that's the antagonism showing up in the feed bill."

*Common misuse:* treating an antagonized trait's drift as a mystery or a management failure, instead of the predictable, documented genetic correlation it is.

### Multi-breed genetic evaluation vs. within-breed EPD
A **within-breed EPD** ranks animals only against others of the same breed's evaluation. A **multi-breed genetic evaluation** (e.g., International Genetic Solutions, spanning Red Angus, Simmental, Gelbvieh, and others) adjusts EPDs onto a common scale so animals of different breeds can be compared directly for a composite or crossbred program.

*Practitioner usage:* "Don't compare his Simmental EPD straight to that Angus bull's — pull both off the IGS multi-breed run if you're actually building a crossbred mating plan."

*Common misuse:* comparing raw within-breed EPDs across two different breeds' own evaluations as if the scales were identical, when they are not adjusted onto a common base without the multi-breed run.

### Contemporary group
The set of animals raised together, under the same management, in the same time and location, that an EPD calculation compares against each other before adjusting to breed-wide genetic trends. It is a data-quality concept, not a marketing term.

*Practitioner usage:* "His weaning weight ratio only means something within his contemporary group — comparing it to a calf raised in a different pasture on different creep feed isn't a fair read."

*Common misuse:* comparing raw weights or ratios across animals from different contemporary groups (different years, locations, or management) as if the numbers were directly comparable without the group adjustment built into the EPD.
