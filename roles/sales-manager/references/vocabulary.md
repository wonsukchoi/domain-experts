# Sales management working vocabulary

Terms a sales manager uses precisely. Format: definition → how a practitioner says it → common misuse.

**Pipeline coverage ratio** — Total pipeline value divided by remaining quota, used as the standard leading indicator for whether a rep or team has enough opportunity in the funnel to plausibly hit their number (commonly a 3x-4x standard, varying by close rate and cycle length).
In use: "1.8x coverage against our 3x standard predicts a miss regardless of how well the rep closes what's already in the pipeline."
Misuse: evaluating a rep's likely performance from close rate or activity alone without checking coverage ratio, missing the leading indicator that would have predicted a miss weeks in advance.

**Quota (bottom-up vs. top-down)** — A bottom-up quota is built from actual territory/account potential estimates; a top-down quota is derived from a company revenue target divided across reps, sometimes evenly, regardless of territory differences. Reconciling the two, rather than defaulting to top-down alone, is what makes a quota a fair and achievable target rather than a self-defeating number.
In use: "The top-down number implied $300K per rep, but bottom-up territory potential says this rep's realistic number is closer to $210K given their smaller account base."
Misuse: assigning quota purely top-down (even division of a revenue target) without reconciling against actual territory potential, setting some reps up to fail and others to coast regardless of effort.

**Ramp curve** — The expected trajectory of a new rep's performance from hire date to full productivity, used to set blended, ramp-adjusted targets rather than expecting full-quota performance from month one.
In use: "This rep is in month 2 of a 6-month ramp — their target this quarter reflects that curve, not the full-quota number."
Misuse: holding a new hire to the same quota as a fully ramped rep, creating an unfair comparison and demoralizing the new rep before they've had a realistic chance to reach full productivity.

**Skill/will/fit diagnosis (sales context)** — Applying the general performance-issue triage (can't do it/won't do it/wrong fit) to an underperforming rep specifically: a skill gap (coachable), a will gap (motivation/incentive/expectations issue), or a fit gap (territory or role mismatch unlikely to resolve with coaching).
In use: "This isn't a skill gap — the rep can clearly close deals when they have them. It's a will or territory-fit question given how thin their pipeline has been."
Misuse: defaulting to a skill-gap intervention (coaching, training) for every underperforming rep regardless of whether the actual cause is a pipeline-volume, incentive, or territory-fit issue.

**Forecast categories (commit/best-case/pipeline)** — Defined confidence tiers for forecasted revenue, each requiring specific objective evidence (e.g., mutual action plan, economic buyer engagement) rather than a rep's subjective confidence level.
In use: "This stays in best-case until there's a signed mutual action plan — the evidence bar for commit isn't met yet, regardless of how the rep feels about it."
Misuse: allowing forecast category to reflect a rep's gut-feel optimism rather than the specific, defined evidence threshold each category requires, which erodes the forecast's usefulness as a planning tool.

**Deal-specific coaching** — Coaching tied to a rep's actual, named deal and a specific skill gap observed in it, as distinct from generic sales training or a status-check conversation that doesn't reference real, current opportunities.
In use: "The coaching 1:1 walked through the rep's top deal's actual next step and who the economic buyer is — that's deal-specific, not a generic pipeline review."
Misuse: running the same generic coaching conversation with every rep regardless of their specific deals and skill gaps, which research and practice consistently show doesn't move behavior the way deal-specific coaching does.

**Territory design** — The process of allocating accounts/market potential across reps based on actual capacity and opportunity (account count, market size, historical performance in comparable territories), as distinct from an administratively convenient or purely even split.
In use: "Territory redesign gave this rep 340 target accounts instead of 220 — that's closer to the team average and should show up in pipeline generation capacity."
Misuse: treating territory assignment as a fixed, rarely-revisited administrative decision rather than a lever that should be actively managed and rebalanced as market conditions or account potential change.

**Sandbagging** — A rep deliberately under-forecasting or delaying deal closure to manage expectations or protect against a future quota increase, often a rational response to an unrealistic quota or a punitive forecast-accuracy culture.
In use: "The pattern of deals consistently closing just after quarter-end suggests sandbagging, which is often a symptom of an unrealistic quota, not a rep integrity problem."
Misuse: treating sandbagging purely as an individual behavior problem without checking whether quota design or forecast-culture incentives are what's actually producing the behavior.

**Blended team target** — A team-level quota that accounts for ramp curves and territory variation across individual reps, rather than simply summing identical full-quota expectations across every rep regardless of tenure or territory size.
In use: "The blended team target accounts for two reps still on their ramp curve — it's not just full quota times headcount."
Misuse: setting team targets by multiplying a single full-quota number by headcount without adjusting for new hires' ramp status or territory differences, producing an unrealistic aggregate target.
