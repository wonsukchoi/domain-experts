# Vocabulary

### Rough-in
The phase of work completed before walls, ceilings, or surfaces are closed — conduit or pipe run, framing modified, boxes set — but before finish devices, fixtures, or trim go in.
**In use:** "Rough-in's done on all six runs, ready for him to land wires whenever he's clear."
**Common misuse:** Used loosely to mean "started," when it's actually a defined, often inspectable milestone that frequently requires sign-off before anything gets covered.

### Authorized employee (LOTO)
The OSHA 1910.147 term for the specific person permitted to apply or remove a lockout/tagout device on a given energy source — distinct from an "affected employee," who merely works near the equipment.
**In use:** "I'm not authorized on that panel — get Mike, he's the one who locked it."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a paperwork formality rather than a legal designation that follows a specific person and a specific device, not the crew in general.

### Scope of practice
The legally defined boundary, set by a state licensing board, of what tasks a given trade license permits a person to perform.
**In use:** "Swapping a like-for-like device is inside scope; changing the breaker sizing to fix it isn't."
**Common misuse:** Conflated with "what I personally know how to do" — competence and legal authorization are separate questions, and the second one is the one that matters here.

### OJT hours / hour log
Documented on-the-job-training hours, recorded against specific task categories, that count toward completing a DOL Registered Apprenticeship program.
**In use:** "That's four hours in rough-in wiring methods today, logged before I left the site."
**Common misuse:** Logged loosely or reconstructed from memory at the end of the week, which under- or mis-categorizes hours and can flag an audit of the apprenticeship record.

### Pull list / take-off
The itemized material list derived directly from drawings and specifications before work starts — quantities, sizes, and locations.
**In use:** "Verify the pull list against the panel schedule before you cut a single piece of conduit."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a rough shopping list where "close enough" quantities are acceptable, instead of a commitment checked line-by-line against the drawing.

### Fatal Four
OSHA's four leading causes of construction worker deaths: falls, struck-by, caught-in/between, and electrocution.
**In use:** "Today touches two of the Fatal Four — ladder work in the morning, temporary power in the afternoon."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a slogan from a training-day slideshow rather than a live filter applied to every day's actual task list.

### Competent person
An OSHA-defined role — someone capable of identifying existing and predictable hazards in the surroundings and authorized to take prompt corrective action — required for specific tasks like excavation, scaffolding, and fall protection.
**In use:** "Ask the competent person before that trench goes past four feet."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to just mean "whoever's most experienced on-site," rather than a specific designation tied to the task and, in some cases, formally documented.

### Journeyworker
A tradesperson who has completed an apprenticeship and holds a license to perform the trade's full scope of work independently.
**In use:** "The journeyworker lands every wire in that panel; I stage everything for it."
**Common misuse:** Used interchangeably with "senior helper" — a journeyworker's signature and license carry legal liability a helper's does not, regardless of how experienced the helper is.

### Change order
A formal, priced modification to a contract's scope, typically triggered by an unforeseen field condition discovered mid-job.
**In use:** "That abandoned wiring isn't on the drawings — that's a change order, not a five-minute fix."
**Common misuse:** Skipped because the fix "looks small," which leaves the contractor unpaid for the extra work and the customer blindsided by scope that quietly grew.

### NIOSH RWL (Recommended Weight Limit)
The calculated safe two-handed lift weight for a specific lift's geometry — horizontal distance, vertical height, frequency, and twist — from the Revised NIOSH Lifting Equation, not a single flat number.
**In use:** "That reel's RWL drops once you add the reach and the twist — grab the dolly instead."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a fixed "50-pound rule" instead of a lift-specific calculation that can land well below 50 lb depending on posture and reach.

### Housekeeping (jobsite)
The OSHA 1926.25 requirement that work areas, passages, and stairs remain clear of debris and obstructions continuously during the work, not only at the end of the shift.
**In use:** "Housekeeping's a running task today, not a five-o'clock task."
**Common misuse:** Performed only as an end-of-shift sweep, which leaves hazards live for every hour that actually mattered.

### Ratio (apprentice-to-journeyworker)
The maximum number of apprentices or helpers a single journeyworker may supervise on licensed-adjacent work, set by state licensing rules or apprenticeship program standards.
**In use:** "We're already at ratio — the second helper shadows today, doesn't run wire solo."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a training-program detail irrelevant to daily staffing, when it's actually a legal ceiling on how much unsupervised work can happen at all.

### As-built
Drawings updated to reflect the actual, final field condition of the installed work, as distinct from the original design drawings.
**In use:** "Flag it, photograph it, and we'll update the as-built once the change order's approved."
**Common misuse:** Confused with the original design drawing — the entire point of an as-built is that it's allowed to differ from the design where the field required it.
