# Animal Trainer — Red Flags

### Sudden-onset behavior change (aggression, resource guarding, house-soiling) with no clear trigger
- **Usually means:** Pain, thyroid dysfunction, neurological issue, or sensory decline (vision/hearing loss in older animals) rather than a purely learned behavior.
- **First question:** "When exactly did this start, and was there any change in the animal's health, routine, or environment in the two weeks before?"
- **Data to pull:** Onset date, any recent vet visits, medication changes, and whether the behavior correlates with touch to a specific body area.

### Bite history, especially with no warning signals reported
- **Usually means:** Either the owner is missing the warning signals (lip lick, whale eye, freezing) that preceded the bite, or the animal has learned that warning signals don't work and has skipped straight to biting.
- **First question:** "Walk me through the 30 seconds before the bite, in as much detail as you can — what was the animal doing, what were you doing?"
- **Data to pull:** Bite history log (frequency, context, severity/bite scale), any prior punishment for growling or warning signals.

### Client requests a shock collar, prong collar, or "dominance" correction as a first-line fix
- **Usually means:** The client has been exposed to outdated dominance-theory framing or wants a fast fix without addressing the underlying function.
- **First question:** "What have you already tried, and what happened right after?"
- **Data to pull:** Prior training history and outcomes — a client who's tried aversives before and seen the behavior return is a different conversation than one who hasn't tried anything yet.

### Success rate plateaus below 60% for more than 3 consecutive sessions on the same step
- **Usually means:** The criteria jump between the current and previous step is too large, the reinforcer isn't high-value enough for the distraction level, or there's an unaddressed antecedent (the environment itself is undermining the plan).
- **First question:** "Has anything about the environment or the reinforcer changed since the plateau started?"
- **Data to pull:** Session logs for the plateau period, reinforcer type/value used, and any environmental changes noted.

### Extinction burst mistaken for the plan failing
- **Usually means:** A temporary increase in the intensity/frequency of the unwanted behavior right after starting extinction (withholding the reinforcer) — this is expected and predicts success if the client holds the line, not evidence the plan isn't working.
- **First question:** "Is the behavior getting worse in a burst, or is it staying elevated with no sign of decreasing?"
- **Data to pull:** Frequency/intensity trend over the specific days since extinction started — a burst-then-decline pattern is normal; a sustained plateau is not.

### Public-facing certification request (service dog, therapy animal) with reliability below 95% in a distracting environment
- **Usually means:** The animal isn't ready for public certification yet, regardless of how reliable it is at home.
- **First question:** "What's the success rate specifically in a high-distraction public environment, not at home?"
- **Data to pull:** Session data from public/semi-public locations, not just controlled training sessions.

### Owner reports the animal "knows it did wrong" after an accident (house-soiling, chewing) discovered after the fact
- **Usually means:** The owner is misreading a fear/appeasement response to their own body language or tone as guilt — the animal has no way to associate a punishment delivered after the fact with the earlier behavior.
- **First question:** "Did you catch the behavior in the act, or find it afterward?"
- **Data to pull:** Timing of discovery relative to the behavior, and whether punishment has been delivered after the fact in the past (which predicts a fear/appeasement response, not learning).

### Multi-animal household where a behavior plan for one animal isn't accounting for a second animal's reinforcement history
- **Usually means:** One animal's behavior is being maintained by the other animal's presence or behavior (resource competition, social facilitation of barking), and a single-animal plan will underperform.
- **First question:** "Does this happen when the other animal isn't present?"
- **Data to pull:** Behavior frequency/intensity comparison with each animal present alone vs. together.
