# Animal Trainer — Vocabulary

### LIMA (Least Intrusive, Minimally Aversive)
A hierarchy of intervention choice: rule out medical/environmental causes, then use the least intrusive and least aversive effective procedure before escalating to anything more forceful.
**In use:** "We're LIMA-compliant here — we haven't touched punishment because the antecedent-management-plus-reinforcement approach is still producing progress."
**Common misuse:** Treated as "purely positive, no exceptions" rather than a hierarchy — LIMA doesn't forbid all aversives, it requires exhausting less intrusive options first and justifying escalation.

### Shaping
Building a complex behavior by reinforcing successive approximations toward the final goal, rather than waiting for the full behavior to occur spontaneously.
**In use:** "We're shaping the down-stay in six steps — right now we're only reinforcing four paws on the mat, not the full down yet."
**Common misuse:** Confused with luring (using a treat to physically guide the animal into position) — shaping reinforces the animal's own approximations; luring prompts the behavior externally and has to be faded out separately.

### Extinction
The reduction of a behavior that occurs when a previously reinforced behavior stops being reinforced.
**In use:** "We're using extinction on the jumping — no attention at all when he jumps, consistent, every time."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to work instantly or smoothly — extinction reliably produces an extinction burst first (see below), and inconsistent application (reinforcing the behavior even occasionally) makes it more resistant to extinction, not less.

### Extinction burst
A temporary increase in the frequency, intensity, or duration of a behavior right after reinforcement is withheld, before the behavior decreases.
**In use:** "The barking got louder for two days after we started ignoring it — that's the extinction burst, not the plan failing."
**Common misuse:** Mistaken for evidence the intervention isn't working, causing the client to give in and reinforce the escalated behavior — which teaches the animal that escalating gets results, making the next extinction attempt harder.

### Reinforcement schedule (continuous / fixed-ratio / variable-ratio)
The pattern by which a correct response is reinforced: every time (continuous), every Nth time (fixed-ratio), or on an unpredictable average (variable-ratio).
**In use:** "We're on a variable-ratio-3 now — she doesn't get a treat every sit, but she never knows which one will pay off."
**Common misuse:** Thinning the schedule too early (before the behavior is reliable) or too fast (skipping fixed-ratio and jumping straight to variable), which weakens the behavior instead of strengthening its resistance to extinction.

### Counter-conditioning
Changing an animal's emotional response to a trigger by pairing that trigger with something the animal already finds positive, distinct from simply exposing the animal to the trigger repeatedly.
**In use:** "We're counter-conditioning the vet-clinic doorway — every time we walk past it, high-value treats appear, whether or not he's calm."
**Common misuse:** Conflated with desensitization — desensitization is graduated exposure to reduce reactivity through habituation; counter-conditioning specifically builds a new positive association. The two are usually paired but are mechanically different.

### Functional assessment (ABC — Antecedent-Behavior-Consequence)
Identifying what triggers a behavior (antecedent) and what maintains it (consequence/reinforcer) before designing an intervention.
**In use:** "Before we pick a technique, let's do the ABC — what happens right before he growls, and what happens right after?"
**Common misuse:** Skipped in favor of jumping straight to a technique based on the behavior's label ("he's aggressive, so we need a correction") — the same topography (growling) can have completely different functions requiring different interventions.

### Criteria (in shaping)
The specific, measurable definition of what counts as a successful response at the current step of a shaping plan.
**In use:** "Criteria for step 4 is a sit held for three full seconds before the release cue — anything less doesn't get reinforced."
**Common misuse:** Left vague ("a good sit") rather than operationalized, which makes it impossible to track a success rate or know when to advance.

### Antecedent management
Changing the environment before a behavior occurs so the unwanted behavior can't happen, rather than addressing the behavior after the fact.
**In use:** "Until the recall is reliable, antecedent management means he's on a long line at the park — not off-leash while we train."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a training failure or a permanent workaround rather than a legitimate, often first-line, part of a behavior plan — management buys safety while the underlying behavior change is still in progress.

### Trade-up (in resource guarding)
Offering an item of higher value than the one being guarded in exchange for voluntary release, rather than physically removing the guarded item.
**In use:** "Don't reach for the bone — trade up with the roast chicken and let her choose to drop it."
**Common misuse:** Attempted once or twice and abandoned as "not working" — trade-up needs to be consistent and never followed by taking the item away permanently, or the animal learns that releasing an item means losing access to good things, which worsens guarding.

### Poisoned cue
A cue that has been associated with both reinforcement and punishment/aversive consequences, making the animal's response to it unreliable or hesitant.
**In use:** "Recall is a poisoned cue for this dog — it's been used to call him over for both treats and for nail trims he hates, so now he only sometimes comes."
**Common misuse:** Not recognized as the cause of an "unreliable" behavior — practitioners sometimes retrain the same cue repeatedly without realizing the cue itself carries a mixed history and needs to be replaced, not just re-taught.
