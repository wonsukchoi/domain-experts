# Working vocabulary

Terms of art practitioners use precisely, that generalists misuse. Load this when writing or reviewing content that needs to sound like an actual property/real estate/community association manager, not a generalist describing one.

### Reserve study
A professional analysis projecting the useful life, remaining life, and replacement cost of a community association's major shared components, used to set a funding schedule.
**In use:** "The reserve study says we need $64,583/year to stay fully funded — we're currently at $48,000, that gap needs to go to the board explicitly."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a one-time reference document rather than something to revisit on a cycle — component costs and useful-life estimates drift, and an outdated study can justify funding that's actually inadequate for current replacement costs.

### Percent funded
The ratio of an association's actual reserve balance to the "ideal" balance the reserve study projects it should have at this point in the funding schedule.
**In use:** "We're at 58% funded — that's not a crisis, but it's a trend to watch, not a number to let drift lower."
**Common misuse:** Confused with "funding the annual recommended contribution" — an association can be contributing the recommended annual amount currently while still being underfunded overall due to historical underfunding, and percent funded captures the cumulative position, not just the current year's contribution.

### Vacancy cost
The lost income from a unit sitting unrented, calculated as rent × vacancy duration, used to evaluate whether holding a higher asking rent is actually more profitable than accepting a lower market-clearing rent.
**In use:** "Vacancy cost is already $2,775 and climbing — that needs to be in the conversation before we talk about holding the asking rent."
**Common misuse:** Left out of the rent-setting conversation entirely — decisions about asking rent are often made by comparing rent levels alone, without calculating what continued vacancy at the higher rent actually costs against the alternative.

### Notice period (tenant action)
The legally required advance notice before a specific tenant action (rent increase, non-renewal, eviction filing) can proceed, varying by jurisdiction and action type.
**In use:** "This jurisdiction requires 60 days' notice for this type of non-renewal — we can't file until that's satisfied, regardless of the timeline pressure."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a guideline to expedite under pressure — notice periods are typically a hard legal requirement, and proceeding without satisfying them can invalidate the action entirely, creating far more delay and cost than following the requirement would have.

### Fair housing compliance
Adherence to federal (and applicable state/local) laws prohibiting housing discrimination based on protected classes, governing tenant screening, communication, and action documentation.
**In use:** "Document the reasoning for this denial in fair-housing-compliant terms — tied to objective screening criteria, not subjective impressions."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a background legal concern rather than something actively shaping day-to-day documentation practice — every tenant-facing decision (screening, notice, action) should be made and documented in a way that's defensible against a fair housing inquiry, not just handled informally.

### Total cost of ownership (vendor selection)
The full cost of a vendor relationship over time — including maintenance, failure-response cost, and quality/reliability differences — not just the initial bid or contract price.
**In use:** "The cheaper bid doesn't account for their higher callback rate on prior jobs — once you factor that in, total cost of ownership favors the other vendor."
**Common misuse:** Vendor decisions made on bid price alone, especially for building systems work, missing that a lower upfront cost can be offset (or exceeded) by higher maintenance, failure rate, or liability exposure over the relationship's life.

### Deferred maintenance
Maintenance work postponed past its recommended schedule, treated by an experienced manager as an accruing liability rather than a cost avoided.
**In use:** "That deferred maintenance list isn't a savings — it's a liability that's compounding every month we don't address it."
**Common misuse:** Framed and reported as budget savings ("we came in under maintenance budget") without acknowledging that deferred items typically degrade faster and cost more to eventually fix, especially once they cross from planned to emergency/reactive territory.

### Net operating income (NOI)
Property income after operating expenses but before debt service and capital expenditures — the standard metric for evaluating a property's operating performance independent of financing structure.
**In use:** "The rent reduction actually improves NOI once you factor in the avoided vacancy cost, even though the per-unit rent is lower."
**Common misuse:** Confused with cash flow or profit after all costs — NOI specifically excludes debt service and capital expenditures, which matters when comparing operating decisions (like a rent-setting choice) that shouldn't be conflated with financing or capital-planning decisions.
