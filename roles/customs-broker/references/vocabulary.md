### Harmonized Tariff Schedule (HTS)
The standardized numerical classification system used to determine the duty rate applicable to imported goods, structured under the General Rules of Interpretation.
**In use:** "This falls under HTS 7318.15.80.30 — steel bolts, 6.2% general rate."
**Common misuse:** Picking a heading by loose product description rather than working through the General Rules of Interpretation and checking for an applicable binding ruling.

### General Rules of Interpretation (GRI)
The ordered set of rules (six in total) used to determine the correct HTS classification when a product could plausibly fit more than one heading.
**In use:** "GRI 1 gets us to the four-digit heading; we need GRI 3 to break the tie between these two subheadings."
**Common misuse:** Jumping to a subjective "best fit" heading without working through the rules in order.

### Transaction value
The price actually paid or payable for goods when sold for export to the United States, plus statutorily required additions (assists, royalties, proceeds) — the primary method for determining customs value.
**In use:** "Transaction value here is the $50,000 invoice price, since freight was itemized separately and excluded."
**Common misuse:** Treating the invoice total as automatically equal to transaction value without checking for unstated assists or related-party pricing issues.

### Assist
Something of value (tooling, dies, molds, materials, engineering) that the buyer supplies to the seller free of charge or at reduced cost for use in producing the imported goods — must be added to customs value even if it never appears on the commercial invoice.
**In use:** "The buyer shipped the mold to the factory in China, so its apportioned value is an assist we have to add to the entered value."
**Common misuse:** Assuming customs value only includes what's billed on the invoice, missing assists entirely.

### Substantial transformation
The legal test for determining country of origin when goods are produced or processed in more than one country — origin shifts to the country where the good's name, character, or use is fundamentally changed, not merely where it was last touched.
**In use:** "Assembly in Vietnam didn't substantially transform Chinese-made components, so origin stays China."
**Common misuse:** Assuming the last country of processing or the shipping country is automatically the country of origin.

### Section 301 tariff
An additional duty imposed under Section 301 of the Trade Act of 1974, typically targeting goods from a specific country (most prominently China) by HTS code, layered on top of the general duty rate.
**In use:** "This HTS code is on the List 3 Section 301 tariff at 25%, on top of the 6.2% general rate."
**Common misuse:** Treating Section 301 exposure as static — exclusion and modification lists change by federal notice and must be checked against the current entry date.

### Antidumping/countervailing duty (AD/CVD)
Additional duties imposed on specific products from specific countries found to be sold below fair value (antidumping) or benefiting from foreign government subsidies (countervailing), determined through Commerce Department investigations and scope rulings.
**In use:** "We need a scope ruling to confirm whether this product configuration falls inside the AD order on steel fasteners from China."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a product is inside or outside an AD/CVD order based on its general description rather than checking the order's specific scope language.

### Merchandise Processing Fee (MPF)
A CBP user fee charged on most formal entries, calculated as a percentage of entered value, subject to a minimum and maximum per entry.
**In use:** "MPF on this entry is 0.3464% of value, landing right in the middle of the fee range — no cap or floor applies."
**Common misuse:** Forgetting to check the fee against its current minimum/maximum, especially on very low- or very high-value entries where the flat cap/floor overrides the percentage.

### Harbor Maintenance Fee (HMF)
A fee charged on cargo imported via ocean vessel through U.S. ports, calculated as a percentage of value — not charged on air or land-border shipments.
**In use:** "This is an ocean shipment, so HMF applies in addition to MPF."
**Common misuse:** Applying HMF to air-freight or truck-border entries, where it doesn't apply.

### Importer Security Filing (ISF, "10+2")
A pre-arrival filing required for ocean cargo, due at least 24 hours before the cargo is loaded onto the vessel at the foreign port, containing importer- and carrier-supplied data elements.
**In use:** "ISF needs to go in before the vessel loads tomorrow, or we're looking at a liquidated damages claim."
**Common misuse:** Treating the ISF deadline as tied to vessel arrival in the U.S. rather than loading at the foreign port of export.

### Entry summary (CBP Form 7501)
The formal document filed with CBP declaring classification, value, origin, and calculated duties/fees for an import entry.
**In use:** "The entry summary needs to be filed within the required window after the goods arrive, separate from the ISF filed before loading."
**Common misuse:** Confusing the entry summary's filing deadline with the ISF's pre-loading deadline — they're governed by different timing rules.

### Customs bond
A financial guarantee (continuous, covering a year of activity, or single-entry, covering one shipment) that CBP requires to ensure duties, taxes, and fees will be paid.
**In use:** "Bond sufficiency needs to be recalculated now that Section 301 tariffs doubled this importer's annual duty exposure."
**Common misuse:** Treating a bond amount set years ago as still adequate without recalculating against current duty rates and import volume.

### Protest
The formal mechanism for an importer to dispute CBP's classification, valuation, or duty assessment on a liquidated entry, which must be filed within 180 days of the liquidation date.
**In use:** "Liquidation happened six weeks ago — we've got about 130 days left to file the protest on this overpayment."
**Common misuse:** Tracking the 180 days from the entry or payment date instead of the actual liquidation date, which can be well after entry.

### Duty drawback
A refund of duties paid on imported goods that are subsequently exported or destroyed, or used to manufacture an exported product — requires documented traceability back to the original import entry.
**In use:** "We can claim drawback on these components since they were re-exported within the required window, but only if we can trace the lots back to specific entries."
**Common misuse:** Filing a drawback claim without the underlying lot-tracking or accounting-method documentation actually linking imports to the qualifying export.
