# Prosthodontist — Vocabulary

### Osseointegration
The direct structural and functional connection between living bone and the surface of a load-bearing implant, without intervening soft tissue.
**In use:** "We're seeing good osseointegration on the six-month follow-up — no radiolucency around the fixture and ISQ has come up from 62 to 74."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a binary (integrated or not) rather than a spectrum measurable over time via ISQ trend and radiographic bone level — a "successful" implant at month one can still fail at month eighteen if integration was marginal.

### Primary stability
The mechanical, friction-based stability of an implant fixture immediately after placement, before biological osseointegration has occurred.
**In use:** "Primary stability was excellent at 74 ISQ, so we went ahead with immediate provisionalization."
**Common misuse:** Confused with osseointegration itself — primary stability is mechanical and present at placement; osseointegration is biological and develops over weeks to months. High primary stability doesn't guarantee successful osseointegration.

### Occlusal vertical dimension (OVD)
The vertical distance between two reference points (typically nose and chin) when the teeth are in maximum intercuspation, i.e., how "tall" the bite is.
**In use:** "This case needs about 4mm of OVD increase to restore the collapsed bite — we'll trial that in the provisional before finalizing."
**Common misuse:** Treated as freely adjustable based on what looks esthetically complete, without accounting for the patient's neuromuscular adaptation limit.

### Freeway space (interocclusal rest space)
The gap between the upper and lower teeth when the jaw is at physiologic rest, normally 2-4mm.
**In use:** "Freeway space measured 2mm, which is tight — we don't have much room to increase OVD without encroaching on rest position."
**Common misuse:** Skipped entirely in OVD planning, leading to an increase that eliminates the patient's rest space and causes chronic muscle fatigue.

### Kennedy classification
A system (Classes I-IV) for categorizing partially edentulous arches by the location of missing-tooth spaces relative to remaining teeth, used to guide removable partial denture design.
**In use:** "This is a Kennedy Class II — free-end saddle on the left, tooth-bordered space on the right — so the retainer design needs to handle both load patterns."
**Common misuse:** Used as a diagnosis rather than a design-input classification — knowing the class doesn't by itself determine the clasp design; bone/periodontal status at each abutment still has to be assessed.

### Cantilever (prosthodontic)
A pontic (artificial tooth) extended beyond the last supporting abutment or implant, unsupported on one end.
**In use:** "A single-tooth cantilever off two abutments in light occlusion is within documented safe limits — this isn't automatically a problem."
**Common misuse:** Treated as either always safe or always forbidden, rather than a load-dependent design choice requiring explicit justification tied to occlusal force and cantilever length.

### Misch bone density classification (D1-D4)
A four-category system (D1 dense cortical through D4 fine trabecular with thin/absent cortical plate) describing bone quality at a potential implant site, distinct from bone quantity (height/width).
**In use:** "Quantity looks fine on the CBCT, but this is D3-D4 bone — primary stability is going to be the limiting factor, not the measurements."
**Common misuse:** Conflated with bone quantity — a site can have ample height and width (quantity) while still being soft, poorly trabeculated bone (quality), and the two require separate assessment.

### Resonance-frequency analysis (ISQ)
An objective, device-measured value (Implant Stability Quotient, scale 1-100) of an implant's mechanical stability, used to inform loading timeline decisions.
**In use:** "ISQ came back at 58, below our threshold for immediate loading — we're delaying restoration to twelve weeks."
**Common misuse:** Skipped in favor of a purely time-based loading protocol ("standard is 3 months") without measuring the actual stability of that specific fixture at that specific site.

### Flexural strength (ceramic materials)
A material property describing a ceramic's resistance to bending/fracture under load, expressed in megapascals (MPa), used to guide material selection by tooth position and occlusal force.
**In use:** "Monolithic zirconia's flexural strength puts it well above what a molar under bruxism load needs — lithium disilicate would be undersized here."
**Common misuse:** Treated as the only selection criterion, ignoring that higher-strength materials (zirconia) can accelerate wear on opposing natural dentition if occlusion/bruxism isn't separately managed.

### Provisional restoration
A temporary crown, bridge, or removable prosthesis used to test fit, function, and (for OVD changes) patient tolerance before final fabrication.
**In use:** "We're keeping her in the provisional for another two weeks — she's still reporting some morning jaw soreness at the new OVD."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a purely cosmetic placeholder rather than a diagnostic trial phase that can and should change the final treatment plan if the patient doesn't adapt well.

### All-on-4 / full-arch implant-supported fixed prosthesis
A design using four (or more) strategically placed implants to support a full-arch fixed prosthesis, often angling posterior implants to avoid anatomical structures (sinus, nerve) and maximize anterior-posterior spread.
**In use:** "Given her bone loss pattern, an All-on-4 with tilted posterior implants avoids the sinus lift she'd otherwise need."
**Common misuse:** Presented to patients as a universal solution for full-arch edentulism regardless of bone volume, when adequate anterior bone and correct implant angulation are still prerequisites, not guaranteed.

### Dehiscence (implant)
Exposure of the implant surface through the buccal or lingual bone plate due to insufficient bone width at placement.
**In use:** "The width measurement was right at the floor — we grafted preemptively rather than risk a dehiscence six months out."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to be immediately visible/symptomatic at placement, when it can develop gradually as thin residual bone resorbs post-placement.
