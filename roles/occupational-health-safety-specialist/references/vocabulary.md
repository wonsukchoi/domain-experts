# Occupational Health and Safety Specialist — Vocabulary

### PEL (Permissible Exposure Limit)
The OSHA legal exposure limit for an air contaminant or physical agent (e.g., noise), typically expressed as an 8-hour time-weighted average.
**In use:** "TWA came back at 93.2 dBA — that's above the 90 dBA PEL, so this is a mandatory-control situation, not just a hearing-conservation trigger."
**Common misuse:** treating the PEL as a safe-exposure target rather than a legal floor — many PELs are decades old and less protective than current NIOSH/ACGIH guidance.

### Action level
The exposure threshold (often 50% of the PEL) that triggers monitoring and program requirements before the PEL itself is reached.
**In use:** "We're at the action level for lead, so periodic monitoring and medical surveillance kick in now, before we're anywhere near an actual exceedance."
**Common misuse:** confusing the action level with the PEL — being "under the action level" is a compliance win; being "under the PEL but over the action level" still requires an active monitoring program.

### TRIR (Total Recordable Incident Rate)
Recordable incidents × 200,000, divided by total hours worked — normalized to represent the rate per 100 full-time workers per year.
**In use:** "TRIR is 4.58 against a 3.0 industry benchmark — we're above where we should be, but the DART breakdown matters more than the headline number."
**Common misuse:** treating TRIR alone as a complete safety picture — it says nothing about severity; a facility with all minor strains and one with an amputation can post the same TRIR.

### DART rate (Days Away, Restricted, or Transferred)
The subset of TRIR representing incidents severe enough to cause lost time, work restriction, or job transfer — a severity indicator.
**In use:** "DART is 43% of our recordables this year, up from 30% last year — frequency is flat but severity is climbing."
**Common misuse:** ignoring DART and reporting TRIR alone, which can mask a worsening-severity trend behind a stable frequency number.

### Hierarchy of controls
The ranked order of hazard-control effectiveness: elimination, substitution, engineering controls, administrative controls, PPE — from most to least reliable.
**In use:** "PPE is on the table as the interim measure, but the recommendation leads with the engineering control per the hierarchy."
**Common misuse:** treating all five levels as interchangeable options to be chosen by cost alone, rather than a ranked default that requires justification to skip downward.

### JSA (Job Safety Analysis) / JHA (Job Hazard Analysis)
A task broken into steps, with the hazard and control identified for each step, before the work is performed.
**In use:** "The JSA didn't specify a tie-off point before the task started — that's the actual root cause, not that the harness wasn't worn."
**Common misuse:** writing a generic JSA once and reusing it unchanged for years without revisiting it after a process or equipment change.

### LOTO (Lockout/Tagout)
The procedure for isolating and de-energizing equipment's hazardous energy sources before service or maintenance work.
**In use:** "This task needs a task-specific LOTO procedure, not a general one — the stored hydraulic pressure isn't covered by the electrical lockout alone."
**Common misuse:** assuming a single lockout point (e.g., the main electrical disconnect) covers all hazardous energy, missing stored pressure, gravity, or capacitive energy sources.

### Standard Threshold Shift (STS)
A defined, statistically significant change in an employee's hearing threshold compared to their baseline audiogram, per OSHA's noise standard.
**In use:** "The STS on the annual audiogram is what triggered the re-sampling — the exposure itself may have predated the finding by years."
**Common misuse:** treating an STS as proof of current-year overexposure rather than cumulative exposure that may have built up over a longer, unmonitored period.

### Root-cause analysis (5-Why / fault tree)
A structured method for tracing an incident past its proximate (immediate) cause to the underlying system, process, or design factor.
**In use:** "Five whys got us from 'wasn't wearing the harness' to 'no tie-off point specified before task start' — that's the fixable cause."
**Common misuse:** stopping at the first "why" — usually a human-error answer — and calling it root cause, which produces a corrective action that doesn't prevent recurrence.

### Feasible (in OSHA compliance context)
A legal standard — a control is "feasible" if it is achievable using existing technology and is economically practicable for the industry, not merely convenient.
**In use:** "Engineering controls are feasible here — a $9,500, two-week fix isn't an undue burden for a facility this size."
**Common misuse:** using "not feasible" informally to mean "we'd rather not," without the technological/economic analysis that the term legally requires.

### Recordable (OSHA)
An injury or illness meeting OSHA's criteria for logging on the 300 log — death, days away from work, restricted work, transfer, medical treatment beyond first aid, loss of consciousness, or a significant diagnosed condition.
**In use:** "This is recordable — it went beyond first aid once they needed prescription-strength medication, even though no days were missed."
**Common misuse:** assuming only lost-time injuries are recordable; the OSHA recordable threshold is lower than "days away from work."

### Permit-required confined space
A confined space with one or more hazards (atmospheric, engulfment, entrapment) that requires a written permit system before entry, distinct from a non-permit confined space.
**In use:** "This tank is permit-required — we need atmospheric testing on file before anyone goes in, every time, not just the first time."
**Common misuse:** treating permit completion as a paperwork formality rather than requiring genuinely current atmospheric readings for each entry.

### Leading vs. lagging indicator
Lagging indicators (injury/incident rates) measure outcomes after the fact; leading indicators (JSA completion rate, near-miss reports, corrective-action closure time) measure the process conditions that predict future outcomes.
**In use:** "TRIR is our lagging number, but near-miss reporting rate is the leading indicator we actually manage week to week."
**Common misuse:** managing a safety program exclusively by the lagging indicator, which only shows a problem after someone is already hurt.
