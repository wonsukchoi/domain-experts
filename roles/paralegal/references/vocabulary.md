# Working vocabulary — terms a generalist misuses

US civil litigation usage. Format per entry: definition, then a sentence a practitioner would actually say, then the common misuse.

---

**Privilege log vs. categorical (metadata-plus-topic) log.**
A privilege log lists documents withheld or redacted on a privilege claim in enough detail for the other side to assess the claim. A categorical log groups those entries by subject and custodian with shared metadata instead of a unique narrative per document — a format the Sedona Conference endorses for large productions.
**In use:** "This collection is too large for document-by-document logging — we're going categorical, grouped by custodian and subject."
**Common misuse:** treating "categorical" as a shortcut that skips real analysis. The categories still have to reflect a genuine, defensible grouping — a category so broad it obscures what's actually withheld invites the same waiver risk as no log at all.

**Bates number vs. exhibit number.**
A Bates number is the sequential identifier stamped on every produced page, tied to the custodian and production volume. An exhibit number is assigned later, for a specific proceeding, to a document being offered into evidence — the same page can carry both, and they serve different functions.
**In use:** "Exhibit 4 is ACME000512 — pull it by the Bates range if you need the surrounding pages, not just the marked page."
**Common misuse:** using the two interchangeably in a filing, which makes the record ambiguous about which numbering system a cross-reference is using.

**Discovery vs. initial disclosure.**
Discovery is the request-and-response process (interrogatories, requests for production, depositions). Initial disclosures under FRCP 26(a) are the categories of information a party must produce automatically, without waiting for a request.
**In use:** "The witness list issue is a disclosure gap, not a discovery dispute — they never served it, we never had to request it."
**Common misuse:** calling every information-exchange obligation "discovery," which blurs the deadline and remedy — a disclosure failure and a discovery-response failure are enforced differently.

**Interrogatory vs. request for production vs. request for admission.**
An interrogatory asks a party to answer a written question under oath. A request for production asks a party to produce documents or things. A request for admission asks a party to admit or deny a specific statement, and an unanswered RFA can be deemed admitted.
**In use:** "Don't let the RFA responses slide past the deadline — silence on an RFA gets treated as an admission, unlike an interrogatory."
**Common misuse:** treating all three discovery devices as interchangeable "discovery requests" with the same consequence for a missed deadline — the RFA's default-admission consequence is materially harsher.

**Meet and confer.**
A required, often court-mandated conference between counsel to attempt resolving a discovery or procedural dispute before either side files a motion.
**In use:** "We can't file the motion to compel yet — the meet-and-confer log needs to show a genuine attempt to resolve this first."
**Common misuse:** treating a single perfunctory email as satisfying the requirement; courts increasingly expect a documented, substantive attempt, not a box-checking exercise.

**Technology-assisted review (TAR) vs. keyword search.**
TAR (predictive coding) uses a trained model, validated against a sample, to rank or classify documents by relevance. Keyword search filters by literal term matches with no learning or validation step.
**In use:** "Keyword search over-collects on this custodian's slang-heavy chat data — we're running TAR with a validated seed set instead."
**Common misuse:** assuming TAR output needs no further QC because the technology is "smart" — TAR still requires a documented seed set and elusion testing to be defensible if challenged.

**Redaction vs. withholding.**
Redaction produces a document with specific privileged or protected portions blacked out, with the rest disclosed. Withholding produces the whole document from the log with nothing disclosed.
**In use:** "That email is 90% business scheduling and 10% privileged legal advice — redact the one paragraph, don't withhold the whole thing."
**Common misuse:** over-withholding an entire document because one line is privileged, when redaction of the specific privileged content is both the correct and the more defensible approach.

**Verification (of a discovery response).**
A signed statement, usually by the client under oath, attesting that the response is true and complete to the client's knowledge — required for interrogatory answers in most jurisdictions, not for document productions generally.
**In use:** "The interrogatory answers can't go out until the client signs the verification — that's separate from the attorney's signature on the response itself."
**Common misuse:** conflating attorney sign-off with client verification; they're different signatures serving different legal purposes, and only the client's verification is under oath as to the facts.

**Subpoena duces tecum vs. subpoena ad testificandum.**
A subpoena duces tecum commands production of documents or things; a subpoena ad testificandum commands a person's testimony. Many subpoenas combine both.
**In use:** "The records custodian only needs to respond to the duces tecum portion — we're not deposing her, just requesting the file."
**Common misuse:** assuming a document subpoena automatically compels an appearance, or vice versa — the two obligations are independent and can be served separately.

**Docket vs. calendar vs. tickler.**
The docket is the court's official record of filings and deadlines for a case. A calendar is the firm's internal scheduling system reflecting those (and other) dates. A tickler is a redundant, often personal, follow-up reminder system layered on top of both.
**In use:** "Check the docket directly before relying on our calendar entry — a clerk's amended scheduling order doesn't always sync automatically."
**Common misuse:** treating the firm's calendar as equivalent to the court's docket; the calendar is a copy, and copies drift out of sync with the source.

**Realization rate vs. utilization rate vs. billable rate.**
Billable rate is the hourly rate charged. Utilization rate is the share of available working hours actually billed. Realization rate is the share of billed time actually collected after write-offs, discounts, and fee disputes.
**In use:** "Our utilization is fine — the problem is realization; too many entries are getting written down at bill review because they're too vague to justify."
**Common misuse:** treating a strong billable rate or high utilization as proof of financial health when a weak realization rate means the firm isn't collecting on the hours actually worked.

**Certified copy vs. true copy vs. exemplified copy.**
A certified copy carries an official attestation (often from a court clerk or notary) that it matches the original. A true copy is an informal representation of accuracy with no official attestation. An exemplified copy is a certified copy with an additional layer of authentication for use in another jurisdiction.
**In use:** "The out-of-state court wants an exemplified copy of the judgment, not just certified — that means an extra authentication step from the clerk."
**Common misuse:** assuming "certified" and "true" copies are interchangeable, or that a certified copy alone satisfies a receiving jurisdiction's exemplification requirement.

**Work product doctrine vs. attorney-client privilege.**
Attorney-client privilege protects confidential communications between attorney and client made for the purpose of legal advice. Work product protects materials prepared in anticipation of litigation, regardless of whether an attorney is a party to the communication — and it can attach to a paralegal's or investigator's own analysis.
**In use:** "The damages spreadsheet the paralegal built for trial prep is work product even though no attorney is on the email chain — it was prepared in anticipation of litigation."
**Common misuse:** assuming every litigation-related document needs an attorney's direct involvement to be protected — work product's trigger is anticipation of litigation, not attorney authorship.

**Short form vs. `id.` vs. `supra` (Bluebook).**
A short form is an abbreviated citation to an authority already cited in full. `Id.` refers to the immediately preceding authority with no intervening citation. `Supra` refers back to an earlier full citation across a gap, used for secondary sources and a few other categories, not for cases.
**In use:** "There's an intervening cite between these two references to the same case — `id.` doesn't work here, use the short form instead."
**Common misuse:** using `id.` across an intervening citation to a different source, which silently points the reader to the wrong authority; also using `supra` for a case citation, which the Bluebook reserves for a narrower set of source types.
