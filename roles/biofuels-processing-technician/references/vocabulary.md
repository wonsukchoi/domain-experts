# Vocabulary

### Free fatty acid (FFA) content
A measure of the proportion of fatty acids in a biodiesel feedstock that have already separated from their glyceride structure, a key characteristic determining correct catalyst dosing for transesterification.
**In use:** "FFA came back at two-point-five percent — well above our half-percent baseline, so catalyst dose needs adjusting up."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a fixed catalyst dose works across all feedstock regardless of FFA content — high-FFA feedstock consumes catalyst in an unwanted side reaction, and a dose calibrated for low-FFA feedstock will be insufficient for higher-FFA material.

### Transesterification
The chemical reaction converting triglycerides (fats/oils) into biodiesel (fatty acid methyl esters) and glycerin, using a catalyst and alcohol, whose completeness must be verified by testing rather than assumed from standard processing.
**In use:** "Transesterification's not complete just because we ran the standard reaction time — checking glyceride content to actually confirm it."
**Common misuse:** Assuming standard reaction parameters (time, temperature, catalyst dose) always achieve complete conversion — feedstock variability (particularly FFA content) can leave the reaction incomplete even under nominally standard conditions, requiring actual testing to confirm.

### Saponification (side reaction)
An unwanted reaction where catalyst reacts with free fatty acids to form soap, rather than participating in the intended transesterification reaction, consuming catalyst that would otherwise drive fuel conversion.
**In use:** "High FFA feedstock means more catalyst goes to saponification instead of transesterification — that's why the dose needs to go up, not just to compensate for a bigger batch."
**Common misuse:** Treating catalyst dose as solely a function of batch size/volume rather than also accounting for FFA-driven saponification consumption — the same batch volume with higher FFA feedstock genuinely requires more catalyst to achieve the same transesterification result.

### Glyceride content test (ASTM D6584)
A specific analytical test measuring residual mono-, di-, and triglycerides remaining in finished biodiesel, used to verify transesterification completeness beyond what visual inspection can reveal.
**In use:** "Glyceride test came back at point-one-eight percent — within our point-two-four spec, confirming conversion was actually complete."
**Common misuse:** Relying on visual clarity or appearance to judge biodiesel quality — residual glycerides that indicate incomplete conversion aren't visually detectable, and only the specific analytical test confirms conversion completeness.

### Stuck fermentation
A fermentation process that stops progressing before reaching expected completion, typically caused by yeast population stress or death from a temperature, pH, or other environmental excursion during the process.
**In use:** "This is a stuck fermentation — checking the temperature log for when it likely happened, since the yeast population probably took damage partway through."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a stuck fermentation can be corrected by simply extending processing time or adding more yeast without investigating the cause — if the original yeast population was stressed or killed by an excursion, the underlying condition needs identification to prevent recurrence, not just a workaround for this specific batch.

### Feedstock characterization (biofuels)
Testing an incoming feedstock batch's relevant characteristics (starch/sugar content for ethanol, FFA content for biodiesel) before processing, rather than assuming it matches a standard specification.
**In use:** "Characterizing this batch before we run it — the source is different from our usual supplier, so we're not assuming it matches our typical feedstock profile."
**Common misuse:** Running a fixed process recipe across all feedstock batches without testing actual characteristics — feedstock quality varies by source and batch, and process parameters calibrated for one quality level produce different (often worse) results on feedstock with different actual characteristics.

### Byproduct quality (biofuels co-products)
The quality characteristics (glycerin purity from biodiesel, distillers grains composition from ethanol) of a biofuel process's secondary output, which have their own market value and specification requirements distinct from the primary fuel product.
**In use:** "Glycerin purity from this batch is high enough for the higher-value pharmaceutical-grade market, not just disposal — that's real economic value we'd lose if process conditions weren't managed with byproduct quality in mind."
**Common misuse:** Treating byproduct quality as an incidental outcome of process conditions optimized purely for primary fuel yield — byproduct quality is a distinct economic consideration that can be actively managed, and process conditions ignoring it can reduce byproduct value even while primary fuel yield stays acceptable.

### Yeast viability (fermentation control)
The living, active state of the yeast population responsible for fermentation, which must be maintained within specific temperature and pH conditions throughout the process since population stress or death doesn't reverse on its own.
**In use:** "Once that population took a hit from the temperature spike, viability doesn't recover on its own — that's a stuck fermentation from here."
**Common misuse:** Assuming yeast population health can be assessed only at the start of fermentation and remains constant throughout — active monitoring is needed because conditions during the process (not just at the start) determine whether the population stays viable and productive.
