# Tour Director — Vocabulary

### Step-on guide
A licensed local expert hired to board the coach and lead the group through a specific attraction or city segment, contracted for a fixed session regardless of the group's actual arrival time within it.
**In use:** "The step-on guide's window is 2 to 4 — that's fixed by her next booking, not by when we happen to arrive."
**Common misuse:** Treated as flexible on the guide's end when the group is running late, when in practice the guide's own next commitment is usually the hard constraint, not the group's schedule.

### Rooming list
The document specifying which guests share which room and at what occupancy (single, double, triple), sent to each hotel ahead of arrival so rooms can be physically blocked before the group shows up.
**In use:** "The rooming list still shows the Hendersons as a double — they split into two singles after the manifest update, so this needs fixing before we send it to the hotel."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a one-time document instead of one that has to be re-verified against the manifest after every late change.

### Passenger manifest
The authoritative headcount and roster for the trip, used to reconcile against the rooming list, coach capacity, and every vendor booking that's priced per person.
**In use:** "The manifest says 34 — the rooming list only accounts for 32, so two people are missing a room assignment somewhere."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to be static once finalized, when late cancellations or additions require updating every document that was built from it.

### Per diem
A daily cash or card allowance, typically $25–35/day on a mid-level tour, provided to cover a guest's or the TD's incidental personal expenses on the road.
**In use:** "Per diem gets distributed each morning at breakfast, and I log the running total against what was budgeted for the day."
**Common misuse:** Confused with the gratuity envelope — per diem covers incidental expenses; gratuities are a separate pool for TD/driver tips.

### Gratuity pool
The collected tip fund for the TD and driver, typically guided by a per-person-per-day figure the operator states as a heuristic, reconciled at trip end against actual days served by each guest.
**In use:** "Two guests left after day six, so their contribution to the pool is prorated to six days, not the full ten."
**Common misuse:** Calculated as a flat even split across every name on the manifest regardless of how many days each guest actually traveled.

### Seat rotation
A systematic, pre-announced pattern for reassigning motorcoach seating over the course of a multi-day tour, used as the default fairness mechanism for who gets front versus back seats.
**In use:** "We rotate front-to-back every two days — I'll announce today's assignment at the morning briefing."
**Common misuse:** Applied so rigidly that a genuine mobility need (a guest who needs to sit near the door) isn't allowed to override the pattern.

### Documents wallet
The TD's carried set of vouchers, insurance certificates, emergency contacts, and photocopies of each guest's key travel documents, assembled before departure specifically to make document-loss situations executable rather than improvised.
**In use:** "Before we call the consulate, let me pull his passport copy from the documents wallet."
**Common misuse:** Assembled incompletely (missing photocopies of guest documents specifically) because it's treated as an administrative folder rather than an emergency-response tool.

### Vendor-chain cascade
The way a delay or failure at one vendor commitment (coach, hotel, guide, restaurant) propagates its effect forward through every subsequent commitment for the rest of the day or trip, rather than staying contained to the current stop.
**In use:** "The coach delay isn't just a late arrival — it's a cascade into the guide's window and possibly the dinner reservation, so let's map both before deciding anything."
**Common misuse:** Ignored in favor of solving only the immediate, visible problem, leaving the downstream effect to surface later as a second, avoidable disruption.

### Optional excursion
An add-on activity offered during the tour, priced separately from the base tour (commonly $35–100+ per person) and typically sold through the TD, who earns a commission on bookings.
**In use:** "Tomorrow's optional excursion is the vineyard visit — it's not included in the base tour, and here's what it costs if you'd like to book it."
**Common misuse:** Presented as though it's included in the base package, or pushed repeatedly on guests who've already declined, rather than offered once and left to guests to opt into.

### Ops desk
The tour operator's emergency/operations contact line, the point of escalation for any decision above the TD's contracted independent spending authority.
**In use:** "This rebooking is going to cost more than I'm authorized to approve on my own — I'm calling the ops desk before we commit to it."
**Common misuse:** Bypassed in favor of an on-the-spot TD decision, on the assumption that "handling it" is always the more professional choice, when it can create liability the operator never agreed to.

### Table turn
A restaurant's required minimum time to seat, serve, and clear a large group's table, used by the TD to check whether an arrival-time change actually threatens a meal reservation.
**In use:** "The reservation has a 90-minute table turn — we've still got three and a half hours of slack before it, so nothing needs to move."
**Common misuse:** Ignored when recalculating a delayed schedule, leading to an unnecessary attempt to renegotiate a reservation that was never actually at risk.

### Single supplement
The additional charge applied to a guest traveling without a roommate, since tour pricing is built on double occupancy by default.
**In use:** "She's traveling solo, so her invoice includes the single supplement — the base price assumes two people splitting the room."
**Common misuse:** Assumed by guests to be an arbitrary upcharge rather than the cost difference between a shared and a full room rate.
