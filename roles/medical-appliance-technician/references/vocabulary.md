# Vocabulary

### Rectification
The process of modifying a positive model, built from a cast or scan, before forming a socket or device — adding or removing material at specific anatomical zones to control pressure distribution.
**In use:** "Rectification map calls for relief at the fibular head and build-up at the patellar tendon — not a uniform clearance across the model."
**Common misuse:** Applying uniform clearance or thickness across a model rather than zone-specific rectification based on pressure-sensitive versus pressure-tolerant anatomy.

### Total contact
A socket design principle where the socket wall maintains continuous contact with the residual limb surface, rather than contacting at only a few points.
**In use:** "This is a total-contact socket — contact's continuous everywhere, but the pressure at each point is intentionally different by zone."
**Common misuse:** Assuming "total contact" means uniform pressure everywhere, when it actually means continuous surface contact with pressure deliberately varying by zone — light at sensitive areas, firm at load-tolerant ones.

### Patellar tendon bar
A specific built-up area in a transtibial prosthetic socket positioned to bear weight against the patellar tendon, a pressure-tolerant structure.
**In use:** "The patellar tendon bar carries a proportionate share of the load here — that's why it's built up 3mm rather than left flush."
**Common misuse:** Treating socket load-bearing as evenly distributed rather than concentrated at specific engineered load-bearing zones like this one.

### Bench alignment
The initial static assembly of a prosthesis's components — socket, knee/ankle, foot — to standard reference angles before the patient walks in it.
**In use:** "Bench alignment's set to standard angles — that's the starting point, not the final configuration."
**Common misuse:** Treating bench alignment as the final alignment configuration rather than a starting point that dynamic gait observation is expected to adjust.

### Dynamic alignment
Adjustment of a prosthesis's component alignment made while observing the patient actually walking or bearing weight in it, correcting for gait abnormalities invisible in static assembly.
**In use:** "Dynamic alignment caught a lateral thrust in the swing phase the bench setup never would have shown."
**Common misuse:** Skipping dynamic alignment verification because the device already "measures correctly" on the bench.

### Relief (rectification)
Additional clearance built into a socket or device at a pressure-sensitive anatomical landmark, so the device doesn't press directly on that structure.
**In use:** "4mm of relief at the fibular head — that keeps the socket wall off that bony point entirely."
**Common misuse:** Assuming more relief is always better or safer everywhere, when excessive relief at a load-bearing zone can undermine the total-contact/weight-transfer function that zone is meant to provide.

### Positive model
A solid replica, traditionally plaster and now often digital, of the patient's limb or anatomy, built from a negative cast or scan, used as the working surface for rectification and fabrication.
**In use:** "Rectify the positive model before we laminate — that's where the actual fit gets decided, not after."
**Common misuse:** Treating the positive model as simply a copy of the patient's anatomy, rather than recognizing it as the surface that gets deliberately modified away from the raw anatomical shape to achieve the intended fit.

### Residual limb
The remaining portion of a limb after amputation, onto which a prosthetic socket is fitted.
**In use:** "Residual limb volume's changed since the initial casting — that's why the socket needs re-evaluating now."
**Common misuse:** Assuming residual limb anatomy is static, when limb volume commonly fluctuates from swelling or muscle atrophy over time, which is why socket fit needs periodic re-evaluation rather than being assumed permanent from initial fitting.

### Thermoplastic vs. laminate (fabrication material)
Two different material and process categories for building orthotic/prosthetic devices, with different rigidity, adjustability, and durability tradeoffs.
**In use:** "Went with laminate here for the durability this activity level needs — thermoplastic wouldn't hold up as well."
**Common misuse:** Choosing one over the other based on fabrication convenience alone, without matching the choice to the patient's actual load, adjustability, and durability requirements.

### Gait pattern
The specific, individual way a patient walks, including any compensations or abnormalities, which alignment must account for beyond generic biomechanical assumptions.
**In use:** "This patient's gait pattern shows a slight vaulting compensation — alignment needs to account for that specifically."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a "normal" gait pattern applies to a specific patient's dynamic alignment needs, rather than observing and adjusting for that patient's actual walking pattern.
