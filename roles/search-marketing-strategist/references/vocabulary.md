### Search intent (informational / navigational / transactional / commercial-investigation)
The underlying purpose behind a search query, classified into these four standard categories, which determines whether organic content or paid bidding is the appropriate lever.
**In use:** "This term is informational — searchers want to learn, not buy, so we're building organic content rather than bidding on it."
**Common misuse:** Bidding heavily on informational-intent keywords or building product-page-style content for transactional-intent keywords, mismatching content type to what the query actually calls for.

### Quality Score
Google Ads' 1-10 rating of an ad's relevance and expected performance, based on expected click-through rate, ad relevance, and landing page experience — directly affecting actual cost-per-click.
**In use:** "Quality Score is a 4 out of 10 here, driven by a slow landing page — that's why CPC is so high despite a modest bid."
**Common misuse:** Raising the bid to compensate for a low Quality Score instead of diagnosing and fixing the underlying relevance or landing page issue.

### Last-click attribution
An attribution model crediting 100% of a conversion to the last channel or ad a user clicked before converting.
**In use:** "Last-click attribution shows this channel underperforming, but it may just never be the final touchpoint."
**Common misuse:** Using last-click attribution alone to make a channel budget decision, missing upper-funnel channels that consistently assist conversions without being the final click.

### Data-driven / multi-touch attribution
An attribution model distributing conversion credit across multiple touchpoints in a user's path, based on their actual observed contribution to conversion likelihood.
**In use:** "Data-driven attribution shows this channel contributing meaningfully earlier in the funnel, even though it rarely gets last-click credit."
**Common misuse:** Assuming multi-touch attribution is always more "correct" without checking whether enough conversion data exists to make the model statistically reliable.

### Incrementality testing
A controlled experiment (such as a geo holdout) measuring the true causal lift a marketing channel or campaign provides, by comparing outcomes with and without the channel active.
**In use:** "Incrementality testing showed only a 9.7% relative lift from brand paid search — most of the attributed conversions would have happened anyway."
**Common misuse:** Relying on attributed conversion counts as a substitute for incrementality testing, especially for brand paid search, which is highly prone to cannibalizing organic traffic.

### Cannibalization (paid/organic)
The phenomenon where a paid ad captures a click or conversion that would have occurred through the organic listing anyway, inflating the paid channel's apparent value without adding true incremental revenue.
**In use:** "Cannibalization is likely high here since we already rank #1 organically for this exact brand term."
**Common misuse:** Assuming any brand paid search campaign is automatically valuable without testing how much of its attributed performance is cannibalized organic traffic.

### Marginal ROAS (return on ad spend)
The return generated by the next incremental dollar of ad spend, as distinct from the average ROAS across all spend to date.
**In use:** "Average ROAS looks great at 6x, but marginal ROAS on the last spend increase was only 1.2x — we're near saturation."
**Common misuse:** Using average ROAS to justify further budget increases without checking whether marginal ROAS at the current spend level still clears the profitability bar.

### Saturation curve
The pattern where each additional dollar of ad spend in a channel or keyword set produces a smaller incremental return than the previous dollar, eventually flattening or declining.
**In use:** "We're on the flat part of the saturation curve for this keyword set — more spend won't move conversions much further."
**Common misuse:** Assuming ad spend returns scale linearly, leading to overspending well past the point where additional budget produces meaningful incremental results.

### Match type (broad / phrase / exact) and negative keywords
Settings controlling how closely a search query must match a targeted keyword to trigger an ad, and negative keywords that explicitly exclude certain queries from triggering it.
**In use:** "Broad match was triggering on irrelevant queries — tightening to phrase match and adding negative keywords should improve relevance and Quality Score."
**Common misuse:** Using broad match without a robust negative keyword list, leading to wasted spend on irrelevant queries that also drag down ad relevance scoring.

### E-E-A-T (Experience, Expertise, Authoritativeness, Trustworthiness)
Google's framework for assessing content quality for organic ranking purposes, particularly emphasized for topics affecting health, finances, or safety.
**In use:** "This page needs a credentialed author byline and cited sources to strengthen E-E-A-T signals for this topic."
**Common misuse:** Treating E-E-A-T as a simple checklist (add an author bio) rather than a substantive quality signal reflected in the actual depth, accuracy, and sourcing of the content.
