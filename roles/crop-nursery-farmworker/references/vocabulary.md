# Vocabulary

Terms generalists conflate or use loosely that a farmworker or crew lead keeps sharply separate, because the difference changes what's legally required or which action to take.

### Piece rate vs. reconciliation pay

**Piece rate** is the per-unit wage (per flat, per bin, per row) that drives most harvest pay. **Reconciliation pay** is the additional amount owed when piece earnings divided by hours worked fall below the applicable minimum wage or AEWR floor — it is not a bonus or a discretionary top-up, it is a legal requirement to make the shortfall whole.

**In use:** "Her piece rate came in at $14.81 an hour today — reconciliation pay covers the gap up to the $16.50 floor, itemized separately on the stub."

**Common misuse:** treating a slow piece-rate day as the worker's problem to absorb, or assuming a generally-generous piece rate excuses checking every individual worker's actual day.

### REI (restricted-entry interval) vs. PHI (pre-harvest interval)

**REI** is the time after a pesticide application before anyone may re-enter the treated area without specific PPE and an exemption. **PHI** is the separate time before that crop may legally be *harvested* after the application, regardless of when workers can re-enter for other tasks. A short REI does not mean the crop is clear to pick — PHI can be much longer.

**In use:** "REI cleared at noon, so we can go in to check irrigation, but the label's PHI means we can't harvest this block until Thursday."

**Common misuse:** treating REI as if it also governs when harvest may resume — they're two separate label clocks that can differ by days.

### AEWR vs. prevailing wage vs. minimum wage

**AEWR** (Adverse Effect Wage Rate) is the wage floor set for H-2A workers, published annually by state and occupation, designed to prevent depressing wages for domestic workers. **Prevailing wage** is a separate, locally-surveyed rate that can also apply to specific H-2A job orders. **Minimum wage** is the general state or federal floor that applies regardless of visa status. The applicable floor for any given worker is the *highest* of whichever of these apply, not whichever is easiest to calculate.

**In use:** "AEWR for this crop and state is above the state minimum wage this year, so that's the floor we reconcile against for the H-2A crew."

**Common misuse:** assuming state minimum wage is always the relevant floor and skipping the AEWR check for H-2A workers, who are frequently entitled to more.

### Brix vs. sugar content vs. sweetness

**Brix** is a specific refractometer reading of dissolved solids (mostly sugars, but not exclusively) in a fruit's juice, expressed as a percentage. **Sugar content** is the actual sugar fraction, which Brix approximates but doesn't measure directly. **Sweetness** is a perceived, subjective quality that also depends on acidity — a high-Brix fruit with high acid can taste less sweet than a lower-Brix fruit with low acid.

**In use:** "Brix is at 24, which is in range for harvest, but check the acid too — a high-acid block at the same Brix will read as less sweet in the field test."

**Common misuse:** using Brix as a direct stand-in for "how sweet it tastes" without accounting for acidity, or as a stand-in for overall ripeness when firmness or color hasn't caught up.

### Color break vs. physiological maturity vs. horticultural maturity

**Physiological maturity** is the point at which a fruit can continue ripening off the plant (seeds viable, internal development complete). **Horticultural maturity** is the point at which a fruit is ready for its *intended use* — which can be earlier or later than physiological maturity depending on market. **Color break** is the visible first sign of ripening (e.g., a tomato's green-to-color transition) used as a practical field marker for one of these, not a precise scientific boundary.

**In use:** "It's at color break, which is horticultural maturity for shipping to this buyer — don't wait for full red, that's a different spec."

**Common misuse:** assuming color break always means the same maturity stage across crops and buyers, when the target stage is set by the market, not the biology alone.

### Climacteric vs. non-climacteric fruit

**Climacteric** fruits (tomatoes, peaches, avocados, bananas) continue to ripen after harvest via a respiration burst, so picking slightly under-ripe for shipping is recoverable. **Non-climacteric** fruits (strawberries, citrus, grapes) do not ripen further once picked — sugar and color at harvest are what the consumer gets, full stop.

**In use:** "Strawberries are non-climacteric, so picking under-color to buy shipping time is a permanent quality loss, not a delay."

**Common misuse:** applying a "it'll ripen on the truck" assumption from climacteric crops to a non-climacteric crop, where it simply won't.

### Action threshold vs. economic injury level (IPM)

**Economic injury level (EIL)** is the pest population at which the cost of the damage equals the cost of control — a theoretical break-even point. **Action threshold** is the practical, lower trigger point set *before* EIL to allow time to act (scout, order biological controls, apply treatment) before the pest population reaches the point of actual economic loss.

**In use:** "We hit the action threshold on whitefly counts this week — order the Encarsia now, well before we'd hit actual economic injury."

**Common misuse:** waiting until damage is visibly costing yield (near or past EIL) before acting, when the action threshold exists specifically to trigger a response earlier than that.

### Heat index vs. ambient air temperature

**Ambient air temperature** is the plain thermometer reading. **Heat index** combines temperature and humidity into the temperature the body actually experiences, and is the correct trigger for heat-protocol thresholds (e.g., 80°F and 95°F) — a humid 88°F day can have a heat index well above a dry 95°F day.

**In use:** "Air temp says 89°F but heat index is 97 with this humidity — we're in high-heat procedures, not basic provisions."

**Common misuse:** checking only the thermometer and concluding conditions are fine, missing that humidity has pushed the effective heat load into the mandatory-break range.

### Application Exclusion Zone (AEZ) vs. REI

**AEZ** is the no-entry buffer zone (commonly 25 or 100 feet) that applies *during* a pesticide application itself, regardless of any REI. **REI** is the interval *after* the application ends before re-entry is allowed. They are sequential, not interchangeable — clearing one says nothing about the other.

**In use:** "Nobody's inside the AEZ while the rig is spraying; once it's done, the REI clock starts."

**Common misuse:** treating "outside the AEZ" as equivalent to "safe to work the field," when the REI still applies once the application is finished.

### Dry matter % vs. moisture content

**Dry matter percentage** is the proportion of a fruit's weight (typically avocado) that isn't water, measured by drying a sample — used as a legal maturity standard because it correlates with oil/flavor development. **Moisture content** is just the water fraction, the inverse of dry matter, but isn't itself the regulated maturity metric in the same way.

**In use:** "Dry matter came back at 21% on this lot — that clears the legal minimum for harvest, even though the fruit still feels rock-hard."

**Common misuse:** using firmness as a maturity proxy for avocado, which doesn't soften pre-harvest regardless of maturity — only the dry-matter test tells you if it's legal to pick.

### Nonproductive time vs. rest/recovery period

**Rest/recovery period** is the specific mandated break (e.g., a 10-minute paid rest every four hours, or a high-heat cool-down) required by law. **Nonproductive time** is the broader category of any compensable time not spent producing piece output — equipment delays, row moves, waiting for a trailer — which, in piece-rate jurisdictions like California, also requires separate pay at the floor rate, distinct from and in addition to rest periods.

**In use:** "That 20 minutes waiting on the bin truck is nonproductive time, paid at the floor — separate from her two rest breaks, which are also paid at the floor."

**Common misuse:** treating "nonproductive time" as only the officially scheduled rest breaks, missing the broader category of unscheduled downtime that also requires separate pay.

### VPD (vapor pressure deficit) vs. relative humidity

**Relative humidity** is the percentage of moisture the air holds relative to what it could hold at that temperature. **VPD** combines temperature and humidity into the actual "drying power" of the air on a plant's leaves — the more useful number for greenhouse climate control, because the same RH% means something different at different temperatures.

**In use:** "RH looks fine at 70%, but at this temperature VPD is under 0.4 kPa — that's still too humid for botrytis risk, even though the RH number alone looks acceptable."

**Common misuse:** managing greenhouse climate off RH alone, missing that the same RH reading at a different temperature can sit on opposite sides of the disease-risk threshold.
