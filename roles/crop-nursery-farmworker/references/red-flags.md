# Red flags

Smell tests a crew lead or safety-conscious worker should catch before a shift goes wrong, not after a wage claim or an injury. Format for each: what it usually means, the first question to ask, and the data to pull.

### Daily piece earnings ÷ hours worked lands within $1/hr of the minimum-wage floor

- **Usually means:** the block's yield, weather, or fruit condition dropped enough that this worker is at or below the legal floor and owed reconciliation pay, even if yesterday's numbers looked fine.
- **First question:** "What were the actual active-picking hours and piece count for this specific worker, this specific day — not the crew average?"
- **Data to pull:** individual piece-count and hour logs for the pay period; separate line items (if any) for rest breaks and nonproductive time; the applicable minimum wage or AEWR for that jurisdiction and date.

### New or first-week workers assigned to a historically low-yield block

- **Usually means:** the assignment structurally can't produce enough piece output to clear the wage floor regardless of effort, setting up a reconciliation-pay obligation before the shift even starts.
- **First question:** "What did experienced pickers average on this same block in the last week?"
- **Data to pull:** block-level yield history by picker tenure; whether new workers are rotated onto lower-yield blocks disproportionately.

### Pay stub shows a single lump piece total with no separate line for rest breaks or nonproductive time

- **Usually means:** rest-break and nonproductive time are being folded into the piece rate instead of paid separately at the required floor, which is a Labor Code §226.2-type violation in states with that requirement.
- **First question:** "Where on this stub is the rest-break pay itemized separately from piece earnings?"
- **Data to pull:** payroll system's pay-code breakdown; sample stubs across several pay periods to see if the omission is consistent.

### H-2A wage stub below the posted Adverse Effect Wage Rate for that state and crop

- **Usually means:** the employer is paying the state minimum wage or a lower piece rate without checking whether AEWR (which is often higher) is the actual floor that applies.
- **First question:** "What is the current published AEWR for this state and this crop category, and does this stub clear it?"
- **Data to pull:** current DOL/USDA AEWR schedule; the worker's H-2A job order wage rate as filed.

### Crew re-enters a treated block within an hour or two of the pesticide application

- **Usually means:** harvest-deadline pressure is overriding the labeled restricted-entry interval, which is a federal Worker Protection Standard violation regardless of how the crew leader judges the risk.
- **First question:** "What was the exact application time and the labeled REI for that specific product and rate — not the shortest REI anyone remembers for something similar?"
- **Data to pull:** the pesticide application record and label; posted notification signage and its timestamp.

### REI/application posting missing, expired, or illegible at the field or greenhouse entrance

- **Usually means:** either the posting requirement is being skipped entirely, or a previous application's sign was never taken down, making it impossible for a worker to verify re-entry is safe without asking someone.
- **First question:** "When was this application record posted, and does it match today's actual spray log?"
- **Data to pull:** the spray/application log for the last 30 days for that field or greenhouse zone; posting compliance history.

### Heat index forecast at or above 95°F with no observed cool-down breaks in the last two hours

- **Usually means:** the high-heat protocol exists on paper but isn't being enforced on the ground, often because piece-rate pay gives workers a financial reason not to ask for the break themselves.
- **First question:** "When was the last scheduled 10-minute cool-down break, and was it on the clock or was output paused informally?"
- **Data to pull:** heat-index log for the shift; break-timing records; any heat-illness symptom reports from the crew that day.

### Water or shade more than a few minutes' walk from the active work area on a high-heat day

- **Usually means:** the basic heat-provision requirement is being met on paper (water exists somewhere on-site) but not in the way that actually gets used under piece-rate time pressure.
- **First question:** "How far is it, in minutes, from where this crew is working right now to the nearest water and shade?"
- **Data to pull:** field/greenhouse layout map with water and shade station locations relative to today's work zones.

### Discard/cull rate spikes mid-week without a corresponding weather or pest event logged

- **Usually means:** the harvest window was misjudged on the front end — either picked too early (immature fruit failing quality spec) or too late (over-ripe/split fruit from a missed window) — and the cause hasn't been diagnosed yet.
- **First question:** "What does the maturity indicator (Brix, color, firmness) show for this block right now, compared to three days ago?"
- **Data to pull:** daily cull/discard percentage by block; maturity-indicator readings across the affected days; recent weather log.

### Greenhouse relative humidity sustained above 85–90% for 48+ hours with no ventilation change logged

- **Usually means:** climate control is not being actively managed, and disease pressure (botrytis, powdery mildew) is building toward a point where a fungicide application becomes the only remaining option instead of the fallback it should be.
- **First question:** "What has ventilation and heating been set to over the last two days, and has anyone adjusted it in response to the humidity trend?"
- **Data to pull:** greenhouse climate-control logs (temperature, RH, VPD); disease-scouting notes for the affected zone.

### Sticky-trap pest counts exceed the action threshold for two or more consecutive scouting visits with no response logged

- **Usually means:** scouting is happening but the resulting decision isn't — either nobody is reviewing the counts against the threshold, or a biological-control release has been delayed past the point where it's still the first-line option.
- **First question:** "What was done after last week's count crossed the threshold, and who made that call?"
- **Data to pull:** trap-count log by zone and week; biological or chemical control application records for the same period.
