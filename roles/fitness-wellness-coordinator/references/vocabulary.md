# Fitness & Wellness Coordinator working vocabulary

Terms this role uses precisely and generalists conflate. Format: definition → how a practitioner says it → common misuse.

**Participatory vs. health-contingent wellness program** — A participatory program rewards the act of engaging (completing an HRA, attending a seminar) with no health-outcome or activity threshold, and HIPAA/ACA place no incentive-percentage cap on it; a health-contingent program ties the reward to an activity level or a biometric outcome and is the category the 30%/50% incentive ceilings actually apply to.
In use: "Reframe the step-count challenge as activity-based health-contingent, not participatory — that's why it needs a reasonable alternative standard attached, and why it's the one that has to stay under 30%."
Misuse: applying the 30%/50% ceiling math to a purely participatory program (it doesn't apply) or, more common and more dangerous, calling a threshold-based challenge "participatory" to dodge the ceiling and the RAS requirement it triggers.

**Activity-based vs. outcome-based health-contingent program** — Both are health-contingent subtypes under HIPAA, but activity-based rewards completing an activity (a walking program, a set number of gym visits) regardless of result, while outcome-based rewards achieving or improving a specific biometric target (BMI under a threshold, blood pressure in range) — outcome-based programs carry a stricter compliance bar because they can penalize people for conditions outside their control.
In use: "This is outcome-based, not activity-based — since it's tied to a BMI target, we need the reasonable alternative standard spelled out on the same page as the reward, not just referenced elsewhere."
Misuse: treating the two as interchangeable paperwork variants; outcome-based design without a clearly stated, physician-endorsable alternative standard is the specific pattern EEOC and HIPAA scrutiny targets hardest.

**Reasonable alternative standard (RAS)** — The HIPAA-required fallback a health-contingent program must offer anyone for whom the stated standard is medically inadvisable or unreasonably difficult to meet — a different, achievable way to earn the same reward, not a waiver of the reward itself.
In use: "He can't hit the BP outcome target due to a diagnosed condition — pull up his reasonable alternative standard; he still earns the incentive through the alternative pathway, not by being excused from participation."
Misuse: treating the RAS as optional fine print instead of a required, actively-offered alternative — a program that only mentions it in a footnote nobody reads doesn't satisfy the requirement even if the words are technically present somewhere in the document.

**Health risk assessment (HRA) vs. biometric screening** — An HRA is a self-reported questionnaire (lifestyle, family history, stress, sleep) that produces a risk score from what the participant states; biometric screening is a direct physical measurement (blood pressure, lipids, glucose, waist circumference) that produces objective values independent of self-report.
In use: "The HRA flagged high stress and poor sleep, but the biometric screen is what actually triggered the referral — don't let a clean HRA talk someone out of following up on a flagged blood pressure reading."
Misuse: treating the two as redundant or substitutable — a favorable self-reported HRA can coexist with a biometric result that requires physician referral, and only the biometric number gates the referral pathway.

**MET (metabolic equivalent)** — A unit of resting energy expenditure (1 MET ≈ 3.5 mL O2/kg/min, roughly quiet sitting) used to express exercise intensity as a multiple of resting metabolism, independent of a participant's body weight or fitness level — the standard unit for classifying an activity's intensity, not for classifying a person's fitness.
In use: "That circuit sits around 6 METs — moderate-to-vigorous — so it needs the same pre-participation screening gate as any other vigorous-intensity assignment, regardless of how the class is marketed."
Misuse: using MET value and perceived difficulty interchangeably — a participant's subjective sense of effort (see RPE) doesn't substitute for the MET-based intensity classification that screening decisions are actually built on.

**RPE (Rating of Perceived Exertion) / Borg scale** — A participant's self-reported subjective exertion level, typically on the 6–20 Borg scale or a simplified 0–10 version, used to self-regulate intensity in real time — a subjective companion to objective measures like MET level or heart-rate zone, not a replacement for either.
In use: "Cue by RPE for the deconditioned group — 12–14 on the Borg scale — since we don't have heart-rate monitors on everyone in that session."
Misuse: substituting RPE for the pre-participation screening decision itself; a participant "feeling fine" (low RPE) after being flagged on PAR-Q+ or a biometric cut point doesn't override the referral requirement — RPE governs pacing within a cleared session, not clearance itself.

**VO2max vs. VO2peak** — VO2max is the true physiological ceiling of oxygen uptake, confirmed by a plateau in oxygen consumption despite increasing workload; VO2peak is the highest oxygen uptake actually observed during a given test, used when the test was stopped for fatigue, symptoms, or protocol limits before a true plateau was reached — most field and even many lab tests report VO2peak, not VO2max.
In use: "Report that as VO2peak in the file — the test was terminated on perceived exertion before we saw a plateau, so we can't claim it hit true max."
Misuse: labeling any maximal-effort test result "VO2max" by default; overstating a peak value as a true max overstates the participant's actual aerobic ceiling and can mis-set training zones derived from it.

**Enrollment vs. participation vs. engagement** — Enrollment is the count of people signed up for a program; participation is the count actually completing qualifying activities within a period; engagement is a deeper, often multi-factor measure (frequency, depth, sustained use) that a raw participation count doesn't capture — the three numbers answer different leadership questions and are routinely reported as if they were one figure.
In use: "We're at 68% enrollment, but participation this quarter is 41%, and by the engagement definition in our HERO submission it's closer to 16% — lead with participation when the ask is about program impact, not the enrollment headline."
Misuse: quoting enrollment (the largest, most flattering number) when a leadership conversation is actually asking about participation or engagement — this is the single most common way a program's actual traction gets overstated.

**Primary, secondary, and tertiary prevention** — Primary prevention stops a condition before it starts in an unaffected population (general fitness programming, health education); secondary prevention catches an existing condition early through screening (biometric screens, HRAs) to intervene before it worsens; tertiary prevention manages an already-diagnosed condition to limit its progression and complications (disease-management coaching for diagnosed diabetes or cardiac patients) — a program mixing these under one banner is answering three different populations' needs with one design.
In use: "The step challenge is primary prevention — fine to run open-enrollment. The diabetes-management coaching track is tertiary — that population needs a clinical referral pathway built in, not the same generic onboarding."
Misuse: designing a single generic wellness offering and applying it across all three populations, most commonly under-serving the tertiary group by giving them primary-prevention-style generic content instead of the clinical coordination that population actually needs.

**Presenteeism vs. absenteeism** — Absenteeism is missed work time (days away); presenteeism is reduced productivity while physically present due to a health condition — often the larger and harder-to-measure cost of poor health in a workforce, and the metric most likely to be omitted from an ROI case built only on absenteeism data.
In use: "Absenteeism savings alone won't clear the ROI bar leadership wants — pull the presenteeism estimate from the HRA productivity module, since that's usually the bigger line item."
Misuse: building a program's financial case on absenteeism reduction alone because it's easier to measure, when presenteeism is typically the larger cost and its omission understates the program's actual value (or, in the other direction, inflates an ROI claim built on presenteeism estimates that are inherently soft self-reported data and shouldn't be presented with the same confidence as hard absenteeism records).

**Scope of practice vs. scope of license** — Scope of practice is what a credential (e.g., NBHWC certification) is trained and authorized to do — behavior-change coaching, motivational interviewing, goal-setting; scope of license is the narrower, jurisdiction-specific legal authorization tied to a regulated title (dietitian, physical therapist, physician) — a coach can be well within scope of practice for their certification while still lacking any license to diagnose or prescribe treatment.
In use: "Coaching him through adherence to his doctor's exercise plan is in scope of practice. Adjusting the plan itself is a licensed clinical decision — neither the coach's certification nor the absence of a specific state license covers that."
Misuse: treating "I'm certified" as equivalent to "I'm licensed to do this" — a coaching certification establishes scope of practice for behavior-change work, not legal authority to diagnose, prescribe, or modify a medical treatment plan, and conflating the two is exactly how a coach drifts into practicing medicine without a license.

**Public-access defibrillation (PAD) program vs. having an AED on-site** — A PAD program is the full written policy: device placement mapped to response-time targets, trained lay responders, a rehearsed call sequence, and maintenance protocol; owning a functional AED is one component of that program, not the program itself — the AHA/ACSM joint position requires the former, and a facility with a wall-mounted device and no drilled policy has satisfied neither the spirit nor, typically, the letter of the standard.
In use: "We have three AEDs, but do we have a PAD program? Show me the written response policy and the last drill log, not just the equipment inventory."
Misuse: describing a facility as "AED-compliant" or "emergency-ready" based on equipment presence alone — compliance and readiness are properties of the drilled, written program, not the hardware.

**Gross negligence vs. ordinary negligence (in a waiver context)** — Ordinary negligence is a failure to exercise reasonable care that a properly worded liability waiver can validly release a facility from; gross negligence is a reckless or conscious disregard for a known risk — courts have repeatedly refused to enforce waivers against gross-negligence claims regardless of how broadly the waiver is worded, making the ordinary/gross line the actual boundary of what a signed waiver protects.
In use: "Continuing the set after he reported chest tightness moves this from a possible ordinary-negligence claim to gross negligence — the waiver he signed on intake won't reach that fact pattern."
Misuse: treating a signed waiver as blanket protection regardless of what staff did after signing — the waiver's coverage ends at the ordinary/gross-negligence line, and that line is crossed by continuing an activity after a reported symptom, not by the mere occurrence of an injury.

**Return-to-activity clearance vs. medical clearance** — Medical clearance is a physician's sign-off that a participant may resume exercise generally, often with no activity-specific detail; return-to-activity clearance is the coordinator's own, more granular translation of that sign-off into a specific cleared intensity level, restricted movements, and a re-screen date — the two are not the same document and a program that treats a generic medical clearance letter as sufficient operational guidance is filling in the specifics itself, informally and inconsistently, at the point of program entry.
In use: "The physician's note says 'cleared to exercise' with no restriction detail — that's medical clearance, not a return-to-activity plan. Get the specifics in writing before assigning an intensity level, don't infer them."
Misuse: filing a generic physician clearance letter and treating the intensity-level, restriction, and re-screen decisions as already answered by it, when those operational specifics are the coordinator's own responsibility to establish and document, not the physician's to have spelled out unprompted.
