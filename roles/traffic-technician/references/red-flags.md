# Red flags

Smell tests a senior traffic technician catches on the first pass through a complaint ticket, warrant request, or timing-sheet review. Format for each: what it usually means, the first question to ask, and the data to pull before committing to a fix.

## 1. All-red clearance interval programmed below (W+L)/v for the approach's 85th-percentile speed

**Usually means:** the timing sheet was set at original installation and never recomputed after an approach-speed increase, a widened cross street, or a longer design vehicle became common on the approach — not a driver-behavior problem, even if the visible symptom is red-light-running.

**First question:** "What speed and intersection width was the current all-red actually calculated against, and does that still match the field conditions today?"

**Data to pull:** current timing sheet, most recent spot-speed study, as-built or current intersection geometry, design-vehicle assumption.

## 2. Repeated right-angle crashes at an intersection with yellow/all-red unchanged since original installation

**Usually means:** approach speeds or geometry have drifted since the original timing was set, producing a genuine clearance-interval deficit that reads externally as reckless driving.

**First question:** "Has anyone recomputed yellow and all-red against a current speed study, or has every review gone straight to enforcement?"

**Data to pull:** crash log with collision type and time-of-day, current timing sheet, recent 85th-percentile speed study on the approach.

## 3. Signal requested or installed without a documented 8-hour or peak-hour warrant count meeting MUTCD Chapter 4C thresholds

**Usually means:** a political or constituent-pressure decision is about to install (or already installed) a signal that will suppress one crash type while introducing an unexpected-stop rear-end pattern the major-street traffic doesn't anticipate.

**First question:** "What warrant was documented as met, on what count data, and for what time period?"

**Data to pull:** original warrant study or its absence, current 8-hour classification count, crash history if a crash-experience warrant is being claimed instead.

## 4. Corridor retiming changes one intersection's split without recomputing downstream offsets

**Usually means:** the fix solved the complaining intersection's problem while breaking the coordinated progression band for every signal downstream of it.

**First question:** "Which other signals share this coordination group, and have their offsets been re-run for the new split?"

**Data to pull:** corridor coordination plan/offset table, the split change just made, distances and target progression speed between adjacent signals.

## 5. Pedestrian clearance interval calculated at a walking speed faster than the documented population needs

**Usually means:** the timing sheet used the general 3.0 ft/s (or older 3.5 ft/s) default at a crossing that serves a known slower-pedestrian population — senior housing, hospital, assisted-living frontage — understating the clearance time that population actually needs.

**First question:** "What population does this specific crossing serve, and does the walking-speed assumption in the timing sheet reflect that, or just the general default?"

**Data to pull:** pedestrian phase timing sheet, adjacent land-use/facility inventory within the crosswalk's service radius, any prior pedestrian-complaint or near-miss reports at this crossing.

## 6. Turning-movement or warrant volume data based on a single 2-hour count

**Usually means:** the count window missed a shoulder-hour peak or captured a one-off event, and any warrant or permanent-timing decision built on it won't hold up when someone re-counts later.

**First question:** "Over what duration, on what day(s), and in what season was this count taken?"

**Data to pull:** count methodology and duration on file, seasonal-adjustment factor applied (or its absence), any comparable count from a different time of year.

## 7. Accessible pedestrian signal (APS) not installed or not upgraded at a signal that was newly installed or reconstructed

**Usually means:** the project scope or budget dropped the APS requirement, or an existing signal's reconstruction trigger for PROWAG compliance wasn't flagged during design.

**First question:** "Was this signal newly installed or structurally reconstructed, and if so, why isn't APS in the as-built scope?"

**Data to pull:** project scope/design documents, PROWAG compliance checklist, current push-button/locator-tone inventory for the intersection.

## 8. Cycle length left well above what Webster's formula (summed critical flow ratios) actually requires

**Usually means:** the corridor is carrying unnecessary delay at every signal on the route — a longer-than-needed cycle was assumed to be the conservative or safer choice rather than checked against current volumes.

**First question:** "When was the critical-flow-ratio analysis last re-run against current counted volumes, and does the current cycle length still match the optimum it produces?"

**Data to pull:** current signal timing sheet and cycle length, most recent turning-movement counts by phase, Webster cycle-length calculation if one exists.

## 9. Actuated signal's minimum green or gap/passage settings unchanged after a driveway or land-use change near the intersection

**Usually means:** a new development, driveway, or access-point change altered the approach's real turning-movement pattern, but the controller's actuated parameters still reflect the pre-change traffic pattern.

**First question:** "What changed in land use or access near this signal since these actuated settings were last reviewed, and has a current turning-movement count been run since then?"

**Data to pull:** controller programming sheet (minimum green, gap/passage settings), recent turning-movement count, land-use/access-permit history for adjacent parcels.

## 10. Leading pedestrian interval (LPI) added or removed without checking permissive left-turn conflict volumes

**Usually means:** the LPI decision was made on pedestrian-crossing considerations alone, without checking whether it changes the exposure of permissive left-turning vehicles to pedestrians who now enter the crosswalk several seconds before the concurrent green.

**First question:** "What's the permissive left-turn volume on this approach, and has the conflict exposure been checked with the LPI in place?"

**Data to pull:** turning-movement count for the affected approach's left-turn volume, current phase sequence and LPI duration, any pedestrian-vehicle conflict or near-miss data at the crossing.
