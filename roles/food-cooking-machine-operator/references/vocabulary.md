# Vocabulary

### Coldest point (cook lethality)
The specific location within a cooked food product — typically the geometric center of the thickest piece — that heats slowest and therefore determines whether the required pathogen lethality has actually been achieved throughout the product.
**In use:** "Probe goes in the center of the thickest piece in the batch — that's the coldest point, and that's the reading that actually matters."
**Common misuse:** Verifying lethality using a surface reading or a reading from an average-sized piece rather than specifically identifying and probing the batch's actual coldest point — surface and average readings can show compliant temperatures while the true coldest point hasn't reached the requirement.

### Log reduction (pathogen lethality)
A measure of how much a pathogen population is reduced by a cooking process, expressed in powers of ten (a 5-log reduction means a 100,000-fold decrease), the underlying microbiological basis for time-temperature cooking requirements.
**In use:** "This time-temperature combination is what achieves the required log reduction for this specific pathogen and product type."
**Common misuse:** Treating a cooking requirement's specific temperature and time values as arbitrary numbers rather than understanding they're calibrated to achieve a specific, required pathogen log reduction — this matters for recognizing why partial compliance (reaching temperature but not holding it) doesn't achieve the intended safety outcome.

### Hold time (lethality requirement)
The duration a product's coldest point must remain at or above a specified temperature to achieve the required pathogen kill, distinct from simply reaching that temperature momentarily.
**In use:** "Hitting 165 for an instant isn't the requirement — it's 165 held for fifteen seconds at the coldest point."
**Common misuse:** Treating "reached the target temperature" as equivalent to satisfying the lethality requirement — many requirements specify both a temperature and a hold duration at that temperature, and momentarily touching the target without holding it doesn't achieve the same pathogen reduction.

### Polar compounds / free fatty acid (oil degradation indicators)
Specific chemical measurements used to objectively assess frying oil degradation from repeated heating and use, providing a more reliable indicator than elapsed time or visual appearance.
**In use:** "Polar compound test came back above threshold — that's oil replacement time, regardless of how many hours it's technically been in service."
**Common misuse:** Using elapsed time in service or visual color/smell as a proxy for oil degradation — actual degradation rate depends on what's being cooked and usage intensity, and a specific chemical indicator is the reliable measure, not a generic time-based assumption.

### Batch parameter adjustment (incoming ingredient variation)
The practice of modifying cook process settings (time, temperature, mixing) based on incoming ingredient characteristics (moisture, viscosity, particle size) rather than holding settings fixed regardless of batch-to-batch variation.
**In use:** "This batch came in wetter than usual — adjusting cook time accordingly rather than running the standard process card unchanged."
**Common misuse:** Applying a fixed process recipe uniformly regardless of actual incoming batch characteristics — a process validated for one batch's moisture/composition can produce a different, potentially non-compliant result on a batch with meaningfully different starting characteristics.

### Sensory quality vs. food-safety lethality
The distinction between a product's taste, appearance, and texture (sensory quality) and whether it has actually achieved the required pathogen kill (food-safety lethality) — two separate measurements that don't confirm each other.
**In use:** "It tastes and looks perfect, but that doesn't tell us anything about whether the coldest point actually hit the lethality requirement — that's a separate check."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a product that passes sensory evaluation has also met its food-safety requirement — sensory quality and pathogen lethality are measured by entirely different methods, and a product can pass one while failing the other.

### Data-logging temperature probe
An instrument that records a continuous temperature-time profile during a cook cycle, as opposed to a single spot-check reading, providing documented proof of the actual lethality achieved.
**In use:** "Data logger shows the full time-temperature curve at the coldest point — that's the actual record, not just a snapshot reading at the end."
**Common misuse:** Relying on a single spot-check reading (often taken at the end of a cook cycle) rather than a continuous data log — a spot-check confirms a single moment in time, while lethality depends on the sustained time-at-temperature profile that only continuous logging captures.

### Sensor calibration (food process control)
The periodic verification that a temperature sensor's displayed reading matches an actual reference temperature, a prerequisite for trusting any temperature-based food safety control.
**In use:** "Sensor's due for calibration check — until that's confirmed, we can't fully trust the setpoint is delivering the actual intended temperature."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a temperature control system's displayed reading is accurate simply because the system is operating and displaying a value — sensor drift can produce a displayed reading that differs from actual product temperature, undermining the entire food-safety control without any visible indication of the problem.
