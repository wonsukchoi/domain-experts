### Draft survey
A cargo-quantity determination made by measuring a vessel's drafts before and after loading (or discharge), converting each draft to a displacement via the vessel's hydrostatic tables, and taking the difference net of deductibles.
**In use:** "The draft survey reconciled to 23,390 tonnes against a shore tally of 23,320 — 0.30% variance, within tolerance."
**Common misuse:** Treating "draft survey" as synonymous with simply reading the draft marks; the marks are one input, not the finished calculation, which still needs trim, list, and density corrections applied.

### Mean of means
The draft-reading method of averaging six readings — forward, midships, and aft, on both port and starboard — into a single representative draft, rather than relying on one point.
**In use:** "Don't sign off a mean of means built from only the midship readings; go back and get all six points."
**Common misuse:** Assuming any average of "a few" readings satisfies the method; skipping the aft or forward points understates the correction for trim.

### Ship's constant
The residual weight difference between a vessel's calculated light-ship displacement and its actual displacement when known cargo, fuel, water, and ballast are deducted — accumulated sediment, unlogged stores, and rounding error, tracked survey to survey.
**In use:** "The constant crept up 40 tonnes since the last independent survey — worth asking the chief officer what changed before accepting it."
**Common misuse:** Treating the constant as a fixed number that never needs re-verification; it drifts over a vessel's service life and a stale constant silently corrupts every subsequent draft survey.

### Ullage
The vertical distance from a reference point at the top of a tank down to the liquid surface, used to look up gross observed volume in the tank's calibration table.
**In use:** "Ullage came in at 2.145 meters — pull the calibration table at that figure, then apply the VCF for 78°F."
**Common misuse:** Confusing ullage with innage (liquid depth from the bottom); the two are read from opposite reference points and use different calibration table columns.

### VCF (Volume Correction Factor)
The API MPMS table factor that converts an observed liquid volume at its actual temperature to the 60°F standard volume used for certified quantity and commercial settlement.
**In use:** "Apply the 0.9887 VCF before you certify the outturn — the gross observed figure at 78°F isn't the contract quantity."
**Common misuse:** Reporting the gross observed volume as the certified figure because "the tank was gauged correctly," skipping the temperature correction step entirely.

### Outturn
The quantity of cargo actually delivered at discharge, as measured and certified — the figure a receiver's payment and any shortage claim are based on, as distinct from the quantity declared as loaded.
**In use:** "The outturn came in 0.4% short of the bill of lading figure — within the contractual franchise, so no claim triggers."
**Common misuse:** Using "outturn" loosely to mean any cargo quantity figure; it specifically refers to the discharge-side certified measurement, not the loading-side one.

### Letter of protest
A formal written notice placed on record at the time a discrepancy is found and cannot be resolved before custody transfers, reserving the surveyor's or party's position without accusing either side of fault.
**In use:** "File the letter of protest on the hold-cleaning certificate now — don't wait until after the vessel sails to raise it."
**Common misuse:** Treating a letter of protest as an accusation or a claim; it is a without-prejudice preservation of the issue, not a determination of liability.

### Mate's receipt / dock receipt exception
A notation entered on the vessel's or terminal's receiving document at the moment cargo condition or count is disputed, before the receipt is signed clean.
**In use:** "Get the exception noted on the mate's receipt before the hatch closes — a verbal heads-up to the chief mate isn't evidence."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a verbal notification to the receiving party is equivalent to a written exception on the receipt itself; only the written notation survives as evidence.

### Stowage factor
The volume a given cargo occupies per unit weight (e.g., cubic meters per tonne), used to plan how much of a hold's cubic capacity a cargo will fill relative to its weight.
**In use:** "This grain's stowage factor means the vessel will run out of cube before it runs out of deadweight — she won't load to her marks."
**Common misuse:** Confusing stowage factor with density; stowage factor is volume per weight (the inverse relationship) and already accounts for typical broken stowage/void space, which raw density does not.

### Dunnage
Material (wood, matting, tarpaulin, airbags) placed between or under cargo to protect it from contact damage, moisture, or contamination from a hold's structure or a previous cargo's residue.
**In use:** "The contamination in hold 4 traces back to dunnage that wasn't fully stripped after the last fertilizer cargo."
**Common misuse:** Treating dunnage inspection as optional when a hold has a clean certificate; a clean-swept hold can still have improperly removed dunnage from the prior cargo.

### Segregation (IMDG)
The IMO code's requirement that certain combinations of dangerous-goods classes be kept a minimum distance apart or in separate compartments/holds, based on a class-pair lookup table rather than a general "keep hazmat apart" rule.
**In use:** "Class 3 and Class 5.1 need full separation below deck — check the table for the exact pair before approving this stow plan."
**Common misuse:** Assuming any two placarded dangerous-goods containers are compliant as long as each is individually placarded correctly; segregation is a between-container requirement independent of each container's own compliance.

### Aggregate working load limit (WLL)
The combined rated capacity of all tiedowns securing an article, which must meet a required fraction of the article's weight (commonly at least half, under 49 CFR 393.106) — evaluated as a total, not per tiedown.
**In use:** "Two tiedowns are tagged and good, but the third's tag is gone — recompute the aggregate without it before you clear this load."
**Common misuse:** Approving securement because each individual tiedown "looks strong enough," without summing rated capacities and comparing the total against the required fraction of cargo weight.

### Franchise (cargo insurance)
A stated minimum shortage or damage percentage below which no claim is payable, distinct from a deductible (which reduces every claim by a fixed amount regardless of size).
**In use:** "A 0.4% shortage is inside the policy's 0.5% franchise — the receiver has no claim on this outturn."
**Common misuse:** Conflating franchise with deductible; a franchise voids the entire claim below the threshold, while a deductible still pays a claim above it, just reduced.

### Certificate of quantity
The signed document stating the surveyor's certified cargo quantity, backed by the reconciled calculation, distinct from a bill of lading (a shipper's/carrier's contractual receipt that may simply adopt the surveyor's figure).
**In use:** "The bill of lading quantity was issued off our certificate of quantity, not the shore tally, per the charter party's clause 12."
**Common misuse:** Treating the bill of lading figure as independently verified cargo fact; it commonly just restates whichever measurement source the contract designates, which may or may not be the surveyor's own reconciled number.
