### Draft-survey vs. shore-tally variance greater than 0.5%
- **Usually means:** a draft misread (wrong point, list/trim uncorrected), a stale or wrong hydrostatic table for current loading condition, or a shore scale calibration lapse — in roughly that order of likelihood.
- **First question:** "Were all six draft points re-read after the discrepancy showed up, or is this still the first pass?"
- **Data to pull:** the raw draft log (all six readings, not just the mean), the vessel's current hydrostatic tables and deadweight scale, and the shore scale's last calibration certificate.

### Tank ullage recorded without a paired temperature reading
- **Usually means:** the gauger skipped the VCF correction step, which will misstate the certified volume the moment it's converted.
- **First question:** "What temperature was the product at when this ullage was taken, and is that logged on the same line?"
- **Data to pull:** the tank gauge sheet's temperature column and the API MPMS VCF table used for that product's API gravity.

### Hold-cleaning or "fit to load" certificate presented with no independent inspection before loading starts
- **Usually means:** the terminal or previous charterer's crew certified the hold from a routine wash-down, not a hold-specific inspection for this cargo's contamination sensitivity.
- **First question:** "Was this hold walked and inspected by this survey, or is the certificate being accepted on its face?"
- **Data to pull:** the hold-cleaning certificate's issuer and date, and this inspection's own photographs of hold condition before loading.

### Dangerous-goods placard present and correct, but no segregation check performed against adjacent stow
- **Usually means:** the packer or stevedore confirmed the individual container's compliance and stopped there, missing that segregation is a between-container rule.
- **First question:** "What's stowed within the segregation distance on every side of this container, and what class is it?"
- **Data to pull:** the stowage plan, the IMDG segregation table for the specific class pair, and the measured or plan-scaled distance between the containers in question.

### Load height measured with no margin against the lowest posted route clearance
- **Usually means:** the measurement was taken and compared directly to the posted number, with no allowance for pavement resurfacing, tire pressure loss, or an off-level measurement surface.
- **First question:** "What margin was applied below the posted clearance, and was the load measured on level ground?"
- **Data to pull:** the route survey listing every posted clearance below the load's measured height, and the ground condition/level noted at time of measurement.

### A tiedown's WLL tag is missing, illegible, or clearly damaged
- **Usually means:** the tiedown is being counted toward the aggregate working-load-limit total by its apparent condition rather than its documented rating, which the rule does not allow.
- **First question:** "If this tiedown counts as zero, does the remaining aggregate still clear the required half-weight threshold?"
- **Data to pull:** the WLL tags on every remaining tiedown and the article's actual weight (bill of lading or scale ticket, not an estimate).

### Damage or shortage noted only after cargo has been fully discharged and commingled
- **Usually means:** the boundary-moment evidence — the condition at the exact point custody changed — no longer exists, and any later claim will rest on circumstantial timing rather than a contemporaneous record.
- **First question:** "What was documented — photographs, measurements, a signed exception — at the moment the damage was first observed, before further handling?"
- **Data to pull:** time-stamped photographs, the mate's receipt or dock receipt exception line, and the discharge sequence log showing when in the unload this cargo came off.

### Ship's constant increased significantly from the vessel's last recorded voyage
- **Usually means:** accumulated sediment, unrecorded stores, or an error carried forward from a prior survey rather than an actual physical change to the vessel.
- **First question:** "What was this vessel's constant on its last independent survey, and what explains the difference?"
- **Data to pull:** the vessel's survey history for the constant trend over its last several voyages.

### A clean survey report is signed to avoid delaying departure despite an unresolved discrepancy
- **Usually means:** commercial pressure at the berth is being allowed to override the evidentiary purpose of the report — once signed clean, the discrepancy has no paper trail to support a later claim.
- **First question:** "Is this discrepancy actually resolved, or is it being left off the report to keep the vessel or truck moving?"
- **Data to pull:** the specific reconciliation figures that remain open, and whether a letter of protest was drafted and simply not issued.

### Density used for a draft-survey water-density correction is taken from a table default rather than measured
- **Usually means:** the surveyor skipped taking an actual hydrometer or density-meter reading of the water at the vessel's location, using a generic seawater or freshwater assumption instead.
- **First question:** "Was the water density at this berth actually measured today, or assumed?"
- **Data to pull:** the hydrometer reading and sample location/time, and how far it differs from the assumed table value.

### A dangerous-goods manifest lists a UN number that doesn't match the placard displayed
- **Usually means:** a placard leftover from a previous load, a data-entry error on the manifest, or a genuine mislabeling at the point of packing.
- **First question:** "Does the shipping paper's UN number and hazard class match what's physically placarded on this unit, exactly?"
- **Data to pull:** the shipping paper/manifest and a direct photograph of the placard as displayed.
