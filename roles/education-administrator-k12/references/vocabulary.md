# K-12 administration working vocabulary

Terms an administrator uses precisely. Format: definition → how a practitioner says it → common misuse.

**Precedent test** — Checking a proposed individual decision (discipline, accommodation, schedule exception) against how comparable past cases were actually resolved, to catch inconsistency before it accumulates into a fairness problem.
In use: "Before we finalize this suspension length, let's run the precedent test against the last dozen comparable cases in the discipline log."
Misuse: treating each case as fully unique with no reference to past comparable decisions, which is exactly how individually-reasonable decisions accumulate into an inconsistent, unfair-looking pattern over time.

**Disproportionality** — A statistically meaningful gap between a demographic group's share of a population (e.g., enrollment) and its share of an outcome (e.g., discipline, special education referral), used as a flag for policy-level review regardless of any single decision's intent.
In use: "The disproportionality in mutual-combat suspensions — 22% of enrollment, 41% of suspensions — needs a policy review, separate from how well any individual case was handled."
Misuse: dismissing a disproportionality finding by pointing to individual cases being handled fairly; the pattern is a system-level question that individual case fairness doesn't resolve on its own.

**Restorative conference / restorative practice** — A structured process bringing affected parties together to address harm and repair relationships, used as an alternative or complement to purely punitive discipline, particularly where there's an ongoing relationship (classmates, peers) that punitive-only discipline doesn't address.
In use: "The consequence includes a mandatory restorative conference, not just the suspension — the goal is addressing the underlying conflict, not only the rule violation."
Misuse: using "restorative" language while the actual process remains purely punitive with no structured conversation or repair component — restorative practice refers to a specific process, not a softer label for the same consequence.

**PBIS (Positive Behavioral Interventions and Supports)** — A tiered, proactive framework for teaching and reinforcing expected behavior school-wide, aimed at preventing behavioral issues and ensuring consistent response, rather than a purely reactive discipline system.
In use: "Our PBIS tier-2 supports kick in after the second documented incident — this is a school-wide framework, not an ad hoc response."
Misuse: applying discipline consequences inconsistently while claiming to "have PBIS," when PBIS specifically requires the consistency and tiered-response structure that inconsistent case-by-case handling undermines.

**IEP (Individualized Education Program) vs. 504 Plan** — An IEP is a legally binding plan for a student who qualifies for special education services under IDEA, including specific goals and services; a 504 Plan provides accommodations for a student with a disability who doesn't require specialized instruction but needs equal access — distinct legal frameworks with different requirements.
In use: "This student has a 504 for extended test time, not an IEP — different legal basis, different compliance obligations."
Misuse: using "IEP" and "504" interchangeably; they're governed by different laws (IDEA vs. Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act) with different procedural requirements and protections.

**Containment vs. investigation (incident response)** — Containment is the immediate safety action taken with available information (separating students, addressing immediate needs); investigation is the fuller, slower process of understanding what happened and why, which shouldn't be compressed to match containment's urgency.
In use: "Containment is done — both students are safe and separated. The investigation into what actually led to this will take another day or two before we finalize consequences."
Misuse: treating the disciplinary determination as part of containment and rushing it to match containment's appropriate urgency, before the investigation that should inform it is actually complete.

**Working-conditions survey** — A structured survey of teacher perceptions of workload, support, and school climate, used as a leading indicator connected to student outcomes rather than a standalone HR satisfaction metric.
In use: "The working-conditions survey flagged workload as the top concern three quarters running — that's the leading indicator behind the turnover we're now seeing."
Misuse: treating working-conditions survey results as a separate HR concern disconnected from student outcome planning, missing that teacher conditions are frequently upstream of the same outcomes.

**Compliance floor vs. quality target** — A compliance floor is the minimum required by law/district/state policy (a report filed, a ratio met); a quality target is a genuine measure of whether students are actually being well served — meeting the floor doesn't establish the target is met.
In use: "We're compliant on the reporting requirement, but that's the floor — the actual quality question is whether the intervention is producing reading gains, which is a separate check."
Misuse: citing compliance completion as if it answers the quality question, when the two require different evidence and different evaluation lenses.

**Mandatory reporter** — A legal designation (applying to most school staff) requiring the reporting of suspected child abuse or neglect to authorities, with specific legal obligations and protections distinct from general disciplinary or family-communication judgment calls.
In use: "This isn't a discretionary disclosure decision — as mandatory reporters, we're required to report this to child protective services regardless of how the family might react."
Misuse: treating a mandatory-reporting situation as subject to the same discretionary judgment used for other family-communication decisions; mandatory reporting has specific legal requirements that override normal discretion.

**Least restrictive environment (LRE)** — The IDEA principle that students with disabilities should be educated alongside non-disabled peers to the maximum extent appropriate, with removal to a more restrictive setting only when the student's needs can't be met in the general setting even with supports.
In use: "The IEP team needs to document why the general classroom with supports isn't sufficient before considering a more restrictive placement — that's the LRE requirement."
Misuse: defaulting to a more restrictive placement for administrative convenience without the documented justification LRE requires, which is both a legal compliance issue and an educational one.
