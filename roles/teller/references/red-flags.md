# Teller — Red Flags

### Same customer's cash transactions total more than $10,000 in one business day, across any window/branch
- **Usually means:** Legitimate large transaction (real estate closing, business payroll, large purchase) — but crosses the mandatory CTR-filing threshold regardless of legitimacy.
- **First question:** Has this customer had any other cash transactions today, at this or another branch?
- **Data to pull:** Same-day cross-transaction lookup in the core banking system.

### Two or more transactions from the same customer, each just under $10,000, within a short window (same day or consecutive days)
- **Usually means:** Deliberate structuring to avoid CTR filing — a federal offense regardless of the money's legitimacy — though occasionally coincidental (e.g., customer genuinely needed exactly $9,500 twice for unrelated reasons).
- **First question:** Does the customer's stated purpose or behavior suggest awareness of the $10,000 threshold (e.g., asking how much can be withdrawn "without paperwork")?
- **Data to pull:** 7-day and 30-day transaction history for the account.

### Repeated deposit-then-immediate-large-withdrawal cycling between two accounts before checks clear
- **Usually means:** Check kiting — using the float between accounts to create the appearance of funds that don't exist.
- **First question:** Is the deposited check drawn on an account at another institution, and has it actually cleared before the withdrawal?
- **Data to pull:** Check-hold status and clearing history for the deposited items.

### Customer presents currency with an off color, texture, or missing security feature under UV light
- **Usually means:** Counterfeit currency — sometimes knowingly passed, often unknowingly received by the customer from an earlier transaction.
- **First question:** Can the specific security feature (watermark, security thread, color-shifting ink) be verified under proper lighting?
- **Data to pull:** N/A — direct physical verification using counterfeit-detection tools.

### Elderly customer requests an unusually large withdrawal, with an unfamiliar companion answering questions on their behalf
- **Usually means:** Possible elder financial exploitation — though could also be a legitimate caregiver or family member assisting with genuine needs.
- **First question:** Asked privately, without the companion present: does the customer understand and want this specific transaction?
- **Data to pull:** Account's prior transaction pattern (typical withdrawal size/frequency) and any power-of-attorney documentation on file.

### Check deposit with an endorsement that doesn't match the payee name, or a payee name altered/added
- **Usually means:** Forged or fraudulently altered endorsement, or a legitimate but improperly executed third-party endorsement.
- **First question:** Does the endorsement chain match the payee exactly, and if not, is there a properly executed assignment or POA?
- **Data to pull:** Original check image and the account's endorsement-exception history.

### Drawer variance (over or short) exceeds the branch's materiality threshold at end of shift
- **Usually means:** Counting error (most common), a shorted or overpaid customer transaction, or — rarely — till manipulation.
- **First question:** Which transactions over the materiality threshold haven't been individually re-verified against their slips?
- **Data to pull:** Shift transaction log sorted by dollar amount.

### Customer requests immediate full availability on a large, out-of-state, or first-time deposit
- **Usually means:** Legitimate urgency, but this is precisely the profile Regulation CC's exception-hold categories (new account, large deposit, reasonable cause) exist to cover.
- **First question:** Does the account and deposit type qualify for standard next-day availability, or does it fall under an exception category?
- **Data to pull:** Account open date and the deposit-category hold schedule.

### Customer's transaction pattern shifts abruptly from small/routine to large/frequent with no stated life event
- **Usually means:** Could indicate the account is being used to move funds for someone else, a scam in progress (e.g., romance scam, tech-support scam), or a legitimate but unexplained change.
- **First question:** Does the customer's explanation for the change match observable facts (destination of funds, urgency, any third-party pressure)?
- **Data to pull:** 90-day transaction history compared against the new pattern.
