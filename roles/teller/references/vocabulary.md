# Teller — Vocabulary

### CTR (Currency Transaction Report)
A federal report filed when a customer's cash transactions (deposits, withdrawals, exchanges) total more than $10,000 in a single business day at one financial institution.
**In use:** "Between the morning withdrawal and the one this afternoon, this account's over the CTR line for today."
**Common misuse:** Treating the $10,000 threshold as per-transaction rather than an aggregate across all of that customer's same-day cash activity.

### SAR (Suspicious Activity Report)
A confidential report filed with FinCEN when a transaction pattern suggests possible money laundering, fraud, or other illegal activity, regardless of dollar amount.
**In use:** "The structuring pattern goes in a SAR, not just a CTR — the transactions themselves are still legal and get processed."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a SAR is only for transactions that get refused or blocked; a SAR is filed *about* activity, the underlying transaction is usually still completed normally.

### Structuring
Deliberately breaking a large cash transaction into smaller pieces specifically to stay under the $10,000 CTR-reporting threshold — a federal crime under 31 U.S.C. § 5324 independent of whether the underlying funds are legitimate.
**In use:** "Two $9,000 withdrawals in two days from an account with no other activity is a textbook structuring pattern."
**Common misuse:** Assuming structuring requires the money to be illegally obtained — the crime is evading the reporting requirement, not the source of funds.

### Kiting
Exploiting the time delay ("float") between depositing a check and it actually clearing to create the appearance of available funds that don't really exist, typically by cycling deposits and withdrawals between two or more accounts.
**In use:** "Depositing from Account A and immediately pulling most of it out before the check clears, then doing the reverse next week — that's a kiting pattern."
**Common misuse:** Confusing kiting with simple overdrafting; kiting specifically relies on the deposit-to-clearing time gap and typically involves multiple accounts.

### Regulation CC
The Federal Reserve regulation governing how quickly banks must make deposited funds available to customers, setting maximum hold periods by deposit category.
**In use:** "This is an out-of-state check on a new account, so Reg CC lets us hold it longer than a standard local check."
**Common misuse:** Treating Reg CC's maximum hold periods as the required hold — banks can (and often do) make funds available faster than the regulation requires; it's a ceiling, not a target.

### Funds availability
The date on which deposited funds become legally accessible to the customer, determined by the deposit's category under Regulation CC.
**In use:** "Funds availability on this deposit is the second business day, not today."
**Common misuse:** Confusing "the check has been deposited" with "the funds are available" — a deposit can be accepted and still be on hold.

### Over/short
The difference between a teller's physical cash count at end of shift and what the transaction log says should be in the drawer; "over" means more cash than expected, "short" means less.
**In use:** "The drawer's $180 over — that's not free money, it usually means a customer got shorted somewhere."
**Common misuse:** Assuming an overage is harmless because it's in the bank's favor — an overage often means a *customer* was underpaid, which is a problem even though the drawer looks fine.

### Reasonable-cause exception
A Regulation CC provision allowing a bank to hold funds longer than the standard schedule when it has a documented, specific reason to doubt the deposit will be honored (e.g., suspected fraud, a check that's been redeposited after bouncing, an account with a history of overdrafts).
**In use:** "This customer's had two bounced deposits this month — that qualifies for a reasonable-cause hold, but we have to give them written notice why."
**Common misuse:** Applying an extended hold based on a hunch without a documented, disclosed reason — the exception requires both a qualifying cause and customer notification.

### Elder financial exploitation
The illegal or improper use of an older adult's funds, property, or assets, often by someone in a position of trust; in banking contexts, frequently flagged through changes in transaction pattern rather than any single transaction.
**In use:** "The pattern shift — decades of small transactions, suddenly large withdrawals with a new companion present — is what triggered the internal referral, not any one withdrawal."
**Common misuse:** Treating the presence of a caregiver or family member during a transaction as itself suspicious; the signal is a *change* from the customer's own established pattern, not assistance in general.

### Endorsement
The signature (and any restrictive language) a payee adds to the back of a check to transfer or restrict its negotiation; required to match the payee for standard deposit processing.
**In use:** "The endorsement doesn't match the payee name on the front — we need a properly executed third-party assignment before this can be deposited."
**Common misuse:** Treating any mismatch as automatically fraudulent rather than checking for a legitimate but improperly formatted third-party endorsement or POA.

### BSA/AML (Bank Secrecy Act / Anti-Money Laundering)
The federal regulatory framework requiring financial institutions to monitor for and report signs of money laundering and other financial crimes, including CTR and SAR filing obligations.
**In use:** "That escalation goes to the BSA/AML officer, not the branch manager — different reporting chain."
**Common misuse:** Treating BSA/AML compliance as the branch manager's job; most institutions route these escalations to a dedicated compliance officer or team, separate from day-to-day branch management.

### Materiality threshold
A branch- or institution-set dollar amount above which a drawer variance requires investigation and/or manager notification, rather than being logged and accepted.
**In use:** "Anything under our $50 materiality threshold gets logged; anything over gets a callback to whoever might've been affected."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a universal dollar figure applies everywhere — materiality thresholds are set per institution/branch, not by regulation.
