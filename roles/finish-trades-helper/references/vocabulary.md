# Finish Trades Helper — Vocabulary

### Boxing
Intermixing multiple cans of the same paint color — especially across different batch/lot numbers — into one larger container before application, to eliminate visible color drift between cans.
**In use:** "We're on can three of the same color but a different lot — box it into the five-gallon before it goes in the sprayer."
**Common misuse:** Treating boxing as optional busywork for "the same color anyway" — batch-to-batch tint drift is real and shows up as a flash line only after the wall dries, when it's too late to blend.

### RRP Rule / lead-safe work practices
EPA's Renovation, Repair, and Painting Rule (40 CFR Part 745, Subpart E) requiring containment, restricted removal methods, and HEPA cleanup whenever prep on a pre-1978 structure disturbs more than 6 sq ft of interior painted surface or 20 sq ft of exterior.
**In use:** "House was built in '71 — we're RRP no matter how small this patch looks."
**Common misuse:** Assuming the threshold applies per job rather than per room (interior) or per elevation (exterior) — a series of "small" patches across several rooms can each individually trigger the rule.

### Containment / critical barrier
The physical control (poly sheeting, taped seams, negative-pressure setup on larger jobs) that keeps lead dust or debris inside a defined work area during RRP-triggered prep.
**In use:** "Containment's up to the property line — nothing gets scraped until the poly's taped down."
**Common misuse:** Treating a drop cloth as equivalent to RRP containment — a drop cloth catches debris that falls straight down; containment is sized and sealed to control airborne dust, a different standard.

### Working life (cement plaster)
The window during which mixed portland-cement plaster mud can still be applied before it begins to stiffen; unlike mortar, it is not retempered once stiffening starts.
**In use:** "That batch is past working life — don't add water to it, mix fresh."
**Common misuse:** Applying mortar's retemper-once rule to plaster mud — the materials behave differently once past their window, and treating them the same produces a coat that debonds later.

### Scratch coat / brown coat / finish coat
The three layers of traditional portland-cement plaster/stucco: scratch (base, mechanically scored for the next coat to key into), brown (leveling layer), and finish (thin decorative topcoat).
**In use:** "Scratch coat's done — don't touch it again until the 48-hour cure clears for brown."
**Common misuse:** Referring to any single coat as "the stucco" — each coat has its own mix, thickness, and cure requirement, and conflating them skips a cure-time check that matters.

### Booking
Folding the pasted faces of a wallcovering strip together and letting it sit briefly so the adhesive activates evenly before hanging.
**In use:** "Book it five minutes before you bring it to the wall — don't rush straight from the paste table."
**Common misuse:** Skipping booking on pre-pasted material because "it's already got paste on it" — unbooked paste activates unevenly and produces edge lift even though paste is technically present.

### Tack time / open time (paste)
The window after pasting during which a wallcovering strip can still be positioned and adjusted before the adhesive grabs and the strip is set.
**In use:** "You've got maybe ten minutes of open time on this one — get it hung before it skins."
**Common misuse:** Confusing tack time with drying time — tack time is about repositioning; full dry/cure is a separate, much longer clock that governs when the seam can be trusted.

### Cutting in
Painting a precise edge freehand (at a ceiling line, trim, or corner) with a brush, without masking tape, ahead of or instead of rolling/spraying the field.
**In use:** "Cut in the ceiling line first, then I'll roll the field before the cut edge dries."
**Common misuse:** Assuming cutting in is a beginner shortcut around masking — it's a distinct skill the mechanic performs, and a helper's job is staging (clean brush, right sheen boxed and ready), not attempting the cut line.

### Spread rate
The area a given volume of coating covers at its specified film thickness, usually stated in sq ft per gallon on the product's technical data sheet.
**In use:** "Data sheet says 375 sq ft/gallon for this primer — that's what the order quantity is based on, not the bucket's round-number label."
**Common misuse:** Using a generic "400 sq ft/gallon" rule of thumb across products — spread rate varies by product and substrate porosity, and using the wrong number under-orders material mid-job.

### Dry film thickness (DFT)
The thickness of a coating once cured, as opposed to wet film thickness (WFT) measured immediately after application; the number the product's rated performance (durability, coverage warranty) is actually based on.
**In use:** "One coat gets us to half the rated DFT — that's why the spec calls for two."
**Common misuse:** Assuming an even, opaque-looking coat means spec DFT was hit — opacity can be reached well below the film thickness a product needs for its rated service life.

### Furring / lath
The metal mesh (lath) fastened to a wall assembly, sometimes over furring strips or channels to create an air gap, that gives portland-cement plaster something to mechanically key into.
**In use:** "Lath's up and inspected — scratch coat can start once the WRB's confirmed behind it."
**Common misuse:** Treating lath installation as the helper's or plasterer's call to make — fastener spacing and furring requirements are set by ASTM C1063 and typically installed and inspected before the plastering crew's scope even starts.

### Feathering
Blending the edge of a fresh coat (paint, joint compound, or plaster) into the surrounding surface so the transition isn't visible once dry.
**In use:** "Feather that patch out another six inches or the repair's going to flash under raking light."
**Common misuse:** Treating feathering as purely cosmetic sanding after the fact — it's a technique applied while the material is still workable, not a fix applied after it's set.
