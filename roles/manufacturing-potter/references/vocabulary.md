# Vocabulary

### Total shrinkage (ceramics)
The combined dimensional reduction a clay piece undergoes from wet-formed state through drying and firing, which must be compensated for in wet-form dimensions to hit a target fired size.
**In use:** "Total shrinkage on this body is 12% — that's what the wet-form dimension has to be calculated against."
**Common misuse:** Forming a piece to the exact target fired dimension without accounting for total shrinkage, or using only a drying-shrinkage or only a firing-shrinkage figure rather than the combined total.

### Glaze fit
The compatibility between a glaze's thermal expansion/contraction behavior and the clay body it's applied to, determining whether the fired result is free of crazing or shivering.
**In use:** "This glaze tests fine on its own, but check documented fit against this specific body before committing the run."
**Common misuse:** Selecting a glaze based on color or surface finish alone without verifying documented fit to the specific clay body being used.

### Crazing
A glaze defect appearing as a network of fine cracks, caused by the glaze contracting less than the underlying clay body during cooling — a glaze-fit mismatch.
**In use:** "That's crazing, not a firing-temperature issue — the glaze's expansion doesn't match this body."
**Common misuse:** Attributing crazing to firing temperature or technique alone, when it's specifically a thermal-expansion mismatch between glaze and body.

### Shivering
A glaze defect where the glaze flakes or peels away from the body, caused by the glaze contracting more than the underlying clay body during cooling — the opposite mismatch from crazing.
**In use:** "Shivering along the rim points to the glaze contracting more than the body, not a poor application technique."
**Common misuse:** Confusing shivering with simple poor glaze adhesion from application technique, when it specifically results from the glaze's contraction exceeding the body's.

### Quartz inversion
A rapid, reversible volume change in silica-containing ceramic bodies occurring around 573°C, requiring a slower firing ramp rate through that specific temperature range to avoid thermal-stress cracking.
**In use:** "Slow the ramp rate way down through quartz inversion — that's where cracking risk spikes regardless of the final peak temperature."
**Common misuse:** Applying a uniform ramp rate across the entire firing cycle without slowing specifically through this and other critical temperature ranges.

### Bisque firing
A first, lower-temperature firing of formed clay ware before glaze application, which sets the body's porosity and handling strength for the glazing step.
**In use:** "Bisque fire first, then glaze — the body's porosity after bisque is what makes the glaze application actually work."
**Common misuse:** Treating bisque firing as an optional or combinable step with glaze firing, rather than a distinct process stage with its own specific purpose.

### Pyrometric cone
A small ceramic device formulated to deform at a specific combination of temperature and time, used to verify actual heat-work delivered during a firing, independent of the kiln controller's temperature readout.
**In use:** "Controller shows peak temp reached, but check the cone — that's the real confirmation of heat-work delivered."
**Common misuse:** Relying on the kiln controller's peak temperature display alone to confirm a firing's completeness, without cone verification of actual heat-work delivered.

### Heat-work
The combined effect of both temperature and time on a ceramic body or glaze's development during firing, as opposed to peak temperature alone.
**In use:** "Same peak temp, but a longer hold means more heat-work delivered — that's why the cone bent further this time."
**Common misuse:** Assuming reaching a specific peak temperature number is sufficient regardless of ramp rate or hold time, when heat-work — the combination of both — is what actually determines the ceramic result.

### Clay body
A specific formulated clay composition, engineered for particular working, shrinkage, and firing characteristics, as opposed to a single raw clay.
**In use:** "This body's documented shrinkage is different from the last one we ran — don't reuse the old wet-form dimensions."
**Common misuse:** Assuming all clay bodies behave identically in shrinkage or firing response, treating "clay" as a single generic material rather than a specific formulated composition requiring its own documented characteristics.

### Vitrification
The degree to which a ceramic body has fused into a dense, glass-like, low-porosity state during firing, governed by heat-work reaching the body's specific maturation point.
**In use:** "This body vitrifies at cone 6 — firing to a different peak temperature without matching heat-work won't get there."
**Common misuse:** Assuming any firing to a "high enough" temperature achieves adequate vitrification, without reference to the specific body's documented maturation heat-work requirement.
