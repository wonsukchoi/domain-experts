# Mental Health and Substance Abuse Social Worker — Vocabulary

### Co-occurring disorder vs. dual diagnosis
**Dual diagnosis** simply names that a client carries both a mental-health and a substance-use diagnosis. **Co-occurring disorder**, in this role's usage, implies the severity-quadrant classification that determines treatment integration — the term that actually drives a decision, not just a label.
**In use:** "He's dual diagnosis, but Quadrant II — his depression is severe and his drinking is mild, so mental health leads with SUD consultation, not an integrated program."
**Common misuse:** Treating "dual diagnosis" as automatically meaning integrated treatment is required — only the high-severity/high-severity quadrant does.

### Civil commitment vs. voluntary admission vs. involuntary hold
**Civil commitment** is a court-ordered, judicially reviewed involuntary confinement for treatment. An **involuntary hold** (state names vary — e.g., a short statutory hold) is a shorter, clinician-initiated emergency detention that precedes a commitment hearing. A **voluntary admission** a client cannot actually leave despite the "voluntary" label is a specific documented failure, not a lesser form of commitment.
**In use:** "We placed her on the 72-hour hold, but the commitment petition still needs the dated evidence for the hearing on day three."
**Common misuse:** Calling any involuntary-feeling admission a "commitment" — commitment specifically means the judicially reviewed, longer-term legal status.

### 42 CFR Part 2 vs. HIPAA
**Part 2** is a federal regulation specific to SUD treatment records that requires purpose-specific consent for redisclosure and restricts use in legal proceedings against the client. **HIPAA** is the general health-privacy law; a HIPAA authorization does not satisfy Part 2's stricter consent requirements.
**In use:** "That's a Part 2 record — I need a separate consent naming this recipient and purpose, the HIPAA release on file doesn't cover it."
**Common misuse:** Assuming any signed release authorizes sharing SUD treatment information, or assuming Part 2 and HIPAA impose the same bar.

### Grave disability
A statutory civil-commitment criterion: current inability to provide for one's own basic needs (food, clothing, shelter) *as a result of* a mental disorder — not homelessness or poverty on their own.
**In use:** "Housing status alone doesn't establish grave disability — I need to document that the inability to secure shelter traces to the psychotic symptoms, not just unemployment."
**Common misuse:** Citing homelessness or visible hardship as sufficient evidence without establishing the causal link to the disorder.

### Stage of change
Prochaska and DiClicente's Transtheoretical Model stage (precontemplation, contemplation, preparation, action, maintenance) describing a client's actual readiness to change a behavior, assessed from behavior, not stated intent.
**In use:** "He says he wants to quit, but no attempt, no reduction, no plan — that's contemplation, not action, and the program intensity needs to match that."
**Common misuse:** Inferring stage of change from what a client says rather than what they've actually done.

### ASAM Criteria dimensions
The American Society of Addiction Medicine's six-dimension multidimensional assessment (intoxication/withdrawal, biomedical, emotional/behavioral/cognitive, readiness, relapse potential, recovery environment) used to justify a level-of-care placement — not a single severity number.
**In use:** "Dimension 5 is what's driving this to residential — no prior treatment episode and a year of daily use with zero established coping skills."
**Common misuse:** Reducing ASAM to a single overall "score" rather than a profile across six dimensions, where the highest-severity dimension often sets the floor for level of care.

### Warm handoff vs. referral
A **warm handoff** is a direct, confirmed, real-time transfer of clinical information and responsibility to the receiving provider. A **referral** is a one-way transmission of contact information or a document, with no confirmation the receiving side has acted.
**In use:** "This needs a warm handoff given his risk flags — a portal referral alone leaves a gap until someone on their end actually picks it up."
**Common misuse:** Treating a completed referral (fax sent, portal submission made) as equivalent to a handoff, which requires two-way confirmation.

### Least-restrictive alternative
The lowest-intensity intervention — outpatient safety planning, voluntary admission, crisis respite — that can adequately address the documented risk, required to be considered and ruled out before pursuing commitment.
**In use:** "Before filing the petition, document why a safety plan and voluntary admission weren't sufficient given the evidence — that's the field a hearing officer asks for first."
**Common misuse:** Treating commitment as the default response to any risk finding rather than the last option after less-restrictive alternatives are documented as inadequate.

### Biopsychosocial-spiritual assessment
A social-work-specific intake framework extending the standard biopsychosocial model to include spiritual/cultural meaning-making and support systems, distinct from a purely clinical mental-status or diagnostic interview.
**In use:** "The biopsychosocial-spiritual picks up that his faith community is his primary relapse-prevention support — that doesn't show up in a mental-status exam."
**Common misuse:** Skipping the spiritual/cultural dimension as optional rather than treating it as a standard component that surfaces real protective factors.

### Assertive Community Treatment (ACT) vs. standard case management
**ACT** is a fidelity-defined, team-based, high-intensity model (staff-to-client ratio near 1:10, 24/7 availability) for clients with severe and persistent mental illness and repeated hospitalizations. **Standard case management** is lower-intensity (1:25–40) coordination for more stable clients.
**In use:** "Two hospitalizations this year plus unstable housing — time to evaluate ACT eligibility, standard case management isn't matching his acuity."
**Common misuse:** Calling any intensive-sounding coordination "ACT" without the actual team structure, ratio, and fidelity criteria behind it.

### CIWA-Ar vs. COWS
**CIWA-Ar** (Clinical Institute Withdrawal Assessment for Alcohol, revised) scores alcohol withdrawal severity. **COWS** (Clinical Opiate Withdrawal Scale) scores opioid withdrawal severity — the two scales are not interchangeable and score different symptom clusters.
**In use:** "CIWA-Ar is 3, she's clear for alcohol withdrawal — but he's on COWS for the opioid piece, and that's still moderate at 15."
**Common misuse:** Using one scale's cutoffs to reason about the other substance's withdrawal risk.

### Quadrant model
The SAMHSA co-occurring-disorder framework classifying clients into four quadrants by crossing mental-health severity with substance-use severity, used to determine whether integrated treatment is required (Quadrant IV) or single-system management with cross-consultation suffices (Quadrants I–III).
**In use:** "She's Quadrant III — substance use is severe but the depression is mild, so SUD treatment leads with mental-health consultation, not the reverse."
**Common misuse:** Applying the quadrant model as a diagnosis-count exercise rather than a severity-based routing decision.
