# Vocabulary

Terms generalists reliably misuse. Format per term: definition, a sentence a practitioner would actually say, the common misuse.

### Copy cataloging vs. original cataloging
Copy cataloging attaches an existing bibliographic record (usually from OCLC/WorldCat) to a local item after verifying it's a true match. Original cataloging creates a new record from scratch when no acceptable match exists.
**In use:** "That self-published title has no OCLC record at all — this is an original-cataloging job, route it to the librarian, not a copy-cataloging shortcut."
**Common misuse:** treating "close enough" as copy cataloging. A title match without an ISBN/pagination/edition check isn't copy cataloging — it's a guess wearing copy cataloging's name.

### Encoding level (MARC leader, byte 17)
A single-character code in the MARC record's leader indicating how complete and authoritative the record is — blank/1 (full, national-library quality) down through 3, 4, 5, 7, 8 (abbreviated, core, partial/preliminary, minimal, prepublication).
**In use:** "It's encoding level 7 — minimal-level record — so I want the ISBN and pagination to match exactly before I trust it over doing this one from scratch."
**Common misuse:** ignoring encoding level entirely and judging a record's reliability by how many holdings libraries are attached to it. A high-holdings record can still be a low-encoding-level record that many libraries accepted without checking.

### Call number vs. classification number
The classification number (from LCC or DDC) places an item within a subject hierarchy; the call number is the classification number plus a Cutter number (and often a location prefix) that makes the specific item's shelf address unique.
**In use:** "The classification number puts it in the right range for American fiction, but I still need to check the Cutter table so it doesn't collide with an existing spine on the shelf."
**Common misuse:** using the two terms interchangeably, which causes confusion when a shelving problem is actually a Cutter collision, not a wrong subject classification.

### Holdings (OCLC) vs. holding (local circulation status)
An OCLC holding is a flag on a WorldCat record indicating a given library owns a copy — used for resource sharing and ILL routing. A local "holding" or item record is the specific physical copy with its own barcode, location, and circulation status.
**In use:** "We have an OCLC holding attached to that record, but the local item record shows it's actually missing — pull the holding until we either find it or reorder."
**Common misuse:** assuming an OCLC holding guarantees the item is actually on the shelf and available; it only guarantees someone once cataloged a copy.

### Claim (serials)
A formal notice to a serials vendor or publisher that an expected issue didn't arrive, requesting a free replacement within the vendor's claim-eligibility window.
**In use:** "It's day 47 past expected arrival — that's inside the claim window, file it today rather than waiting for next month's check-in."
**Common misuse:** treating a claim as an escalation or complaint rather than routine, low-stakes paperwork; delaying it "to be sure it's really missing" is the behavior that actually causes losses, since the window closes on a calendar, not on certainty.

### IFM (Interlibrary Loan Fee Management) / lending fee
OCLC's system for billing and crediting lending fees between libraries automatically as part of the ILL transaction, replacing manual invoicing.
**In use:** "The IFM statement is short three transactions from our log — two are reciprocal partners who don't get billed under IFM, and one is still marked in-transit on their end."
**Common misuse:** assuming every completed local ILL transaction generates an IFM charge; reciprocal agreements and consortium partners often bypass fee billing entirely.

### MUSTIE / CREW method
CREW (Continuous Review, Evaluation, and Weeding) is a structured weeding methodology; MUSTIE is its mnemonic checklist for flagging candidates — Misleading, Ugly, Superseded, Trivial, Irrelevant, available Elsewhere.
**In use:** "It passes three MUSTIE criteria on appearance, but pull the circ history before it goes on the discard cart — MUSTIE flags a candidate, it doesn't confirm one."
**Common misuse:** treating MUSTIE as sufficient on its own, without cross-checking circulation data, which risks discarding a title that's simply shelved somewhere patrons don't browse rather than one nobody wants.

### Authority record vs. bibliographic record
An authority record establishes the standardized, controlled form of a name, subject, or series (so "Twain, Mark" is used consistently instead of variant forms); a bibliographic record describes a specific work or edition.
**In use:** "The author's name on the cover doesn't match the authority record's established form — use the authority form in the 100 field regardless of how the cover prints it."
**Common misuse:** cataloging a name or subject exactly as printed on the item instead of checking the authority file, which fragments a single author's works across multiple inconsistent headings in search results.

### Reciprocal borrowing vs. interlibrary loan
Reciprocal borrowing lets a patron use their card directly at a partner library under a standing agreement (no item ships); interlibrary loan physically transfers an item between libraries through a request-and-shipping process.
**In use:** "If they're willing to visit the partner campus in person, reciprocal borrowing gets them the book today — ILL would take a week."
**Common misuse:** defaulting to ILL for every unavailable-locally request without checking whether a faster reciprocal option exists for that particular partner network.

### Accession vs. acquisition
Acquisition is the process of selecting and obtaining an item (ordering, receiving, invoicing); accessioning is the specific act of formally adding it to the collection's inventory record with a unique identifier.
**In use:** "It's been received and invoiced — acquisition's done — but it still needs to be accessioned before it shows up in inventory counts."
**Common misuse:** using "acquired" to mean the item is fully processed and trackable, when accessioning (and often cataloging) still hasn't happened.

### Weeding vs. deselection
Both describe removing items from the active collection, but "weeding" is the operational/informal term for the ongoing maintenance process; "deselection" is the more formal collection-development term often used in written policy.
**In use:** "Policy calls it deselection, but day to day we just call it weeding the 300s this month."
**Common misuse:** treating the terms as signaling different processes rather than different registers of the same activity — the confusion mostly matters when quoting policy language verbatim.

### Full-level vs. core-level (and below) cataloging record
Full-level records meet complete national-standard descriptive and subject-access requirements; core-level and below are progressively abbreviated, often missing full subject analysis or notes.
**In use:** "It's core-level, not full — that's fine for a trade paperback with a clean ISBN match, but I'd want full-level before trusting the subject headings on a specialized nonfiction title."
**Common misuse:** assuming any accepted OCLC record has full subject and note fields; core-level and minimal-level records are common and can leave an item unfindable by subject search in practice.
