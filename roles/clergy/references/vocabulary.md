# Clergy — Vocabulary

### Clergy-penitent privilege
A legal protection for confidential communications made to clergy in a recognized spiritual or confessional context, preventing compelled testimony about that communication in court.
**In use:** "That conversation happened in a confessional context, so privilege likely applies — but the abuse disclosure inside it may still trigger a mandatory report regardless."
**Common misuse:** Treating the privilege as blanket confidentiality covering everything told to clergy, rather than a narrow legal protection that mandatory-reporting duties frequently override.

### Warm handoff
Introducing a person directly to a referral resource — making the call together, walking them to an appointment — rather than giving them a name or number to follow up on alone.
**In use:** "Don't just hand her the counseling center's number — call intake with her in the room, that's a warm handoff."
**Common misuse:** Calling any referral a "warm handoff" even when it's just a verbal recommendation with no direct connection made.

### Triangulation
A family-systems concept (Bowen) where two people in conflict pull a third person into the tension to stabilize their own anxiety, rather than resolving the conflict directly.
**In use:** "The music director's being triangulated — both factions are trying to make her take a side so they don't have to talk to each other."
**Common misuse:** Labeling every three-person disagreement as triangulation, when sometimes three people simply disagree without one being pulled in to relieve anxiety.

### Identified patient
A family-systems term for the person or issue an anxious system focuses blame on, treating them as "the problem" when the real tension is systemic.
**In use:** "The new choir member isn't actually the problem — she's become the identified patient for anxiety that predates her arrival."
**Common misuse:** Using the term to dismiss legitimate individual accountability by reflexively reframing every behavioral concern as systemic.

### Mandatory reporting
A legal duty, applying to clergy in most US jurisdictions, to report suspected abuse or neglect of a minor or dependent adult to authorities, regardless of how the disclosure was made.
**In use:** "This is a mandatory-reporting situation — the privilege doesn't cover disclosures of ongoing abuse to a minor in most states."
**Common misuse:** Assuming mandatory-reporting duty only applies to therapists and teachers, not clergy — most US jurisdictions include clergy as mandated reporters, sometimes with narrow confessional exceptions.

### Board Certified Chaplain (BCC)
A professional credential (via organizations like APC or NACC) for institutional chaplaincy — hospital, military, correctional — requiring supervised clinical pastoral education and a certification process.
**In use:** "Hospital chaplaincy at that system requires BCC credentialing, not just ordination."
**Common misuse:** Assuming ordination alone qualifies someone for institutional chaplaincy roles, which typically require separate clinical certification.

### Clinical Pastoral Education (CPE)
A supervised, experiential training model (via ACPE) for pastoral care, using verbatim case reflection to develop a chaplain's or clergy member's self-awareness and care skill.
**In use:** "She did a unit of CPE at the hospital before starting hospice chaplaincy."
**Common misuse:** Treating CPE as a one-time class rather than an ongoing supervised-practice model built around reflecting on actual pastoral encounters.

### Verbatim
In CPE/pastoral supervision, a written reconstruction of an actual pastoral conversation, used for supervised reflection on what was said and why.
**In use:** "Bring a verbatim from this week's hospital visits to group supervision."
**Common misuse:** Confusing it with a general reflection or journal entry rather than a near-transcript of a specific encounter used for structured feedback.

### Differentiation of self
A family-systems concept describing the capacity to stay emotionally connected to an anxious system while maintaining a clear, non-reactive sense of one's own position.
**In use:** "Staying differentiated in that meeting meant not getting pulled into either faction's framing of the conflict."
**Common misuse:** Confusing differentiation with emotional distance or detachment, when it specifically means staying connected while not being reactive.

### Officiant of record
The legally recognized person who signs and files the marriage license or other legal document tied to a ceremony, distinct from anyone else who may participate in the ceremony itself.
**In use:** "Confirm who the officiant of record is before the wedding — it has to match the name on the filed license."
**Common misuse:** Assuming any ordained person present at a ceremony automatically has legal signing authority in that jurisdiction, without confirming licensing requirements.

### Pastoral presence
The practice of being attentively, non-anxiously present with someone in distress, distinct from problem-solving or advice-giving.
**In use:** "There was nothing to fix in that hospital room — the job was pastoral presence, not solutions."
**Common misuse:** Using it as a justification for passivity in situations that actually require action, like a safety screen or a referral.

### Congregational systems theory
The application of family-systems concepts (triangulation, differentiation, anxiety contagion) to congregations and other organizations, distinct from individual pastoral counseling theory.
**In use:** "This isn't an individual counseling problem — read it through congregational systems theory, the anxiety is systemic."
**Common misuse:** Applying systems language to every interpersonal disagreement, obscuring cases where an individual simply needs direct accountability.
