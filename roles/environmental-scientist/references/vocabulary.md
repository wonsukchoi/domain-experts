# Environmental Scientist — Vocabulary

### Recognized Environmental Condition (REC)
Under ASTM E1527, the presence or likely presence of a hazardous substance release on a property under conditions that indicate an existing release, a past release, or a material threat of a future release.
**In use:** "We've got a REC at the former lift pit — no closure documentation, so we can't rule out a release."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a binary lab result ("contaminated/not contaminated") rather than a documented professional judgment call that two EPs could reasonably disagree on.

### Controlled Recognized Environmental Condition (CREC)
A REC resulting from a past release that has been addressed to the satisfaction of the regulatory authority, with residual contamination remaining subject to an institutional or engineering control (e.g., a deed restriction or vapor barrier).
**In use:** "It's a CREC — the state closed it in 2015 with a deed restriction against residential use."
**Common misuse:** Assumed permanently resolved without re-verifying the control is still in place and recorded.

### Conceptual Site Model (CSM)
A working hypothesis of the contamination sources, migration pathways, and potential receptors at a site, built from historical and field data and updated as new data arrives.
**In use:** "The CSM says the plume should be heading northeast toward the creek — let's put a boring there to check."
**Common misuse:** Built once at project kickoff and never revised, so contradicting data gets rationalized instead of updating the model.

### Action level
The concentration of a contaminant in a given medium (soil, water, air) above which a regulatory response is required, specific to the exposure pathway and land use.
**In use:** "0.31 mg/kg benzene is under our commercial action level of 1.2, so that one's clean."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a single universal number rather than land-use- and pathway-specific — a residential action level for the same contaminant can be an order of magnitude lower.

### Vapor intrusion
The migration of volatile chemical vapors from contaminated soil or groundwater into the indoor air of overlying or nearby buildings.
**In use:** "We need a sub-slab soil gas sample before we can rule out vapor intrusion into that building."
**Common misuse:** Assumed irrelevant if groundwater is "deep" — vapor intrusion pathways can be complete even with groundwater 20+ feet below grade, depending on soil type and preferential pathways (utility corridors).

### Finding of No Significant Impact (FONSI)
A NEPA determination, following an Environmental Assessment, that a proposed federal action will not significantly affect the environment and therefore doesn't require a full Environmental Impact Statement.
**In use:** "Air and traffic impacts are both below threshold, but the wetlands fill pushes this past a FONSI into full EIS territory."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to follow from a low absolute impact level, when it's actually about impact relative to baseline and cumulative effects.

### Cumulative effects (NEPA)
The combined environmental impact of a proposed action together with other past, present, and reasonably foreseeable future actions, regardless of which agency or entity undertakes them.
**In use:** "Alone this project is under the air-quality de minimis threshold, but cumulative with the two other pending permits it's not."
**Common misuse:** Analysis stops at the single project's direct impact, ignoring other pending or planned actions in the same geographic scope.

### Fate and transport (contaminant)
The study of how a contaminant moves and changes over time and space in the environment — advection, dispersion, sorption, and degradation.
**In use:** "The fate-and-transport screening says the plume attenuates below the action level before it reaches the property line, under these assumptions."
**Common misuse:** Model output quoted as a precise predicted concentration rather than an order-of-magnitude screening estimate contingent on input assumptions.

### All Appropriate Inquiries (AAI)
The federal standard (40 CFR Part 312) defining the process a prospective purchaser must follow to qualify for certain CERCLA landowner liability protections — operationally satisfied by an ASTM E1527-compliant Phase I.
**In use:** "As long as the Phase I meets AAI, the buyer preserves their innocent-landowner defense."
**Common misuse:** Confused with the Phase I report itself — AAI is the legal standard the Phase I must meet, not a separate document.

### Detection limit vs. reporting limit
The detection limit (MDL) is the lowest concentration a method can reliably distinguish from zero; the reporting limit (RL, or PQL) is the lowest concentration the lab will actually report as a quantified value, typically higher than the MDL.
**In use:** "The RL for PCE is 0.05 mg/kg, above our 0.03 action level — we need a lower-RL method before we can close this out."
**Common misuse:** Treated as interchangeable, or ignored entirely when comparing a non-detect result against an action level.

### Institutional control vs. engineering control
An institutional control is a legal/administrative restriction (deed notice, land-use restriction); an engineering control is a physical barrier or system (cap, vapor barrier, slurry wall) that limits exposure to residual contamination.
**In use:** "The closure relies on both an engineering control — the vapor barrier — and an institutional control — the deed restriction against residential use."
**Common misuse:** The two are conflated, or a site's reliance on an engineering control is assumed permanent without a maintenance/inspection schedule.

### No Further Action (NFA) letter
A regulatory agency's written determination that a site has met the applicable cleanup standard and requires no further investigation or remediation (possibly subject to controls).
**In use:** "We got the NFA letter in 2019, but it's conditioned on maintaining the cap — that's still a CREC, not a clean closure."
**Common misuse:** Treated as an unconditional clean bill of health without checking whether it's conditioned on an ongoing control.
