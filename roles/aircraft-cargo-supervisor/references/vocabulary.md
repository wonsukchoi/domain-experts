# Vocabulary

Terms generalists conflate or oversimplify — practitioners keep them precise because the weight-and-balance and hazmat math depends on it.

## ULD (Unit Load Device)

A certified container or pallet used to consolidate cargo for aircraft loading, built to a type-specific certified max gross weight rather than a general "cargo container" weight guess.

*In use:* "It's not overweight for the flight, it's overweight for the ULD — this LD3's certified to 1,588, and it's built at 1,750."

*Common misuse:* treating any ULD as interchangeable capacity, rather than checking the specific type code's own certified rating.

## LD3 (AKE)

The most common lower-deck ULD type code, certified to a max gross weight of 1,588 kg (3,500 lb); the everyday reference container most belly-hold planning is built around.

*In use:* "That's an AKE position, not an ALF — the max gross weight is 1,588, not 3,175."

*Common misuse:* quoting a max gross weight without specifying which type code it applies to, since different lower-deck containers carry very different certified limits.

## PMC / pallet-and-net

An 88×125-inch main-deck or large-belly pallet loaded and restrained with a certified cargo net, structurally rated up to 6,033 kg (13,300 lb) — but the aircraft-specific position or floor-loading limit at a given spot can be lower than the pallet's own structural certification.

*In use:* "The pallet's rated for 6,033, but this position's floor-loading limit caps it well under that — the lower number governs."

*Common misuse:* citing the pallet's structural certification as the operative limit and skipping the aircraft-specific position check that can cap it further.

## Build-Up (BUP) / Break-Down (BDN)

BUP is assembling loose cargo into a ULD at the load plan's position and weight; BDN is disassembling it on arrival. Both are logged, weighed events, not informal handling steps.

*In use:* "Log the BUP weight off the scale before it goes to the aircraft, not the manifest total."

*Common misuse:* treating BUP as done once the pieces are inside the container, without a scale weight logged against the certified max.

## Contour

The physical envelope a built ULD must stay within to pass through the compartment door and along guide rails without interference — a separate check from weight, checked with a contour gauge or template.

*In use:* "It cleared the scale fine, but it's over contour on the left side — rebuild it flatter before it goes to the door."

*Common misuse:* assuming a ULD that's under its weight limit is automatically clear to load, when contour is an independent physical-fit constraint.

## Load Plan / Loading Instruction Report (LIR)

The dispatcher's position-by-position cargo assignment — ULD, weight, and hazmat content per position — that the ramp build is checked against; it embeds the weight-and-balance math already run for the flight.

*In use:* "The LIR has 42R at 1,540 general cargo — anything built differently from that has to go back through load control."

*Common misuse:* treating the load plan as a suggested arrangement the ramp can substitute within, rather than the actual weight-and-balance authority for the flight.

## Last-Minute Change (LMC)

A carrier-defined threshold within which a ramp-level deviation from the load plan can be logged and closed out locally, without triggering a full loadsheet reissue; anything outside that threshold — including any added or removed ULD position — requires load control to reissue.

*In use:* "That's a 40 kg shift with no position change — inside our LMC threshold, log it and move on."

*Common misuse:* assuming any small-looking change qualifies as an LMC, when adding or removing a whole position is always outside LMC scope regardless of the weight involved.

## NOTOC (Notification to Captain)

The document listing every dangerous-goods item actually on board, by position, handed to the flight crew before departure — it must match the final build, not the original plan, if anything changed.

*In use:* "The oxidizer pallet moved to Bulk-5 — update the NOTOC before this goes to the flight deck."

*Common misuse:* treating the NOTOC as generated once from the load plan and final, instead of re-verifying it against the actual final compartment build.

## Segregation table (IATA DGR Table 9.3.A)

The lookup table defining which hazard classes may share a compartment, must be kept "Away From" each other (minimum horizontal separation), must be "Separated From" each other (different compartment or full barrier), or may not be loaded together at all.

*In use:* "Class 3 and Class 5.1 need Separated From, not just Away From — that pallet has to go to a different compartment, not just three positions down."

*Common misuse:* treating all incompatible-class conflicts as solvable with a bit of physical spacing, when some pairings require full compartment separation and some are prohibited outright.

## CG index / envelope

A single-number representation of the aircraft's center-of-gravity position derived from the weight and arm of every compartment's load, which must fall within a published range (the envelope) for the flight's zero-fuel and takeoff weights.

*In use:* "That last-minute addition put the index right at the aft edge of the envelope — rerun it, don't eyeball it as close enough."

*Common misuse:* assuming a small weight change moves the index by a proportionally small amount everywhere in the envelope, when a shift near the edge moves it more per kilogram than the same shift mid-envelope.

## Restraint (lock / net)

The mechanical system — floor locks, guide rails, and/or a rated cargo net — holding a ULD in position against the aircraft's certified restraint load factors; verification is a per-point pass/fail check, not an overall visual impression.

*In use:* "Two of the four floor locks aren't showing engaged — that's not a mostly-secured ULD, it's an unrestrained one."

*Common misuse:* accepting an ULD as secured because most restraint points are engaged, when partial engagement carries none of the rated restraint capacity.

## FOD (Foreign Object Debris)

Any loose object on the ramp or in a compartment — fasteners, packaging, pavement debris — capable of causing damage if ingested by an engine or striking an aircraft surface; managed through scheduled walk-arounds, not incidental noticing.

*In use:* "Log the FOD walk before this turn starts — GSE's been through the stand twice since the last one."

*Common misuse:* treating FOD control as a one-time walk at shift start rather than an interval tied to actual equipment traffic on the stand.

## Danger-zone / clearance distance

The published radius around an aircraft's engine intake or exhaust, or around the fuselage/wingtip, within which personnel and GSE face intake-suction or jet-blast hazard — a distance that scales sharply with the engine's current power setting, not a fixed number.

*In use:* "That's the idle-power clearance — once start sequencing begins, the danger zone is much larger and that tug needs to be well clear."

*Common misuse:* applying one generic "stay back" distance regardless of whether the engine is at idle, taxi power, or start/high power.
