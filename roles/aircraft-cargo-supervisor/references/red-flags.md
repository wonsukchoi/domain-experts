# Red flags

Smell tests to catch before they become a hold report, a rejected ULD at the door, or an incident.

### 1. ULD built to within a few kilograms of, or over, its type's certified max gross weight, with no scale reweigh logged

**Usually means:** the build crew is estimating from the manifest total rather than reading the platform scale, especially on a consolidation with a late-added piece.

**First question:** "What did the scale actually read at build, and does it match the max gross weight on the ULD's own placard?"

**Data to pull:** scale ticket/build tag reading, the ULD type's certified max gross weight, the manifest-summed weight for comparison.

### 2. Two dangerous-goods classes assigned to adjacent positions in the same compartment with no segregation-table check logged

**Usually means:** each hazmat item's placement was checked against the load plan in isolation, not against its actual physical neighbors once built.

**First question:** "What does IATA DGR Table 9.3.A say for these two classes, and are these positions actually adjacent in this compartment?"

**Data to pull:** DGR Table 9.3.A entry for the pairing, compartment loading diagram, current NOTOC.

### 3. A ULD lock or net indicator not fully engaged at door-closure time, cleared anyway under schedule pressure

**Usually means:** the crew read a partial engagement as close enough to hit the pushback time rather than re-latching.

**First question:** "Is every restraint point on this ULD actually locked, or has one been left on 'probably caught'?"

**Data to pull:** point-by-point restraint check sheet, photo/visual confirmation if available, the schedule pressure driving the closure decision.

### 4. Actual compartment weight differs from the load plan by more than the carrier's LMC threshold, or a position was added/removed, closed out locally without a load-control call

**Usually means:** the ramp treated a load-plan deviation as ordinary discretion instead of routing it back for a reissued loadsheet.

**First question:** "Does this change exceed our LMC threshold, or does it add/remove a position — and has load control actually reissued the loadsheet?"

**Data to pull:** original LIR, actual build weights, carrier LMC threshold policy, reissued-loadsheet timestamp.

### 5. A built ULD visibly exceeding the compartment's contour gauge or door clearance

**Usually means:** an unevenly built pallet or overstuffed net wasn't run past the contour template before being spotted for loading.

**First question:** "Was this ULD checked against the contour gauge, and where does it protrude?"

**Data to pull:** contour-check record, build photos, the compartment's door/guide-rail clearance spec.

### 6. No FOD walk logged for the stand's scheduled interval, or none since the last GSE movement

**Usually means:** ramp tempo — a tight turn, gate congestion — pushed the walk past the point it would have caught debris left by recent equipment traffic.

**First question:** "When was the last logged FOD walk on this stand, and has GSE moved through since?"

**Data to pull:** FOD walk log/timestamp, GSE movement log for the stand, debris (if any) found on the most recent walk.

### 7. Ground support equipment staged inside the published engine danger-zone radius during start sequencing, with no wing-walker assigned

**Usually means:** equipment wasn't repositioned to the published clearance distance once engine-start sequencing began, on the assumption that idle-power clearance still applied.

**First question:** "What's this engine's published danger-zone radius at start power, and is that equipment actually outside it?"

**Data to pull:** aircraft-type ground-safety chart engine hazard radii, GSE position log, wing-walker assignment record.

### 8. NOTOC missing an item that's actually loaded, or listing a position that doesn't match the final build

**Usually means:** a late hazmat substitution or a segregation-driven relocation wasn't fed back into the NOTOC before it went to the flight crew.

**First question:** "Does every dangerous-goods item physically on board appear on the NOTOC at its actual, current position?"

**Data to pull:** current NOTOC, final compartment build sheet, any late-change log for hazmat items.

### 9. Shipper's declaration or UN-number labeling illegible, missing, or inconsistent with the class shown on the manifest

**Usually means:** the item was accepted without a full dangerous-goods acceptance check, and it's now on the ramp with no reliable basis for a segregation lookup.

**First question:** "Can we confirm the actual UN number and class from the package or its documentation right now, or are we working from the manifest line alone?"

**Data to pull:** shipper's declaration, package marking/labeling, dangerous-goods acceptance checklist sign-off.

### 10. Revised loadsheet CG index sitting within a few tenths of a percent of the envelope limit after a last-minute cargo change, with no explicit recalculation shown

**Usually means:** the change was treated as small without load control actually rerunning the index math — a shift near the edge of the envelope moves the index more per kilogram than the same shift would mid-envelope.

**First question:** "Has load control rerun the CG index with this specific change, and where does it land relative to the limit?"

**Data to pull:** revised loadsheet, CG envelope chart, the exact weight and position of the last change.
