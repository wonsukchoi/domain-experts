# Oral and Maxillofacial Surgeon — Vocabulary

### Pederson Difficulty Index
A numeric score (angulation + ramus relationship + depth, range 3–10) estimating third-molar extraction complexity.
**In use:** "Scored a 5 — moderate case, block out 45 minutes."
**Common misuse:** treating any score above a personal comfort threshold as an automatic referral, rather than a planning input that changes time and anesthesia, not whether to proceed.

### Pell and Gregory classification
Classifies impaction by available ramus space (Class I–III) and depth relative to the second molar's cervical line (Position A–C).
**In use:** "Class II-B mandibular third molar."
**Common misuse:** conflating this with Winter's classification — Pell and Gregory measures space and depth; Winter's measures angulation. They're scored separately and combine in the Pederson index.

### Winter's classification
Describes the angulation of an impacted tooth relative to the long axis of the adjacent second molar (mesioangular, distoangular, horizontal, vertical, transverse).
**In use:** "Mesioangular impactions tend to be less difficult than distoangular."
**Common misuse:** assuming angulation alone predicts surgical difficulty without accounting for ramus relationship and depth.

### Rood's criteria
Radiographic signs on a panoramic film suggesting true proximity between a root and the inferior alveolar canal: root darkening, canal deflection, white-line interruption, canal narrowing.
**In use:** "Two Rood's signs present — ordering a CBCT before we finalize the plan."
**Common misuse:** treating the absence of Rood's signs as guaranteeing no nerve risk; absence lowers probability, it doesn't eliminate it.

### Inferior alveolar nerve (IAN)
The nerve running through the mandibular canal, providing sensation to the lower teeth, lip, and chin; the nerve most often discussed in third-molar nerve-injury risk.
**In use:** "Root apex sits directly on the IAN canal on CBCT — expect this consent to name a higher paresthesia risk."
**Common misuse:** using "nerve injury" generically without specifying IAN vs. lingual nerve — the injury mechanisms and risk factors differ.

### Lingual nerve
A separate nerve at risk during mandibular third-molar surgery, typically injured via soft-tissue flap retraction rather than root proximity to bone.
**In use:** "Numbness is on the tongue side, not the lip — that's lingual nerve, not IAN."
**Common misuse:** attributing lingual-side numbness to IAN injury; the two nerves have different anatomic courses and different causes of injury during the same procedure.

### ASA Physical Status Classification
A six-tier system (I–VI) from the American Society of Anesthesiologists stratifying a patient's overall medical risk for anesthesia.
**In use:** "ASA II, no additional clearance needed for local plus nitrous."
**Common misuse:** using ASA class alone to set anesthesia depth without also weighing procedure complexity — the two factors multiply, they don't average.

### Coronectomy
Intentional partial tooth removal that leaves the root near the nerve in place, undertaken specifically to avoid nerve injury on a high-risk extraction.
**In use:** "Given the confirmed nerve contact, we discussed coronectomy as an alternative to full extraction."
**Common misuse:** assuming coronectomy eliminates all future risk — a retained root can still require removal later if it migrates or becomes infected.

### Alveolar osteitis (dry socket)
Localized bone inflammation following loss or premature dissolution of the post-extraction clot, presenting as pain onset after an initial pain-free interval.
**In use:** "Pain started on day 3 after being pain-free — check for dry socket before assuming infection."
**Common misuse:** labeling any post-operative pain "dry socket" without confirming an empty socket with exposed bone on exam — persistent swelling with fever points to infection instead.

### MRONJ (medication-related osteonecrosis of the jaw)
Bone necrosis risk associated with antiresorptive medications (bisphosphonates, denosumab), most significant with long-term IV oncologic dosing.
**In use:** "She's been on IV zoledronic acid for two years for bone mets — flag MRONJ risk before any extraction."
**Common misuse:** applying the same precautions to short-term oral bisphosphonate use (e.g., for osteoporosis) and long-term IV oncologic dosing — the risk magnitude differs by orders of magnitude between the two.

### Orthognathic surgery
Surgical repositioning of the jaw(s) to correct skeletal (not just dental) malocclusion or facial asymmetry.
**In use:** "This is a skeletal Class III — orthognathic referral, not just orthodontics."
**Common misuse:** conflating orthognathic surgery's planning complexity and recovery timeline with routine extraction — it is a distinct surgical category with its own presurgical orthodontic phase.

### Oroantral communication
An opening between the oral cavity and the maxillary sinus, most often created during extraction of an upper posterior tooth whose root sits close to the sinus floor.
**In use:** "Sinus floor was under 2mm from the root apex — do a Valsalva check right after extraction."
**Common misuse:** not checking for it after posterior maxillary extractions where imaging showed the sinus floor was close to the root apex — small communications can go unnoticed without a specific check (e.g., Valsalva test).

### Panoramic radiograph
A single rotational film showing the full upper and lower jaw in one image; the standard first-line imaging for impaction assessment.
**In use:** "Pan shows two Rood's signs — that's our trigger for CBCT, not a reason to skip it."
**Common misuse:** treating it as sufficient for nerve-proximity confirmation when Rood's signs are present — it's a 2D projection and cannot resolve buccolingual position.

### CBCT (cone-beam computed tomography)
A three-dimensional imaging modality used to confirm true spatial relationships (e.g., root-to-canal proximity) that a panoramic film can only suggest.
**In use:** "CBCT confirmed lingual position — sectioning plan changes to avoid lingual retraction."
**Common misuse:** ordering it reflexively for every impaction regardless of panoramic findings, adding cost and radiation exposure without changing the plan for clearly low-risk cases.
