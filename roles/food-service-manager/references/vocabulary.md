# Working vocabulary

Terms of art practitioners use precisely, that generalists misuse. Load this when writing or reviewing content that needs to sound like an actual food service manager, not a generalist describing one.

### Theoretical food cost
What food cost should be based on recipe specs and sales mix (beginning inventory + purchases − ending inventory, or recipe cost × units sold), as opposed to actual booked cost.
**In use:** "Theoretical says we should be at 30.5% this week — actual came in at 34.2%, so we've got a 3.7-point leak to find."
**Common misuse:** Treated as interchangeable with actual cost — the gap between theoretical and actual is exactly the diagnostic signal; if they're always assumed equal, the variance analysis never happens.

### Contribution margin (per plate)
Revenue per dish minus its direct food cost — what that item actually contributes toward covering labor, overhead, and profit, distinct from its food cost percentage alone.
**In use:** "The steak's food cost % looks bad at 38%, but its contribution margin per plate is still our second-highest — don't cut it on the percentage alone."
**Common misuse:** Confusing food cost percentage with profitability — a high-cost-percentage, high-price item can contribute more real dollars than a low-cost-percentage, low-price item.

### 86 (verb)
To remove an item from availability, usually due to a stock-out, quality issue, or kitchen capacity constraint, communicated to front-of-house and often guests.
**In use:** "86 the halibut — the delivery came in with a smell we didn't like, don't substitute it silently."
**Common misuse:** Used only for true stock-outs — it should also be the response to a quality problem, not just running out, and should be communicated rather than quietly substituted.

### Par level
The target inventory quantity for an item, expressed as a max (how much to stock up to) and often a min/reorder point (when to reorder).
**In use:** "Par on chicken breast is 121 lbs max, 46 lbs reorder point — we're ordering today."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a one-time setup number rather than something to recalculate as sales mix shifts — a stale par is a common source of both spoilage and stockouts.

### Comp vs. void
A comp is a completed sale given away for free (usually for service recovery or hospitality); a void is a transaction canceled before it's finalized (an order entry error). They have very different implications for cost and controls.
**In use:** "That's a void, not a comp — the order was never fired to the kitchen, so there's no food cost impact, just a POS correction."
**Common misuse:** Conflated in casual reporting — a rising "comp rate" that's actually mostly voids (data entry corrections) means something very different for controls than one that's genuinely food given away.

### HACCP (Hazard Analysis and Critical Control Points)
A systematic food safety approach identifying specific points in food handling where hazards (biological, chemical, physical) could occur and require monitored control (e.g., cooking temperature, cooling time).
**In use:** "That walk-in temp excursion is a HACCP critical control point failure — it needs a documented corrective action, not just a note in the log."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a paperwork requirement rather than the actual control system — logging a temperature without a defined corrective-action trigger isn't HACCP, it's just record-keeping.

### Menu engineering
Analyzing menu items on both popularity (sales mix) and profitability (contribution margin) to decide pricing, placement, and whether to keep an item.
**In use:** "Menu engineering says the veggie plate is a dog — low mix, low margin — either cut it or figure out why we're keeping it."
**Common misuse:** Done on popularity alone ("it sells well, keep it") without checking margin, or on margin alone without checking whether anyone actually orders it — it's explicitly a two-axis analysis.

### Yield percentage
The usable portion of a raw ingredient after trimming, cooking loss, or waste, expressed as a percentage of the as-purchased weight — determines the true cost per usable/plated ounce.
**In use:** "As-purchased price looks cheap, but this cut's yield is only 68% after trim — recalculate cost per usable ounce before repricing the dish."
**Common misuse:** Recipe costing done off as-purchased price without adjusting for yield loss, systematically understating true plate cost for high-trim-loss ingredients.
