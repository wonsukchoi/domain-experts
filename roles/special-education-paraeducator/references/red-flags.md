# Red flags

Smell tests a veteran paraeducator or supervising teacher catches early. Format for each: what it usually means, the first question to ask, and the data to pull.

### A single student's paraeducator is within arm's reach for 90%+ of the school day

**Usually means:** proximity has become the default rather than a response to a specific need — the "Velcro" pattern documented in Giangreco's research, where constant adult presence measurably reduces peer interaction and independent problem-solving rather than supporting either.

**First question:** "Which parts of today actually required that proximity, per the IEP or safety plan — and which were just habit?"

**Data to pull:** a proximity log for one full day (minutes within 3 feet vs. not), IEP goals that specifically call for 1:1 support vs. those that don't, peer-interaction counts compared to a same-grade peer without an aide.

### Prompt count on a skill has stayed flat or risen over 3+ weeks

**Usually means:** the prompt level isn't being faded on the schedule the hierarchy calls for — either prompts are being delivered before the wait-time window elapses, or a rung movement rule isn't being followed (moving up on a single miss instead of two, or never moving down after successes).

**First question:** "Show me the rung used per trial for the last two weeks — not just the prompt count."

**Data to pull:** trial-by-trial rung data (not just a daily total), wait-time compliance (are prompts given inside the 3–5 second window?), whether the movement rule was actually applied trial-to-trial.

### A behavior gets logged as "defiance" or "attention-seeking" with no ABC data behind the label

**Usually means:** the behavior hasn't actually been analyzed for function — "attention-seeking" is being used as a conclusion when it's actually a hypothesis that needs antecedent/consequence data to support or rule out.

**First question:** "What happened in the two minutes right before this, every time it's occurred?"

**Data to pull:** ABC narrative logs across every occurrence in the last two weeks, time-of-day and activity clustering, what the adult response was each time (an unintentional reinforcement pattern often hides in the "consequence" column).

### The same accommodation keeps getting provided informally but isn't written in the IEP or 504

**Usually means:** something that works has been discovered ad hoc and never routed back to the team — which means it's not documented, not consistent across staff or substitutes, and not defensible if a parent or auditor asks why it's happening.

**First question:** "Is this in the plan? If it's working, why hasn't it gone to the case manager to get written in?"

**Data to pull:** the current IEP/504 accommodations page, a log of how often and by whom the informal accommodation has been given, and whether it's been consistent across every adult working with the student.

### End-of-week data sheets get filled in all at once, from memory

**Usually means:** same-day recording isn't happening, which means the "data" driving IEP progress decisions is reconstructed impression, not observation — reconstructed frequency counts in particular tend to round toward whatever the week felt like overall.

**First question:** "Walk me through Tuesday specifically — what happened, session by session?" (If the answer is vague, the data sheet was built from a vibe, not a log.)

**Data to pull:** timestamps on data-sheet entries if the system logs them, comparison of same-day vs. delayed entries' variance, whether entries cluster suspiciously close to round numbers.

### A student's IEP progress report says a goal is "met" based on one or two good sessions

**Usually means:** normal session-to-session variability is being mistaken for a stable trend — one strong session inside a noisy baseline isn't the same as the criteria (usually stated as a percentage across a set number of consecutive sessions or opportunities) actually being satisfied.

**First question:** "How many consecutive sessions hit criteria, and what does the session right before that look like?"

**Data to pull:** the full session-by-session data series (not a summary average), the specific mastery criteria as written in the goal (e.g., "4 of 5 consecutive sessions"), and whether the good session(s) followed a novel setting/material change that might not generalize.

### Physical intervention (restraint) is used and no incident report gets filed the same day

**Usually means:** either the severity of the event is being minimized, or the reporting requirement isn't understood — district policy and most state regulations require documentation of every physical intervention regardless of how minor it seemed or how quickly the student calmed.

**First question:** "Has the incident report been filed yet — and if not, why not?"

**Data to pull:** the CPI (or district-equivalent) incident report, the escalation continuum stage at which de-escalation was attempted before physical intervention, staff certification status for whoever intervened.

### A paraeducator is making instructional or behavioral-plan decisions independently, without the supervising teacher's sign-off

**Usually means:** role boundaries have blurred — the paraeducator is functioning as the de facto instructional decision-maker on goals, pacing, or behavior consequences that are supposed to be the certified teacher's call, often because supervision time has quietly shrunk.

**First question:** "When did you last have dedicated planning/check-in time with the supervising teacher on this student?"

**Data to pull:** scheduled supervision/consultation time on the master schedule vs. what's actually happening, who signed off on the last change to strategy or consequence, CEC paraeducator standards on supervision ratios as the benchmark.

### A student's behavior consistently escalates around one specific adult or one specific task, and it's treated as "personality"

**Usually means:** there's an unidentified antecedent (a specific demand, a specific adult's interaction style, a sensory trigger tied to that setting) that hasn't been isolated because the pattern's been attributed to the student's disposition instead of investigated as data.

**First question:** "Does this happen with other adults doing the same task, or only this one? Same task, different adults?"

**Data to pull:** an ABC log cross-tabulated by adult and by task/setting, to isolate which variable actually correlates with the escalation.
