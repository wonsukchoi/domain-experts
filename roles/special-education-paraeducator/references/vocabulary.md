# Vocabulary

Terms generalists flatten together that paraeducators keep sharply separate. Each: definition, how a practitioner actually uses it in a sentence, and the common misuse to avoid.

### Accommodation vs. modification

An **accommodation** changes *how* a student accesses or demonstrates the same grade-level content (extra time, text-to-speech, a scribe) without changing what's being measured. A **modification** changes *what* is being taught or expected (fewer problems, a lower reading level, different content standard) — the bar itself moves.

**In use:** "Extended time is an accommodation, he's still doing the same test. Cutting the assignment to 5 problems instead of 20 is a modification — that's a different conversation with the team."

**Common misuse:** calling a modification an accommodation to avoid the paperwork trail modifications require, or providing a modification informally because it "seems like it should count" without the team having actually decided to change the standard.

### Prompt vs. cue

A **prompt** is adult-delivered assistance (verbal, gestural, model, physical) intended to be temporary and faded over time. A **cue** is typically a naturally occurring or environmental signal (a bell, a visual schedule card, a peer starting an activity) the student is being taught to respond to *instead of* an adult prompt.

**In use:** "We're fading the verbal prompt and replacing it with the visual schedule cue — the goal is the schedule doing the work the adult's voice used to do."

**Common misuse:** treating a prompt as a permanent support strategy rather than a rung on a fading ladder, or calling an adult's habitual verbal reminder a "cue" when it's actually an unfaded prompt.

### Prompt dependency

A learned pattern where a student performs a skill only when a specific prompt is present, and doesn't attempt the response without it — created by prompts that were never systematically faded.

**In use:** "He can do every step of this routine, but only if I stand at his shoulder — that's prompt dependency, not a skill deficit, and the fix is fading my proximity, not more instruction."

**Common misuse:** diagnosing prompt dependency as low ability or noncompliance, which leads to more prompting (worsening it) instead of a deliberate fade plan.

### Least restrictive prompt vs. most-to-least prompting

**System of least prompts (least-to-most)** starts at independent and escalates only as needed — used when a student is expected to already have some of the skill. **Most-to-least prompting** starts at a high support level (often full physical) and fades down on a set schedule regardless of moment-to-moment performance — used for brand-new skills where errors would be actively harmful to allow.

**In use:** "This is a new skill for her, so we're starting most-to-least for the first two weeks, then switching to least-to-most once she's got the basic motor pattern."

**Common misuse:** defaulting to least-to-most for every skill on principle, including genuinely novel ones, which produces a frustrating trial-and-error period the student didn't need to go through.

### Functional behavior assessment (FBA) vs. behavior intervention plan (BIP)

An **FBA** is the data-gathering process (observation, ABC data, sometimes formal testing) to identify *why* a behavior is occurring — its function. A **BIP** is the resulting written plan of strategies, built from the FBA's conclusions, that the team implements.

**In use:** "We can't write a BIP that actually works until the FBA tells us whether this is escape or attention — right now we're guessing."

**Common misuse:** writing or requesting a BIP before an FBA has actually identified a function, which produces a plan of generic strategies unlikely to address the real cause.

### Generalization vs. maintenance

**Generalization** is whether a skill transfers to new people, settings, or materials beyond where it was taught. **Maintenance** is whether a skill, once learned, is retained over time without ongoing instruction.

**In use:** "He's got the skill with me in the resource room — now we need to test generalization with the classroom teacher and a different worksheet before we call it mastered."

**Common misuse:** marking a goal "mastered" based on performance with one adult in one setting, without ever testing whether it holds with a different adult, room, or material set.

### Least restrictive environment (LRE)

The IDEA requirement that students with disabilities be educated with nondisabled peers to the maximum extent appropriate, with removal to separate settings only when the nature of the disability makes that impossible even with supplementary aids.

**In use:** "Before we talk about a pull-out schedule for this skill, what supplementary aids have we tried in the general ed room first — LRE means that's the starting point, not the fallback."

**Common misuse:** treating LRE as "whatever setting is most convenient to staff" rather than the specific legal default toward the general education setting with supports added, removed only as a last resort.

### Restraint vs. seclusion

**Restraint** is physically restricting a student's movement (including hand-over-hand physical intervention beyond instructional guidance). **Seclusion** is confining a student alone in a space they cannot leave. Both are last-resort, safety-only interventions with separate legal reporting requirements — neither is a routine behavior-management tool.

**In use:** "That wasn't a restraint, that was hand-over-hand instructional guidance on the task — different thing, but if his arm can't move at all and he's trying to pull away, we're now in restraint territory and the incident report is required."

**Common misuse:** using either term loosely for any physical contact or any time-out space, which both under-reports genuine incidents and over-reports routine instructional touch — precision here has real compliance consequences.

### Present levels of academic achievement and functional performance (PLAAFP/PLOP)

The IEP section describing where the student currently performs, in measurable terms, that every annual goal must be written to move from. Not a narrative summary — a data-anchored baseline.

**In use:** "The goal has to start from what the PLAAFP actually says his current rate is — if the baseline in the IEP says 40% accuracy and my data says 65%, that's a conversation for the case manager before we set next year's target off it."

**Common misuse:** treating the PLAAFP as boilerplate written once and never revisited, when it's the fixed reference point every goal, and every progress report, is measured against.

### Discrete trial vs. naturalistic teaching

**Discrete trial training (DTT)** is structured, repeated, adult-directed practice of a single skill in isolation (often table-top) with a clear prompt-response-consequence sequence. **Naturalistic teaching** embeds the same skill target into ongoing, student-motivated activities and routines.

**In use:** "We built the requesting skill with DTT first to get the motor pattern solid, now we're only working on it naturalistically during snack and centers so it actually shows up when he wants something."

**Common misuse:** assuming DTT-taught skills automatically transfer to naturalistic settings without deliberately programming for that transfer — DTT and generalization are two separate steps, not one.
