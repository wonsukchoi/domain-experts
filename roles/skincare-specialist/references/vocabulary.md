# Skincare Specialist — Vocabulary

### Fitzpatrick type
A six-category scale (I-VI) classifying skin's genetic response to UV exposure, based on burning/tanning tendency rather than visible skin color alone.
**In use:** "She's a Fitzpatrick III, but with that recent sun exposure I'm treating her like a IV for PIH risk this visit."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a fixed label based on skin tone at a glance, when it's meant to be assessed by burn/tan history and can inform but shouldn't be the sole strength-selection factor.

### PIH (post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation)
Darkened skin patches that develop after inflammation or injury to the skin (including from a peel or aggressive extraction), more common and more persistent on higher-Fitzpatrick skin.
**In use:** "I downgraded the peel strength specifically to reduce PIH risk, not because her skin can't tolerate the acid itself."
**Common misuse:** Confused with a normal, expected, short-lived post-peel flush — PIH is a distinct, longer-lasting pigment change, not the immediate redness.

### Barrier status / skin barrier
The functional state of the stratum corneum's ability to retain moisture and resist irritants; "compromised" means visibly reactive, over-exfoliated, sunburned, or otherwise not in a baseline-healthy state.
**In use:** "Barrier's compromised from the retinol four nights ago — I'm not doing the full-strength peel today."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to be a fixed trait ("she has a sensitive barrier") rather than a variable state that changes visit to visit based on recent products and exposure.

### ABCDE signs
A mnemonic screening tool (Asymmetry, Border irregularity, Color variation, Diameter >6mm, Evolving) for identifying a skin lesion that warrants dermatologist referral rather than cosmetic treatment.
**In use:** "That mole hit two ABCDE signs — irregular border and it's changed size — so I referred her instead of doing the extraction."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a diagnostic tool ("it didn't hit any ABCDE signs, so it's fine") when it's a referral trigger, not a clearance test — a lesion can still be concerning without triggering any listed sign.

### Contraindication
A condition or factor that makes a specific treatment unsafe or inadvisable for a given client at a given time.
**In use:** "Current isotretinoin use is an absolute contraindication for chemical peels — not a 'proceed with caution' situation."
**Common misuse:** Treated as binary/permanent when many skincare contraindications are time-limited (e.g., retinoid use resolves after a pause) rather than absolute and lasting.

### Exfoliation strength / peel strength
The concentration and pH of a chemical exfoliant, which determines how deep into the skin it penetrates and how strong the resulting reaction/benefit is.
**In use:** "We're going with 15% instead of the 30% she asked for, given her barrier status today."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to scale linearly with results ("stronger is always more effective") rather than being matched to what the client's skin can currently tolerate.

### Active ingredient
A skincare ingredient with a measurable physiological effect on skin (retinoids, AHA/BHA acids, vitamin C, benzoyl peroxide), as distinct from inert/carrier ingredients.
**In use:** "Let's map out which actives she's using at home before we plan today's in-office treatment."
**Common misuse:** Treated as always compatible/stackable with other actives, ignoring that combining multiple actives compounds irritation risk without proportional benefit.

### Patch test
A small-area application of a new product or treatment strength, done in advance, to observe for an adverse reaction before full application.
**In use:** "New product line, new client — patch test 48 hours before we do the full face."
**Common misuse:** Skipped for returning clients on the assumption that past tolerance guarantees future tolerance, ignoring that skin state changes between visits.

### Extraction
Manual removal of comedones (blackheads/whiteheads) using tools or pressure, done as part of a facial.
**In use:** "I'm holding off on extractions in that area until the lesion gets checked by a dermatologist."
**Common misuse:** Treated as always appropriate for any visible bump or spot, without first distinguishing a comedone from a lesion that needs referral.

### Photosensitizing medication
A medication that increases skin's sensitivity to UV light, elevating burn and hyperpigmentation risk during and after certain skincare treatments.
**In use:** "She's on doxycycline right now, so I want to talk through her sun-exposure plan before we do anything peel-adjacent."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to only matter for treatments that directly involve light/laser, when it also affects burn/PIH risk from ordinary sun exposure during the post-treatment healing window.
