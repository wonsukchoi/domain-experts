# Red Flags — Smell Tests a Historian Catches Instantly

Each entry: what the smell usually means, the first question to ask, and what to actually pull to check.

---

### A single source carries the argument's central, load-bearing claim

- **Usually means:** the claim was accepted on first read because it was vivid or convenient, and no one went looking for a second, independent source — or a second source was sought and not found, which should have been stated rather than papered over.
- **First question:** "If this one document were destroyed tomorrow, would the claim survive?"
- **Data to pull:** any independently created, contemporaneous record that would corroborate or contradict it — a different record-keeping system (payroll, court, church, ledger), not another retelling of the same original.

### An anonymous or undated document used as load-bearing evidence

- **Usually means:** provenance work was skipped because the content was persuasive, and persuasive-but-unverified is exactly how forged or misattributed sources enter a narrative.
- **First question:** "What is this document's chain of custody from creation to the archive box it's sitting in now?"
- **Data to pull:** physical/textual analysis if available (paper, ink, typeface consistency with the claimed date), accession records, and any catalog note on how the repository dated or attributed it.

### A quote lifted from a secondary source's footnote without checking the original

- **Usually means:** the citation trail was followed one link and stopped; errors and omissions in the secondary source (misquotation, missing context, wrong page) propagate silently into the new work.
- **First question:** "Has anyone on this project actually opened the primary document this footnote points to?"
- **Data to pull:** the original document at the cited page/folio; compare the quoted text word-for-word.

### An "original" document from a chaotic period with no correction marks, variants, or wear consistent with its claimed handling history

- **Usually means:** possible forgery or later reconstruction — genuine period documents produced under pressure (wartime, crisis, hurried transcription) usually show the marks of that pressure.
- **First question:** "Does this document's physical condition match how it claims to have been created and stored?"
- **Data to pull:** comparable authenticated documents from the same office/period for a condition and format baseline; conservator or forensic-document opinion for high-stakes cases.

### A CRM survey report states "no significant history" after checking fewer than 2–3 independent record types

- **Usually means:** the survey scope was set by budget or deadline pressure rather than by what a defensible negative finding requires — county deed records alone, without newspaper archives, oral history, or state historic-property inventories, is not enough to support "no significance."
- **First question:** "Which specific repositories and record series were searched, and which weren't?"
- **Data to pull:** the survey's methodology section; cross-check against the state historic preservation office's minimum survey standards.

### An oral-history interview recording exists with no signed release form on file

- **Usually means:** the interview was conducted informally or under time pressure, and the recording is now legally unusable for deposit, publication, or broadcast regardless of its content value.
- **First question:** "Do we have a signed release, and does it cover the specific use we're planning?"
- **Data to pull:** the release form itself; check its scope (archival deposit only vs. publication vs. broadcast) against the intended use.

### A translated document is quoted in support of a load-bearing claim without checking the source-language term

- **Usually means:** a translator's word choice — sometimes a defensible judgment call, sometimes an era- or agenda-driven gloss — has been treated as a neutral pass-through of the original meaning.
- **First question:** "What is the source-language term here, and does the English gloss carry the same weight?"
- **Data to pull:** the original-language text; a second, independent translation if the term is genuinely contested.

### A bibliography with no citations from the last 10–15 years, or a synthesis that never states where it agrees or breaks from the standing interpretation

- **Usually means:** the work summarizes older scholarship rather than joining the current debate — it may be well-researched and still not be a contribution, because it doesn't say what's new.
- **First question:** "What does this argument add to, or dispute in, the most recent major work on the topic?"
- **Data to pull:** the most-cited works on the topic from the past decade; check whether the draft engages them by name or ignores them.

### A narrative states a contested figure (a casualty count, a date, a causal claim) as a single precise number with no acknowledgment that sources disagree

- **Usually means:** disagreement between sources was resolved by silent selection rather than argued corroboration — false precision standing in for an unresolved dispute.
- **First question:** "Do the underlying sources actually agree on this number, or did someone pick one?"
- **Data to pull:** every source that reports the figure; if they diverge, the text should say so and justify the number used.

### Museum or exhibit interpretive text is subject to sponsor or funder pre-approval on content, not just accuracy

- **Usually means:** the institution has traded interpretive independence for funding, and contested or unflattering findings are at risk of being softened before a visitor ever sees them.
- **First question:** "Does the sponsor have approval rights over interpretation, or only over factual accuracy and safety/legal review?"
- **Data to pull:** the sponsorship or funding agreement's review clause; compare draft text before and after sponsor review for content (not just copyediting) changes.

### A claim of "no record exists" with no statement of which repositories or record series were searched

- **Usually means:** the absence was treated as conclusive without being investigated as its own historical fact — record gaps are frequently the result of selective preservation, not selective events.
- **First question:** "Whose records would we expect to hold this, and were those specific holdings actually checked?"
- **Data to pull:** finding aids or catalogs for the repositories most likely to hold the record; note explicitly which were searched and which weren't.
