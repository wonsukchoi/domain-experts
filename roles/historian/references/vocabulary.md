# Working Vocabulary — Terms Generalists Misuse

Format per term: definition, a sentence a practitioner would actually say, and the common misuse.

---

**Primary source vs. secondary source**
A primary source was created during or close to the event by someone with direct knowledge (a letter, a ledger, a photograph, an eyewitness account). A secondary source interprets or synthesizes primary sources after the fact (a monograph, a textbook, a documentary).
*Usage:* "The memoir is a primary source for what the author remembered believing in 1975, and a secondary source at best for what actually happened that year."
*Misuse:* treating "old" as a synonym for "primary" — a 1960s textbook about the Civil War is old but still a secondary source; a 2020 oral-history interview about a 1960s event is new but still primary.

**Historiography**
The history of how a topic has been interpreted and argued over by historians — the state of the scholarly debate, not just a reading list.
*Usage:* "The historiography split in the 1980s between an economic and a cultural explanation for the collapse; this chapter argues the split is a false choice."
*Misuse:* using "historiography" to mean "background reading" or "sources cited." A historiography section states where interpretations disagree and why; a reading list just lists them.

**Internal criticism vs. external criticism**
External criticism asks whether a document is authentic (date, authorship, provenance, physical consistency). Internal criticism asks whether its content is credible, given the author's position, motive, and proximity to the event. External always comes first.
*Usage:* "We passed external criticism — the paper and ink date correctly — but internal criticism flags the author as a company man reporting on his own conduct."
*Misuse:* skipping external criticism because the content "sounds right" — this is exactly how forged documents (fabricated diaries, misattributed letters) get cited as fact.

**Presentism**
Judging or interpreting past actors using present-day values, categories, or knowledge as though they were self-evident at the time.
*Usage:* "Calling the 1920s reformers 'progressive' by today's definition is presentism — check what the word meant to them, not to us."
*Misuse:* using "presentism" as a blanket objection to any moral judgment of the past, which conflates explaining historical actors' reasoning with excusing it — presentism is a sourcing error, not a prohibition on evaluation.

**Anachronism**
Attributing an idea, object, category, or institution to a period before it existed or was in use.
*Usage:* "Calling a 1750s political faction a 'party' in the modern organizational sense is an anachronism — the term existed, but not the thing."
*Misuse:* confused with presentism; anachronism is a factual/conceptual error (the thing didn't exist yet), presentism is an evaluative error (judging by the wrong era's standards).

**Periodization**
The act of dividing the past into named eras with boundaries, which implicitly asserts what caused the change at each boundary.
*Usage:* "Where you put the start of 'the strike era' already argues about what caused it — 1912 privileges the first coordinated walkout, 1908 would privilege the wage cut that provoked it."
*Misuse:* treating standard period labels (e.g., "the Gilded Age," "the Cold War") as neutral containers rather than inherited interpretive claims.

**Corroboration (source triangulation)**
Confirming a claim using an independently created source — one not derived from or copying the first.
*Usage:* "The coroner's count is corroborated by the mutual-aid ledger — two independent record-keeping systems, not two retellings of the same report."
*Misuse:* counting two sources as corroborating when one copied or derived from the other (two newspapers reprinting the same wire story is one source, not two).

**Provenance**
The documented chain of custody and origin of a source or object, from creation to its current location.
*Usage:* "The letter's provenance is solid — family papers, donated intact, with an unbroken accession trail — which is why we trust the external criticism."
*Misuse:* used loosely to mean "where it came from" in a vague sense, rather than the specific, checkable custody chain that authenticates a source.

**Determination of Eligibility (DOE)**
A formal finding, under National Register of Historic Places criteria, that a property qualifies for listing — a distinct step from actual nomination/listing, and the trigger for Section 106 review on federally involved projects.
*Usage:* "We issued a DOE of eligible under Criterion C; that triggers Section 106 review before the highway alternative can proceed."
*Misuse:* treating "eligible" and "listed" as the same status — a property can be determined eligible and never formally listed, and still carry the same Section 106 review obligation.

**Integrity (National Register sense)**
The extent to which a property retains the physical characteristics (location, design, setting, materials, workmanship, feeling, association) that convey its historic significance — distinct from "good condition."
*Usage:* "The building is structurally sound, but it's lost design and setting integrity — the original façade was re-clad and the surrounding block was demolished."
*Misuse:* equating integrity with physical condition; a deteriorated but unaltered building can have high integrity, and a well-maintained but heavily renovated one can have almost none.

**Oral history vs. oral tradition**
Oral history is a recorded interview with a living person about their own remembered experience, generally treated as a primary source. Oral tradition is a story transmitted across generations, treated as evidence of collective memory and meaning-making, not a direct eyewitness record of the original event.
*Usage:* "The 1970s interview is oral history — she was there. The origin story her grandchildren tell is oral tradition — evidence of what the family believes happened, not a primary account of the event itself."
*Misuse:* citing oral tradition with the same evidentiary weight as oral history for factual details about a specific dated event.

**Load-bearing claim / load-bearing citation**
A claim the overall argument depends on — if it's wrong, the interpretation changes. Distinguished from decorative or contextual detail that could be cut without affecting the argument.
*Usage:* "That casualty figure is load-bearing — the whole chapter's argument about escalation depends on whether it's three or nine."
*Misuse:* citing every claim, load-bearing or not, at the same density, which buries the reader's ability to tell which footnotes actually matter to the argument.

**Revisionism**
Technically, the ordinary scholarly act of reinterpreting the past in light of new evidence or new questions — not inherently good or bad.
*Usage:* "This is revisionist in the technical sense: new archival access changed the causal account, not just the politics of who's telling it."
*Misuse:* using "revisionist" purely as a pejorative for any interpretation one disagrees with, collapsing routine scholarly revision with bad-faith distortion — the two require different rebuttals.

**Counterfactual**
A structured "what if" analysis used to test a causal claim by asking whether the outcome would plausibly have differed absent a specific factor — a reasoning tool, not idle speculation.
*Usage:* "The counterfactual test: remove the 1912 wage cut and ask whether a strike still plausibly happens on this timeline — if yes, the wage cut isn't doing the causal work the chapter claims."
*Misuse:* treated as unfalsifiable storytelling ("what if X had won") rather than a disciplined tool for isolating which factor a causal claim actually depends on.
