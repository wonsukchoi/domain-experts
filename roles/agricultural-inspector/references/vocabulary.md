### U.S. No. 1 (grade designation)
A USDA grade level for fresh fruits and vegetables defined by a specific overall defect tolerance and one or more nested sub-tolerances for particular defect categories.
**In use:** "This lot passes on aggregate defects but fails U.S. No. 1 on the decay sub-tolerance."
**Common misuse:** Treating the grade as passed once the aggregate defect percentage is under the overall tolerance, without checking whether any individual sub-tolerance is independently exceeded.

### Sub-tolerance
A tighter defect-rate limit nested inside a grade standard's overall tolerance, applied to a specific defect category (commonly decay or serious damage) because that defect is progressive or contagious in transit.
**In use:** "Aggregate defects are at 9%, but decay alone is at 2% against a 1% sub-tolerance — the lot fails."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a lot that passes the overall tolerance has passed the grade, without checking each sub-tolerance category separately.

### Geometric mean (GM), agricultural water
The rolling multiplicative average of generic *E. coli* concentration across an agricultural water source's sample history, used as one of two thresholds in FSMA's water quality standard.
**In use:** "The geometric mean across four samples came out to 245 CFU/100 mL, well over the 126 CFU/100 mL limit."
**Common misuse:** Judging water quality off a single sample's raw value instead of recomputing the rolling geometric mean across the full dataset.

### Statistical threshold value (STV)
The second FSMA water-quality metric, representing an estimated 90th-percentile concentration across the rolling sample dataset, applied alongside (not instead of) the geometric mean.
**In use:** "GM passed, but recompute the STV before clearing the source — one high reading can still fail it."
**Common misuse:** Treating the geometric mean and statistical threshold value as interchangeable or as alternative checks rather than both being required.

### Generic *E. coli* (indicator organism)
A non-pathogenic bacterial species used as a proxy indicator for fecal contamination in agricultural water testing, rather than being tested as a hazard in itself.
**In use:** "Generic *E. coli* count is the indicator we test — it's a proxy for fecal contamination risk, not the pathogen of concern itself."
**Common misuse:** Treating a generic *E. coli* result as a direct measure of a specific foodborne pathogen rather than an indicator organism.

### Tolerance (pesticide residue)
The maximum legal concentration (in ppm) of a specific pesticide chemical permitted on a specific commodity, established under 40 CFR Part 180.
**In use:** "Azoxystrobin on tomatoes has a 3.0 ppm tolerance — this 0.4 ppm result is well within it."
**Common misuse:** Applying a single remembered ppm ceiling across commodities instead of pulling the tolerance table entry for the specific commodity-chemical pair.

### Preharvest interval (PHI)
The minimum number of days required between a pesticide's last labeled application and harvest, set on the product label.
**In use:** "PHI here is 14 days — harvesting 9 days after application is a violation regardless of residue test results."
**Common misuse:** Treating PHI as a soft guideline satisfied by "enough time having passed" rather than a hard label-specified day count.

### Acceptance sampling plan
A statistical schedule specifying sample size and acceptance number (maximum defective units still allowing a pass) for a given lot size, used to infer a lot's overall quality from a partial sample.
**In use:** "For a lot this size, the schedule calls for a sample of 50 with an acceptance number of 3."
**Common misuse:** Drawing a convenience-sized sample or resampling the same lot after a failure instead of following the size and acceptance number the plan specifies for that lot size.

### Key Data Element (KDE) / Critical Tracking Event (CTE)
The specific data points (KDEs) that must be captured at each defined point in the supply chain (CTEs — harvesting, cooling, packing, shipping) under FSMA's traceability rule.
**In use:** "The packing CTE is missing its lot code KDE — that's a traceability gap, not just a paperwork note."
**Common misuse:** Treating a missing KDE at one CTE as a minor omission rather than a specific, citable traceability rule gap.

### Stop-sale order
A formal directive prohibiting further sale or movement of a specific lot or product, issued for findings with a food-safety component (confirmed residue exceedance, contamination, decay sub-tolerance failure).
**In use:** "This is a confirmed pesticide exceedance — that's a stop-sale, not a corrective-action letter."
**Common misuse:** Treating a stop-sale order as equivalent in severity to a documentation or labeling corrective-action letter.

### Recall classification (Class I/II/III)
The FDA/USDA severity tier assigned to a recall based on the health-hazard evaluation, ranging from reasonable probability of serious harm (Class I) to no adverse health consequences (Class III).
**In use:** "The hazard assessment supports Class I here — reclassifying to Class II needs new evidence, not just a request from the firm."
**Common misuse:** Treating recall classification as negotiable based on the recalling firm's preference rather than the underlying hazard assessment.

### Chain of custody (sample)
The documented, unbroken record of who collected a sample, how it was handled, and how it was transported and stored, establishing that the tested sample is the same one drawn from the lot.
**In use:** "Chain of custody on this water sample is intact — collection time, cooler transport, and lab receipt all logged."
**Common misuse:** Treating chain-of-custody documentation as a formality rather than the specific record that makes a lab result defensible if challenged.

### Die-off interval (microbial)
The time period between water application and harvest during which microbial contamination on a crop naturally declines, sometimes used as a corrective measure justification when a water source exceeds its quality standard.
**In use:** "Given the exceedance, they're relying on a documented die-off interval rather than treating the water before the next application."
**Common misuse:** Assuming any elapsed time counts as a valid die-off interval without a documented, standard-referenced basis for the specific interval claimed.
