### FIM efficiency
FIM-point gain divided by length of stay in days — the rate metric that turns a functional trajectory into a length-of-stay projection.
**In use:** "She's tracking 1.5 points a day against a 1.8 benchmark, so I'm re-projecting the discharge date, not holding the original one."
**Common misuse:** treating a single admission-to-discharge FIM efficiency as a fixed patient trait rather than recomputing it against interim data as therapy progresses.

### ASIA Impairment Scale (AIS)
The A–E grading of spinal cord injury completeness from the International Standards for Neurological Classification of SCI exam — A is motor/sensory complete, E is normal.
**In use:** "He's a C6 ASIA B — sensory incomplete below the level, so I'm not quoting a complete-injury prognosis."
**Common misuse:** using "ASIA A" and "complete injury" as if they describe severity generally, when the grade is specifically about sacral-segment sparing, not overall limb strength.

### Spasticity
Velocity-dependent increase in muscle tone from an upper motor neuron lesion, distinct from a fixed contracture.
**In use:** "That's spasticity, not a contracture — it releases with slow stretch, so botulinum toxin is still on the table before we talk about surgical release."
**Common misuse:** using "spasticity" and "contracture" interchangeably; a true contracture (fixed, non-velocity-dependent) doesn't respond to chemodenervation the way spasticity does.

### Autonomic dysreflexia
An uninhibited sympathetic response to a noxious stimulus below the level of a spinal cord injury at or above T6, causing a dangerous BP spike.
**In use:** "New headache and BP 170/100 on a T4 complete injury — that's autonomic dysreflexia until the bladder and bowel are checked."
**Common misuse:** working it up like a primary hypertensive urgency or cardiac event before checking the bladder/bowel/skin differential specific to the syndrome.

### Heterotopic ossification (HO)
Abnormal bone formation in soft tissue around a joint, most common after SCI, TBI, or major joint trauma.
**In use:** "The knee's been warm and increasingly stiff for two weeks post-injury — get an alkaline phosphatase and a bone scan before calling it just post-traumatic swelling."
**Common misuse:** attributing a warm, stiffening joint in a recent SCI/TBI patient to routine post-traumatic inflammation without considering HO in the differential.

### K-level (Medicare Functional Classification Level)
A K0–K4 classification of a patient's demonstrated ambulation potential, used to justify the complexity of a prosthetic prescription.
**In use:** "He trialed as a K2 on the temporary prosthesis, so I'm prescribing a multi-axial foot, not the energy-storing K3 component he's asking for."
**Common misuse:** assigning the K-level from the patient's pre-amputation activity level or stated goals rather than from demonstrated trial performance — the most common reason a prescription gets denied.

### Rancho Los Amigos Scale
An eight-level scale describing a TBI patient's stage of cognitive and behavioral recovery, used to set the management approach.
**In use:** "She's Rancho IV — confused-agitated — so we're reducing stimulation and redirecting, not running orientation drills."
**Common misuse:** assuming the level only advances with time since injury, and failing to reassess it at each team conference.

### Modified Ashworth Scale (MAS)
A 0–4 (with a 1+ step) ordinal scale grading resistance to passive stretch, used to document spasticity severity.
**In use:** "MAS 2 at the ankle, but it's not blocking anything functionally right now — documenting it, not treating it yet."
**Common misuse:** treating the MAS number itself as the treatment target instead of the functional impairment the tone is or isn't causing.

### IRF 60% rule
The CMS requirement that at least 60% of an inpatient rehab facility's total patient population fall into one of 13 qualifying diagnostic categories, or the facility risks reclassification and lower reimbursement.
**In use:** "This is a facility-level compliance rule, but it's why a 'debility' referral without a qualifying diagnosis gets pushback at the admission-screen stage."
**Common misuse:** treating it as a per-patient admission criterion rather than a facility-wide population threshold — an individual patient's admission decision still runs through medical necessity, participation tolerance, and diagnosis criteria separately.

### Neurogenic bladder
Bladder dysfunction from a neurologic lesion (SCI, MS, stroke) disrupting normal detrusor-sphincter coordination, distinct from age-related or structural incontinence.
**In use:** "Detrusor-sphincter dyssynergia on urodynamics — that changes the bladder program from timed voiding to intermittent catheterization."
**Common misuse:** managing all post-neurologic-injury incontinence with the same generic bladder program without urodynamic characterization of the specific pattern.

### Orthosis vs. prosthesis
An orthosis supports or assists an existing limb/joint (e.g., an ankle-foot orthosis); a prosthesis replaces a missing limb segment.
**In use:** "He needs an AFO for foot drop, not a prosthesis — the limb is intact, just weak."
**Common misuse:** using the terms interchangeably in documentation, which changes billing codes, the referral pathway (orthotist vs. prosthetist), and payer coverage criteria.

### Rehab plateau
A sustained period without measurable functional gain despite continued therapy at the prior intensity.
**In use:** "She's plateaued for five days on transfers — before I call it a ceiling, I want a pain and depression screen and a medication review."
**Common misuse:** treating any short stall in progress as the ceiling and moving straight to discharge planning without checking the reversible-cause differential first.

### Durable medical equipment (DME) justification
The documentation linking a specific equipment item to a specific functional deficit and use case, required for payer approval.
**In use:** "The wheelchair justification needs the seated pressure-mapping result and the transfer FIM score, not just 'patient has SCI.'"
**Common misuse:** writing the diagnosis as the justification instead of the specific functional finding and use case the equipment addresses — the most common reason DME requests get denied or delayed.
