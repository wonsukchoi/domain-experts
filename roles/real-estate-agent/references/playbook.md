# Real estate agent playbook — filled templates

Numbers throughout follow the 439,900 Oak Ridge listing scenario in SKILL.md's worked example unless noted.

## 1. Lead follow-up cadence (8x8, by temperature)

| Temperature | Definition | Cadence | First action |
|---|---|---|---|
| Hot | Pre-approved, actively touring, or a seller with a firm listing date | Same-day call, appointment ask within 24 hours | Phone call within 5 minutes of inquiry |
| Warm | Engaged (opened emails, replied once) but no timeline commitment | 8 touches over 8 weeks — mix of call, text, value-add email (market update, new listing match) | Phone call within 1 hour, text same day if no answer |
| Cold | Single inquiry, no response after first contact attempt | Long-term drip — monthly market report + quarterly personal check-in for 12 months before reclassifying dead | Email + one text within 24 hours, then enter drip |
| Sphere/past client | Closed transaction or personal network | Quarterly touch (call, handwritten note, or event invite) plus an annual "home value check-in" | Add to CRM the day of closing, not "when there's time" |

**Portal lead economics (worked, one channel):** Zillow Flex lead cost ≈ $54 average acquisition cost in the source market; trailing-12-month CRM data shows 1-in-40 portal leads convert to a closed transaction over the 8x8 cadence. At $54/lead, 40 leads cost $2,160 to produce one closing. Compare against a $8,073 net commission (per SKILL.md worked example) — the lead-cost-to-net ratio (2,160 / 8,073 ≈ 27%) is the number to track per source; a source crossing roughly 40% of net commission per closing is a candidate to cut or renegotiate.

## 2. Listing presentation structure (in order)

1. **Rapport and agenda** — state what will be covered (market data, pricing recommendation, marketing plan, next steps) so the seller isn't guessing when the price conversation is coming.
2. **Market conditions** — local absorption rate and median DOM for the price tier, presented before any subject-property number.
3. **CMA walkthrough** — comps table (see §3), adjustments explained line by line, adjusted range stated before any seller number is asked for.
4. **Pricing recommendation** — a single number, just above the CMA midpoint, with the reasoning stated in one sentence (see SKILL.md deliverable for exact phrasing).
5. **Address the anchor directly** — if the seller has cited a Zestimate or a neighbor's sale, name it and reconcile it against the adjusted comps before moving on; an anchor left unaddressed resurfaces at every price-reduction conversation later.
6. **Marketing plan and timeline** — photography date, MLS go-live, showing availability window.
7. **Commission and agreement terms** — listing type, term length, commission percentage, protection period, stated plainly, not buried.
8. **Close** — ask for the signature; don't leave a "let me think about it" without a scheduled follow-up date.

## 4. Buyer consultation checklist (before any private showing)

- [ ] Signed written buyer-representation agreement on file (state-approved form) — required before private showings in MLSs implementing the 2024 settlement rules; open houses are the standard exception.
- [ ] Underwritten pre-approval letter dated within the trailing 60 days — a "pre-qualification" letter or one older than 60 days reflects a soft credit pull, not an underwritten file; ask whether the loan has been submitted to underwriting.
- [ ] Buyer's priority ranked: price, speed, or contingency flexibility — drives every later offer decision.
- [ ] Financing type confirmed (conventional/FHA/VA/cash) — sets realistic contingency deadlines and appraisal-gap exposure.
- [ ] Target neighborhoods and must-haves documented in writing, sourced from the buyer's stated criteria only — protects against a later steering claim.
- [ ] Earnest money capacity discussed — flag if likely deposit will sit under 1% of target price in a competitive segment.

## 5. Offer negotiation script fragments (buyer-side)

- **Presenting a below-list offer:** "Based on [specific comp/condition reasoning], I'm recommending we start at $X. If the data changes once we're in negotiation, we adjust — but we don't lead with the list price on a home that's been sitting."
- **Responding to a competing multiple-offer situation:** confirm the buyer's real ceiling in writing before drafting the escalation clause; state the increment ($1,000–$5,000, scaled to price band) and the hard cap plainly, and confirm appraisal-gap coverage capacity separately from the cap number — they solve different problems.
- **When the buyer wants to waive the appraisal contingency:** require documented proof of liquid funds covering the realistic gap (CMA range minus contract price) before drafting the waiver; route the actual contingency language to the supervising broker for review if the local form isn't the standard one.

## 6. Commission-cap tracking ledger (worked example)

| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| Annual cap (brokerage share) | $23,000 |
| Split before cap | 70% agent / 30% brokerage |
| Split after cap | 100% agent, minus $395 flat transaction fee per closing |
| YTD brokerage-paid, before this deal | $14,300 |
| This deal's commission | $12,097.25 (2.75% of $439,900) |
| Brokerage's 30% share, this deal | $3,629.18 |
| YTD brokerage-paid, after this deal | $17,929.18 (still under $23,000 — 70/30 still applies) |
| Agent's net, this deal | $12,097.25 − $3,629.18 − $395 = **$8,073.07** |
| Deals remaining to hit cap (est.) | ($23,000 − $17,929.18) / average brokerage share per deal ≈ 1.4 more average-sized deals |

Track this ledger per deal, not annually in arrears — quoting a client-facing "what I'll net" number without checking cap status is the single most common commission-math error agents make mid-year.
