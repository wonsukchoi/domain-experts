# Vocabulary

Terms generalists conflate or misuse. Each: definition, how a practitioner actually uses it in a sentence, and the common misuse.

## Amplitude vs. rate vs. beat error

**Amplitude** is how far the balance wheel swings, in degrees, on each oscillation — a measure of the power actually reaching the escapement. **Rate** is how fast or slow the movement runs, in seconds/day. **Beat error** is the timing asymmetry between the balance's swing in each direction, in milliseconds — a healthy movement ticks evenly; an unhealthy one ticks unevenly even if the average rate looks fine.

*Practitioner usage:* "Rate's only −8 seconds a day, looks great — but amplitude's collapsing to 152° vertical, so don't sign off on this one yet."

*Common misuse:* treating "the rate looks fine" as "the movement is fine." Rate is an average; amplitude and beat error expose the fault rate alone hides.

## Positional error / positions

The variation in rate and amplitude as a movement's orientation changes relative to gravity — tested across up to six standard positions (dial up, dial down, crown up/down/left/right). A well-adjusted movement holds close to the same rate across positions; a faulty one swings widely.

*Practitioner usage:* "Positional error's the whole story here — dial-up rate's perfect, crown-down it loses over three minutes a day."

*Common misuse:* diagnosing from a single dial-up reading on the bench and missing a fault that only shows up in the position the watch is actually worn or stored.

## Isochronism

The (imperfect, in practice) property that a balance wheel's oscillation period stays constant regardless of amplitude, so the watch keeps time the same whether fully wound or nearly run down. Real movements only approximate it; poor isochronism shows up as rate drifting as the mainspring unwinds through the day.

*Practitioner usage:* "Rate holds at full wind but drifts +40 seconds by hour 30 — that's an isochronism problem, likely the hairspring's overcoil or pinning point, not a one-time adjustment."

*Common misuse:* assuming any mechanical watch keeps identical time throughout its power reserve; some drift is normal, and the question is how much, not whether.

## NOS (New Old Stock) vs. generic/aftermarket part

**NOS** is unused factory-original stock for a specific caliber, still circulating through parts dealers or collector channels years or decades after production ended. **Generic/aftermarket** is a part made to fit the caliber's dimensions but not manufactured or sanctioned by the original maker — common for ébauche movements shared across brands.

*Practitioner usage:* "Found NOS mainsprings for the 72 but they're triple the price of a generic equivalent — worth it here since the piece is being sold, not just worn."

*Common misuse:* calling any non-OEM part "NOS" — NOS specifically means genuine, unused, original-manufacture stock, not any substitute that happens to fit.

## Donor movement

A running or non-running movement of the same or closely related caliber, kept or sourced specifically to harvest individual parts from — the first stop in the sourcing order for a discontinued caliber, ahead of NOS or custom fabrication.

*Practitioner usage:* "No point turning a new staff on the lathe — I've got a donor 72 with a good staff and a dead mainspring, perfect trade."

*Common misuse:* treating "donor movement" as synonymous with "parts watch" generally, without weighing whether destroying it for one part is worth more or less than the part's NOS price.

## Frankenwatch

A watch assembled, over time or at once, from parts that didn't originally ship together — a period case with a wrong-era dial, a signed case with an unsigned movement, replacement hands from a different reference. Not always fraudulent (an honest prior repair can create one unintentionally), but it materially changes value versus an all-original example.

*Practitioner usage:* "Case and movement check out, but the dial font's wrong for this year — this is a franken, and it needs to be disclosed before it's quoted as original."

*Common misuse:* assuming "frankenwatch" always means intentional fraud; it's a factual description of mismatched parts regardless of the reason they ended up together.

## Redial vs. refinished dial

A **redial** is a dial that has had its printing removed and reprinted, sometimes decades ago by an authorized service center, sometimes recently and poorly. **Refinished** more broadly covers any dial restoration, including relume of the markers. Both differ from an original, unaltered dial in ways that affect collector value even when done well.

*Practitioner usage:* "It's a period-correct redial done at an authorized service center in the '80s, not a recent reprint — still not original, but it's a different value conversation than a bad aftermarket job."

*Common misuse:* treating any dial service as automatically fraud-adjacent, or the opposite — treating "it looks new" as good news without asking why a 50-year-old dial looks new.

## COSC vs. in-house chronometer certification

**COSC** (Contrôle Officiel Suisse des Chronomètres) is the independent Swiss body that certifies movements to −4/+6 seconds/day mean daily rate across 5 positions and 3 temperatures over 15 days. **In-house certification** is a manufacturer's own chronometer-equivalent standard, sometimes stricter, tested and administered internally rather than by COSC.

*Practitioner usage:* "It's not COSC-certified, but the manufacturer's in-house spec is actually tighter on this reference — check their own tolerance sheet before assuming COSC's numbers apply."

*Common misuse:* assuming every "chronometer" watch is COSC-certified, or that COSC's tolerance is a universal target rather than one specific certifying body's standard.

## Dry test vs. wet (immersion/condensation) test

A **dry test** pressurizes the case with air or vacuum and checks for deformation or leakage electronically — fast, non-destructive, and the standard screening step. A **wet test** submerges the case, then chills it and checks for condensation inside the crystal — slower, but catches seal failures a dry test can miss (thread deformation, marginal crystal seals).

*Practitioner usage:* "Passed dry at 6 bar, but wet test showed condensation after ten minutes — reseal the crystal before this one goes back."

*Common misuse:* treating a dry-test pass as a guarantee of water resistance; it screens for gross leaks, not marginal ones.

## Hacking (seconds)

A movement feature that stops the seconds hand when the crown is pulled to the setting position, allowing the time to be set to the second against a reference clock. Absence of hacking on an older or simpler caliber is normal, not a fault.

*Practitioner usage:* "This caliber doesn't hack — don't try to set it to the second like the customer's used to with a modern watch, explain the difference instead."

*Common misuse:* reporting a non-hacking movement as broken because the seconds hand keeps sweeping when the crown is pulled out.

## Overbanking

An escapement fault where the balance wheel swings far enough to knock the pallet fork past its normal range, causing the watch to stop or run erratically — distinct from ordinary low amplitude, and often caused by a shock or a badly fitted balance.

*Practitioner usage:* "It's not just weak amplitude, the balance is overbanking on the vertical positions — check the pallet fork's banking pins and the balance's freedom of motion."

*Common misuse:* using "overbanking" as a generic term for "the watch stopped," when it describes a specific mechanical over-travel fault distinguishable on the timegrapher and under the microscope.
