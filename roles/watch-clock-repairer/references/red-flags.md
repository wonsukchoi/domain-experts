# Red flags

Smell tests a bench veteran catches at intake or on the first timegrapher reading. Format for each: what it usually means, the first question to ask, and the data to pull before quoting or promising anything.

## 1. Dial-up amplitude below 200° at full wind

**Usually means:** mainspring, barrel, or gear-train lubrication is the fault — the balance is starved of power before it ever gets to the escapement. Reaching straight for escapement adjustment here fixes nothing.

**First question:** "When was this last fully serviced, and has the power reserve noticeably shortened?"

**Data to pull:** full six-position timegrapher set; power-reserve timing (wind fully, time to stop); mainspring type and age if known from prior service records.

## 2. Amplitude drops more than 100° from horizontal to vertical position

**Usually means:** a worn or bent balance-staff pivot, or a dry/damaged cap jewel — not general dirt, which would depress amplitude evenly across positions rather than selectively in the vertical ones.

**First question:** "Is the complaint worse depending on how the watch is stored or worn — face up on a nightstand versus on the wrist?"

**Data to pull:** dial-up and crown-down amplitude side by side; microscope inspection of the balance staff pivots for scoring or bending.

## 3. Beat error above 1.5–2.0 ms

**Usually means:** hairspring collet has rotated out of position, often from a prior service or a shock; produces an audibly uneven tick-tock even when the average rate looks acceptable.

**First question:** "Has the watch been dropped or serviced recently, and does the rate itself still look reasonable despite the tick sounding uneven?"

**Data to pull:** beat error across all six positions (it should be roughly constant regardless of position, unlike amplitude); service history if available.

## 4. Rate jumps sharply positive (often 100+ s/day) with no amplitude change

**Usually means:** the movement has picked up magnetism — a common, fully reversible false alarm that gets misdiagnosed as a mechanical fault and sent straight to disassembly.

**First question:** "Has this watch been near a phone case magnet, laptop, or handbag clasp recently?"

**Data to pull:** rate before and after a pass through a demagnetizer — if the fault clears, stop there; no further teardown is warranted.

## 5. Case fails the pressure test below its rated depth

**Usually means:** gaskets have perished — expected after any prior case opening, and inevitable given enough years regardless of whether the case was ever opened. Not a manufacturing defect on an older piece; a maintenance item.

**First question:** "When was the case last opened, and were the gaskets replaced at that service?"

**Data to pull:** dry-test pressure at failure versus rated pressure; gasket part availability (OEM vs. generic substitute) before quoting.

## 6. Watch rated for its stated depth is described by the owner as "worn swimming/diving"

**Usually means:** the owner is relying on a rating that may not reflect the case's current seal condition, or is exceeding a splash-only rating (30m/3ATM) by actually immersing it.

**First question:** "What's the current pressure-test result, and does the owner understand the difference between splash resistance and swim/dive rating?"

**Data to pull:** current wet-test result at rated pressure; date of last gasket replacement.

## 7. Serial number or reference falls outside the manufacturer's known production range for that case

**Usually means:** a re-cased movement, a re-stamped case back, or an outright counterfeit assembly — common on desirable vintage references where parts command a premium separate from the whole watch.

**First question:** "Do you have any provenance — purchase records, prior service papers, or a box/papers set — for this piece?"

**Data to pull:** cross-reference serial and reference against published production-range data; movement caliber and finish versus what that reference shipped with.

## 8. Dial looks unusually crisp or "mint" relative to a 40–60-year-old case

**Usually means:** a redial — a dial reprinted or refinished, sometimes decades ago, sometimes recently to disguise damage. Not inherently fraudulent if disclosed, but it materially changes the piece's collector value.

**First question:** "Is there any history of this dial being serviced, refinished, or replaced?"

**Data to pull:** font weight and logo placement versus known genuine examples for the production year; lume-plot pattern under UV.

## 9. Hands or lume type inconsistent with the dial's production era

**Usually means:** parts substitution from a different reference or era, often to replace damaged original hands — a classic "frankenwatch" tell even when the case and dial are both genuine.

**First question:** "Were these hands original to the piece, or replaced at some point?"

**Data to pull:** lume material era-match (radium vs. tritium vs. Super-LumiNova cutoff windows) against the dial's known production year.

## 10. A part is available for a discontinued caliber at a price well below the NOS-market range

**Usually means:** the part is a mismatched cross-reference sold as genuine, a reproduction, or stolen/gray-market stock — genuine NOS for a genuinely discontinued caliber trades at a scarcity premium, not a discount.

**First question:** "What's the source, and does the seller represent this as genuine OEM, NOS, or generic?"

**Data to pull:** comparable NOS listings and price range for the same part; dimensional match against the movement in hand before fitting anything.

## 11. Customer requests a "quick fix" on a piece already flagged during authentication

**Usually means:** pressure to service or refinish a piece (redial, repolish a case) that would destroy value the customer may not know is at stake — collector value depends on originality in ways a casual owner often doesn't price in.

**First question:** "Are you aware this piece may be worth meaningfully more, or less, in its current unaltered state than after the refinish you're asking for?"

**Data to pull:** authentication findings documented before this request; comparable sold prices for original versus refinished examples of the same reference.
