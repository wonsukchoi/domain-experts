# Electrician Helper — Vocabulary

### Box fill
The NEC 314.16 calculation determining whether a box's rated cubic-inch capacity is sufficient for the conductors, devices, clamps, and grounds landing in it.
**In use:** "Box fill on B comes up 22.5 against a 21.0 box — we need the deeper one before this gets mounted."
**Common misuse:** Treating box fill as a check that only matters if the cover physically won't close — a box can close fine and still be over its rated fill; the cover fitting is not the pass/fail test.

### Conduit fill
The percentage of a conduit's interior cross-sectional area occupied by conductors, capped at 40% for three or more conductors per NEC Chapter 9, Table 1.
**In use:** "That EMT run is already at nine 12s in a half-inch — check the fill table before we add the tenth."
**Common misuse:** Eyeballing fill by whether conductors "still slide through" rather than counting against the table — a pull that goes in tight at 45% fill is a friction problem on the next pull, not a passed test on this one.

### Home run
The single conductor run from a panel to the first device or junction box on a circuit, before any branching.
**In use:** "Home run for circuit 7 is already pulled — start branching from box A."
**Common misuse:** Using "home run" loosely for any long cable run — it specifically means the panel-to-first-point segment, not any lengthy pull on the job.

### Whip
A short, flexible conduit or cable connection — commonly liquidtight flexible conduit — from a junction box or disconnect to a piece of equipment like an HVAC unit or a light fixture.
**In use:** "Whip to the disconnect is 18 inches — that's within the code max for flex on that connection."
**Common misuse:** Calling any short flexible cable a "whip" regardless of whether it's a listed connection type for the application — flex conduit length and support requirements are code-specific, not just "short and bendy."

### Feed-through wiring
A device wired so the circuit continues downstream through it (line and load terminals both used), as opposed to a device at the end of a run with only line-side connections.
**In use:** "Box A is feed-through — it's protecting B, C, and D downstream, so the GFCI test at A covers all four."
**Common misuse:** Assuming every downstream device needs its own GFCI or AFCI protection when one upstream feed-through device already provides it for the whole branch — redundant protection isn't wrong, but assuming it's required everywhere leads to over-ordering devices.

### Pigtail
A short conductor spliced to two or more circuit conductors at a wire nut, providing a single lead to land on a device terminal instead of landing multiple conductors under one screw.
**In use:** "Pigtail the grounds at that box — don't stack three grounds under one screw."
**Common misuse:** Treating pigtailing as optional tidiness rather than the correct method for landing multiple conductors on a single-terminal device; stacking conductors under one screw is a loose-connection risk, not a shortcut.

### Knockout
A partially punched, removable disc in a metal box or panel that's pushed or pried out to create a conduit or cable entry point.
**In use:** "Use the top knockout for the whip, not a hole drilled through the side — the box listing only covers the factory knockouts."
**Common misuse:** Drilling an extra entry point when a knockout in the wrong location seems more convenient — an unlisted opening voids the box's fill and enclosure rating for that entry.

### NCVT (non-contact voltage tester)
A pen-style tester that detects the presence of AC voltage near a conductor without physical contact, used as a first screening check.
**In use:** "NCVT reads hot on this one — still needs the electrician's meter and lockout before anyone opens it."
**Common misuse:** Treating an NCVT's silence as proof a circuit is dead and safe to work on — it can false-negative near grounded conduit or on induced voltage, and it is not a substitute for a verified-dead check with a rated meter.

### Megger / insulation resistance test
A test using a megohmmeter to measure the resistance of a conductor's insulation, used to detect damage from over-tensioned pulls, moisture, or age — distinct from a continuity test.
**In use:** "That pull had a rough spot at the second bend — worth a megger check before we call the run good."
**Common misuse:** Confusing a megger test with a simple continuity check — continuity confirms a conductor is unbroken; a megger test confirms the insulation around it hasn't degraded, and a conductor can pass one while failing the other.

### Torque spec
The manufacturer-listed tightening value (in inch-pounds or foot-pounds) for a termination or lug, required for a compliant, low-resistance connection.
**In use:** "Lug's not landed until it's at the listed torque spec — 'snug' isn't a number."
**Common misuse:** Estimating torque by feel ("hand-tight plus a quarter turn") instead of using a calibrated torque driver or wrench — under-torque causes a loose, heat-generating connection; over-torque can damage the conductor or strip the terminal.

### Ampacity
The maximum current a conductor can carry continuously under specified conditions without exceeding its temperature rating, per NEC Table 310.16.
**In use:** "12 AWG on a 20A breaker is fine — ampacity's rated for it. 14 on that same breaker isn't."
**Common misuse:** Treating wire gauge and breaker size as independently adjustable — the breaker protects the conductor's ampacity, not the load; undersizing the wire for the breaker is a fire-risk mismatch, not a minor spec deviation.

### Grounding vs. bonding
Grounding connects a system or equipment to earth; bonding connects metal parts together to ensure they're at the same electrical potential and provide a fault-current path. Related but distinct functions under NEC Article 250.
**In use:** "That's a bonding jumper across the water meter, not a grounding electrode conductor — different job, don't swap the terms in the log."
**Common misuse:** Using "ground" and "bond" interchangeably in field notes and logs — an inspector or the electrician reading the log needs to know which specific conductor and function is being referenced.
