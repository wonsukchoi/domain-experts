# Orthotist/Prosthetist — Vocabulary

### K-level
Medicare's five-tier (K0–K4) functional classification for lower-limb prosthesis users, defined in CMS LCD L33787 by current and reasonably expected ambulation ability, not by amputation level or age.
**In use:** "He's functionally a K3 now — the chart just hasn't caught up since his last note is fourteen months old."
**Common misuse:** Treating K-level as fixed at the original post-op prescription, or as a ceiling on what the patient is allowed to do, rather than a documentation category that gets re-assessed.

### Check socket (diagnostic socket)
A clear or translucent thermoplastic test socket, fitted and evaluated under load before any definitive (laminated) socket is fabricated, so pressure patterns and fit can be inspected and corrected cheaply.
**In use:** "Don't laminate yet — put him back in the check socket and let's see how that medial wall loads before we commit."
**Common misuse:** Skipping it under scheduling pressure and treating the first definitive socket as the fitting trial, which turns a fit problem into a refabrication instead of a quick modification.

### Total surface bearing (TSB)
A socket design philosophy that distributes load evenly (near-hydrostatically) across the entire residual limb surface via a compliant liner, in contrast to discrete load-bearing and relief zones.
**In use:** "She's a good TSB candidate — the limb's regular enough that even loading will actually feel better than PTB relief zones did."
**Common misuse:** Assuming TSB is simply "the modern replacement" for PTB in every case; an irregular or very bony residual limb can still do better with discrete pressure relief.

### Patellar-tendon-bearing (PTB) socket
A transtibial socket design that routes load preferentially onto pressure-tolerant structures (the patellar tendon, medial tibial flare) while relieving pressure-sensitive bony areas, per the original Radcliffe/Foort design logic.
**In use:** "His tibial crest can't take even loading — stick with a PTB-style relief pattern here."
**Common misuse:** Calling any transtibial socket "PTB" as a generic term, when it refers specifically to that load-routing philosophy, not to transtibial sockets in general.

### Bench alignment
The initial static setup of a prosthesis's reference lines and angles, done on a bench with nobody wearing the device, before the patient stands or walks in it.
**In use:** "Recheck bench alignment before you chase this in gait — if the reference line's off here, dynamic adjustments are just compensating for it."
**Common misuse:** Treating bench alignment as a one-time setup step that doesn't need re-verification when a gait problem shows up later; it's often the first place to look, not the last.

### Pistoning
Vertical or rotational movement of the residual limb inside the socket during gait, usually from inadequate suspension or a socket that has become too large for a shrunken limb.
**In use:** "That choke mark on the distal end is classic pistoning — check the suspension before you blame his skin care."
**Common misuse:** Attributing pistoning-caused skin marks to hygiene or skin type rather than to a mechanical fit problem that needs a suspension or volume fix.

### Sock ply
The thickness rating (1-ply, 3-ply, 5-ply) of prosthetic socks used to compensate for daily residual-limb volume fluctuation by building up socket fit incrementally.
**In use:** "Add a 1-ply on top of your usual 3-ply by evening and see if that stops the looseness."
**Common misuse:** Treating an ever-climbing total sock-ply count as a permanent fix rather than the trigger for a socket refit once it exceeds what's reasonable for daily fluctuation.

### Ground-reaction (floor-reaction) AFO
An ankle-foot orthosis with a rigid anterior shell that uses the ground reaction force at heel strike to generate a knee-extension moment through stance, used for quadriceps weakness or genu recurvatum.
**In use:** "He needs the knee-extension assist through stance — put him in a ground-reaction AFO, not a standard hinged one."
**Common misuse:** Prescribing it reflexively for any reported knee instability, including pure foot-drop cases with intact quads, where a lighter posterior leaf spring would serve as well.

### Posterior leaf spring (PLS) AFO
A thin, flexible plastic AFO that assists dorsiflexion during swing phase for isolated foot drop, without controlling mediolateral or knee stability.
**In use:** "No spasticity, quads are fine — a PLS is all he needs for the foot drop."
**Common misuse:** Using it for patients with spasticity or coronal-plane instability, where its lack of rigidity can't control the deviation it's being asked to fix.

### Vaulting
A compensatory gait pattern where the patient rises onto the sound-side toes during swing phase to gain ground clearance for the prosthetic limb.
**In use:** "He's vaulting on the sound side — check prosthetic length before you touch the knee's swing resistance."
**Common misuse:** Assuming vaulting always means "prosthesis too long" without checking swing-phase toe clearance and fear-of-stubbing as equally likely alternative causes.

### Letter of medical necessity (LMN)
The clinical justification document submitted to a payer, mapping the patient's diagnosis, functional level, and objective testing directly to the payer's coverage criteria (e.g., an LCD's K-level definitions) for a specific component.
**In use:** "The denial's because the LMN described him clinically but never quoted the K3 definition the reviewer's checking against."
**Common misuse:** Writing it as a general clinical narrative instead of mapping language directly onto the specific coverage policy text a reviewer is auditing against.

### L-code
The HCPCS Level II billing code set specific to orthotic and prosthetic components and services, used for reimbursement and tied to documentation requirements (including K-level, for lower-limb items).
**In use:** "That knee's L-code requires K3-or-above documentation on file, or it gets denied on audit."
**Common misuse:** Selecting an L-code based on the component alone without confirming the supporting K-level and diagnosis documentation the code actually requires.

### Residual limb maturation
The 6–18 month post-amputation period during which limb volume decreases substantially as post-surgical edema resolves and muscle atrophies, typically requiring a preparatory (not definitive) prosthesis.
**In use:** "He's only four months out — he's still maturing, stay in the preparatory socket."
**Common misuse:** Treating maturation as a fixed universal timeline rather than something confirmed by stable volume across several consecutive visits before committing to a definitive socket.

### Energy cost of ambulation (by amputation level)
The measurable increase in oxygen consumption/effort a lower-limb amputee expends to walk at a given speed relative to a non-amputee, which rises with a more proximal amputation level — transfemoral (through the knee) amputees expend markedly more energy per meter walked than transtibial amputees at comparable speeds (Waters & Mulroy, 1999).
**In use:** "He's transfemoral and deconditioned — set the initial gait-training pace expecting a real energy penalty, not just 'a bit slower.'"
**Common misuse:** Assuming energy cost scales the same way for any "lower-limb amputee" regardless of level, which understates how much harder a transfemoral fitting has to work to be functional and overstates what a K-level goal is reasonable early on.
