# Orthotist/Prosthetist — Playbook

## Transtibial (below-knee) gait deviation-to-cause table

Filled worksheet — observe the deviation on the sound and prosthetic side, then work down the likely-cause column before touching a component.

| Deviation observed | Most likely cause | Second most likely | Check first |
|---|---|---|---|
| Vaulting (rising onto sound-side toes in mid-swing) | Prosthesis too long, or insufficient swing-phase toe clearance | Patient fear of stubbing toe after a prior stumble | Measure prosthetic vs. sound-limb length with patient standing; ask about recent trips |
| Lateral trunk bending toward prosthetic side | Prosthesis too short, or socket too narrow mediolaterally | Residual-limb pain causing the patient to lean off the load | Length check first; if length is correct, palpate residual limb for tender points under load |
| Circumduction (swinging leg outward in an arc) | Prosthesis too long, or knee/ankle too stiff to clear the ground | Habit carried over from an earlier, longer prosthesis | Compare current vs. prior prosthesis length; check ankle/knee range |
| Drop-off / early heel-rise at terminal stance | Foot too stiff (keel too rigid) or dorsiflexion stop set too early | Socket set in excessive flexion | Flex/extend test with hand pressure on keel; review bench alignment sheet |
| Foot slap at heel strike | Heel cushion/plantarflexion resistance too soft | Foot set in excessive dorsiflexion | Compress heel wedge by hand and compare stiffness to prescribed durometer |
| Medial or lateral whip at heel-off | Socket or foot rotated (malaligned) relative to the knee axis | Excessive socket flexion or extension | Check rotational alignment marks against bench-alignment reference lines |
| Uneven step length (shorter step on sound side) | Patient apprehension/pain bearing weight on prosthetic side | Socket fit too loose (pistoning) causing instability | Ask about pain; check for pistoning with patient standing and shifting weight |
| Abducted gait (wide-based walking) | Socket too large mediolaterally, or medial wall relief excessive | Habit from a prior socket that was genuinely oversized | Check medial-lateral socket fit under load; review fitting history |

## Sock-ply volume-management worksheet (filled example)

Patient: mature transtibial residual limb, TSB definitive socket fabricated to a mid-day reference circumference of 31.0 cm at the mid-patellar-tendon level.

| Time of day | Measured circumference | Delta vs. 31.0 cm reference | Sock action |
|---|---|---|---|
| 7:00 AM (post-overnight, limb larger from recumbency) | 32.0 cm | +1.0 cm (limb larger than reference) | No sock, or liner only — socket will feel snug without added ply |
| 12:00 PM (reference point) | 31.0 cm | 0 | Baseline sock combination (e.g., one 3-ply sock over liner) |
| 5:00 PM (after full day, fluid shift out of limb) | 30.5 cm | −0.5 cm (limb smaller than reference) | Add a 1-ply sock over the baseline combination |
| 9:00 PM (end of long/active day) | 30.0 cm | −1.0 cm | Add a second 1-ply, or swap the 1-ply for a 3-ply, over baseline |

Rule of thumb used in practice: socks are labeled by ply (1, 3, 5), and the combination is built empirically per patient — add the thinnest ply that restores snugness, check for pistoning and comfort, then commit. Total added ply drifting past roughly 15-ply-equivalent over the original fitting, or a socket needing sock changes within the same day despite a stable routine the week before, is the trigger for a refit rather than more socks (see `references/red-flags.md`).

## Alignment sequence, worked example (transtibial)

1. **Bench alignment.** With no patient in the socket: set the socket's anterior-posterior reference line through the mid-patellar-tendon point and the socket's flexion angle at 5° flexion (typical starting reference for a TSB transtibial socket), foot in neutral rotation, standard trochanter-knee-ankle reference line vertical. Record the starting numbers on the alignment sheet.
2. **Static alignment.** Patient standing, weight evenly split via a plumb line or load-line device (e.g., an L.A.S.A.R. Posture-type system): confirm the load line passes through the prescribed reference point at the socket and near the center of the foot. A load line falling more than roughly 1 cm off the target at the foot is corrected here, before any walking.
3. **Dynamic alignment.** Patient walks; observe stance-phase knee stability and swing-phase foot clearance. Make small angular adjustments (on the order of 1–2° at a time — alignment is sensitive enough that changes of a couple of degrees measurably shift socket reaction moments) and re-observe before stacking further changes.
4. **Sign-off.** Only after a deviation has resolved at the appropriate stage (bench/static/dynamic) does the case move to definitive fabrication; a deviation "solved" by skipping ahead to dynamic tends to resurface once the patient's gait changes with practice on the device.

## Orthosis selection quick-reference

| Presentation | Default orthosis | When to escalate |
|---|---|---|
| Isolated foot drop, no spasticity, intact quads | Posterior leaf spring (PLS) AFO | Spasticity or medial-lateral instability present → hinged/articulated AFO |
| Foot drop plus mild spasticity or need for plantarflexion control | Hinged (articulated) AFO with dorsiflexion assist/stop | Recurrent knee instability despite correct ankle control → reassess for GRAFO |
| Genu recurvatum or quadriceps weakness needing a knee-extension moment in stance | Ground-reaction (floor-reaction) AFO | Weakness extends proximal to the knee (hip-level instability) → KAFO evaluation |
| Flail limb / no volitional knee control (e.g., polio, complete quad paralysis) | KAFO with a stance-control or locking knee joint | — |
