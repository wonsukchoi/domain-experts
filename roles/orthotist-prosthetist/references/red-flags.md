# Orthotist/Prosthetist — Red Flags

### Socket feels loose within the same day despite a stable sock routine the week before
- **Usually means:** Residual-limb volume has genuinely decreased (maturation/shrinkage), outpacing what sock additions can compensate for; second most likely is a new medical issue (dehydration, medication change, illness) causing rapid fluid loss.
- **First question:** "How many plies are you wearing now compared to two weeks ago, and has that number kept climbing?"
- **Data to pull:** Sock-ply log from recent visits; residual-limb circumference measurements over the last 3–4 visits.

### Verrucous hyperplasia or a distal "choke" ring on the residual limb
- **Usually means:** Pistoning from a socket that has become too large (volume loss without a refit) or inadequate suspension, causing repeated distal friction/negative pressure; second most likely is inadequate donning technique leaving an air gap.
- **First question:** "Show me exactly how you're donning the socket right now."
- **Data to pull:** Direct observation of donning; socket volume/fit check under load; suspension system inspection.

### Redness on the residual limb that hasn't faded 20+ minutes after doffing
- **Usually means:** A focal high-pressure area in the socket (relief zone insufficient or shifted); second most likely, in a diabetic or vascular patient, early tissue breakdown that needs same-week evaluation, not a routine-interval follow-up.
- **First question:** "Point to exactly where it's red, and how long after you take the prosthesis off before it fades."
- **Data to pull:** Location mapped against the socket's pressure-relief diagram; vascular/sensory status from the chart; time-to-fade if the patient can track it.

### Gait deviation that reappears within weeks of a component change that "fixed" it
- **Usually means:** The original cause was alignment (bench or static), not the component, and the swap only masked it temporarily; second most likely is volume change altering effective alignment.
- **First question:** "When exactly did it come back — right after a specific activity, or gradually?"
- **Data to pull:** Original alignment sheet from before the component change; current bench/static alignment re-check.

### K-level in the chart hasn't been updated in 12+ months despite a clear change in the patient's activity
- **Usually means:** Documentation lag, not a clinical ceiling — the patient's actual function has moved but nobody re-assessed and re-documented it.
- **First question:** "What does this patient actually do in a typical week now, compared to when this K-level was set?"
- **Data to pull:** Most recent physician note; objective functional test (6-minute walk test, timed up-and-go) if not already on file.

### Patient reports a stumble or near-fall with no clear mechanical cause found on inspection
- **Usually means:** Alignment has drifted slightly (a degree or two) since the last visit, or volume loss has changed effective socket fit under dynamic load; second most likely is a genuine component-level control issue (knee stability, ankle range).
- **First question:** "Was it on level ground or a transition — curb, stairs, uneven surface?"
- **Data to pull:** Full bench/static/dynamic alignment re-check; socket fit check under load; component function test independent of alignment.

### Definitive socket fitted and fabricated based on a single-time-of-day measurement
- **Usually means:** The socket will fit correctly only at that time of day and be loose or tight the rest of the day, generating repeat "it doesn't fit" complaints that look like a fabrication error but are a fitting-protocol gap.
- **First question:** "What time of day was the casting/scanning done, and does the complaint track a specific time of day?"
- **Data to pull:** Circumference measurements at two or more times of day going forward; casting/scan timestamp from the record.

### Payer denial citing "K-level not supported" on a component that was clinically appropriate
- **Usually means:** The letter of medical necessity described clinical reasoning without mapping it to the specific LCD language and functional-level definition the reviewer is checking against.
- **First question:** "Does the letter quote the K-level definition itself, or does it just describe the patient?"
- **Data to pull:** The submitted letter of medical necessity; the relevant LCD's exact K-level text; objective functional test results on file.

### AFO patient reports new knee buckling that wasn't present at dispensing
- **Usually means:** Ankle-orthosis alignment has drifted (dorsiflexion stop no longer providing the same knee-extension moment) or muscle strength has changed; second most likely is the shoe being worn has changed heel height, altering the orthosis's effective alignment.
- **First question:** "Are you wearing the same shoes you were fitted with?"
- **Data to pull:** Shoe heel-height check against the fitting record; orthosis alignment re-check; strength/tone re-assessment if shoes are unchanged.

### Patient requesting a specific advanced component (e.g., microprocessor knee) after seeing it online
- **Usually means:** Legitimate functional need that hasn't been formally documented yet — the request itself isn't evidence, but it's a prompt to run the objective testing that would support or rule out the upgrade.
- **First question:** "What are you finding hard to do right now with the current device, specifically?"
- **Data to pull:** Current K-level documentation date; objective functional test (6MWT, TUG); vocational/recreational demand details.
