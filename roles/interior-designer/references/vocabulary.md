# Interior Designer — Vocabulary

### Clear floor space
The minimum unobstructed floor area (typically 30"×48") required for a single stationary wheelchair to approach a fixture or element.
**In use:** "The vending-machine alcove doesn't have clear floor space in front of it — the trash can is sitting inside the 30-by-48."
**Common misuse:** Treated as interchangeable with a turning radius; clear floor space is for a stationary forward or parallel approach, not for turning around.

### Turning radius / turning space
The minimum area (60" diameter circle, or a T-shaped space) required for a wheelchair user to make a 180-degree turn.
**In use:** "We need a full 60-inch turning circle at the reception desk, not just clear floor space — a guest has to be able to turn away from the counter."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to be satisfied by any open-looking area without measuring the actual unobstructed diameter against furniture and fixture overhangs.

### Reach range
The vertical zone (forward or side approach) within which a control, dispenser, or operable element must be mounted to be reachable from a seated position.
**In use:** "That thermostat is at 52 inches AFF — outside the 48-inch forward reach-range maximum, it needs to come down or move to a side approach."
**Common misuse:** Using the "standard" mounting height from a manufacturer's cut sheet without checking whether the specific approach at that location is forward or side, since the allowable range differs between the two.

### FF&E
Furniture, Fixtures, and Equipment — the movable/non-structural items specified and procured separately from the building's fixed construction.
**In use:** "The FF&E budget is separate from the construction allowance — casegoods and seating come out of this line, not the general contractor's scope."
**Common misuse:** Conflated with the finish schedule; finishes (flooring, paint, millwork) are typically part of the construction contract, while FF&E is a separate procurement track with its own lead times.

### Footcandle
A unit of illuminance measuring the amount of light reaching a surface, used to set and verify lighting-design targets by task.
**In use:** "Task lighting at the reception desk needs to hit 50 footcandles at the work surface — the pendant plan alone only gets us to 42."
**Common misuse:** Confused with lumens (a fixture's total light output) — footcandles measure light landing on a specific surface, which depends on distance, fixture spacing, and reflectance, not just the fixture's rated output.

### Wear rating / wear layer
A manufacturer-assigned classification (e.g., ASTM flooring wear-class, upholstery double-rub count) indicating a material's durability under repeated use.
**In use:** "This LVT is only rated Class 22 residential — we need Class 33 commercial-heavy for a lobby with this much foot traffic."
**Common misuse:** Assumed uniform across a product line; the same visual pattern is often sold in multiple wear-tiers at different price points, and specifying by appearance alone can select the wrong tier.

### Egress width vs. accessible route width
Two related but distinct code requirements: egress width is calculated from occupant load for life-safety exiting; accessible route width (36" typical, 32" min at doorways) is a separate accessibility minimum that applies regardless of occupant load.
**In use:** "The corridor clears the accessible-route minimum at 36 inches, but we still need the architect to confirm the egress-width calc for this occupant load separately."
**Common misuse:** Treating a corridor that satisfies one requirement as automatically satisfying the other — the two are calculated independently and the larger of the two governs.

### Allowance
A client-stated budget ceiling, usually per square foot or as a lump sum, against which finish and FF&E selections are priced.
**In use:** "We're $12 over the $42-per-square-foot allowance on flooring alone — I want to bring an alternative before we price the rest of the schedule."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a target to hit exactly rather than a ceiling to stay under, leading to selections priced right at the limit with zero room for the inevitable field-verification cost bump.

### Long-lead item
A specified material, fixture, or furniture piece with a lead time (commonly 8+ weeks) long enough to affect the project schedule if not ordered early.
**In use:** "The custom casegoods are a long-lead item at 14 weeks — we need client sign-off this week or the move-in date slips."
**Common misuse:** Not distinguished from stock items until purchase-order stage, when the lead time becomes a schedule surprise instead of a planned milestone.

### Punch list
The final walkthrough list of incomplete, incorrect, or non-compliant items to be corrected before project closeout.
**In use:** "The punch list flagged the reception turning radius as non-compliant as-built, even though it passed as-designed — the planter got moved back during install."
**Common misuse:** Limited to aesthetic and finish-quality items, omitting a re-check of accessible-route and clearance dimensions as actually built, which can differ from the approved design.

### Specification / spec
The written document naming the exact product, finish code, manufacturer, and installation method for a given item — distinct from the drawing that shows its location.
**In use:** "The drawing shows where the tile goes; the spec says it has to be this manufacturer's SKU in this exact finish, no 'or equal' substitution without approval."
**Common misuse:** Using "similar to" language without a defined substitution-approval process, which lets a contractor swap in a lower-cost product that doesn't match the intended performance or appearance.

### COM (customer's own material)
An arrangement where the client or designer supplies the fabric or material directly to a furniture manufacturer for upholstery, rather than selecting from the manufacturer's standard offerings.
**In use:** "This chair is COM — we're sending our own fabric yardage to the factory, so the lead time includes fabric production, not just the frame."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to follow the same lead time as a standard-fabric order; COM often adds weeks because the fabric has to arrive at the factory before upholstery work starts.

### Value engineering (VE)
A cost-reduction process that substitutes lower-cost materials, systems, or scope to bring a project within budget, ideally without compromising design intent or code compliance.
**In use:** "The VE pass on this project cut the stone accent wall to a porcelain look-alike — the design intent survives, the budget doesn't."
**Common misuse:** Applied to code-minimum or life-safety elements (clearances, fire-rated assemblies) as if they carry the same negotiating room as an aesthetic finish — those have zero built-in margin to cut.
