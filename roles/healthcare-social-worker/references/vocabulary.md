# Healthcare Social Worker — Vocabulary

### LACE index
A validated readmission-risk score combining Length of stay, Acuity of admission, Comorbidity burden, and prior ED visits into a single 0–19 composite.
**In use:** "Her LACE came back at 14, so we're escalating to home health plus a confirmed 7-day follow-up."
**Common misuse:** Treating LACE as a diagnosis-severity score rather than a utilization-risk score — a low-acuity patient with heavy prior ED use can still score high.

### Warm handoff
A direct, real-time (usually phone or in-person) transfer of clinical information to the receiving clinician, as opposed to a written referral alone.
**In use:** "This is a warm handoff, not just a fax — I want to confirm the SNF has the diuretic-dosing note before she arrives."
**Common misuse:** Calling a completed referral fax a "handoff" — a handoff requires two-way confirmation, not one-way transmission.

### Discharge disposition
The specific post-hospital destination (home, home with home health, SNF, hospice, etc.) determined by matching functional need against payer authorization criteria.
**In use:** "Her disposition depends on whether utilization review authorizes SNF-level care given the documented transfer deficits."
**Common misuse:** Using "discharge disposition" to mean only "where the patient wants to go" rather than the clinical-and-payer-constrained decision it actually is.

### Medical necessity documentation
Chart documentation written specifically to meet a payer's authorization criteria for a given level of post-acute care, using numbered functional deficits rather than general descriptors.
**In use:** "Rewrite that as medical necessity documentation — 'generalized weakness' won't authorize SNF, but 'max assist x2 for transfers' will."
**Common misuse:** Assuming clinically accurate documentation is automatically payer-legible — the two audiences require different levels of specificity.

### Caregiver capacity
The caregiver's actual, verified ability to provide a specific type and amount of assistance — as distinct from their stated willingness.
**In use:** "He says he'll manage everything, but we haven't verified caregiver capacity for the physical transfers she needs."
**Common misuse:** Treating a caregiver's verbal agreement as sufficient documentation without confirming hours, physical ability, or competing obligations.

### Psychosocial barrier
A non-medical factor — cost, transportation, housing, caregiver support, health literacy — that materially affects a patient's ability to follow a discharge plan and avoid readmission.
**In use:** "The readmission wasn't a medical failure — it was an unscreened psychosocial barrier: she couldn't afford the diuretic refill."
**Common misuse:** Treating psychosocial screening as a compliance checkbox rather than the likely mechanism behind recurrent utilization.

### Teach-back
A verification technique where the patient explains a care instruction back in their own words, used to confirm actual understanding rather than passive agreement.
**In use:** "Teach-back on the medication schedule failed twice — we need a 48-hour follow-up call, not just written instructions."
**Common misuse:** Substituting "do you understand?" (a yes/no prompt) for genuine teach-back, which has a much higher false-positive rate for comprehension.

### Utilization review
The payer-side clinical review process that determines whether a requested level of post-acute care (SNF, home health, extended stay) meets authorization criteria.
**In use:** "Utilization review is going to deny this SNF request unless we document a specific skilled need, not just debility."
**Common misuse:** Treating utilization review as an adversarial rubber-stamp process rather than a criteria-driven review that responds to specific documentation.

### Least-restrictive discharge destination
The lowest-intensity post-acute setting that can safely and adequately meet the patient's documented functional and clinical needs.
**In use:** "Home health can address her deficits — SNF would be more restrictive than her actual need requires."
**Common misuse:** Defaulting to the most restrictive setting (SNF) "to be safe" without assessing whether a lower-intensity option would actually be adequate — this is itself a documented failure mode, not caution.

### Charlson comorbidity index
A weighted score summarizing a patient's chronic disease burden, used as an input to composite risk tools like LACE.
**In use:** "Her Charlson comes out to 4 given the CHF, diabetes, and CKD stage 3 — that's the C component of her LACE score."
**Common misuse:** Confusing the Charlson index (a standardized, weighted score) with an informal count of diagnoses on the problem list.

### Non-emergency medical transportation (NEMT)
A payer-covered transportation benefit (common under Medicaid and some Medicare Advantage plans) for getting to medical appointments, distinct from emergency transport.
**In use:** "She has NEMT coverage through her plan — let's book it for the cardiology follow-up before she leaves, not leave it to her to arrange."
**Common misuse:** Assuming NEMT exists or is authorized without verifying the specific plan's benefit and booking window.

### Skilled need
A clinical service (IV medication, wound care, complex medication titration) that specifically requires a licensed clinician to perform or supervise, as distinct from custodial/personal care.
**In use:** "Home health will authorize on the skilled need for IV diuretic titration, not on general assistance with ADLs alone."
**Common misuse:** Requesting home health or SNF based only on ADL assistance needs without identifying the skilled component payers actually require for authorization.
