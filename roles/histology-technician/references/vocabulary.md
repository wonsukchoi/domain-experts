# Vocabulary

### Floater
A piece of tissue on a slide that doesn't belong to the specimen the slide is labeled for, arriving via cross-contamination at grossing, embedding, the water bath, or the microtome.
**In use:** "There's a floater on the A2 slide — pull the block and check it against yesterday's run before we assume it's nothing."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a cosmetic stain artifact to wipe or note and move past, rather than a possible specimen-identity event that needs block-level investigation.

### Fixation
The chemical process (typically formaldehyde crosslinking protein) that stops tissue autolysis and preserves structure for processing.
**In use:** "This one's only had four hours in formalin and it's 2cm thick — that's not fixed yet, that's marinating."
**Common misuse:** Reduced to a time-only checkbox ("it sat in formalin overnight") while ignoring the specimen:fixative ratio and thickness, the two variables that actually determine whether the center fixed.

### Decalcification (decal)
Chemical removal of calcium from bone or heavily calcified tissue so it can be sectioned on a standard microtome.
**In use:** "Run the oxalate test before you pull it — don't just go by the clock, this femur head is denser than the last one."
**Common misuse:** Timed by elapsed days rather than a chemical end-point test, which over-decalcifies (destroying antigenicity for later IHC) or under-decalcifies (causing tearing and chatter) depending on bone density that varies case to case.

### Antigen retrieval (HIER)
Heat-induced epitope retrieval — reversing formalin's protein crosslinking at specific epitope sites so an antibody can bind for IHC.
**In use:** "That antibody's validated for citrate pH 6, not EDTA — switch the retrieval buffer before you touch the concentration."
**Common misuse:** Treated as one universal step rather than antibody-specific; the retrieval buffer and pH are part of that antibody's validation, not an interchangeable default.

### Microtomy chatter
Fine, regular compression lines across a section caused by blade vibration relative to the block face.
**In use:** "That's chatter, not a bad stain — reface the block and check the blade before you touch the hematoxylin."
**Common misuse:** Lumped into vague "bad technique" rather than traced to its specific mechanical cause (dull/nicked blade, wrong clearance angle, block temperature).

### Bread-loafing
Serially sectioning a specimen into parallel slices before or during fixation to expose more surface area to the fixative.
**In use:** "This is 3cm thick intact — bread-loaf it at half-centimeter intervals before it goes in the jar overnight."
**Common misuse:** Skipped on large intact specimens under time pressure on the assumption that surface immersion in formalin is equivalent to adequate fixation throughout.

### Differentiation (H&E)
The step after hematoxylin staining that removes excess stain (typically in acid alcohol) to leave nuclei crisply defined rather than uniformly dark.
**In use:** "Cut the differentiation time down a few seconds — everything's coming out over-blued."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a minor cosmetic step rather than a controlled, timed reaction; over- or under-differentiation directly changes whether nuclear detail is diagnostically readable.

### Positive/negative control (IHC)
Tissue known to express (positive) or lack (negative) the target antigen, run alongside patient tissue in the same batch to validate that the stain worked.
**In use:** "The positive control's flat — this whole run's invalid, it's not that the patient's negative."
**Common misuse:** Patient negative results accepted at face value without first confirming the positive control actually performed in that same run.

### Turnaround time (TAT)
The elapsed time from specimen accession to case sign-out, tracked as a lab quality metric.
**In use:** "Our TAT number looks fine overall, but embedding is where every delayed case is actually stacking up."
**Common misuse:** Measured only end-to-end without breaking it into pipeline stages (gross, fix, process, embed, cut, stain), which hides exactly where the real bottleneck sits.

### Embedding orientation
The physical positioning of tissue in the paraffin block so the microtome cuts the plane the pathologist actually needs to assess (e.g., perpendicular to an inked margin).
**In use:** "Face it so the margin's in the cutting plane — if it's embedded flat we can't call that margin at all."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a purely mechanical placement step; wrong orientation silently invalidates margin or orientation-dependent assessment no matter how clean the resulting stain looks.

### Frozen section
A rapid, unfixed technique using a cryostat to produce a slide within minutes, typically for intraoperative margin or diagnosis decisions.
**In use:** "Frozen call is preliminary — permanent sections after fixation are what the final report hangs on."
**Common misuse:** Treated as equivalent quality to a permanent section rather than a deliberately faster, lower-fidelity method with known freezing artifacts that can obscure fine architecture.

### Cassette
The small perforated container holding a tissue sample through processing and embedding, labeled to track it back to its specimen and case.
**In use:** "Check the cassette numbers against the gross log before you load the processor — a mislabel here becomes a floater later."
**Common misuse:** Assumed interchangeable or "just a container," when cassette mislabeling is one of the most common root causes of floaters and specimen-identity errors downstream.

### Water bath (flotation bath)
A heated water bath used to float and flatten sections after cutting, before they're picked up onto a slide.
**In use:** "Bath's running cold today — that's why everything's wrinkling, not the blade."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to be a fixed, "set and forget" piece of equipment, when a few degrees of drift produces the same wrinkling or tissue-loss artifacts usually blamed on cutting technique.

### Special stain
A non-H&E histochemical stain (e.g., PAS, trichrome, iron) targeting a specific tissue component to answer a specific morphologic question.
**In use:** "The differential's fungal versus foreign material — order a GMS, not a general panel."
**Common misuse:** Ordered reflexively as a broad panel without a specific differential in mind, burning through limited block face for stains that won't actually answer the question at hand.

### Recut
A newly cut section from an existing paraffin block, requested when the first slide is unusable or a different level/stain is needed.
**In use:** "We're two levels deep already on this core — one more recut and there may not be enough block left for the ordered IHC."
**Common misuse:** Requested casually as if it's a free, unlimited action, when a badly faced, thin, or heavily-recut block — especially on a small biopsy — can run out of usable tissue to answer the actual clinical question.
