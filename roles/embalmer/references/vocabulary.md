# Embalmer — Vocabulary

### Arterial injection
The primary embalming technique: injecting preservative fluid into the arterial system (typically via the carotid, femoral, or axillary artery) while blood and fluid drain from a corresponding vein.
**In use:** "Single-point right common carotid injection, standard rate."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a single fixed technique rather than one that changes site count and pattern based on case condition (autopsy status, vascular compromise).

### Index (fluid index)
A manufacturer's rating of an embalming fluid's formaldehyde concentration/strength before dilution — higher index means stronger fluid, used for more difficult preservation cases.
**In use:** "This case needs a 24-index fluid diluted up to a 25-index-equivalent working strength given the edema."
**Common misuse:** Confused with the final working-strength percentage in the injected solution — index describes the concentrated fluid, working strength describes it after dilution.

### Working strength
The final formaldehyde concentration of the arterial solution after dilution, typically expressed as a percentage.
**In use:** "Standard case runs about 5% working strength; this one's adjusted to 5.8%."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to scale linearly with index number alone, ignoring that dilution water volume is the other variable determining final strength.

### Cavity treatment
Aspiration of gas and fluid from the body cavities followed by injection of a separate, more concentrated cavity fluid — distinct from arterial injection, which doesn't reach the cavities directly.
**In use:** "Cavity treatment first on this one — there's gas distension, arterial injection alone won't address it."
**Common misuse:** Skipped or treated as optional on cases with decomposition indicators, when it's the step that specifically addresses cavity-level gas/fluid buildup arterial injection can't reach.

### Livor mortis
Post-mortem discoloration from blood settling under gravity, initially blanching (fades under pressure) and eventually fixing (permanent) as blood breaks down.
**In use:** "Livor is fixed, consistent with a longer time-since-death than the intake paperwork suggested."
**Common misuse:** Read as a precise clock rather than one data point among several (rigor, skin condition, marbling) used together to assess decomposition stage.

### Marbling
A vascular discoloration pattern (visible veins with a greenish-black or purple tint) caused by bacterial hemolysis — a reliable indicator that decomposition has begun.
**In use:** "There's marbling on the abdomen, so we're treating this as early decomposition regardless of the reported 10-hour interval."
**Common misuse:** Mistaken for bruising or a circulatory condition rather than recognized as a decomposition-specific sign requiring a treatment-plan adjustment.

### Restorative art
Reconstructive and cosmetic technique used to repair trauma, discoloration, or tissue loss to restore a natural appearance for viewing.
**In use:** "The trauma to the left temple needs restorative art before we can proceed to cosmetics."
**Common misuse:** Treated as pure cosmetics (makeup application) rather than a technical form-and-color-matching discipline that often precedes cosmetic work.

### Formaldehyde PEL / STEL
OSHA's Permissible Exposure Limit (8-hour time-weighted average, 0.75 ppm) and Short-Term Exposure Limit (15-minute average, 2 ppm) for formaldehyde under 29 CFR 1910.1048.
**In use:** "We're scheduling the high-index cases with gaps between them to stay under the PEL for the shift."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a per-case threshold rather than a cumulative shift-long exposure budget that every case on the schedule draws against.

### Six-point injection
A multi-site arterial injection technique (typically used on autopsied cases) using six injection points to reach vasculature that single-point carotid injection can't adequately distribute fluid through after autopsy disruption.
**In use:** "This is an autopsy case — six-point injection per facility protocol, not standard single-site."
**Common misuse:** Applied inconsistently or skipped in favor of single-point injection to save time, leading to uneven preservation in an autopsied case.

### Edema (case-condition factor)
Excess interstitial fluid in tissue, which locally dilutes injected arterial fluid and requires an index or volume adjustment to reach adequate preservation strength in the affected area.
**In use:** "Bilateral lower-extremity edema — bumping the index and cutting volume per the case-condition table."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a cosmetic swelling issue rather than a chemistry problem that changes the dilution-ratio calculation.

### Jaundice (case-condition factor)
Yellow-tinted skin/tissue discoloration from bilirubin buildup, requiring both a chemical treatment adjustment (bleaching agent in the arterial fluid) and cosmetic correction for viewing.
**In use:** "Jaundice case — bleaching agent added to the fluid, and we'll need cosmetic correction on top of that."
**Common misuse:** Addressed only cosmetically (makeup) without the corresponding chemical-fluid adjustment, leaving the underlying discoloration to reassert itself.
