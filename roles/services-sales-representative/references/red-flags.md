# Red flags

Smell tests a territory sales manager catches reviewing a rep's pipeline or a deal file. Format: what it usually means, the first question to ask, the data to pull.

## 1. Proposal built from the incumbent's base rate only, no fee-stack teardown

**Usually means:** the "savings" number is inflated or wrong — fuel surcharges, environmental fees, and contamination/minimum charges routinely add 8–20% on top of a competitor's quoted rate, and a proposal that ignores them either overstates the win or misses the real gap entirely.

**First question:** "Did we pull their last three invoices, or are we quoting off their rate card?"

**Data to pull:** the incumbent's actual recent invoices if the prospect will share them; if not, the typical fee-stack pattern for that vendor/category from past teardown files.

## 2. Deal marked "in progress" with no cancellation-notice deadline logged

**Usually means:** nobody checked the incumbent's contract terms, so the rep doesn't actually know whether this deal can close inside the current term or is racing a deadline that already passed.

**First question:** "What's the notice deadline on their current contract, and did we confirm it in writing?"

**Data to pull:** the incumbent contract's renewal and notice-window clauses (from the prospect, or from the category's typical terms if unavailable); today's date against that deadline.

## 3. New logo booked with no install date on the calendar

**Usually means:** the deal is being counted as won before operations has confirmed it can actually be serviced on the promised schedule — a common source of 90-day churn when the "win" turns into a missed first pickup or delayed delivery.

**First question:** "What's the confirmed install date, and has ops signed off on the route/schedule?"

**Data to pull:** ops capacity confirmation for the promised schedule; route assignment; first-service date logged in the CRM, not just "closed-won" status.

## 4. Save call opens with a discount before root cause is discussed

**Usually means:** the rep is treating a cancellation as a price negotiation instead of a service diagnosis — this both fails to fix the actual trigger (which will cause the next cancellation too) and trains the account that threatening to leave is how you get a lower rate.

**First question:** "What specifically triggered the cancellation — pull the account's last 6 months of service incidents before the call."

**Data to pull:** missed-service count, average replacement/repair time, billing disputes over the trailing 2 quarters for that account.

## 5. Blended booking number holding flat while attrition rate rises

**Usually means:** new-logo bookings are masking a decaying existing book — the same failure mode as a blended CAC hiding a decaying channel. By the time the blended number visibly drops, the underlying attrition has usually been running for a quarter or more.

**First question:** "Show me new-logo revenue and lost-account revenue separately, by month, for the last two quarters."

**Data to pull:** gross new bookings vs. gross attrition (lost accounts × their revenue) by month; net figure only after both are visible independently.

## 6. Commission plan pays the same rate on a renewal as on a brand-new logo

**Usually means:** the incentive structure rewards churn-and-rebook behavior — a rep can quietly let an account lapse and re-sign it (or a near-identical replacement) at new-logo commission rather than retain it at a lower renewal rate, and the plan won't show the difference.

**First question:** "Are any of this quarter's 'new logos' actually win-backs or re-signs of an account that lapsed in the last 12 months?"

**Data to pull:** new-logo list cross-referenced against the lost-account list from the trailing year; commission paid by deal type.

## 7. Account signed on price alone with no documented service-failure trigger

**Usually means:** a purely price-driven switcher is a high early-churn risk — the same sensitivity that brought them in will take them to the next price-cutter, and there's no reliability story to fall back on if a competitor undercuts your renewal price.

**First question:** "What was the actual trigger for this prospect taking the meeting — a specific complaint, or an unsolicited price shop?"

**Data to pull:** discovery notes on the stated reason for the meeting; whether any service-failure evidence was gathered, or the deal was priced-to-win from the first call.

## 8. Win rate stable but average deal size shrinking quarter over quarter

**Usually means:** the rep (or the territory) is chasing smaller, easier-to-close accounts instead of the harder, larger displacements that actually grow the territory — a stable win rate on shrinking deal size can look healthy on a scoreboard while the territory's real growth stalls.

**First question:** "What's the average deal size and the largest deal closed each of the last four quarters?"

**Data to pull:** deal-size distribution by quarter; pipeline mix by account size; whether large-account prospecting activity has dropped off.

## 9. RFP response submitted with no visibility on the incumbent's contract end date

**Usually means:** the response was built blind to whether the opportunity is even live this cycle — some RFPs are run as a required formality by procurement while the account has no intention of switching before its own renewal date, and pursuing it burns a proposal cycle for a deal that can't close.

**First question:** "Do we know the incumbent's contract end date, or is this RFP a routine market-check?"

**Data to pull:** any public procurement record of the current contract's term; prior-year RFP outcome if this is a recurring bid, to check whether it's historically a real competition or a formality.

## 10. First 90-day service metrics not tracked on newly signed accounts

**Usually means:** the account is invisible to the retention track until it either renews cleanly or cancels — by the time a 90-day churn shows up as a lost account, the early warning signs (a missed first pickup, a late delivery, a billing setup error) went unmonitored.

**First question:** "What's the on-time percentage and complaint count for this account's first 90 days?"

**Data to pull:** service log for the account since install date; comparison against the territory's baseline on-time rate for newly installed accounts.
