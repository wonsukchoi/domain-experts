# Vocabulary

Terms generalists conflate that territory reps and their managers keep sharply separate. Each: definition, how a practitioner actually uses it, and the common misuse.

## Route density / stop density

The revenue (or account count) serviced per mile or per driving hour on a given route. Determines whether a new account is profitable to add versus whether it requires a new spur route with its own drive-time cost.

**In use:** "That account's a good rate, but it's nine miles off Route C — check density before you promise a schedule."

**Common misuse:** evaluating a new account purely on quoted price or contract value without checking whether it fits an existing route; a fairly priced deal outside route density can still be a negative-margin win.

## Attrition rate vs. churn

**Attrition rate** is the percentage of the existing account base lost over a period, usually tracked monthly or annualized. **Churn** is used loosely for the same thing but sometimes bundles in downsells or scope reductions that aren't full cancellations. Practitioners keep "lost account" (full cancellation) separate from "reduced scope" (still a customer, smaller contract).

**In use:** "Our attrition rate is 1.8% monthly, but half of that is scope reduction, not lost accounts — the true lost-account number is 0.9%."

**Common misuse:** reporting a single churn number that mixes cancellations and downsells, which hides whether the real problem is losing accounts or shrinking them.

## Accessorial fee / fuel surcharge

Charges added on top of a service's base rate — fuel surcharge, environmental fee, contamination fee, minimum service charge — that are often uncapped or variable and rarely shown in a headline rate comparison.

**In use:** "Their base rate's actually lower than ours, but their fuel surcharge is uncapped at 18% — reconstruct the true monthly total before you quote a savings number."

**Common misuse:** comparing two vendors on base rate alone, which is exactly what an incumbent's fee stack is designed to survive scrutiny on.

## Auto-renewal clause / evergreen contract

A contract term that renews automatically for another period unless the customer sends written cancellation inside a specific notice window before the term ends. Missing that window locks the customer in for a full additional term regardless of dissatisfaction or a better competing offer.

**In use:** "Their contract is evergreen with a 60-day notice window — if we don't get a signature by the 15th, we've lost this cycle entirely."

**Common misuse:** treating the contract's stated term-end date as the actual deadline; the real deadline is the notice window, which falls well before term-end.

## Notice window / cancellation window

The specific date range (commonly 30–90 days before contract term-end) during which a customer must submit written cancellation to avoid auto-renewal. Distinct from the term-end date itself.

**In use:** "Term ends December 1, but the notice window closes November 15 — that's the date we're actually racing."

**Common misuse:** confusing the notice window with the term-end date, which can cost the entire sales cycle if discovery and proposal work aren't finished 15+ days before the real deadline.

## Same-store growth vs. new-logo growth

**Same-store growth** (also called organic growth within the base) is revenue increase from existing accounts — cross-sell, upsell, price increases that stick. **New-logo growth** is revenue from newly signed accounts. A territory can hit its total number entirely from new logos while the existing base shrinks, which isn't the same health signal.

**In use:** "New-logo bookings are up 12%, but same-store is down 4% — net growth looks fine and the base is actually eroding."

**Common misuse:** reporting a single blended growth number that lets new-logo bookings mask a decaying existing book.

## Hunter vs. farmer

**Hunter** activity is new-account acquisition — prospecting, displacing incumbents, cold outreach. **Farmer** activity is managing and growing the existing book — retention, cross-sell, renewal negotiation. Some reps are compensated and measured on both; the two require different weekly behavior and should be tracked as separate numbers.

**In use:** "He's a strong hunter but his book's attrition is above territory average — he's not doing the farmer half of the job."

**Common misuse:** using one blended "sales number" for a rep who carries both responsibilities, which hides whether growth is coming from new business or masking losses in the existing base.

## Total cost of ownership (TCO) vs. sticker price

**Sticker price** is the quoted base rate. **TCO** is the full cost including fees, escalators, minimum charges, and any switching or onboarding costs, over the life of the contract. Two vendors with identical sticker prices can have meaningfully different TCO.

**In use:** "Their sticker's ten cents cheaper per unit, but once you add their uncapped escalator, our TCO wins over a three-year term."

**Common misuse:** presenting or evaluating a "savings" number built from the base rate alone, ignoring fees and escalators on both sides of the comparison.

## Save call / win-back campaign

A **save call** is the structured attempt to retain an account that has given cancellation notice, run by diagnosing root cause before offering any concession. A **win-back campaign** is the separate, later effort to re-approach an account that already left, once enough time has passed that a competitor's own service issues are likely to have surfaced.

**In use:** "The save call didn't work, but log the root cause — that account goes on the win-back list for a check-in in nine months."

**Common misuse:** treating a save call as a one-time discount offer rather than a diagnostic process, or skipping the win-back list entirely once an account is marked lost.

## RFP vs. RFQ vs. sole-source

An **RFP** (request for proposal) invites vendors to propose a solution and pricing, typically used when service design matters, not just price. An **RFQ** (request for quote) is a price-only competition against a defined, fixed scope. **Sole-source** means no competition is run — often because an incumbent relationship or regulatory reason exempts the purchase from bidding.

**In use:** "This is an RFQ, not an RFP — they've already specified the exact scope, so differentiation has to come from price and terms, not service design."

**Common misuse:** responding to an RFQ with a service-design pitch when the buyer has already fixed the scope and is evaluating on price and terms alone, or treating a routine re-bid RFP as a live competitive opportunity when it's procurement running a required market-check with no real intent to switch.

## Escalator / CPI pass-through

A contract clause allowing the vendor to raise price during the contract term, commonly tied to the Consumer Price Index (CPI), either capped at a maximum annual percentage or uncapped. Distinct from a fee that appears and disappears (like a fuel surcharge) — an escalator is a permanent price increase.

**In use:** "Their escalator is CPI plus two points with no cap — over a five-year term that's a materially different number than our four-percent-capped version."

**Common misuse:** treating all price-increase mechanisms as the same "increase," without distinguishing a capped, predictable escalator from an uncapped one that can compound unpredictably over a multi-year term.

## Book of business

The full set of accounts a rep manages and is measured against for retention — distinct from their new-logo pipeline, which is prospective and not yet signed.

**In use:** "His book of business is 95 accounts worth $1.2M annually — the pipeline number is separate and doesn't replace it in his quota calculation."

**Common misuse:** conflating pipeline value (potential future business) with book of business (current signed, recurring revenue under management) when evaluating a rep's actual territory health.

## Close ratio / win rate

The percentage of qualified opportunities that convert to signed deals over a period. Distinct from activity metrics (calls made, meetings held), which measure effort, not conversion.

**In use:** "Close ratio's steady at 28%, but average deal size dropped — he's winning the same share of a weaker pipeline."

**Common misuse:** treating a stable or rising close ratio alone as evidence of a healthy territory, without checking deal size or pipeline quality alongside it.
