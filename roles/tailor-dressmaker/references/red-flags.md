# Red flags

### A specific diagnostic wrinkle (diagonal pull, horizontal pull, excess fabric bubble) is corrected by uniform resizing rather than a targeted adjustment at the diagnosed location
- **Usually means:** the correction will disturb areas that already fit correctly while not actually fixing the problem area.
- **First question:** what does the specific wrinkle's direction and location indicate about which pattern area needs adjustment?
- **Data to pull:** fitting notes describing the wrinkle's location and direction compared against the adjustment actually made.

### Ease allowance applied is the same fixed amount regardless of garment type or fabric
- **Usually means:** a garment technically made to measurement that still doesn't fit or drape as the design intended.
- **First question:** what ease allowance was actually used versus the standard range for this specific garment type and fabric?
- **Data to pull:** the ease allowance used compared against the standard range for this style/fabric combination.

### The same construction approach (seam allowance, stabilization) is used on a stretch or bias fabric as on a stable woven
- **Usually means:** the finished garment's fit or drape will differ from what was intended once the fabric's actual behavior asserts itself.
- **First question:** what did the fabric's stretch or bias test actually show, and does the construction approach account for it?
- **Data to pull:** fabric stretch/bias test results compared against the construction approach used.

### An alteration is quoted or promised (e.g. letting out a waist by a specific amount) without first checking the garment's actual available seam allowance
- **Usually means:** promising an adjustment the garment physically cannot deliver.
- **First question:** how much seam allowance does this specific garment actually have available to let out?
- **Data to pull:** the measured seam allowance compared against the amount of adjustment quoted.

### A pattern piece is cut without verifying grain line alignment against the body's structural axis
- **Usually means:** the finished garment will likely twist or hang incorrectly after wear or washing, even if it looks fine freshly pressed.
- **First question:** does the grain line marking on the pattern actually match how it was cut on the fabric?
- **Data to pull:** the grain line marking on the pattern piece compared against the actual cut on the fabric.

### A repeat customer's fit issue recurs across multiple garments in the same location
- **Usually means:** an unaddressed asymmetry or posture characteristic in the customer's body that a standard block pattern doesn't account for, not a series of unrelated cutting errors.
- **First question:** does this customer's fitting history show the same issue location across prior garments?
- **Data to pull:** the customer's fitting history across multiple garments.

### Final garment inspection skips checking grain/hang after the garment has been worn or laundered once, relying only on the freshly-pressed fitting
- **Usually means:** an off-grain cut or a fabric behavior issue won't be caught until the customer notices it later.
- **First question:** was a post-wear or post-wash check actually performed as part of the fitting process?
- **Data to pull:** whether a post-wear/wash check was completed and its result.
