# Vocabulary

### Ease (allowance)
The difference between a body measurement and the corresponding finished garment measurement, deliberately chosen per garment type and fabric.
**In use:** "This fitted jacket carries 2" of ease at the waist — a loose blouse in the same fabric might need 4" or more."
**Common misuse:** Applying a fixed ease amount regardless of the specific garment style or fabric, when the correct ease varies by how fitted or loose a design is intended to be.

### Grain line
The direction of a fabric's woven threads (lengthwise or crosswise) or the bias diagonal, which a pattern piece must be aligned to for correct drape and shape retention.
**In use:** "Check the grain line marking against the selvage before you cut — that's what keeps this from twisting after the first wash."
**Common misuse:** Cutting a pattern piece by eye without verifying it's actually aligned to the marked grain line, producing a garment that twists or hangs wrong after wear or washing even if it looks fine freshly cut.

### Muslin / toile
A test garment made in inexpensive fabric to check fit and pattern accuracy before cutting the final fabric.
**In use:** "Ran a muslin first on this one — the fit issues showed up before we touched the silk."
**Common misuse:** Skipping the test garment on a complex or high-stakes piece to save time, discovering fit problems only after the expensive final fabric has already been cut.

### Dart
A folded, stitched wedge of fabric used to shape a flat piece of fabric to a body's curve — bust, waist, shoulder.
**In use:** "That pull is a dart placement issue, not a size issue — move the bust dart up half an inch."
**Common misuse:** Treating every fit problem as solvable by adding fabric at the seam, when many fit issues are actually a dart placement or size problem instead.

### Bias (cutting)
Cutting fabric at a 45-degree angle to the grain, which gives the fabric more stretch and drape than straight-grain cutting.
**In use:** "This skirt's cut on the bias — expect it to stretch and hang differently than the straight-grain version."
**Common misuse:** Treating bias-cut fabric with the same seam allowance and construction technique as straight-grain fabric, ignoring that bias fabric behaves and stretches differently during both construction and wear.

### Seam allowance
The extra fabric beyond the stitching line, which determines how much a garment can later be let out or taken in.
**In use:** "Only got 5/8" seam allowance here — that limits how much we can actually let this out."
**Common misuse:** Quoting or promising an alteration amount without first checking how much actual seam allowance the specific garment has available.

### Balance (garment fit)
How evenly a garment hangs and distributes ease around the body, distinct from overall size.
**In use:** "It's not too tight overall — it's a balance problem localized to the back panel."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a "too tight" or "too loose" diagnosis applies uniformly around the whole garment, when a balance problem is often localized to one specific panel or seam.

### Block pattern (sloper)
A basic, fitted pattern template for a body type, used as the foundation for drafting styled patterns.
**In use:** "Drafted this design off the standard block, then adjusted for the customer's actual proportions."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a standard block automatically fits any customer's body without adjustment for individual variation — posture, asymmetry — from the block's assumed proportions.

### Let out / take in
Alteration techniques that use available seam allowance to increase or decrease a garment's finished measurement.
**In use:** "Can let this out about half an inch at the waist — that's the limit of the seam allowance on this dress."
**Common misuse:** Assuming any garment can be let out by any desired amount, without accounting for the physical limit set by its actual seam allowance.

### Drape
How fabric falls and moves on the body once constructed into a garment, determined by fabric weight, weave, and grain, not just the flat pattern shape.
**In use:** "Looked great on the dress form, but the drape changes completely once it's actually moving with the body."
**Common misuse:** Judging a pattern's success from how it looks flat or on a dress form alone, without accounting for how the specific fabric's drape will actually behave on a moving body.
