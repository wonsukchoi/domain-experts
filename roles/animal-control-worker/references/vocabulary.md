# Animal Control Worker — Vocabulary

### Rabies vector species (RVS)
A defined list of species (typically raccoons, skunks, foxes, bats, and coyotes) for which no validated rabies quarantine period exists, so capture-and-observe is not a substitute for testing.
**In use:** "It's a raccoon bite, not a dog — RVS doesn't get quarantined, we euthanize and submit for testing."
**Common misuse:** Applying the domestic 10-day quarantine framework to an RVS bite; the observation period only has validated meaning for dogs, cats, and ferrets.

### Dunbar bite scale
Dr. Ian Dunbar's 6-level scale grading dog bite severity from no skin contact (Level 1) through severe multiple-bite attacks (Level 6), used to standardize bite-report severity.
**In use:** "Puncture with tearing, four stitches — that's a clean Level 4, not just 'a bad bite.'"
**Common misuse:** Using "minor" or "major" as if they were interchangeable with the scale; Level 1–2 and Level 3+ route to materially different statutory tracks in most ordinances, so the specific level matters, not a rough category.

### Stray hold period
The statutory minimum number of days an impounded animal must be held before adoption, transfer, or euthanasia, giving an owner a defined window to reclaim it.
**In use:** "Hold doesn't expire until Thursday — can't release to rescue before then no matter how long she's been unclaimed-looking."
**Common misuse:** Treating the hold as advisory when an animal appears clearly abandoned; the clock is procedural and runs the same regardless of how the case looks.

### Dangerous dog vs. vicious dog
Two distinct statutory tiers in many ordinances: "dangerous" typically covers a single unprovoked bite breaking skin or a pattern of aggressive-but-non-biting incidents; "vicious" typically requires a more severe injury, a death, or a dangerous-dog violation committed again after classification.
**In use:** "One skin-breaking bite gets her classified dangerous — vicious is a higher bar we're not at yet."
**Common misuse:** Using the two terms interchangeably; they carry different containment requirements, fees, and sometimes mandatory-euthanasia consequences depending on jurisdiction.

### Quarantine (home vs. facility)
The two locations a bite-exposed domestic animal can serve its rabies observation period, chosen by vaccination status, bite history, and the owner's ability to secure the animal — not by owner preference alone.
**In use:** "Current vaccination, no priors — she qualifies for home quarantine if the owner signs the agreement."
**Common misuse:** Treating quarantine location as an owner convenience decision rather than a risk-based determination tied to documented criteria.

### Body condition score (BCS)
A standardized 9-point scale (Purina/WSAVA model) scoring an animal's fat and muscle condition from emaciated (1) to severely obese (9), used to document neglect objectively.
**In use:** "BCS 2 on intake, ribs and hip bones visible with no fat cover — that's the finding that supports the neglect citation."
**Common misuse:** Substituting a narrative description ("looked thin") for the numeric score; the score is what a hearing or court can weigh consistently, a description is not.

### Provocation
A legal concept in most bite statutes distinguishing an unprovoked bite (which typically triggers classification and citation) from a provoked one (teasing, trespass, attacking the animal first), which can be a full or partial defense to citation.
**In use:** "He's claiming provocation — the mail carrier was on the porch delivering, that's not going to hold up as provocation."
**Common misuse:** Treating provocation as relevant to the rabies-exposure protocol; it is not — the quarantine clock runs regardless of fault, provocation only affects citation and classification outcomes.

### Live-release rate
The percentage of shelter intake animals that leave alive (adoption, transfer, return-to-owner) rather than by euthanasia, commonly benchmarked against a 90% "no-kill" threshold.
**In use:** "We're running 92% live-release year to date — that's the shelter-wide number, not a promise on any one animal."
**Common misuse:** Applying the 90% benchmark to an individual case as a reason to avoid a documented-protocol euthanasia decision; it's a program-level metric, not an individual-animal rule.

### Chemical capture (immobilization)
Use of a dart or injectable sedative to restrain a large, dangerous, or otherwise uncatchable animal, typically requiring specialized certification distinct from standard catch-pole handling.
**In use:** "That's not a standard capture — loose livestock in traffic, we need the chemical-capture unit, not a catch pole."
**Common misuse:** Assuming any officer can perform chemical capture; it usually requires species-specific dosing knowledge and certification most general field officers don't hold.

### Return-to-owner (RTO)
The outcome where an impounded stray is reclaimed by its owner, typically requiring proof of ownership and payment of impound/quarantine fees before release.
**In use:** "Microchip scan matched an owner — this is an RTO once fees are settled, not an adoption-track animal."
**Common misuse:** Treating RTO as automatic once an owner is identified; unpaid fees or an active dangerous-dog classification can still condition or delay release.

### Catch pole
A restraint tool consisting of a rigid pole with a controllable loop, used to control an animal's head/neck at a distance without direct hand contact.
**In use:** "Catch pole first on the loose pit bull in the yard — no hands-on until she's secured."
**Common misuse:** Defaulting to the catch pole for every species; it is generally inappropriate or insufficient for wildlife-in-structure calls, livestock, or venomous/exotic animals, which need different equipment entirely.
