# Playbook

Filled protocols and templates for the recurring TA tasks. Populate the bracketed fields; the structure and thresholds are the point.

## 1. Grading norming session (before any shared exam/paper is scored)

1. Supervising TA or professor pulls 10-15 already-submitted answers to the question in dispute — real answers, not hypotheticals, spanning the plausible range from clearly-full-credit to clearly-no-credit.
2. Every grader scores the same 10-15 answers independently, blind to each other's scores, against the written rubric criteria one line at a time (not a single overall impression).
3. Compare scores per answer. Flag any answer where graders differ by more than 1 point out of a criterion worth 2, or more than 2 points out of a criterion worth 5.
4. For each flagged answer, the group states out loud which rubric words justify their score — the disagreement is almost always in how a phrase like "specific example" or "correctly applies the concept" is being read, not in effort.
5. Rewrite the ambiguous rubric line with the specific answer that caused the split as a worked example, then re-score that same batch to confirm convergence (target: all graders within 1 point on every criterion) before grading any live submissions.
6. Timebox: budget 30-45 minutes per norming session for a 15-answer, 3-criterion rubric; skipping this step to save time is the single most common cause of a grade-appeal spike two weeks later.

## 2. Grade-dispute response template

**First reply (same day, within a few hours):**

> "Thanks for flagging this — I want to look at it against the rubric rather than reply from memory. I'll have a full response by [24-48h target, name the day]. If you have a specific rubric line you think was misapplied, point me to it and I'll check that first."

**Resolution reply (within the stated window), grade stands:**

> "I re-checked question [N] against the rubric: [quote the exact criterion]. Your answer [specific reason it did/didn't meet the criterion — quote the answer, not a paraphrase]. The score stands. If you think the rubric itself should be interpreted differently, that's a call for [supervising professor] — happy to forward this exact exchange to them."

**Resolution reply, grade changes:**

> "You're right — I misread [specific line] against the rubric on re-check. Corrected score: [X → Y]. This will be updated in [gradebook] by [date]. Thanks for catching it; I'm also going to flag this to the other TAs so we regrade consistently if it affects other students."

## 3. Discussion-section structure (50-minute default block)

| Segment | Time | Structure |
|---|---|---|
| Retrieval warm-up | 5 min | Closed-book: 2-3 short-answer prompts from last week's material, written, not verbal — surfaces who actually retained it before anyone talks |
| Structured question 1 | 12 min | Written prompt on the board/slide → 2 min silent think time → 3+ sec wait after calling a name → cold-call by roster order, not raised hands |
| Small-group applied problem | 15 min | Groups of 3-4 work a problem tied directly to the current homework/exam question type, TA circulates and checks work, not just answers questions asked |
| Structured question 2 / misconception check | 10 min | Target the specific error the graded homework/exam showed was common — not a generic "any questions" |
| Exit ticket | 5 min | One graded-for-completion question submitted before leaving — gives a section-level read on what didn't land, before the next exam does |

Cold-call rotation: keep a roster checklist and mark who's been called on across the term; target every student called at least once every 3 sessions. This produces a participation record that survives a "you only call on X" complaint.

## 4. Office-hours triage

| Signal | Action |
|---|---|
| 0-1 students show up weekly, class of 100+ | Self-selection is filtering out students who need it most — announce office hours are for "any confusion, not just the stuck," and consider a signup sheet with named topics from recent grading to make attendance feel targeted rather than remedial |
| Same 2-3 students every week, high-performing | Fine as-is, but don't let this be the only signal used to judge "how the class is doing" |
| A queue forms only in the 48 hours before an exam | Predictable — block extra review-session time in that window instead of extending regular office hours, since the bottleneck is scheduling, not availability |
| A student repeatedly asks about content two sections behind | Flag to the supervising professor — this may be a case for the disability/accommodations office or academic support, not something to solve one office-hour visit at a time |

## 5. Exam-proctoring / academic-integrity first response

1. **During the exam:** if you observe a suspected violation (visible phone, matching wrong-answer patterns in adjacent seats, a crib sheet), do not confront the student mid-exam. Note the seat number, time, and exact observation in writing immediately.
2. **Do not** accuse, confiscate beyond standard proctoring procedure, or discuss the observation with other students in the room.
3. **After the exam:** report the observation to the supervising professor and follow the institution's academic-integrity office process — most institutions require the professor or the integrity office, not the TA, to initiate the formal charge.
4. **If asked by the student directly** ("did you see something?"), decline to discuss the details and redirect to the professor/integrity office — mid-process discussion can itself complicate the case.

## 6. Title IX / FERPA quick-reference

| Situation | Action |
|---|---|
| Student discloses harassment, assault, discrimination, or an immediate safety concern (office hours, email, or in a paper) | Report to the Title IX office / CARE team the same day. This is a report, not a promise of a specific outcome — tell the student you're obligated to report and what that does and doesn't mean for them. |
| Parent emails asking about their (adult) student's grade | Decline without a signed FERPA release on file; redirect the parent to the student or the registrar. Do not confirm the grade even to say it's "fine." |
| Another student asks how a classmate did | Decline. Do not discuss any student's grade, attendance, or performance with anyone other than that student and the supervising faculty member. |
| Posting grades | Never post grades against a name, full student ID, or any identifier a classmate could match to a person — use a private LMS gradebook, not a public spreadsheet or posted list, even anonymized-looking ones. |
