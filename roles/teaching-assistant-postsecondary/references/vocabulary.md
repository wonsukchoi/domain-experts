# Vocabulary

Terms generalists reliably misuse when talking about postsecondary TA work. Format per term: definition, how a practitioner actually uses it, the common misuse.

### Norming / calibration session

- **Definition:** a scheduled session where multiple graders independently score the same sample answers against a rubric before grading any live submissions, then reconcile disagreements by rewriting the ambiguous rubric line.
- **In use:** "We're not grading the final until we've normed on the proof question — last time we skipped it, the section spread was 0.7 points and it was entirely a rubric-reading gap, not a skill gap."
- **Common misuse:** treating "we all have the same rubric" as equivalent to norming. A shared document doesn't produce shared interpretation; only scoring the same real answers side by side does.

### Blind grading (anonymized grading)

- **Definition:** grading with student identity (and often section) hidden from the grader, typically via an ID number or a platform feature, so the score reflects the answer, not who wrote it or which TA taught them.
- **In use:** "Run the regrade sample blind — hide section labels too, or whoever's checking will unconsciously grade Section 3 more carefully because they already expect a problem there."
- **Common misuse:** assuming blind grading is only about preventing favoritism toward specific students. It also removes the grader's own expectation bias when checking a section they already suspect is off.

### Responsible employee (Title IX)

- **Definition:** an employee category under Title IX whose members are obligated to report disclosures of sexual harassment, assault, or discrimination to the institution's Title IX office, regardless of the disclosing student's wishes. Most TAs fall into this category by default.
- **In use:** "I had to tell her up front that I'm a responsible employee — if she tells me something reportable, it goes to the Title IX office whether or not she wants action taken."
- **Common misuse:** assuming the TA's own judgment about "does this really need reporting" substitutes for the report. The reporting obligation isn't conditional on the TA's assessment of severity.

### FERPA / directory information

- **Definition:** the Family Educational Rights and Privacy Act sets the baseline for who can access a student's education records (grades, attendance) without consent; directory information (name, major) may be disclosable, but grades and performance are not, absent a signed release.
- **In use:** "No, I can't confirm your son's midterm grade over email — I'd need a FERPA release on file, and even then I'd rather he requests it himself."
- **Common misuse:** treating FERPA as only about not posting a public grade list. It also covers discussing a specific student's performance with a parent, another student, or anyone without a legitimate educational interest and, where required, consent.

### Rubric drift

- **Definition:** the gradual divergence between how a rubric criterion is scored at the start of a grading session versus the end, or between different graders using the same written rubric, absent a norming check.
- **In use:** "Check the first 10 and last 10 papers you graded against each other — rubric drift is real, especially on the fuzzy 'writing clarity' line."
- **Common misuse:** assuming drift only happens between different people. A single grader drifts across a long grading session too, usually loosening as fatigue sets in.

### Wait time

- **Definition:** the pause (research-backed target: 3+ seconds) between posing a question and calling on a student, and again between a student's answer and the next response.
- **In use:** "I counted myself last section — I was calling on someone within a second of asking. Extending that to a real three-count changed who raised a hand."
- **Common misuse:** treating "give it a second" as already satisfying wait time. Most instructors underestimate their actual pause by several seconds until they time themselves.

### Cold-calling (structured)

- **Definition:** calling on a specific student by name to answer, using a tracked rotation (roster order, popsicle sticks, a call-log) rather than picking whoever seems ready — paired with think time before the call, not instead of it.
- **In use:** "I'm cold-calling off the roster this term, not just whoever makes eye contact — the participation record actually covers the whole section now."
- **Common misuse:** cold-calling with no preceding think time, which just makes unprepared students dread being caught rather than engaging more students in the material.

### Discrimination index (item analysis)

- **Definition:** on a multi-question exam, a measure of whether a specific question is answered correctly more often by students who scored well overall than by students who scored poorly overall; a low or negative index flags a question that isn't distinguishing skill level (often because it's ambiguous or mis-keyed).
- **In use:** "Question 7 has a negative discrimination index — the top-scoring students are missing it more than the bottom group. Check the answer key before we grade the rest."
- **Common misuse:** conflating a question's difficulty (percent correct) with its discrimination index. A question can be hard and still discriminate well, or be medium-difficulty and discriminate terribly if it's ambiguously worded.

### Regrade request vs. grade appeal (escalation ladder)

- **Definition:** a regrade request is a first-line ask to the grading TA to recheck a specific criterion; a grade appeal is an escalation to the supervising faculty member (and sometimes a department or registrar process) after the TA-level regrade is contested.
- **In use:** "Handle this as a regrade request first — if they're still not satisfied after I show them the rubric line, that's when it goes to the professor as an appeal."
- **Common misuse:** treating every disagreement as an appeal from the start, which skips the faster, lower-stakes TA-level correction step and escalates conflicts that a rubric citation would have resolved.

### Half-time appointment / FTE cap

- **Definition:** the contracted fraction of a full-time position a TA is paid for (commonly 50%, i.e., "half-time"), which typically sets a weekly hour ceiling (commonly cited around 20 hours/week, though this varies by institution and bargaining unit) that the TA's actual workload is supposed to fit inside.
- **In use:** "I'm at 26 hours this week against a 20-hour cap — that's not a one-off, that's the third week running, and I'm flagging it before it becomes the assumed norm."
- **Common misuse:** treating the cap as aspirational rather than contractual, and absorbing overage silently instead of reporting it — which resets next term's workload expectation to the padded number.

### Academic integrity referral

- **Definition:** the formal process, usually run by an institution's academic-integrity office or the supervising professor, for adjudicating a suspected violation (plagiarism, unauthorized collaboration, exam misconduct) — distinct from a TA unilaterally deciding guilt or a penalty.
- **In use:** "I documented what I saw and sent it to the professor for referral — it's not my call to decide the penalty, and confronting the student myself would've muddied the process."
- **Common misuse:** a TA either ignoring a suspected violation to avoid conflict, or handling it themselves (confronting the student, assigning an informal penalty) instead of routing it through the institution's actual process.

### Office-hours self-selection

- **Definition:** the pattern where the students who attend voluntary office hours are disproportionately the ones who need them least, because attendance requires already recognizing you're stuck and being comfortable enough to show up.
- **In use:** "Zero attendance for three weeks doesn't mean the section understands the material — it might mean the students who need help the most are the ones self-selecting out."
- **Common misuse:** reading low office-hours attendance as evidence the section is doing fine, rather than checking it against actual graded performance.

### Section (discussion/lab section) vs. lecture

- **Definition:** the smaller, TA-led meeting (discussion or lab section) distinct from the large lecture the supervising professor runs — sections exist for structured practice, application, and smaller-group interaction that doesn't scale in a 200-person lecture hall.
- **In use:** "Section time is for working problems and getting stuck in front of me, not for re-explaining the lecture — if I'm lecturing for 40 of the 50 minutes, the format's failed."
- **Common misuse:** running a section as a compressed re-lecture, which duplicates content students already had and skips the practice time the section slot was scheduled for.
