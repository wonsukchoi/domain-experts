# Red flags

Field smell tests a licensed plumber/pipefitter catches in the first walkthrough or plan review. Format for each: what it usually means, the first question to ask, and the data to pull before the fix or the sign-off happens.

## 1. Trap arm run measured over 2 ft 6 in. for a 1.25 in. trap (or the equivalent max distance for its size)

**Usually means:** the trap arm exceeds its size's trap-to-vent maximum distance — the vent can't equalize negative pressure fast enough during drainage, and the trap self-siphons dry with no visible symptom until sewer-gas odor is noticed.

**First question:** "What's the measured distance from the trap weir to the vent connection, not just the slope?"

**Data to pull:** trap-arm measured length, trap size, applicable maximum-distance table value, whether an individual vent can be added within range.

## 2. Any fixture drain under 3 in. carrying a water closet

**Usually means:** a minimum-pipe-size violation — water closets require a minimum 3 in. drain regardless of the fixture-unit load, and a smaller pipe will clog on solids even well under its nominal DFU capacity.

**First question:** "What size is the branch this water closet ties into?"

**Data to pull:** branch pipe size at the water closet connection, DFU load on the branch, code minimum for water-closet drains in the adopted edition.

## 3. Drainage slope flatter than 1/8 in./ft (4 in.+) or 1/4 in./ft (3 in. and under), or noticeably steeper than the local ceiling

**Usually means:** flat slope lets solids settle and the line clogs; overly steep slope sheets water ahead of solids, defeating the same self-scouring flow the minimum slope is designed to produce — both produce a "keeps clogging" complaint from opposite causes.

**First question:** "What's the measured fall over the actual run length, not the design intent?"

**Data to pull:** measured slope (rise/fall over run), pipe size, applicable minimum and any locally amended maximum.

## 4. DWV air test losing more than roughly 0.1 psi over a 15-minute hold

**Usually means:** a joint or fitting isn't sealed — a loss beyond the code's allowed drop indicates a real leak path, not test-equipment drift.

**First question:** "Where along the run does the drop concentrate when each section is isolated?"

**Data to pull:** starting test pressure, pressure at each interval, joint-by-joint isolation results if the drop persists.

## 5. Health-hazard cross-connection protected by only a check valve or double-check assembly

**Usually means:** the wrong device class — a check valve or DCVA stops backpressure, not back-siphonage under vacuum, and a health-hazard connection (submersible hose bibb, chemical feed, boiler makeup) needs an air gap, RPZ, or vacuum-breaker-class device instead.

**First question:** "Can this connection ever be submerged, backpressured by a pump, or exposed to a non-potable source under vacuum?"

**Data to pull:** connection's hazard classification (health vs. non-health), installed device model and ASSE rating, device installation height relative to the flood-level rim.

## 6. New PRV, check valve, or backflow preventer installed on the cold main with no thermal-expansion tank present

**Usually means:** the system was just converted from open to closed with no expansion control added — the T&P relief valve will absorb the expansion instead, producing intermittent weeping that reads as a bad relief valve rather than a missing expansion tank.

**First question:** "What created the closed system here, and where's the expansion tank?"

**Data to pull:** device just installed (PRV/check valve/backflow preventer), water heater volume and system pressure, expansion-tank presence and sizing chart reference.

## 7. Repeated water-hammer complaint after a washer, dishwasher, or flush-valve install with no arrestor, or an arrestor installed far from the valve

**Usually means:** either no water-hammer arrestor was installed, or one was installed centrally on the main rather than at the quick-closing valve generating the spike — arrestor effectiveness drops sharply with distance from the valve.

**First question:** "Is there an arrestor at this valve specifically, and what fixture-unit load is it rated for?"

**Data to pull:** arrestor presence and PDI-WH201 size rating at the valve in question, fixture-unit load of that valve group, distance from arrestor to valve.

## 8. Gas or steam pressure test run at a flat "3 psi for 10 minutes" regardless of system class

**Usually means:** low-pressure residential gas test parameters were applied to a higher-pressure gas or steam system, which requires a test pressure that's a multiple of its own working pressure — the test may have passed without actually stressing the system's real operating condition.

**First question:** "What's this system's working pressure, and does the test pressure reflect that, or a generic default?"

**Data to pull:** system's rated working pressure, test pressure and hold time actually used, code-required multiple for that pressure class.

## 9. Branch drain visibly oversized relative to its actual fixture-unit load, with a chronic slow-drain complaint

**Usually means:** low flow depth in an oversized pipe fails to scour solids off the invert — an upsized-for-safety pipe produces the same clogging complaint as an undersized one, for the opposite reason.

**First question:** "What's the actual DFU load on this branch versus the pipe size installed?"

**Data to pull:** fixture-unit load, installed pipe diameter, fixture-unit table capacity for that diameter, description of what's clogging and how often.

## 10. Backflow-prevention device with no current test tag in a jurisdiction requiring annual certified testing

**Usually means:** the device's protection can't be confirmed — RPZ and other testable assemblies degrade (fouled check discs, worn seats) and the annual certified test is what confirms it's still functioning, not a formality.

**First question:** "When was this device last certified-tested, and by whom?"

**Data to pull:** test tag date and tester certification number, device model and ASSE rating, jurisdiction's required test interval.

## 11. New closed-loop or PRV-fed system reporting a T&P relief valve that weeps intermittently

**Usually means:** thermal expansion with nowhere to go — the relief valve is doing the expansion tank's job and will fail early from repeated partial openings.

**First question:** "Is there a properly sized expansion tank on this heater, and when was its pre-charge last checked?"

**Data to pull:** expansion tank presence and acceptance-volume rating, tank pre-charge pressure versus system pressure, relief-valve weep frequency and duration.
