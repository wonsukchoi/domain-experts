### Basis
The difference between the local cash price and the relevant futures price, expressed as cents over or under the board (e.g., "minus 35," meaning 35 cents under the futures price).
**In use:** "December's at $4.32, but our basis is minus 35, so we're bidding $3.97 before grade."
**Common misuse:** Treating basis as a fixed number rather than something that moves daily with local supply, freight cost, and demand — independent of futures.

### Board price / futures price
The price of a standardized futures contract for a commodity and delivery month, traded on an exchange (CBOT/CME) — the national reference point cash prices are set against.
**In use:** "The board's at $4.32 for December corn."
**Common misuse:** Quoting the board price to a grower as if it's what they'll be paid, without applying basis and grade discounts.

### Hedge-to-arrive (HTA)
A forward contract that locks the futures price now while leaving basis to be set later, closer to delivery.
**In use:** "She wants to lock the board today but isn't ready to set basis, so we'll write it as an HTA."
**Common misuse:** Treating an HTA as a fully priced contract — only the futures leg is locked; basis risk is still open.

### Basis contract
A forward contract that locks the basis now while leaving the futures price to be set later (the reverse of an HTA).
**In use:** "He's comfortable with today's basis but wants to wait on the board, so we're writing a basis contract."
**Common misuse:** Confusing this with an HTA — the two lock opposite legs of the price.

### Cash forward contract
A contract that locks both futures and basis (i.e., the full flat price) for delivery in a defined future window.
**In use:** "At $4.50 flat, delivery in October — that's a straight cash forward."
**Common misuse:** Assuming any forward contract is a cash forward; many lock only one price component.

### Shrink
The percentage reduction applied to a load's weight or bushels to account for moisture above a contract's baseline, reflecting the water weight that will be removed in drying.
**In use:** "2.2 points over baseline at 1.4% shrink per point puts us at just over 3% shrink on this load."
**Common misuse:** Confusing shrink (a weight/bushel reduction) with the separate per-bushel drying charge — schedules commonly apply both, not one or the other.

### Test weight
The weight of grain per standard volume (e.g., pounds per bushel), used as a grade factor — lower test weight indicates lighter, often lower-quality kernels.
**In use:** "Test weight came in at 57 — above the 56-pound standard, so no discount there."
**Common misuse:** Confusing test weight (a density/quality measure) with the load's total scale weight.

### Foreign material (FM)
Non-grain material (chaff, weed seeds, broken kernels below a size threshold, etc.) present in a grain sample, measured as a percentage and subject to a tolerance before a discount applies.
**In use:** "FM's at 1.8%, still inside the 3% tolerance for #2 corn."
**Common misuse:** Treating any visible debris as automatically discountable, without checking it against the specific grade's tolerance percentage.

### USDA grade (e.g., US #2 Yellow Corn)
A standardized quality classification set by USDA's Federal Grain Inspection Service, defining maximum allowable levels of moisture, test weight, foreign material, and damage for a commodity to qualify at that grade.
**In use:** "Contract specifies US #2 Yellow Corn — anything outside those tolerances gets graded down or discounted."
**Common misuse:** Assuming grade standards are the same across commodities or contract types; each grade has its own specific tolerance table.

### Open position
The net quantity of a commodity a company is exposed to (long or short) that hasn't been offset by a matching futures or physical trade.
**In use:** "We've got 40,000 bushels forward-contracted and unhedged — that's a 40,000-bushel open short position."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a booked forward contract by itself closes out risk; it creates a physical obligation that still needs a futures hedge to close the price risk.

### Scale ticket
The document recording a delivered load's gross weight, tare weight, and net weight, along with grade readings — the source record for settlement.
**In use:** "Pull the scale ticket and the grade sample before we calculate the settlement."
**Common misuse:** Treating the scale ticket as the final price document — it records weight and grade, not the priced settlement, which also needs the day's board and basis applied.

### Settlement statement
The document issued to the grower showing the full price calculation (board, basis, discounts) and the final amount paid for a delivered load.
**In use:** "The settlement statement shows the shrink and drying charge separately so the grower can see exactly where the deduction came from."
**Common misuse:** Summarizing the settlement as a single net price without showing the component math, which is what growers actually dispute.

### Delivery window
The date range specified in a contract during which the grower is obligated to deliver the contracted quantity.
**In use:** "Contract calls for delivery between September 15 and October 15."
**Common misuse:** Treating the delivery window as a soft target rather than a binding contract term with real consequences for late or non-delivery.

### Contract month
The specific month a futures contract (and the cash price derived from it) references for delivery/settlement — not necessarily the current calendar month.
**In use:** "We're pricing off December futures even though it's September, because that's the contract month specified."
**Common misuse:** Defaulting to "the front month" without checking which contract month the specific bid sheet or contract actually references.
