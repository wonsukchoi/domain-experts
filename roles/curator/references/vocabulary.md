# Vocabulary

### Accession
The formal act of adding an object to the permanent collection, with a unique accession number and a legal record of how title was acquired.
**In use:** "It's not accessioned yet — it's on loan pending committee review, so it doesn't get an accession number until the vote passes."
**Common misuse:** Treating "in the building" and "accessioned" as the same thing; an object can sit in storage for months as an unaccessioned incoming loan, gift proposal, or study object.

### Deed of gift
The signed legal instrument transferring title and specifying any restrictions (naming, display, resale) from donor to institution.
**In use:** "We can't finalize the acquisition memo until the deed of gift is signed — a verbal promise from the donor isn't title."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a donor's stated intent to give, even in writing as a letter of intent, is equivalent to a signed deed of gift transferring actual title.

### Direct care
The AAMD/AAM-aligned definition of allowable uses for deaccessioning proceeds: conservation, storage materials, and other direct costs of caring for the collection — explicitly excluding staff salaries and exhibition-mounting costs.
**In use:** "That storage cabinet is direct care; the exhibition catalogue for the show it'll be displayed in is not."
**Common misuse:** Treating "direct care" as a broad, judgment-call category rather than the specific, narrowly defined list it actually is.

### Provenance
The documented chain of ownership of an object from creation to the present.
**In use:** "The provenance has a clean chain back to 1952, but there's a gap in continental Europe between 1938 and 1946 — that goes to the portal before we do anything else."
**Common misuse:** Treating provenance as equivalent to authenticity (they're related but separate questions) or assuming a gap automatically means the object is stolen.

### Facility report
A standardized document describing a borrowing (or lending) institution's environmental controls, security, staffing, and exhibit-case specifications, reviewed before a loan is approved.
**In use:** "No facility report, no loan agreement — doesn't matter how well-known the borrowing museum is."
**Common misuse:** Accepting a borrower's general reputation or a brief email description in place of the actual standardized report.

### Indemnity
A government or institutional guarantee covering exhibition-related loss or damage, often used instead of (or alongside) commercial insurance for high-value traveling exhibitions.
**In use:** "The show qualifies for federal indemnity, which drops our commercial insurance premium significantly for the tour."
**Common misuse:** Assuming indemnity and insurance are interchangeable terms rather than distinct coverage mechanisms with different application processes and scope.

### Courier
A staff member (often the curator or a registrar) who physically accompanies a high-value or fragile object during transit and installation/deinstallation at the borrowing venue.
**In use:** "This piece travels with a courier per the loan agreement — no courier, no shipment."
**Common misuse:** Treating courier requirements as a formality that can be waived for a "trusted" borrower rather than a condition specified in the signed agreement.

### Restricted gift
A donation accepted with binding conditions attached — naming rights, permanent display, resale prohibition — that constrain the institution's future use of the object beyond ordinary stewardship.
**In use:** "This is a restricted gift with a 20-year display clause — we need to negotiate that down before the committee votes."
**Common misuse:** Treating any donation with a stated preference ("I'd love to see it displayed") as equivalent to a legally binding restriction; the two require very different responses.

### Deaccession
The formal, committee-approved removal of an object from the permanent collection, followed by disposal per the institution's documented preference order.
**In use:** "We're deaccessioning the duplicate weathervane — transfer to the county historical society first, auction only if that falls through."
**Common misuse:** Using "deaccession" and "sell" interchangeably; deaccessioning is the removal decision, sale is only one possible (and lowest-preference) disposal method.

### Object label vs. didactic panel
The object label identifies and briefly interprets a single work (title, maker, date, medium, one retained idea); the didactic or orientation panel carries the exhibition's central argument across multiple objects.
**In use:** "That's panel content, not label content — it's making an argument that spans the whole gallery, not describing this one quilt."
**Common misuse:** Writing object labels dense enough to carry the exhibition's thesis, which buries the one idea a visitor needs in content meant for a different document.

### Credit line
The standardized attribution text accompanying a label or catalogue entry, naming the donor or acquisition method and often a required, negotiated condition of the gift.
**In use:** "The credit line wording is fixed in the deed of gift — we can't shorten it for the label just because it reads long."
**Common misuse:** Treating credit-line wording as a house-style choice rather than a contractual term when it was a condition of the gift.

### Collecting plan
The department's written statement of collecting scope, priorities, and documented gaps, against which acquisition proposals are evaluated.
**In use:** "This fills a documented gap in our collecting plan — pre-1900 quilts from this region — which is why it's worth the carrying cost."
**Common misuse:** Treating the collecting plan as a formality to cite after the acquisition decision is already made on other grounds, rather than the actual first filter.

### Total cost of ownership (collections context)
The full projected cost of holding an object — one-time cataloging/rehousing plus ongoing storage, insurance, and conservation — over its expected life in the collection.
**In use:** "The appraised value is $1.9M, but the number that matters for this decision is the $24,300 carrying cost over 15 years."
**Common misuse:** Evaluating an acquisition on appraised value alone without pricing the ongoing cost the institution is committing to indefinitely.

### Fair, prior, and informed consent (NAGPRA context)
The documented agreement of an affiliated Tribe or Native Hawaiian Organization, obtained before exhibition, research, or access to a covered cultural item — not sought retroactively after display has already begun.
**In use:** "We covered that case in January because we didn't have documented consent yet — access waits for consultation, not the other way around."
**Common misuse:** Treating a general land acknowledgment or informal conversation with a Tribal representative as equivalent to the documented consent the regulation actually requires.
