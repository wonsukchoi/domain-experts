# Vocabulary

### Percent yield
The ratio of actual product mass obtained to the theoretical maximum calculated from stoichiometry, expressed as a percentage.
**In use:** "Sixty-one percent yield here, but the melting point confirms it's clean product — that number isn't the problem."
**Common misuse:** Treating percent yield as the primary or sole indicator of lab success — a high yield with no purity check can reflect residual solvent or impurity mass rather than genuine product, and a lower yield with documented cause and confirmed purity can reflect better technique.

### Error analysis
The identification and, where possible, quantification of the sources of deviation between an experimental result and a theoretical or expected value.
**In use:** "The error analysis section is what tells me whether that low yield was sloppy technique or a documented filtration loss."
**Common misuse:** Treating error analysis as an optional add-on to a lab report rather than the section that actually determines whether a deviating result should be penalized — a plausible, quantified error source often earns close to full credit.

### Limiting reagent
The reactant that is fully consumed first in a reaction, determining the maximum theoretical yield of product.
**In use:** "Check which reagent is limiting before you calculate theoretical yield — using the wrong one changes the entire baseline you're grading against."
**Common misuse:** Assuming the reagent added in the smallest quantity is automatically limiting without checking the stoichiometric ratio — a reagent present in a smaller mass can still be in molar excess relative to what the reaction requires.

### Purity check (melting point / titration / TLC)
An independent measurement used to corroborate that a claimed product is actually the intended compound at the stated quality, rather than relying on mass alone.
**In use:** "That yield number means nothing to me until I see a melting point or a TLC plate confirming it's actually aspirin."
**Common misuse:** Accepting a yield figure as self-verifying — mass measured on a scale confirms only that something was weighed, not that it is the correct, pure compound.

### Conceptual error (vs. computational error)
A mistake in the underlying setup or reasoning of a problem — wrong mechanism, wrong limiting reagent, wrong stoichiometric relationship — as distinct from an arithmetic mistake applied to an otherwise correct setup.
**In use:** "The stoichiometry was set up backward — that's conceptual, not a slip in the long division afterward."
**Common misuse:** Grading and giving feedback on a wrong numeric answer without checking which category the error falls into — the remediation for a conceptual gap (reteaching the underlying idea) is different from the remediation for a computational slip (careful rework of the same correct setup).

### Simplified (pedagogical) model
A deliberately incomplete model of a chemical phenomenon taught because it is tractable at a given course level, with known conditions under which it fails to hold.
**In use:** "We're teaching the octet rule here — it's a simplification, and I'm telling you now it breaks for expanded-octet species like SF6."
**Common misuse:** Presenting a simplified model as if it were the complete, final explanation — students then either misapply it beyond its valid range or feel misled when a later course introduces the more accurate model.

### Dry run
A supervised test execution of a demo or lab procedure under the actual conditions (room, ventilation, equipment, reagent batch) it will be run in with students, performed before the live session.
**In use:** "We're dry-running this demo in the actual lecture hall fume hood before Tuesday's class, not just trusting the textbook writeup."
**Common misuse:** Treating a procedure as verified because it's published or was run successfully at another institution — ventilation, scale, and equipment condition are room-specific and have to be checked here, not assumed to transfer.

### Rubric weighting
The explicit point allocation across components of a graded assignment (e.g., methodology, error analysis, numeric outcome), set before grading begins.
**In use:** "Methodology and error analysis are forty and thirty percent of this rubric — the yield number by itself is only twenty."
**Common misuse:** Grading lab reports against an implicit, outcome-only standard ("did they get the right number") without a rubric that separately weights methodology and documentation — this incentivizes students to optimize the reported number rather than the underlying technique.

### Hazard review (procedure-specific)
A safety assessment performed on the specific reagents, quantities, and steps of a particular procedure — including student-proposed variations — rather than a general lab-safety orientation.
**In use:** "The general safety orientation covers the room; this hazard review is specific to the procedure this group proposed."
**Common misuse:** Treating a general, one-time safety orientation as sufficient clearance for any subsequent student-designed or modified procedure — a new or altered procedure needs its own review of its specific hazard points.
