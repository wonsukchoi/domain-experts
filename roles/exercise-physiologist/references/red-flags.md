# Red flags

### Systolic BP drop >10 mmHg from baseline with increased workload
- **Usually means:** Left-ventricular dysfunction or exercise-induced ischemia unable to sustain cardiac output at rising demand; second most likely is over-titrated antihypertensive/vasodilator medication.
- **First question:** Did the drop occur with any other ischemia evidence (angina, ST changes, dyspnea) at the same stage, or in isolation?
- **Data to pull:** The stage-by-stage BP log from the test, current antihypertensive med list and last dose timing, and the echo/EF on file.

### ST-segment depression ≥2mm, horizontal or downsloping, on the exercise ECG
- **Usually means:** Exercise-induced myocardial ischemia at that workload; second is a false-positive from digoxin, LVH, or baseline conduction abnormality.
- **First question:** At what rate-pressure product (HR × SBP) did it appear, and does the patient's resting ECG already show a confound (LBBB, LVH, digoxin effect)?
- **Data to pull:** The 12-lead strip at onset, the resting baseline ECG, and the medication list for confounding drugs.

### RPE rising disproportionately to workload increase (e.g., +2 stages, RPE jumps 4+ points)
- **Usually means:** Fatigue or deconditioning outpacing the prescribed progression; second is an unreported symptom the patient isn't verbalizing as "pain."
- **First question:** "Is anything hurting or feeling different today, even if it's not what I asked about?"
- **Data to pull:** This session's vitals trend versus the prior 2 sessions at the same load, and any missed-medication or poor-sleep report.

### SpO2 drop below 88% during exertion (pulmonary rehab population)
- **Usually means:** Exercise-induced desaturation requiring supplemental oxygen titration; second is a pulse-oximeter reading artifact from poor perfusion or motion.
- **First question:** Does the reading correlate with visible dyspnea or cyanosis, or is it an isolated number with the patient reporting no distress?
- **Data to pull:** Continuous SpO2 trend for the session, current home O2 prescription (if any), and the pulmonologist's most recent PFT/ABG.

### Failure of HR to rise appropriately with workload (chronotropic incompetence pattern)
- **Usually means:** Beta-blocker or other rate-limiting medication effect; second most likely is sinus node dysfunction or autonomic neuropathy (e.g., in longstanding diabetes).
- **First question:** Is the patient on a rate-limiting medication, and if so, was the dose or timing recently changed?
- **Data to pull:** Current medication list with dose/timing, and the HR-versus-workload curve compared to the entry GXT.

### Two consecutive missed target workloads at the same prescribed intensity
- **Usually means:** The prescription is set above true current tolerance, or a new deconditioning/illness event has occurred since the last test; second is a medication change altering the HR or symptom response.
- **First question:** What changed between the last successful session and now — medication, sleep, illness, or a missed dose?
- **Data to pull:** The session log for the last 2 weeks and a medication-reconciliation check.

### New or worsening chest discomfort, disproportionate dyspnea, or palpitations mid-session
- **Usually means:** An acute ischemic or arrhythmic event in progress; second most likely (only after ruling out the first) is musculoskeletal or anxiety-related discomfort.
- **First question:** Does the character, location, and radiation pattern match this patient's known anginal pattern, or is it new/different?
- **Data to pull:** Immediate vitals and rhythm strip, and the patient's documented anginal-equivalent history if any.

### 6MWT distance change smaller than the condition's published MCID
- **Usually means:** No real functional change has occurred yet — the number is within measurement noise; second is that only one baseline test was performed, so a practice-effect artifact is being read as decline or improvement.
- **First question:** Was this the second of two baseline tests, or the first (and therefore unreliable on its own)?
- **Data to pull:** Both baseline test distances if available, and the condition-specific MCID being used for comparison.

### Sustained ventricular tachycardia or signs of poor perfusion (pallor, cyanosis, ataxia) during a test
- **Usually means:** An absolute indication for immediate test termination and physician notification; this is not a "second most likely" category — treat as the primary and only read.
- **First question:** Has the test already been stopped and is emergency response activated?
- **Data to pull:** The rhythm strip capturing the event and the time/workload at onset for the post-event report.

### Program entry METs estimate exceeds the patient's self-reported daily functional activity by a wide margin
- **Usually means:** A test protocol mismatch (standard Bruce's large jumps overestimated a frail patient's stage-based METs); second is a submaximal effort during testing that inflated the estimate less than expected, masking a lower true ceiling.
- **First question:** Which protocol was used, and does the termination reason (fatigue vs. symptom vs. protocol time limit) match a genuine maximal effort?
- **Data to pull:** The protocol used, termination reason and RPE at termination, and a same-week ADL/functional-activity questionnaire.
