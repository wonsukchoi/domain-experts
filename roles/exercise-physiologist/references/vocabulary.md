# Vocabulary

### MET (metabolic equivalent)
1 MET ≈ 3.5 ml O2/kg/min, the resting oxygen cost, used as a unit to express exercise workload independent of body weight.
**In use:** "She's at 6.2 METs peak on the entry GXT, so a 4-MET household task is well within her ceiling but 8-MET shoveling isn't yet."
**Common misuse:** Treating METs as directly comparable across protocols without noting which equation or protocol generated the estimate — a Bruce-derived METs figure and a directly measured VO2peak from gas analysis aren't the same precision of number.

### VO2max / VO2peak
The maximal rate of oxygen consumption during exercise; VO2max implies a true physiologic plateau was reached, VO2peak is the highest value observed without confirming a plateau.
**In use:** "We reported VO2peak, not VO2max — she stopped for orthopedic discomfort before HR or VO2 plateaued."
**Common misuse:** Using "VO2max" for any symptom-limited clinical test, when almost all clinical GXTs (stopped for fatigue, symptoms, or protocol completion) produce a VO2peak, not a confirmed max.

### Heart rate reserve (HRR) / Karvonen formula
Target HR = resting HR + (%intensity × (peak HR − resting HR)); uses the *usable range* between rest and peak rather than percent of peak HR alone.
**In use:** "His HRR off the measured peak of 118 is 56 — 40 to 60% gives us 84 to 96, not the age-predicted range."
**Common misuse:** Plugging in an age-predicted HRmax (220 − age) instead of a measured peak HR for a patient on a chronotropic-limiting medication, producing a target range built on a number the patient will never actually reach.

### Chronotropic incompetence
Failure of heart rate to rise appropriately in proportion to increased workload or metabolic demand during exercise.
**In use:** "His HR barely moved past 95 by Stage 3 — that's a chronotropic-incompetence pattern, probably the metoprolol, but rule out sinus node disease before assuming it's just the drug."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a flat HR response always means "well-conditioned" or "not trying hard enough" rather than checking medications and conduction system function first.

### Ischemic threshold
The heart rate (or rate-pressure product) at which myocardial oxygen demand exceeds supply, producing angina or ST-segment changes.
**In use:** "The ischemic threshold on his entry test was HR 130 with 1.5mm ST depression — we're prescribing 15 beats under that, not up to it."
**Common misuse:** Prescribing exercise intensity right up to the threshold seen on the test instead of leaving a margin below it, since day-to-day variability can shift the threshold lower.

### Rate-pressure product (double product)
HR × systolic BP, a proxy for myocardial oxygen demand and workload on the heart.
**In use:** "The ST changes appeared at a rate-pressure product of about 21,000 — that's the number to track session over session, not just the HR alone."
**Common misuse:** Ignoring it and tracking HR alone, missing that a patient can reach an ischemic-equivalent workload at a lower HR if BP is elevated.

### Borg RPE (rate of perceived exertion) scale
A 6–20 (or 0–10 category-ratio) scale for the patient's subjective effort, anchored so that the number roughly tracks HR/10 on the original scale.
**In use:** "He's at RPE 14 on the 6–20 scale at today's workload — that's above target, hold the load next session."
**Common misuse:** Treating RPE as a soft, unreliable substitute for "real" data rather than the primary and more trustworthy signal when the HR response is medication-altered or arrhythmic.

### Risk stratification (AACVPR)
A low/moderate/high classification assigned at program entry from EF, ischemia/arrhythmia findings, and functional capacity, that sets the required monitoring and supervision level.
**In use:** "She stratifies moderate on EF alone even though the rest of the test was clean — that's still six sessions of telemetry."
**Common misuse:** Basing the category only on the diagnosis (e.g., "post-MI = automatically moderate") rather than the specific test findings and EF that the algorithm actually weighs.

### Symptom-limited test
An exercise test terminated by the patient's symptoms or volitional fatigue rather than by reaching a pre-set target (age-predicted HR, fixed protocol endpoint).
**In use:** "This was a symptom-limited test to RPE 15 for fatigue — no ischemic ceiling was actually found, so we have room to progress faster than a test that stopped for angina."
**Common misuse:** Conflating "symptom-limited" with "maximal effort achieved" — a test can be symptom-limited by an early, non-cardiac symptom (leg fatigue, anxiety) well before a true physiologic ceiling.

### Functional capacity
The measured or estimated maximal aerobic work a patient can perform, usually expressed in METs.
**In use:** "Functional capacity came back at 6.2 METs — enough for most ADLs but below the 7-MET threshold some programs use as a low-risk cutoff."
**Common misuse:** Reporting functional capacity from a protocol-time regression equation as if it were directly measured VO2, without noting the estimate's dependency on the specific protocol used.

### Six-minute walk test (6MWT)
A standardized, self-paced walk over a flat measured corridor for six minutes, used to track submaximal functional capacity, especially in pulmonary and heart-failure populations.
**In use:** "Her 6MWT improved 45 meters over 8 weeks — that clears the heart-failure MCID, so this is real functional gain, not test-retest noise."
**Common misuse:** Comparing a single 6MWT to a prior single 6MWT without a practice-effect baseline, and calling any nonzero change meaningful regardless of the condition's MCID.

### Minimal clinically important difference (MCID)
The smallest change in an outcome measure that patients or clinicians would perceive as meaningful, distinguishing real change from measurement noise.
**In use:** "A 20-meter 6MWT change doesn't clear the COPD MCID of 54 to 80 meters — call it stable, not improved."
**Common misuse:** Treating any statistically detectable or nonzero change as clinically meaningful without checking it against the condition-specific MCID.

### Telemetry
Continuous remote ECG monitoring during an exercise session, distinct from a single resting or exercise-test ECG snapshot.
**In use:** "He's still on telemetry through session six per his risk category — pull the rhythm strips before clearing him to the non-monitored track."
**Common misuse:** Treating telemetry as a formality to check off rather than the actual mechanism by which an arrhythmia during exercise (not just at rest) gets caught.

### Deconditioning
A reduction in cardiovascular and musculoskeletal capacity from inactivity or illness, distinct from a disease-specific physiologic ceiling.
**In use:** "Her low functional capacity is mostly deconditioning from six weeks of bed rest, not a fixed cardiac ceiling — expect faster early gains than her EF alone would suggest."
**Common misuse:** Attributing a low functional-capacity number entirely to the underlying disease when a real component is reversible deconditioning, which changes how aggressively the program can progress.
