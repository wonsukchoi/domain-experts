# Hairdresser / Cosmetologist — Vocabulary

### Level
The lightness or darkness of a hair color on a 1 (black) to 10 (palest blonde) numeric scale, independent of tone.
**In use:** "She's a natural level 5, and she wants a level 7 — that's two levels of lift."
**Common misuse:** Treated as interchangeable with "shade" or "color," which conflates level (lightness) with tone (warmth/coolness) — a level-7 warm blonde and a level-7 ash blonde are the same level, completely different tones.

### Tone
The underlying warmth or coolness of a color — red, orange, yellow (warm) vs. ash, violet, blue (cool) — visible as the "base" once level is set.
**In use:** "The lift exposed an orange tone we need to neutralize with a blue-based toner."
**Common misuse:** Used as a synonym for the final color name on a box ("I want that ash tone") without understanding tone is what's added or neutralized after level is achieved, not selected independently of the starting pigment.

### Developer volume
The concentration of hydrogen peroxide in a color developer (10/20/30/40-volume), which determines how much lift (lightening) occurs alongside deposit.
**In use:** "We're only depositing today, no lift needed, so 10-volume is right."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to control "how strong" the color is in general, rather than specifically controlling lift — using a higher volume than the lift requires adds damage risk without improving color payoff.

### Strand test
A trial application of the planned formula on a small, hidden section of the client's actual hair, processed under real service conditions, to verify result and safety before full application.
**In use:** "The strand test came back with an uneven cast, so we're not doing the full head today."
**Common misuse:** Confused with a color swatch/sample shown on a chart or synthetic hair — a strand test uses the client's own hair with its own history, which a printed swatch cannot predict.

### Patch test
A small application of a chemical product (typically color) to a discreet area of skin, checked 24–48 hours later for an allergic reaction before a full service.
**In use:** "She mentioned a reaction last time, so we're patch-testing 48 hours before her appointment."
**Common misuse:** Skipped as a formality for "regular" clients — sensitization to PPD and other allergens can develop over time even in someone who's had the same product for years without a prior reaction.

### Porosity
How readily the hair shaft absorbs and releases moisture/chemicals, ranging from low (resistant, tightly closed cuticle) to high (absorbs quickly, often from prior damage).
**In use:** "High porosity on the ends means we're cutting processing time there to avoid over-lifting."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to be uniform across the whole head — porosity commonly differs between roots (less porous, less processed) and ends (more porous, more processed history), requiring different timing within the same service.

### Metallic-salt dye
A category of over-the-counter "gradual" or "natural" hair-color products that coat the hair shaft with metallic compounds, which can react unpredictably (heat, discoloration) with peroxide-based professional lighteners.
**In use:** "The green cast on the strand test is a classic metallic-salt reaction — we need to clarify before any bleach service."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to be identifiable by asking "have you used box color," when metallic-salt products are often marketed as "natural" or "gradual" and not recognized by clients as a chemical dye at all.

### Chemical-service compatibility
Whether a planned chemical service (color, relaxer, keratin treatment) is safe to perform given a client's recent chemical-service history, based on manufacturer intervals and current hair condition.
**In use:** "Relaxer three weeks ago means we're not doing a full-lift color today — that's a compatibility stop, not a scheduling inconvenience."
**Common misuse:** Reduced to a simple time-elapsed check ("it's been a month, so it's fine") without accounting for the actual condition of the hair, which can remain compromised well past a generic interval if the prior service was aggressive.

### Elasticity
The hair's ability to stretch and return to its original length without breaking, used as a structural-integrity indicator independent of how the hair looks.
**In use:** "It looks fine, but the elasticity test showed it barely stretches before snapping — that's a stop signal."
**Common misuse:** Skipped because the hair "looks healthy" — visible shine and smoothness (which can be enhanced by product coating) don't indicate the structural integrity elasticity testing reveals.

### Deposit-only service
A color service using little or no lift (typically 10-volume or a demi-permanent/no-developer product) that adds pigment without lightening the natural or existing color level.
**In use:** "She just wants her gray covered at her natural level — that's deposit-only, no lift required."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to be lower-risk by default and therefore not worth a strand test — deposit-only services can still cause allergic reactions (PPD) or uneven results on porous hair, independent of lift-related damage risk.

### Level-and-tone chart
A reference tool mapping natural hair levels to the underlying pigment exposed at each level when lifted, used to predict what tone will show before toning.
**In use:** "According to the chart, lifting from a level 4 to a level 7 exposes orange — we need to plan the toner for that."
**Common misuse:** Treated as giving the final result color, when it actually shows the un-toned lift color that still requires a toning step to reach most client-requested "finished" shades.
