# Vocabulary

Terms generalists conflate that recreation practitioners keep sharply separate.

## Ratio vs. blended ratio

**Ratio** is staff-to-participant count for a specific age band and activity type, set by an accreditation standard (e.g., ACA's 1:8 for ages 6–8). **Blended ratio** is total staff ÷ total participants across every age band on-site, a single number that can look compliant while a specific subgroup is actually short.

*In use:* "Don't tell me the blended number — give me Explorers' ratio on its own."

**Common misuse:** citing the blended ratio as proof of compliance when the standard is written per age band, not per site.

## Two-deep leadership vs. ratio

**Ratio** is a headcount formula that scales with group size. **Two-deep leadership** is a flat structural rule — a minimum of two unrelated adults present for any activity involving minors — that applies regardless of how the ratio math comes out, because it exists to prevent engineered one-on-one situations, not to manage group size.

*In use:* "Ratio math says one counselor covers this group of nine 13-year-olds, but two-deep still requires two — the rules aren't the same thing."

**Common misuse:** treating two-deep as satisfied whenever ratio is satisfied; a group of 9 twelve-to-fourteen-year-olds clears 1:12 ratio with a single staff member but still fails two-deep.

## Incident vs. near-miss

**Incident** is an event that resulted in injury, property damage, or a safety-plan failure that actually played out. **Near-miss** is an event that had the same causal conditions but didn't result in harm — the arrow that wasn't released, the trip that didn't happen. Both get logged; only one shows up if a program tracks harm instead of hazard.

*In use:* "That's our third near-miss on that transition this month — we're fixing the transition before it becomes an incident."

**Common misuse:** logging only incidents, which discards the leading indicator that would have caught the pattern before someone got hurt.

## Inherent risk vs. negligence

**Inherent risk** is a hazard built into the nature of the activity itself that a waiver can validly ask a participant to accept (a ball can hit someone during kickball). **Negligence** is a failure to meet the duty of care the program owed — inadequate supervision, unmaintained equipment, ignoring a known hazard — and a waiver does not cover it, regardless of what the waiver's language claims.

*In use:* "The waiver covers a camper twisting an ankle running the bases. It does not cover us running the game with half the required staff."

**Common misuse:** treating a signed waiver as blanket liability protection, including for harm the program's own negligence caused.

## Accommodation vs. modification vs. segregation

**Accommodation** changes how a participant accesses an existing activity (an alternate route, extra time) without changing the activity's substance. **Modification** changes the activity itself for the whole group. **Segregation** removes the participant to a separate program. ADA Title II defaults public programs toward accommodation first, segregation only after an individualized assessment shows accommodation can't make the standard program safe or accessible.

*In use:* "This is an accommodation, not a modification — everyone else still hikes the same trail, she just takes the paved loop to the same endpoint."

**Common misuse:** jumping straight to segregation ("she should do the adapted program instead") without running the individualized assessment the standard actually requires first.

## Programming vs. scheduling

**Programming** is the design work — matching activity, progression, and group ability to a stated outcome. **Scheduling** is placing designed activities into time slots and staff assignments. A calendar full of scheduled activities with no underlying programming logic is activity for its own sake, not a program.

*In use:* "We have a full calendar, but nobody can tell me what skill Week 4 builds on Week 3 — that's scheduling, not programming."

**Common misuse:** treating a filled activity calendar as evidence of a good program when there's no stated progression or outcome behind it.

## Certified vs. current

**Certified** means a staff member completed a qualifying course and holds a card. **Current** means that card hasn't expired. A roster can show 100% certified staff while several cards lapsed weeks ago — certification is a point-in-time fact, coverage is only real the day the card is current.

*In use:* "He's certified, sure, but his lifeguard card expired in May — he's not current, and current is what covers us."

**Common misuse:** checking the "certified" box at hiring and never re-verifying expiry dates through the season.

## Enrollment vs. capacity vs. waitlist

**Enrollment** is participants currently registered. **Capacity** is the maximum the ratio and facility allow. **Waitlist** is demand beyond capacity. Flat enrollment with a growing waitlist means the constraint is capacity, not demand — adding marketing spend won't fix it; adding staff or a session will.

*In use:* "Enrollment's been flat at capacity for two seasons with a 15-person waitlist — we don't have a demand problem, we have a capacity problem."

**Common misuse:** reading flat enrollment as declining interest and responding with a marketing push instead of checking the waitlist first.

## Heat index vs. air temperature

**Air temperature** is the thermometer reading. **Heat index** ("feels like" temperature) factors in humidity and is what actually predicts heat-illness risk during activity — two days at the same air temperature can carry very different heat index values and require different activity modifications.

*In use:* "It's only 91 out, but the heat index is 106 with this humidity — we're moving to the modified schedule."

**Common misuse:** setting activity-modification thresholds off air temperature alone instead of the heat index, which understates risk on humid days.
