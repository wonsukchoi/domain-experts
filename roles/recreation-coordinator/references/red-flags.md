# Red flags

Smell tests a recreation coordinator catches early. Format: what it usually means, the first question to ask, and the data to pull before deciding anything.

## 1. Blended staff-to-participant ratio quoted instead of per-group ratio

**Usually means:** someone is about to justify a staffing shortfall by averaging across age groups, which hides the specific subgroup that's actually below its required ratio.

**First question:** "What's the ratio for each age band separately, and does each one clear two-deep leadership on its own?"

**Data to pull:** roster count and staff assignment per age band for the specific day and activity, not a season-average headcount.

## 2. A staff or volunteer certification card is more than 60 days from expiry with no renewal scheduled

**Usually means:** the roster is "certified" on paper but coverage will lapse mid-season, often unnoticed until an incident forces someone to check the card.

**First question:** "Show me the certification-expiry list sorted by date — who renews in the next 60 days, and is it booked?"

**Data to pull:** CPR/First Aid/AED and lifeguard certification expiry dates for every active staff member; renewal class registration status.

## 3. Near-miss log shows zero entries for a season with normal or above-normal enrollment

**Usually means:** either an unusually well-run program (rare) or, far more often, staff are only logging actual injuries and letting near-misses go unrecorded — which erases the leading indicator that would catch a pattern before someone gets hurt.

**First question:** "Walk me through the last time a staff member almost had to file an incident report but didn't — was it logged?"

**Data to pull:** near-miss log entries by month and location; compare against injury log — a healthy program logs several near-misses for every actual injury, not the reverse.

## 4. Same activity or location appears twice in the incident/near-miss log within one season

**Usually means:** an unaddressed environmental or procedural hazard (a fading line marking, a blind transition corner, a piece of equipment) rather than two unrelated camper errors.

**First question:** "What changed at that location or in that activity's procedure after the first report?"

**Data to pull:** the original report's follow-up/routing field — did it result in a facilities ticket or procedure change, and was that ticket closed before the second occurrence?

## 5. A field trip or off-site activity is staffed at exactly the two-deep minimum with no float

**Usually means:** the trip will run with zero capacity for a bathroom escort, minor injury, or lost-participant search — any single unplanned event forces someone to leave the group's required ratio to handle it.

**First question:** "If a camper needs a 1:1 escort mid-trip, who covers the group they leave behind?"

**Data to pull:** the day's staffing roster against the ratio worksheet, specifically whether any float or backup staff exists beyond the per-group minimum.

## 6. A program participant with a disclosed disability is being routed to a separate "special" session by default

**Usually means:** staff are defaulting to segregation for convenience or liability anxiety rather than running the individualized assessment ADA Title II requires before ruling out inclusion in the standard program.

**First question:** "What specifically about the standard program can't be made safe or accessible for this participant — has that been assessed, or assumed?"

**Data to pull:** the accommodation worksheet (if one exists) documenting the assessment; if none exists, that absence is the finding.

## 7. Behavior incidents cluster at the same time of day or transition point across multiple sessions

**Usually means:** a structural gap (an unsupervised transition, a group size mismatch, a timing conflict with meals/heat) is producing the behavior, not that a particular camper is "difficult."

**First question:** "What's actually happening structurally in the ten minutes before these incidents — who's supervising, and what's the group doing?"

**Data to pull:** incident log timestamps and locations across the season, cross-referenced against the daily schedule.

## 8. Heat index has crossed 100°F and the outdoor activity schedule is unchanged from a normal day

**Usually means:** the day's plan was set the night before and nobody re-checked conditions that morning — a scheduling-inertia failure, not a judgment call.

**First question:** "What's today's heat index at activity time, and does today's plan reflect it?"

**Data to pull:** current NWS heat index reading and forecast; the activity-modification threshold table for the program (commonly: modify strenuous activity above 103°F, cancel above 115°F per NWS "extreme danger").

## 9. A background check on a regular volunteer is more than one renewal cycle overdue

**Usually means:** onboarding paperwork was completed once and never refreshed, which is the exact gap that engineered one-on-one abuse situations exploit in youth-serving organizations.

**First question:** "When was this volunteer's background check last run, and is anyone tracking renewal dates department-wide?"

**Data to pull:** background-check completion and renewal dates for all staff and regular volunteers with minor contact; department policy on renewal cadence.

## 10. Program enrollment is flat or declining while satisfaction survey scores stay high

**Usually means:** the program is fine for the people already in it but isn't reaching new participants — a marketing/awareness gap, not a quality gap, and treating it as a quality problem wastes effort fixing what isn't broken.

**First question:** "Where did this season's registrants hear about the program, and has that channel mix changed?"

**Data to pull:** registration-source breakdown over the last 3 seasons; waitlist size (a waitlist with flat enrollment points at capacity, not demand, as the constraint).
