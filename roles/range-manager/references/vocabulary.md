# Vocabulary

Terms generalists conflate that range managers keep sharply separate. Each: definition, how a practitioner actually uses it in a sentence, and the common misuse to avoid.

## AUM vs. stocking rate vs. carrying capacity

**AUM** (animal unit month) is a unit of forage demand — the forage one 1,000-lb animal (or equivalent) consumes in a month, conventionally figured around 780–800 lbs air-dry forage depending on the agency's stated convention. **Stocking rate** is the number of animals actually placed on a given area for a given time (a management decision). **Carrying capacity** is the maximum stocking rate an area can sustain long-term without degrading the resource (an ecological ceiling).

*Practitioner usage:* "The permit's stocking rate is 100 pairs for five months — that's 500 AUMs — but this year's carrying capacity, given production, is closer to 275."

*Common misuse:* using "carrying capacity" to mean whatever the permit says, when the permit is a legal allocation that may sit above or below the land's actual current capacity.

## Permitted use vs. actual use vs. active use vs. suspended (non-use)

**Permitted use** is the AUM ceiling written into the grazing permit. **Actual use** is what was actually grazed, reported after the season. **Active use** is the portion of permitted use currently authorized for grazing in a given year (permitted use minus any suspended non-use). **Suspended non-use** is AUMs temporarily withheld from grazing (e.g., a drought reduction) without permanently reducing the permit.

*Practitioner usage:* "Permitted use stays at 500 AUMs on paper; active use this year is 275 after the drought suspension — the other 225 is non-use, not a permit cut."

*Common misuse:* treating a drought-year non-use adjustment as if it permanently reduced the permit, or conversely assuming actual use always equals active use (permittees sometimes graze less than authorized, or occasionally more, which actual-use reporting is meant to catch).

## Utilization vs. residue / stubble height

**Utilization** is the percentage of current year's forage production removed by grazing, usually read on key species at a key area. **Residue** or **stubble height** is the physical amount of plant material left standing after grazing, often used as a faster proxy where a height-weight curve has been calibrated for the key species.

*Practitioner usage:* "We don't have time for the full grazed-plant method on every pasture — stubble height on the calibrated key species gets us a fast utilization estimate for the pastures we're not worried about."

*Common misuse:* treating a stubble-height read as interchangeable with a true utilization percentage when no height-weight calibration curve exists for that species/site combination — the relationship between height and weight removed is species- and site-specific.

## Ecological site vs. range site (legacy term) vs. state-and-transition model

An **ecological site** is a distinct kind of land, defined by soil and climate, capable of producing a specific reference plant community and range of production. **Range site** is the older (pre-1997) term for the same concept, still found in legacy documents. A **state-and-transition model** describes the possible vegetation states a given ecological site can occupy and the (often threshold-gated, sometimes irreversible without intervention) transitions between them.

*Practitioner usage:* "This pasture spans two ecological sites — the loamy upland and a shallow claypan inclusion — so one utilization number for the whole pasture is averaging two different production ceilings together."

*Common misuse:* treating "ecological site" as a synonym for "pasture" or "allotment" — a single pasture routinely contains multiple ecological sites, each with its own reference community and its own condition assessment.

## Proper Functioning Condition (PFC) vs. Functional-at-Risk (FAR) vs. Nonfunctional

**PFC** is a riparian-wetland assessment category meaning adequate vegetation, landform, and large woody debris are present to dissipate stream energy, filter sediment, and support the physical processes that build/maintain the channel. **FAR** means the reach retains some of that capacity but is impaired, and carries a required trend call (upward, downward, or not apparent). **Nonfunctional** means the reach lacks the capacity to perform those functions.

*Practitioner usage:* "It's FAR, but the trend is upward off the 2021 grazing-system change — that reach is a lower priority than the FAR-downward reach two pastures over, even though the category label is identical."

*Common misuse:* ranking riparian problems by category alone (treating all "FAR" reaches as equal priority) instead of by category plus trend, which inverts the actual urgency ranking.

## Key area vs. key species

A **key area** is a representative, identified monitoring location within a pasture or allotment, chosen to reflect the management concern (typically an accessible, moderately-to-heavily used area, not the least-used corner). A **key species** is a plant species at that key area chosen because it's palatable, abundant, and a management indicator — utilization and trend are read on the key species at the key area, not on every species present.

*Practitioner usage:* "Bluebunch wheatgrass is our key species here because it's both the most palatable perennial and the one most sensitive to overuse — if it's declining, we'll see it before the less palatable associates show anything."

*Common misuse:* selecting a key area for convenience of access rather than representativeness, which produces trend data that doesn't reflect the actual management concern (see red-flags.md #1 and #10).

## Grazing systems: rest-rotation vs. deferred-rotation vs. continuous vs. high-intensity low-frequency vs. holistic planned grazing

**Continuous** grazing leaves livestock on one pasture the whole season. **Deferred-rotation** rotates pastures with the timing of use varied year to year so the same pasture isn't grazed at the same growth stage every season. **Rest-rotation** adds a full-season rest for at least one pasture in the rotation each year. **High-intensity low-frequency** (Merrill system) and **holistic planned grazing** (Savory) both use short-duration, high-density grazing with long recovery periods, differing mainly in planning method and the strength of their evidentiary support.

*Practitioner usage:* "We moved this allotment from continuous to deferred-rotation in 2019 — the trend data since shows recovering vigor on the previously season-long-grazed pastures."

*Common misuse:* citing holistic planned grazing's short-duration/high-density approach as proven superior to conventional rest-rotation on plant or soil outcomes — the peer-reviewed evidence (Briske et al. 2008) doesn't support a consistent advantage; it's a legitimate planning discipline, not a validated ecological technique.

## Range condition (legacy 4-class) vs. rangeland health vs. trend

**Range condition** is the older method: a single excellent/good/fair/poor score based on how far current vegetation departs from a presumed climax community. **Rangeland health** is the current three-attribute qualitative method (soil/site stability, hydrologic function, biotic integrity), which doesn't assume a single climax endpoint and can be applied to sites with a legitimate non-climax reference state. **Trend** is the direction of change between two or more reads over time, on either method.

*Practitioner usage:* "The old file has this as 'fair condition' from a 1990s survey — that's a single-score legacy read; we need a current rangeland-health assessment to know which attribute is actually driving that."

*Common misuse:* treating a static condition or health score as sufficient on its own — a score with no trend history can't distinguish a recovering degraded site from a degrading good one, which is the distinction that actually drives a management decision.

## Forage production vs. forage utilization vs. forage demand

**Forage production** is how much forage a site grows in a given year (lbs/acre, measured or estimated). **Forage utilization** is the percentage of that production actually consumed by grazing. **Forage demand** is what a given herd size and duration requires, in AUMs, translated to lbs of forage via the AUM conversion factor.

*Practitioner usage:* "Production's down to 55% of normal this year, so demand has to come down proportionally too, or utilization goes well past the site's proper-use factor."

*Common misuse:* setting stocking rate (which determines demand) from a fixed historical production number instead of the current year's actual or estimated production — this is the single most common driver of both drought-year overuse and needless wet-year non-use.
