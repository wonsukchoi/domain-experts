# Playbook

Field worksheets and templates a range manager actually fills out, with structure and example numbers. Starting points to adapt, not scripts.

## 1. Utilization study — grazed-plant method (filled example)

Run at the end of the grazing period, at the key area, on the key species. Walk a set transect (typically 100 paces/plants), classify each plant into a grazed-use class.

**Key area:** Loamy 10–14" p.z. ecological site, Pasture 2. **Key species:** bluebunch wheatgrass.

| Use class | % of key species production removed | Plants tallied | % of transect |
|---|---|---|---|
| Class 1 (0–5%, ungrazed) | 0–5% | 8 | 8% |
| Class 2 (6–20%, slight) | 6–20% | 14 | 14% |
| Class 3 (21–40%, moderate) | 21–40% | 31 | 31% |
| Class 4 (41–60%, moderate-heavy) | 41–60% | 29 | 29% |
| Class 5 (61–80%, heavy) | 61–80% | 15 | 15% |
| Class 6 (81–100%, severe) | 81–100% | 3 | 3% |

**Weighted average utilization** ≈ 42% (midpoint of each class × its frequency, summed). Against a proper-use factor of 45% for this site's current rangeland-health rating (see §2), this reads as at or slightly under allowable use — no mid-season action required, but close enough to the ceiling to flag for next year's turnout-date review.

**Method choice by vegetation type:** grazed-plant method for bunchgrass rangeland (above); height-weight method where a calibrated weight curve exists for the key species (faster, needs prior curve-building); landscape appearance method for quick multi-pasture reconnaissance where a rough 5-class read (slight/light/moderate/close/severe) is enough to flag which pastures need the detailed method.

## 2. Rangeland health assessment (filled example, abbreviated)

17 indicators scored (extreme-to-total / moderate-to-extreme / moderate / slight-to-moderate / none-to-slight departure from the ecological site's reference community), then synthesized into three attribute ratings — never a single overall score.

| Attribute | Key indicators read | Rating (this site) | What drove it |
|---|---|---|---|
| Soil/site stability | Rills, gullies, pedestals, litter movement | None-to-slight | Litter cover intact, no active rilling |
| Hydrologic function | Compaction layer, plant mortality/decadence, infiltration | Slight-to-moderate | Minor compaction near water source, otherwise intact |
| Biotic integrity | Functional/structural groups, invasive plants, reproductive capability of perennials | Moderate | Cheatgrass frequency up from 4% (2019 baseline) to 17% (2025 read); bluebunch reproductive capability rated slight-to-moderate departure |

**Reading the result:** soil and hydrology are still doing their job; biotic integrity is the process actually failing, driven specifically by cheatgrass encroachment, not by grazing-caused soil loss. That routes the fix toward invasive-species treatment and possibly a reduced proper-use factor to protect perennial vigor while treatment takes effect — not toward a blanket stocking cut across the whole allotment, which would address the wrong indicator.

**Threshold check:** compare this year's biotic-integrity indicators against the prior baseline read (here, 2019). A moderate rating that was also moderate in 2019 is a stable-but-degraded state; a moderate rating that was slight-to-moderate in 2019 is an active decline — the second gets escalated to restoration scoping (§4) immediately, the first can go on a standard 3–5 year re-read cycle.

## 3. Proper Functioning Condition (PFC) checklist (filled example)

Applied per lotic reach, independent of the upland pasture rating. 17 checklist items across hydrology, vegetation, and erosion/deposition, answered yes/no/N-A, synthesized into a category.

**Reach 3, [creek name], 1.2 miles, assessed 2024:**

| Category | Finding |
|---|---|
| Hydrology | Floodplain access present but reduced on outside bends; flow energy not well dissipated during high flow |
| Vegetation | Woody riparian species (willow) present but heavily hedged; herbaceous greenline retreat visible on ~30% of reach length |
| Erosion/deposition | Active cutbanks on 3 of 8 surveyed bends; point-bar deposition suggests excess sediment load |

**Category assigned: Functional-at-Risk, downward trend.** (Not Nonfunctional — floodplain connectivity and some woody riparian structure remain; not PFC — active erosion and vegetation departure indicators fail.)

**Trend basis:** compared against the 2019 PFC read on the same reach (also FAR, but trend rated "not apparent" at that time) — the 2024 read is the first to document active cutbank progression, which is what moves this from a watch-list reach to a priority-action reach.

**Action implication:** per the SKILL.md heuristic, a downward-trend FAR reach with herbaceous utilization at 55% (against a 30% riparian threshold for this vegetation type) gets a full-season rest first, with a re-read the following year — exclusion fencing is held in reserve for if the rest period doesn't arrest the downward trend, not applied immediately, since a working reach that responds to rest keeps the cheaper, reversible option open.

## 4. AUM / drought adjustment worksheet (filled example)

```
ALLOTMENT: 04521                              SEASON: 2026
PERMITTED USE: 500 AUMs, 5/1–9/30 (5 months), 100 cow/calf pairs

KEY AREA PRODUCTION READ (4/18/2026):    165 lbs/acre air-dry
20-YEAR KEY AREA NORMAL:                  300 lbs/acre air-dry
PERCENT OF NORMAL:                        55%

DROUGHT TRIGGER (allotment monitoring plan, approved 2019):
  Production < 60% of normal -> proportional non-use adjustment
  55% < 60% trigger  ->  ADJUSTMENT REQUIRED

ADJUSTED AUMs = 500 x 0.55 = 275 AUMs
NON-USE = 500 - 275 = 225 AUMs (45%)

TRANSLATION TO OPERATION (options, permittee's choice):
  Option A: 100 pairs x (275 AUM / 100 pairs) = 2.75 months
            = ~84 days from 5/1 turnout -> off ~7/23
  Option B: (275 AUM / 5 months) = 55 pairs, full 5/1-9/30 season

RIPARIAN (Reach 3): FULL-SEASON REST regardless of upland option
  (FAR-downward trend, see PFC checklist above; not captured in
  upland production math since key area is an upland site)
```

**Restoration scoping trigger:** if next year's key-area read again comes in under 60% of normal *and* the biotic-integrity indicator (§2) has moved from moderate to moderate-to-extreme departure, escalate from a drought non-use adjustment (temporary, reversible) to restoration scoping (below) — two consecutive years of trigger plus a worsening biotic-integrity trend is the threshold-crossing signal, not drought alone.

## 5. Restoration scoping (when monitoring shows a threshold crossing)

Fallback options in preference order, cheapest/least-disruptive first — try each only where site conditions support it:

1. **Targeted grazing / prescribed grazing timing** to suppress the invader during its vulnerable growth window (e.g., early-spring grazing on cheatgrass before seed-set) — lowest cost, works only at low-to-moderate invasion percentages (roughly under 20% frequency) and needs to be repeated for several years.
2. **Prescribed fire timed against the invader's phenology**, where fuel continuity and a burn window exist and the reference community includes fire-adapted species — mid-cost, higher risk (escape, unintended mortality of desired species if timed wrong).
3. **Herbicide treatment** (e.g., pre-emergent for annual grasses) — moderate-to-high cost, most reliable single-season knockdown, but does nothing for reproductive capacity of remaining perennials without a follow-up seeding.
4. **Mechanical treatment + seeding** (drill or aerial seeding of a reference-community perennial mix) — highest cost, required once the site has crossed far enough that a residual perennial seed bank can't recover the site on its own.

**Documentation requirement:** whichever option is chosen, the allotment file gets a before/after key-area photo point, the ecological site ID and the state-and-transition diagram showing which transition is being targeted, and a re-read scheduled at 2 and 5 years — a restoration treatment with no scheduled re-read is functionally a one-time expense with no way to know if it worked.
