# Red flags

Smell tests a range manager catches early in a file review or field visit. Format for each: what it usually means, the first question to ask, and the data to pull before anyone makes a decision.

## 1. Utilization reads uniform across the whole pasture, no gradient near water

**Usually means:** the key area was placed somewhere convenient rather than where livestock actually concentrate — real utilization is almost always heaviest within a quarter mile of water/salt and drops off with distance. A flat reading usually means the transect missed the concentration zone, not that use is genuinely even.

**First question:** "Where's the water and salt relative to this key area, and did we walk a distance-from-water gradient or just the one transect?"

**Data to pull:** key-area location relative to water developments; a supplemental utilization read at 0.25, 0.5, and 1+ mile bands from water if one hasn't been done; salting/mineral placement history.

## 2. Permitted AUMs unchanged for 15+ years with no monitoring update in the file

**Usually means:** the permit number reflects the last NEPA analysis, not current capacity — production, condition, and even the ecological site's state may have shifted since, in either direction. An unreviewed number is a legal ceiling, not evidence the ceiling is still correct.

**First question:** "What's the most recent trend or utilization data on file for this allotment, and how many years since the last full monitoring cycle?"

**Data to pull:** monitoring file dates; last AMP/NEPA decision date; any interim drought or wet-year adjustments made outside the formal cycle.

## 3. "Take half, leave half" cited with no condition-class qualifier

**Usually means:** whoever's citing it learned the number without the caveat — 50% is a good-condition, normal-year starting point, not a constant. Applied to fair/poor-condition range or a drought year, it locks in continued decline.

**First question:** "What's this site's current condition rating and this year's percent-of-normal production — is 50% actually this site's proper-use factor this year?"

**Data to pull:** current rangeland-health rating; production percent-of-normal; the allotment's or ranch's stated proper-use factor by pasture, if one exists.

## 4. Riparian greenline retreat or active cutbanks on a meaningful share of a reach

**Usually means:** the reach has crossed from stable to actively eroding — deposition and cutbank progression are a hydrologic-function failure, not a vegetation-cosmetics issue, and they compound (each cutbank widens the channel, which lowers the water table, which stresses the riparian vegetation that was holding the bank).

**First question:** "What's the trend since the last PFC read on this specific reach — is this the first read showing active erosion, or has it been declining for multiple cycles?"

**Data to pull:** prior PFC assessments on the same reach; herbaceous/woody utilization on the riparian pasture; grazing season-of-use and duration history for that pasture.

## 5. Annual-grass (cheatgrass, medusahead) frequency rising across successive transect reads

**Usually means:** the site is mid-crossing on a state-and-transition threshold — this is the single strongest early signal that rest alone will not restore the site, because annual grasses out-compete perennial seedlings for the same window rest is supposed to protect.

**First question:** "What was this transect's annual-grass frequency at the last two reads, and is the trend still accelerating or has it plateaued?"

**Data to pull:** frequency/cover trend data across at least two prior reads; biotic-integrity indicator rating from the most recent rangeland health assessment; any prior treatment history on the site.

## 6. Permittee requests a one-time exception to the grazing system every year

**Usually means:** the "exception" has become the de facto system, and the pasture rotation on paper no longer matches actual use — this quietly defeats whatever rest or deferment the rotation was designed to provide, since a pasture that's "just this once" grazed early every year is functionally always grazed early.

**First question:** "Pull the last five years of actual-use reports against the AOI — how many years was the stated rotation actually followed?"

**Data to pull:** actual-use reports vs. AOI-authorized dates and pastures, five-year history; utilization trend on the pasture receiving repeated early/extended use.

## 7. Exclusion fencing proposed as the first riparian fix, before a PFC trend read exists

**Usually means:** someone is reaching for the most visible, most capital-intensive intervention before establishing whether a cheaper, reversible option (season-of-use change, reduced duration, herding) would arrest the trend — fencing also removes the pasture from the grazing rotation permanently unless someone later removes it, which rarely happens.

**First question:** "Do we have a PFC trend read on this specific reach, and has a lower-cost management change been tried and given at least one full season to show a result?"

**Data to pull:** most recent PFC assessment and trend; riparian pasture grazing history (season, duration, intensity) for the prior 3–5 years; cost estimate for fencing vs. the management-change alternative.

## 8. Drought non-use decided mid-summer after visible forage damage

**Usually means:** there was no pre-season drought trigger in the allotment's monitoring plan, or it existed but wasn't checked before turnout — by the time damage is visible, the plants have already spent root reserves that take more than one growing season to rebuild.

**First question:** "What was the key-area production read before turnout this year, and does the allotment have a documented drought trigger threshold?"

**Data to pull:** pre-turnout key-area production reading; the allotment's drought contingency plan, if one exists; prior-year production trend leading into this season.

## 9. Rangeland health summarized as a single overall rating

**Usually means:** the three-attribute structure was collapsed for convenience, which hides which specific process (soil stability, hydrologic function, or biotic integrity) is actually failing — and a fence, a burn, and an invasive-species treatment fix three different problems, so the collapsed rating can't point to the right one.

**First question:** "Can I see the attribute-level ratings, not just the summary — which of the three drove this rating down?"

**Data to pull:** the full 17-indicator worksheet by attribute; which indicators were rated moderate-to-extreme or worse; prior read for the same attributes to establish trend.

## 10. Key area established without a repeat photo point or fixed transect location

**Usually means:** future reads won't be comparable to this one — "trend" requires the same physical location read the same way each cycle, and a re-established or relocated key area quietly resets the baseline without anyone noticing until someone tries to compare two reads that were never actually the same site.

**First question:** "Is there a GPS-located, photo-documented key area here, or did the field crew pick a spot by eye this visit?"

**Data to pull:** key-area location records (GPS coordinates, photo point documentation) across prior monitoring cycles; whether the same personnel/method was used each cycle.
