# Vocabulary

Terms generalists conflate that practitioners keep sharply separate. Each: definition, how a practitioner actually uses it in a sentence, and the common misuse to avoid.

### IFQ vs. TAC vs. quota share (QS)

**TAC** (total allowable catch) is the fishery-wide harvest limit a regulator sets for a season. **Quota share (QS)** is a person's permanent, tradeable percentage entitlement to that fishery. **IFQ** (individual fishing quota) is the pounds that QS percentage translates into for the current season — the number that actually expires at season close.

*In use:* "My QS percentage didn't change, but this year's TAC dropped, so my IFQ pounds are down even though I own the same share I always have."

**Common misuse:** using "quota" to mean all three interchangeably. A captain who says "I'm out of quota" almost always means IFQ pounds for the season, not that they've sold or lost their QS.

### Crew share vs. wage vs. lay system

**Crew share** is a percentage of a trip's *net* proceeds (after operating costs) paid to each participant, including the owner-operator. **Wage** is a fixed rate independent of trip outcome, rare on small owner-operator boats. **Lay system** is the broader traditional term for the whole share-based compensation structure, historically from whaling and still used in some fleets' paperwork.

*In use:* "We're on a straight lay — boat takes 40%, three full shares split the rest, and nobody gets paid off gross."

**Common misuse:** quoting a "day rate" as if it applies on a share boat, or assuming a bad trip only costs the owner — on a lay system it costs every share equally.

### Ex-vessel price vs. landed price vs. retail price

**Ex-vessel price** is what the processor/buyer pays the boat per pound at the dock, before any further handling. **Landed price** is sometimes used interchangeably with ex-vessel, though some fisheries distinguish it as the price after any grading adjustment. **Retail price** is what a consumer eventually pays and is largely irrelevant to trip economics — it can be several multiples of ex-vessel and moves on a different cycle.

*In use:* "Retail halibut is up again this year, but our ex-vessel price is basically flat — that spread is going to the processor and distributor, not the boat."

**Common misuse:** citing retail price trends as evidence a trip should be more profitable; the boat is only ever paid ex-vessel, and the two prices don't move together reliably.

### Freeboard vs. draft vs. GM (metacentric height)

**Freeboard** is the vertical distance from the waterline to the main deck — the visible safety margin against water coming aboard. **Draft** is how deep the hull sits in the water. **GM (metacentric height)** is the underlying stability measure — roughly, how strongly the vessel wants to right itself when heeled — that freeboard and draft only partially reveal to the eye.

*In use:* "Freeboard still looks okay, but GM's been eaten into by the ice on the rigging — the boat won't tell you that by how it sits, only by how it rolls."

**Common misuse:** treating freeboard as a full stability check on its own. A boat can have adequate visible freeboard and still have dangerously reduced GM from high, off-center weight like ice on the mast or a full boom.

### Bycatch vs. discard vs. prohibited species catch (PSC)

**Bycatch** is any non-target species or size caught incidentally. **Discard** is bycatch (or unwanted target catch) returned to the water, dead or alive. **PSC (prohibited species catch)** is a bycatch category the regulator specifically caps and requires be discarded regardless of condition, with a hard fishery-wide or vessel limit that can trigger closure.

*In use:* "That's PSC, not just bycatch — it comes off our cap the second it's on deck, whether or not we planned to keep it."

**Common misuse:** treating all bycatch as equally low-stakes. Ordinary bycatch is a cost and a sustainability concern; PSC is a hard regulatory tripwire that can end a season early for the whole fleet.

### TED vs. BRD

**TED** (turtle excluder device) is a rigid grid fitted in a trawl net that lets sea turtles and other large bycatch escape through a flap, required since 1987 and refined to a maximum 3-inch bar spacing with roughly 97% turtle-exclusion effectiveness in current designs. **BRD** (bycatch reduction device) is a broader category of gear modifications — different mesh, escape panels, sorting grids — aimed at reducing bycatch of finfish and other non-turtle species, required in some trawl fisheries independent of TED requirements.

*In use:* "We're required to run both — the TED for turtles, plus a fisheye BRD for finfish bycatch — and they're inspected separately."

**Common misuse:** treating "TED" as a catch-all term for any bycatch gear. A boat can be TED-compliant and still be out of compliance on its BRD requirement, or vice versa — they're separate certifications.

### Season vs. opener vs. derby fishery

**Season** is the full regulatory window a fishery is legally open. **Opener** is a specific, often short, announced period within a season when a particular gear type or area is allowed to fish, common in salmon and crab management. **Derby fishery** is a management style (largely phased out by IFQ programs but still present in some fisheries) where the whole season's TAC is caught in a short, unrestricted race rather than allocated as individual quota.

*In use:* "This isn't a derby anymore — under IFQ we can spread our pounds across the season, but the openers within it are still set by area."

**Common misuse:** assuming an "open season" means fishing is unrestricted throughout it; many seasons operate through discrete openers, and missing one can mean missing that gear type's access entirely even with quota still unlanded.

### Cost recovery fee vs. landing tax vs. license fee

**Cost recovery fee** is a specific NOAA mechanism (in IFQ programs like Alaska halibut/sablefish) charging quota holders a percentage of ex-vessel value, currently around 3%, to fund the quota program's administration. **Landing tax** is a state or local tax on landed catch value, separate from and additional to federal cost recovery. **License fee** is a fixed or scaled cost for the permit or quota itself, paid independent of any given trip's catch.

*In use:* "After cost recovery and the state landing tax, we're down almost 6% of gross before a single operating cost comes off."

**Common misuse:** lumping all three into "fees" when budgeting a trip — they're calculated differently (percentage of value vs. flat vs. per-pound) and missing one in the break-even worksheet understates the true cost stack.

### VMS vs. EPIRB vs. AIS

**VMS** (vessel monitoring system) is a regulatory tracking transponder reporting position to fishery management for quota-area and closure enforcement. **EPIRB** (emergency position-indicating radio beacon) is a distress device, activated manually or on immersion, that alerts search-and-rescue via satellite. **AIS** (automatic identification system) is a navigational safety broadcast used for collision avoidance between vessels, not a distress or regulatory-compliance tool.

*In use:* "VMS tells the council where we were fishing; it won't call anyone if we go over — that's what the EPIRB is for."

**Common misuse:** assuming any one of the three substitutes for another — a boat with VMS reporting fine can still have no working distress capability if its EPIRB registration or battery has lapsed.

### Stability letter vs. load line

**Stability letter** is the vessel-specific document from a USCG dockside exam stating the tested safe-operating envelope (loading condition, weather limits) for that particular boat. **Load line** is a hull marking (mainly on larger, classed vessels) indicating the maximum draft permitted for given water and season conditions — most small fishing vessels under Coast Guard commercial fishing rules aren't load-line vessels and rely on the stability letter instead.

*In use:* "We don't carry a load line — under 79 feet, our safe-loading reference is the stability letter, and that's what the go/no-go call gets checked against."

**Common misuse:** looking for a load-line mark on a small fishing vessel that doesn't have one, or treating the absence of a load line as the absence of any stability limit at all.

### Soak time vs. set vs. haul

**Set** is the act of deploying gear (net, longline, pots). **Soak time** is how long the gear sits fishing before retrieval — a variable a captain actively tunes for target species and weather. **Haul** (or haulback) is the retrieval of the gear and its catch.

*In use:* "We're cutting soak time in half today — the swell's building and I'd rather haul more often on shorter sets than have gear out when it really picks up."

**Common misuse:** treating soak time as fixed by habit or species biology alone. It's also a live risk-management lever — shorter soaks mean more haul cycles (more deck-work exposure) but less gear left unattended in worsening weather, and a captain adjusts it trip to trip.
