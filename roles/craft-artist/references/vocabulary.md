# Vocabulary

Working vocabulary generalists reliably misuse. Format per term: definition, how a practitioner actually uses it, the common misuse.

### Keystone pricing

- **Definition:** the convention of doubling cost to reach wholesale price, then doubling wholesale to reach retail — a total 4× multiple on materials-plus-labor cost.
- **In use:** "This mug costs $19 to make — keystone puts it at $38 wholesale, $76 retail. If a boutique wants a better wholesale number, that comes out of my margin, not out of the retail price."
- **Common misuse:** treating keystone as the ceiling instead of the floor for OOAK or gallery-tier work, leaving real scarcity value uncollected; also confusing it with a straight 2× cost-to-retail markup, which skips the wholesale step entirely and underprices wholesale accounts.

### One-of-a-kind (OOAK)

- **Definition:** a single, non-repeatable piece, as distinct from a production-line design made in multiples.
- **In use:** "The production mugs are keystone-priced at $76. This one's OOAK — hand-carved, four hours of design time that won't recur — it's priced at $340, not formula."
- **Common misuse:** calling a production piece "one-of-a-kind" for marketing effect when it's actually a repeatable design with minor variation; this erodes the term's pricing power for genuinely unique work.

### Line sheet

- **Definition:** the wholesale sales document listing SKU, description, wholesale price, suggested retail, minimum order quantity, and lead time.
- **In use:** "Send the buyer the current line sheet, not last season's — the silver line repriced two weeks ago."
- **Common misuse:** conflating a line sheet with a retail catalog or lookbook; a line sheet's job is transactional (price, minimum, lead time), not to sell the story of the work.

### Consignment vs. wholesale

- **Definition:** consignment — the venue takes the piece without paying upfront and remits a commission split only on pieces actually sold, returning the rest; wholesale — the venue buys the piece outright at the wholesale price and owns it (and the resale risk) from that point.
- **In use:** "I turned down consignment at 50/50 for wholesale at $139 — slower per-unit dollars, but the cash comes in 30 days instead of whenever it sells, if it sells."
- **Common misuse:** treating consignment as automatically more lucrative because the headline commission split (60/40 or better) looks better than the wholesale number, without pricing in settlement delay and unsold-inventory risk.

### Jurying

- **Definition:** the process by which a show or gallery's selection panel evaluates submitted images and a stated body of work to decide admission, typically scored on originality, craftsmanship as visible in the images, and presentation.
- **In use:** "We got rejected on jurying two years running — the feedback both times cited an inconsistent image set, not the work itself."
- **Common misuse:** assuming a jury rejection is a verdict on the work's quality rather than, often, on the photography and presentation of the submission.

### Sell-through rate

- **Definition:** the percentage of stocked or consigned inventory actually sold within a given period, as distinct from units shipped or placed.
- **In use:** "Shipped 20 units to that boutique three months ago; sell-through is 35% — the price point's probably off for that store's customer base."
- **Common misuse:** reporting "units placed" or "units wholesaled" as if it were sales — a wholesale order is revenue for the maker but tells nothing about whether the piece is actually moving at retail.

### Booth fee vs. jury fee

- **Definition:** jury fee — a smaller, non-refundable fee paid to apply/be considered for a show, charged regardless of acceptance; booth fee — the (larger) cost of the physical space, charged only on acceptance.
- **In use:** "Budget the jury fee as sunk the moment you apply — the booth fee only becomes a real cost decision once you're actually in."
- **Common misuse:** lumping both into "show costs" before acceptance, which overstates the cost of applying broadly to shows as a strategy.

### Spot price / repricing trigger

- **Definition:** the current market price of a commodity input (e.g., precious metal traded by weight); a repricing trigger is a stated percentage move in that price past which a maker recomputes and reissues pricing.
- **In use:** "Silver moved 12% since I last costed the line — that's past my 10% trigger, so the wholesale sheet gets reissued before the next restock, not at the usual quarterly review."
- **Common misuse:** repricing only on a fixed calendar schedule regardless of how far the input has moved, which either reprices too often on noise or too rarely after a real jump.

### Artist statement

- **Definition:** a short written description of process, materials, and intent accompanying a body of work, used in jury submissions, gallery placements, and show materials.
- **In use:** "Keep the statement to process and materials — the jury never meets me, so biography does nothing for the score."
- **Common misuse:** writing the statement as a biography or artist's-journey narrative when the venue's context (jurying, wholesale line sheet) calls for process-and-materials specificity instead.

### Production line vs. custom/commission

- **Definition:** production line — a repeatable design made to the same specification across units; custom/commission — a piece designed to a specific client's request, typically priced independently of the standard line.
- **In use:** "The commission price includes the design consultation hours on top of the standard formula — it's not the same math as the production necklace."
- **Common misuse:** pricing a commission using the production-line formula, which under-recovers the extra design and revision time a custom request actually costs.

### Design copyright / knockoff

- **Definition:** original craft designs are protected under copyright as soon as fixed in a tangible form (a photo, a sketch, the object itself); formal registration with the U.S. Copyright Office is a separate, additional step required before filing an infringement suit or claiming statutory damages.
- **In use:** "The design's protected from the day I photographed and listed it — but if a bigger retailer copies it, I'd need to register before I could sue for statutory damages, not just prove I made it first."
- **Common misuse:** believing no protection exists without registration (protection exists automatically on fixation), or conversely believing "I made it first" alone is enough to win an infringement suit without ever registering.

### Marketplace fee stack (e.g., Etsy)

- **Definition:** the combined listing fee (charged per listing over a fixed period), transaction fee (a percentage of the sale price including shipping), and payment-processing fee (a percentage plus a flat amount) that a marketplace platform charges per sale, distinct from a maker's own cost-of-goods.
- **In use:** "The listing looks like it cleared $76, but after the marketplace's transaction and processing cut, net is closer to $68 — that has to be in the retail price, not absorbed after the fact."
- **Common misuse:** pricing to the sticker retail number without backing out the platform's fee stack, which quietly reduces realized margin below the costing worksheet's target.
