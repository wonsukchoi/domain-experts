# Red Flags

Smell tests for pricing, channel mix, and venue relationships. Format per flag: what it usually means, the first question to ask, the data to pull.

### Retail price under 3.5× material cost with labor hours above 1

- **Usually means:** labor was dropped from the formula, or the stated hourly rate is below a sustainable wage for the skill level. The lowest-margin makers are often the busiest, because low prices move inventory fast without covering time.
- **First question:** "What hourly rate does this price imply once materials are backed out?"
- **Data to pull:** the per-SKU costing worksheet; divide (retail ÷ 4 − materials) by labor hours and compare to the maker's stated rate.

### A wholesale account's first order sold through but the reorder never came, after 60+ days

- **Usually means:** a sell-through problem at that specific store — wrong price point for the shop's customer base, poor shelf placement, or a merchandising mismatch — not a relationship problem.
- **First question:** "Did the pieces actually sell, or are they still sitting in the store's inventory?"
- **Data to pull:** ask the buyer directly for sell-through by SKU; if the buyer won't answer, treat non-response itself as the signal.

### Consignment settlement running past the agreed schedule by 30+ days, more than once

- **Usually means:** the venue is using consigned inventory proceeds as informal working capital, or has an accounting backlog that deprioritizes consignors. Both patterns tend to repeat.
- **First question:** "Has this happened before with this venue, and did they cite a reason unprompted?"
- **Data to pull:** payment-date history against the signed agreement's settlement terms for the last 3 consignment cycles.

### More than roughly 60% of annual revenue concentrated in a single show or single wholesale account

- **Usually means:** a single cancelled show, dropped account, or venue policy change (new commission structure, new booth-fee tier) can remove the majority of income in one event.
- **First question:** "What's the plan if this show or account is gone next season?"
- **Data to pull:** revenue by venue/account for the trailing 12 months, ranked and summed as a share of total.

### Jury rejection from a show the work has previously been accepted into, with no change in technique

- **Usually means:** the submitted images, not the work, changed for the worse — new photographer, inconsistent lighting/background across the set, or a body-of-work selection that reads as scattered.
- **First question:** "Did the image set for this submission match the consistency of the set that got accepted before?"
- **Data to pull:** side-by-side comparison of this year's and last accepted year's jury images (lighting, background, framing consistency).

### Show cost (booth + travel + jury fee) exceeding roughly 20–25% of that show's historical revenue

- **Usually means:** the show has drifted from profit center to marketing spend — still worth attending for visibility or wholesale-lead generation, but not for direct sales math alone.
- **First question:** "Is this show still earning its place on cost, or only on habit and hope?"
- **Data to pull:** trailing 2–3 years of revenue and total cost for that specific show; compute cost as a percentage of revenue each year and check the trend direction.

### Commodity input price moved >10% since the last costing and pricing hasn't been revisited

- **Usually means:** margin is eroding silently on every unit sold at the old cost basis; the erosion compounds across a full production run before anyone notices in the books.
- **First question:** "When was this SKU's material cost last checked against current spot or supplier price?"
- **Data to pull:** current spot/supplier price versus the price used in the last costing worksheet for that SKU; compute the percentage move.

### A design closely resembling the maker's own showing up at a much lower price point at a large retailer

- **Usually means:** independent-designer knockoffs by larger manufacturers are common enough in jewelry and textiles that similarity alone isn't proof, but timing (posted online before the retailer's version shipped) and specificity (an unusual construction detail, not just a general style) strengthen the case.
- **First question:** "Is the design original enough, and was it fixed in a tangible/documented form (photo, sketch, listing) with a provable date, before the retailer's version appeared?"
- **Data to pull:** dated documentation of the original design (first listing date, dated sketch, social post) and a side-by-side comparison of the specific construction or motif in question.

### A gallery or boutique proposes consignment when a comparable wholesale relationship is available elsewhere

- **Usually means:** consignment shifts inventory risk and cash-flow timing to the maker while offering a higher headline commission split; it is worth it only when the venue's traffic or prestige materially exceeds the wholesale alternative's.
- **First question:** "What does this venue's foot traffic or reputation add that a wholesale account wouldn't?"
- **Data to pull:** the venue's typical sell-through rate for comparable makers, if it will share it, and the settlement-schedule terms being offered.

### Booth/display or packaging spend is zero or near-zero while sell-through at shows lags similar makers

- **Usually means:** presentation, not craftsmanship or price, is suppressing conversion — jurors and customers both react to booth and packaging quality as a proxy for the work's quality.
- **First question:** "Would a customer's first five seconds at this booth signal the price point the work is actually asking for?"
- **Data to pull:** sell-through rate at the last 2–3 shows compared against booth/display spend as a share of total show cost.
