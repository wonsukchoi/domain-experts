# Vocabulary

Terms generalists conflate or use loosely that an aircraft service attendant keeps precise, because the precision is what a contamination-control step or a pushback decision actually turns on.

### Cross-connection (potable water)
A physical or plumbing link, even momentary, between an aircraft's potable water system and any non-potable system (lavatory/waste, non-potable rinse water) — the specific failure mode the Aircraft Drinking Water Rule's dedicated-equipment requirements exist to prevent.

**In use:** "That adapter would have cross-connected the lav hose to the potable port — swap the cart, don't force the fitting."

**Common misuse:** treating cross-connection as a hypothetical worst case rather than a documented, sampled-and-measured contamination pathway (EPA's 2004 survey found roughly 15% of tested aircraft coliform-positive).

### Aircraft Drinking Water Rule (ADWR)
The EPA regulation (40 CFR Part 141 Subpart X, finalized 2009) requiring periodic total-coliform sampling, disinfection, and operational controls — including dedicated servicing equipment — for aircraft potable water systems.

**In use:** "This tail is due for its ADWR sample this week — pull the coliform test kit, not just the fill hose."

**Common misuse:** assuming water-system compliance is a one-time equipment spec rather than an ongoing sampling and disinfection schedule.

### Lavatory (lav) service vs. potable water service
Two distinct ground-servicing tasks on separate systems: lav service drains and rinses the waste tank and replenishes precharge fluid; potable water service fills the drinking-water tank. They use separate, non-interchangeable carts and couplings by design.

**In use:** "Lav's done on 2R, moving to potable on the separate cart now."

**Common misuse:** referring to "water service" generically as if it covers both systems, which is exactly the loose language that leads to grabbing the wrong cart.

### Holdover time (HOT)
The estimated duration, from a published table by fluid type/concentration, precipitation, and outside air temperature, that an anti-icing fluid application protects a surface from refreezing before it must be reapplied or reinspected. The clock starts at the beginning of the final application, not its end.

**In use:** "HOT on this table is 35 minutes from the start of the spray, not from when the trucks pull away — we're already eight minutes into it."

**Common misuse:** starting the mental clock at the end of application or at pushback, which overstates the remaining protection window by the length of the application itself.

### Deicing vs. anti-icing (Type I vs. Type II/IV fluid)
Deicing removes existing frost, ice, or snow (commonly heated, unthickened Type I fluid, which has little to no meaningful holdover once it drains off). Anti-icing applies a thickened fluid (Type II or IV) after deicing to delay new ice formation, and it's this second-step fluid the holdover-time table applies to.

**In use:** "Type I got the wings clean; Type IV afterward is what's actually buying us the 35-minute window."

**Common misuse:** treating a Type I application alone as providing a meaningful holdover buffer ahead of a ground delay, when it's designed to remove contamination, not delay new accumulation.

### Clean aircraft concept
The regulatory principle (14 CFR 121.629 in the US) that no aircraft may take off with frost, ice, or snow adhering to critical surfaces — the basis for the pre-takeoff contamination check when a holdover-time window is in question.

**In use:** "If we're past holdover before rotation, that's a clean-aircraft-concept call for the flight crew — pre-takeoff check or re-treat, not a judgment call for ground crew to skip."

**Common misuse:** treating the clean-aircraft requirement as satisfied once deicing has been performed, regardless of how much time and what conditions have passed since.

### GSE (Ground Support Equipment)
Any vehicle or powered equipment used to service, load, or move an aircraft on the ground — potable/lav carts, belt loaders, pushback tugs, deicing trucks — each carrying its own clearance and staging rules relative to an active aircraft.

**In use:** "Get that GSE clear of the wingtip before the tug hooks up."

**Common misuse:** treating "GSE" as interchangeable with "any vehicle on the ramp," which obscures that different GSE types carry different clearance rules tied to what they do and where they operate.

### Anti-collision beacon
The rotating or strobing light on an aircraft's fuselage/tail that, by ramp-operations convention, indicates engines are running or about to start — the actual signal ground crew uses to decide whether it's safe to approach with GSE.

**In use:** "Beacon's still on — hold the cart until it's off, doesn't matter that the engines look spooled down."

**Common misuse:** substituting a visual read of engine/propeller rotation for the beacon status, when the beacon is the governing convention specifically because visual rotation cues can be misleading or delayed.

### Jet blast hazard zone
The area directly aft of a running engine where exhaust velocity poses an injury and equipment-damage risk, extending much farther at higher power settings than at idle.

**In use:** "That's takeoff-thrust jet blast behind 2 — nobody stages equipment back there, full stop."

**Common misuse:** assuming the hazard zone is a fixed distance regardless of power setting, rather than scaling sharply with thrust.

### Total coliform
A group of bacteria used as an indicator organism for water-system contamination; a positive result on an aircraft potable water sample doesn't itself mean the water caused illness, but it indicates the disinfection/protection barrier has failed and triggers the ADWR-required response.

**In use:** "Coliform-positive on this tail — that's a disinfection flush and a documented cause investigation, not a re-sample and move on."

**Common misuse:** treating a single positive result as inconclusive noise rather than the trigger for the mandated response sequence.

### FOD (Foreign Object Debris)
Any object or material on the ramp/taxiway capable of damaging an aircraft or engine — a broken cart part, a dropped tool, ramp debris — distinct from the servicing tasks themselves but part of the same GSE-safety domain.

**In use:** "Sweep that dropped coupling cap before pushback — that's FOD sitting in the taxi path."

**Common misuse:** treating FOD control as someone else's job (ramp sweeper, maintenance) rather than every ground-crew member's responsibility at the point of discovery.

### Ground time / turn time (aviation-specific)
The scheduled interval between an aircraft's arrival at the gate and its scheduled departure, within which all ground tasks — fuel, catering, cargo, cleaning, potable/lav service — must fit; distinct from a generic "turnaround time" in that it's tied to a specific gate schedule and departure slot, not a reservation system.

**In use:** "We've got 40 minutes ground time on this tail — lav and potable are the last two tasks, everything else already cleared."

**Common misuse:** treating ground time as a soft estimate rather than the number every other ramp task, including deicing timing, has to be sequenced against.
