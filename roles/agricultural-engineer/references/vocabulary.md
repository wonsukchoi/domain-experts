# Vocabulary

Terms generalists flatten together that an agricultural engineer keeps sharply separate — the value is in the misuse, not the definition.

## Peak ET vs. average/seasonal ET

**Peak evapotranspiration (ET)** is the crop's water-use rate on its single highest-demand day, typically during a specific growth stage (tasseling-silking for corn). **Seasonal average ET** is total water use divided across the whole growing season — a much smaller daily number. Irrigation capacity is designed to the peak, never the average.

**In use:** "The well covers the seasonal average fine — the question is whether it covers the 0.32 in/day peak during tasseling, not the 0.18 in/day average."

**Common misuse:** sizing or approving a system's capacity against average seasonal water use, which every system with adequate total-season volume will pass regardless of whether it can keep pace on the worst day.

## Management allowed depletion (MAD)

The fraction of a crop's available soil water that can be depleted before yield-limiting stress begins — commonly 50% for row crops at sensitive growth stages. Usable soil water storage = available water capacity (AWC) × effective root depth × MAD.

**In use:** "At 50% MAD, this sandy loam profile has 3.0 inches of usable buffer — that's what absorbs a short-term irrigation deficit without a yield hit."

**Common misuse:** treating the full available water capacity as usable buffer, ignoring that crop stress begins well before the soil is fully depleted.

## Application efficiency vs. distribution uniformity (DU)

**Application efficiency** is the fraction of water pumped that actually reaches and is stored in the crop root zone (losses: evaporation, wind drift, deep percolation, runoff). **Distribution uniformity** measures how evenly that delivered water is spread across the field — a system can have high average efficiency and still under-water part of the field if DU is poor.

**In use:** "Efficiency's fine at 87%, but the DU test showed the pivot's far span under-applying by 15% — that's a uniformity problem, not a capacity problem."

**Common misuse:** citing a single efficiency percentage as proof the whole field is adequately watered, without a separate uniformity check.

## Design storm / return period

A statistically defined storm event (e.g., "10-year, 24-hour storm") used as the sizing basis for a structure — meaning that storm has roughly a 10% chance of occurring or being exceeded in any given year, not that it happens exactly once per decade or represents the largest storm the structure will ever see.

**In use:** "We're sizing this waterway to the 10-year storm; a bigger event will still overtop it — that's the accepted risk at this consequence class, not a design error."

**Common misuse:** treating the design storm as a guarantee against failure, or as literally the worst-case event, rather than a chosen exceedance probability.

## Manning's roughness coefficient (n)

An empirical value representing a channel surface's resistance to flow, used in Manning's equation to compute open-channel capacity. It varies by lining material and condition (concrete, bare earth, grass at different growth stages) and directly and inversely scales computed capacity.

**In use:** "Same channel geometry, but n jumps from 0.03 bare earth to 0.05 once the grass establishes — that's a real capacity drop, not a rounding difference."

**Common misuse:** reusing a single n-value across a channel's life cycle without accounting for how vegetation establishment changes it.

## Janssen's equation / lateral pressure ratio (K)

**Janssen's equation** computes lateral wall pressure from stored granular material (grain) accounting for wall friction, which fluid hydrostatic pressure does not. **K** is the ratio of lateral to vertical pressure within the grain mass, a grain-specific empirical value distinct from the wall-friction coefficient (μ').

**In use:** "K and μ' are both grain-and-wall-specific — don't reuse corn's values for a soybean bin without checking EP433's table."

**Common misuse:** applying a generic K and μ' pair across different grain types or wall materials, or applying hydrostatic pressure logic (linear growth with depth, no asymptote) to a grain-storage structure at all.

## Drainage coefficient

The design rate (in/day) a subsurface drainage system must remove water from the field to maintain the target water-table depth for trafficability and root aeration — a design input to tile size and spacing, not a measured field value.

**In use:** "This field's drainage coefficient is 3/8 in/day on the silt loam, but drops to 1/4 in/day where the soil survey shows heavier clay — that changes the spacing calc on that end of the field."

**Common misuse:** applying one drainage coefficient across a field with multiple soil types instead of segmenting the design by mapped soil unit.

## First-year nitrogen availability / mineralization

The fraction of manure's total analyzed nitrogen that becomes plant-available in the season it's applied; the remainder is organic N that mineralizes over subsequent seasons and must be tracked as carryover credit, not treated as delivered and consumed in year one.

**In use:** "Total N on the ticket is 200 lb/ac, but at 40% first-year availability for incorporated solid manure, only 80 lb/ac counts against this season's crop need."

**Common misuse:** crediting the full analyzed total N against the current season's fertilizer requirement, over-applying in year one and missing the carryover credit in years two and three.

## Swampbuster / wetland conversion

The Food Security Act provision (16 U.S.C. §3821) making USDA program benefits (crop insurance subsidies, conservation payments, farm loans) conditional on not converting a wetland for agricultural production without an approved exemption or minimal-effect determination — a federal compliance gate distinct from, and prior to, any hydraulic design question.

**In use:** "Hydraulically the tile design is sound, but this is mapped hydric soil — get the wetland determination from NRCS before we finalize anything."

**Common misuse:** treating wetland-conversion compliance as a paperwork formality that follows the engineering design, rather than a gating determination that has to precede it.

## Hydraulic conductivity (Ksat) vs. infiltration rate

**Hydraulic conductivity (Ksat)** is a soil property describing how readily water moves through the saturated soil profile, used in drainage spacing calculations (e.g., Hooghoudt's equation). **Infiltration rate** describes how fast water enters the soil surface, relevant to irrigation-system design and runoff estimation. The two describe different points in the soil-water pathway and aren't interchangeable inputs.

**In use:** "Ksat sets the tile spacing; infiltration rate is what tells us whether the pivot's application rate is outrunning the soil's ability to take the water in."

**Common misuse:** using a surface infiltration rate as a stand-in for subsurface Ksat in a drainage-spacing calculation, or vice versa.

## Freeboard

The vertical margin between a structure's design operating level (water surface in a channel, waste level in a storage structure) and its top — a safety margin against wave action, precipitation surge, or measurement error, sized as an explicit design allowance, not left as whatever margin happens to remain.

**In use:** "Design flow depth is 1.5 ft; specify a 4 ft channel depth so freeboard is 2.5 ft, well above the 0.3–0.5 ft floor for this class of waterway."

**Common misuse:** treating freeboard as leftover capacity rather than a required, explicitly sized design allowance that has to be checked against a governing standard.

## NRCS Conservation Practice Standard (CPS)

A numbered, standardized design specification (e.g., 412 Grassed Waterway, 590 Nutrient Management, 606 Subsurface Drain) that sets the binding design criteria for any work receiving USDA cost-share funding or falling under a compliance program — citing the practice code is how a design demonstrates it meets the applicable floor, not an optional reference.

**In use:** "This waterway design has to meet CPS 412's criteria to qualify for cost-share — cite the standard number in the design memo, not just 'NRCS guidelines.'"

**Common misuse:** referencing "NRCS standards" generically without naming the specific practice code and edition the design was checked against.
