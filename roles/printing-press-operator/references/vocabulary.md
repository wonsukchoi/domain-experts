# Vocabulary

### Dot gain
The phenomenon where a printed dot appears larger on the finished sheet than the dot size built into the plate, from ink spreading during transfer and absorption.
**In use:** "Gain's running at 26 points on this job, well over the 18 the plate was built for."
**Common misuse:** Correcting for dot gain by reducing overall ink density on press, which shifts the entire tonal range rather than fixing the actual gain at the specific tonal point where it's occurring.

### Registration
The precise alignment of multiple print passes or color plates relative to each other.
**In use:** "Registration's off by two thousandths on the magenta plate — that's enough to show as fringing on fine type."
**Common misuse:** Accepting a registration error as acceptable because it isn't visible checking a single color, when process-color work compounds small misalignments across all the plates into visible fringing.

### Densitometer
An instrument measuring the optical density of printed ink, used to verify color consistency against a numeric target rather than visual judgment.
**In use:** "Densitometer's reading 1.4 on the cyan patch — right on target for this job."
**Common misuse:** Relying on visual color matching under press-room lighting instead of densitometer readings, which stay consistent across a long run in a way visual judgment doesn't.

### Dot gain compensation curve
The adjustment built into a printing plate that reduces the plate's dot size below the target percentage, anticipating that gain on press will bring it up to the intended final size.
**In use:** "Rebuild the compensation curve for this press — its actual gain runs higher than the standard curve assumes."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a compensation curve is universal across presses and substrates, when actual gain — and therefore the correct compensation — varies by press condition and substrate.

### Makeready
The setup process before a full production run, during which ink/water balance, registration, and pressure are adjusted and initial proofs are approved.
**In use:** "Makeready's approved, but keep pulling control-patch checks through the first few hundred sheets."
**Common misuse:** Treating a single makeready approval as valid for the entire run, rather than continuing to spot-check, since press conditions can keep drifting after that initial approval.

### Ink/water balance
The relative amounts of ink and dampening solution on an offset press, which have to be balanced together to avoid scumming (too much water) or excessive gain/ink buildup (too much ink).
**In use:** "Backed off the water and eased up the ink slightly — balance was skewed toward scumming."
**Common misuse:** Adjusting only ink or only water in response to a color problem without considering that the two interact and have to be balanced together.

### Screen ruling (lines per inch, lpi)
The resolution of the halftone dot pattern used to reproduce a continuous-tone image, expressed in lines per inch.
**In use:** "This job's spec'd at 150 lpi — check the press has held that ruling cleanly on this stock before."
**Common misuse:** Assuming higher lpi is always better regardless of whether the press can actually hold that resolution on the specified substrate.

### Substrate
The paper or material being printed on, whose absorbency and surface finish — coated versus uncoated — affect ink behavior independently of press settings.
**In use:** "Switching this job to uncoated stock — recheck density and gain before running, don't reuse the coated settings."
**Common misuse:** Assuming press settings developed on one substrate transfer directly to a different substrate without recalibration.

### Control patch (color bar)
A strip of standardized color and tint patches printed alongside the job, used to take consistent density and registration measurements throughout a run.
**In use:** "Read the control patch every 500 sheets — that's what catches drift before it's visible in the image."
**Common misuse:** Skipping control patch readings in favor of comparing the printed image itself to the proof, a far less consistent and precise comparison method.

### Blanket pressure
The pressure applied by an offset press's rubber blanket cylinder against the substrate, affecting ink transfer and dot gain.
**In use:** "Backed off blanket pressure slightly — that was pushing gain higher than the plate's compensated for."
**Common misuse:** Treating blanket pressure as a fixed press setting unrelated to dot-gain troubleshooting, when excess pressure is a common direct cause of higher-than-expected gain.
