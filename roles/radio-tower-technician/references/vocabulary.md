# Vocabulary

Terms generalists conflate that practitioners keep sharply separate.

## MPE vs. SAR

**MPE** (maximum permissible exposure) is a power-density limit (mW/cm²) at a given frequency and distance — the field-level exposure standard used for evaluating occupational/RF-worker exposure near antennas, per 47 CFR 1.1310. **SAR** (specific absorption rate) is a whole-body or localized energy-absorption measure (W/kg) used mainly for device compliance (handsets near the body), not for evaluating a climber's exposure near a transmitting antenna.

**In use:** "We survey against MPE at the work position, not SAR — SAR's a device-compliance number, it doesn't tell us anything about standing three feet from a live sector antenna."

**Common misuse:** citing a phone's SAR rating as if it's relevant to tower-climber RF safety — it's the wrong measurement for the wrong exposure scenario.

## Occupational/controlled vs. general population/uncontrolled exposure

Two separate MPE tiers under 47 CFR 1.1310. **Occupational/controlled** applies to people who are aware of their RF exposure and can control it (trained tower crew) — a higher, less restrictive limit. **General population/uncontrolled** applies where the public could be present without RF awareness — a limit roughly 5x more restrictive at the same frequency.

**In use:** "That reading clears occupational MPE, but if the public can access this rooftop, we're bound by the general-population limit instead — different number, different answer."

**Common misuse:** applying the occupational limit to a location the public can reach, or assuming "MPE" is a single universal number rather than tier- and frequency-dependent.

## VSWR vs. return loss

**VSWR** (voltage standing wave ratio) expresses impedance mismatch as a ratio (e.g. 1.5:1). **Return loss** expresses the same mismatch in dB — the two are mathematically equivalent, just different units, and higher return loss (in dB) means better performance while higher VSWR means worse performance.

**In use:** "VSWR's reading 1.4:1, call it about 15 dB return loss — well inside spec."

**Common misuse:** treating a "higher number is better" instinct consistently across both — a rising VSWR is worse, but a rising return-loss dB figure is better; conflating the two direction-of-improvement conventions leads to misreading a test report.

## PIM vs. IMD

**PIM** (passive intermodulation) is intermodulation distortion generated by nonlinearities in passive components — loose connectors, corroded contacts, dissimilar metals in contact — under high RF power, without any active/powered device involved. **IMD** (intermodulation distortion) is the broader general term, which also covers distortion generated by active components (amplifiers, mixers).

**In use:** "This is a PIM problem, not an amp issue — nothing active in this signal path, so it's a connector or a rusted grounding kit somewhere between the antenna and the jumper."

**Common misuse:** treating any intermodulation finding as an active-equipment fault and sending it to the radio vendor, when a passive connection issue in the antenna/feed-line path is the far more common cause on a tower.

## RFDS (RF Design Sheet)

The carrier's site-specific engineering document stating target azimuth, mechanical and electrical downtilt, antenna model/mounting height, and acceptance tolerances (VSWR, PIM, alignment) for that particular site — the actual spec a completed installation is checked against.

**In use:** "Forget the generic ±3° azimuth rule of thumb — what does this site's RFDS actually call for?"

**Common misuse:** applying an industry rule-of-thumb tolerance instead of checking the site's own RFDS, which is the document with contractual and compliance weight; the rule of thumb is a fallback when no RFDS value exists, not a substitute for one that does.

## NOTAM

**NOTAM** (Notice to Airmen) is the FAA's mechanism for warning pilots of a hazard to flight — including an obstruction light outage on a marked tower. Filed or updated after the tower owner notifies the FAA per 47 CFR 17.48.

**In use:** "Outage started at 9:15, we notified FAA at 9:40, NOTAM's live now covering the light until repair."

**Common misuse:** treating the NOTAM as the compliance action itself — the actual regulatory obligation is the 30-minute notification to the FAA; the NOTAM is what the FAA issues in response, and it has to be updated again if repair runs past its stated window.

## Authorized vs. Competent Climber/Rescuer

NATE's (National Association of Tower Erectors) CRTS (Climber/Rescuer Training Standard) defines tiers: **Authorized Climber/Rescuer** has completed required training; **Competent Climber/Rescuer** has the additional knowledge, skill, and experience — per employer evaluation — to identify hazards and make independent go/no-go calls without direct supervision.

**In use:** "He's Authorized, but this rescue-plan call needs a Competent sign-off before the crew climbs."

**Common misuse:** treating the two designations as interchangeable seniority labels rather than a specific, employer-evaluated authority distinction that determines who can make an independent safety call.

## 100% tie-off

Continuous attachment to the structure at all times a climber's feet are off solid footing — including during transitions between anchor points, not just during sustained work at a fixed position.

**In use:** "100% tie-off means the transition from the ladder to the platform is covered too — that's not a gap where the rule pauses."

**Common misuse:** treating tie-off as satisfied by attachment at the start and end of a climb segment, leaving the actual transition moments — where incident data concentrates — as an implicit gray area.

## Near-field vs. far-field (RF)

**Near-field** is the region close to an antenna where field behavior is complex and doesn't follow simple inverse-square power-density falloff; **far-field** is the region where power density falls predictably with distance and standard MPE calculation methods apply cleanly. Climber work positions near an antenna face are frequently in or near the near-field region.

**In use:** "That inverse-square estimate isn't reliable at three feet from the antenna — we're inside or near the near-field region, use the actual survey reading, not a distance-based calculation."

**Common misuse:** applying a simple distance-based power-density formula to a close-in work position and treating the result as authoritative, when only a direct survey reading is reliable that close to the radiating face.

## Mechanical vs. electrical downtilt

**Mechanical downtilt** physically tilts the entire antenna housing. **Electrical downtilt** shifts the phase of individual antenna elements to tilt the radiation pattern without physically moving the antenna — often adjustable remotely. The two can be combined, and the RFDS specifies each separately.

**In use:** "RFDS calls for 2° mechanical plus 4° electrical — check the physical tilt at the mount and confirm the electrical setting separately, they're not the same adjustment."

**Common misuse:** adjusting the physical mount to hit a total downtilt target that was actually meant to be split between mechanical and electrical settings, or assuming a remote electrical-downtilt change is visible from a ground-level physical inspection.
