# Vocabulary

### Chip load
The amount of material each cutting edge removes during one revolution of a cutter, calculated as feed rate divided by (RPM × number of cutting edges), determining both surface finish quality and burn risk.
**In use:** "Chip load's at point-oh-oh-four now after slowing the feed — that's actually lower, which is the wrong direction if dulling's the real problem."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a slower feed rate always improves surface finish — too low a chip load for the RPM causes the cutter to rub rather than cleanly shear the wood, generating heat and burning, which is the opposite of the intended improvement.

### Grain reversal
A point within a single piece of wood where the direction of the wood grain changes, common in figured or curly-grain wood, requiring a feed direction or technique adjustment at that specific location.
**In use:** "Feed direction that's clean for the first two feet of this board will tear out right where the grain reverses near the knot."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a single feed direction determined at the start of a cut remains correct for the entire workpiece — grain direction can reverse partway through a board, and the technique needs to adjust at that point, not stay fixed.

### Tearout
A wood surface defect where fibers are torn or lifted rather than cleanly cut, typically caused by feeding against the grain direction or using a dull cutter.
**In use:** "That tearout's right at the grain reversal — feed direction needs to change there, not just slow down."
**Common misuse:** Assuming tearout always indicates cutter dullness — feeding against grain direction produces the identical visual defect even with a perfectly sharp cutter, and the two causes require different fixes (technique change vs. tool replacement).

### Moisture equilibration
The ongoing process by which wood gains or loses moisture to match the humidity of its surrounding environment, continuing after machining and capable of changing a part's dimensions over time.
**In use:** "That warp isn't a machining error — the part's been sitting in a much drier environment than where it was machined, and it's equilibrating to that."
**Common misuse:** Diagnosing a post-machining dimensional change as a cutting/machining defect without checking the part's moisture and environmental history — equilibration-driven movement is a distinct, common failure mode separate from an actual machining error.

### Moisture content (wood)
The percentage of water weight relative to the wood's oven-dry weight, a critical variable for dimensional stability that should be matched to a part's intended final use environment, not just its current storage condition.
**In use:** "Checking moisture content against the target for indoor climate-controlled use, not just whatever the stock happens to read right now."
**Common misuse:** Machining wood to precise final dimension without considering whether its current moisture content matches its intended end-use environment — a part correctly dimensioned at the wrong moisture content will move once it reaches a different-humidity final environment.

### Feed direction (with/against grain)
The direction a workpiece moves relative to a cutter's rotation and the wood's grain orientation, determining whether cutting occurs with or against the grain at any given point.
**In use:** "We're feeding with the grain on this face, but flipping the piece to keep the same relationship once we hit the reversed-grain section."
**Common misuse:** Treating feed direction as a single, fixed decision made once at the start of a cut — on figured or irregular wood, correct feed direction can require adjustment partway through the same piece.

### Cutter service interval
The specified usage limit (hours or parts produced) after which a cutting tool should be resharpened or replaced, based on expected wear accumulation rather than visual dullness alone.
**In use:** "We're at fifty-two hours against a forty-hour interval — that's overdue, and it lines up with when the burning started."
**Common misuse:** Waiting for visible dullness or an obvious quality failure before servicing a cutter — a usage-based interval catches wear-driven defects before they become visually obvious or start affecting production.

### Surface finish (wood machining)
The quality of a machined wood surface's smoothness and freedom from defects (tearout, burn, chatter), determined by the combined effect of chip load, grain direction, and cutter sharpness.
**In use:** "Poor finish here could be chip load, grain, or a dull cutter — checking all three before assuming which one it is."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a single default cause (usually feed rate) for any finish quality issue — multiple distinct variables affect finish, and a fix aimed at the wrong one can leave the actual cause unaddressed or even worsen the result.

### Rubbing (cutter action)
A cutting condition where a dull or improperly fed cutter slides across wood fibers generating friction and heat, rather than cleanly shearing them, the mechanical cause of burning.
**In use:** "That's rubbing, not cutting — either the edge is dull or the chip load's too low for this RPM."
**Common misuse:** Assuming burning always traces to excessive heat from cutting too fast — burning is actually caused by rubbing (insufficient material removal per pass relative to friction generated), which can result from either a dull cutter or too low a chip load, not from cutting "too fast" in the sense of excessive material removal rate.
