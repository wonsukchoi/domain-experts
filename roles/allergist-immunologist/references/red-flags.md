# Red Flags

Smell tests for mislabeled allergies, undertreated anaphylaxis, and missed immunodeficiency. Format per flag: what it usually means, the first question to ask, the check to run.

## Anaphylaxis discharged after under 4 hours of observation

- **Usually means:** the discharging clinician treated resolution of visible symptoms as the endpoint rather than the biphasic-reaction window.
- **First question:** "Did this patient need more than one dose of epinephrine, or have any hypotension?"
- **Data to pull:** ED/urgent-care note timestamps for epinephrine doses and vital signs through the visit; discharge time relative to first-dose time.

## "Penicillin allergy" label with no reaction detail charted

- **Usually means:** a childhood rash, often viral and coincidental with the antibiotic, hardened into a permanent label nobody has revisited; the patient has likely been on broader-spectrum, more toxic antibiotics for years as a result.
- **First question:** "What exactly happened, how long ago, and was anything given to treat the reaction?"
- **Data to pull:** any prior tolerated exposure to related beta-lactams (cephalosporins) documented in the record; PEN-FAST-relevant details (timing, angioedema, treatment required).

## More than one epinephrine dose without improvement

- **Usually means:** either under-dosing/poor injection technique (subcutaneous instead of IM, wrong site) or the patient is on a beta-blocker blunting the response.
- **First question:** "Where and how was the epinephrine given, and is this patient on a beta-blocker?"
- **Data to pull:** medication list; injection site and technique documentation; consider glucagon administration record.

## Total IgE over roughly 2000 kIU/L

- **Usually means:** not simply "severe atopy" — consider atopic dermatitis flare, parasitic infection, or hyper-IgE syndrome (Job syndrome) if accompanied by recurrent skin/lung infections and eczema since infancy.
- **First question:** "Is there a history of recurrent skin abscesses, pneumonia, or retained primary teeth?"
- **Data to pull:** STAT3 or DOCK8 genetic testing if the syndromic pattern fits; eosinophil count; travel/exposure history for parasitic causes.

## Recurrent sinopulmonary infections with low or borderline IgG

- **Usually means:** common variable immunodeficiency (CVID) or specific antibody deficiency, not "just prone to colds."
- **First question:** "Has this patient had a documented response to pneumococcal vaccination?"
- **Data to pull:** pre/post pneumococcal titers, IgG/IgA/IgM levels drawn while not on antibiotics or IVIG.

## Chronic urticaria over 6 weeks with fever, joint pain, or weight loss

- **Usually means:** an autoinflammatory or vasculitic process (urticarial vasculitis, Schnitzler syndrome) rather than typical chronic spontaneous urticaria, which doesn't carry systemic symptoms.
- **First question:** "Do the individual hives last more than 24 hours or leave bruising when they fade?"
- **Data to pull:** skin biopsy of an active lesion, inflammatory markers (ESR/CRP), complement levels.

## Normal spirometry and FeNO with reported daily symptoms and heavy reliever use

- **Usually means:** inhaler technique failure, non-adherence, or a masquerading diagnosis (vocal cord dysfunction, deconditioning) rather than "difficult-to-control asthma."
- **First question:** "Can you show me how you use your inhaler right now?"
- **Data to pull:** pharmacy refill/adherence data; direct technique observation; laryngoscopy referral if VCD is suspected.

## Positive skin test or sIgE to a food the patient has never reported reacting to

- **Usually means:** polysensitization or cross-reactivity (pollen-food syndrome) rather than a clinical allergy requiring avoidance.
- **First question:** "Has this food ever been eaten, and what happened?"
- **Data to pull:** component-resolved diagnostics if available; consider a supervised food challenge before recommending avoidance.

## Large local reaction (>10 cm) to an immunotherapy injection

- **Usually means:** the current dose has outpaced this patient's local tolerance at this point in build-up — it predicts future large local reactions, not a systemic one.
- **First question:** "How large was the reaction and how long did it last?"
- **Data to pull:** injection log (dose, vial, site); compare against the build-up schedule to see if escalation was too fast.

## Persistent eosinophil count over 1500/uL

- **Usually means:** hypereosinophilic syndrome or another eosinophilic organ disease (EoE, EGPA) needs ruling out, not just "allergic tendency."
- **First question:** "Are there any signs of organ involvement — cardiac, pulmonary, GI symptoms?"
- **Data to pull:** repeat CBC with differential over time, troponin/echocardiogram if cardiac symptoms present, GI referral if esophageal symptoms.

## Recurrent angioedema without urticaria, unresponsive to antihistamines

- **Usually means:** hereditary angioedema (C1 esterase inhibitor deficiency) or ACE-inhibitor-induced angioedema, not classic mast-cell-mediated allergic angioedema.
- **First question:** "Is the patient on an ACE inhibitor, and is there a family history of similar swelling episodes?"
- **Data to pull:** C4 level (screening test), C1 esterase inhibitor level and function, medication list.

## Oral steroid bursts twice or more in a year for asthma

- **Usually means:** the patient has moved into severe/uncontrolled asthma territory warranting biologic evaluation, not further ICS-LABA dose escalation alone.
- **First question:** "What triggered each burst, and what step of therapy was the patient on at the time?"
- **Data to pull:** exacerbation history with dates and triggers, blood eosinophil count and total IgE (biologic-eligibility workup), FeNO trend.
