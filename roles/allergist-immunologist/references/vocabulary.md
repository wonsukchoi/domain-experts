# Vocabulary

Terms generalists reliably misuse. Format per term: definition, the sentence a practitioner would actually say, the common misuse.

## Sensitization vs. allergy

- **Definition:** sensitization is the presence of allergen-specific IgE (detectable by skin test or serum assay); allergy is a reproducible clinical reaction on exposure. Sensitization is necessary but not sufficient for a diagnosis.
- **Practitioner usage:** "She's sensitized to peanut on the panel, but she eats peanut butter twice a week with no symptoms — that's sensitization, not allergy, and it doesn't change her diet."
- **Common misuse:** treating any positive skin test or sIgE result as a diagnosis, driving unnecessary avoidance of foods or drugs the patient actually tolerates.

## Specific IgE (sIgE) vs. total IgE

- **Definition:** specific IgE quantifies antibody against one named allergen; total IgE is the sum across everything, elevated by atopy in general, parasitic infection, or certain immune disorders.
- **Practitioner usage:** "Total IgE of 800 tells me she's atopic overall; it doesn't tell me which of her five positive sIgE results is the one causing her symptoms."
- **Common misuse:** ordering "an IgE level" as if it answers "is she allergic to X" — total IgE cannot identify a specific trigger.

## Skin prick test vs. intradermal test

- **Definition:** skin prick introduces allergen through the epidermis (fast, safer, standard first-line); intradermal injects allergen into the dermis (more sensitive, higher false-positive and systemic-reaction risk, reserved for specific indications like drug or venom allergy workup after a negative prick test).
- **Practitioner usage:** "Prick test was negative for penicillin — moving to intradermal at the minor determinant concentration before we call it clear."
- **Common misuse:** using intradermal testing as a routine first-line screen; it trades a modest sensitivity gain for a real increase in systemic reaction risk and false positives.

## Cross-reactivity vs. co-allergy

- **Definition:** cross-reactivity is one IgE antibody binding similar protein structures across different sources (birch pollen IgE binding apple protein); co-allergy is separate, independent sensitization to each allergen.
- **Practitioner usage:** "His oral itching with raw apple and pear is pollen-food cross-reactivity from his birch sensitization — he doesn't need to avoid cooked apple, and it's not a second, independent food allergy."
- **Common misuse:** assuming reaction to a related food means a second true allergy requiring the same strict avoidance as the primary trigger, when it's usually a milder cross-reactive syndrome limited to raw forms.

## Anaphylaxis (Sampson/NIAID criteria)

- **Definition:** a specific clinical definition — acute onset with skin/mucosal involvement plus respiratory or cardiovascular compromise, OR two or more organ systems involved after likely exposure, OR isolated hypotension after known-allergen exposure — not a synonym for "a bad allergic reaction."
- **Practitioner usage:** "Hives alone, even widespread, isn't anaphylaxis by criteria — but hives plus throat tightness meets it, and gets epinephrine, not just an antihistamine."
- **Common misuse:** reserving the anaphylaxis label (and epinephrine) for shock or clear airway compromise, missing the two-organ-system presentation that also qualifies and needs the same first-line treatment.

## Biphasic reaction

- **Definition:** recurrence of anaphylaxis symptoms after apparent resolution and without any re-exposure to the trigger, typically within 1-72 hours.
- **Practitioner usage:** "He's biphasic-risk from his prior episode, so we're keeping him overnight even though he looks fine right now."
- **Common misuse:** treating a symptom-free patient an hour post-epinephrine as safe to discharge, ignoring that the biphasic window extends well past initial resolution.

## Desensitization vs. tolerance (immunotherapy / OIT)

- **Definition:** desensitization is a temporary elevated reaction threshold that requires continued regular exposure to maintain; sustained unresponsiveness (sometimes loosely called "tolerance") persists even after a period off the allergen — a much higher, less commonly achieved bar.
- **Practitioner usage:** "Oral immunotherapy got him desensitized to two peanuts a day, but we don't know yet if that's sustained unresponsiveness — he still needs to eat peanut regularly, this isn't a cure."
- **Common misuse:** telling families OIT "cures" the allergy; most patients achieve desensitization, which lapses if dosing stops, not durable tolerance.

## Atopy

- **Definition:** a genetic predisposition to produce IgE antibodies against common environmental allergens, clinically expressed as some combination of eczema, allergic rhinitis, asthma, and food allergy (the "atopic march").
- **Practitioner usage:** "Given his atopic history — eczema as an infant, now rhinitis and asthma — a new wheeze is more likely allergic than infectious."
- **Common misuse:** using "atopic" as a synonym for "allergic to something specific," when it describes a predisposition and pattern, not a single diagnosis.

## Component-resolved diagnostics

- **Definition:** testing IgE against individual allergen proteins (e.g., Ara h2 for peanut, Bet v1 for birch) rather than the whole crude extract, distinguishing storage proteins that predict severe reactions from cross-reactive proteins that usually don't.
- **Practitioner usage:** "Whole-peanut sIgE is positive but Ara h2 is negative and Bet v1 is strongly positive — this looks like birch cross-reactivity, not a primary peanut allergy."
- **Common misuse:** relying on whole-allergen extract testing alone in a patient with a confusing or pollen-associated history, missing the distinction that changes the actual risk.

## Idiopathic vs. chronic spontaneous urticaria (CSU)

- **Definition:** CSU is the current preferred term for hives lasting more than 6 weeks with no identifiable external trigger found on standard workup — "idiopathic" is the older, less precise synonym, and neither implies extensive allergy testing will find a cause.
- **Practitioner usage:** "This is CSU — we're not going to keep testing for exotic allergens, we're going straight to antihistamine updosing per the algorithm."
- **Common misuse:** ordering escalating allergy panels for CSU on the assumption an external allergen must be found, when the pathophysiology in most cases is autoimmune/autoreactive, not classic IgE-mediated allergy.

## Drug allergy vs. drug intolerance/side effect

- **Definition:** drug allergy is an immune-mediated reaction (IgE, T-cell, or immune-complex); intolerance or side effect is a predictable, non-immune reaction (GI upset from antibiotics, for example) that doesn't require a delabeling workup or avoidance of the whole class.
- **Practitioner usage:** "Nausea with amoxicillin is a side effect, not an allergy — it doesn't belong on the allergy list and doesn't need PEN-FAST scoring."
- **Common misuse:** charting any unpleasant drug reaction as an "allergy," inflating the allergy list with non-immune events that then wrongly restrict future prescribing.

## Local reaction vs. systemic reaction (immunotherapy)

- **Definition:** a local reaction is swelling/redness confined to the injection site, however large; a systemic reaction involves symptoms away from the injection site (hives elsewhere, wheeze, throat tightness, hypotension) — graded separately by the WAO scale.
- **Practitioner usage:** "That's a 12 cm local reaction — uncomfortable, but it's not systemic, so we're adjusting the dose, not stopping immunotherapy."
- **Common misuse:** treating any large local reaction as a warning sign of impending anaphylaxis; large local reactions predict future large local reactions, not systemic ones.

## Oral food challenge (OFC) vs. elimination diet

- **Definition:** OFC is a supervised, graded ingestion of the suspected food under medical observation to confirm or refute allergy; an elimination diet removes a food based on suspicion, without ever testing whether reintroduction causes a reaction.
- **Practitioner usage:** "We've had her off dairy for six months on suspicion alone — it's time for a supervised challenge, not another six months of elimination without an answer."
- **Common misuse:** treating symptom improvement on an elimination diet as proof of the allergy, when placebo effect, natural symptom fluctuation, or coincidental timing can produce the same apparent improvement without a challenge to confirm causation.

## Primary immunodeficiency (PID) vs. secondary immunodeficiency

- **Definition:** PID is an intrinsic genetic defect in immune function; secondary immunodeficiency is caused by an external factor — medication (steroids, chemotherapy, rituximab), malnutrition, malignancy, or infection (HIV) — suppressing an otherwise normal immune system.
- **Practitioner usage:** "Before we work this up as a primary immunodeficiency, rule out secondary causes — check his medication list and an HIV test first, they're far more common."
- **Common misuse:** jumping straight to genetic PID testing in an adult with recurrent infections without first excluding the much more common secondary causes.
