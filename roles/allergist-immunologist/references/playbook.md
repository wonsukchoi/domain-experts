# Diagnostic & Management Playbook

Filled pathways with real thresholds. Defaults, not laws — deviate consciously and document why.

## 1. Drug allergy delabeling — PEN-FAST scoring

Score the reported reaction; total determines the pathway. Points:

| Criterion | Points if present |
|---|---|
| Reaction occurred **5 years or less** ago | 2 |
| **A**naphylaxis or angioedema | 2 |
| **S**evere cutaneous adverse reaction (SJS/TEN, DRESS, AGEP) | 1 |
| **T**reatment required (ED visit, epinephrine, hospitalization) | 1 |

**Pathway by score:**

| Score | Risk band | Action |
|---|---|---|
| 0-1 | Low (well under 5%) | Direct observed oral challenge, no preceding skin test |
| 2-3 | Moderate | Skin test first (prick then intradermal to major/minor determinants); challenge if negative |
| 4+ | High | Full allergy referral before any re-exposure; skin test, possibly graded challenge under closer monitoring |
| Any score + history of SJS/TEN/DRESS/AGEP | Contraindicated | No re-exposure, no challenge — permanent avoidance of the culprit drug class |

**Direct challenge dosing (low-risk band):** 1/20 of a standard dose PO → observe 20 min for objective signs (urticaria, wheeze, hypotension) → full standard dose PO → observe 60 min. Any objective sign at either step stops the challenge and the label stands.

## 2. Food-specific IgE — sensitization vs. likely allergy

Approximate 95%-positive-predictive-value cutoffs (children over age 2; younger children run lower thresholds), from Sampson's cohort data. Below the cutoff, treat as sensitization requiring history correlation or a challenge; at or above, treat as strongly suggestive pending confirmation in an ambiguous history:

| Allergen | ~95% PPV cutoff (kU/L) |
|---|---|
| Egg white | ~7 |
| Cow's milk | ~15 |
| Peanut | ~14 |
| Fish | ~20 |
| Tree nut (varies by nut) | ~15 |
| Soy, wheat | No reliable single cutoff — history and challenge carry more weight |

**Decision rule:** history + level above cutoff → counsel avoidance without challenge. History absent or level below cutoff and food is nutritionally important or frequently cross-contaminating → supervised oral food challenge before committing to long-term avoidance. Component-resolved testing (e.g., Ara h2 for peanut) helps separate true primary sensitization from pollen-food cross-reactivity when the raw allergen-extract sIgE is positive but the history doesn't fit.

## 3. Anaphylaxis management and observation

**Recognition (any one criterion, per NIAID/FAAN working definition):**
1. Acute onset (minutes to hours) with skin/mucosal involvement plus respiratory compromise or hypotension.
2. Two or more organ systems involved after likely allergen exposure (skin, respiratory, GI, cardiovascular).
3. Hypotension after exposure to a known allergen for that patient, even alone.

**Treatment sequence — no substitutions, no reordering:**
1. Intramuscular epinephrine, mid-outer thigh, immediately. Weight-based: 0.15 mg for roughly 15-30 kg, 0.3 mg for over 30 kg (adult autoinjector), repeat every 5-15 minutes if no improvement.
2. Position patient supine (or seated if respiratory distress dominates), high-flow oxygen, IV fluids for hypotension.
3. Only after epinephrine: H1 antihistamine for cutaneous symptoms, corticosteroid for delayed-phase inflammation. Neither substitutes for a second epinephrine dose if symptoms persist.
4. Glucagon if the patient is on a beta-blocker and not responding to epinephrine (beta-blockade can blunt epinephrine's effect).

**Observation length after resolution:**

| Presentation | Minimum observation |
|---|---|
| Single dose of epinephrine, full resolution, no risk factors | 4-6 hours |
| More than one epinephrine dose required | Overnight/admit |
| Hypotension or respiratory compromise at any point | Overnight/admit |
| Prior history of a biphasic reaction | Overnight/admit |
| Poorly controlled asthma as a comorbidity | Extend observation, lower threshold to admit |

Biphasic recurrence (symptoms returning after apparent resolution, without re-exposure) occurs across published series roughly in the range of 1 in 10 to 1 in 5 cases — common enough that a fixed short discharge window for every patient is a guess, not a protocol.

## 4. Asthma step therapy — control-driven adjustment

Assess control at every visit (validated tool — ACT/C-ACT or symptom-frequency criteria), not by asking "how are you doing." Controlled = daytime symptoms ≤2 days/week, no nighttime awakening, reliever use ≤2 days/week, no activity limitation, no exacerbations.

| Step | Typical therapy |
|---|---|
| 1 | As-needed low-dose ICS-formoterol (preferred over SABA-only per current GINA guidance) |
| 2 | Daily low-dose ICS, or as-needed ICS-formoterol |
| 3 | Low-dose ICS-LABA daily |
| 4 | Medium/high-dose ICS-LABA |
| 5 | High-dose ICS-LABA + add-on (LAMA, biologic, or oral steroid as last resort) |

**Step-down rule:** controlled for ≥3 months on current step → step down one tier, unless an exacerbation occurred in that window or the patient is entering a known high-risk season. **Step-up rule:** any exacerbation requiring oral steroids, or control criteria not met for ≥1 month on current step → step up one tier and re-check inhaler technique and adherence first — technique failure is a more common cause of "step failure" than true undertreatment.

**Oral steroid bursts ≥2/year on any step** → refer for biologic evaluation (anti-IgE, anti-IL-5, anti-IL-4/13) rather than continuing to escalate ICS-LABA dose indefinitely.

## 5. Environmental allergen immunotherapy — build-up and maintenance

**SCIT build-up (weekly, illustrative concentrate schedule):** start at a dilute concentration (e.g., 1:1,000,000 or per manufacturer protocol), increase volume/concentration weekly over roughly 4-6 months to reach maintenance dose, then maintenance injections every 4 weeks for 3-5 years total.

**WAO systemic reaction grading (react by grade, not by gut feel):**

| Grade | Presentation | Action |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Localized skin (generalized pruritus, urticaria) | Antihistamine; continue same dose next visit |
| 2 | Mild systemic (rhinitis, mild asthma symptoms) | Antihistamine +/- bronchodilator; reduce next dose |
| 3 | Moderate systemic (wheeze, angioedema, mild hypotension) | Treat per anaphylaxis protocol as needed; hold escalation, reduce dose significantly |
| 4-5 | Severe systemic / respiratory-cardiovascular failure | Full anaphylaxis protocol; discontinue immunotherapy or restart at markedly reduced dose only after allergist re-evaluation |

**Large local reaction (induration >10 cm at injection site) is not a systemic reaction** — it predicts future large local reactions, not systemic ones, and usually means the current dose has outpaced this patient's local tolerance; the fix is dose adjustment, not discontinuation.

## 6. Primary immunodeficiency workup gate

A "no" at any step redirects the workup, doesn't stop it:

1. **Pattern check** — 2+ sinopulmonary infections/year, or infections with unusual organisms, or failure to thrive: proceed.
2. **Quantify immunoglobulins (IgG, IgA, IgM)** — draw when the patient is not on IgG replacement and not acutely ill; either invalidates the result.
3. **Functional antibody response** — pre/post pneumococcal polysaccharide vaccine titers; a normal total IgG with a poor vaccine response still supports a specific-antibody-deficiency diagnosis.
4. **Cellular workup if humoral is normal but pattern persists** — lymphocyte subsets, T-cell function, complement (CH50) for the alternate branches of the differential.
5. **IgG replacement dosing once CVID/agammaglobulinemia confirmed** — typically 400-600 mg/kg/month IV or weekly-equivalent SC dosing, titrated to trough IgG level and, more importantly, to infection frequency — a "normal" trough with ongoing infections still needs a dose increase.
