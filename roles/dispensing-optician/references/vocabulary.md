# Dispensing Optician — Vocabulary

### Vertex distance
The distance from the back surface of the lens to the front of the cornea, measured in millimeters.
**In use:** "This is a -9.00 sphere, so I need her vertex distance recorded before we cut anything — a couple millimeters off and the effective power at the eye won't match what the OD refracted."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a fixed constant (assumed always ~12mm) rather than measured, which is fine below about ±4.00D and wrong at high power.

### Pantoscopic tilt
The angle of the lens plane relative to vertical when the frame is worn, typically 8-12° tilted so the bottom of the lens sits slightly closer to the face than the top.
**In use:** "The frame's sitting almost dead vertical on her — I need to add some pantoscopic tilt or the reading portion of that progressive won't land where it's supposed to."
**Common misuse:** Confused with frame "wrap" (horizontal curvature), which is a different axis of adjustment with different optical consequences.

### Prentice's Rule
The formula Δ = c × F, where Δ is induced prism in prism diopters, c is the distance in centimeters from the optical center to the point of regard, and F is the lens power in diopters.
**In use:** "At 8 millimeters off the OC on a -4.50, that's 3.6 prism diopters induced right at her reading position — that's the imbalance, not adaptation."
**Common misuse:** Applied only to the lens with the highest power and ignored for the fellow eye, missing that it's the *differential* between the two eyes that produces symptoms.

### Slab-off (reverse slab)
A prism-grinding technique that adds base-up prism to the bottom (usually near) portion of the higher-minus or lower-plus lens in an anisometropic pair, to compensate for vertical imbalance at the reading position.
**In use:** "Order this with a 1.75 slab-off on the OS to bring the vertical differential under a diopter at near."
**Common misuse:** Ordered as a blanket fix for any anisometropia rather than calculated from the actual induced-prism differential at the specific reading point.

### Seg height (fitting height)
The vertical distance from the lowest point of the finished lens shape (in the chosen frame) to the position of the segment line (bifocal) or the fitting cross (progressive).
**In use:** "Her seg height comes in under this design's stated minimum for a +2.50 add — we either need a taller frame or a shorter-corridor design."
**Common misuse:** Measured against the frame's geometric center or the frame board's printed markings instead of the patient's actual pupil position with the frame worn.

### Minimum fitting height
The smallest seg height a specific progressive lens design can be ground at and still deliver its intended near/intermediate zones, published per design and often varying by add power.
**In use:** "This design needs at least 18 at a plus-two-fifty add — her frame only gives us 16, so we're either going to a different frame or a short-corridor design."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to be a single universal number across all progressive designs rather than design- and add-power-specific.

### Base curve
The curvature of the front surface of a lens, expressed in diopters, that partly determines the lens's overall form and how it matches a given frame's curvature (wrap).
**In use:** "That lens is ground on a flatter base curve than the frame's wrap calls for — she's going to get some peripheral distortion even though the power verifies fine centrally."
**Common misuse:** Treated as irrelevant once the power checks out on the lensmeter, when base curve mismatch with frame wrap is a distinct source of peripheral distortion.

### Monocular PD
Pupillary distance measured separately for each eye from the center of the bridge of the nose, rather than a single combined binocular number.
**In use:** "Her binocular PD is 64, but she's a 33/31 monocular split — if I center both lenses at 32, the left eye's optical center is off by a millimeter."
**Common misuse:** Assumed symmetric (simply halving the binocular PD) without measuring each eye, which misses natural facial asymmetry.

### Effective power
The actual optical power a lens delivers at the eye once vertex distance and tilt are accounted for, as opposed to the nominal power ground into the lens.
**In use:** "The nominal power hasn't changed, but moving this frame 4 millimeters closer to her face changed the effective power enough that she's noticing it."
**Common misuse:** Conflated with the lensmeter-verified nominal power, ignoring that the two only match at the vertex distance the Rx assumed.

### ANSI Z80.1 tolerance
The American National Standards Institute specification defining acceptable manufacturing deviation for prescription ophthalmic lens power, cylinder axis, and prism.
**In use:** "Both lenses read within Z80.1 tolerance individually, but paired together the vertical difference is enough to symptomatic her — tolerance-compliant isn't the same as comfortable."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a comfort or symptom threshold rather than a manufacturing-deviation spec — a pair can be fully in tolerance and still cause symptoms when errors compound between the two lenses.

### ANSI Z87.1
The American National Standards Institute specification for occupational and educational personal eye and face protection, governing impact-resistance requirements for safety eyewear.
**In use:** "If this is for the machine shop, we need Z87-rated polycarbonate in a frame that's actually tested to the standard, not just any polycarbonate lens in a regular frame."
**Common misuse:** Assumed satisfied by simply using polycarbonate lenses, when the standard also covers frame construction and testing, not lens material alone.

### FTC Eyeglass Rule
The Federal Trade Commission regulation (16 CFR Part 456) requiring that patients receive a copy of their prescription at no extra charge, independent of whether they purchase eyewear from that provider.
**In use:** "She's asking to take her Rx elsewhere to compare pricing — hand her the copy, that's her right under the Eyeglass Rule regardless of where she buys."
**Common misuse:** Treated as optional or as something the practice can condition on a completed sale, which the rule explicitly prohibits.

### Index of refraction
A lens material property describing how strongly it bends light; higher-index materials achieve a given optical power with a thinner, flatter lens profile.
**In use:** "At minus-six-fifty we should be talking 1.67 index minimum, or she's wearing the edge of a hockey puck."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to improve optical quality or clarity — higher index mainly reduces thickness/weight and can slightly increase chromatic aberration, it does not sharpen vision.
