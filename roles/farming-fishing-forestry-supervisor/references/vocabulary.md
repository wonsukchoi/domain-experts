# Vocabulary

Terms generalists conflate that a farm/crew supervisor keeps sharply separate — the value is in the misuse column, not the definition.

### AEWR vs. prevailing wage vs. minimum wage

**AEWR** (Adverse Effect Wage Rate) is the DOL-published, state-specific hourly rate H-2A employers must pay so guest-worker hiring doesn't depress wages for domestic workers in similar jobs; it's set annually from the USDA Farm Labor Survey. **Prevailing wage** is a separately determined rate for a specific job/area that can exceed the AEWR. **Minimum wage** is the state or federal wage floor that applies to all workers regardless of H-2A status. The employer must pay the highest of the three that applies to a given job.

*In use:* "AEWR for this classification is $19.97, but the prevailing wage survey for hand-harvest crews in this county came back at $20.50 — we pay $20.50."

*Common misuse:* treating AEWR as "the H-2A minimum wage" and stopping there, without checking whether a higher prevailing wage or CBA rate applies to the specific job classification.

### REI vs. PHI

**REI** (Restricted Entry Interval) is the labeled time workers must stay out of a treated field after a pesticide application, set under the EPA Worker Protection Standard. **PHI** (Pre-Harvest Interval) is a separate labeled interval governing how long before *harvest* a pesticide can legally be applied, tied to food-residue tolerances, not worker safety. A product can have a short REI and a long PHI, or the reverse — they answer different questions.

*In use:* "REI cleared at 24 hours, crew can enter tomorrow morning — but PHI on this product is 14 days, so we can't harvest until the 19th regardless of when we send workers back in."

*Common misuse:* assuming REI clearance also clears the crop for harvest, or checking only PHI when planning crew re-entry.

### Piece rate vs. make-up pay

**Piece rate** is compensation tied to units of output (per lug, per bin, per board-foot). **Make-up pay** is the required supplemental payment when a worker's piece-rate earnings for the period, divided by hours worked, fall below the applicable minimum wage — it isn't a bonus or discretionary adjustment, it's a legal floor.

*In use:* "Pace ran under floor for three days straight — that's $26.40 a day in make-up pay per worker, owed this pay period, not netted against next week's better pace."

*Common misuse:* averaging a slow day against a fast day across the pay period and assuming it nets out; the floor test applies each pay period per applicable wage-and-hour rules, and a good day doesn't retroactively excuse a bad one under most state rules.

### Application Exclusion Zone (AEZ) vs. REI

**AEZ** is the area immediately surrounding pesticide application equipment (up to 100 feet for some methods) that must be cleared of workers *during* the application itself. **REI** governs re-entry *after* application is complete. AEZ is a during-application safety perimeter; REI is a post-application waiting period.

*In use:* "Pull the crew back 100 feet for the AEZ while the rig is running — once it's done, the REI clock starts and that's a separate 24-hour hold."

*Common misuse:* treating AEZ compliance as sufficient WPS compliance and skipping the REI check once the applicator has left the field.

### Field sanitation unit vs. general industry sanitation

**Field sanitation** under OSHA's agricultural standard (29 CFR 1928.110) requires toilet, handwashing, and potable drinking water access within roughly a quarter mile or five-minute walk for hand-labor crews of 11 or more working 3+ hours. **General industry sanitation** (29 CFR 1910.141) covers fixed-facility ratios (toilets per employee count) that don't directly apply to open-field agricultural work but are commonly borrowed as a planning ratio (roughly 1 unit per 20 workers).

*In use:* "We're borrowing the 1-per-20 ratio from general industry for planning, but the actual trigger out here is 1928.110 — crew size and distance, not a fixed facility count."

*Common misuse:* citing a specific toilet-per-worker ratio as if it's written into the field sanitation standard itself, when 1928.110's actual trigger is proximity and crew size/duration, not a numeric ratio.

### Farm Labor Contractor (FLC) vs. direct-hire crew

An **FLC** is a third party registered under MSPA to recruit, transport, and/or supervise agricultural workers for a fee, with its own disclosure and registration obligations. A **direct-hire crew** is employed directly by the farm operation, which carries full wage-and-hour and safety liability without an intermediary.

*In use:* "Confirm the contractor's FLC registration is current before the crew shows up Monday — if it's expired, we're the ones holding the wage-and-hour liability, not them."

*Common misuse:* assuming using a labor contractor shifts all liability away from the farm operation; MSPA and FLSA liability frequently follows to the farm as a joint employer regardless of the contractor relationship.

### Day-haul vs. H-2A vs. domestic seasonal

**Day-haul** workers are recruited and transported daily (often through an FLC) for short, unplanned labor needs, with minimal lead time and no guarantee obligation. **H-2A** workers are guest workers under a certified labor contract with AEWR, housing, transport, and 3/4-guarantee obligations. **Domestic seasonal** workers are locally hired for the season under standard state wage-and-hour rules, no guarantee or housing requirement.

*In use:* "This is a three-day gap, not a season-long need — day-haul through the contractor, not an H-2A request that takes six weeks to clear."

*Common misuse:* defaulting to whichever labor source was used last time without pricing the landed cost (wage + housing + transport + guarantee) against the actual duration and lead time of the current need.

### Brix vs. "ripe"

**Brix** is a measured sugar-content percentage used as an objective harvest-readiness threshold for many fruit crops, read with a refractometer. "Ripe" as used by a generalist is a subjective visual/textural judgment that doesn't reconcile against a target number or a weather-window decision.

*In use:* "We're at 17.8 Brix against a 17.5 target — that's the number driving the push decision, not how the berries look from the row end."

*Common misuse:* making a harvest-timing call on visual appearance alone when the crop has an established Brix (or equivalent) target that should anchor the go/no-go threshold.

### Regular rate vs. piece-rate earnings

**Piece-rate earnings** are the straight-time compensation for units produced. **Regular rate** is that total divided by total hours worked in the period, used as the base for computing the required overtime premium (regular rate × 0.5 × OT hours) — it is not the piece rate itself, and it changes every day pace changes.

*In use:* "Regular rate today is $16.80, not the $1.40 piece rate — that's what the OT premium calculation runs off, and it moves every time the average pace moves."

*Common misuse:* computing overtime pay as 1.5× the piece rate per unit instead of 1.5× the regular rate per hour (equivalently, 0.5× regular rate as a premium added to full piece earnings) — the two methods aren't interchangeable and the wrong one under- or over-pays.

### MSPA vs. FLSA agricultural exemption

**MSPA** (Migrant and Seasonal Agricultural Worker Protection Act) governs farm labor contractor registration, worker disclosures, and transportation/housing safety specifically for migrant and seasonal agricultural workers. **FLSA's agricultural exemptions** (29 CFR 780) carve out partial relief from federal overtime rules for some agricultural work, but do not exempt piece-rate work from the federal minimum-wage floor. The two statutes overlap but answer different compliance questions.

*In use:* "FLSA's ag overtime exemption may apply federally, but that doesn't touch the minimum-wage floor test, and it doesn't override the state's own ag-overtime rule if the state rule is stricter."

*Common misuse:* citing "agricultural exemption" as a blanket excuse to skip overtime or minimum-wage compliance, when the exemption is narrow (federal overtime only, in some circumstances) and state law is frequently stricter than the federal floor.

### Corresponding employment vs. job order

**Corresponding employment** is the H-2A term for non-H-2A workers doing substantially the same work as the certified H-2A workers, who are entitled to the same wage and working-condition protections. The **job order** is the specific certified description of duties, location, and rate filed with the state workforce agency for that H-2A petition.

*In use:* "The domestic pickers on this crew are corresponding employment — they get the AEWR rate too, because they're doing the same job the job order describes, not because they're on H-2A paper."

*Common misuse:* assuming only the named H-2A workers on the petition are covered by the AEWR and related protections, when any worker in "corresponding employment" on the same job is covered as well.

### Go/no-go call vs. routine scheduling

A **go/no-go call** is a pre-thresholded decision (weather probability, ripeness measure, cost/value tradeoff) made explicitly and logged when conditions cross a set trigger. **Routine scheduling** is the default plan that continues absent such a trigger. Conflating the two means a schedule holds by inertia through a weather event that should have forced an active decision.

*In use:* "We hit the 60% rain-probability trigger this morning — that's a go/no-go call, not just another scheduling day, and it needs to be logged with who made the call."

*Common misuse:* letting a harvest plan continue unchanged through a threshold-crossing forecast because nobody explicitly flagged it as a decision point requiring a documented call.
