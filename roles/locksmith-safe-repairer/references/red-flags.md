# Red flags

Smell tests a licensed locksmith catches before agreeing to a job. Format for each: what it usually means, the first question to ask, and the data or document to pull before any tool touches the lock.

## 1. Requester wants to meet at the property but avoids showing ID or address proof

**Usually means:** either a legitimate but disorganized customer who left ID inside the locked property (common, recoverable with a secondary document), or someone who doesn't have standing to authorize entry at all.

**First question:** "Do you have any document with this address on it — a lease, mail, a utility bill — even a photo of one on your phone?"

**Data to pull:** government ID; any secondary document tying name to address; if neither is available, a property manager's or landlord's direct confirmation before proceeding.

## 2. Landlord or property manager wants a mass rekey right after an eviction, with no paperwork

**Usually means:** usually a legitimate turnover, occasionally a landlord acting before a legal eviction is actually finalized (which can expose the locksmith to liability for an illegal lockout).

**First question:** "Can you show me the court order or signed move-out agreement for this unit?"

**Data to pull:** eviction order or documented move-out agreement; management agreement establishing the requester's authority over the unit.

## 3. "Just need it open, don't worry about how" on a lockout call

**Usually means:** most often an impatient legitimate customer; occasionally someone testing whether the technician skips verification or method discipline under pressure.

**First question:** "I'll use the least damaging method that works — that's separate from confirming this is your property, which I still need to do first."

**Data to pull:** standard owner-verification form, completed before proceeding regardless of the requester's urgency.

## 4. Safe "reset" or drilling request for a safe with no prior service history at that business/address

**Usually means:** most often a legitimate new owner (estate sale, business purchase) who never got the combination; occasionally a safe that isn't the requester's to open.

**First question:** "What's your relationship to this business or property, and do you have a purchase record, prior service invoice, or corporate registration I can note on file?"

**Data to pull:** proof of ownership or officer status; the safe's model/serial plate, photographed before drilling.

## 5. Master key or "spare" key duplication request with no matching keying schedule record

**Usually means:** a legitimate lost-key replacement most of the time; occasionally someone requesting a key at a hierarchy level beyond what they're actually authorized to hold.

**First question:** "Whose master key is this system tied to, and can the property manager confirm you're authorized to hold a key at this level?"

**Data to pull:** the system's keying schedule; property manager or building owner confirmation for any request above change-key level.

## 6. Repeated lockout calls at the same address within a short window (e.g., 3+ calls in 30 days)

**Usually means:** most often a genuinely forgetful tenant or a failing mechanism (worn cylinder, misaligned strike); occasionally a sign of a dispute over access between multiple people claiming rights to the same unit.

**First question:** "Has anything changed with who else has a key to this door, or has the lock itself been sticking before this?"

**Data to pull:** service history for the address; whether the same or different individuals have called each time.

## 7. Cylinder wear complaint on hardware installed well within its rated cycle life

**Usually means:** the installed grade doesn't match actual traffic — a Grade 3 residential-spec cylinder installed on a heavy-traffic shared door, wearing out far faster than the ANSI cycle rating would suggest for typical use.

**First question:** "How many people are actually using this door daily, and does that match what this cylinder is rated for?"

**Data to pull:** the installed hardware's ANSI/BHMA grade; an honest daily-use estimate; compare against the 800k/400k/200k cycle tiers.

## 8. Post-break-in rekey or safe-repair request with no police report or claim number

**Usually means:** most often a customer prioritizing security over paperwork in the moment, which is understandable but leaves nothing for their insurer; occasionally staged damage for a fraudulent claim.

**First question:** "Do you have a police report number yet? I'll document the damage either way, but that record matters for your claim."

**Data to pull:** police report or case number if available; photographs of forced-entry damage before any repair begins, regardless of report status.

## 9. Access-control or alarm integration job where old credentials are asked to stay active "for now"

**Usually means:** a transition being done in a hurry, most often after a personnel change — but leaving old credentials live defeats the purpose of the job and is the exact gap a departed employee or contractor can walk back through.

**First question:** "Whose credentials specifically need to stay active, and who signs off on that exception in writing?"

**Data to pull:** current access-control credential list; written sign-off from a facility manager or owner for any credential kept active past the change.

## 10. Request to remove or bypass rated hardware on a fire door "for convenience"

**Usually means:** almost always a legitimate access annoyance (a fire door that locks awkwardly for daily staff traffic), but complying can violate fire code and voids the door's listed rating.

**First question:** "Is this door on the building's fire/life-safety plan, and have you checked with the AHJ (authority having jurisdiction) or fire marshal about what's allowed here instead of removing the rated hardware?"

**Data to pull:** the door's fire rating and listing; code-compliant alternatives (delayed-egress hardware, access control that doesn't defeat the rating) before agreeing to any modification.
