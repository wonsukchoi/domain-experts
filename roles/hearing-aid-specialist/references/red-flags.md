# Red Flags

Smell tests for case histories, fittings, and practice patterns. Format per flag: what it usually means, the first question to ask, the data to pull.

### Sudden or rapidly progressive hearing loss within the past 90 days

- **Usually means:** an FDA-listed referral condition (21 CFR 801.420) — possibly sudden sensorineural hearing loss, which is time-sensitive and may respond to prompt steroid treatment if caught early; second most likely, a resolving conductive issue (fluid, wax).
- **First question:** "When exactly did you first notice it, and has it changed since?"
- **Data to pull:** onset date relative to today (is it inside or outside the 90-day window), audiogram if one exists already, whether an ENT visit has occurred.

### Air-bone gap ≥ 15 dB at 500/1000/2000 Hz

- **Usually means:** a conductive component — otosclerosis, ossicular chain problem, middle-ear fluid — that may be medically or surgically treatable, sometimes fully, before amplification is even considered.
- **First question:** "Has this ear ever been evaluated by an ENT?"
- **Data to pull:** the full audiogram (air and bone thresholds at every frequency, not just the PTA average), tympanometry if available.

### Unilateral complaint or asymmetric loss without a symmetric explanation

- **Usually means:** most commonly a straightforward asymmetric presbycusis or noise-exposure pattern; less commonly (but seriously) a retrocochlear finding such as vestibular schwannoma.
- **First question:** "Is one ear worse, and has that always been true or did it change recently?"
- **Data to pull:** side-by-side audiogram comparison, word-recognition scores per ear (a word-recognition score much worse than the pure-tone loss would predict is itself a referral flag).

### Visible cerumen occlusion or foreign body on otoscopy

- **Usually means:** the "hearing loss" or "device doesn't work" complaint is actually a blocked canal, not a device or amplification problem.
- **First question:** "Has anyone looked in this ear with an otoscope in the last few months?"
- **Data to pull:** otoscopy photo/note; whether cerumen management is within this practitioner's state scope of practice or requires referral.

### No real-ear measurement on file for an active fitting

- **Usually means:** the fitting was closed out on the manufacturer's first-fit alone; industry survey data (Mueller & Picou, 2010) found routine probe-microphone verification used by well under half of dispensers, and unverified fittings correlate with higher return and complaint rates.
- **First question:** "What does the measured real-ear response show against the prescriptive target — not what does the fitting software say the target is?"
- **Data to pull:** REAR/REUR curves and the target curve from the verification system; if absent, that absence is the finding.

### Sound-quality complaint ("tinny," "sharp," "muffled," "boomy") within the trial period

- **Usually means:** a measurable frequency-response deviation from prescriptive target, most often from an unverified manual EQ tweak layered on the first-fit — not an adaptation-period issue.
- **First question:** "Has this fitting been re-verified with real-ear measurement since the complaint started?"
- **Data to pull:** current vs. target REAR curve at 250 Hz–4 kHz; compare to the curve on file from the original fitting to see what changed.

### Intermittent whistling/feedback reported by the patient

- **Usually means:** insufficient feedback margin (programmed gain too close to the device's maximum stable gain in the 2–4 kHz range), a poor-fitting shell/dome, or excessive venting for the loss configuration.
- **First question:** "Does it happen at rest, near objects (phone, hand near the ear), or during specific head positions?"
- **Data to pull:** measured feedback margin from the verification system; shell/dome fit check; vent size vs. loss severity.

### Patient reports own voice sounds hollow, loud, or like "talking in a barrel"

- **Usually means:** occlusion effect from insufficient venting relative to the degree of low-frequency loss, especially in patients with normal-to-near-normal low-frequency hearing fit with a closed dome or full shell.
- **First question:** "Does your own voice bother you more than external sounds do?"
- **Data to pull:** vent size on the current shell/dome versus the low-frequency thresholds; occlusion-effect check via REM with the device off vs. on.

### Return-for-credit or remake rate on one device model trending above the practice baseline

- **Usually means:** a specific model's default settings, dome/shell fit, or feature set is mismatched to the patient population being sold it — a business-pattern signal, not just individual bad fittings.
- **First question:** "Are the returns clustered by model, by fitter, or by neither?"
- **Data to pull:** return/remake log for the last 90 days, tagged by device model and by staff member.

### Bundled sale closed on the first visit with no trial-period terms disclosed in writing

- **Usually means:** the disclosure happened verbally or not at all, which becomes a dispute exactly when a patient later wants to return the device.
- **First question:** "Where in the paperwork does the patient see the trial length and restocking terms?"
- **Data to pull:** the signed intake/sales document; check for a trial-period clause with a specific day count, not "reasonable trial period."

### Premium device recommended to a patient whose stated environments don't use its headline feature

- **Usually means:** the recommendation followed the promotion or commission structure rather than the patient's actual listening situations.
- **First question:** "Which of this device's top three features did the patient describe needing, in their own words, before it was recommended?"
- **Data to pull:** the intake notes on listening environments versus the device's feature sheet.

### Referral-happy pattern — every routine presbycusis case sent to ENT with no red flag present

- **Usually means:** overcorrection after a past missed red flag, now costing patients time and copays for cases with no medical indication.
- **First question:** "Which specific item on the eight-condition screen triggered this referral?"
- **Data to pull:** referral log against the red-flag checklist — if referrals aren't traceable to a specific checklist item, the screen isn't being used as a gate, it's being used as a reflex.
