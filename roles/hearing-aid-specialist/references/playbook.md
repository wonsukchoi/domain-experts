# Fitting Playbook

Operational sequences with concrete numbers and thresholds. These are defaults, not laws — deviate consciously and document why.

## 1. Case-history and FDA red-flag screen (before otoscopy, before impression)

Run this exact checklist against 21 CFR 801.420 before proceeding. A "yes" on any item means: offer medical referral before fitting; if the patient is 18+ and declines, document the offer and have the patient sign the FDA-compliant waiver before continuing.

1. Visible congenital or traumatic deformity of the ear?
2. History of active drainage from the ear within the past 90 days?
3. History of sudden or rapidly progressive hearing loss within the past 90 days (either ear)?
4. Acute or chronic dizziness?
5. Unilateral hearing loss of sudden or recent onset within 90 days?
6. Audiometric air-bone gap ≥ 15 dB at 500, 1000, and 2000 Hz?
7. Visible evidence of significant cerumen accumulation or a foreign body in the ear canal?
8. Pain or discomfort in the ear?

If none apply and the patient is 18+ with a self-perceived mild-to-moderate loss, an OTC device is a legitimate option to discuss alongside professionally fitted devices — say so; it isn't a lost sale, it's the correct scope call for that presentation.

## 2. Device selection matrix

Match the device to the three most common listening environments named by the patient, then to dexterity and budget — not to the highest tier in stock.

| Patient profile | Default device class | Skip unless justified |
|---|---|---|
| Mostly quiet 1:1 conversation, TV, occasional restaurant | Mid-tier RIC, 2–3 channel noise management | Premium impulse/wind management (rarely encountered) |
| Frequent group meetings, restaurants, open-plan office | Mid-to-premium RIC/BTE with adaptive directionality | — |
| Significant dexterity limitation (arthritis, tremor) | Rechargeable, larger control surface, remote-control or app-only adjustment | Small battery-door CIC/IIC requiring fine motor control |
| Active outdoor/wind exposure (cycling, farming) | Premium wind-noise management tier | — |
| Budget-constrained, mild loss, comfortable with self-fitting | OTC self-fitting device, still offered a professional REM check as an add-on service | Bundled premium package sold on first visit |

Fallback order when the ideal tier is out of budget: (1) same manufacturer's prior-generation platform at the needed channel count, (2) mid-tier with the one non-negotiable feature (e.g., directionality) added à la carte, (3) itemized single-visit fitting with a plan to upgrade at next replacement cycle — never silently substitute a lesser device without disclosing the tradeoff.

## 3. Real-ear verification sequence

1. **Measure REUR (real-ear unaided response)** — baseline canal resonance before inserting the device.
2. **Enter first-fit** from manufacturer software using the audiogram and a validated prescriptive formula (NAL-NL2 for adults, DSL v5.0 for pediatric/first-time fittings where audibility of soft speech is prioritized over loudness comfort).
3. **Measure REAR (real-ear aided response)** at minimum three input levels — soft (50–55 dB SPL), average (65 dB SPL), loud (80 dB SPL) — across 250 Hz to 6 kHz.
4. **Compare REAR to target at each frequency. Tolerance: ±5 dB from 250 Hz–4 kHz.** Any band outside tolerance gets reprogrammed in the fitting software (not just "wait and see") before the visit ends.
5. **Check MPO (maximum power output) against loudness-discomfort level**, not just against OSPL90 spec sheet — a device whose ceiling exceeds the patient's discomfort level by more than ~10 dB risks intermittent loud-sound rejection.
6. **Document all five measured curves plus target** in the chart — this is the record that proves the fitting was verified, not assumed, if a return or a complaint follows.

## 4. Complaint triage — "it doesn't sound right"

Cheapest, most common causes first; most complaints resolve at step 1 or 2.

1. **Re-verify with REM before touching anything else.** A deviation from target explains the large majority of sound-quality complaints (see worked example in SKILL.md). Do not re-counsel "give it time" without a fresh measurement in hand.
2. **Check venting and occlusion effect** if the complaint is "my own voice sounds hollow/loud" or "boomy" — increase vent diameter or switch to a more open dome/shell before adjusting gain.
3. **Check for feedback margin** if the complaint is intermittent whistling — target at least 10 dB of headroom between the aid's maximum stable gain and the programmed gain at 2–4 kHz; a margin under that reliably produces feedback in some head positions even with feedback-cancellation active.
4. **Check acclimatization timeline only after 1–3 are ruled out.** Genuine adaptation (own-voice quality, new environmental sounds) typically resolves over 2–4 weeks; a complaint that hasn't moved after two follow-up visits and two re-verifications is a fitting problem, not a patient problem.
5. **Escalate to device fault** only after verification confirms the electroacoustic output matches what the fitting software is programmed to deliver — if measured output doesn't match the programmed target even after a fresh first-fit, that's a hardware/repair path, not a reprogram.

## 5. Pricing and trial-period disclosure

- Default to itemized pricing: device cost, fitting/verification fee, and follow-up-visit allotment (e.g., 3 visits included in the first 60 days) disclosed as separate line items.
- State the trial period length and restocking terms in writing at the point of sale — verbally disclosing it after a return request is a hindsight conversation, not informed consent.
- If bundling is required to match local competitive pricing, still itemize internally so a later repair-only or reprogram-only visit has a clear, defensible cost basis rather than feeling like a second bill for something "already paid for."
