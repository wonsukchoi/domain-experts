# Red flags

Smell tests an experienced economics instructor catches early. Format: what it usually means, the first question to ask, the data to pull.

## 1. Exam item with point-biserial discrimination below 0.15, or negative

**Usually means:** a miskeyed answer, an ambiguous stem, or two defensible correct answers — not that the material is unusually hard.

**First question:** "Which method does the syllabus and lecture specify for this calculation, and does the answer key match it?"

**Data to pull:** the item's p-value alongside discrimination, the answer key's derivation, and a recomputation of the problem using the method actually taught.

## 2. Item p-value below 0.30 across multiple sections of the same course

**Usually means:** ambiguity or a prerequisite gap shared across sections, not a coincidence of two weak cohorts.

**First question:** "Did every section see the same wording, and does the item assume a prerequisite (a specific method, a prior week's model) that wasn't covered before this exam?"

**Data to pull:** item wording as delivered in each section, position in the exam (fatigue effects late in a long exam), and the syllabus date the underlying concept was taught relative to the exam date.

## 3. TAs disagree by more than 10% of a question's point value on the same submission

**Usually means:** no shared rubric anchor set existed before grading opened, or the rubric language is ambiguous on a specific criterion.

**First question:** "Show me the rubric and the anchor answers we scored together before grading started."

**Data to pull:** a random 10% sample of TA-graded submissions for the same question, re-scored by the instructor against the rubric; the specific criterion where scores diverge.

## 4. Midterm or final class mean under 50%

**Usually means:** exam miscalibration (a bad item or a mismatch between what was taught and what was tested), not a uniformly underprepared cohort — cohorts rarely underperform uniformly across a well-calibrated 30-item exam.

**First question:** "What does the item analysis show — is the low mean concentrated in a few broken items or spread evenly?"

**Data to pull:** full item-level p-value and discrimination table; whether the lowest-scoring items cluster around one topic or method.

## 5. Multiple submissions on a problem set share a non-obvious, identical error

**Usually means:** a shared online solution source, more reliably than a shared correct final answer does — correct answers converge by chance on simple numeric problems, but identical wrong intermediate steps rarely do.

**First question:** "Does the exact problem wording, including numbers, appear on a solution-sharing site, and does the posted solution predate the assignment's due date?"

**Data to pull:** a direct search of the problem-set text; timestamps on any matching posted solution; the roster of students whose submissions share the error.

## 6. A homework-platform answer marked wrong on an elasticity, multiplier, or growth-rate problem

**Usually means:** a tolerance or sign-convention mismatch between the platform's auto-grader and the method taught in lecture, at least as often as a genuine student error.

**First question:** "Does the platform's expected answer use the midpoint method and the sign convention we taught, or the endpoint method?"

**Data to pull:** the platform's stored correct answer and tolerance setting for the item; a hand-recomputation using the taught method; how many students missed the same item.

## 7. DFW (D/F/withdraw) rate in Intermediate Micro or Macro spikes after a principles-sequencing change

**Usually means:** the new principles sequencing (e.g., an engagement-first redesign) under-delivered the calculus-ready toolkit Intermediate assumes, not that the incoming cohort is weaker.

**First question:** "What share of the Intermediate cohort came from principles sections taught under the old sequencing versus the new one, and does the DFW gap track that split?"

**Data to pull:** DFW rate by principles-section cohort; math-prerequisite completion rate for each cohort; item analysis on the first Intermediate midterm broken out by prior-sequencing cohort.

## 8. Participation or cold-call grading shows a persistent gap in section persistence by gender or URM status

**Usually means:** a participation-grading design that rewards unprompted volunteering may be filtering out students who understand the material but don't cold-call comfortably, not a difference in ability — a pattern documented in the economics-major "leaky pipeline" literature (CSWEP).

**First question:** "Is participation graded on unprompted volunteering only, or does it include structured formats (written reflections, small-group reporting) that don't require competing to speak first?"

**Data to pull:** principles-to-major continuation rate by gender/URM status compared to grade distribution by the same groups; participation-grading rubric as currently written.

## 9. A publisher test-bank item has been used unmodified for three or more consecutive terms

**Usually means:** rising odds the item (and its answer) is circulating on a solution-sharing site, inflating its p-value in a way that no longer reflects genuine understanding.

**First question:** "When did we last check this item's p-value trend across terms, and has it been climbing independent of any teaching change?"

**Data to pull:** the item's p-value by term for the last 3-5 terms; a search for the item's exact wording online.

## 10. An adjunct or lecturer is assigned 5+ sections in a term with no TA support

**Usually means:** assessment mix will drift toward whatever grades fastest (pure multiple-choice) rather than what tests the target reasoning, without anyone deciding that tradeoff deliberately.

**First question:** "Has the graded-assignment mix (free-response share vs. multiple-choice share) changed since the load increased, and was that a deliberate pedagogical call or a grading-capacity one?"

**Data to pull:** assignment-type mix by term for the instructor's sections; grading turnaround time; student office-hour attendance trend as a proxy for support capacity.
