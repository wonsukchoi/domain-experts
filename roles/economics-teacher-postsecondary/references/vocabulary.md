# Vocabulary

Terms students and generalists conflate that an economics instructor keeps sharply separate — the value is in the misuse each entry corrects.

## Marginal vs. average reasoning

**Marginal** is the effect of one additional unit (the next hour studied, the next unit produced). **Average** is the total divided by the count so far. A choice can raise the average while destroying value at the margin, or vice versa.

**In use:** "Total profit is still rising, but marginal profit on the last unit is negative — they're past the optimum even though the total-profit chart looks fine."

**Common misuse:** students verify "profit maximized" by checking that total profit rose from the previous unit, instead of checking whether the *next* unit's marginal revenue exceeds its marginal cost.

## Positive vs. normative economics

**Positive** statements are testable claims about how the economy works ("a price ceiling below equilibrium creates a shortage"). **Normative** statements are value judgments about what should happen ("rent should be capped for affordability"). Positive claims can be right or wrong; normative claims can't be settled by data alone.

**In use:** "That's a normative claim dressed as a positive one — 'the minimum wage should rise' isn't testable, but 'a $2 increase would reduce teen employment by X%' is."

**Common misuse:** treating a normative policy preference as if it followed automatically from a positive economic result, or dismissing a positive finding because it's inconvenient for a normative position.

## Ceteris paribus

Latin for "other things equal" — the condition under which a model's stated relationship (e.g., price up, quantity demanded down) holds, assuming every other variable is held fixed.

**In use:** "Demand for the good didn't actually fall when price rose — ceteris paribus was violated, income rose in the same period."

**Common misuse:** treating a model's prediction as falsified by real-world data without checking whether the "other things equal" condition was violated by a confounding change.

## Point elasticity vs. midpoint (arc) elasticity

**Point elasticity** uses one reference price/quantity as the base for percentage change (%ΔQ/%ΔP using the starting values). **Midpoint (arc) elasticity** uses the average of the two prices and two quantities as the base, giving a symmetric answer regardless of direction of the price change.

**In use:** "The two methods disagree here — midpoint gives elastic, endpoint gives unit elastic — and the syllabus specifies midpoint, so midpoint is the graded answer."

**Common misuse:** mixing the two methods within the same calculation, or assuming they always agree — they diverge more as the percentage change grows larger.

## Economic profit vs. accounting profit

**Accounting profit** is revenue minus explicit (cash) costs. **Economic profit** is revenue minus explicit costs *and* implicit costs (opportunity costs, like forgone salary or forgone interest on invested capital).

**In use:** "The bakery shows $40,000 accounting profit, but the owner gave up a $55,000 salary to run it — economic profit is negative $15,000."

**Common misuse:** computing "profit" using only cash costs and concluding a venture is worthwhile because accounting profit is positive, while economic profit is negative.

## Movement along vs. shift of a curve

A **movement along** a curve is a change in quantity caused by a change in that good's own price, holding the curve fixed. A **shift of** the curve is caused by a change in anything else (income, prices of other goods, expectations, input costs) and moves the whole curve.

**In use:** "That's not a demand shift, that's a movement along demand — the price of the good itself changed, nothing else did."

**Common misuse:** attributing every quantity change to "demand increased" regardless of whether the good's own price or some other variable moved.

## Deadweight loss

The loss in total surplus (consumer plus producer) from a market outcome that isn't allocatively efficient — created by taxes, price controls, or market power, measured as the surplus that would have existed at the efficient quantity but doesn't exist at any price at the distorted quantity.

**In use:** "The tax raises $2M in revenue but destroys $2.6M in surplus — the extra $600K is deadweight loss, transferred to no one."

**Common misuse:** treating any transfer (tax revenue, monopoly profit) as deadweight loss — deadweight loss is specifically the surplus that vanishes, not surplus that moves from one party to another.

## Item discrimination (point-biserial)

A psychometric statistic measuring how well a single exam item separates students who scored well overall from students who scored poorly overall — the correlation between getting that item right and total exam score (excluding the item itself).

**In use:** "Discrimination on Q14 came back negative — pull it before the grades post, that's not a difficulty problem, that's a miskey."

**Common misuse:** judging an item's quality purely by its p-value (how many got it right) without checking discrimination — a 50% p-value can come from a well-functioning item or a coin-flip guess item, and only discrimination tells the two apart.

## DFW rate

The share of enrolled students who receive a D, an F, or withdraw from the course — the standard institutional metric for course-level attrition and difficulty, tracked by department and curriculum committees over time.

**In use:** "DFW in Intermediate Macro jumped 9 points the term after we switched the principles sequencing — that's the signal to check the math-prerequisite alignment, not just 'a tougher cohort.'"

**Common misuse:** comparing DFW rates across courses with different prerequisite structures or grading norms as if the number alone measures course quality.

## Nominal vs. real variables

**Nominal** values are measured in current-dollar terms, unadjusted for price-level changes. **Real** values are adjusted for inflation to a base-year price level, isolating the actual change in quantity or purchasing power.

**In use:** "Nominal GDP grew 6%, but with 4% inflation that term, real GDP growth was closer to 2% — that's the number that reflects actual output."

**Common misuse:** citing nominal growth figures (wages, GDP, revenue) as if they represented real economic gains without checking the inflation adjustment.

## Comparative statics

The method of comparing two equilibrium states (before and after a single change in an exogenous variable) without describing the path or speed of adjustment between them — the standard tool for "what happens to price and quantity if X changes" questions.

**In use:** "Comparative statics tells us the new equilibrium price is higher — it doesn't tell us how many weeks it takes the market to get there, and that's a different, harder question."

**Common misuse:** treating a comparative-statics result as a prediction about the adjustment path or timing, which the method is not built to answer.
