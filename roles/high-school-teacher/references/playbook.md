# Playbook: Unit Design, Escalation, Reteach, and Grade Disputes

Filled process for the four recurring executions of the role. Reference context throughout: 10th-grade biology, five sections, ~28–29 students each.

## 1. Backward-Design Unit Plan (filled example)

Unit: Cellular Energy Transformations. Standard: HS-LS1-7 ("Use a model to illustrate that cellular respiration is a chemical process whereby the bonds of food molecules and oxygen molecules are broken and the bonds in new compounds are formed, releasing energy").

**Stage 1 — Desired results**

- Transfer goal: students can explain, for an unfamiliar organism or process, where energy is captured and where it's released across a metabolic pathway.
- Essential question: "Where does the energy actually go between a glucose molecule and a muscle contraction?"
- Sub-objectives (each becomes a formative item cluster, not a single quiz question): (a) label structures (mitochondria, chloroplast) to function, (b) sequence energy-carrier handoffs across glycolysis → Krebs → ETC, (c) contrast photosynthesis light-dependent/independent stages by energy form, (d) explain ATP as a reusable, not consumed-once, energy currency.

**Stage 2 — Assessment evidence** (designed before Stage 3 activities)

- Formative: 14-item tagged quiz after the respiration/photosynthesis sequence (items pre-mapped to sub-objectives a–d so results are sliceable per sub-objective, not just a total score).
- Summative: unit test with a model-drawing item requiring the transfer goal (an unfamiliar pathway, not the one taught) — this is what actually assesses "transfer," a total-recall test doesn't.

**Stage 3 — Learning plan** (six 50-minute periods)

| Day | Focus | Activity | Ties to |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Structures | Mitochondria/chloroplast structure-function card sort | sub-obj (a) |
| 2 | Glycolysis → Krebs | Direct instruction (12 min) + card-sequencing kinesthetic activity | sub-obj (b) |
| 3 | Electron transport chain | Direct instruction (12 min) + diagram-building | sub-obj (b) |
| 4 | Photosynthesis stages | Direct instruction (12 min) + light/dark stage sort | sub-obj (c) |
| 5 | ATP as currency, formative quiz | 14-item tagged quiz, last 15 min of period | (a)–(d), diagnostic |
| 6 | Reteach or extension, contingent on item analysis | See Section 3 below | contingent |

Day 6 is deliberately left contingent rather than pre-planned as a review game — the plan is designed to be overwritten by the data, not to survive it unchanged.

## 2. Classroom Management Escalation Ladder

Least-to-most-restrictive, in order. Skip a rung only for a safety issue; otherwise work the ladder in sequence and don't jump to a public consequence on a first offense.

| Rung | Response | When to move to next rung |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Proximity — move near the student while continuing instruction, no verbal acknowledgment | If off-task behavior continues past ~30 seconds of proximity |
| 2 | Non-verbal cue (eye contact, a tap on the desk, a pre-agreed signal) | If behavior continues past the cue |
| 3 | Quiet, private verbal redirect at the student's desk, naming the specific expected behavior ("I need eyes on the board") — never from across the room | If redirect is ignored or repeated within the same period |
| 4 | Brief private conversation just outside the door or at a quiet moment (not during instruction, not in front of peers): name the pattern, ask what's going on, restate the expectation and the next consequence | If the pattern repeats the same day or the next day |
| 5 | Documented consequence per the classroom/school behavior plan (e.g., seat change, brief loss of a privilege), delivered privately where possible, logged with date/behavior/response | If the behavior is safety-related, repeated across multiple days despite rungs 1–4, or disrupts instruction for others |
| 6 | Office referral / administrator involvement, with the documented rung 1–5 history attached | Rung 6 escalation without documented lower rungs reads as an overreaction to whoever reviews it — always attach the log |

**Why the ladder matters beyond politeness:** a public correction at rung 3 or later, delivered in front of peers, converts a behavior issue into a status contest the student can only "win" by escalating further — the private rungs exist specifically to remove the audience, not to be lenient.

## 3. Formative Assessment Item-Analysis → Reteach Decision Process

Run this immediately after any tagged formative check, before deciding what Day 6 (or equivalent) looks like.

**Step 1 — Score at the item level, tagged to sub-objective, not just totaled.**
Example dataset (14-item quiz, n=142, all 5 sections): per-item % correct: 96, 94, 91, 45, 88, 90, 38, 84, 41, 86, 89, 87, 90, 88. Overall mean 79.1%.

**Step 2 — Cluster items by sub-objective and compare cluster means, not just look for the single worst item.**
Items 4, 7, 9 map to sub-objective (b), sequencing across glycolysis/Krebs/ETC: cluster mean 41.3%. All other items (sub-objectives a, c, d): cluster mean 88.5%. The 38-point gap between clusters is the signal the overall mean erases.

**Step 3 — Slice the low cluster by section to rule out a single-section delivery issue.**
Section A 40%, B 39%, C 44%, D 42%, E 41% (weighted average 41.2%, n=142). Range is 5 points across 5 sections — uniform, meaning the fix is a content/sequencing reteach for everyone, not a review of what happened in one specific section.

**Step 4 — Before including any individual low score in the class-wide pattern, verify accommodation delivery for flagged students.**
Cross-check the accommodation-delivery log against the quiz date. If a student's active accommodation (extended time, read-aloud, chunked text) wasn't delivered that day, exclude that student's score from the standards-conclusion and route it as a separate accommodation-fidelity flag to the case manager — it is not evidence about the sub-objective.

**Step 5 — Apply the reteach threshold.**
Sub-objective cluster mean under ~70% correct, uniform across sections → reteach before the summative. 70–85% → targeted small-group work for the specific students who missed it, not a whole-class reteach. Above 85% → proceed as planned.

**Step 6 — Design the reteach to match the error pattern, not a generic repeat.**
If wrong answers cluster on ordering (students place ATP production before glycolysis), the fix is a kinesthetic sequencing activity, not a re-lecture of definitions the students already have (sub-objective a was at 94%+ — that's not where the gap is).

**Step 7 — Recheck with a short, single-objective instrument, not the original quiz repeated.**
A 4-item exit ticket on sub-objective (b) alone, same day as the reteach, before the summative.

## 4. Grade Dispute Resolution (rubric-anchored point math)

Lab report rubric, 4 categories, 5 points each, 20 points possible:

| Category | Descriptor for 5 | Descriptor for 2 |
|---|---|---|
| Hypothesis | Testable, states predicted relationship between IV/DV | Vague or non-testable, or omits the predicted relationship |
| Data table | Complete, correctly labeled, correct units | Missing labels, units, or 2+ data points |
| Analysis | Identifies IV/DV relationship AND at least one source of error | Identifies the relationship only, no error discussion, or contains a conceptual error |
| Conclusion | Restates relationship, ties back to hypothesis, states one limitation | States a result without tying back to the hypothesis or without a limitation |

**Original grading:** Hypothesis 4/5, Data table 5/5, Analysis 2/5, Conclusion 3/5 = 14/20 = 70%.

**Parent dispute:** argues Analysis should be a 4 or 5, not a 2.

**Resolution process:**
1. Re-read the student's Analysis section against the descriptor language only, not against the parent's framing or the student's effort. The submission correctly identifies the IV/DV relationship (temperature increases reaction rate) but contains no discussion of error sources anywhere in the section.
2. Per the rubric, "identifies the relationship only, no error discussion" is explicitly the 2-descriptor — the score stands on the Analysis category. State this back citing the exact descriptor line, not "I felt it needed more."
3. While re-reading, check the other three categories for a clerical error as a matter of course (this is a genuine second check, not a concession offered to soften the disagreement). Finds the Data table is missing correct units on one of four measurements — per the Data table descriptor, that's a 4, not the 5 originally recorded.
4. Corrected total: Hypothesis 4 + Data table 4 + Analysis 2 + Conclusion 3 = 13/20 = 65%.
5. Communicate both findings together: the Analysis score is confirmed and unchanged, citing the descriptor; the Data table score is corrected downward by 1 point for a units omission caught on re-review, which the original grading missed. Net grade moves from 70% to 65% — a swing the parent didn't ask for, but one that follows from applying the rubric consistently rather than only re-checking the category under dispute.

**Deliverable (quoted, sent to parent):** "Reviewing the Analysis section against our lab rubric: the 2/5 reflects that the section identifies the temperature/reaction-rate relationship but does not discuss any source of error, which is the rubric's dividing line between a 2 and a 4 (see attached rubric, Analysis row). That score stands. In re-reviewing the full report, I also found the data table is missing units on the day-3 measurement, which brings that category from 5 to 4 per the same rubric. Corrected total: 13/20 (65%), down from the original 14/20 (70%). Happy to go through the rubric line by line with [student] before the next lab."

## 5. IEP/504 Accommodation Implementation Checklist

Not a plan-writing task — implementation only, for accommodations already decided by the IEP/504 team.

- Confirm the accommodation applies to the specific task at hand (e.g., "extended time on tests and quizzes" applies to the formative quiz, not to a 4-item same-day exit ticket unless the plan says otherwise — check the plan's actual scope, don't assume).
- Deliver it every time it applies, not only when the classroom happens to be set up for it — an accommodation delivered inconsistently is not delivered.
- Log delivery per instance (date, task, accommodation, delivered y/n) so a missed delivery is visible before it contaminates a data-driven decision (see Section 3, Step 4).
- If an accommodation isn't working as written (e.g., extended time isn't enough for a student who still can't finish), report the specific gap to the case manager with the evidence — don't unilaterally add time, remove requirements, or otherwise edit the plan in the classroom.
