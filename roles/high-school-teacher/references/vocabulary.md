# Working vocabulary — terms a teacher uses precisely

Format: definition → how a practitioner says it → common misuse.

**Backward design** — Planning a unit starting from the desired evidence of mastery (the assessment), then designing instruction to produce that evidence, rather than starting from an activity or textbook chapter.
In use: "Before I touch an activity, I need the summative item that proves transfer — everything else gets built backward from that."
Misuse: treating it as "write objectives first" only, then planning activities as usual — the actual discipline is designing the assessment itself before the lesson sequence, not just stating a goal.

**Formative vs. summative assessment** — Formative = low/no-stakes, diagnostic, meant to change the next lesson. Summative = higher-stakes, evaluative, meant to certify mastery at the end.
In use: "That quiz is formative — I'm not grading it heavily, I'm using it to decide Wednesday's lesson."
Misuse: calling something "formative" while weighting it like a test in the gradebook — the label doesn't change the function if the stakes don't match it.

**Item analysis** — Breaking down assessment results by individual question (and the sub-objective it's tagged to), not just the total score, to find which specific skill or knowledge point is weak.
In use: "The overall mean was fine, but item analysis showed the ATP-sequencing cluster at 41%."
Misuse: reading only the overall percentage and concluding the unit "went well" or "went poorly" without checking whether the average is masking a specific gap.

**Mastery threshold** — The percent-correct level, usually set per sub-objective rather than per whole assessment, below which a reteach is triggered rather than proceeding to the next unit.
In use: "That cluster is at 41%, well under our 70% reteach threshold — we're not moving to the summative yet."
Misuse: applying one threshold to the whole test rather than to each sub-objective, which is exactly the averaging error the threshold exists to catch.

**Differentiation** — Varying the instructional path, scaffold, pacing, or product by which students reach the same standard, based on readiness, interest, or learning profile.
In use: "Same target for everyone — the tiered task changes how much scaffolding each group gets to get there."
Misuse: using it to mean giving some students an easier target or lower expectation — that's a lowered standard, not differentiation, and the two get confused constantly.

**Scaffolding** — Temporary support (a sentence starter, a graphic organizer, a worked example) that's deliberately removed as a student gains independence with a skill.
In use: "I'm scaffolding the lab write-up with a sentence frame this unit; by the spring they write it cold."
Misuse: treating a permanent modification (e.g., a permanently shortened assignment) as scaffolding — scaffolding has a removal plan, a permanent accommodation does not.

**Accommodation vs. modification** — Accommodation changes how a student accesses or demonstrates learning without changing what's being measured (extended time, read-aloud, chunked text). Modification changes the standard or content being measured itself (fewer questions testing a lower-complexity version of the same skill).
In use: "Extended time is an accommodation — same standard, same rigor, more time. A shortened, easier version of the test would be a modification, and that's a different decision made by the IEP team, not by me."
Misuse: using the terms interchangeably, or treating an accommodation as a favor rather than a legally required, standard-preserving access change.

**IEP vs. 504 plan** — IEP (Individualized Education Program, under IDEA) provides specialized instruction and services for a qualifying disability affecting educational performance. A 504 plan (under Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act) provides accommodations for access, without specialized instruction, for a broader range of qualifying conditions.
In use: "She's on a 504 for extended time and preferential seating — no specialized instruction goals, just access accommodations."
Misuse: assuming an IEP is just "a stronger 504" — they're different legal instruments with different eligibility criteria and different obligations, not a severity scale on the same document.

**Least restrictive response (behavior)** — Choosing the least disruptive, least public intervention that's likely to be effective, escalating only as lower-level responses fail, rather than defaulting to the most visible consequence.
In use: "Proximity and a non-verbal cue first — I only go to a private redirect if that doesn't land."
Misuse: treating "least restrictive" as "do nothing" or "ignore it" — it's a sequenced escalation, not an argument against ever escalating.

**Rubric-anchored grading** — Scoring against specific, pre-written descriptor language for each score level, so the same submission would receive the same score from a different grader.
In use: "The 2 versus 4 distinction on Analysis is whether the write-up discusses a source of error — that's in the descriptor, not my judgment call."
Misuse: writing a rubric with vague descriptors ("good analysis" vs. "weak analysis") and then treating scores as objective because a rubric technically exists.

**Cognitive load** — The working-memory demand a task places on a learner at a given moment; instruction chunked to that limit is retained better than instruction that exceeds it.
In use: "I'm capping direct instruction at 12 minutes on a new-vocabulary day — past that, retention drops regardless of how clear the explanation is."
Misuse: using "cognitive load" to justify simply shortening a lesson for pacing reasons, rather than actually chunking novel content against working-memory limits.

**Exit ticket** — A brief, single-objective formative check completed at the end of a class period, used for a same-day read on one specific skill, distinct from a full unit quiz.
In use: "Four-item exit ticket on the sequencing sub-objective only, right after the reteach, before we move to the summative."
Misuse: using a full-length quiz and calling it an exit ticket — the format's value is being short and single-objective enough to score and act on the same day.

**Standards-based grading** — Recording and reporting achievement against specific standards or sub-objectives rather than (or in addition to) a single blended letter grade.
In use: "The gradebook shows separate marks for sequencing and for structure-function — a blended 79% would have hidden the gap standards-based reporting shows directly."
Misuse: assuming it means "no zeros" or "unlimited retakes" — those are separate policy choices some standards-based systems adopt, not the definition of the practice itself.

**Tiered assignment** — A task designed at multiple levels of complexity or scaffolding that all target the same underlying standard, assigned by readiness rather than by track or label.
In use: "Same objective, three versions — the scaffolded, the standard, and the extension version, based on last week's data, not a fixed group."
Misuse: creating a permanently lower-complexity "easy version" that measures a different, lesser standard — that's tracking to a lower ceiling, not tiering to the same one.
