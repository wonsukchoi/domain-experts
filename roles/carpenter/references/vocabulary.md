# Vocabulary

Terms generalists and handymen use loosely that a carpenter keeps sharply separate — the separation is usually where the error lives.

### Bearing wall vs. partition wall

A **bearing wall** carries load from above (joists, rafters, another wall) down to the foundation. A **partition wall** divides space and carries only its own weight and whatever's hung on it. Both can look identical from inside a room — the distinction lives in the framing above and below, not in the wall itself.

**In use:** "Don't go by whether it's got a door in it — that's a partition detail. Go up in the attic and see which way the joists run relative to this wall."

**Common misuse:** assuming any wall running perpendicular to the exterior walls, or any wall that "looks structural," is bearing — or the opposite error, assuming an interior wall can't be bearing because it's not load-bearing-looking from below.

### Header vs. beam vs. lintel

A **header** spans an opening within a wall (door, window, wall opening) and transfers load to the jack studs on either side. A **beam** is a larger structural member spanning between posts or bearing points, often carrying multiple joists or a wall above, not just an opening. A **lintel** is the masonry/steel-specific term for the same function as a header, over a masonry opening. All three do the same job — carry load across a gap — at different scales and in different materials.

**In use:** "That's not a header job anymore once it's picking up a point load from the wall above — that's a beam, and it needs its own posts and footing, not jack studs."

**Common misuse:** calling any horizontal structural wood member a "header" regardless of scale, which hides the fact that a beam-scale load needs beam-scale bearing, not header-scale jack studs.

### Point load vs. distributed load

A **distributed load** spreads evenly along a member's length (a floor's live load across a joist). A **point load** concentrates at one location (a post, a beam end, a girder truss bearing on a single spot). Point loads need a direct path to the foundation — blocking, squash blocks, or a post directly below — that a distributed load doesn't require in the same way.

**In use:** "That post isn't just resting on the subfloor — it's a point load, it needs a squash block down to the joist below or it's going to crush the sheathing over time."

**Common misuse:** treating a post or beam-end reaction as if normal joist framing underneath will handle it the way it handles distributed floor load; a joist or subfloor rated fine for 40 psf distributed can still fail locally under a concentrated reaction.

### Jack stud vs. king stud vs. cripple stud

The **king stud** runs full height, floor to top plate, on either side of an opening. The **jack stud** (trimmer) sits inside the king stud and stops at the header, actually bearing the header's load. **Cripple studs** fill the gap above the header up to the top plate, or below a window sill — they carry minimal load and mainly hold sheathing/drywall in place.

**In use:** "Header's fine on span and bending, but check it's landing on jack studs, not just cripples — cripples aren't sized or positioned to take that reaction."

**Common misuse:** using "jack stud" and "king stud" interchangeably, or assuming any stud next to an opening is doing bearing work when it might be a non-bearing cripple.

### Rough opening vs. finished opening

The **rough opening** is the framed gap before the door or window unit goes in, sized per the manufacturer's spec sheet (not a fixed rule-of-thumb) to leave shim space. The **finished opening** is the visible opening after the unit, jambs, and trim are installed — smaller than the rough opening by the thickness of the unit's frame.

**In use:** "Don't frame to the door's actual width — pull the rough-opening dimension off this unit's spec sheet, it's not the same add-on for every manufacturer."

**Common misuse:** using a generic "add 2 inches" rule for every door or window regardless of manufacturer and unit type, which produces openings that are too tight for some units and sloppy-loose for others.

### Dead load vs. live load

**Dead load** is the permanent weight of the structure itself — framing, sheathing, roofing, drywall. **Live load** is variable, occupancy-driven weight — people, furniture, snow, stored items — and is what code tables like 40 psf (residential floors) or region-specific snow loads represent.

**In use:** "We're at 50 psf combined on this span — 40 live, 10 dead — that's the number the joist table's built around, not just the live load alone."

**Common misuse:** quoting only the live-load number (like "40 psf") as if it's the total demand on a member, when dead load is added on top of it in every span table calculation.

### Deflection limit (L/360 vs. L/240)

**Deflection** is how much a member bends under load, expressed as a fraction of its span. **L/360** (span in inches ÷ 360) is the standard limit for floors under live load, chosen to keep drywall and tile from cracking. **L/240** is a looser limit used for roof members without a plaster/drywall ceiling below, where visible sag matters more than finish cracking.

**In use:** "It clears bending fine, but check deflection separately — L/360 on a 96-inch span is 0.27 inches, and this beam's right up against it."

**Common misuse:** treating "it doesn't break" (a bending/strength check) as the same thing as "it doesn't flex too much" (a deflection check) — a member can pass one and fail the other, and deflection is what causes cracked finishes, not collapse.

### Moisture content vs. equilibrium moisture content (EMC)

**Moisture content (MC)** is the water weight in a piece of lumber as a percentage of its oven-dry weight, measured with a meter at a point in time. **Equilibrium moisture content (EMC)** is the MC that lumber settles to over time in a given climate and conditioned space — typically 8–12% inside a heated/cooled house. Framing installed above EMC will keep shrinking after it's enclosed.

**In use:** "It's reading 17% now — that's under the 19% code ceiling, but this house's EMC is going to land around 9%, so expect more shrinkage than the code number suggests."

**Common misuse:** treating the 19% code threshold as a "safe" number in itself, without accounting for how far above the building's eventual EMC that reading actually sits.

### Notch vs. bore (hole)

A **notch** removes material from the edge of a member. A **bore** (hole) is drilled through the member's face, staying inside its edges. Code treats them differently because a notch removes material from the extreme fiber where bending stress is highest, while a centered hole in the middle of the depth sits closer to the neutral axis where stress is lower — a bored hole is allowed to be proportionally larger than a notch at the same location.

**In use:** "Can't notch it there — middle third of the span, that's off-limits for notches. A bored hole in the same spot is fine if it's under a third of the depth and centered."

**Common misuse:** applying hole-sizing rules to a notch or vice versa, since the two have different depth limits and different restricted zones along the span.

### Tributary width / tributary area

The strip of floor or roof that a given structural member (joist, wall, beam) is understood to support — typically half the distance to the next parallel support on each side. It's the multiplier that turns a psf load into a plf (per-linear-foot) or total-pounds load on a specific member.

**In use:** "This wall's tributary width is 12 feet — half of the 24-foot joist span it's picking up — so at 50 psf combined that's 600 plf on the header, not the full 24 feet."

**Common misuse:** using the full span or full room width instead of half the distance to the next support, which overstates or understates the actual load by roughly double.

### Racking vs. bending

**Racking** is a wall or frame distorting sideways (parallelogramming) under lateral load — wind, seismic — resisted by sheathing, bracing, or shear panels. **Bending** is a horizontal member flexing vertically under a gravity load, resisted by its depth and section modulus. Different failure mode, different fix — more sheathing nails address racking; a deeper or stiffer member addresses bending.

**In use:** "That wall's not sagging, it's racking — the corner's out of square under wind load. Check the shear nailing schedule, not the header size."

**Common misuse:** diagnosing a sagging or out-of-level condition and a leaning/racked condition as the same problem, leading to a fix (bigger beam) that doesn't address the actual failure mode (missing shear bracing).

### Rafter tie vs. collar tie

A **rafter tie** (often the ceiling joist itself) resists outward thrust at the bottom third of the rafter span, near the top plate, and is structurally required wherever there's no ridge beam. A **collar tie**, placed in the upper third near the ridge, resists wind uplift/ridge separation but does little for outward thrust — it is not a substitute for a rafter tie.

**In use:** "That's a collar tie up near the ridge — it's not doing the rafter tie's job. If the ceiling joists don't run continuous across this span, the walls are going to spread."

**Common misuse:** installing a collar tie and treating the thrust problem as solved, when only a true rafter tie (or a structural ridge beam) actually resists the outward push at the wall plates.

### Species-grade combination (e.g., DF-L No. 2 vs. SPF No. 2)

Lumber's allowable design values (bending stress Fb, compression perpendicular to grain Fc⊥, modulus of elasticity E) come from a specific species-and-grade combination published in the NDS Supplement — they are not interchangeable across species at the same nominal size and grade label. Douglas Fir-Larch No. 2 and Spruce-Pine-Fir No. 2 are both "No. 2" but carry meaningfully different allowable stresses.

**In use:** "Span table's built on SPF No. 2 — if the yard substitutes DF-L, the span doesn't get worse, it improves, but don't assume the reverse without checking the table again."

**Common misuse:** reading "No. 2" as a single universal grade regardless of species, and pulling span-table numbers for one species while framing with lumber actually stamped as another.

### Squash block

A short, vertically-grained block installed tight against an I-joist's web, directly under a point load (post, beam end, girder truss), that transfers the load past the joist's web and flange down to the plate and foundation below — installed before the subfloor goes down, not added after the fact.

**In use:** "Post lands right there — get a squash block under it before we sheet the floor, or that point load's riding on the I-joist flange alone."

**Common misuse:** assuming any blocking between joists (which is typically for edge support or fire blocking) serves the same purpose as a squash block, which is specifically sized and placed to carry a vertical point load down through the floor system.
