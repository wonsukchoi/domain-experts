# Red flags

Smell tests a working carpenter catches on walkthrough, before a single measurement is taken. Each entry: what it usually means, the first question to ask, and the data or measurement to pull before deciding anything.

### Stair riser or tread varies more than 3/8" within one flight

**Usually means:** the stringer layout was cut from an assumed unit rise instead of total-rise-÷-number-of-risers math, and the error got absorbed into whichever step was cut last — usually the top or bottom riser.
**First question:** "What's the total floor-to-floor rise, and how many risers did the layout assume?"
**Data to pull:** measured rise at every riser in the flight with a story pole or tape, not a sample of three; recompute unit rise = total rise ÷ number of risers and compare to what was actually cut.

### Header sized from a roof-only span table for a wall carrying a floor load

**Usually means:** the header spec was copied from a prior job or a generic "double 2x10" habit without checking whether this wall's tributary load includes a floor above it, not just roof and ceiling.
**First question:** "What's above this wall — another floor, an attic, just roof rafters — and what's the tributary width to the next support?"
**Data to pull:** framing plan or a physical trace of joist/rafter direction above the wall; tributary width; live and dead load psf for that use (floor vs. roof vs. snow); the specific span table cell for that species/grade/spacing/load combination, not a remembered number.

### Moisture meter reads 16–19% on framing at the pre-drywall walk

**Usually means:** lumber was framed wet (rain exposure, green stock) and hasn't had enough dry-in time; it's inside the IRC's 19% legal ceiling but above the ~15% professional target where shrinkage-driven nail pops and drywall cracking start showing up months later.
**First question:** "How long has this framing been enclosed or exposed to weather, and what's the reading at the rim joist and at 3 ft up on studs, not just the top plate?"
**Data to pull:** pin-type meter readings (insulated pins, not surface probes) at multiple members and heights; weather/exposure history since framing; schedule slack available before drywall.

### A hole or notch cut into an I-joist flange, or a notch cut past 1/6 of a dimensional member's depth

**Usually means:** whoever ran the plumbing or electrical treated the engineered member like a 2x, or a framer eyeballed a notch depth instead of measuring against the 1/6-depth, 1/3-length, outer-third-only rule (IRC R502.8) for dimensional lumber — or against the manufacturer's chart for engineered lumber, which prohibits flange cuts outright.
**First question:** "Is this a dimensional joist or an I-joist/LVL, and was this cut made against a chart or by eye?"
**Data to pull:** the manufacturer's span/hole chart for the specific product line if engineered; a tape measurement of notch depth/length and its position relative to the span's middle third if dimensional.

### A bearing wall was opened, shortened, or removed and nobody can say what it carries

**Usually means:** the wall's role was assumed from how it looks in the room, not traced through the framing above it — the most common precursor to an undersized header or an unsupported point load.
**First question:** "What's directly above this wall, and does it run parallel or perpendicular to the joists here?"
**Data to pull:** attic or upper-floor framing photos/plan; joist span and direction relative to this wall; any point loads (posts, other bearing walls) landing on this wall from above.

### A point load (post, beam end, or another wall) lands on a floor system with no blocking beneath it

**Usually means:** the load is bypassing the joist and bearing on subfloor sheathing alone, or on an I-joist web with no squash block — subfloor and I-joist webs aren't rated to take a concentrated load without a solid path to the foundation.
**First question:** "Where does this load go below this floor — is there a doubled joist, a squash block, or a post directly underneath, or does it just land on sheathing?"
**Data to pull:** the reaction load in lbs at that point; framing below (joist layout, any existing blocking); manufacturer's squash-block/point-load detail if the floor system is engineered.

### Rough opening diagonals differ by 1/4" or more

**Usually means:** the opening was framed to width and height without a square check, or has racked since framing — either way, the unit will bind, leak air at a corner, or need shims thick enough to affect the reveal.
**First question:** "Have both diagonals been measured, corner to corner, both directions?"
**Data to pull:** both diagonal measurements; jamb width/height against the unit's actual rough-opening spec (not a rule-of-thumb add-2-inches number, which varies by manufacturer).

### Rafters with no ridge beam and no continuous ceiling-joist or rafter-tie connection

**Usually means:** the roof is relying on the ceiling framing to resist rafter thrust, and if that tie is missing, cut short, or interrupted by an opening, the ridge will sag and the walls will spread outward over time — a slow failure that's invisible until drywall cracks appear at the top of exterior walls.
**First question:** "Is the ridge a structural beam, or does this roof depend on the ceiling joists to tie the rafter thrust together — and is that tie continuous?"
**Data to pull:** ridge type (beam vs. board) from the framing plan; rafter-tie or ceiling-joist continuity across the span, especially where a stairwell or vaulted ceiling interrupts it.

### Nailing pattern on an engineered rim board or shear wall doesn't match the specified schedule

**Usually means:** the crew used the nail gun's default spacing/depth instead of the schedule the engineered assembly's rating assumes — fastening is part of the rated capacity on engineered rim board and shear panels, not a finishing detail.
**First question:** "What nailing schedule does the plan or the manufacturer's literature call for here, and is that what's actually in the wood?"
**Data to pull:** specified nail size/spacing/edge distance from plans or manufacturer literature; a sample count and spacing measurement on the installed assembly.
