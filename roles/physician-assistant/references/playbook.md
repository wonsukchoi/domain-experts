# Physician Assistant — Playbook

## Clinical decision rules (filled examples)

### Ottawa Ankle Rules
X-ray indicated only if pain in malleolar zone AND one of:
- Bone tenderness at posterior edge/tip of lateral malleolus (distal 6cm), OR
- Bone tenderness at posterior edge/tip of medial malleolus (distal 6cm), OR
- Inability to bear weight both immediately after injury and for 4 steps at exam

Sensitivity ~99% for clinically significant fracture (Stiell et al.). A negative rule reliably excludes fracture without imaging.

### Centor Score (strep pharyngitis, adult)
| Criterion | Points |
|---|---|
| Tonsillar exudate | +1 |
| Tender anterior cervical adenopathy | +1 |
| Fever by history | +1 |
| Absence of cough | +1 |
| Age 15-44 | 0 |
| Age <15 | +1 |
| Age ≥45 | -1 |

Score 0-1: no testing/antibiotics. Score 2-3: rapid strep test, treat if positive. Score ≥4: consider empiric treatment or test-and-treat.

### Wells' Criteria (PE, simplified)
| Criterion | Points |
|---|---|
| Clinical signs of DVT | +3 |
| PE is #1 diagnosis or equally likely | +3 |
| Heart rate >100 | +1.5 |
| Immobilization/surgery in prior 4 weeks | +1.5 |
| Prior DVT/PE | +1.5 |
| Hemoptysis | +1 |
| Malignancy (treated within 6 months) | +1 |

Score ≤4: PE unlikely, consider D-dimer. Score >4: PE likely, consider CT-PA. A score in this range on its own does not authorize independent PA management of a suspected PE in most agreements — treat a positive-range result as an escalation trigger, not just a testing trigger.

## Delegation agreement excerpt (illustrative)

> §3.2 Independently Manageable Conditions: musculoskeletal sprain/strain (Grade I-II), uncomplicated upper respiratory infection, uncomplicated UTI in non-pregnant adult females, OTC-equivalent analgesic/antipyretic prescribing.
>
> §3.5 Requires Physician Consultation Prior to Treatment: any Schedule II-III prescription exceeding 3 days' supply; any diagnosis of malignancy (new); pediatric patient under 2 years with fever >100.4°F; any patient requiring hospital admission.
>
> §3.7 Retrospective Chart Review: collaborating physician reviews 10% of independently managed charts within 30 days; PA must flag any case with an unexpected outcome for review regardless of the 10% sample.

Test: could a PA read this agreement and correctly sort a new case into "manage" vs "consult first" without asking a colleague? If not, the agreement is too vague to rely on — escalate to update it, not to guess at intent.

## Escalation-trigger checklist

1. Is the diagnosis/procedure explicitly listed as independently manageable? If not listed at all (not just "not excluded"), treat as requiring consultation.
2. Does the proposed prescription cross a specific threshold (days' supply, schedule, refill count) named in the agreement or state law?
3. Has the patient failed to respond as expected to an in-scope treatment already tried?
4. Is this the PA's first 3-6 months managing this specific condition type independently (new specialty rotation)?
5. Would a reasonable physician reviewing the chart in 30 days be surprised this wasn't discussed first?

Any "yes" → consult before finalizing the plan, document the call (or the attempt and outcome if unreachable), and note the specific agreement provision or threshold that triggered the escalation.
