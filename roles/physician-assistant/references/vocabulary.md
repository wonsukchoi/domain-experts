# Physician Assistant — Vocabulary

### Collaboration agreement (or supervision agreement)
The signed, state-specific document between a PA and one or more physicians defining the scope of independently manageable conditions, required consultation triggers, and chart-review terms.
**In use:** "That diagnosis isn't in our collaboration agreement's independently-manageable list — I need to call before I treat it."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a formality signed once at hiring and never referenced again, rather than a working document consulted per case.

### Optimal Team Practice (OTP)
AAPA's policy framework advocating for state laws that remove the requirement for a specific, named supervising physician, replacing it with a collaborative team-practice model.
**In use:** "This state adopted OTP, so I don't need a single named collaborating physician on file anymore, just a functioning care team."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to mean "no physician involvement required at all" — OTP changes the administrative structure of collaboration, not the underlying clinical accountability.

### Delegated scope of practice
The specific set of conditions, procedures, and prescribing authority a PA is authorized to manage independently, set by state law and narrowed or specified further by the collaboration agreement.
**In use:** "Competence to treat this and authorization to treat this independently are different questions — check the delegated scope, not just my own judgment."
**Common misuse:** Equated with clinical competence — a PA can be fully capable of managing a condition and still be outside their delegated scope to do so without consultation.

### Cosignature requirement
A specific rule (often tied to controlled-substance prescribing, procedure type, or diagnosis) requiring the collaborating physician's signature on a chart or order before or after the fact.
**In use:** "This opioid prescription exceeds the 3-day threshold, so it needs a cosignature per §3.5."
**Common misuse:** Confused with general chart co-review — a cosignature requirement is a specific, often legally mandated trigger, not a routine quality check.

### Retrospective chart review
The collaborating physician's periodic review of a sample (or all) of a PA's independently managed charts, done after the encounter rather than in real time.
**In use:** "Our agreement specifies 10% retrospective review within 30 days — that's the safety net for independent decisions, not a rubber stamp."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to catch problems in real time — it's a delayed audit, which is why explicit escalation triggers matter more than relying on review to catch a bad call before harm occurs.

### PANCE / PANRE
NCCPA's initial certification exam (PANCE) and periodic recertification exam (PANRE) that maintain a PA's national certification, distinct from state licensure.
**In use:** "National certification via PANCE doesn't grant scope of practice by itself — that's set separately by the state license and the collaboration agreement."
**Common misuse:** Conflated with state licensure — certification and licensure are separate systems with separate renewal requirements.

### Generalist training model
PA education's structure of broad didactic coursework plus clinical rotations across multiple specialties, in contrast to a physician's specialty-specific residency.
**In use:** "The generalist model is why I can move from family medicine to orthopedics without recertifying into a new specialty — but it also means I have less depth in ortho on day one than a physician who did an ortho residency."
**Common misuse:** Read as "less rigorous" rather than "differently structured" — the tradeoff is career flexibility for initial specialty depth, not overall training quality.

### Clinical decision rule
A validated, published algorithm (Ottawa Ankle Rules, Wells' Criteria, Centor score) that converts clinical findings into a reproducible probability estimate or action threshold.
**In use:** "Applying the Ottawa Ankle Rules here gives a documented, defensible reason to skip the X-ray, not just my gestalt."
**Common misuse:** Treated as optional when the clinician is experienced — the value of a decision rule for a PA is partly clinical accuracy and partly a chart-documented, defensible rationale for a scope-of-practice-adjacent decision.

### Scope creep (by competence)
The gradual expansion of what a PA manages independently, driven by increasing clinical skill rather than any actual change to the delegation agreement.
**In use:** "I've been managing this off-list because I'm confident I can — that's scope creep, not authorized practice."
**Common misuse:** Not recognized as a problem at all, since the clinical outcomes may be fine — the issue is administrative/legal authorization, not clinical quality.

### Practice act
The state law establishing what a PA may do at all, setting the outer boundary that a collaboration agreement can narrow but never exceed.
**In use:** "Even if our agreement were silent on this, the state practice act caps what I can prescribe without physician involvement."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to be the same across states, or assumed to be superseded by a permissive agreement — the agreement operates inside the practice act's limits, never beyond them.
