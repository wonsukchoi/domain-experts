# Red flags — what a center administrator notices instantly

Smell tests with thresholds. Any single one can be innocent; two or three together are a pattern.

### Staffing scheduled at exactly the legal minimum ratio with no buffer for an absence or incident
- **Usually means:** a single unplanned absence or incident can immediately put the center out of compliance and, more importantly, out of adequate real supervision — the legal minimum isn't a safety margin, it's a floor.
- **First question:** "If one staff member calls out today, does this room fall below the required ratio?"
- **Data to pull:** current staffing schedule against required ratios by room, historical frequency of same-day call-outs.

### Annual staff turnover above roughly 30-35% with no documented investigation into cause
- **Usually means:** turnover this high is a leading indicator of program quality and consistency problems, not just a hiring-pipeline issue to backfill.
- **First question:** "What do exit interviews say is actually driving departures — compensation, workload, management, something else?"
- **Data to pull:** turnover rate trend, exit interview themes, compensation benchmarking against comparable local centers.

### An incident (even minor) handled without formal documentation or family notification
- **Usually means:** the center is selectively deciding what's "worth mentioning" rather than disclosing consistently, which risks a larger trust breach if the family later learns the full picture wasn't shared.
- **First question:** "Was this incident documented and communicated to the family per standard protocol, regardless of how minor it seemed?"
- **Data to pull:** incident log, family communication log, comparison of what was documented internally versus what was actually communicated.

### Licensing/compliance documentation prepared specifically around scheduled inspection dates rather than maintained continuously
- **Usually means:** compliance may only be genuinely met at inspection time, with gaps in between that a snapshot inspection wouldn't catch.
- **First question:** "Is this documentation current right now, independent of when the next inspection is scheduled?"
- **Data to pull:** compliance documentation timestamps relative to inspection schedule, any gaps identified during non-inspection periods.

### A quality investment (lower ratios, new curriculum materials, higher staff pay) approved without checking it against a realistic financial sustainability model
- **Usually means:** the investment may be funded by unsustainable margin compression or an unrealistic tuition/enrollment assumption, risking a program that's excellent until it can no longer operate.
- **First question:** "What does this investment cost annually, and what specifically funds it on a sustained basis, not just this year?"
- **Data to pull:** the investment's full annual cost, the specific revenue source or margin change intended to sustain it long-term.

### A staff compensation or benefits decision evaluated purely as a cost line with no connection made to turnover or quality
- **Usually means:** the real ROI of the decision (retention, caregiving consistency) is being missed by treating it as a pure expense rather than a quality lever with a quantifiable return.
- **First question:** "What's the current turnover cost, and how would this compensation change be expected to affect it?"
- **Data to pull:** current turnover rate and per-departure replacement cost, any available data on the compensation change's likely retention effect.

### Enrollment or tuition decisions made without checking the center's actual current margin
- **Usually means:** a pricing or enrollment change could push the center past sustainable margin in either direction without a clear check on the actual number.
- **First question:** "What's our current margin, and how does this specific change affect it?"
- **Data to pull:** current revenue, cost structure, and margin; the specific enrollment or tuition change under consideration.

### A curriculum or program quality claim (e.g., "NAEYC-aligned") with no recent verification against the actual standard
- **Usually means:** a quality claim may be stale or aspirational rather than currently and specifically verified, similar to any compliance-vs-quality gap.
- **First question:** "When was this alignment/accreditation last verified, and against the current version of the standard?"
- **Data to pull:** most recent accreditation or standards-alignment review date and findings.
