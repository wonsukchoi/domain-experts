# Childcare administration working vocabulary

Terms an administrator uses precisely. Format: definition → how a practitioner says it → common misuse.

**Ratio (staff-to-child ratio)** — The state-mandated maximum number of children per staff member for a given age group, a legal floor and a non-negotiable input into every staffing decision, not one factor to weigh against cost.
In use: "Toddler ratio is 1:4 — that's the floor we staff to, and we build in a buffer above it, not to it exactly."
Misuse: treating the legal minimum ratio as an adequate operating target rather than a floor, leaving no margin for an unplanned absence or incident.

**Turnover rate** — The percentage of staff who leave over a period (commonly annualized), tracked here as a leading quality indicator connected to caregiving consistency, not purely an HR/recruiting metric.
In use: "42% annual turnover is well above the 25-30% range we'd consider healthy for this market — that's a quality signal, not just a staffing cost."
Misuse: reporting turnover purely as a recruiting/backfill metric without connecting it to its effect on caregiving consistency and program quality, which is the more consequential dimension for families.

**Licensing compliance vs. quality** — Licensing compliance is meeting the state's minimum legal requirements (ratios, background checks, health/safety codes); quality is a separate, higher standard of actual developmental and relational program value that compliance doesn't measure.
In use: "We're fully licensed and compliant — that's the floor. NAEYC-aligned curriculum and our retention numbers are what we track for actual quality."
Misuse: treating licensing compliance as evidence of program quality, when it specifically certifies minimum legal requirements rather than developmental excellence.

**NAEYC accreditation** — Accreditation from the National Association for the Education of Young Children, a recognized early childhood quality standard above and beyond state minimum licensing requirements.
In use: "We're pursuing NAEYC accreditation specifically because it sets a higher bar than state licensing on curriculum and staff qualifications."
Misuse: conflating NAEYC accreditation status with basic state licensing — they're different standards with different rigor, and neither substitutes for the other.

**Incident disclosure protocol** — A standing operational practice for documenting and communicating any incident (minor or serious) to families promptly and completely, rather than an ad hoc judgment call about what's "worth mentioning."
In use: "Per our incident disclosure protocol, even a minor scrape gets documented and the family is told what happened, what we did, and how the child is doing — every time, not selectively."
Misuse: applying disclosure inconsistently based on the staff member's own judgment of severity, which risks a family later discovering an undisclosed incident and losing trust in how a genuinely serious one would be handled.

**Turnover replacement cost** — The full estimated cost of a single staff departure, including recruiting, onboarding/training, temporary coverage, and lost productivity during ramp-up — the correct basis for evaluating whether a retention investment pays for itself.
In use: "At $4,800 per departure, cutting turnover from 10 to 6 departures a year saves $19,200 — that's the number that offsets part of the wage increase's cost."
Misuse: evaluating a compensation increase purely as an added cost without netting out the turnover-cost savings it's projected to produce.

**Contribution to sustainable margin** — Checking whether a proposed quality investment (staffing, compensation, materials) is funded by a specific, sustainable revenue source or margin change, as opposed to temporary margin compression that isn't sustainable long-term.
In use: "The wage increase is funded by a permanent $2.15/month tuition increase, not by drawing down reserves — that's what makes it sustainable."
Misuse: approving a quality investment funded by a one-time or unsustainable source (e.g., temporarily deferred maintenance, a grant that won't renew), producing a program that looks good until the funding source runs out.

**Developmentally appropriate practice (DAP)** — A framework for evaluating whether curriculum and activities are matched to children's actual age-specific developmental stage and needs, as distinct from either academically accelerated content or unstructured, purely custodial care.
In use: "This activity plan is developmentally appropriate for 3-year-olds — it builds fine motor skills through play, not worksheet-style instruction."
Misuse: assuming any structured curriculum is automatically developmentally appropriate, or assuming free play alone satisfies DAP — the standard specifically requires matching activities to the age group's actual developmental needs.

**Subsidy (childcare subsidy/voucher)** — Government or employer-funded assistance covering part or all of a family's tuition cost, a revenue source with its own reimbursement rate and compliance requirements distinct from private-pay tuition.
In use: "About 30% of our enrollment is subsidy-funded, and the reimbursement rate is below our full private-pay tuition — that gap has to be accounted for in the financial model."
Misuse: modeling subsidy-funded enrollment revenue at the same rate as private-pay tuition, missing that subsidy reimbursement rates are often lower and have their own compliance and payment-timing requirements.

**Wage elasticity of turnover** — The empirical relationship between a compensation change and the resulting change in staff turnover rate, used to project whether a wage increase is likely to meaningfully reduce departures versus have a marginal effect.
In use: "Based on comparable wage-elasticity data for this role, a $1.50/hour increase is projected to cut turnover from 42% to about 25% — that's the basis for the retention projection, not a guess."
Misuse: assuming any wage increase produces a proportional retention improvement without referencing comparable data, when the actual relationship can vary significantly by role, market, and the size of the increase.
