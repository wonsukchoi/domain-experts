### ASA physical status (ASA-PS)
A six-class system (I–VI) rating a patient's baseline systemic disease burden, not their airway or a full risk model.
**In use:** "She's an ASA III with the OSA and the poorly controlled hypertension — that alone doesn't tell me anything about her airway, I still need the exam."
**Common misuse:** Treating the ASA class as a complete preoperative risk assessment; it correlates with mortality risk but says nothing about airway difficulty, OSA severity, or PONV risk.

### MAC
Overloaded abbreviation: monitored anesthesia care (a technique/service level) versus minimum alveolar concentration (a volatile-agent potency measure).
**In use:** "We're doing this under MAC with local infiltration" (technique) vs. "run the sevo at 1 MAC" (concentration) — context has to disambiguate, the term alone doesn't.
**Common misuse:** Using "MAC" in documentation without the qualifier, leaving it ambiguous which meaning is intended.

### STOP-BANG
An 8-item screening score (Snoring, Tiredness, Observed apnea, Pressure/hypertension, BMI, Age, Neck circumference, Gender) for obstructive sleep apnea risk, not a diagnosis.
**In use:** "STOP-BANG is 6 — I'm treating him as high-risk OSA even without a sleep study."
**Common misuse:** Waiting for a formal polysomnography diagnosis before adjusting the plan; the score is meant to change management pre-diagnosis.

### Mallampati score
A 4-class visual estimate of oropharyngeal space (tongue-to-pharynx ratio) used as one input to airway difficulty prediction, not a standalone predictor.
**In use:** "Mallampati III plus a short thyromental distance — that combination is what worries me, not the Mallampati alone."
**Common misuse:** Relying on Mallampati score in isolation; it has poor sensitivity alone and is meant to be combined with thyromental distance, neck mobility, and mouth opening.

### Train-of-four (TOF)
A quantitative neuromuscular monitoring technique measuring the ratio of the fourth to first twitch after four supramaximal nerve stimulations, used to confirm reversal of paralysis.
**In use:** "TOF ratio's 0.85 on the monitor — that's not adequate reversal yet, hold the extubation."
**Common misuse:** Using qualitative "fade" assessment by feel ("four twitches, feels equal") instead of a quantitative monitor; qualitative assessment reliably misses residual block below a TOF ratio of about 0.4.

### Rapid sequence induction (RSI)
An induction technique minimizing the time between loss of consciousness and a secured airway to reduce aspiration risk, typically omitting mask ventilation between induction and intubation.
**In use:** "Full stomach, doing an RSI with cricoid pressure held until tube position confirmed."
**Common misuse:** Applying RSI reflexively to every "urgent" case regardless of actual aspiration risk, or omitting the pre-oxygenation step that makes it safe to skip mask ventilation.

### Local anesthetic systemic toxicity (LAST)
Systemic (CNS then cardiovascular) toxicity from excessive local anesthetic absorption or inadvertent intravascular injection, treated with lipid emulsion.
**In use:** "Cumulative bupivacaine's at 2.3mg/kg and she just said her lips feel numb — that's LAST until proven otherwise, stop injecting."
**Common misuse:** Waiting for a seizure or cardiac arrest to diagnose LAST; early CNS signs (perioral numbness, metallic taste, tinnitus) precede the dangerous cardiovascular phase and are the point to intervene.

### Malignant hyperthermia (MH)
A rare, life-threatening hypermetabolic reaction to volatile anesthetics or succinylcholine in genetically susceptible patients (ryanodine receptor mutation), treated with dantrolene.
**In use:** "EtCO2's climbing and his jaw's rigid — I'm calling it MH now, not waiting on the temperature."
**Common misuse:** Treating fever as the diagnostic trigger for calling MH; temperature rise is typically a late sign, and treatment delayed until it appears costs critical minutes.

### Can't intubate, can't oxygenate (CICO)
The declared emergency state when both intubation and mask/SGA ventilation have failed, triggering immediate surgical airway.
**In use:** "We're CICO — starting the cric now, not attempting another laryngoscopy."
**Common misuse:** Either declaring CICO too early on a merely difficult (not failed) airway, or too late by attempting one more laryngoscopy instead of moving to front-of-neck access.

### Bispectral index (BIS)
A processed-EEG number (0–100) used as one input for titrating anesthetic depth and reducing intraoperative awareness risk, not a direct measure of consciousness.
**In use:** "BIS is sitting at 55 with the current TIVA rate — that tracks with adequate depth for this case."
**Common misuse:** Treating a single BIS number as a guarantee against awareness; it's an adjunct to clinical signs (movement, hemodynamics), not a replacement for them, and can be confounded by electrocautery artifact.

### Closed-loop communication
A crisis-communication protocol where an instruction is directed to a named person, who repeats it back, and confirms completion aloud.
**In use:** "Sarah, draw up dantrolene 200mg — confirm dose and let me know when it's ready."
**Common misuse:** Giving instructions to "the room" generally instead of a named individual, which is how critical actions get silently skipped when everyone assumes someone else is doing it.

### Apfel score
A 4-factor simplified risk score (female sex, nonsmoker, history of PONV/motion sickness, planned postoperative opioids) predicting postoperative nausea and vomiting risk.
**In use:** "Apfel's a 3 — she's getting triple prophylaxis and I'm minimizing opioids where I can."
**Common misuse:** Applying a flat single-antiemetic default regardless of score, instead of stacking prophylactic agents proportional to the risk factor count.

### Neuraxial block level
The highest dermatome at which sensory (and associated sympathetic) blockade is present after spinal or epidural anesthesia, checked by exam, not assumed from the dose given.
**In use:** "Block's at T4 and climbing — that's high enough to explain the bradycardia and hypotension."
**Common misuse:** Assuming the block level from the drug dose and volume alone instead of checking it on physical exam; block height varies with patient position, dosing technique, and anatomy.

### Rescue airway device / supraglottic airway (SGA)
A device (e.g., laryngeal mask airway) placed above the vocal cords to provide ventilation when tracheal intubation isn't the plan or has failed, serving as Plan B in the difficult airway algorithm.
**In use:** "Two failed attempts — going to the LMA now per the algorithm, not a third look."
**Common misuse:** Treating the SGA purely as a "backup for emergencies" rather than a legitimate primary airway for many cases, or skipping straight past it to a surgical airway when it hasn't actually been tried.
