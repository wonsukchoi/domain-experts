# Vocabulary

### Timely warning
The Clery Act–mandated notification to the campus community when a reported crime represents a serious or continuing threat to students and employees.
**In use:** "That robbery near the west lot meets the timely-warning test — get it to the Clery officer before you draft anything."
**Common misuse:** Treated as "notify everyone about anything serious," when the actual test is whether the threat could recur and affect others, not how severe the single incident felt.

### Mandated reporter
A staff role legally required to report certain disclosures (abuse, and under Title IX, sexual misconduct) to a designated office, regardless of the reporting party's preference.
**In use:** "I know she asked me not to say anything, but I'm a mandated reporter on this — it goes to the Title IX office today."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to mean "report if you judge it serious enough," when it's actually a status triggered by disclosure content, not a discretionary call.

### Roommate agreement / community living agreement
A written, revisitable document specifying concrete behavioral expectations between roommates (guests, noise, cleanliness, shared items) — distinct from the housing contract.
**In use:** "Pull up their roommate agreement before the mediation — see what they actually signed off on in September."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a one-time move-in formality instead of a living document that should be revisited the moment friction appears.

### Restorative conduct process
A conduct-response model focused on repairing harm to the community (dialogue, accountability conversations, community service) rather than purely punitive sanction.
**In use:** "This is a good restorative case — noise complaint, no safety issue, and both residents are willing to talk it through."
**Common misuse:** Applied by default to every conduct case, including ones with a safety or power-imbalance component, where an accountability structure is needed instead of dialogue.

### BASICS
Brief Alcohol Screening and Intervention for College Students — a structured, evidence-based two-session intervention for alcohol-policy violations, run alongside (not instead of) conduct sanctions.
**In use:** "First offense, no risk indicators — BASICS referral plus the educational sanction, both tracks in parallel."
**Common misuse:** Treated as an alternative to conduct action rather than a parallel intervention track; the two serve different purposes and neither substitutes for the other.

### CARE team
Campus Assessment, Response and Evaluation team — a cross-functional group (counseling, dean of students, campus police, housing) that reviews concerning-behavior referrals for students who may need coordinated intervention.
**In use:** "This isn't a conduct case on its own — file a CARE referral, this is a pattern-of-concern situation."
**Common misuse:** Confused with the conduct process; a CARE referral is about coordinated support and risk assessment, not disciplinary outcome.

### Live-in requirement
The condition, standard in residential-life professional roles, that the RD resides in or immediately adjacent to the building they supervise, to be reachable for crisis response.
**In use:** "The live-in requirement means duty coverage assumes a five-minute response time, not a phone call from off-site."
**Common misuse:** Read as a compensation perk description rather than an operational design constraint that shapes response-time expectations.

### Span of control (RA:resident ratio)
The staffing benchmark for how many residents a single RA can reasonably cover, roughly 1:40–55 per ACUHO-I data.
**In use:** "We're at 1:65 on that floor after the resignation — that's outside benchmark, and it'll show up in duty response times before it shows up anywhere else."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a soft target to hit on paper rather than a leading indicator that predicts burnout and slower crisis response when breached.

### Timely documentation ("just the facts" standard)
The practice of writing incident reports using direct observations and quotes rather than conclusions or characterizations.
**In use:** "Rewrite that — 'resident was combative' is a conclusion. What did they actually do?"
**Common misuse:** New RAs write what they inferred about the resident's state of mind instead of what they observed, which weakens the report's evidentiary value later.

### Occupancy consolidation
The administrative process of combining residents from partially filled rooms into shared spaces to free up rooms, typically run early each semester.
**In use:** "We're at 78% occupancy on that wing — consolidation notices go out this week before we lose the room to another department."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to be purely a facilities/business-office process; RD input on which residents to pair matters for avoiding a forced pairing that recreates a prior conflict.

### FERPA health-or-safety emergency exception
The narrow carve-out in FERPA allowing disclosure of otherwise-protected student records to a parent or third party when there's an articulable and significant threat to health or safety.
**In use:** "This qualifies — hospitalization after the intoxication incident is a health-and-safety exception, so we can loop the parent in."
**Common misuse:** Invoked broadly for any concerning situation ("they seemed sad, so I called the parents") rather than reserved for genuine imminent-risk situations.

### Bystander intervention
A trained framework (notice, interpret as a problem, take responsibility, know how, act) for residents or staff to intervene in a situation before it escalates, rather than assuming someone else will.
**In use:** "We ran a bystander training this month specifically on the pregame-to-transport pattern we've been seeing."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a one-time training checkbox rather than a skill that needs reinforcement tied to the specific incident patterns a building is actually seeing.

### Escalation tree
The pre-defined, posted chain of who gets contacted in what order during an incident (RA → RD → Area Coordinator → Dean of Students → police), with defined response-time expectations at each link.
**In use:** "Don't skip the tree — RD gets the call first even if you know the Area Coordinator personally."
**Common misuse:** Bypassed informally based on staff relationships or convenience, which breaks the accountability and response-time structure the tree exists to guarantee.

### Sanction vs. educational outcome
The distinction between a punitive consequence (probation, suspension, restricted privileges) and a developmental one (reflection assignment, workshop, restorative conversation) assigned for a conduct violation.
**In use:** "First offense, no risk factors — this is an educational outcome, not a sanction."
**Common misuse:** Used interchangeably, which blurs the actual decision the RD is making about whether the goal is behavior correction or accountability for repeated/severe harm.
