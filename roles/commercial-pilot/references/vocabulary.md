# Vocabulary — Commercial Pilot

### Density altitude
Pressure altitude corrected for temperature deviation from ISA — the altitude the aircraft and engine actually "feel" for performance purposes, independent of the airport's charted elevation.
**In use:** "Field elevation's 6,500, but we're sitting at 9,632 density altitude right now, so pull the chart at 10,000, not 6,000."
**Common misuse:** Quoting the number as an abstraction rather than the direct input to the performance chart — pilots cite it correctly and then still read distance off the field-elevation row.

### Pressure altitude
Altitude indicated when the altimeter is set to 29.92 in Hg (standard) — the first correction applied before computing density altitude.
**In use:** "Altimeter's 30.10, so pressure altitude is 180 feet below field elevation before we even add the temperature correction."
**Common misuse:** Conflating it with density altitude itself, or skipping straight from field elevation and OAT to a DA estimate without it.

### Personal minimums
A pilot's own written-in-advance ceiling, visibility, crosswind, fuel, and runway-margin thresholds, set stricter than regulatory minimums for that pilot's current experience and that aircraft.
**In use:** "Legal alternate minimums are 2 and 3, but my personal minimum for a strange field at night is 500 and 2."
**Common misuse:** Treating "legal" as synonymous with "my minimum," or setting them once at training and never tightening them after a lapse in currency.

### PAVE checklist
Risk-assessment framework — Pilot, Aircraft, enVironment, External pressures — meant to be run before every go decision, not just during initial certificate training.
**In use:** "External pressures is the column actually driving this trip, not the weather column — let's be honest about that in the PAVE."
**Common misuse:** Treating it as a one-time training exercise from years ago rather than a live per-flight check, especially skipping the External pressures line since it's the least comfortable one.

### Fuel reserve (91.151 / 91.167)
The regulatory minimum fuel that must remain at destination after normal cruise: 30 min day VFR / 45 min night VFR under §91.151, or 45 minutes at normal cruise beyond destination and alternate under §91.167 for IFR.
**In use:** "We land with 45 minutes in the tanks under 91.167 — that's the floor, not the plan."
**Common misuse:** Planning fuel to arrive with exactly the reserve, then treating the reserve itself as usable fuel once airborne.

### 1-2-3 rule
The trigger for requiring a filed IFR alternate: if forecast ceiling and visibility at ETA ±1 hour are below 2,000 ft and 3 sm at destination, an alternate must be filed.
**In use:** "TAF's showing 1,800 broken right at our ETA, so the 1-2-3 rule kicks in — we need an alternate."
**Common misuse:** Treating the rule as proof the chosen alternate is adequate, when it only establishes that one is required — the alternate's own minimums are a separate check.

### Weight and balance envelope / CG
The graphed range of center-of-gravity positions, at each weight, within which the aircraft is certified controllable; a point outside it isn't authorized to fly regardless of total weight.
**In use:** "We're 200 pounds under gross, but that rear cargo pushed CG to the aft edge of the envelope — moving 50 pounds forward, not removing weight, is what fixes it."
**Common misuse:** Confirming the aircraft is under max gross weight and stopping there, without ever plotting the actual CG point on the envelope.

### Useful load vs. payload
Useful load is max gross weight minus empty weight (includes fuel, crew, cargo, passengers); payload is useful load minus the fuel actually carried.
**In use:** "Useful load's 3,500, but with full fuel our actual payload is only 1,250."
**Common misuse:** Quoting useful load as if it were available capacity for passengers and cargo, ignoring that fuel comes out of the same number.

### TEMPO / PROB30 / PROB40 (TAF groups)
TAF qualifiers for conditions expected temporarily (TEMPO) or with a stated probability (PROB30/40) during part of the forecast period, distinct from the prevailing forecast.
**In use:** "PROB40 TEMPO 1800Z–2000Z 1SM −SN — that's a 40% chance of a temporary drop to a mile in snow right at our ETA."
**Common misuse:** Reading a PROB/TEMPO group as "unlikely, so ignore it" rather than as the condition to actually plan fuel and alternate around.

### Plan continuation bias ("get-there-itis")
The tendency to keep executing a plan as conditions degrade because turning back has a visible, immediate cost and continuing has a deferred, uncertain one.
**In use:** "We're not descending through this layer just because the customer's waiting — that's exactly the get-there-itis pattern from CRM training."
**Common misuse:** Treating it as a personality flaw in weak pilots rather than a predictable bias affecting any pilot under schedule pressure, including experienced ones.

### Single-Pilot Resource Management (SRM)
The single-pilot adaptation of Crew Resource Management — using automation, ATC, dispatch, and passengers as resources when there's no other pilot to share workload or catch an error.
**In use:** "No first officer to catch this, so SRM says offload the radio work to the autopilot and get the approach briefed early."
**Common misuse:** Assuming CRM concepts like cross-checking don't apply at all in single-pilot ops, rather than translating them to whatever resources are actually available.

### Accelerate-stop distance / balanced field length
The runway length needed to accelerate to a decision speed and then stop if the takeoff is rejected — distinct from, and typically longer than, takeoff distance to a climb speed.
**In use:** "Book takeoff distance is 3,100 feet, but accelerate-stop on this runway is what actually matters if we lose the engine at rotation speed."
**Common misuse:** Checking only the AFM "takeoff distance" figure and assuming it already covers a rejected-takeoff scenario.

### Sterile cockpit
The rule (14 CFR 121.542 and equivalent operator policy) restricting non-essential conversation and activity below 10,000 ft or during other critical phases of flight.
**In use:** "Below ten, sterile cockpit — that conversation waits till cruise."
**Common misuse:** Applying it only to casual chit-chat while still allowing non-essential radio calls, paperwork, or phone use during the same critical phase.

### Minimum Equipment List (MEL) — pilot's dispatch use
The operator-specific list, built from the FAA Master MEL, of equipment that may be inoperative for dispatch, with conditions (M/O procedures) attached to each item.
**In use:** "Autopilot's on the MEL as deferred, Category C — but the (M) procedure means I need a second qualified pilot or it's a no-go for this route."
**Common misuse:** Treating "it's on the MEL" as automatic clearance to go, without checking that item's specific (M)/(O) procedure conditions for this particular flight.
