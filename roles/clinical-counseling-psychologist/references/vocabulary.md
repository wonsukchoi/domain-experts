# Clinical/Counseling Psychologist — Vocabulary

### Standard score
A score normalized to a fixed mean and standard deviation for the test's reference population (WAIS-IV indices: mean 100, SD 15).
**In use:** "Working Memory came in at 89 — that's 0.73 SD below the mean, not a dramatic deficit on its own."
**Common misuse:** Treating standard scores as raw ability rather than a comparison to a specific normative sample; a score is meaningless without knowing which population it's normed against.

### Scaled score
A subtest-level score normalized to mean 10, SD 3 — the building block that composite index scores are derived from.
**In use:** "Two subtests scored 6 and 14 — same index, wildly different scaled scores, which is why we don't just report the composite."
**Common misuse:** Averaging scaled scores across unrelated subtests and treating the average as meaningful without checking whether the subtests measure a coherent construct.

### T-score
A standardized score with mean 50, SD 10, used on inventories like the MMPI-3; T>=65 is conventionally the threshold for clinical significance.
**In use:** "RC7 came back at a T of 72 — clinically significant, that's the anxiety finding driving the formulation."
**Common misuse:** Confusing T-scores (mean 50) with standard scores (mean 100) and misjudging how elevated a value actually is.

### Confidence interval (CI)
The range within which the true score is estimated to fall at a given probability (typically 95%), accounting for measurement error.
**In use:** "The 95% CI on that Processing Speed score is 78-94 — don't report 85 as if it were exact."
**Common misuse:** Reporting the point estimate alone and letting the reader assume it's precise to the point.

### Validity scale
A scale built into an instrument (e.g. MMPI-3 F-r, VRIN-r) that flags over-reporting, under-reporting, or inconsistent responding, independent of the content scales.
**In use:** "F-r is within normal limits, so I'm comfortable interpreting the content scales."
**Common misuse:** Skipping the validity scales and going straight to content-scale interpretation, especially under time pressure.

### Base rate
How common a condition or finding is in the relevant population, used to calibrate how much weight a single piece of evidence should carry.
**In use:** "Anxiety is far more common than adult-onset ADHD in this age group — the base rate alone should make us lean anxiety until the evidence says otherwise."
**Common misuse:** Ignoring base rates and letting a vivid symptom match override how rare the matching diagnosis actually is (availability bias).

### Performance Validity Test (PVT) / Symptom Validity Test (SVT)
Standalone or embedded measures designed to detect whether a client is putting forth genuine effort (PVT, for cognitive testing) or accurately reporting symptoms (SVT), independent of the content being tested.
**In use:** "TOMM Trial 2 came back below the standard cutoff — before I interpret any cognitive scores, I need to address the validity failure."
**Common misuse:** Treating a failed PVT as proof of intentional malingering rather than one of several possible explanations (including genuine severe impairment in rare cases) that still needs to be worked through.

### Convergent validity
The degree to which independent measures of the same construct agree with each other.
**In use:** "We have convergent validity here — self-report, performance-based measure, and collateral interview all point the same direction."
**Common misuse:** Claiming convergent validity when the "independent" measures are actually the same method twice (e.g. two self-report scales) rather than genuinely different methods.

### Discriminant validity
The degree to which a measure does NOT correlate with constructs it shouldn't measure — evidence the instrument is measuring something specific, not general distress.
**In use:** "The anxiety and depression scales are correlated but not identical, which is exactly what you'd expect if they have discriminant validity."
**Common misuse:** Assuming any two elevated scales must reflect a single underlying problem rather than checking whether they're actually distinguishable constructs.

### Index score vs. Full Scale IQ
Index scores summarize a specific cognitive domain (verbal, perceptual, working memory, processing speed); Full Scale IQ averages across all of them into one number.
**In use:** "The indices spread 27 points, so we're reporting them separately instead of a Full Scale IQ that would average away the real pattern."
**Common misuse:** Defaulting to Full Scale IQ as "the" score even when the index profile is the clinically meaningful finding.

### Malingering
The deliberate fabrication or exaggeration of symptoms for an external incentive (e.g. disability payment, legal outcome, avoiding responsibility).
**In use:** "Failed PVT plus a pending disability claim raises malingering as a hypothesis, but it's not the only explanation for a failed validity test."
**Common misuse:** Using "malingering" as a diagnosis to write in a report rather than a hypothesis requiring its own supporting evidence, distinct from a failed validity test alone.

### Rule-out
A candidate diagnosis explicitly considered and excluded, documented with the reasoning, not just omitted from the final formulation.
**In use:** "ADHD is a rule-out here — no childhood-onset evidence, and the CPT's ADHD-specific signal (commission errors) was normal."
**Common misuse:** Silently dropping alternative diagnoses from the report instead of documenting why they were excluded, which weakens the formulation's defensibility.

### Ecological validity
Whether a test score predicts real-world functioning in the settings that actually matter to the referral question (work, school, home), not just performance in the testing room.
**In use:** "Processing Speed at the 8th percentile has ecological validity here because the job involves time-pressured data entry — the accommodation request follows directly."
**Common misuse:** Reporting a score as clinically significant without connecting it to the specific real-world function the referral question is actually about.

### Feedback session
The structured meeting where test findings and diagnostic formulation are explained to the client, distinct from and prior to (or alongside) the written report.
**In use:** "The feedback session is where the diagnosis either lands or doesn't — a rushed one undoes weeks of careful assessment."
**Common misuse:** Treating feedback as a formality after "the real work" of testing and scoring, rather than as part of the clinical intervention itself.
