# Vocabulary

### Fluorescein sodium
The dye used in fundus fluorescein angiography (FFA); a small, water-soluble molecule that fluoresces under blue excitation light as it passes through retinal and choroidal vessels. Contains no iodine.
**In use:** "FFA's clear to proceed — her 'iodine allergy' doesn't apply to fluorescein, it's not an iodinated compound."
**Common misuse:** Treating any reported iodine or shellfish allergy as an automatic contraindication to fluorescein angiography specifically, rather than checking which dye is actually iodinated (ICG).

### Indocyanine green (ICG)
A tricarbocyanine dye used for choroidal-circulation imaging (ICGA), formulated with a sodium iodide component and cleared hepatically rather than renally.
**In use:** "Hold the ICG until the doctor's seen this — his iodine history is real contraindication territory for ICG, even though it wasn't for the FFA we already ran."
**Common misuse:** Assuming ICG and fluorescein carry the same contraindication profile because they're both "angiography dyes."

### Signal strength / quality index
A device-reported numeric or categorical score (e.g., Cirrus 0–10, Spectralis Q-band) indicating how much of an OCT scan's segmentation the software could reliably compute from actual signal versus interpolation.
**In use:** "That macular thickness reading isn't real — signal strength was 4, we need a rescan before it goes in the progression series."
**Common misuse:** Reading OCT thickness numbers as findings without checking whether the signal strength that produced them cleared the reliability floor.

### Immersion biometry
An ultrasound axial-length measurement technique using a fluid-filled shell or scleral cup so the probe never directly contacts the cornea, avoiding compression artifact.
**In use:** "Contact reading was 23.1, immersion came back 23.4 — that 0.3 was corneal compression, not anatomy."
**Common misuse:** Treating any axial-length number as technique-agnostic, without recording or checking which method (contact, immersion, optical) produced it.

### Contact (applanation) biometry
An ultrasound axial-length technique with the probe touching the corneal surface directly; faster but prone to underestimating true axial length via corneal indentation.
**In use:** "That's a contact reading — expect it to run short by a couple tenths against immersion or optical."
**Common misuse:** Using it as the default technique for convenience rather than reserving it for cases where immersion or optical biometry genuinely isn't feasible.

### Axial length
The anterior-to-posterior distance of the eye, the single most influential input into intraocular lens (IOL) power calculation.
**In use:** "0.3mm of axial length error moves the IOL power calculation by roughly a diopter — that's not a rounding difference, it's a wrong lens."
**Common misuse:** Treating small fellow-eye discordances as normal biological variation rather than checking for a measurement-technique explanation first.

### Extravasation
Leakage of injected dye out of the vein into surrounding tissue during angiography, rather than passing into the intended vascular circulation.
**In use:** "That bright spot at the IV site isn't a vascular pattern — that's extravasation, not a retinal finding."
**Common misuse:** Interpreting an injection-site hyperfluorescent artifact as part of the angiographic finding rather than recognizing it as a delivery problem.

### Late leakage vs. staining
On fluorescein angiography, "leakage" is progressive extravascular dye accumulation over the study's later frames (active vascular incompetence); "staining" is a fixed tissue or scar uptake that doesn't spread over time.
**In use:** "That's staining from the old scar, not leakage — it hasn't grown from the mid-phase to the late frames."
**Common misuse:** Calling any late-frame hyperfluorescence "leakage" regardless of whether it actually expanded over the sequence.

### Window defect
An area of early hyperfluorescence on FFA from RPE atrophy or thinning that lets underlying choroidal fluorescence show through, with no true leakage or staining over time.
**In use:** "That hyperfluorescence doesn't change shape or intensity across the run — it's a window defect from RPE atrophy, not a vascular lesion."
**Common misuse:** Confusing an unchanging window defect with true leakage because both appear bright on early frames.

### Ultrasound biomicroscopy (UBM)
A very-high-frequency ultrasound technique for imaging the anterior segment (angle, ciliary body) at a depth and detail standard OCT can't reach.
**In use:** "OCT can't see past the iris root here — this needs UBM to rule out a ciliary body mass."
**Common misuse:** Reaching for anterior-segment OCT and reporting "unremarkable" when the actual structure of concern (behind the iris) was never in that modality's reach.

### Wide-field / ultra-widefield imaging
Fundus photography or angiography systems capturing up to roughly 200° of retina in a single image, versus the ~30–50° field of a standard fundus camera.
**In use:** "The peripheral neovascularization is outside what a standard 45° photo would ever have caught — that's exactly what the wide-field capture was for."
**Common misuse:** Capturing the wide-field image but only reviewing and documenting the central, standard-field-equivalent portion of it.

### COMT (Certified Ophthalmic Medical Technologist)
The top tier of JCAHPO's ophthalmic allied-health certification ladder (above COA and COT), scoped to advanced imaging, biometry, procedure assisting, and supervisory responsibilities.
**In use:** "That biometry discordance goes through COMT-level review before it feeds an IOL calculation, not just whoever ran the scan."
**Common misuse:** Treating COMT as simply "more experienced COT" rather than a distinct scope that includes assisting in minor surgery and independent judgment calls a COT's scope doesn't cover.

### Time-out (surgical)
The final pre-procedure verification of patient identity, laterality, procedure, and site performed by the full team immediately before starting, mandated by Joint Commission Universal Protocol.
**In use:** "I read laterality off the signed consent independently before the time-out, so it's a real second check, not an echo of what the surgeon just said."
**Common misuse:** Treating the time-out as satisfied by the surgeon stating the laterality and everyone in the room simply agreeing, without anyone independently having checked the consent themselves beforehand.
