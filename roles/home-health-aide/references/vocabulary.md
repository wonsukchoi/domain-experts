# Home Health Aide — Vocabulary

### Plan of Care (POC) / CMS-485
The physician-ordered document specifying an individual client's authorized services, frequency, and any special instructions or delegation orders — the aide executes it, doesn't write it.
**In use:** "Her POC only lists ADL assistance and medication reminding — there's no delegation for glucose checks, so I don't do one even if she asks."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a generic aide task list rather than the specific, legally binding scope document for that one client.

### Delegation
A written order, typically from an RN, authorizing an unlicensed aide to perform a specific task (like a blood-glucose check) that would otherwise be outside scope — task-specific and client-specific, not a blanket permission.
**In use:** "I don't have a delegation order for fingersticks on this client, so a blood-sugar check isn't something I can do today."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to transfer automatically because the aide performed the same task for a different client, or because a family member gave verbal permission.

### Scope of practice
The legally defined boundary of tasks an aide is certified and permitted to perform, set by state regulation and the employing agency — distinct from what the aide is physically capable of doing.
**In use:** "I can offer her juice for the low blood sugar — that's feeding assistance. I can't check her glucose myself; that's outside my scope here."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a soft guideline that bends under a sympathetic situation, rather than a compliance and licensing boundary.

### EVV (Electronic Visit Verification)
A federally mandated system (Cures Act, Section 12006) that electronically records the type of service, individual receiving it, date, location, and start/end times of each visit.
**In use:** "My EVV check-in shows 7:00 AM at her address — that timestamp is what backs up my visit note if anyone questions the timeline."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a payroll formality rather than the record that establishes exactly when an observation or a call to the nurse occurred.

### Supervisory visit
The periodic in-person (or, under current CMS flexibilities, permitted remote) visit a supervising RN makes to observe the aide's performance and reassess the client — a Medicare Condition of Participation baseline of at least every 14 days for skilled-aide cases.
**In use:** "My supervisory visit is due this week — the RN will observe me doing the transfer and check in on how the client's doing."
**Common misuse:** Confused with a routine clinical visit to the client — its primary purpose is evaluating the aide's performance, not a stand-alone clinical assessment.

### ADL (Activities of Daily Living)
The basic self-care tasks — bathing, dressing, toileting, transferring, feeding, continence — an aide assists with and tracks independence-level changes on.
**In use:** "She went from independent to assist-x1 on transfers this week — that's a functional-change finding, not just a slower morning."
**Common misuse:** Logged as a static status rather than a day-over-day comparison where the change itself is the reportable signal.

### Rule of 15
A hypoglycemia-management heuristic: give 15g of fast-acting carbohydrate, then recheck symptoms in 15 minutes — used at the aide level as a symptom-and-timing response, not a glucose-reading-driven protocol.
**In use:** "I gave her 4 ounces of juice at 7:57 and we're rechecking at 8:12 — that's the 15-and-15 the plan of care calls for."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to require a glucose check to apply — most aides don't have delegation to do the check, so the symptom-based version is the one actually usable.

### Mandatory reporter
A legal designation, applying to HHAs in most states, requiring the reporting of suspected abuse, neglect, or exploitation of a vulnerable adult regardless of the client's wishes.
**In use:** "She asked me not to say anything about the bruising, but I'm a mandatory reporter — I called the agency the same day."
**Common misuse:** Treated as optional or as something the aide can weigh against the client's stated preference — it isn't discretionary once the threshold is met.

### Adult Protective Services (APS)
The state agency that investigates reports of abuse, neglect, or financial exploitation of older or vulnerable adults; aides typically report to their agency, which routes to APS, rather than contacting APS directly.
**In use:** "The agency said they'd file the APS report today based on what I documented."
**Common misuse:** Aide contacts APS directly without going through agency protocol, or assumes the agency's job ends at receiving the report rather than routing it forward.

### Home health agency (Medicare-certified) vs. personal-care/private-duty agency
A Medicare-certified home health agency operates under 42 CFR §484 Conditions of Participation with physician orders and RN oversight; a personal-care or private-duty agency (often funded through Medicaid HCBS waivers or private pay) may have lighter supervisory requirements and a narrower task list.
**In use:** "This client's under the Medicare-certified agency's POC, so the 14-day supervisory visit rule applies — my other client's private-pay, different oversight structure."
**Common misuse:** Assuming the same rules and task authorizations apply across both settings without checking which one governs the specific client.

### Competency evaluation
The federally required assessment (part of the 75-hour minimum training standard under 42 CFR §484.80) verifying an aide can perform each of 12 required skill areas before working unsupervised.
**In use:** "I was checked off on transfers and vital signs during my competency evaluation before I started seeing clients solo."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a one-time formality rather than the baseline the agency can re-verify if a performance concern comes up.

### Isolation gap
A period during which no one — family, aide, or other provider — has confirmed contact with the client, distinct from a single missed visit.
**In use:** "It's been four days since anyone's checked on her besides me — that isolation gap is worth flagging on its own, even though nothing else looks wrong today."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a scheduling inconvenience rather than a standalone risk factor for undetected decline.

### Client refusal (documentation context)
A client's declination of a recommended safety change or care task, which the aide documents rather than overrides, respecting the client's autonomy in their own home.
**In use:** "She declined the grab bar install again this visit — documented, third time, will flag to the RN in case the fall risk needs a different approach."
**Common misuse:** Left undocumented, which erases the record that the recommendation was made and leaves the aide's due diligence unverifiable later.
