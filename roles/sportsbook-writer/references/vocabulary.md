### Vig (vigorish) / juice
The book's built-in commission, expressed as the extra price charged on a bet beyond true even odds — standard −110/−110 pricing on a spread implies roughly 104.8% combined probability, a ~4.8% hold.
**In use:** "We're not moving the number, we're just adjusting the juice to −120/+100 to balance the handle."
**Common misuse:** treated as a fixed, universal number ("the vig is always 10%") rather than something that varies by bet type and can itself be adjusted instead of the line.

### Key number
A final-margin value that occurs disproportionately often in a sport, most importantly 3 and 7 in the NFL (roughly 15% and 8% of games respectively), which is why a half-point move across a key number changes a bet's real risk far more than the same half-point move elsewhere.
**In use:** "Don't move that line off 3 without checking what it does to our teaser exposure."
**Common misuse:** assumed to apply equally across all sports and bet types, when key-number distributions are sport- and scoring-system-specific (irrelevant for a moneyline, different entirely in basketball or soccer).

### Push
A graded result where the final outcome lands exactly on the posted number, resulting in the wager being refunded — no win, no loss, no winnings.
**In use:** "Final was exactly 3, that's a push, refund the $110."
**Common misuse:** confused with a "no action" grade; a push is a specific number landing exactly on the line, while no-action is an unmet bet condition (postponement, scratched required participant).

### No action / no interest
A grade applied when a required condition of the bet wasn't met — a game postponed past the rule's window, a "must start" player who doesn't start — resulting in a full refund regardless of what happened on the field.
**In use:** "Pitcher got scratched pregame, that's no action, refund the moneyline."
**Common misuse:** used interchangeably with "push," which specifically means an exact-number outcome, not an unmet precondition.

### Handle
The total dollar amount wagered on an event or market, as distinct from the book's hold (profit) on that handle.
**In use:** "Handle on this game is $340,000 and we're holding about 4%."
**Common misuse:** confused with revenue or profit — handle is gross money wagered, not what the book keeps.

### Hold
The percentage of handle a book keeps as profit after paying out winners, over a given period or event.
**In use:** "Hold on NFL Sundays runs 6–7% most weeks, higher when favorites cover."
**Common misuse:** treated as a guaranteed per-bet margin; hold is a portfolio-level outcome across many bets, not the vig baked into any single ticket, and can swing hard on a single lopsided result.

### Circled game
An event where the book has capped or removed normal betting limits, typically due to uncertainty (injury news, weather) or a desire to limit exposure while pricing is unreliable.
**In use:** "That game's circled until we get the injury report, limits are $200 max."
**Common misuse:** assumed to mean the game is off the board entirely; a circled game is usually still bettable, just at sharply reduced limits.

### Off the board
An event with no betting currently accepted — either past its cutoff or pulled by the book for another reason (data feed issue, unresolved dispute).
**In use:** "That fight's off the board the second the ring walk starts."
**Common misuse:** used loosely to mean "closing soon"; off the board means no bets are being accepted at all, not that the window is narrowing.

### Layoff (bet)
A wager one book places with another book (or another market) specifically to offset excess one-sided exposure, rather than to win on the merits of the pick.
**In use:** "We're too heavy on the Chiefs, let's lay off $8,000 to bring our net exposure down."
**Common misuse:** confused with a house employee's personal bet; a layoff is a risk-management transaction between books, not a wager placed for expected value.

### Sharp / square (money)
"Sharp" describes professional, information-driven, typically line-moving bettors; "square" describes recreational bettors whose action tends not to predict outcomes.
**In use:** "Line opened at 3, sharp money took it to 2.5 within an hour — that's not square action."
**Common misuse:** assumed to be about bet size alone; a sharp bettor is identified by pattern and market impact, not just a large stake (a large square bet moves nothing predictive).

### Closing line value (CLV)
The difference between the price a bettor got and the line's final closing price before the event — consistently beating the close is the strongest available signal of a bettor's actual skill.
**In use:** "That account's been beating the close by half a point for three months straight, that's why they're limited."
**Common misuse:** treated as equivalent to winning; a bettor can lose a given bet while still showing positive CLV, and CLV (not short-term win rate) is what predicts long-term sharp status.

### Teaser
A multi-leg bet where the bettor moves the point spread or total in their favor on every leg, in exchange for reduced parlay-style odds versus a standard parlay.
**In use:** "Two-team 6-point teaser, moved both sides across the 3 and the 7."
**Common misuse:** assumed to always be a weak bet for the bettor; specific teaser structures crossing key numbers (a "Wong teaser") have historically shown a real, if shrinking, mathematical edge — the pricing, not the bet type itself, determines the edge.

### Round robin
A set of smaller parlays automatically generated from a larger group of picks (e.g., three teams entered as a round robin produces all three possible 2-team parlay combinations), reducing variance versus one large parlay.
**In use:** "Three-team round robin, so that's three separate 2-team parlays at $20 each."
**Common misuse:** confused with a single larger parlay of the same picks; a round robin pays out on partial correctness where a straight parlay of all legs pays nothing unless every leg wins.

### W-2G
The IRS form documenting certain gambling winnings, required for wagering-pool-type payouts (including many sports bets) when winnings are at least $2,000 and at least 300 times the wager under current IRS instructions.
**In use:** "That one clears both the $2,000 and 300x tests, we need a W-2G before payout."
**Common misuse:** treated as triggered by either condition alone — both the dollar threshold and the multiplier threshold must be met simultaneously.

### Excise tax / occupational tax (wagering)
The federal 0.25%-of-wager excise tax on state-authorized sports bets (26 U.S.C. §4401) and the companion $50/year occupational tax (26 U.S.C. §4411) owed by a person engaged in receiving wagers on behalf of an authorized operator — filed via Form 730 and Form 11-C respectively.
**In use:** "Handle tax gets filed monthly on Form 730, that's separate from what shows on a patron's W-2G."
**Common misuse:** confused with the patron-facing W-2G income-reporting rules; the excise/occupational tax is a business-level tax on the operator's wagering handle, not a per-patron winnings report.
