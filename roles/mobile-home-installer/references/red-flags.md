# Red flags

Smell tests a licensed installer catches walking a site or reviewing another crew's completed set-up. Format for each: what it usually means, the first question to ask, and the data to pull before signing off.

## 1. Footing/pier layout matches the manual's default table exactly, but no soil test exists on file

**Usually means:** the crew installed to the Part 3285 1,000 psf default assumption without checking whether the actual site supports it — fine on good soil, a latent settlement problem on fill dirt or a damp low corner.

**First question:** "Where's the soil test, and did anyone walk the low corners of the lot before pouring?"

**Data to pull:** penetrometer or auger-test records per footing zone; site grading photos from before footings went in; any nearby structures (slabs, other homes) with visible cracking that would have flagged soft soil.

## 2. Pier stack over ~36" unsupported with plain stacked concrete block, no engineering note

**Usually means:** the crew improvised a taller foundation on a sloped or uneven lot rather than pulling an engineered footing or reinforced pier detail from the manual — stacked block above the rated height loses lateral stability exactly when wind load needs it most.

**First question:** "What does the manual specify for pier height over the standard cap-block limit, and where's the engineering sign-off for this stack?"

**Data to pull:** the manual's maximum unsupported pier height table; grading survey showing the actual elevation differential across the footprint; any structural engineer letter on file for the tall-pier locations.

## 3. Anchor count looks identical to the installer's last three jobs in the same county

**Usually means:** anchor count was carried over from memory or habit rather than pulled from this home's actual wind-zone data plate and pier spacing — a documented pattern in post-storm damage surveys where under-anchored homes cluster around installers reusing a stale count.

**First question:** "What wind zone is on this home's data plate, and does the anchor count on-site match the manual's table for that zone and this floor plan?"

**Data to pull:** data plate photo; manual's anchor table for the stated wind zone; as-built anchor location diagram from this specific job.

## 4. Ground anchors installed but never pull- or torque-tested

**Usually means:** anchors look set visually but may be under-torqued in soil weaker than assumed — a visually "installed" anchor and a load-rated anchor are not the same thing, especially in loose, sandy, or wet soil.

**First question:** "What were the torque or pull-test readings on the anchors, and were any below the rated working load?"

**Data to pull:** field torque-test log per anchor; soil classification at each anchor location; any anchors flagged for re-drive, relocation, or helical replacement.

## 5. Marriage line out of level by more than 1/4" or sections out of square by more than 1/2" diagonal, discovered after interior finish work started

**Usually means:** the crew leveled individual rooms before truing the marriage line, or rushed the mate-line setting to hit a schedule date — the error compounds into doors that won't latch, drywall seam cracking, and a roof cap that never sits flush.

**First question:** "What was the marriage-line tolerance measurement taken at set time, and was it verified before or after trim/finish work began?"

**Data to pull:** set-day tolerance measurements (should be a recorded number, not "looked level"); callback/warranty log for door, trim, or drywall issues at the mate line.

## 6. Unsealed or untested HVAC/plumbing crossover joints at the marriage wall

**Usually means:** crossover work was completed but not pressure- or leak-tested before drywall closed over it — one of the most common sources of post-move-in comfort complaints (duct leakage) and hidden water damage (plumbing crossover leaks inside a closed marriage wall cavity).

**First question:** "Do we have a pressure test or leak-check record for the marriage-wall crossovers, and when was it done relative to closing the wall?"

**Data to pull:** crossover test log per system; date of drywall/finish close-in vs. date of crossover test (finish work after test, never before).

## 7. Footing depth matches a generic county frost-line number, not the manual's site-specific requirement

**Usually means:** the crew used a single county-wide frost-depth figure instead of checking whether the manual or local jurisdiction requires a deeper footing for this specific soil/climate combination — frost heave lifts an under-depth footing unevenly, which shows up as doors sticking seasonally, not immediately.

**First question:** "What frost depth does the manual specify for this climate zone, and does it match what's actually in the ground?"

**Data to pull:** manual's frost-depth table; local building department's frost-line requirement; as-built footing depth measurements (not just the spec sheet).

## 8. Skirting/perimeter enclosure skipped or deferred "until later" at final inspection

**Usually means:** schedule pressure pushed the perimeter close-in past the inspection date — the enclosure is part of the manufacturer's moisture and pest barrier and often part of the state's thermal envelope requirement, not a cosmetic finish item.

**First question:** "Why is the perimeter open at final inspection, and what's actually protecting the belly board and ductwork underneath right now?"

**Data to pull:** manual's perimeter enclosure requirement and timeline; state inspection checklist item for skirting; photos of belly-board/ductwork condition if left exposed through a weather event.

## 9. Homeowner reports a warranty claim denied after a DIY foundation "repair"

**Usually means:** someone (often the homeowner or a general handyman) adjusted a pier or added a shim after installer sign-off, without documentation — manufacturers routinely deny structural claims once the as-built foundation no longer matches the certified install record.

**First question:** "What does the original installation certification show for this pier location, and what's actually there now?"

**Data to pull:** original installer's certification and photos; any permit or change record for post-install foundation work; current field measurement at the disputed location.
