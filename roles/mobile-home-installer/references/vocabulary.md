# Vocabulary

Terms generalists conflate or use loosely that a licensed installer keeps sharply separate. Each: definition, how a practitioner actually uses it in a sentence, and the common misuse to avoid.

## HUD Code vs. installation standard

**HUD Code** (24 CFR Part 3280) governs how the home is *built* at the factory — materials, structural design, wind/roof-load zone assignment stamped on the data plate. The **installation standard** (24 CFR Part 3285 federally, or a state's own HUD-approved standard) governs how it's *set* on-site — piers, footings, anchoring, crossovers. They're different regulatory documents with different enforcement bodies.

*Practitioner usage:* "The home passed HUD Code at the factory — that's not in question. What we're inspecting is whether it was installed to Part 3285, and right now it isn't."

*Common misuse:* saying a home is "up to code" as a single blanket statement when construction and installation are separately certified, and a factory-compliant home can still fail installation inspection.

## Wind zone vs. roof load zone

**Wind zone** (I, II, or III) rates the home's structural design and required anchoring against wind pressure for where it will be sited. **Roof load zone** (I, II, or III, separately numbered) rates the roof structure against snow/live load for the climate. A home can be Wind Zone II and Roof Load Zone I simultaneously — they're independent ratings on the same data plate.

*Practitioner usage:* "Don't just check the wind zone box on the plate — this home's going to a snow-load county, confirm the roof zone matches too."

*Common misuse:* treating "Zone II" as a single combined rating and assuming a high wind zone implies a high roof-load zone, or vice versa.

## Soil bearing capacity vs. soil-bearing test

**Soil bearing capacity** is the load per unit area (psf) the ground can support without excessive settlement — a property of the actual site. The **soil-bearing test** (penetrometer, auger boring, or geotechnical report) is how that number gets measured. The federal 1,000 psf figure in Part 3285 is a *default assumption used absent a test*, not a measured value.

*Practitioner usage:* "We're not guessing 1,000 psf on this lot — it's fill dirt, get an auger test before I size the footings."

*Common misuse:* citing "1,000 psf" as if it were the soil's actual measured capacity rather than a fallback default that specifically exists for when no test was done.

## Pier vs. footing vs. pad

A **pier** is the vertical support column (steel, concrete block stack, or manufactured pier system) under the chassis beam. The **footing** is the load-spreading foundation element the pier sits on. The **pad** is the specific footing material/size (e.g., a precast concrete pad) at that location. "The footing failed" and "the pier failed" describe different failure modes — a footing failure is usually a sizing/soil problem, a pier failure is usually a height/lateral-stability problem.

*Practitioner usage:* "The pier didn't buckle, the footing sank — that's a bearing-capacity problem, not a pier-height problem, so re-engineering the stack won't fix it."

*Common misuse:* using "footing" and "pier" interchangeably in a defect report, which sends the fix in the wrong direction.

## Ground anchor vs. tie-down vs. stabilizer

A **ground anchor** is the device driven/augered into the soil that provides the anchor point. A **tie-down** (or "tie") is the strap or cable connecting the anchor to the home's frame, run diagonally or vertically depending on load direction. A **stabilizer** (or stabilizer plate/device) manages lateral sway at the pier without necessarily resisting uplift the way a tie-down does. All three work together; none substitutes for another.

*Practitioner usage:* "The anchors are rated fine, but half the tie-down straps are frayed — that's a tie-down replacement, not an anchor problem."

*Common misuse:* calling the whole anchoring system "the tie-downs" and skipping a distinct check on anchor holding capacity, since a good strap on an under-torqued anchor still fails.

## Marriage line vs. crossover

The **marriage line** (or "mate line") is the physical joint where two home sections meet and get structurally fastened together. The **crossover** is the utility connection (electrical, HVAC, plumbing, gas) that spans that joint to unify systems that were split at the factory for shipping. The marriage line is a structural concept; the crossover is a systems concept — a home can have a perfect marriage-line structural connection and a leaking crossover, or vice versa.

*Practitioner usage:* "Marriage line's within tolerance and bolted down — now let's pressure-test the HVAC crossover before we close the wall."

*Common misuse:* referring to "closing the marriage line" as if it's one step, when it's actually a sequence (structural connection, then each utility crossover, then exterior/interior finish) and skipping the order creates rework.

## Data plate vs. HUD certification label

The **data plate** is the paper document inside the home (typically in a cabinet or utility closet) listing wind zone, roof load zone, design loads, and manufacturer specs for that specific serial number. The **HUD certification label** (the red/silver metal tag on the home's exterior) certifies factory compliance with the HUD Code but doesn't carry the same level of site-specific design detail as the data plate.

*Practitioner usage:* "The exterior label just confirms it's HUD-certified — pull the data plate inside for the actual wind zone and load numbers before you design the foundation."

*Common misuse:* treating the exterior metal label as sufficient documentation for foundation/anchoring design and never locating the interior data plate.

## Frost line vs. footing depth

The **frost line** is the maximum depth to which the ground is expected to freeze in a given climate — a geological/climate fact, independent of any home. **Footing depth** is the installer's chosen depth for the foundation, which must sit at or below the frost line to avoid frost heave, but is also driven by soil-bearing requirements independently of frost. A footing can be deep enough for frost but still undersized for bearing capacity, or vice versa.

*Practitioner usage:* "We're below frost line at 30 inches, that part's fine — the problem is the pad's too small for what this soil can carry at that depth."

*Common misuse:* treating "we met the frost depth" as equivalent to "the footing is adequate," conflating two independent requirements that happen to both be measured in inches of depth.

## Installer license vs. installation certification

The **installer license** is the state-issued credential authorizing a person or company to perform manufactured home installations (required in most, not all, states — requirements and reciprocity vary by state program). The **installation certification** is the job-specific document/sign-off (often required by the state and referenced in warranty disputes) attesting that a particular home was installed to standard. A licensed installer can still produce an installation that fails certification if the specific job wasn't done to spec.

*Practitioner usage:* "Being licensed in this state doesn't mean this job passes — where's the signed installation certification for this specific address?"

*Common misuse:* treating "the installer is licensed" as proof the installation itself was compliant, when licensing is a credential about the person/company and certification is a record about the specific job.
