# Museum Conservator — Vocabulary

### Preventive conservation
Environmental, handling, and storage measures that slow deterioration without touching the object directly — climate control, IPM, proper mounts, dark storage rotation.
**In use:** "We can hold this textile stable indefinitely with preventive conservation alone — rotation off display and a buffered case — without any interventive treatment."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a lesser or optional step before "real" conservation work, when in practice it prevents more total damage across an object's lifetime than any single treatment.

### Interventive (remedial) conservation
Direct, hands-on treatment of an object to stabilize active deterioration — consolidation, cleaning, structural repair.
**In use:** "The flaking is active, so this moves from monitoring into interventive conservation — we need to consolidate before the next handling event."
**Common misuse:** Used interchangeably with "restoration," which specifically means returning appearance/function, a narrower and more aesthetically-driven goal than stabilization.

### Restoration
Treatment aimed at returning an object to a known or assumed earlier appearance or function, distinct from stabilization.
**In use:** "The client is asking for restoration of the missing inlay, but our proposal is stabilization only — we don't have documentation of the original pattern to restore accurately."
**Common misuse:** Requested by non-conservators as a synonym for "fix it," without distinguishing whether the goal is structural stability (conservation) or appearance (restoration), which have different risk profiles.

### Minimal intervention
The principle of doing the least treatment necessary to stabilize an object, not the least treatment period.
**In use:** "Minimal intervention here means consolidating the flaking paint, not inpainting the losses — the losses aren't structurally at risk."
**Common misuse:** Read as "do nothing" or "do the cheapest option," when it actually means matching the scope of treatment to the actual conservation need, which can still be extensive if the need is extensive.

### Reversibility
The property of a treatment material or method being removable without damaging the original object.
**In use:** "Paraloid B-72 is our default consolidant here because it's soluble in solvents the original media won't react to — full reversibility."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a binary, permanent guarantee; some materials marketed as reversible when introduced degrade into practically irreversible states after years of aging (cross-linking, embrittlement), so claims need re-verification against current literature, not the original product literature.

### Inherent vice
Deterioration caused by the object's own original materials or construction, independent of environment or handling.
**In use:** "The cellulose acetate film base is degrading from inherent vice — vinegar syndrome — regardless of how well we control the vault's climate."
**Common misuse:** Used as a catch-all excuse for any deterioration, when much damage is actually environmental or handling-caused and preventable; inherent vice specifically means the object was going to degrade this way from its own chemistry.

### Condition report
The point-in-time documented assessment of an object's physical state, distinct from a treatment report.
**In use:** "Pull the last condition report before we assess — we need to know if this damage is new or already noted."
**Common misuse:** Conflated with a treatment report; a condition report documents state without prescribing or recording action, while a treatment report documents what was actually done.

### Accession vs. loan
An accessioned object is permanently owned by the institution; a loan is temporary custody, in or out, with its own condition-report pair and facility requirements.
**In use:** "This is an incoming loan, not an accession — the condition report and facility report both have to clear before it comes off the truck."
**Common misuse:** Registrarial/legal distinction assumed to be conservation-irrelevant, when loan status changes documentation obligations, insurance nail-to-nail coverage, and often environmental/handling requirements set by the lender.

### Integrated Pest Management (IPM)
A monitoring-and-threshold-based pest control approach (trapping, identification, targeted response) rather than routine chemical treatment.
**In use:** "IPM trap data is what triggers a treatment decision here, not a calendar — we don't spray on a schedule."
**Common misuse:** Reduced to "put out sticky traps," skipping the identification and threshold-comparison steps that actually make it a management system rather than passive monitoring.

### Class AA/A/B/C/D environmental control (ASHRAE)
ASHRAE's tiered climate-control specifications for museums, from tightest (Class AA, ±2°C/±5% RH) to loosest (Class D, no strict control, protection from extremes only).
**In use:** "This historic house can realistically hold Class B, not Class AA — we set the treatment and loan expectations against what the building can actually deliver."
**Common misuse:** Assumed every collection needs Class AA; the class should match the collection's material sensitivity and the building's real capability, and an honest lower class beats an unmet aspirational one.

### Lux-hour budget
A cumulative light-exposure allowance (lux level x hours displayed) for light-sensitive materials, used instead of a flat lux ceiling to manage exposure over a year.
**In use:** "This watercolor has 50,000 lux-hours/year allotted — at current gallery levels it needs to come off display in April, not stay up for the whole run."
**Common misuse:** Confused with an instantaneous lux limit alone; two objects lit at the identical lux level can have very different remaining safe display time depending on hours already accumulated that year.

### Facility report (General Facility Report / Standard Facility Report)
A standardized document describing a venue's environmental control, security, fire suppression, and handling capabilities, used to evaluate loan requests.
- **In use:** "Their facility report shows no fire suppression in the gallery — that's a lending condition we can't waive for this object."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a formality to file rather than a substantive gating document; a facility report gap should change the loan decision or add binding conditions, not just get filed.

### Anoxic treatment
Pest eradication or long-term storage method that removes oxygen (via CO2, nitrogen, or oxygen-scavenger enclosure) to kill insects across all life stages without chemicals or temperature extremes.
**In use:** "We used an oxygen-scavenger bag instead of freezing for this object because the composite materials (wood plus ivory inlay) don't tolerate the freeze/thaw cycle well."
**Common misuse:** Assumed interchangeable with low-temperature treatment in all cases; anoxic treatment takes longer (often 2–3 weeks vs. days) and isn't appropriate for every material combination, so the choice between them is itself a material-based decision.
