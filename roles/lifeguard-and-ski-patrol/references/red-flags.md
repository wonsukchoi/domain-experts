# Red flags

Smell tests a senior lifeguard or ski patroller checks before they turn into an incident report. Format for each: what it usually means, the first question to ask, and the data to pull.

## 1. Guard stand or patrol post uncovered for more than 2 minutes during peak load

**Usually means:** rotation schedule broken, or staffing was cut below the count the 10/20 zone design assumes.

**First question:** "Who was supposed to relieve this post, and what broke in the rotation?"

**Data to pull:** shift rotation log, headcount vs. bather/skier-visit load for the hour, whether the gap was noticed by radio or only in review.

## 2. Rescue count at one location spikes to 3x the seasonal weekly average

**Usually means:** a sandbar, current channel, or slope condition has shifted and the hazard is no longer where signage and staffing assume it is.

**First question:** "Are these rescues clustering at the same buoy/run marker, and has anything changed there physically?"

**Data to pull:** rescue log with location tags, tide/surf report or recent snowpack/weather data, date signage or rope lines were last checked against current conditions.

## 3. Avalanche danger rated Considerable (3) or higher with no logged closure or control work

**Usually means:** the morning forecast wasn't acted on, or a verbal override happened without documentation.

**First question:** "Show me the control log and the closure map for this rating level — where do they disagree?"

**Data to pull:** morning avalanche forecast, control route log, closure/rope-line map, patrol director sign-off record.

## 4. Backboard/spinal motion restriction used on fewer than expected trauma calls in a season

**Usually means:** either genuinely low-risk terrain/water conditions, or clearance criteria are being skipped rather than applied — under-immobilizing a real spinal risk is a liability and a patient-safety problem, not efficiency.

**First question:** "Walk me through the clearance criteria on the last three trauma calls that didn't get boarded."

**Data to pull:** incident reports for trauma calls, documented clearance-criteria checklist per call (see `references/playbook.md`), mechanism description for each.

## 5. Same swimmer or skier rescued more than twice in one day

**Usually means:** the hazard isn't adequately signed or the person hasn't been warned off directly — a repeat rescue at the same spot is a prevention failure, not bad luck.

**First question:** "Was this person warned or moved after the first rescue, and if not, why not?"

**Data to pull:** rescue log cross-referenced by name/description, warnings-issued log, signage/rope-line photos from that day.

## 6. AED pads or battery past expiration, discovered during an actual call rather than a check

**Usually means:** the daily equipment check is being signed off without being performed.

**First question:** "When was this AED last function-tested, and by whom?"

**Data to pull:** equipment check log with signatures, AED's internal self-test history if the unit logs it.

## 7. Toboggan or water-rescue response time exceeds the site's stated benchmark (e.g., >30 sec whistle-to-water-entry, >5 min flag-to-toboggan-on-scene)

**Usually means:** post spacing no longer matches guest volume/terrain, or equipment isn't staged where the EAP says it is.

**First question:** "Where exactly was the responding guard/patroller relative to the incident, and where does the EAP say they should have been?"

**Data to pull:** EAP staging map, radio timestamps for the actual response, current post/route assignments.

## 8. Guest complaints about closures or warnings rise sharply with no corresponding rise in hazard rating

**Usually means:** either overcorrection after a prior incident (closures well past what conditions justify), or communication of the reason is missing, not the closure itself.

**First question:** "Does each active closure trace to a specific, current hazard rating or observation, or is one of them left over from last week's incident?"

**Data to pull:** closure log with justification field, current vs. historical danger ratings/current readings, guest complaint log with dates.

## 9. Companion avalanche rescue exceeds 15 minutes from burial to airway access on a known single burial

**Usually means:** search-strip width was too wide for real transceiver range, the searcher followed a straight bearing instead of the flux line, or shoveling went straight down instead of strategic/horizontal.

**First question:** "Walk the timeline: when did signal search start, when did probe strike happen, when did the shovel start moving snow?"

**Data to pull:** rescue timeline reconstruction, transceiver model/rated range, burial depth, shovel technique used (see `references/playbook.md` companion-rescue protocol).

## 10. A certification lapse is discovered only after an incident, not during a routine audit

**Usually means:** certification tracking is informal (a binder, a memory) rather than a checked roster.

**First question:** "When was the last time every active staff certification was cross-checked against the roster, independent of the incident?"

**Data to pull:** certification expiration dates for all active staff, date of last roster audit, whether the lapsed individual was on shift and in what role.
