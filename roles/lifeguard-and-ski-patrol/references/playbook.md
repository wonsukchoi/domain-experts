# Playbook

Filled operational templates a guard or patroller actually runs. Adapt the numbers to the specific site; keep the structure.

## 1. Emergency Action Plan (EAP) — filled example, mid-size guarded lake beach

An EAP that can't answer these fields in under a minute during a drill isn't ready.

```
SITE: Cedar Lake Beach, 400m guarded shoreline, 2 towers + 1 roving guard
HAZARDS RANKED: (1) drop-off at 25m from shore, 1.8m→4m sudden depth change,
  (2) afternoon thermocline-driven current near the swim raft, (3) thunderstorm
  cells (May–Sept avg. 2/week regional)

SIGNAL CODES: 3 long whistle blasts = emergency, all guards respond;
  1 long blast = clear the water (storm); arm raised straight up = guard
  requesting backup, stay at post.

BACKUP CHAIN: Tower guard spots → radios roving guard (primary backup) →
  roving guard radios shift supervisor → supervisor calls 911 if not already
  called. Any guard may call 911 directly; do not wait for the chain.

EQUIPMENT STAGED: rescue tube + fins at each tower; backboard, O2, AED at
  supervisor stand (max 45m from any tower — inside a 20-second retrieval
  jog); spinal kit in first-aid room.

ROLES ON ACTIVATION:
  1st guard on scene: victim contact, primary care
  2nd guard: crowd control + clears the immediate area
  3rd/supervisor: EMS liaison, incident report starts on activation, not after

POST-INCIDENT: Debrief within 24h; incident report filed same shift;
  hazard reassessed (does staffing/signage need to change) before next open.

DRILL CADENCE: Full EAP drill at season open, then unannounced monthly;
  average acceptable activation-to-first-contact time: under 30 seconds
  from whistle to guard entering water.
```

## 2. Avalanche companion rescue — protocol with the survival curve

**Burial survival curve** (Falk, Brugger & Adler-Kastner, 1994; as presented in Tremper, *Staying Alive in Avalanche Terrain*):

| Time buried | Approx. survival probability |
|---|---|
| 0–15 min | ~92% |
| 15–35 min | falls sharply to ~30% (asphyxia phase) |
| 35–90 min | plateaus around 25–30% |
| 90 min+ | ~20–25%, dependent on air pocket |

The 15-minute mark is the operational target, not a suggestion — organized (professional) rescue teams rarely arrive inside 30–45 minutes from most backcountry terrain, so **companion rescue by the group already on scene is what determines survival**, not the incoming patrol response.

**Filled scenario:** two-person party, one buried after a slab release; burial depth estimated 1.2m; last-seen point (LSP) marked at a rock band 30m upslope from the debris toe.

1. **Scene safety, 0:00–0:30.** Surviving partner confirms no one else is exposed to a secondary release; posts a visual watch on the start zone before moving onto the debris. All transceivers switched to Search.
2. **Signal search, 0:30–3:30.** Sweep in a strip pattern no wider than the transceiver's reliable real-world range — conservatively ~20m to each side (a 40m-wide strip), even though marketed ranges run 40–60m, because multiple-burial interference and real terrain cut effective range roughly in half. Distance readout drops steadily; searcher follows the flux line, not a straight compass bearing.
3. **Pinpoint search, 3:30–4:30.** Within ~3m of the reading, lower the transceiver to the snow surface and grid-search in a cross pattern to find the minimum-distance point.
4. **Probe, 4:30–5:30.** Probe line at the marked minimum point, spiral outward at 25cm spacing (spiral search) if the first probe strike misses. First strike confirms burial depth for shovel planning.
5. **Strategic shoveling, 5:30–10:00 (depth-dependent).** For a 1.2m burial, dig from ~1.5x the burial depth downhill of the probe strike, moving snow horizontally rather than straight down — straight-down digging on deep burials routinely doubles excavation time.
6. **Airway and extrication, 10:00–12:00.** Clear the airway first, on scene, before full extrication if burial depth and snow density make full dig-out slower than airway access alone.

**Reconciling total:** 0:00 to first airway access at roughly 10–12 minutes for a well-drilled two-person companion rescue on a single, known burial — inside the 15-minute survival-curve threshold with a 3–5 minute margin. A search that misses the strip width, walks a straight bearing instead of the flux line, or shovels straight down instead of strategically routinely pushes total time past 15 minutes on the same burial depth.

## 3. Spinal motion restriction — clearance criteria (filled)

Applied at OEC/EMR scope before deciding whether a water or trauma patient gets full spinal motion restriction:

```
MECHANISM SUGGESTS RISK? (diving into shallow/unknown water, head-first
  fall, high-speed collision, unwitnessed mechanism) → if NO, no SMR needed.
  If YES, check clearance criteria:

  ALERT (GCS 15, oriented)                    Y / N
  NO midline neck/back tenderness on palpation  Y / N
  NO distracting injury (obvious fracture,
    significant pain elsewhere)                Y / N
  NO intoxication (alcohol/drugs, by
    behavior or reported)                       Y / N
  NO focal neuro deficit (grip strength,
    foot push, sensation, symmetric)            Y / N

ALL FIVE "N/A for restriction" (i.e., all clear) → SMR not required,
  monitor and reassess.
ANY "Y" on a risk item → full spinal motion restriction: manual
  stabilization first, then backboard/head immobilizer, transport.
```

Reassess after any status change (patient becomes less alert, new pain reported) — clearance is a point-in-time call, not a one-time decision for the rest of the incident.

## 4. Pre-shift / pre-season equipment check (filled checklist)

**Daily, aquatic:**
- Rescue tube line: no fraying, clip functions, buoyancy intact
- Fins: no cracks at strap mount
- AED: pad expiration date current, battery indicator green, pads sealed
- Radio: charged, on assigned channel, test call to supervisor completed

**Daily, avalanche/ski patrol:**
- Transceiver: self-test passed, batteries >50% (never start a shift under 50%), send-mode confirmed on a second unit
- Probe: sections lock, no bent segments
- Shovel: blade attachment secure, handle extends fully
- Toboggan: brake/tail rope functional, litter straps present

**Seasonal, both:**
- Full EAP drill completed and timed against the 30-second (aquatic) / 12-minute (companion rescue) benchmarks above
- All staff certifications current — cross-checked against the roster, not assumed
