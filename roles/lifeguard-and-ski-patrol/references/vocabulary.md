# Vocabulary

Terms generalists blur together that guards and patrollers keep sharply separate.

## Active vs. passive drowning victim

**Active** victims match the Instinctive Drowning Response — vertical, silent, arms pressing down, no supporting kick, 20–60 seconds of surface time. **Passive** victims are already unresponsive/submerged, found rather than spotted struggling.

**In use:** "That's an active victim on the sandbar edge — quiet, vertical, no kick — prioritize over the one who's yelling."

**Common misuse:** assuming "active" means loud/thrashing. In lifeguard usage it describes the physiological drowning response, which is nearly silent — the opposite of what the word suggests to a layperson.

## Reach, throw, row, go

The escalation order for rescue method by rescuer exposure, lowest first: reaching assist (arm, pole, shepherd's crook), throw (rescue can, throw bag), row (boat, paddleboard), go (swim entry).

**In use:** "We had a throw bag in range — no reason he went in the water for that."

**Common misuse:** treating "go" (swimming in) as the default competent response rather than the last-resort tier; skipping straight to it is a common overcorrection by inexperienced guards trying to look decisive.

## 10/20 protection standard

Ellis & Associates' 1991 standard: a lifeguard's zone must be scannable in 10 seconds and reachable (victim contact) in 20 seconds.

**In use:** "That zone hasn't been 10/20-compliant since they cut a tower — it's a 200-meter stretch now."

**Common misuse:** treating 10/20 as a scanning technique alone rather than a zone-design constraint; the number drives how big a zone can be, not just how a guard should look around.

## Duty to act vs. Good Samaritan protection

**Duty to act** applies to a guard or patroller on shift, in role — they are legally and organizationally obligated to respond. **Good Samaritan** protections apply to a bystander (or an off-duty guard) who chooses to help and are shielded from liability for reasonable, good-faith aid.

**In use:** "Off duty, I'm a Good Samaritan if I help. On duty, I don't have that choice — it's duty to act."

**Common misuse:** assuming Good Samaritan protection covers on-duty staff; it generally doesn't, because the obligation to respond removes the "voluntary bystander" premise the protection is built on.

## Scope of practice (OEC/EMR level)

The defined set of interventions a certification authorizes — Outdoor Emergency Care and most lifeguard medical training sit roughly at Emergency Medical Responder level: airway, bleeding control, splinting, spinal motion restriction, AED, oxygen in some jurisdictions — not paramedic-level interventions (IV, advanced airway, medications).

**In use:** "We stabilize and package for transport — that's our scope. Anything past that waits for EMS."

**Common misuse:** treating "certified" as a blanket license to do whatever seems necessary in the moment, rather than a specific, bounded skill set.

## Rip current vs. "riptide"

A **rip current** is a narrow, fast-moving channel of water flowing away from shore, formed by wave energy funneling through a break in a sandbar. A **tide** is the much slower rise/fall driven by lunar/solar gravity; "riptide" conflates the two and isn't the correct mechanism.

**In use:** "That's a rip current at buoy 6, not a tide effect — it'll be there at low and high tide both."

**Common misuse:** calling any strong nearshore current a "riptide," which misdirects public warnings toward tide tables instead of the actual visual cues (channel of choppy, discolored, or foam-streaked water).

## Companion rescue vs. organized/professional rescue

**Companion rescue** is performed by the group present with the buried person, starting within seconds. **Organized rescue** is the incoming professional/patrol response, which on most backcountry terrain arrives well past the 15-minute survival threshold.

**In use:** "By the time organized rescue gets here it's a recovery, not a rescue — companion response is what saves this person."

**Common misuse:** treating a 911 call or radio call for help as the rescue action itself, rather than the parallel step alongside immediate companion search.

## North American Public Avalanche Danger Scale ratings

A 5-level scale (Low, Moderate, Considerable, High, Extreme) describing avalanche likelihood and consequence, not a simple "safe/unsafe" binary — "Considerable" already means natural avalanches possible and human-triggered avalanches likely on specific terrain features.

**In use:** "Considerable isn't a caution word here — it's the rating where most avalanche fatalities in North America actually happen, because people read it as 'medium.'"

**Common misuse:** treating "Considerable" as a mild, in-between rating; historically it accounts for a large share of recreational avalanche deaths precisely because it doesn't sound as alarming as "High."

## Spinal motion restriction vs. "backboarding"

**Spinal motion restriction (SMR)** is the current, criteria-based practice of limiting spinal movement using whatever combination of manual stabilization, cervical collar, and/or board is appropriate. "Backboarding" colloquially implies automatic, full immobilization on a rigid board for any mechanism, which current EMS/OEC protocol has moved away from as a default.

**In use:** "He clears all five SMR criteria — no board, just monitor him."

**Common misuse:** assuming any trauma call automatically means a backboard; modern protocol applies clearance criteria first (see `references/playbook.md`).

## Cold shock vs. hypothermia

**Cold shock** is the immediate 0–3 minute gasp/hyperventilation response to sudden cold immersion, the phase that actually drowns most cold-water victims. **Hypothermia** (core temperature drop) takes far longer — often 30+ minutes to become dangerous — and is a distinct, later mechanism.

**In use:** "He didn't drown from hypothermia — cold shock hit in the first ten seconds and he inhaled water gasping."

**Common misuse:** attributing all cold-water deaths to hypothermia; most happen in the cold shock or swim failure phase, long before core temperature drops meaningfully (see the 1‑10‑1 principle).

## Multiple casualty incident (MCI) triage vs. single-victim priority

**MCI triage** sorts many simultaneous patients by survivability and resource allocation (often a formal system like START). **Single-victim priority** (e.g., IDR-based prioritization between two swimmers) is a simpler judgment about who has less time, not a formal triage system.

**In use:** "Two swimmers isn't an MCI — that's just IDR priority. If we'd had six, we'd be triaging."

**Common misuse:** invoking "triage" for a two- or three-person incident, which imports MCI-scale process where it slows the response down instead of helping.

## Terrain closure vs. "the trail is marked closed"

A **terrain closure** is an active hazard-management action tied to a current avalanche or condition assessment, re-evaluated each shift. A rope left up from a prior day without reassessment is stale control, not a current closure.

**In use:** "That rope's from Tuesday's storm cycle — reassess it before assuming it still reflects today's rating."

**Common misuse:** treating a closure as permanent rather than a decision re-made against current conditions each operating day.
