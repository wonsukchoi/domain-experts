# Vocabulary

Terms generalists conflate that a brake/signal/switch operator keeps sharply separate, because the difference changes what was actually checked or whether a securement or move was actually verified.

## Sufficient hand brakes (vs. "some hand brakes")

Under 49 CFR 218.99, the hand-brake count applied to a standing cut must be enough to hold it without any reliance on the air brake system — a specific, computed number tied to grade and tonnage, not a general sense that brakes are on.

**In use:** "Two brakes isn't 'some hand brakes are on' territory here — at this grade the table calls for three, and until we've got three tested, it isn't secured."

**Common misuse:** treating any nonzero hand-brake count as "secured," when the rule requires a specific sufficient count for the actual grade and tonnage, tested with air released.

## Effectiveness test

The step of releasing all air brakes (automatic and independent) after applying hand brakes and observing the cut for a specified interval to confirm it doesn't move — the only way to verify a securement against its actual failure condition (air gone).

**In use:** "We're not walking away until we've run the effectiveness test — air off, five minutes, no creep."

**Common misuse:** treating a cut as tested because it "hasn't moved" while air brakes are still charged, which doesn't test the condition the rule exists to cover.

## Reliance on air brakes (prohibited practice)

Counting on the train's or cut's air brake system to hold part of the required securement force, leaving the hand-brake count alone insufficient to hold the equipment on its own. This is the specific failure pattern the post-Lac-Mégantic amendments to 49 CFR 218.99 were written to close.

**In use:** "We can't count the air brakes toward this — if the compressor shuts down overnight, the hand brakes are all that's left holding it."

**Common misuse:** assuming a locomotive left running with air brakes charged makes a marginal hand-brake count acceptable, when the rule requires sufficiency from hand brakes alone.

## Split-point derail vs. hinged/portable derail

**Split-point derail** is a permanently installed device operated like a switch, with points that route a wheel off the rail. **Hinged/portable derail** is a device moved into position on top of the rail and locked, used where a fixed installation isn't warranted.

**In use:** "This spur's got a split-point derail built in — the industry track down the way just uses a portable one we set and lock each time."

**Common misuse:** treating either type as self-verifying once installed, when both require a direct position/lock check before every move that depends on them.

## Switch lining vs. switch locking

**Lining** a switch means throwing the points to the position (normal or reverse) a move requires. **Locking** means securing that position with the stand's hasp and padlock (or a spring mechanism seating) so it can't shift. A switch can be lined without being locked, and locked without the points being fully seated.

**In use:** "It's lined for the siding, but I haven't locked it yet — don't send anything over it until the hasp's closed."

**Common misuse:** treating "lined" and "locked" as the same verification step, when a lined-but-unlocked switch can shift under a passing move or vibration.

## Point-to-stock-rail gap

The physical clearance between a switch's movable point rail and the fixed stock rail it closes against; 49 CFR 213.135 sets a maximum tolerance by track class. A gap within the target's read but outside this tolerance is a defect the target doesn't reveal.

**In use:** "Target says we're lined, but there's a gap on the points I can get a hand in — that's not closing, we're not sending anything over it."

**Common misuse:** relying on the target or lamp reading correctly as proof the points are seated, without a physical check of the gap.

## Locked normal

The default, secured state a switch or derail is left in when not actively needed for a move — locked in the position that protects the main or through track, restored immediately after any move that required a different position.

**In use:** "Soon as this cut clears the derail, we're putting it back locked normal — not leaving it off till the end of the shift."

**Common misuse:** treating "locked normal" as a one-time setup rather than a state restored after every single deviation from it.

## Grade percentage

The rise or fall of a track segment expressed as a percentage (feet of elevation change per 100 feet of track), the primary variable — along with tonnage — that determines the required hand-brake count for a standing cut.

**In use:** "This siding's 1.2% descending toward the main — that's a different band on the table than the flat end of the yard."

**Common misuse:** estimating grade by how a track "feels" or looks rather than pulling the actual figure from the track profile or employee timetable.

## Trailing tonnage

The total weight of the cars behind the point being secured or moved, the other variable (with grade) driving the hand-brake calculation — distinct from car count, since cars of different lading weigh very differently.

**In use:** "Don't count cars for this calc — twelve loaded tank cars is 1,560 tons, not the same trailing tonnage as twelve empties."

**Common misuse:** using car count as a proxy for tonnage when computing hand-brake sufficiency, which produces the wrong answer whenever the cut isn't uniformly loaded.

## Point protection

Continuous radio or hand-signal contact between the crew member at the point of movement (the leading end of a shove) and the engineer, giving the engineer the only information they have about what's ahead on a move they can't see directly.

**In use:** "I'm on point protection for this shove — lose me on the radio and you stop, you don't finish on the last count I gave you."

**Common misuse:** treating intermittent or assumed contact as equivalent to continuous point protection, when the standard is to stop immediately on any loss of contact.

## Runaway

Equipment moving under uncontrolled momentum, typically down a grade, after securement (hand brakes, air brakes, or both) has failed to hold it — the specific outcome the hand-brake sufficiency calculation and effectiveness test exist to prevent.

**In use:** "That's exactly the runaway scenario the grade table is built to prevent — insufficient hand brakes on a descending grade with the air not holding."

**Common misuse:** using "runaway" loosely for any unplanned car movement, when the term specifically describes a securement failure under grade, distinct from a coupling or rollability miscalculation in flat switching.

## Locomotive firer (historical/current duty)

Historically the crew member who fired a steam locomotive's boiler and monitored its gauges from the second seat in the cab; on modern diesel-electric power the position, where it still exists, functions as an additional qualified set of eyes and hands for switching and signaling duties rather than fuel-firing.

**In use:** "On this district the firer's seat is there for the extra lookout on switching moves, not for tending a fire that hasn't existed on this equipment in decades."

**Common misuse:** assuming "firer" implies an active fuel-tending function on modern equipment, when the title has persisted past the steam-era duty it originally named.
