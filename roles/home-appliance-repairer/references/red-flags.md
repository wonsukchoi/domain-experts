# Red flags

Smell tests a veteran technician catches before committing to a diagnosis or a quote. Format for each: what it usually means, the first question to ask, and the data to pull before touching a part.

## 1. No-cool refrigerator call where condenser coils haven't been checked yet

**Usually means:** the highest-frequency cause (dirty/blocked coils, roughly 30% of not-cooling calls) hasn't been ruled out, and any part quoted before that check risks being the wrong one.

**First question:** "When was the last time the coils were vacuumed or the area behind/under the unit cleared?"

**Data to pull:** visual coil condition, airflow at the condenser fan, ambient clearance around the unit (built-in units with blocked venting are a repeat-offender pattern).

## 2. Fault appears within 90 days of a new install or a prior repair visit

**Usually means:** installation or workmanship, not a new part failure — an unlevel dishwasher wearing a pump prematurely, a kinked dryer vent overheating a new element, a washer not fully seated on its feet vibrating a new bearing loose.

**First question:** "Was this unit installed or last serviced within the last 90 days, and by whom?"

**Data to pull:** install/service date and technician, level/leveling-foot condition, vent/hose routing photos if accessible.

## 3. Model and serial number match an open CPSC or manufacturer recall

**Usually means:** the correct action is a manufacturer-covered remedy (free part, free repair, or replacement), not a billable service call — proceeding to diagnose and quote anyway risks charging a customer for something already owed to them.

**First question:** "Has the manufacturer or CPSC ever contacted you about this model?"

**Data to pull:** CPSC.gov/SaferProducts.gov match by model/serial, manufacturer recall/service-bulletin page, any prior notice the customer may have discarded.

## 4. Repeated service calls on the same unit within 90 days for different symptoms

**Usually means:** the first visit treated a symptom rather than a root cause, or there's an underlying condition (voltage issue, installation defect, water-quality problem) causing a string of unrelated-looking failures.

**First question:** "What was fixed on the last two visits, and did the new complaint start right after, or independently?"

**Data to pull:** service history for this unit, parts replaced on each prior visit, house electrical/water conditions if the pattern spans multiple appliances.

## 5. Repair estimate landing within about 10 points of the 50%-of-replacement threshold

**Usually means:** a genuinely ambiguous case where the technician's own preference (protecting the ticket vs. avoiding comeback risk) can bias the recommendation without the customer ever seeing the tradeoff.

**First question:** "Have I shown the customer both numbers plainly, or did I just state a verdict?"

**Data to pull:** total repair cost (parts + labor), a real comparable replacement quote (not a guess), and the unit's position in its expected-life range.

## 6. Sealed-system repair proposed without recovery equipment or the right EPA 608 certification level on hand

**Usually means:** the diagnosis is correct but the crew isn't equipped or licensed to execute it today — proceeding anyway is a federal compliance exposure, not just a scheduling inconvenience.

**First question:** "Am I certified for this equipment's refrigerant class, and is a recovery machine on the truck right now?"

**Data to pull:** technician's EPA 608 certification level and expiration, appliance's refrigerant charge/type from the nameplate, recovery equipment inspection/calibration date.

## 7. Burning smell reported on a dryer with unknown vent-cleaning history

**Usually means:** lint accumulation in the exhaust path is a fire-risk condition, not just a heating-element wear issue — the U.S. Consumer Product Safety Commission and NFPA both attribute a large share of dryer fires to restricted vent airflow, not component failure.

**First question:** "When was the exhaust vent, not just the lint trap, last cleaned end to end?"

**Data to pull:** static airflow reading at the exhaust outlet, visual/camera inspection of the vent run, cycle time trend (a lengthening dry-cycle time is an early airflow-restriction signal).

## 8. Breaker or GFCI trips repeatedly on a washer or dishwasher circuit

**Usually means:** a ground fault or wiring condition upstream of the appliance itself — replacing the appliance's motor or control board without checking the circuit fixes nothing and can mask a shock hazard.

**First question:** "Does the breaker trip only on this appliance's dedicated circuit, or elsewhere in the house too?"

**Data to pull:** circuit amperage rating vs. appliance draw, insulation resistance test on the appliance's motor windings, any recent electrical work on that circuit.

## 9. Customer paid out-of-warranty for a part still inside its published parts-warranty window

**Usually means:** the manufacturer warranty lookup step was skipped — sealed-system compressors commonly carry 5-10 year parts-only warranties well past the 1-year whole-unit window, and a technician working from "it's out of warranty" as a blanket assumption misses this routinely.

**First question:** "Did we check the serial number against the manufacturer's extended parts-warranty terms for this specific component, not just the whole-unit warranty?"

**Data to pull:** manufacturer warranty registration/lookup by serial number, component-specific warranty terms (compressor, sealed system, and any class-action or extended-service settlements that apply to this model line).

## 10. Diagnosis jumps straight to the rare, expensive cause without ruling out the mundane one

**Usually means:** overcorrection from a past missed diagnosis — treating the fault-frequency table as a script rather than a starting sequence, and skipping the five-minute checks (tripped breaker, unplugged unit, kinked hose, closed water valve) that explain a real fraction of "broken" calls.

**First question:** "Has power, water supply, and the simplest mechanical check been confirmed before the sealed-system or control-board diagnosis starts?"

**Data to pull:** breaker/outlet status, water supply valve position, door/lid switch state, error code (if the unit displays one) cross-checked against the manufacturer's code table before assuming the rare cause.
