## 1. Contribution margin model (filled — see Worked example in SKILL.md for full narrative)

| Item | Value |
|---|---|
| Declared majors | 22 (down from 45 a decade ago) |
| Course sections taught/year | 18 |
| Average students/section | 15 |
| Total student enrollments/year | 270 |
| Tuition rate | $950/credit-hour × 3 credit hours = $2,850/student/course |
| Total tuition-attributable revenue | 270 × $2,850 = $769,500 |
| Direct department cost (3 faculty + overhead share) | $620,000 |
| **Net contribution margin** | **+$149,500** |

**Reading it:** major count (22) is the wrong basis for a financial viability judgment here — the department's actual revenue driver is total credit-hour production, most of it from non-majors fulfilling a gen-ed requirement.

## 4. Enrollment stress test (filled example, for a new program proposal)

| Scenario | Enrollment/cohort | Revenue | Cost | Net |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Base case (proposal projection) | 40 | $342,000 | $280,000 | +$62,000 |
| Conservative stress test | 30 | $256,500 | $280,000 | −$23,500 |
| Break-even enrollment | 32.7 | — | — | $0 |

**Rule applied:** the program is approved only if it clears an acceptable outcome at the conservative stress-test enrollment level, not only at the optimistic base case — here, the stress test shows a loss, prompting a redesign of the cost structure (e.g., shared faculty lines with an adjacent program) before approval, rather than approving on the base case alone.
