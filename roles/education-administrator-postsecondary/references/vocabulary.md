# Postsecondary administration working vocabulary

Terms an administrator uses precisely. Format: definition → how a practitioner says it → common misuse.

**Shared governance** — The structural arrangement in US higher education where faculty hold real, often formal authority over curriculum, academic standards, and related decisions, distinct from a purely advisory or consultative role.
In use: "This curriculum change needs to go through the department's shared governance process — faculty vote, not just administrative sign-off."
Misuse: treating shared governance as a courtesy briefing after a decision is effectively already made, rather than the structural requirement it actually is — this typically fails or creates lasting trust damage even when the underlying decision had merit.

**Contribution margin (academic program context)** — A program or department's tuition-attributable revenue (from all enrolled students, majors and non-majors) minus its direct costs, the correct basis for evaluating financial viability, as distinct from major count alone.
In use: "The department's contribution margin is positive $149,500 once we count gen-ed enrollment, even though major count alone looks unsustainable."
Misuse: evaluating a department's financial viability using major/enrollment count alone, missing that credit-hour production from non-majors (service courses, gen-ed requirements) is often the larger share of actual revenue.

**Tenure** — A faculty employment status providing strong job protection specifically to safeguard academic freedom and long-term intellectual inquiry from short-term political or market pressure, requiring a distinct governance process and timeline for any structural change affecting it.
In use: "This reorganization touches two tenured lines — that requires the faculty governance review process for tenure-affecting changes, not the standard staff reorganization timeline."
Misuse: treating tenured faculty positions like at-will staff roles in a restructuring, applying a generic business reorganization process without the different legal and governance protections tenure specifically carries.

**Academic freedom** — The principle protecting faculty members' right to teach, research, and publish without undue institutional or political interference, closely related to but distinct from tenure (which is one mechanism that protects it).
In use: "The curriculum content is protected under academic freedom — that's a faculty governance and academic-standards question, not an administrative content decision."
Misuse: conflating academic freedom with unlimited discretion over administrative matters (scheduling, resource allocation) that fall outside its actual scope of protecting intellectual and pedagogical content.

**Accreditation** — External validation that an institution or program meets defined minimum standards, required for the institution to operate and for students to access certain funding (e.g., federal financial aid) — a compliance floor, not a measure of educational excellence.
In use: "We're in good accreditation standing, but that's the floor — the separate quality review is what tells us if we're actually serving students well."
Misuse: citing accreditation status as evidence of strong educational quality, when accreditation specifically certifies minimum standards rather than excellence.

**Program review (academic program viability review)** — A structured, periodic evaluation of an academic program combining enrollment/outcome data with academic/mission value assessment, used to make an explicit, documented decision about a program's continuation, modification, or closure.
In use: "The program review set explicit triggers — majors below 10, or two consecutive years of negative contribution margin — for when the next formal review happens."
Misuse: conducting a program review only reactively during an acute budget crisis with no pre-agreed criteria, producing rushed decisions under pressure rather than deliberate ones against known standards.

**Service course** — A course primarily taken by students outside the offering department's own majors (e.g., a gen-ed requirement, a prerequisite for another major), often representing a large share of a department's actual teaching load and revenue independent of its major count.
In use: "Half our credit-hour production comes from service courses for the gen-ed requirement — that's why major count alone understates the department's actual footprint."
Misuse: excluding service-course enrollment from a department's viability analysis, focusing only on courses taken by declared majors and missing a major share of the department's real financial and institutional footprint.

**Cross-listed course** — A single course offered jointly under two or more departments' course catalogs, counting toward requirements in multiple programs, often used to build enrollment pipelines between related disciplines.
In use: "We're proposing two new cross-listed courses with Linguistics to strengthen the pipeline and shore up enrollment in both departments."
Misuse: treating cross-listing as a purely administrative/scheduling convenience rather than a deliberate curricular and enrollment-pipeline strategy between related programs.

**Faculty governance body (curriculum committee, faculty senate, department vote)** — The specific formal structures through which shared governance is exercised, each with defined authority over particular categories of decisions (curriculum, academic policy, personnel).
In use: "This needs to go to the curriculum committee first, then a full department vote — not just my sign-off as chair."
Misuse: routing a decision through an informal or lower-authority body when the specific decision type actually requires review by a higher or different formal governance body per institutional policy.

**Enrollment stress test (program financial planning)** — Evaluating a program's financial model against a conservative, lower-than-projected enrollment scenario, to check whether the program remains viable if optimistic assumptions don't materialize.
In use: "The base case assumes 40 students/cohort — the stress test at 30 still breaks even, so we're comfortable launching."
Misuse: building and approving a program's financial case only on the optimistic base-case enrollment projection, without checking sustainability under a realistic downside scenario.
