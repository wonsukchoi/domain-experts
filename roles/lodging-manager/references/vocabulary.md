# Working vocabulary

Terms of art practitioners use precisely, that generalists misuse. Load this when writing or reviewing content that needs to sound like an actual lodging/hotel manager, not a generalist describing one.

### RevPAR (Revenue Per Available Room)
ADR × Occupancy % — the standard hotel performance metric that combines rate and occupancy into a single comparable number, calculated across all available rooms (not just sold ones).
**In use:** "Occupancy's up 5 points but RevPAR is down $3 — the rate cut cost more than the occupancy gain."
**Common misuse:** Confused with ADR alone, or with total revenue — RevPAR is specifically per available room (sold or not), which is what makes it comparable across properties of different sizes and occupancy levels.

### ADR (Average Daily Rate)
Total room revenue divided by rooms sold (not rooms available) — the average rate actually charged, distinct from rack rate or RevPAR.
**In use:** "ADR held at $185 even though we discounted the group block — the transient mix carried the average."
**Common misuse:** Confused with RevPAR — ADR only reflects rooms actually sold; a property can have a strong ADR and weak RevPAR if occupancy is low.

### Walk (verb/noun)
When a guest with a confirmed reservation arrives and no room is available (usually due to overbooking combined with lower-than-expected no-shows), requiring relocation to another property.
**In use:** "We walked two guests Saturday — both got the standard relocation package, but that's still two guests we need to win back."
**Common misuse:** Treated as purely a logistics problem to solve in the moment — the guest-trust cost of a walk is real and ongoing regardless of how smoothly the relocation itself is executed.

### Pickup (booking pace)
The rate at which reservations are added for a future date as that date approaches, used to forecast final occupancy from current bookings-on-the-books.
**In use:** "We're tracking below typical pickup pace for that weekend — worth revisiting the rate before we assume it'll fill on its own."
**Common misuse:** Ignored in favor of looking only at current bookings-on-the-books — the pace of pickup relative to historical pattern is often more informative than the absolute current booking count, especially at longer lead times.

### Rate parity
An agreement (often with OTAs) that a property's rate for a given room type/date is consistent across all distribution channels, preventing a channel from consistently undercutting others.
**In use:** "That OTA is showing $12 below our direct rate — that's a parity violation, needs to go to our channel manager."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to mean "same rate everywhere all the time" without accounting for legitimate package/loyalty-rate exceptions that parity agreements typically carve out.

### Displacement (group business)
The revenue lost from turning away or not being able to sell higher-rate transient demand because rooms are held for a lower-rate group block.
**In use:** "The group rate looks fine in isolation, but the displacement cost against forecast transient demand for that weekend makes it a net loss."
**Common misuse:** Ignored entirely — group business evaluated only on its own rate without asking what higher-value business it's crowding out for the same dates.

### STR report (Smith Travel Research, now CoStar)
An industry-standard competitive benchmarking report comparing a property's occupancy, ADR, and RevPAR against a defined competitive set.
**In use:** "Our STR index is 92 — we're underperforming our comp set on RevPAR even though our own numbers look fine year-over-year."
**Common misuse:** Evaluating performance only against the property's own historical trend, without checking the competitive-set-relative index, which can reveal underperformance (or outperformance) that a purely internal trend comparison would miss.

### No-show
A guest with a confirmed, unguaranteed-or-guaranteed reservation who doesn't arrive and doesn't cancel — the basis for calculating a defensible overbooking level.
**In use:** "No-show rate for this specific Saturday pattern runs 4% historically — that's what the overbooking math should be based on, not a flat policy number."
**Common misuse:** Estimated as a flat, property-wide percentage rather than segmented by day-of-week/season, which understates or overstates the real rate for any specific date and produces a miscalibrated overbooking decision.
