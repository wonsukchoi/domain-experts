# Medical Transcriptionist — Vocabulary

### ASR (Automatic Speech Recognition) draft
The machine-generated first-pass transcription a human editor corrects against the audio, as opposed to typing verbatim from scratch.
**In use:** "The ASR draft came through clean except for one drug name I need to verify against the audio."
**Common misuse:** Treating a fluent-reading ASR draft as more reliable than it is — ASR confidently substitutes plausible wrong words, which reads smoother than an obvious garble but is more dangerous.

### Verbatim vs. edited style
Verbatim transcription reproduces exactly what was said including false starts and filler; edited style cleans up grammar and removes non-substantive verbal habits while preserving all clinical content.
**In use:** "This account's style guide calls for edited, not verbatim — drop the 'um's but never touch a dosage."
**Common misuse:** Assuming "edited" style permits editorial judgment on clinical content, not just grammar — the edit license never extends to guessing a drug name or value.

### Sound-alike / look-alike (SALA) drug pair
Two drugs whose names are phonetically or visually similar enough to be confused, a recognized medication-error category tracked by ISMP.
**In use:** "Clonidine and clonazepam are a classic SALA pair — the dose is what usually disambiguates them, and here it didn't."
**Common misuse:** Treating SALA confusion as a spelling problem rather than a patient-safety category with its own tracked list and required flagging discipline.

### Flagged query
A specific, timestamped question sent back to the dictating provider about an ambiguity that can't be resolved with certainty from context.
**In use:** "I sent a flagged query on the medication name — the note's held as draft until the surgeon responds."
**Common misuse:** Writing an open-ended "please clarify" instead of naming the exact word, timestamp, and candidate resolutions — this forces the provider to re-listen to the whole dictation instead of answering a specific question.

### Silent correction
Fixing an unambiguous, clinically-neutral error (grammar, an obviously transposed digit resolvable from context) without flagging it to the provider.
**In use:** "That's a silent correction — the date typo is obvious once you check the rest of the note's dates."
**Common misuse:** Extending silent correction to anything the transcriptionist feels confident about, rather than restricting it to errors with zero clinical-meaning risk.

### Laterality
Which side of the body (left/right) a finding, procedure, or condition applies to.
**In use:** "Laterality's inconsistent between paragraph two and the procedure header — that's a flag, not a judgment call."
**Common misuse:** Assuming laterality can be inferred from "which side is usually affected" for a given condition — wrong-site documentation is a distinct risk category from a typical-case assumption.

### HIM (Health Information Management)
The healthcare discipline governing the creation, accuracy, security, and lifecycle of the medical record, of which transcription/documentation is one function.
**In use:** "HIM policy sets the retention schedule; transcription just has to get the content right at the point of creation."
**Common misuse:** Conflating HIM (record governance broadly) with transcription (one specific documentation-creation function within it).

### Turnaround time (TAT)
The elapsed time from dictation received to final document delivered, typically tiered by document urgency (STAT/routine/discharge).
**In use:** "This op note is STAT TAT — the surgeon needs it before the next case starts."
**Common misuse:** Treating TAT as a single account-wide number rather than a tiered target that varies by document type and clinical use.

### Confidence score (ASR)
A per-word or per-phrase metric the speech-recognition engine assigns, reflecting how certain the model is about its transcription.
**In use:** "The confidence score flagged that phrase as low-certainty, which is exactly the kind of ambiguity worth double-checking against audio."
**Common misuse:** Trusting a high confidence score as proof of correctness — ASR can be confidently wrong, especially on sound-alike drug names.

### QA (quality assurance) sample
A periodic audit of a transcriptionist's or account's completed work, reviewed against source audio for accuracy.
**In use:** "This month's QA sample flagged a higher-than-usual silent-correction rate — worth checking if turnaround pressure is driving under-flagging."
**Common misuse:** Measuring QA purely by error count rather than weighting by clinical significance — twenty punctuation misses matter far less than one wrong drug name.

### Chart addendum
A formal correction added to a finalized record after the fact, as opposed to editing the document before it's finalized.
**In use:** "Once it's signed, a correction goes in as a chart addendum — you don't edit a finalized note directly."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a transcription error caught after finalization can simply be fixed in place; the finalized record requires a formal addendum, not a silent edit.
