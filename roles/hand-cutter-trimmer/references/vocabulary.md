# Vocabulary

### Nesting
The arrangement of pattern pieces across a piece of material to maximize yield while respecting constraints like grain direction and defect avoidance.
**In use:** "This nest gets us all eight panels, but it costs a few points of yield to keep everything on-grain and clear of that flaw."
**Common misuse:** Treating nesting as a pure geometric packing problem optimizing only for yield — grain direction and defect avoidance are real constraints that take priority over squeezing out maximum material utilization.

### Grain direction
The predominant directional orientation of a material's fibers or structure (in fabric, leather, or wood), which affects a cut piece's strength, stretch behavior, and visual appearance.
**In use:** "Grain runs lengthwise on this template — rotating a piece ninety degrees to fit better would change how it stretches and looks next to the others."
**Common misuse:** Treating grain direction as an aesthetic preference that can flex for yield efficiency — for many materials, grain direction affects functional properties (strength, stretch) as much as or more than appearance.

### Natural defect (material flaw)
An inherent imperfection in natural material — a scar, thin spot, knot, or similar — that varies unpredictably by piece and has to be identified and worked around during layout.
**In use:** "That thin spot's a natural defect in the hide — routing the pattern around it, not through it."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a defect is inconsequential if it's small or near a piece's edge — a defect's actual risk depends on whether it falls in a structurally or visually critical area of the finished part, not just its size.

### Bias cut
A cutting orientation running diagonally (typically 45°) relative to a fabric's grain, producing a piece with different stretch and drape characteristics than a piece cut on-grain.
**In use:** "This piece is bias-cut on purpose — that's why it drapes differently, and why we let it relax before final measurement."
**Common misuse:** Treating a bias-cut piece's measurement immediately after cutting as final — bias-cut material characteristically relaxes and shifts dimension after being released from cutting tension, more so than on-grain material.

### Master pattern
The authoritative, verified reference pattern that all working templates and cutting layouts should trace back to and be periodically checked against.
**In use:** "Checking this template against the master pattern before we cut another run — it's been in heavy use and could have drifted."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a working template remains accurate indefinitely once initially verified — repeated handling and use can cause a template to wear, stretch, or shift, and periodic re-verification against the master pattern is what catches that drift.

### Yield (material utilization)
The percentage of a material piece's total area or volume actually used in cut parts, versus scrap/waste — a metric that trades off against grain direction and defect-avoidance constraints.
**In use:** "Yield came in at seventy-one percent on this hide — lower than our max-efficiency theoretical number, but that's the cost of staying on-grain and clear of the flaw."
**Common misuse:** Treating yield as the single metric to maximize without regard to what constraints were relaxed to achieve it — a high yield number achieved by ignoring grain direction or defect avoidance represents a worse outcome, not a better one, for the finished product.

### Clicker press / die cutting (hand-guided)
Equipment using a shaped steel-rule die to cut a repeated pattern shape from material, commonly used for high-volume, consistent hand-cut-equivalent parts in leather goods and similar industries.
**In use:** "Die's set up for this panel shape — faster and more consistent than free-hand cutting for a run this size."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a die-cut part is automatically dimensionally accurate regardless of die condition — a worn or damaged die produces the same kind of dimensional drift a dull hand blade does, and needs its own periodic accuracy check.

### Relaxation (material)
The tendency of certain materials, especially elastic or bias-cut fabrics, to shift dimension after being released from the tension applied during handling or cutting.
**In use:** "Let it relax for a minute before we take the final measurement — right off the cutting table it's still stretched from handling."
**Common misuse:** Measuring a piece immediately after cutting while it's still under handling tension and treating that as the final dimension — the piece's relaxed, untensioned state is what the finished product will actually reflect.

### Cut quality (edge condition)
The condition of a cut edge — clean, straight, free of fraying or dragging — used as a practical indicator of blade sharpness and dimensional accuracy.
**In use:** "Edge is starting to drag a little on this cut — that's our signal to swap the blade before it starts affecting dimensions."
**Common misuse:** Waiting for a blade to become obviously dull (visibly tearing material) before replacing it — cut quality can degrade gradually in a way that's subtle enough to miss at a glance while still producing real dimensional inaccuracy.

### Lot (material lot)
A specific batch of material (a hide, a fabric roll, a foam sheet) with its own individual characteristics — defect concentration, grain consistency — distinct from the general material specification.
**In use:** "This lot's got more flaws than usual — that's a material issue with this specific hide, not a cutting process problem."
**Common misuse:** Assuming yield or defect data should be consistent across all material lots of the same general spec — natural materials in particular vary meaningfully lot-to-lot, and low yield on one lot doesn't necessarily indicate a process issue if that lot's actual condition explains it.
