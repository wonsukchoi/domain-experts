# Vocabulary

### Communication apprehension (CA)
A trait or state-level fear/anxiety associated with real or anticipated communication with others, measured by instruments like the PRCA-24.
**In use:** "Her PRCA-24 total is 86 — that's high trait CA, so we adjust the audience size for her speeches, not the rubric."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a synonym for shyness in general, or assumed to be something a student can simply "get over" with more practice, ignoring that trait CA is stable and the course's job is managing the state-anxiety spike around one event.

### PRCA-24
A 24-item self-report instrument (McCroskey) scoring communication apprehension across four contexts — public speaking, meetings, group discussions, dyadic conversation — each 6-30, total range 24-120.
**In use:** "Screen in week one; anyone over 80 gets a rehearsal-slot accommodation before the first graded speech."
**Common misuse:** Used as a diagnostic label ("this student has social anxiety disorder") rather than a screening flag for accommodation planning.

### Ethos, pathos, logos
Aristotle's three modes of persuasive appeal — credibility, emotional appeal, and logical reasoning — used as analytic categories for evaluating how a text persuades.
**In use:** "The op-ed leans almost entirely on pathos in paragraph three — no evidence, just outrage — that's worth flagging in your analysis."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a checklist to include in a speech ("I used all three!") rather than categories for evaluating an existing text's persuasive strategy.

### Neo-Aristotelian criticism
A rhetorical-criticism method (via Foss) that evaluates a text's use of the classical canons — invention, arrangement, style, delivery — against its persuasive purpose and audience.
**In use:** "For the first analysis assignment, default to Neo-Aristotelian — it maps cleanly onto a linear speech text."
**Common misuse:** Applied to any artifact by default, including visual or fragmented digital media (memes, short-form video) where there's no linear argument structure for the canons to evaluate.

### Norming / calibration session
A joint scoring session where all raters independently score the same anchor examples, then discuss disagreements until they converge on a shared interpretation of the rubric.
**In use:** "Before live grading opens, every GTA scores the same three anchor speeches and we reconcile any gap over a point."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to be a one-time event at the start of a teaching career, rather than a per-term practice needed every time raters or rubrics change.

### Inter-rater reliability
The degree to which independent raters assign the same score to the same performance using the same rubric.
**In use:** "Two raters averaging 8 points apart on the same prompt is an inter-rater reliability problem, not a cohort problem."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to be guaranteed by having a written rubric, without any joint scoring practice to confirm raters interpret it the same way.

### Communication competence
The demonstrated ability to communicate in a way that is both effective (achieves the goal) and appropriate (fits the context), assessed through observable behaviors rather than a global impression.
**In use:** "The rubric scores competence — did they state a clear thesis, cite evidence, adapt to the audience — not how likable they came across."
**Common misuse:** Conflated with charisma, confidence, or grammatical correctness alone, none of which are the same as demonstrated situational effectiveness.

### Immediacy (nonverbal)
Behaviors that signal psychological closeness and approachability — eye contact, vocal warmth, open posture, physical proximity.
**In use:** "Her immediacy score is low mostly on vocal variety, not eye contact — she's reading in a monotone even while looking at the audience."
**Common misuse:** Reduced to eye contact alone, ignoring vocal and physical-proximity cues that carry equal weight in the construct.

### Defensive communication climate
A set of behaviors (per Gibb: evaluation, control, strategy, neutrality, superiority, certainty) that provoke defensiveness in a listener, contrasted with supportive-climate behaviors.
**In use:** "The case study's breakdown is a control move — dictating the solution instead of asking — that's what triggered the defensive response, not the disagreement itself."
**Common misuse:** Labeled onto any disagreement or conflict generally, rather than the specific Gibb category of behavior actually present in the exchange.

### Agenda-setting
A mass-communication theory holding that media coverage influences which issues audiences consider important, not what opinion they hold about those issues.
**In use:** "That's agenda-setting — the network decided immigration was the top story, not how viewers should feel about it."
**Common misuse:** Conflated with framing theory, which concerns how an issue is presented rather than whether it's covered at all — the two are related but distinct claims.

### Groupthink
A group decision-making failure (Janis) driven by specific antecedent conditions — high cohesion, insulation from outside opinion, directive leadership, high stress — that suppress dissent.
**In use:** "Before you call this groupthink in your case study, check whether the antecedent conditions were actually present, not just that the group made a bad call."
**Common misuse:** Applied to any poor group decision as a catch-all label, without checking for the specific conditions that define the phenomenon versus ordinary bad judgment.

### Extemporaneous delivery
A delivery mode that is rehearsed and outlined in advance but not memorized or read word-for-word — the standard mode taught and assessed in most basic public-speaking courses.
**In use:** "Extemporaneous doesn't mean unprepared — it means working from a keyword outline, not a manuscript."
**Common misuse:** Equated with "improvised" or "unrehearsed," when the taught skill specifically requires substantial preparation short of memorization.

### Speech bank
A site or service selling or sharing pre-written speech scripts, the spoken-assignment analog of a written solution bank.
**In use:** "The delivery was smooth but generic, and he couldn't explain his own source — check whether that script is sitting on a speech bank."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to be identifiable only through plagiarism-detection software, when a direct verbal follow-up question is often the faster and more reliable check for spoken assignments.

### DFW rate
The percentage of enrolled students in a course who receive a D, an F, or withdraw, tracked by term as a course-health metric.
**In use:** "DFW jumped 6 points the term we changed the rubric — pull the item breakdown before blaming the cohort."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a direct, standalone measure of teaching quality without controlling for section composition, time slot, or a concurrent assignment/rubric change.
