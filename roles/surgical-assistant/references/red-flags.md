# Red flags

Intraoperative smell tests a first assistant catches before they become a complication. Format for each: what it usually means, the first question to ask, and the data/finding to check before anyone acts.

## 1. Retractor requiring progressively more force to hold the same exposure

**Usually means:** wrong blade size/angle for the anatomy, a shifted patient position, or the retractor is now fighting a structure it wasn't designed to hold — not that the exposure genuinely needs more tension.

**First question:** "Has anything about the angle or the patient's position changed since I set this?"

**Data to pull:** blade size and angle relative to the original setup; whether table position (Trendelenburg, lateral tilt) has changed since placement; visual check for blanching or tissue distortion at the contact points.

## 2. Energy device activated with the tip partially or fully off-screen (laparoscopic/robotic)

**Usually means:** camera drift during a busy dissection step, or the surgeon assuming the assistant is tracking the tip when attention shifted elsewhere. Thermal spread outside the visible field is the classic mechanism behind delayed bowel/ureteral injury that doesn't present until days later.

**First question:** "Can you see the full tip before you activate?"

**Data to pull:** current frame composition before activation; whether smoke or fluid is obscuring the working end; whether the camera was repositioned in the last several steps.

## 3. Sponge/instrument count discrepancy at any checkpoint

**Usually means:** most often a miscount or an item still in the field (drapes, floor, trash) rather than a truly retained item — but must be treated as the latter until ruled out. Retained-item events cluster in cases assumed low-risk beforehand (short, "simple," no shift change expected).

**First question:** "Where haven't we looked yet — drapes, floor, linen, trash?"

**Data to pull:** the specific item missing and its expected count; whether any staff relief/shift change happened uncounted; imaging availability if the visual search comes up empty. Closure does not proceed on an unreconciled count.

## 4. Vein graft or conduit under visible tension at the final anastomosis

**Usually means:** the harvested length was under-budgeted for the anastomosis-to-anastomosis path plus trim and post-harvest shortening, or the surgeon's planned route changed after harvest. Tension at anastomosis is a direct risk factor for early graft failure regardless of harvest technique quality.

**First question:** "Do we have any slack at all, or is this graft actually short?"

**Data to pull:** original harvested length vs. the arithmetic that was planned (path length + trim allowance + shortening margin); whether the anastomosis sequence changed intraoperatively from what was mapped before harvest.

## 5. Alcohol-based skin prep visibly pooled, or drape applied before full dry time

**Usually means:** time pressure overriding the prep protocol. Controlled testing shows alcohol-based preps still ignite in roughly 1 in 10 trials even at the standard 3-minute dry time, rising sharply when prep pools rather than drying flat.

**First question:** "Is there any visible pooling, and has the full labeled dry time passed?"

**Data to pull:** prep product's labeled dry time (longer over hair-bearing areas or skin folds); visual confirmation of no pooling before drape placement; oxygen source proximity if the case involves head/neck/upper chest field (higher fire-triangle risk with an open oxygen source nearby).

## 6. Unexpected anatomic finding that doesn't match pre-op imaging or the stated plan

**Usually means:** either a variant anatomy the imaging didn't capture, disease more extensive than staged, or a wrong-site/wrong-structure risk if the mismatch is severe enough. Silence in this moment defaults to "proceed as planned," which is the wrong default once the plan's assumptions no longer hold.

**First question:** "Does this match what we expected to find, or should we pause and reconfirm?"

**Data to pull:** pre-op imaging on the OR monitor for direct comparison; whether the consent covers the finding if it changes the planned procedure; surgeon's stated threshold for calling an intraoperative consult.

## 7. Retractor held in the same fixed position past 30 minutes

**Usually means:** the assistant has skipped the mandatory release interval, which is the mechanism behind position-related nerve injury (classically femoral or peroneal); a gynecologic-surgery QI study tying release to this interval cut femoral nerve injury from 6.5% to 0.9%.

**First question:** "How long has this retractor position been static, and can it be released for a moment right now?"

**Data to pull:** elapsed time since last repositioning; whether the current operative step actually requires continuous hold on this exact structure or tolerates a brief release; visible skin/tissue changes at the contact point.

## 8. Critical View of Safety not clearly achieved in a laparoscopic cholecystectomy

**Usually means:** inflammation, fibrosis, or anatomic variant is obscuring the hepatocystic triangle — proceeding to clip/cut structures without a clear CVS is the single highest-leverage moment for a bile duct injury.

**First question:** "Can we name all three CVS criteria as achieved right now, or are we assuming?"

**Data to pull:** whether the hepatocystic triangle is genuinely cleared of fat/fibrous tissue; whether the cystic plate is exposed; whether exactly two structures (duct, artery) are seen entering the gallbladder — documented ideally with a photo, per unit protocol.

## 9. Specimen or graft handed off without a verbal condition report

**Usually means:** the person who will judge the specimen (pathologist) or the conduit (surgeon at the anastomosis, or a cath lab years later) has no record of handling quality from the one person who actually saw it fresh.

**First question:** "Did I state condition, orientation, and any irregularities out loud before handoff?"

**Data to pull:** whether orientation marking matches the surgeon's stated convention; whether any wall irregularity, branch ligation, or handling issue was verbally logged at the moment of transfer.
