# Vocabulary

Terms generalists conflate that first assistants keep sharply separate.

## Surgical assistant vs. surgical technologist

**Surgical assistant (CSA)** works inside the sterile field alongside the surgeon, actively retracting, controlling hemostasis, suturing, and sometimes handling tissue. **Surgical technologist** manages the sterile back table, passes instruments, and maintains the sterile field from outside the operative site itself, without operating on tissue.

*In use:* "We need a CSA on this case, not just a tech — the surgeon wants a second set of hands actually working the field, not just passing instruments."

*Common misuse:* using the titles interchangeably because both wear the same sterile gown; the scope of practice, training pathway, and certification (NCCSA/NBSTSA CSA vs. NBSTSA CST) are distinct.

## First assist vs. second assist

**First assist** is the primary in-field assistant, directly supporting the operating surgeon's hands at the critical steps (exposure, hemostasis, closure). **Second assist**, when present, typically holds retraction or the camera at a supporting position and defers active field decisions entirely to the first assist and surgeon.

*In use:* "Med student's second-assisting today — camera and retraction only, I've got the field."

*Common misuse:* assuming any warm body scrubbed in counts as a "first assist"; the term implies an active, credentialed role, not just physical presence.

## Retraction vs. exposure

**Retraction** is the mechanical act of holding tissue out of the way with an instrument. **Exposure** is the actual surgical goal — a clear, stable view of the operative target — which retraction is one method of achieving, alongside patient positioning, insufflation pressure, and instrument choice.

*In use:* "Retracting harder isn't giving him exposure — reposition the blade, don't add tension."

*Common misuse:* treating "pull harder" as the default fix for poor exposure, when the actual fix is usually a positioning or instrument-selection change.

## Hemostasis vs. coagulation

**Hemostasis** is the broader goal — stopping bleeding by any appropriate method (pressure, clip, tie, cautery). **Coagulation** is one specific method, using thermal energy to seal a vessel, and carries its own risk profile (thermal spread, char, delayed necrosis) that pressure or mechanical ligation doesn't.

*In use:* "That vessel's too close to the ureter for coagulation — clip it instead, keep hemostasis mechanical there."

*Common misuse:* reaching for cautery as the default hemostasis tool regardless of proximity to heat-sensitive structures (ureter, bowel, nerve).

## No-touch vs. conventional vein harvest

**No-touch harvest** removes the saphenous vein with its perivascular fat pedicle intact and without high-pressure distension, preserving the vessel's own vasa vasorum and reducing venospasm. **Conventional harvest** strips the vein bare and typically uses pressurized saline to check patency, which is faster but associated with lower long-term graft patency.

*In use:* "Unit protocol's no-touch now — skip the distension step, leave the fat pedicle on."

*Common misuse:* treating the choice as purely a speed/convenience decision rather than a patency decision with measurable long-term consequences (occlusion rates roughly 10–30% at one year for conventional technique in trial populations, vs. no-touch patency comparable to arterial grafts at long-term follow-up).

## Critical View of Safety (CVS) vs. "identifying the cystic duct"

**CVS** is a specific three-part anatomic confirmation (hepatocystic triangle cleared, cystic plate exposed, only two structures entering the gallbladder) that must all be true before clipping/cutting in a laparoscopic cholecystectomy. **"Identifying the cystic duct"** alone — seeing a tubular structure and assuming it's the cystic duct — is exactly the assumption CVS was designed to replace, because it's also how the common bile duct gets mistaken for the cystic duct.

*In use:* "I see a duct-like structure, but we don't have all three CVS criteria yet — I'm not calling it clear."

*Common misuse:* treating "I can see the cystic duct" as sufficient confirmation on its own, without the triangle-clearance and two-structures criteria that make the identification reliable.

## Retained surgical item vs. count discrepancy

**Count discrepancy** is a mismatch between expected and counted items at a checkpoint — it may or may not indicate a retained item once fully investigated. **Retained surgical item (RSI)** is the confirmed outcome: an item left inside the patient at closure. Every RSI starts as a discrepancy, but not every discrepancy becomes an RSI — the search-and-imaging protocol exists to make sure it doesn't.

*In use:* "We have a discrepancy, not confirmed retained yet — full visual search first, X-ray if it's still off."

*Common misuse:* treating a discrepancy as automatically resolved once a recount "looks right," without the structured search-then-imaging sequence.

## Countertraction vs. traction

**Traction** pulls tissue in the direction the surgeon is dissecting toward. **Countertraction** pulls in the opposing direction, creating tension across the plane the surgeon is cutting, which is usually the assistant's actual job — providing traction on the same plane the surgeon is working often fights the surgeon's own instrument.

*In use:* "Give me countertraction here, not traction — pull the gallbladder up and away while I work the plane down."

*Common misuse:* an assistant pulling tissue in the same direction as the surgeon's dissecting instrument, which collapses the tissue plane instead of opening it.

## Sequential graft vs. separate grafts (CABG)

**Sequential graft** uses one harvested conduit to bypass multiple target vessels via side-to-side (diamond) anastomoses along its length, ending in one terminal end-to-side anastomosis. **Separate grafts** use a distinct conduit segment for each target vessel, each with its own proximal aortic anastomosis.

*In use:* "One sequential graft off this length spares us two aortic anastomosis sites — but the harvest length has to reconcile against three target distances, not just be generously long."

*Common misuse:* assuming "sequential" just means a longer piece of the same vein with no different length-planning requirement — the trim allowance multiplies with each additional anastomosis on the same conduit.

## Scope of practice vs. credentialing/privileging

**Scope of practice** is what a role is legally permitted to do under state law. **Credentialing/privileging** is what a specific hospital has verified and authorized a specific individual to do at that facility, which can be narrower than the legal scope but never broader.

*In use:* "State scope allows me to do this, but I'm not privileged for it at this hospital yet — I can't just do it because the law permits it."

*Common misuse:* treating state-level scope-of-practice rules as the only constraint, ignoring that facility privileging is a separate, sometimes stricter gate.
