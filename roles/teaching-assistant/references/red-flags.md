# Red flags

Smells a veteran teaching assistant catches early, with the first question to ask and the data to pull before treating it as normal.

### A student hasn't worked independently in 3+ consecutive sessions

**Usually means:** the prompt level was never stepped down after it first worked, or a fading plan was never written in the first place — the support that helped in week one is now the ceiling in week six.

**First question:** "What prompt level are we using today, and when did we last try one level lower?"

**Data to pull:** session-by-session independent-response rate and prompt level for the last 2 weeks; the written step-down rule, if one exists.

### A teacher's fix for flat progress is "give them more 1:1 time" with no change to method

**Usually means:** a quantity fix is being applied to a fading problem; more time at the same prompt level produces more comfortable dependency, not more independence.

**First question:** "Has the prompt level changed at all in the last two weeks, or just the minutes?"

**Data to pull:** minutes of direct support per day (before and after the proposed change) alongside independent-response rate over the same window — if minutes rise and the rate doesn't, that's the tell.

### Same student, same seat, same adult, every single day

**Usually means:** an informal one-to-one arrangement has calcified without anyone deciding it should be permanent — often starting as a reasonable short-term response to a rough patch.

**First question:** "Whose decision was it that this student gets this specific adult every day, and when was it last reviewed?"

**Data to pull:** the student's IEP/504 accommodations as written (do they specify 1:1 support, or has the classroom improvised beyond what's documented?); peer-interaction or teacher-initiated-instruction counts for that student versus classmates.

### A behavior incident report says "he was defiant" with no antecedent listed

**Usually means:** no ABC data is being collected, so the team is working from an interpretation instead of a pattern — and interpretations vary by which adult was in the room.

**First question:** "What happened in the two minutes right before this — what was being asked of him?"

**Data to pull:** at least 5 ABC log entries across different times/settings before anyone proposes a behavior plan.

### Praise-to-correction ratio feels like it's mostly correction

**Usually means:** the group or student is in a rough stretch and the adult's language has drifted to almost all redirection, which research associates with more escalation, not less compliance.

**First question:** "In the last 10 minutes, how many specific praise statements went out versus corrections?"

**Data to pull:** a 10-minute tally, live or from memory immediately after — most adults are surprised by the real ratio once they count it.

### Physical intervention happened and nobody had CPI (or equivalent) training

**Usually means:** a verbal or spatial de-escalation step was skipped under time pressure, and an untrained adult improvised a physical response.

**First question:** "What happened in the 60 seconds right before physical contact, and who was trained to do what actually happened?"

**Data to pull:** the building's incident report form, completed same-day; a check of which staff present hold current de-escalation certification.

### A student's IEP-documented accommodation was skipped to keep a group activity moving

**Usually means:** the accommodation was treated as optional under time pressure rather than as the legally binding minimum it is.

**First question:** "Did the teacher explicitly modify this accommodation for today, or did it just get skipped to stay on schedule?"

**Data to pull:** the current IEP/504 accommodation list; whether a modification was documented anywhere.

### Confidential student information came up in the staff room, hallway, or with another parent

**Usually means:** a FERPA-relevant boundary was crossed casually, usually without any intent to harm — often framed as "just venting" or "just context."

**First question:** "Did the person you told need to know this to do their job?"

**Data to pull:** none — this is a coaching moment, not a data question; document that it was addressed if it recurs.

### A student who was previously making steady progress suddenly plateaus or regresses across a week

**Usually means:** an environmental change (schedule shift, new material, sleep/home disruption, a substitute in the room) more often than a "the plan stopped working" story — check the boring explanation before rewriting the plan.

**First question:** "What changed this week that has nothing to do with the instruction itself?"

**Data to pull:** attendance, schedule changes, any note from home; compare this week's session notes against the prior baseline weeks for anything besides the data trend.
