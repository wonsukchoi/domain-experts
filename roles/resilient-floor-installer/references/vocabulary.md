# Resilient Floor Installer Vocabulary

### Cutback adhesive (mastic)
A black, asphalt-based adhesive widely used to install resilient flooring before roughly 1980, commonly asbestos-containing even when the tile installed over it is not.
**In use:** "Tile came back negative, but pull a separate sample on the cutback under it before anyone touches a scraper."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a negative test on the visible tile clears the whole floor for demo; the mastic is a distinct material and is tested and disposed of separately from the surface layer.

### Presumed asbestos-containing material (ACM)
The regulatory default status for any material installed before roughly 1980, or of unknown installation date, until a bulk sample proves otherwise.
**In use:** "Building's from '75 — we're treating both the tile and the mastic as presumed ACM until the lab results come back."
**Common misuse:** Treating "presumed" as a formality that only matters on paper; it governs what tools and methods are legal to use on the material before results return (no dry sanding, grinding, or scraping).

### Encapsulation
Sealing an intact, well-bonded legacy resilient layer (and any asbestos-containing adhesive under it) in place with a primer and skim coat, rather than removing it — a recognized alternative to abatement when the layer is stable.
**In use:** "Bond test passed and it's flat — encapsulate it and float the new LVT over the top instead of pulling a full abatement permit."
**Common misuse:** Applying it to a layer that's delaminating or cracked; encapsulation only holds up on a substrate that's already stable — it doesn't stabilize a failing bond.

### Bond-pull test
A field or lab test that measures whether an existing flooring layer is fully adhered to the substrate beneath it, used to decide whether that layer is a viable base for encapsulation or new flooring.
**In use:** "Ran the pull test at four spots — all fully adhered, no delamination, so we're clear to encapsulate."
**Common misuse:** Substituting a visual check ("it looks stuck down") for an actual pull test; a layer can look intact at the surface while lifting underneath.

### Telegraphing
A substrate flaw, seam, or old flooring's pattern becoming visible through a new, thin resilient covering because there's no cushioning layer to distribute and hide it.
**In use:** "That ridge is telegraphing through the new sheet vinyl already — the substrate needed to be leveled, not just swept, before we laid it."
**Common misuse:** Assuming any thin resilient material will "settle in" and hide minor substrate flaws over time the way a thicker or cushioned floor might; resilient goods show the flaw immediately and it doesn't improve.

### Calcium chloride test (ASTM F1869)
A moisture test measuring the concrete's moisture vapor emission rate (MVER) in lbs/1,000 ft²/24hr by weighing a sealed calcium chloride dish on the slab over 60-72 hours.
**In use:** "Ran five CaCl spots on the 2,400 — average came back under 3, standard adhesive is fine."
**Common misuse:** Treating one test location as representative of an entire floor; the protocol scales test count to square footage because readings vary meaningfully across a single slab.

### Alkalinity (pH) test
A test of the concrete surface's pH, run wet with a strip or meter, used to catch a failure mode moisture testing alone misses — high pH breaks down standard adhesives through a chemical reaction independent of moisture content.
**In use:** "Moisture passed clean, but pH came back at 11 — that's what's going to eat the adhesive, not the humidity."
**Common misuse:** Skipping the pH test because the moisture readings passed; a slab can be dry enough for a standard adhesive and still fail from alkalinity alone.

### Self-leveling underlayment (SLU)
A pourable cementitious compound used to correct substrate flatness across a broad area, rather than patching individual low spots.
**In use:** "Whole east half of the room is out of tolerance — pour SLU across the section, not spot-patch the three worst dips."
**Common misuse:** Spot-patching only the deepest points; a resilient floor telegraphs the edge of an isolated patch as readily as it would the original unevenness.

### Notch trowel / transfer rate
The trowel notch size specified by an adhesive's data sheet to control how much adhesive is deposited and how it transfers to the back of the flooring when rolled — different adhesives call for different notch sizes even for similar-looking use cases.
**In use:** "This adhesive calls for a 1/16 square notch, not the U-notch we used last job — check the sheet before troweling."
**Common misuse:** Reusing whatever trowel is already in the truck instead of checking the current adhesive's specified notch size, which under- or over-doses the bed and shows up as bond failure or telegraphing.

### Open time
The window after adhesive is troweled during which the flooring material can still be set into it and achieve full transfer and bond, before the adhesive skins over.
**In use:** "We've got maybe fifteen minutes of open time on this one — don't trowel more area than the crew can set in that window."
**Common misuse:** Troweling a large area ahead of the crew's actual setting pace; adhesive that skins over before material is pressed into it never fully wets out and bonds weak.

### Heat welding
Fusing a plastic weld rod into a routed groove along a sheet-vinyl seam using a hot-air welder, producing a continuous, water-resistant joint rather than a taped or glued seam.
**In use:** "This is a wet area, so it's heat-welded seams throughout, not just taped."
**Common misuse:** Trimming the weld bead only after it's fully cooled; the correct sequence is a rough trim while still warm and pliable, then a finish trim after cooling — a single cold trim tears the bead.

### Floating floor
An installation method (used for most click-lock LVT/LVP) where the flooring interlocks into a connected plane that isn't fixed to the substrate, and instead relies on a perimeter expansion gap to move as a unit.
**In use:** "It's floating, so leave the gap at every wall — don't caulk it solid or nail through the field."
**Common misuse:** Detailing a floating floor's edges the way a glue-down floor is detailed (tight to the wall, no gap); a floating floor needs room to expand as a connected plane or it buckles.

### Acclimation
Storing flooring material on-site, in the room it will be installed, for a set period before installation so it reaches the room's actual temperature and humidity before being locked into place.
**In use:** "Planks have been acclimating since yesterday morning — we're clear on the 48-hour window as of this afternoon."
**Common misuse:** Treating acclimation as optional on a tight schedule; skipping it is the most common cause of post-install buckling on large-format floating floors.

### Vinyl composition tile (VCT) vs. luxury vinyl tile (LVT)
VCT is a filler-heavy composite tile (historically the most common commercial resilient product, often installed pre-1980 over asbestos-containing mastic) requiring waxing/finish; LVT is a denser, largely PVC-based product designed to look finished without a wax coating and often installed click-lock or with adhesive.
**In use:** "This is old VCT, not LVT — that's why it needs finish coats and why the mastic under it needs testing before we touch it."
**Common misuse:** Using the terms interchangeably; the distinction changes both the finishing requirement (VCT needs wax/finish, LVT generally doesn't) and the legacy-material testing risk (VCT-era jobs are far more likely to sit on asbestos-containing mastic).
