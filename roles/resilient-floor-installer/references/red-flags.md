# Resilient Floor Installer Red Flags

### Existing resilient flooring predates 1980 (or install date unknown) and demo is scheduled without testing
- **Usually means:** the GC or estimator assumed a quick tear-out job without checking the building's age against presumed-ACM rules; second most likely is a prior renovation that already removed and replaced the flooring once, but no record of that removal's compliance survives.
- **First question:** "Has this material or the mastic under it ever been bulk-sampled, or is 'it's probably fine' the only evidence?"
- **Data to pull:** building construction/renovation date, any prior asbestos survey or abatement records for the space.

### Bulk sample on the tile is negative but no separate sample was pulled for the adhesive underneath
- **Usually means:** the sampler treated the visible surface material as the only material present; second most likely is a rushed sampling pass that skipped the adhesive layer to save lab turnaround time.
- **First question:** "Was the mastic sampled as its own material, separate from the tile?"
- **Data to pull:** the lab report's material descriptions — confirm it lists tile and adhesive as distinct samples, not one combined result.

### Calcium chloride reading between 3 and 5 lbs MVER with a standard adhesive specified
- **Usually means:** the adhesive is marginal for this slab and will pass inspection today but is a warranty risk within 12-18 months; second most likely is a test run before the space was conditioned to service temperature, understating the true reading.
- **First question:** "What's this specific adhesive's stated MVER limit, and how far under it is this reading really?"
- **Data to pull:** adhesive manufacturer's data sheet moisture limits, the test conditioning log.

### pH reading above 10 on bare concrete with a standard adhesive already on order
- **Usually means:** the slab has residual alkaline salts (common in newer or under-cured concrete, or slabs with a history of water intrusion) that will saponify a standard adhesive; second most likely is a wetted-strip test read too early, before the reaction fully develops.
- **First question:** "Was this pH tested wet, at the recommended dwell time, and does the adhesive on order have a stated pH ceiling?"
- **Data to pull:** pH test log with dwell times and locations, adhesive data sheet pH tolerance.

### Substrate shows a visible dip or ridge under a 10-ft straightedge before flooring goes down
- **Usually means:** the slab was never checked against ASTM F710's flatness tolerance, or a prior floor covering (carpet, thick tile) masked the flaw and it was never corrected; second most likely is a expansion-joint or construction-joint mismatch that was never ground or filled flush.
- **First question:** "Has this floor been checked with a straightedge against the 3/16 in. per 10 ft tolerance, or is 'it looked flat' the only check?"
- **Data to pull:** straightedge measurements at multiple points and directions, self-leveling scope if any was already planned.

### New LVT/LVP planks show buckling or peaking at the walls within weeks of a floating install
- **Usually means:** acclimation was skipped or cut short, or the perimeter expansion gap was omitted or covered solid with caulk/base trim fastened too tight; second most likely is a long uninterrupted run with no transition joint at the interval the manufacturer specifies.
- **First question:** "Was this material acclimated on-site per the manufacturer's spec, and is there an expansion gap at every wall and transition?"
- **Data to pull:** acclimation log (dates, room temp/humidity), install photos showing gap presence, run length between transitions.

### Telegraphing (a visible pattern or ridge from the substrate) appears through new sheet vinyl or LVT within days
- **Usually means:** the substrate wasn't leveled to tolerance before install, or a legacy tile/mastic layer's edges or grout lines were left as the working surface; second most likely is adhesive applied with the wrong notch trowel, leaving ridges in the adhesive bed itself.
- **First question:** "Was the substrate leveled and checked flat before this material went down, or installed over the existing surface as-is?"
- **Data to pull:** substrate prep records/photos, trowel notch size used vs. the adhesive's specified size.

### Heat-welded seam bead is lifting, cracking, or shows a ragged trim edge shortly after install
- **Usually means:** the weld temperature was wrong for the material (too cool to fully fuse, or hot enough to scorch), or the bead was trimmed after it fully cooled instead of the warm-then-cooled two-pass sequence; second most likely is a groove cut too shallow for the rod to seat properly.
- **First question:** "What weld temperature and groove depth were used, and was the bead trimmed warm or after it cooled?"
- **Data to pull:** welder temperature setting logged against the material's rated range, groove depth measurement.

### A legacy resilient layer is being encapsulated in place but hasn't had a bond-pull or flatness test
- **Usually means:** the crew assumed encapsulation is always the cheaper default without confirming the layer is actually stable enough to build on; second most likely is a bond test that was done visually ("it looks stuck down") rather than with a pull test.
- **First question:** "Has this layer actually been bond-pull tested, or is 'it looks solid' the basis for encapsulating instead of removing?"
- **Data to pull:** bond-pull test results and location map, any visible delamination noted during inspection.

### Client requests dry-sanding or grinding of an old resilient floor to "speed up" prep before test results are back
- **Usually means:** schedule pressure is pushing the crew toward a shortcut that turns a potential nonfriable material into a friable, regulated exposure event; second most likely is a client who genuinely doesn't understand that grinding changes the material's regulatory classification.
- **First question:** "Are the bulk sample results back yet, and does the client understand grinding this before results return is a compliance and safety issue, not a scheduling one?"
- **Data to pull:** lab turnaround status, written confirmation the client was informed of the risk if they're pushing to proceed anyway.

### Blistering or bubbling appears in glue-down sheet vinyl within the first few weeks
- **Usually means:** trapped moisture vapor pushing through an adhesive that wasn't rated for the slab's actual reading, or adhesive that was rolled in before its open time allowed proper transfer; second most likely is air trapped during install from insufficient rolling with the 100-lb roller.
- **First question:** "What moisture/RH readings did this slab test at, and what adhesive class was actually used against those numbers?"
- **Data to pull:** original moisture test results, adhesive data sheet, rolling/installation log.
