# Enforcement working vocabulary

Terms a parking enforcement officer uses precisely and generalists misuse.

### Scofflaw (eligibility, not just "has tickets")

A plate that meets an ordinance's *specific* boot/tow trigger — a qualifying count or debt of tickets that are both unpaid and aged past a set number of days, with disputed tickets excluded.
**In use:** "It's not scofflaw-eligible yet — only two of the four tickets are past the 100-day mark, and the third one's under dispute."
**Common misuse:** treating any plate with several unpaid tickets as "a scofflaw" — eligibility is a specific, checkable threshold, not a general impression of a bad record.

### Boot (immobilization device)

A wheel clamp applied to a vehicle that has independently been confirmed eligible under the scofflaw ordinance — a distinct action from citation, with its own due-process requirements.
**In use:** "Plate matched on a second source and the debt check confirmed eligibility — cleared to boot."
**Common misuse:** applying it as an escalation for a single serious-looking violation or a hostile driver; it's authorized only by the specific numeric eligibility check, never by officer discretion in the moment.

### Prima facie evidence / presumption of correctness

The evidentiary status most administrative hearings give a properly issued citation — sufficient to support the violation unless rebutted, but only as strong as the specific facts recorded in it.
**In use:** "The citation carries a presumption of correctness, but with nothing but the code section on it, a 'sign was obscured' defense beats it easily."
**Common misuse:** treating the presumption as automatic proof that doesn't need supporting documentation — it's a starting advantage, not a guaranteed win.

### Return date

The date by which a cited driver must respond to a violation (pay, contest, or request a hearing) before penalties or eligibility clocks (like scofflaw aging) begin running from it.
**In use:** "That ticket's return date was 145 days ago and it's still unpaid — it clears the aging bar."
**Common misuse:** confusing the return date with the issue date; some jurisdictions set the return date days or weeks after issuance, which shifts when the aging clock actually starts.

### Community caretaking exception

A doctrine sometimes invoked to justify a warrantless search or seizure on the theory that it serves a non-investigatory public-safety purpose, not law enforcement — rejected as a justification for routine tire chalking in *Taylor v. City of Saginaw* on remand.
**In use:** "The city argued community caretaking for the chalking program; the court didn't buy it without individualized suspicion."
**Common misuse:** assuming any administrative or civil enforcement act automatically qualifies as caretaking rather than a search — courts scrutinize the actual purpose and the physical trespass involved.

### Curb color code

A widely copied model (originating in statutes like California Vehicle Code §21458) assigning meaning to painted curbs — red (no stop/stand/park), yellow (commercial loading), white (passenger loading), green (time-limited), blue (disabled).
**In use:** "It's a green curb here, 20-minute limit — check the posted time before citing."
**Common misuse:** assuming the palette is universal; several jurisdictions modify, omit, or reassign colors, so the officer verifies against the local code, not memory of the common version.

### Placard misuse (vs. ordinary space violation)

A citation for a displayed disability placard that doesn't match the registry (wrong holder, expired, revoked), distinct from and usually carrying a higher fine than parking in a marked space without any placard at all.
**In use:** "Registry showed the placard belongs to a different, deceased holder — cited for misuse, not just the space violation."
**Common misuse:** citing "no placard" or a generic space violation when a placard is present but invalid — the correct, stronger charge is misuse, built from the registry mismatch.

### Access aisle

The striped, marked space adjacent to an accessible parking space, reserved for wheelchair lift/ramp deployment — a separate protected zone from the parking space itself.
**In use:** "Placard's valid, but the vehicle's blocking the access aisle — that's its own citation."
**Common misuse:** assuming a valid placard covers any position near the accessible space, including blocking the aisle; the aisle violation stands independently.

### Grace period / grace window

A department-policy buffer (commonly around 5 minutes) after a posted time limit expires, during which a citation shouldn't yet be issued.
**In use:** "Citation photo's timestamped four minutes past the limit — that's inside our grace window, void it."
**Common misuse:** treating the grace window as a legal requirement uniform across all jurisdictions; it's typically a department policy choice, not a statutory mandate, and its length varies.

### Notice of Violation (NOV)

The formal citation document establishing the violation, code section, fine, and evidentiary basis — the record a hearing examiner reviews weeks or months after the encounter.
**In use:** "The NOV needs the sign photo attached, not just the overstay photos, or the presumption doesn't hold up."
**Common misuse:** treating the NOV as a formality completed by writing the fine amount — its evidentiary content is what actually gets tested at hearing.

### LPR (license plate recognition)

Camera-based systems that read and log plates with GPS and timestamp data, used both to time overstays without touching a vehicle and to flag scofflaw/exempt/stolen status against a database in real time.
**In use:** "LPR flagged the plate on the pass-by — now checking eligibility manually before acting on it."
**Common misuse:** treating an LPR flag as a final determination rather than a prompt to independently verify plate match, ticket ages, and dispute status.

### Adjudication (parking violations hearing)

The administrative process, separate from criminal court, where a cited driver contests a citation before a hearing examiner, typically on a preponderance-of-evidence standard.
**In use:** "It's going to adjudication — make sure the evidence packet has the sign photo and both timestamp photos."
**Common misuse:** assuming the process resembles a criminal trial with a "beyond reasonable doubt" standard; the burden and procedure are both lighter and faster than that.

### Exempt/diplomatic plate

A registration category (diplomatic, certain government, or other statutorily exempt vehicles) that alters or removes standard fine, boot, or tow procedures.
**In use:** "Plate's diplomatic — checking the department's specific protocol before doing anything beyond documenting the violation."
**Common misuse:** assuming exemption means no citation can be written at all; often it changes the *enforcement mechanism* (no boot/tow, different collection process) rather than eliminating the underlying violation.
