# Field playbook — timing, eligibility checks, and hearing packets

Executed process, not description. Four sequences: (1) LPR overstay timing, (2) scofflaw boot/tow eligibility check, (3) placard verification, (4) hearing evidence packet.

## LPR / "digital chalk" overstay-timing sequence

1. **Photograph a fixed reference point, not just the vehicle.** Valve stem against the curb seam, or a wheel against a meter post base — something that shows movement or non-movement independent of the vehicle's own position in the space.
2. **Log the session-expiration timestamp from the meter or posted time limit**, not the time of the photo — a car that arrived at 9:03 on a 2-hour meter expires at 11:03 regardless of when the officer happens to pass by.
3. **Apply the department's grace window before the violation is live** — commonly 5 minutes past expiration; a citation timestamped inside the grace window is a contest the department loses on its own policy.
4. **Re-photograph the same reference point at citation time.** Position unchanged confirms no move-and-return; position changed means the officer needs to reassess whether a new session started (return to the same space only counts as a new violation if local rules require moving out of the zone entirely, not just the one space — verify the local re-parking rule before citing).
5. **Never touch the vehicle to gather the timing evidence** — no chalk, no placing an object against the tire. The photo pair is both the citation's evidence and the department's answer to a Fourth Amendment challenge.
6. **Cite the specific code section the meter or posted limit actually enforces** (metered-parking overstay vs. general time-limited zone) — the two are usually different sections with different fines.

## Scofflaw boot/tow eligibility check

Do not act on a system flag alone. Pull the individual ticket list and check each one against both filters below before immobilizing.

| Filter | Rule of thumb | Effect if failed |
|---|---|---|
| Age | Ticket's return/due date is >100 days past, unpaid | Excluded from qualifying count/debt |
| Dispute status | No active, undecided adjudication on that ticket | Excluded entirely while pending, regardless of age |
| Threshold | Qualifying count ≥3 tickets, OR qualifying debt ≥$350 | Below either bar = not eligible |
| Plate/vehicle match | LPR read confirmed against a second source (manual read or registration lookup) | Mismatch = do not act, re-verify |
| Exemption | Not diplomatic, government, or otherwise exempt plate | Exempt plate = do not boot regardless of debt |

1. **Pull every open ticket on the plate, not just the flagged summary total.** The summary is a prompt to check, not a verified eligibility determination.
2. **Apply the age filter to each ticket individually.** A ticket 60 days old doesn't become "close enough" — it either clears the ordinance's aging bar or it's excluded, no partial credit.
3. **Apply the dispute filter next.** A ticket under active adjudication is excluded from both the count and the debt sum until the dispute resolves, even if it would otherwise qualify on age.
4. **Sum only the surviving qualifying tickets** and compare against both the count and debt thresholds — either one being met is usually sufficient under most municipal ordinances, but confirm the local rule uses "or" and not "and."
5. **Confirm plate match through a second source** before immobilizing — an LPR misread on a single character turns a lawful boot into an unlawful one on the wrong vehicle.
6. **If eligible, photograph the boot placement, the plate, and a timestamp together**, and log the specific qualifying tickets by number — the boot notice should list exactly which debts made the vehicle eligible, not "unpaid tickets" as a category.
7. **If not eligible, log the check and the reason** (which filter failed) even though no action was taken — a documented "checked, not eligible" record is what protects the department if the same plate is flagged again later and someone assumes today's non-action was an oversight.

## Placard verification sequence

1. **Read the placard number, not just its presence**, and run it against the state disability-placard registry.
2. **Confirm the registry record matches the vehicle or the person present**, depending on what the jurisdiction's placard rules require (some tie the placard to a person, portable across vehicles; others require plate match).
3. **If the registry returns no match, an expired record, or a different holder**, cite for placard misuse — a distinct, usually higher-fine violation from ordinary space misuse.
4. **Do not use physical appearance or apparent mobility as a basis to question placard validity** — a non-visible disability is a valid basis for a placard, and demanding an explanation from the holder is generally outside a patrol-level officer's authority; the citation is built from the registry mismatch, not an on-scene judgment call.
5. **If the registry matches but the vehicle is blocking a marked access aisle rather than sitting in the space itself**, cite the access-aisle violation separately — it's usually a distinct, higher-fine section under ADA-referenced local code, and a valid placard doesn't cover blocking the aisle.

## Hearing evidence packet

Assembled from exactly the facts recorded at citation time — nothing added from memory afterward.

1. **Violation photo pair** — timing reference photos (LPR) or placard/registry printout, with visible timestamps.
2. **Sign/meter/curb-marking photo**, in frame with the violation where possible, confirming the governing rule was visibly posted and legible at the time.
3. **Citation code section and fine**, matched to the specific sign/meter photographed, not a nearby or similar rule.
4. **Scofflaw/boot documentation, if applicable** — the specific qualifying ticket numbers, ages, and debt sum used to establish eligibility.
5. **Officer's contemporaneous notes**, timestamped at or near the citation, limited to observed facts — vehicle position, sign condition, driver interaction if relevant to the specific defense likely to be raised.

### Fact vs. conclusion — before/after examples

| Conclusion (unusable alone) | Specific fact (usable) |
|---|---|
| "Vehicle was clearly overstaying." | "Valve-stem position unchanged in photos taken at 11:03 and 11:52, 44 minutes past the 5-minute grace window on a 2-hour meter." |
| "Sign was visible and driver should have seen it." | "Sign photographed in the same frame as the vehicle, legible text, mounted at approximately 7 feet, unobstructed by foliage or other signage." |
| "Placard looked fake." | "Placard number 4471982 returned no match in the state disability-placard registry as of citation time." |
| "Driver was uncooperative." | "Driver was asked to move the vehicle and stated 'I'm not moving it,' then re-entered the vehicle and drove away without further comment." |
