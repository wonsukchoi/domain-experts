# Insurance Sales Agent — Vocabulary

### Needs analysis
The structured calculation of a client's insurance coverage requirement — income replacement, debt payoff, and future obligations, net of existing liquid assets and coverage.
**In use:** "Before I quote anything, let's run the needs analysis so the coverage number is defensible, not a guess."
**Common misuse:** Treated as an optional add-on step rather than the required starting point — generalists quote a round number the client mentioned instead.

### DIME method
A needs-analysis framework: Debt, Income replacement, Mortgage, Education — summed to estimate total life-insurance coverage need.
**In use:** "Running DIME on this client gets us to $1.27 million before we net out their savings."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a single universal formula rather than one stated heuristic among several income-replacement methodologies; the multiplier chosen for the income component varies by dependent age and should be stated explicitly.

### First-year commission
The commission paid to the agent on the first year's premium of a new policy, typically the largest single commission payment in the policy's life.
**In use:** "The first-year commission on the whole-life product is higher, which is exactly why I want to show you the coverage comparison stripped of that."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to be the same across all product types being compared — it isn't, and the difference is a disclosure obligation when it could plausibly be influencing the recommendation.

### Renewal commission
The (typically smaller) commission paid in years after the first policy year, as long as the policy stays in force.
**In use:** "Renewal commission on this line is about 5% of premium, which is part of why persistency — keeping policies in force — matters more than one-time sales volume."
**Common misuse:** Ignored when evaluating whether an agent's recommendation is client-aligned; renewal-heavy compensation structures create less incentive to churn clients into new policies than first-year-heavy ones.

### Risk class (underwriting class)
The category (e.g., Preferred Plus, Preferred, Standard, rated/substandard) a carrier's underwriter assigns an applicant after reviewing medical exam results and history, which determines the final premium.
**In use:** "I quoted at Standard; if your exam comes back cleaner than that, the bound premium could be lower."
**Common misuse:** Confused with the initial quote, which is generated against an assumed risk class before underwriting confirms it — clients (and inexperienced agents) treat the quote as locked.

### Contestability period
The window (typically 2 years) after a policy is issued during which the insurer can investigate and deny a claim based on a material misrepresentation on the application.
**In use:** "Replacing that policy resets your contestability clock to day one — that's part of the real cost of switching, not just the premium difference."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to be a one-time feature of the original policy rather than something that resets with a new policy — replacement always restarts it.

### Surrender charge
A fee deducted from a permanent life-insurance policy's cash value if the policyholder cancels or withdraws during a defined surrender-charge schedule (often 7-15 years).
**In use:** "The surrender charge in year 4 of this schedule is over $3,000 — that has to be in the replacement-cost comparison, not just the premium delta."
**Common misuse:** Left out of replacement pitches that only compare monthly premiums, making the switch look more favorable than the full financial picture supports.

### Suitability
The regulatory and ethical standard that a recommended product must be appropriate for the client's financial situation, objectives, and risk tolerance — a lower bar than a fiduciary standard but still enforceable.
**In use:** "Selling a policy with a 15-year surrender schedule to an 80-year-old raises a real suitability question."
**Common misuse:** Conflated with fiduciary duty; suitability requires the product be appropriate, not that it be the single best option available — the distinction matters in how a recommendation gets documented and defended.

### Accelerated death benefit (rider)
A policy feature allowing the insured to access a portion of the death benefit while still living, typically upon a qualifying terminal or chronic illness diagnosis.
**In use:** "Carrier C includes an accelerated death benefit rider at no extra cost — worth walking through even if it's not the deciding factor."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to be a standard feature across all policies; it's often an optional rider that varies by carrier and should be called out explicitly in a comparison.

### Term vs. permanent (whole life / universal life)
Term insurance covers a fixed period and has no cash value; permanent insurance covers the insured's whole life and accumulates cash value, at a materially higher premium.
**In use:** "For a 20-year income-replacement need with a defined end date, term is the fit — permanent would be solving a different problem."
**Common misuse:** Presented as a strictly better-or-worse comparison rather than a fit-to-need decision; permanent insurance is correct for specific needs (estate liquidity, lifelong dependents) and overpriced for a temporary income-replacement need.

### Free-look period
A window (typically 10-30 days, state-mandated) after a policy is delivered during which the client can cancel for a full refund with no penalty.
**In use:** "You've got a 20-day free-look period on this state's policies — review the actual contract language, not just my summary, before that closes."
**Common misuse:** Rarely mentioned proactively, even though it's a required disclosure in most states and a meaningful consumer protection.

### Rated policy (substandard rating)
A policy issued at a higher premium than standard because underwriting identified an elevated risk factor (health condition, hazardous occupation or hobby).
**In use:** "The client came back rated at Table 2 because of the pilot's license — that adds about 25% to the standard premium."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a decline in disguise; a rated policy is still coverage, just priced for the actual risk, and is often still the right recommendation.

### Illustration (life insurance)
A carrier-generated projection showing how a permanent life policy's cash value and death benefit could grow under stated (non-guaranteed) assumptions.
**In use:** "The illustration shows cash value doubling by year 20, but that's the non-guaranteed column — walk the client through the guaranteed minimum too."
**Common misuse:** Presented to clients as a guarantee rather than a projection; non-guaranteed columns depend on carrier performance and crediting-rate assumptions that can change.
