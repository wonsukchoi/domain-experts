# Safety assessment, safety plan, and documentation playbook

Filled worksheets for the three recurring tasks: present/impending danger classification, safety plan construction, and court-involved documentation.

## 1. Present vs. impending danger classification worksheet

```
Reported/observed concern: <specific facts>

Present danger check (is there an active, observable, out-of-control threat RIGHT NOW):
  - Is a caregiver's behavior currently out of control (active substance impairment,
    violent behavior, acute mental health crisis) with no protective adult able to
    intervene? <yes/no>
  - Is there an immediate physical hazard actively endangering the child right now
    (unattended infant near water, active violence in the home)? <yes/no>
  - If ANY present danger indicator is yes -> immediate protective action required
    (may include removal), not a planning process.

Impending danger check (is there a caregiver/family vulnerability that COULD become
  dangerous without specific intervention, but isn't active/out-of-control right now):
  - Specific vulnerability identified: <e.g. "single point of failure in childcare
    arrangement">
  - Is this vulnerability specific and addressable through a monitored plan? <yes/no>
  - If yes -> in-home safety plan appropriate
  - If no (vulnerability too severe/caregiver unable to participate in a plan) ->
    removal may be required
```

Rule: never let a present-danger response (immediate protective action) get delayed by treating it as an impending-danger planning question, and never escalate an impending-danger vulnerability to removal without first assessing whether a specific, monitorable safety plan could control it.

## 2. Safety plan construction template

```
Identified danger: <specific, not general - e.g. "children unsupervised during evening
  work hours when primary backup caregiver unavailable," not "inadequate supervision">

Controlling action(s): <specific, concrete steps - e.g. "mother identifies two backup
  caregivers with contact information on file">

Responsible party: <who does what, by when - e.g. "mother, within 1 week">

Verification method: <specific, scheduled - e.g. "caseworker conducts 2 unannounced
  evening check-ins over 3 weeks">

Plan reviewed with family: <date, family's own input incorporated>
Plan reassessment date: <scheduled, not open-ended>
```

Rule: every safety plan needs all four fields filled with specifics, not general commitments — a plan that says "family will ensure supervision" without naming the specific action, responsible party, and verification method is not an actual safety plan.

## 3. Court-involved case documentation checklist

- [ ] Every factual observation recorded with date, time, and source (who said/did what, not a paraphrased summary)
- [ ] Safety determination (present/impending danger classification) documented with the specific reasoning, not just the conclusion
- [ ] Safety plan elements documented in full (danger, action, responsible party, verification)
- [ ] Family strengths and context noted, not just deficits/concerns
- [ ] Any collateral contact (neighbor, school, prior reports) documented with date and specific content
- [ ] Reassessment findings documented at each scheduled check-in, not just the initial assessment

Rule: a case file read months or years later (by a court, a supervisor, or after a subsequent incident) needs to show the specific facts and reasoning behind every determination — a conclusory entry ("family appears stable") without the underlying specific observations doesn't hold up under later scrutiny.

## 4. Structured decision-making (SDM) tool application

```
Risk assessment (predictive, longer-term):
  <structured risk factors per the agency's validated instrument, scored>
  Risk level: <low/moderate/high>

Safety assessment (present-tense, urgent):
  <present/impending danger factors per the agency's validated instrument>
  Safety decision: <safe / safe with plan / unsafe - removal indicated>

Note: these are two SEPARATE assessments with separate purposes - a "moderate risk"
score does NOT by itself indicate an unsafe determination, and a "low risk" score does
NOT override a present danger finding requiring immediate protective action.
```

Rule: never substitute the risk score for the safety determination or vice versa — they answer different questions and use different structured tools for a reason.
