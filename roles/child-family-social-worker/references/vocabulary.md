# Child welfare social work working vocabulary

Terms a child, family, and school social worker uses precisely and a generalist blurs together. Format: definition → how a practitioner says it → common misuse.

**Present danger vs. impending danger** — Present danger is an active, observable, out-of-control threat requiring immediate protective action right now; impending danger is a caregiver or family vulnerability that could become dangerous without specific intervention, but isn't currently active or out of control.
In use: "This is impending danger, not present danger — there's a specific childcare vulnerability, but no active out-of-control threat happening right now."
Misuse: treating any identified risk factor as present danger, which can trigger disproportionate immediate action (removal) when a monitored in-home safety plan would adequately address an impending, not present, danger.

**Risk vs. safety assessment** — Risk assessment predicts the likelihood of future maltreatment recurring over time, typically using a validated actuarial instrument; safety assessment determines whether a child is in present or impending danger requiring protective action now. They're distinct tools answering distinct questions.
In use: "The risk score is moderate, but that's a separate question from today's safety determination — we need the safety assessment to answer whether protective action is needed right now."
Misuse: using a risk score to make an immediate safety decision, or treating a "low risk" score as if it answers whether a child is safe today — the two assessments serve different purposes and shouldn't substitute for each other.

**Safety plan** — A specific, monitorable set of interventions designed to control an identified impending danger while keeping a child in the home, distinct from a general commitment or promise, requiring named danger, controlling action, responsible party, and verification method.
In use: "The safety plan names the specific danger, the two backup caregivers as the controlling action, and the unannounced check-in schedule as verification — that's what makes it an actual plan, not just a promise."
Misuse: accepting a vague family commitment ("we'll make sure it doesn't happen again") as a safety plan — without the specific elements, it's not verifiable and won't function as protection if the underlying vulnerability recurs.

**Least restrictive intervention (family preservation principle)** — The child welfare policy principle that the intervention chosen should be the least disruptive one that can reasonably and verifiably assure child safety, with removal reserved for situations where in-home safety planning genuinely cannot control the danger.
In use: "Removal isn't indicated here — the least restrictive intervention that controls this specific vulnerability is an in-home safety plan."
Misuse: treating removal as the default, safety-maximizing response to any identified concern, without first assessing whether a less disruptive, specifically-tailored safety plan could adequately control the actual danger.

**Structured Decision Making (SDM)** — A validated assessment framework used by many child welfare agencies, separating risk assessment (predictive) from safety assessment (present-tense), intended to standardize and improve the consistency of these determinations across workers and cases.
In use: "The SDM safety assessment tool is what we use for the present/impending danger determination — it's a distinct instrument from the risk assessment tool."
Misuse: treating "SDM" as a single generic tool rather than understanding it comprises distinct instruments for distinct purposes (risk vs. safety), each requiring its own separate application.

**Case plan vs. safety plan** — A safety plan addresses an identified present or impending danger with specific, monitorable interventions; a case plan is the broader, longer-term set of goals and services (treatment, housing, employment support) addressing the family's overall functioning and the conditions driving risk.
In use: "The safety plan addresses the immediate childcare vulnerability; the case plan includes the longer-term goal of connecting mom with a stable childcare subsidy program."
Misuse: conflating the two — a safety plan needs to control an identified danger specifically and immediately, while a case plan can be broader and longer-term; treating a case plan's general goals as if they satisfy the safety plan's specificity requirement leaves the actual danger unaddressed.

**Collateral contact** — Information gathered from a source other than the family directly (a neighbor, teacher, prior caseworker, medical provider) to corroborate or contextualize a report or assessment.
In use: "The collateral contact with the school confirms the children's attendance and appearance are consistent with the mother's account — that's relevant corroboration for the assessment."
Misuse: relying solely on the family's self-report or solely on the reporting party's account without seeking corroborating collateral information, which can produce an inaccurate assessment in either direction.

**Family team meeting / family group conferencing** — A structured process bringing together the family, relevant relatives/supports, and service providers to collaboratively develop the case or safety plan, rather than the caseworker unilaterally imposing a plan.
In use: "We're using a family team meeting to develop this safety plan together, rather than me handing them a plan I wrote alone."
Misuse: treating case/safety planning as a purely unilateral caseworker determination, missing that family and collateral input both improves plan buy-in and can surface relevant context (existing supports, cultural considerations) the worker wouldn't otherwise know.

**Cultural humility** — An ongoing practice of self-reflective, non-judgmental engagement with a family's specific cultural context and practices, distinct from generic checklist-based assessment of "acceptable" family functioning.
In use: "Assessing this family's childcare arrangement with cultural humility means understanding their specific community support network, not just checking it against a generic supervision-ratio standard."
Misuse: applying a single, decontextualized standard of "normal" family functioning across all families regardless of their specific cultural, community, or resource context, which risks both inaccurate assessment and unnecessary disruption.

**Dependency court** — The court with jurisdiction over child welfare/protective proceedings, distinct from criminal or family (divorce/custody) court, where removal, reunification, and permanency decisions are formally reviewed and ordered.
In use: "This case is going to dependency court for a removal hearing — the documentation needs to hold up under that specific judicial review."
Misuse: assuming dependency court proceedings follow the same standards or process as criminal or divorce/custody court — it's a distinct jurisdiction with its own specific standards (often a "preponderance" or similar civil standard, not criminal beyond-a-reasonable-doubt) and procedural requirements.
