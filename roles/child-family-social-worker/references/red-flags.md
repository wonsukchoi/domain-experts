# Red flags — signals a child, family, and school social worker notices instantly

Smell tests with thresholds. Any one alone can be a manageable case-planning moment; two or more together warrants an immediate reassessment before proceeding.

### A removal decision is made without first assessing whether an in-home safety plan could control the identified danger
- **Usually means:** the least restrictive intervention principle may not have been genuinely applied, resulting in a traumatic intervention (removal) that a monitored safety plan could have adequately addressed instead.
- **First question:** "Was a specific safety plan considered and ruled out as insufficient, or was removal the first option assessed?"
- **Data to pull:** present/impending danger classification worksheet (see `references/playbook.md` §1), any draft safety plan considered and why it was deemed insufficient.

### A safety plan contains general commitments ("family will ensure supervision") with no specific danger, action, responsible party, or verification method named
- **Usually means:** this isn't an actual functioning safety plan — it won't hold up if something goes wrong, and it doesn't give the caseworker or family a concrete standard to meet or verify against.
- **First question:** "What's the specific danger this plan addresses, and what's the specific verification method — not just the general goal?"
- **Data to pull:** safety plan construction template (see `references/playbook.md` §2) filled out in full for the actual case.

### A general risk assessment score is being used to make an immediate safety determination
- **Usually means:** risk (a longer-term predictive question) and safety (a present-tense, urgent question) are being conflated, which can produce either an under-protective delay or an over-protective removal that the actual present situation doesn't support.
- **First question:** "Has a distinct safety assessment been conducted separate from the risk score, addressing whether the child is in danger right now?"
- **Data to pull:** SDM tool application worksheet (see `references/playbook.md` §4), separate risk and safety assessment results.

### A case file entry states a conclusory characterization ("home was unsafe," "mother is not credible") without the specific underlying facts
- **Usually means:** the file may not hold up under later scrutiny (court review, supervisor review, or reassessment after a subsequent incident), since the actual reasoning behind the conclusion isn't documented.
- **First question:** "What specific observations, statements, or facts support this characterization?"
- **Data to pull:** court-involved case documentation checklist (see `references/playbook.md` §3), the specific interview/observation notes behind the conclusory statement.

### A family's living situation is assessed against a generic checklist without considering their specific context, resources, or established strengths
- **Usually means:** the assessment may miss existing protective factors (e.g., a partially-working childcare arrangement) and risks recommending unnecessary disruption rather than building on what's already functioning.
- **First question:** "What's already working in this family's situation, and does the plan build on that, or does it treat the family as if starting from zero?"
- **Data to pull:** family interview notes documenting existing arrangements/supports, case plan's specific interventions relative to those existing strengths.

### A single incident is treated as establishing a chronic pattern without checking whether it's actually isolated
- **Usually means:** the response (potentially including removal) may be disproportionate to a specific, addressable vulnerability rather than a demonstrated ongoing pattern of danger.
- **First question:** "Is this a documented pattern over time, or a single incident with an identifiable, specific cause that a targeted safety plan could address?"
- **Data to pull:** history of prior reports/incidents (if any), specific circumstances of the current incident, whether it connects to a demonstrated ongoing pattern or a one-time vulnerability.

### A safety plan has no scheduled reassessment date, or reassessment hasn't occurred as scheduled
- **Usually means:** the plan's actual effectiveness may not be verified, leaving an unconfirmed assumption that the danger is being controlled.
- **First question:** "When was this safety plan last verified as functioning, and is that within the scheduled reassessment window?"
- **Data to pull:** safety plan's reassessment date, documentation of the most recent verification check-in.

### A worker's documentation or case approach shows signs of caseload-driven shortcuts (e.g., a closed case with minimal documentation despite a reported concern)
- **Usually means:** caseload pressure may be substituting for a genuine safety assessment, risking a missed danger that a fuller assessment would have caught.
- **First question:** "Does the documentation reflect a full safety assessment process, or does it read as a minimal-effort closure driven by time constraints?"
- **Data to pull:** documentation completeness against the court-involved case documentation checklist, time spent on the assessment relative to agency standards.
