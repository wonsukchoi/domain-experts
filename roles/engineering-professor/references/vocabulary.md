# Vocabulary

Working vocabulary that generalists reliably misuse. Format per term: definition, how a practitioner actually uses it, the common misuse.

## Concept inventory

- **Definition:** a validated, standardized multiple-choice test that measures conceptual understanding of a topic, distinct from a course's own exams.
- **In use:** "Their exam average went up, but the Statics Concept Inventory gain didn't move — they got better at the procedure, not the physics."
- **Common misuse:** treating a course exam average as if it measures the same thing a concept inventory does. Course exams are written by the instructor, weighted toward what was taught, and vulnerable to procedural memorization; a concept inventory is designed specifically to catch students who can compute an answer while holding the wrong mental model.

## Normalized gain (Hake's g)

- **Definition:** g = (posttest% − pretest%) / (100% − pretest%) — the fraction of the possible improvement a class actually achieved, which makes gains comparable across classes with different starting points.
- **In use:** "Raw posttest was similar across sections, but the normalized gain in Section C was less than half of A and B's — they started from a lower pretest, so raw comparison undersold the gap."
- **Common misuse:** comparing raw posttest scores (or raw point gains) across sections or years with different pretest means, which can make a strong class with weaker incoming students look worse than a weak class with stronger incoming students.

## Closing the loop

- **Definition:** ABET Criterion 4's requirement that assessment results actually drive a documented change to the program, not just get collected and reported.
- **In use:** "The data's collected — now show me the loop actually closed: what changed in the syllabus because of it?"
- **Common misuse:** treating "we discussed the data in a faculty meeting" as closing the loop. Discussion without a traceable curricular change, recorded before it's taught, is exactly the pattern program evaluators flag as a weakness.

## Student outcomes vs. Program Educational Objectives (PEOs)

- **Definition:** student outcomes are what students can do at the time of graduation (ABET Criterion 3, assessed directly in coursework); PEOs are what a program expects its graduates to achieve a few years into their careers (program-defined, assessed indirectly via alumni/employer feedback).
- **In use:** "That's a PEO, not an outcome — we can't measure 'becomes a technical leader within five years' with a rubric on a senior exam."
- **Common misuse:** writing PEOs as if they were graduation-time-measurable outcomes, or conversely writing outcomes as vague career aspirations that can't be assessed with a direct instrument inside the curriculum.

## Culminating major design experience

- **Definition:** ABET Criterion 5's specific language for the capstone requirement — a design experience that incorporates engineering standards and multiple realistic constraints and is built on knowledge and skills from earlier coursework.
- **In use:** "Calling this a capstone doesn't make it one — it doesn't touch a single standard or constraint from the earlier design sequence."
- **Common misuse:** labeling any senior-level project-based course a "capstone" regardless of whether it integrates prior coursework or imposes real engineering constraints (cost, standards, safety, manufacturability).

## Direct vs. indirect assessment

- **Definition:** direct assessment measures actual student work against a criterion (exam items, rubric-scored artifacts, design reviews); indirect assessment is self-report (course evaluations, exit surveys, alumni surveys).
- **In use:** "The self-study leans entirely on exit-survey confidence ratings for that outcome — we need at least one direct measure before the site visit."
- **Common misuse:** relying primarily on indirect measures to demonstrate an outcome is met. Evaluators expect direct evidence to carry the claim; indirect measures corroborate, they don't substitute.

## Free-rider effect (capstone teams)

- **Definition:** the pattern where one or more team members contribute disproportionately little while still receiving the team's shared grade.
- **In use:** "The flat team grade is exactly what's letting the free rider ride — nothing in the grading structure differentiates contribution."
- **Common misuse:** assuming a shared team grade properly incentivizes everyone, or that catching free-riders after the fact (via a complaint) is sufficient instead of building peer-evaluation weighting into the grade from the start.

## FE exam / PE licensure

- **Definition:** the Fundamentals of Engineering exam is the first of two exams (with the Professional Engineer exam) on the path to PE licensure in the US.
- **In use:** "Civil and structural grads take the FE near-universally; our CS-adjacent tracks barely register — don't benchmark FE pass rate the same way across programs."
- **Common misuse:** assuming FE/PE licensure relevance and uptake are uniform across engineering disciplines — civil, structural, and environmental programs treat it as near-mandatory; software- and computer-adjacent programs often see minimal uptake, and a low aggregate FE pass rate can just reflect who self-selected to sit for it.

## Active learning

- **Definition:** instructional methods that require students to do cognitively demanding work during class time — problem-solving, explaining, constructing — rather than receiving information passively.
- **In use:** "A demo video during lecture isn't active learning — nobody's doing anything with it. Give them the problem and have them work it in groups."
- **Common misuse:** labeling any non-lecture activity (a video, a live demo, a Q&A) as "active learning." The term specifically implies students generating or manipulating content themselves, not just watching something different from a textbook.

## Threshold concept

- **Definition:** a concept that is troublesome to learn but transformative once understood, and that gates progress on everything built on top of it.
- **In use:** "The free-body diagram is a threshold concept for the whole statics sequence — misconceptions there don't show up as 'statics is hard,' they show up as 'dynamics and mechanics of materials are hard' two semesters later."
- **Common misuse:** treating a persistent, recurring misconception as a simple "the student didn't study enough" problem rather than recognizing it as a specific conceptual gate that needs a targeted intervention, not more repetition of the same explanation.

## Site visit / program evaluator (PEV)

- **Definition:** the on-campus review conducted by an ABET-assigned program evaluator as part of the accreditation cycle.
- **In use:** "The PEV is going to ask for the continuous-improvement log from three cycles back, not just this year's — make sure it's continuous, not reconstructed."
- **Common misuse:** treating the site visit as a discrete event to prepare for in the weeks before, rather than the self-study and continuous-improvement records being real, ongoing documentation the visit simply audits.

## Assessment matrix (outcome-to-course mapping)

- **Definition:** the table mapping which course(s) directly assess each ABET student outcome, so accountability for each outcome sits somewhere specific.
- **In use:** "Outcome 6 is owned by the fluids lab and the senior design course — those are the two places we actually collect direct evidence for it."
- **Common misuse:** spreading an outcome's assessment thinly across every course in the curriculum so that no single course actually owns collecting direct evidence for it, which is functionally the same as not assessing it.
