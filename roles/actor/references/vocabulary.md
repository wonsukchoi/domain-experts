# Vocabulary

### Slate
The actor stating name, height, and role/project before the scene begins on tape.
**In use:** "Don't rush the slate — that's the first thing they see, treat it like the first beat."
**Common misuse:** Treated as pure paperwork to get through quickly; casting directors actually read presence and energy off the slate itself, before "action."

### Sides
The specific pages of a script given for an audition — usually one scene, not the full script.
**In use:** "I only got sides for scene 12, no idea what happens before or after."
**Common misuse:** Generalists say "the script" when the actor has received a two- or three-page excerpt and deliberately doesn't have the rest.

### Callback
A second (or later) audition round for a narrowed shortlist of actors already seen once.
**In use:** "We got a callback for Thursday, they want to see the scene with the director in the room."
**Common misuse:** Confused with an agent simply "calling back" — a callback is specifically a further audition, not a phone call.

### Credit tier (Lead / Recurring / Guest Star / Co-Star / Background)
The billing hierarchy that determines pay, prominence, and how a role reads on a resume, roughly in that descending order for television.
**In use:** "It's a nice check, but it's a Co-Star — I need a Guest Star this year to move the needle."
**Common misuse:** Outsiders use "guest star" loosely for any TV appearance, regardless of which tier it actually is.

### Scale / scale-plus-10
"Scale" is the union-set minimum payment for a role; "scale-plus-10" pays an additional 10% on top so the actor's agent commission doesn't eat into the actor's net.
**In use:** "It's scale-plus, so my net is still the full scale rate after commission."
**Common misuse:** Assuming "scale" always means the actor's final take-home, without accounting for whether the deal is scale-plus or scale-flat.

### Taft-Hartley (report)
A one-time provision letting a production hire a non-union actor for a principal role and report it to the union, starting that actor's eligibility window to join.
**In use:** "They Taft-Hartleyed me on that indie — now I have 30 days before I have to decide on joining."
**Common misuse:** Framed as "getting discovered"; it's specifically a union-eligibility mechanism, not a mark of talent or a guaranteed career turning point.

### Holding fee
Payment to keep an actor exclusively unavailable to competing advertisers for a commercial between air cycles, separate from the fee for the work itself airing.
**In use:** "The spot isn't airing this cycle, but I'm still getting the holding fee to stay exclusive."
**Common misuse:** Confused with the pay for the commercial actually being broadcast — a holding fee can be owed even with zero airtime.

### Given circumstances
Stanislavski's term for the operative facts of a scene's world — who, where, when, the relationship, and what just happened — that generate and constrain an actor's choices.
**In use:** "Before we talk about intention, what are the given circumstances — where are we, and what just happened?"
**Common misuse:** Treated as the character's entire backstory; it's specifically the facts active in this scene, not a full biography.

### Indicating
Playing an emotion presentationally, for the audience or camera to visibly register, instead of pursuing an actable want that produces the emotion as a byproduct.
**In use:** "That take reads as indicating — she's showing us she's sad instead of trying to get something from him."
**Common misuse:** Mistaken by non-practitioners for strong, committed acting, when trained eyes read it as false.

### Substitution
Replacing a fictional given circumstance with a comparable real one from the actor's own life to generate a genuine response (Hagen's technique).
**In use:** "I substituted my own grandmother for this scene — the circumstances are comparable, not identical."
**Common misuse:** Over-applied to every beat instead of reserved for moments where the actor's own resource is genuinely thin, which disconnects the actor from the actual scene partner.

### Actioning
Weston's technique of assigning a playable transitive verb to a line instead of an adjective or emotional result.
**In use:** "The action on that line is 'to disarm him,' not 'to be vulnerable.'"
**Common misuse:** Confused with simply naming the emotion in verb form ("to feel sad") rather than a verb that can literally be done to another person.

### Type
The casting-practical bucket — age range, look, energy — a casting director files an actor under for quick shortlisting.
**In use:** "I book reliably as the skeptical best friend — that's my type right now."
**Common misuse:** Dismissed as shallow or reductive, when it's the actual entry point casting directors use to narrow thousands of submissions to a workable list.

### Beat / unit
A segment of a scene defined by a shift in objective or tactic — not a pause, and not a line break.
**In use:** "New beat here — she stops trying to convince him and starts trying to leave."
**Common misuse:** Used colloquially to mean "a moment" or "a dramatic pause," rather than the specific objective-defined unit it names.

### Booked out
A formal status an actor's reps put in the system to stop submissions for a defined period.
**In use:** "I'm booked out the last two weeks of the month for the shoot."
**Common misuse:** Treated as the same as being generally busy; it's a specific instruction to reps that actively halts new submissions rather than a personal note.

### Favored nations
A contract clause guaranteeing no other performer at the same tier gets better terms than this one on the same project.
**In use:** "My deal's favored nations with the other three leads — whatever bump they get, I get."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to mean a fixed, equal number for everyone, when it actually ties one performer's terms to whatever the others end up negotiating.

### Room tone
A silent recording of a space's actual ambient sound, requested as part of self-tape specs for sound editing.
**In use:** "Grab ten seconds of room tone after the take before you cut the camera."
**Common misuse:** Skipped as pointless because "there's no dialogue in it" — it's needed precisely because it has no dialogue, for clean audio edits later.
