# Red flags

Smell tests a bench technician catches during intake, before committing to a diagnosis or a quote.

## 1. Actuation count above ~90% of rated shutter life, presented by the previous shop as "just needs a sensor clean"

**Usually means:** the prior diagnosis skipped a curtain-timing check; at this ratio, banding or intermittent symptoms are far more likely to be shutter-mechanism wear than sensor debris.

**First question:** "Has anyone measured curtain travel time on this body, or was the last visit a cleaning only?"

**Data to pull:** actuation count vs. the model's rated durability spec; prior service record if one exists; a fresh f/16–f/22 test shot to rule sensor dust in or out before touching the shutter.

## 2. Banding artifacts that appear only at shutter speeds above roughly 1/1000s

**Usually means:** curtain-timing drift — the second curtain is beginning to lag before it fully fails, a pre-failure signature specific to focal-plane mechanical shutters near end of rated life.

**First question:** "Does the banding show at 1/500s and slower, or only above 1/1000s?"

**Data to pull:** actuation count; curtain-timing rig reading for both curtains against factory tolerance; frequency of the artifact (every frame vs. intermittent).

## 3. Dust spot reappears in the exact same frame position immediately after a sensor clean

**Usually means:** the debris is adhered (not loose dust) or is actually on the mirror/viewfinder path or rear lens element, not the sensor surface — a dry-blower clean won't move adhered material or debris that isn't on the sensor at all.

**First question:** "Did we run the lens-swap test before this clean, or did we assume sensor on the first pass?"

**Data to pull:** before/after f/22 test shots at 100% magnification; lens-swap test result; sensor-loupe inspection at magnification for a scratch or etched contamination.

## 4. Autofocus miss that changes sign between close-focus and infinity

**Usually means:** a mechanical collimation or element-spacing fault, not an electronic front/back-focus offset — AF micro-adjustment cannot correct an offset that isn't constant across distance.

**First question:** "Is the miss the same direction and roughly the same amount at every distance, or does it flip?"

**Data to pull:** 3-distance resolution-chart test results; flange-focal-distance measurement against mount spec; current AFMA value already applied, if any.

## 5. Image-stabilization unit buzzes or grinds while actively engaged, not just when powering down

**Usually means:** motor or bearing wear in the floating IS group — a genuine mechanical fault, distinct from the normal lock-pin rattle heard only at power-off.

**First question:** "Does the noise happen with IS on and the shutter half-pressed, or only in the second after you power the lens off?"

**Data to pull:** noise timing relative to power state; any IS error code displayed on the body; service-manual cross-reference for that error code before quoting a full unit swap.

## 6. Repair quote lands above roughly 50% of the unit's used-market value at its current actuation count

**Usually means:** the repair is defensible only for a professional/rental context with sunk workflow costs (calibrated lenses, known-clean sensor); for a casual owner, replacement is usually the honest recommendation.

**First question:** "What would a comparable used body at this actuation count cost right now, and does the customer have workflow reasons to keep this specific unit?"

**Data to pull:** current used-market comps for the model at similar actuation count; itemized parts + labor quote; customer's stated use case.

## 7. Customer requests a repair on a body or lens 8+ years past discontinuation without asking about parts first

**Usually means:** the customer (and sometimes the intake tech) assumes parts availability that no longer exists — OEM stock is frequently gone by this point and the real question is donor-unit availability.

**First question:** "Have we checked whether the OEM part or a donor unit is available before quoting a turnaround time?"

**Data to pull:** OEM parts-portal lookup by exact part number; donor-unit listings on used-parts marketplaces; date of model discontinuation.

## 8. A focus problem "fixed" by AF micro-adjustment alone, with no distance-varying test run

**Usually means:** the tech corrected the offset at whatever single distance was tested and never checked whether the offset held at other distances — a mechanical fault will still show up the first time the customer shoots at a different distance.

**First question:** "Was the AFMA value confirmed at more than one distance, or just the one the customer complained about?"

**Data to pull:** 3-distance chart-test results post-correction; whether a collimation bench check was ever run.

## 9. Shutter unit replaced but curtain-timing not re-measured after installation

**Usually means:** the shop treated the swap as mechanically self-evident instead of re-verifying — a misaligned or improperly seated replacement unit can be back in-tolerance-looking but still out of spec.

**First question:** "Do we have a post-install curtain-timing reading on file for this unit, or just the pre-repair one?"

**Data to pull:** post-repair curtain-timing rig results for both curtains; comparison against the same tolerance used in diagnosis.

## 10. Sensor cleaning requested, but the defect only appears with one specific lens attached

**Usually means:** the fault is in that lens (rear element or aperture blades), not the sensor — cleaning the sensor will not resolve it and risks an unnecessary sensor-side intervention.

**First question:** "Does the spot show up with a second lens on the same body?"

**Data to pull:** lens-swap isolation test result; aperture-blade inspection on the suspect lens; rear-element cleanliness check.
