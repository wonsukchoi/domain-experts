# Vocabulary

Terms generalists (GCs, property managers, homeowners) use loosely that a glazier keeps sharply separate — each with a definition, how it's actually used on the job, and the common misuse.

## Tempered vs. heat-strengthened vs. annealed glass

**Annealed** glass is ordinary float glass, slowly cooled, with no induced surface compression — it breaks into large, sharp shards. **Heat-strengthened** glass is reheated and cooled faster, inducing moderate surface compression (roughly 3,500–7,500 psi per ASTM C1048) — about twice annealed's strength, breaks into larger fragments than tempered, and resists thermal stress better than annealed, but is not a safety glazing. **Fully tempered** glass is cooled fastest, inducing high surface compression (10,000+ psi per ASTM C1048) — about four times annealed's strength, breaks into small granular pieces, and is the safety-glazing default for hazardous locations.

**In use:** "This location needs safety glazing, not just something stronger — heat-strengthened won't satisfy the code even though it's tougher than annealed."

**Common misuse:** assuming "stronger glass" automatically satisfies a hazardous-location requirement. Heat-strengthened is stronger than annealed but breaks in large, sharp pieces like annealed — it does not meet safety-glazing code in a hazardous location. Only tempered or laminated does.

## Laminated glass vs. insulating glass unit (IGU)

**Laminated glass** is two or more glass plies bonded with an interlayer (typically PVB) — it stays adhered to the interlayer when broken, satisfying safety glazing and offering sound/UV benefits. An **IGU** (insulating glass unit) is two or more panes separated by a sealed airspace (often argon-filled) for thermal performance — the seal, not the glass, is what fails over time.

**In use:** "The fogging is an IGU seal problem — laminated glass doesn't have an airspace to fail in the first place."

**Common misuse:** treating "double-pane" and "laminated" as the same thing because both use two lites. An IGU can also be laminated on one or both lites (a laminated IGU), but the seal and the interlayer are separate failure points that need to be diagnosed separately.

## Safety glazing vs. hazardous location

**Hazardous location** is the code's term (IBC 2406.4) for where glass is at meaningfully elevated risk of human impact — door-adjacent, guards, stair-adjacent, wet enclosures. **Safety glazing** is the glass type (tempered or laminated) the code requires once a location is classified as hazardous.

**In use:** "It's a hazardous location by the door-swing rule, so it needs safety glazing — the location classification comes first, the glass type follows from it."

**Common misuse:** using "safety glass" to describe any glass that seems durable, or assuming a location is or isn't hazardous based on appearance rather than the specific measured thresholds (24" door arc, 36"/60" stair proximity, 60" wet-enclosure radius).

## Design pressure (DP) vs. structural load rating

**Design pressure (DP)**, expressed in psf (pounds per square foot), is the wind pressure a specific opening is engineered to resist, positive and negative, usually set by the project's structural engineer per ASCE 7 and the building's height/exposure. The **structural load rating** or **load resistance (LR)** is what a specific glass makeup and size can actually withstand, per ASTM E1300.

**In use:** "The opening's DP is −55 psf; this makeup's LR only clears the low-20s, so it doesn't work regardless of what glass type the code requires."

**Common misuse:** treating the safety-glazing code requirement as if it also certifies structural adequacy. Code compliance and load capacity are answered by two different documents (IBC vs. the engineer's glazing schedule) and neither substitutes for the other.

## Setting block vs. edge block

A **setting block** sits at the bottom of the glazing pocket and carries the glass's dead weight, transferring it to the sill. An **edge block** (or spacer/shim) sits at the sides and top, centering the glass in the pocket and maintaining edge clearance — it carries little to no weight.

**In use:** "The setting blocks are sized for the full lite weight; the edge blocks are just there to hold the clearance, not to bear load."

**Common misuse:** sizing edge blocks like setting blocks (unnecessary and can restrict thermal movement), or, worse, omitting setting blocks and letting edge blocks bear weight they weren't specified for.

## Edge clearance vs. face clearance (bite)

**Edge clearance** is the gap between the glass edge and the bottom of the glazing pocket, giving room for thermal expansion and deflection. **Face clearance**, or **bite**, is how far the frame overlaps the glass face — how much of the glass edge is actually engaged and held by the frame.

**In use:** "The bite is fine at 5/8", but the edge clearance is tight — the glass has room to be held, just not room to move."

**Common misuse:** conflating the two when troubleshooting a fit issue — a lite that's hard to set is usually an edge-clearance problem (too little room at the bottom), while a lite that rattles or can walk out under wind load is usually a bite problem (not enough engagement).

## Seal failure vs. desiccant saturation

**Seal failure** is a physical break in the IGU's edge seal (structural or secondary sealant), allowing moisture and outside air to migrate into the airspace. **Desiccant saturation** is the spacer's moisture-absorbing material reaching capacity — a downstream consequence of a seal failure, not an independent cause, since an intact seal keeps outside moisture from ever reaching the desiccant.

**In use:** "The desiccant's saturated, which confirms the seal's been compromised for a while — this isn't a fresh failure."

**Common misuse:** describing desiccant saturation as if it happens on its own inside a properly sealed unit. If the desiccant is saturated, the seal has already failed; treating them as two separate potential problems misses that one causes the other.

## Argon fill / fill retention rate

Many IGUs replace the air between panes with **argon** for better thermal performance. **Fill retention rate** is the percentage of gas fill lost per year — published IGMA/manufacturer specs typically target retention losses under roughly 1%/year for a properly sealed unit; a unit measurably losing fill faster than that is on a failure trajectory even before visible fogging appears.

**In use:** "The unit's still clear, but the fill-retention numbers on this batch have been running high — that's an early warning, not a current defect."

**Common misuse:** assuming argon fill is a permanent, unmonitored feature rather than a spec with a measurable decay rate — and assuming visible fogging is the first sign of a problem, when fill loss (detectable only by testing) usually precedes it.

## Thermal-stress crack vs. impact break

A **thermal-stress crack** originates at the glass edge, runs roughly perpendicular to it, and occurs with no contact — caused by a center-to-edge temperature differential the glass's edge strength can't absorb. An **impact break** originates from a point of contact on the glass face or edge, often with a visible chip or cone fracture at the origin, and radiates outward from that point.

**In use:** "No chip at the origin, and it starts clean at the edge — that's thermal, not somebody bumping it."

**Common misuse:** assuming any unexplained crack must be vandalism or a bad install, without checking the origin point — thermal cracks are a distinct, diagnosable failure mode with its own exposure conditions (shading, frame color, interior treatments).

## Structural silicone glazing (SSG) vs. captured (mechanically glazed) system

**SSG** bonds glass to the frame with structural silicone sealant, with little or no exposed mechanical frame on the exterior face — the sealant itself carries wind load, sized per ASTM C1401. A **captured** system holds glass mechanically in a frame with pressure plates or stops, visible from the exterior — the frame, not an adhesive, carries the load.

**In use:** "This is four-side SSG, so the bite calculation has to check the sealant's tensile rating against the DP, not just glass thickness."

**Common misuse:** treating an SSG bite as a cosmetic dimension rather than a load-bearing calculation — an undersized bite reduces the safety factor against the sealant's rated capacity, independent of whether the glass itself meets the wind load.

## Glazing pocket vs. rabbet

The **glazing pocket** is the full recessed channel in the frame that receives the glass, including the setting blocks, edge blocks, and sealant or gasket. The **rabbet** is specifically the stepped ledge or lip within that pocket that the glass edge rests against or is captured by.

**In use:** "The pocket depth's fine, but the rabbet's a little shallow for this bite — we'll need a deeper stop."

**Common misuse:** using "pocket" and "rabbet" interchangeably when specifying a fix — a pocket-depth problem (not enough room for the whole assembly) and a rabbet problem (not enough engagement at the ledge) call for different corrections.
