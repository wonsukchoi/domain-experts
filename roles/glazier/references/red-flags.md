# Red flags

Smells a glazier catches before quoting a job or closing out a service call — what the signal usually means, the first question to ask, and the data to pull before committing to a fix.

### Fogging or haze visible between the panes of a sealed IGU

- **Usually means:** edge-seal failure — the argon or dry-air fill has escaped and moisture has migrated in; second most likely is a desiccant bed that's fully saturated and no longer absorbing interior moisture.
- **First question:** "Is the haze on the glass surface itself, or between the two panes — does it wipe off either exterior face?"
- **Data to pull:** IGMA certification and warranty documentation on the original unit, install date, and manufacturer fill-retention spec — a failure inside the warranty term is a manufacturer claim, not a billable repair.

### Glass call-out is 1/4" annealed within 24" of a door's swing arc

- **Usually means:** either the original installation predates current code enforcement, or someone is matching "what was there" without checking whether that location is a hazardous location under IBC 2406.4.1.
- **First question:** "Is this panel within 24 inches of the door edge, in the plane of the door or an intersecting wall?"
- **Data to pull:** field measurement from the door's swing arc to the panel edge; the local code official's records if the building's occupancy or door swing has changed since original construction.

### Edge clearance under roughly 3/16" on a lite over 10 sq ft

- **Usually means:** the lite was set by habit or against a tight glazing pocket rather than sized to the actual makeup and thermal exposure — this is the setup for a delayed edge crack, not an install-day failure.
- **First question:** "What edge clearance does this specific makeup and frame color call for, and what's actually there?"
- **Data to pull:** measured edge clearance at all four sides, glazing-pocket depth, frame color/finish, and sun exposure at that elevation.

### A crack radiating from the glass edge with no impact point, appearing weeks or months after install

- **Usually means:** thermal-stress cracking from a center-to-edge temperature differential above roughly 40–50°F — partial shading, a dark frame, or an interior treatment set close to the glass; second most likely is edge damage from fabrication (a nick that propagated under thermal cycling).
- **First question:** "Is there a chip or contact point at the crack's origin, or does it start clean at the edge?"
- **Data to pull:** photos of the crack origin, frame color and orientation, shading pattern across the day, and whether the original spec was annealed or heat-strengthened/tempered.

### Setting blocks missing, undersized, or placed at the sill's center instead of the quarter points

- **Usually means:** an installer skipped the sizing calculation and eyeballed the placement — under a heavy or laminated lite this transfers load unevenly and stresses the frame corners.
- **First question:** "What's the lite's total weight, and does the block sizing back-calculate to under 50 psi bearing stress?"
- **Data to pull:** lite weight (area × makeup weight per sq ft), block width and length as installed, and the quarter-point measurement from each corner.

### An RFI or bid request asks for glass thickness with no design-pressure value given

- **Usually means:** whoever wrote the spec assumed safety-glazing code compliance also covers structural wind load, or simply hasn't gotten a DP number from the structural engineer yet.
- **First question:** "What design pressure is this opening engineered for — do you have the glazing schedule?"
- **Data to pull:** the project's structural glazing schedule or the framing system's tested DP rating; without it, any thickness quoted is a guess.

### Client wants to reuse an existing frame/pocket depth for a heavier laminated or IGU replacement

- **Usually means:** the original pocket was sized for a lighter monolithic lite, and reusing it without checking bite and edge clearance risks under-engagement — the new, heavier lite has less margin before it can walk out of the pocket under wind load or its own deflection.
- **First question:** "What bite and edge clearance does the new makeup need, and does the existing pocket depth meet it?"
- **Data to pull:** existing pocket depth and bite measurement, new makeup's required minimum bite (from the fabricator or NGA tables), and the frame manufacturer's rated makeup range for that pocket.

### A wet-glazed structural silicone joint with a bite that looks thin against ASTM C1401's calculated minimum

- **Usually means:** the joint was sized off a rule of thumb rather than the project's actual DP and glass dimensions — an undersized bite reduces the safety factor against the sealant's rated tensile capacity.
- **First question:** "What DP and glass size was this bite calculated against, and can I see the C1401 calculation?"
- **Data to pull:** the SSG shop drawings with the bite calculation, sealant manufacturer's rated tensile strength, and the design safety factor applied.

### Interior blinds, film, or furniture installed flush against a large annealed lite on a west- or south-facing elevation

- **Usually means:** nobody flagged the thermal-stress risk before the interior finish went in — trapped heat behind the treatment raises the center temperature relative to the shaded/framed edge.
- **First question:** "Is this glass annealed, and is anything sitting within a few inches of the interior surface?"
- **Data to pull:** glass type as installed, distance from any interior treatment to the glass, and the elevation's sun exposure across the day.

### A same-day or next-day turnaround quoted for a hazardous-location repair with no glass type specified

- **Usually means:** the quote was built around matching the broken glass and hitting the schedule, without the code check happening first — the fastest available stock (often annealed) becomes the default by omission.
- **First question:** "What glass type does this location require, and is that what's actually being ordered?"
- **Data to pull:** the hazardous-location checklist result for that opening, and the fabricator's in-stock tempered/laminated inventory versus special-order lead time.
