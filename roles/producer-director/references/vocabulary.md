# Producer/Director — Vocabulary

### Above-the-line (ATL)
Budget costs tied to creative talent negotiated before shooting starts — director, principal cast, producer, writer.
**In use:** "The ATL is locked at $650K; everything else is BTL and moves with the schedule."
**Common misuse:** treating ATL as negotiable mid-shoot the same way BTL is — ATL deals are usually contractually fixed once signed.

### Below-the-line (BTL)
Budget costs tied to production execution — crew day rates, equipment, locations, catering, transport.
**In use:** "We're $45K over on BTL through Day 12, all of it weather-hold retainer."
**Common misuse:** lumping post-production costs into BTL — post is typically its own budget category, separate from principal-photography BTL.

### Stripboard / one-line schedule
A scheduling tool grouping scenes by location, cast, and day/night rather than script order, to minimize company moves.
**In use:** "Restrip Days 15 and 16 — Locations B and C are close enough to combine into one company move."
**Common misuse:** assuming the stripboard should follow story order for the director's convenience; it's optimized for cost, not narrative logic.

### Company move
Relocating the entire cast, crew, and equipment from one location to another during a single shooting day.
**In use:** "Two company moves in one day is why we lost three hours of shooting time."
**Common misuse:** underestimating a company move's time cost — it's not just travel time, it's full re-setup at the new location.

### Day-out-of-days (DOOD)
A report showing each cast member's first and last work day, total days worked, and paid hold days.
**In use:** "Lead A's DOOD shows two hold days — that's $30K in pure schedule risk, not acting cost."
**Common misuse:** reading DOOD purely for cast cost-efficiency without cross-checking it against location groupings, which can create a cast-efficient but location-inefficient schedule.

### Contingency
A reserved percentage of the below-the-line budget (commonly ~10%) held against unplanned overages.
**In use:** "We've drawn 44.6% of contingency by the midpoint — that's above the review threshold."
**Common misuse:** treating contingency as available budget from the start of the shoot rather than a reserve to be protected until genuinely needed.

### Completion bond
Insurance guaranteeing a film's financiers that the production will be completed and delivered, or the bonding company will step in (and can take over creative control) if it isn't.
**In use:** "The bonder flagged our schedule variance — that's an external signal, not just our own cost report."
**Common misuse:** treating a completion bond as a safety net that removes the need for a realistic schedule, rather than insurance against genuinely unpredictable risk.

### Greenlight
The financing decision to formally begin pre-production/production, made when budget, schedule, and financing structure all close together.
**In use:** "We can't greenlight until the tax-credit financing closes — the script being ready doesn't change that."
**Common misuse:** treating greenlight as a creative approval of the script rather than a financing decision that happens to require the script to be ready.

### Coverage
The set of camera angles and takes shot for a given scene, giving the editor options in post.
**In use:** "Cut coverage on Scene 47 — we don't need the reverse angle, it's a transitional beat."
**Common misuse:** assuming more coverage is always safer; excess coverage on a low-priority scene steals time from a high-priority one later in the day.

### Setup
A single camera position and lighting configuration for one shot; the basic unit of shooting-day pace.
**In use:** "We're averaging 22 setups a day — today's plan calls for 30, that's not realistic."
**Common misuse:** planning a shooting day's ambition around an idealized setup count instead of the crew's demonstrated historical pace.

### Below-the-line day rate
The average per-day cost of crew, equipment, and location fees for a single shooting day.
**In use:** "Every day we add to the schedule costs us the BTL day rate, not the ATL total."
**Common misuse:** conflating a single day's overage with the whole-project overage rate — a $45K overage on 12 days is a different signal than $45K spread across 24.

### Standby / hold
Paying a cast or crew member their full rate for a day they don't work, typically due to a weather hold or schedule bump.
**In use:** "Two standby days on Pier 7 cost us $45K in retainer with zero footage to show for it."
**Common misuse:** treating hold days as a sunk cost with no further implication, rather than a signal that the same risk (weather, location) remains unretired on the rest of the schedule.

### Pre-sales / gap financing
Revenue committed by distributors before a film is finished (pre-sales) or a loan bridging the difference between secured financing and total budget (gap financing) — two pieces of the financing waterfall that must close before greenlight.
**In use:** "We're 70% financed through pre-sales; the gap loan closes the rest."
**Common misuse:** counting pre-sales estimates that are "in negotiation" as closed financing when building the greenlight case.
