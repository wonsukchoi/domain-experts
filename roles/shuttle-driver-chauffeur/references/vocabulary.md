# Vocabulary

Terms generalists conflate that a chauffeur/dispatcher keeps sharply separate. Each: definition, a sentence a practitioner would actually say, and the common misuse.

### Buffer vs. padding

**Buffer** is time allocated to a specific, identifiable source of variance (a stop's boarding time, a known-slow property) and tracked against actual usage as the run proceeds. **Padding** is an undifferentiated round number added to a total without attribution to any specific stop.

**In use:** "Don't just pad the total by twenty minutes — give Hotel C its own seven-minute buffer, because that's the stop that actually runs long."

**Common misuse:** treating a flat add-on to total drive time as equivalent to per-stop buffer; padding hides which stop is the actual risk until it's too late to recover.

### Slack (or float)

The unspent portion of a run's contingency margin at any point in execution, tracked live as stops are completed. Borrowed from project-scheduling terminology, applied here to route timing.

**In use:** "We burned twelve of our fifteen minutes of slack at Hotel B — three minutes left before Hotel C and the drive to the airport."

**Common misuse:** treating slack as fixed for the whole trip instead of a running balance that depletes stop by stop and can partially be recovered.

### Grace period vs. no-show

**Grace period** is the contractually or conventionally defined window a driver waits past the scheduled pickup time before treating a passenger as a no-show — commonly 15 minutes for airport meet-and-greet, 5–10 minutes for residential/office curbside. **No-show** is the status (and billing trigger) once that window has elapsed without the passenger appearing or making contact.

**In use:** "We're at grace plus four minutes on the residential pickup — call it a no-show and bill per the account terms."

**Common misuse:** waiting indefinitely out of politeness, which converts one no-show into a missed deadline for every other passenger on the manifest; or invoking no-show billing before the contracted grace period has actually elapsed.

### Meet-and-greet

An airport pickup service level where the chauffeur waits inside the terminal (often past security, holding a name sign) and assists with baggage claim, rather than curbside-only pickup. Carries a longer standard grace period than curbside because flight arrival and deplaning time are inherently variable.

**In use:** "This is meet-and-greet, not curbside — the fifteen-minute grace starts from wheels-down, not from the original scheduled arrival time."

**Common misuse:** applying curbside grace-period logic (5–10 minutes) to a meet-and-greet booking, which produces premature no-show calls against passengers still clearing customs or baggage claim.

### Manifest

The full passenger-and-cargo roster for a run: names, pickup stops, passenger count per stop, and luggage count per passenger — the input the entire schedule and vehicle assignment are computed from.

**In use:** "Get me the manifest before I confirm the vehicle — I need bag counts per stop, not just passenger names."

**Common misuse:** confirming a vehicle or schedule from passenger count alone, without the luggage detail that actually determines whether the assigned vehicle's cargo volume clears.

### Cargo cube (cubic footage)

The usable cargo volume behind the last occupied row of seating in a specific vehicle configuration — varies substantially within the same model depending on whether a third row is folded or occupied.

**In use:** "It's an Escalade, but with the third row up for six passengers we're down to about 15 cubic feet of cube, not the 41 you get with it folded."

**Common misuse:** assuming a vehicle's advertised or nameplate cargo capacity applies regardless of current seating configuration.

### Following interval

The time-based (not distance-based) gap maintained behind the vehicle ahead, measured in seconds so it scales correctly with speed. Passenger-transport standards widen this beyond the generic solo-driving rule.

**In use:** "Four seconds minimum in daylight with a passenger aboard — six at night or wet, because they're not bracing for the stop the way you are."

**Common misuse:** using a fixed distance ("two car lengths") instead of a time interval, which doesn't scale with speed and collapses the actual safety margin on highways.

### As-directed vs. point-to-point vs. scheduled service

**As-directed (hourly)** service has the client actively redirecting the route and stops in real time, billed by time rather than by destination. **Point-to-point** is a single fixed origin-destination trip. **Scheduled service** covers recurring or pre-planned multi-stop runs (airport pickups, shuttle circuits) built against a hard external deadline.

**In use:** "This account is as-directed — don't build a rigid stop schedule for it, the client will redirect us twice before lunch."

**Common misuse:** applying scheduled-service buffer discipline to an as-directed account (over-constraining a client who wants flexibility), or treating a point-to-point trip's simplicity as license to skip the luggage-vs-cargo check.

### Deadheading

Driving a vehicle with no revenue passenger aboard — repositioning to the next pickup, or returning to base after a drop-off — which consumes driver time and fuel without billable service.

**In use:** "Factor the twenty-minute deadhead back from the airport into the driver's next assignment, or we'll be late to the following pickup."

**Common misuse:** planning back-to-back assignments off drop-off time alone, ignoring the deadhead segment needed to reach the next pickup.

### Curb dwell time

The duration a commercial vehicle is permitted to occupy an airport curbside zone before being subject to citation or a move-along order by airport authority staff — commonly a 3–5 minute window, varying by airport.

**In use:** "We're at dwell plus two minutes with bags still in the trunk — pull to the holding lot and re-approach once the passenger's actually curbside."

**Common misuse:** treating curb dwell as unlimited "as long as we're not blocking anyone," when airport authorities enforce it regardless of traffic conditions.

### Discretion (confidentiality norm)

The professional obligation to never repeat, to anyone outside a direct safety/legal-reporting obligation, anything seen or heard while transporting a passenger — including conversations, itinerary details, and the identity of who was in the vehicle.

**In use:** "I don't discuss who's in the car or what they talked about — with other clients, with dispatch, or online — that's the job, not a favor."

**Common misuse:** treating discretion as only covering dramatic secrets, when the actual standard covers any detail that could let someone else identify the passenger, their conversation, or their destination — "harmless" gossip is still a breach.
