# Red flags — what a fund manager notices instantly

Smell tests with thresholds. Any single one can be innocent; two or three together are a pattern.

### A strong track record presented to investors with no factor/beta attribution separating skill from favorable style exposure
- **Usually means:** some or most of the apparent outperformance may be explained by a favorable market regime for the fund's specific style, not repeatable skill.
- **First question:** "What percentage of this outperformance is attributable to factor/style exposure versus genuine security selection?"
- **Data to pull:** the fund's factor exposures (beta to relevant style factors), the same factor's performance over the period, a decomposition of excess return.

### A position sized above standard concentration limits because conviction "feels" unusually high
- **Usually means:** high conviction alone doesn't change the consequence of being wrong — an oversized position on a wrong thesis can be catastrophic in a way a properly-sized one isn't.
- **First question:** "What does this position size look like against our standard concentration limits, and what's the specific, articulable edge justifying an exception?"
- **Data to pull:** current position size versus policy limits, the specific stated information/skill edge for this position.

### A trade or strategy shift that falls outside the fund's stated mandate, executed without investor disclosure or approval
- **Usually means:** an implicit promise about the fund's risk profile is being broken, regardless of whether the specific trade turns out to be profitable.
- **First question:** "Does this fall within our stated mandate, and if not, has it been disclosed or approved through the appropriate process?"
- **Data to pull:** the fund's stated mandate/investment guidelines, the specific trade or strategy in question.

### A portfolio that looks diversified under normal market conditions with no stress-scenario correlation check
- **Usually means:** components that appear uncorrelated in calm markets can move together under crisis conditions, providing much less real protection than the calm-market correlation matrix suggests.
- **First question:** "What does this portfolio's correlation structure look like under a stress scenario, not just current/calm-market conditions?"
- **Data to pull:** current correlation matrix, historical stress-period correlation data for the same or similar assets.

### A fee structure or sub-advisor relationship evaluated on gross performance without a net-of-fee comparison against the relevant benchmark
- **Usually means:** the strategy may not actually be adding value for the end investor once fees are properly accounted for, regardless of how the gross number looks.
- **First question:** "What's the net-of-fee performance against the benchmark, not the gross number?"
- **Data to pull:** gross returns, full fee structure, net-of-fee performance versus benchmark over a comparable period.

### A manager's own historical performance cited as evidence of skill with no check on how many similar strategies existed over the same period
- **Usually means:** survivorship bias may be inflating the apparent significance of the track record — many similar strategies could show a strong record purely by chance, with the unsuccessful ones no longer visible.
- **First question:** "How many comparable strategies/funds existed over this period, and how many would show a similarly strong record by chance alone?"
- **Data to pull:** the size of the comparable strategy universe over the period, base-rate expectations for strong performance by chance.

### Investor communication that attributes good periods to skill and bad periods to external market conditions asymmetrically
- **Usually means:** performance attribution is being applied inconsistently — the same rigor used to explain away a bad period should be applied to a good one before claiming credit.
- **First question:** "Would the same beta/alpha decomposition we'd apply to a bad period also get applied here, or is this period getting a different standard?"
- **Data to pull:** the attribution methodology used in past investor communications during both strong and weak periods, checked for consistency.

### Risk metrics reported as a single point-in-time snapshot with no trend or stress-test context
- **Usually means:** a risk figure that looks acceptable today may be masking a trend or a vulnerability that only shows up under a specific adverse scenario.
- **First question:** "How has this risk metric trended over the recent period, and how does it behave under a relevant stress scenario?"
- **Data to pull:** the risk metric's trend over recent quarters, stress-test or scenario-analysis results if available.
