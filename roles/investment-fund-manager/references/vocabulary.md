# Investment fund management working vocabulary

Terms a fund manager uses precisely. Format: definition → how a practitioner says it → common misuse.

**Alpha** — The portion of a fund's return attributable to genuine skill (security selection, timing) after removing the portion explained by market or factor exposure (beta), the actual measure of manager-added value.
In use: "Of our 16 points of outperformance, only 3 points is estimated alpha — the rest is factor exposure that happened to be in a strong regime."
Misuse: presenting total outperformance as if it were all alpha, without decomposing how much is explained by favorable beta/factor exposure during the measurement period.

**Beta (factor exposure)** — The portion of a fund's return explained by its exposure to the broader market or a specific style factor (value, momentum, size), as distinct from genuine, idiosyncratic skill.
In use: "Our 1.4x tilt to the value factor explains most of this year's outperformance — that's beta, not alpha."
Misuse: attributing factor-driven returns to manager skill, especially during a period when the relevant factor happened to perform unusually well.

**Sharpe ratio** — A risk-adjusted return measure: excess return over the risk-free rate divided by the standard deviation of returns, used to compare performance on a "return per unit of risk" basis rather than raw return alone.
In use: "The higher-return fund actually has a lower Sharpe ratio — it's earning that return by taking proportionally more risk, not by being better managed."
Misuse: comparing funds or strategies on raw return alone without normalizing for the risk taken to achieve it, which can make a riskier bet that happened to pay off look like superior management.

**Survivorship bias** — The distortion in evaluating a track record caused by only observing strategies/funds that survived and performed well, while similar strategies that failed or underperformed are no longer visible in the comparison set.
In use: "Before treating this as proof of skill, how many similar small-cap value funds existed 5 years ago, and how many of those would show a strong record purely by chance?"
Misuse: evaluating a strong track record in isolation without accounting for how many comparable strategies existed and would show similarly strong results by chance alone, inflating the apparent statistical significance of the record.

**Mandate (fund mandate)** — The stated scope of permissible investments, risk levels, and strategy that a fund committed to when raising capital, constraining what deviations are acceptable regardless of whether they'd be profitable.
In use: "That trade would generate real return, but it's outside our stated mandate — it needs investor disclosure or a mandate amendment, not silent execution."
Misuse: treating the mandate as a flexible guideline to be quietly stretched for a compelling opportunity, rather than a specific commitment made to investors about what risk they signed up for.

**Fee drag** — The cumulative negative effect of fees on investor returns over time, compounding similarly to how returns compound, making even a small annual fee difference materially significant over a long horizon.
In use: "A 1% higher fee doesn't sound like much, but over 20 years of compounding, fee drag alone explains a meaningfully lower ending balance."
Misuse: evaluating fee differences only on their immediate annual size without considering their compounding effect on long-horizon investor outcomes.

**Position sizing (concentration limits)** — The practice of sizing individual positions according to systematic rules (conviction level bounded by diversification/risk limits) rather than by how attractive an opportunity seems in isolation.
In use: "Even at high conviction, this position is capped at 8% of the portfolio per our concentration policy — no single wrong bet can be catastrophic."
Misuse: overriding standard position-sizing limits for a specific opportunity that "feels" unusually compelling, exposing the portfolio to outsized damage if that specific high-conviction thesis turns out wrong.

**Correlation (stress-scenario)** — The degree to which different assets move together, which can differ significantly between calm markets (where correlations may appear low, suggesting diversification) and crisis conditions (where correlations often spike, reducing real diversification benefit).
In use: "These assets look uncorrelated in the current data, but stress-period history shows correlation spiking to 0.85+ during past crises — that's the number that matters for tail-risk planning."
Misuse: relying on calm-market correlation data alone to assess a portfolio's diversification, missing that real diversification benefit often disappears exactly when it's needed most (during a crisis).

**Performance attribution** — The systematic process of decomposing a fund's return into its component sources (market/beta exposure, factor tilts, security selection/alpha, timing), used to honestly explain what actually drove a period's results.
In use: "Performance attribution shows the outperformance is 81% factor-driven and 19% security-selection alpha — that's what goes in the investor letter, not just the headline number."
Misuse: reporting only the aggregate return number to investors without a decomposition of what actually drove it, leaving investors unable to distinguish repeatable skill from a favorable but temporary market regime.

**Value-at-risk (VaR)** — A statistical estimate of the maximum expected loss over a given time period at a given confidence level, used as one input to portfolio risk management, not a guarantee or a complete risk picture on its own.
In use: "Our 1-day 95% VaR is $2.4M, but that doesn't capture the tail scenario beyond the 95th percentile — that's what the separate stress test is for."
Misuse: treating VaR as a complete risk measure or a hard ceiling on possible losses, when it specifically describes an expected threshold at a given confidence level and says relatively little about the severity of losses beyond that threshold.
