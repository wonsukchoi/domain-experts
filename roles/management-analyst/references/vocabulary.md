# Management-analyst working vocabulary

Terms a strategy/ops consultant uses precisely and generalists blur together. Format: definition → how a practitioner says it → common misuse.

**MECE (mutually exclusive, collectively exhaustive)** — A structuring discipline for an issue tree: branches don't overlap (mutually exclusive) and together cover the whole problem (collectively exhaustive).
In use: "Org design, shared-services, and indirect spend are MECE here — no FTE shows up in two branches, and there's no fourth bucket we're ignoring."
Misuse: calling any bulleted list of considerations "MECE" without checking overlap or completeness — most first-draft issue trees fail one or the other.

**Span of control** — The number of direct reports a manager has.
In use: "Manager span is 4.3 in a function doing standardized transactional work — that's the delayering signal, not the headcount number itself."
Misuse: treating a single benchmark (e.g., "8 is right") as universal regardless of whether the work is homogeneous or genuinely complex/coaching-intensive.

**Layers** — The count of hierarchical levels between the top of the org and the front line.
In use: "Seven layers between CEO and shop-floor supervisor, and the Dept Head layer has a 2.8 span — that's a coordination layer, not a management one."
Misuse: discussing layers and span separately, as if removing one doesn't mechanically require changing the other.

**Zero-based organization design** — Staffing a function from a blank sheet based on today's actual workload, rather than incrementally adjusting last year's headcount.
In use: "Zero-based, Procurement needs 5 category managers and 7 transactional processors — not the 25 FTE currently spread across three uncoordinated plant teams."
Misuse: applying it as an across-the-board headcount cap rather than a workload-driven staffing exercise — it becomes an excuse for an arbitrary number instead of an analysis.

**Hypothesis-driven approach** — Forming a likely answer early from experience and pattern-matching, then structuring the engagement to prove or disprove it, rather than gathering data until an answer emerges.
In use: "Our week-one hypothesis is that the SSC branch carries most of the savings — week three's process data either confirms that or we redirect effort to procurement."
Misuse: treating the hypothesis as the conclusion and reading disconfirming data as noise instead of a signal to revise.

**Run-rate savings vs. in-year (realized) savings** — Run-rate is the normalized annual saving once fully implemented; in-year is what actually hits the current fiscal year's P&L, reduced for notice periods and phased rollout.
In use: "$12.7M run-rate, but only about $7.6M lands this year — full run-rate isn't reached until month 18."
Misuse: quoting only run-rate to a board or CFO, which reads as a bigger and faster win than the P&L will actually show.

**RACI (Responsible, Accountable, Consulted, Informed)** — A matrix assigning exactly one Accountable owner and any number of Responsible/Consulted/Informed parties to each recurring decision.
In use: "Two people were marked Accountable on invoice approval — that's the redesign's real defect, fix it before go-live."
Misuse: building the matrix once and filing it, instead of testing it against the last several real instances of each decision to see who actually decided.

**SIPOC (Suppliers, Inputs, Process, Outputs, Customers)** — A one-page process-scoping table used to baseline a process before redesigning it.
In use: "The SIPOC surfaced that Texas does a manual three-way match while Ohio has automation already licensed — that's the gap, not headcount."
Misuse: writing a SIPOC as a narrative description rather than a filled table with the actual steps, inputs, and outputs named.

**Shared-services center (SSC)** — A consolidated unit performing a standardized transactional function (payroll, AP, tier-1 IT) for multiple business units or sites from one location.
In use: "SSC covers payroll and AP across all three plants; Quality stays plant-embedded because the underlying process genuinely differs by production line."
Misuse: assuming any back-office function can be centralized regardless of whether the underlying process is actually the same across sites.

**Build-vs-buy analysis** — A structured comparison of internal capability build versus outsourcing/purchasing, on total cost and control, not price alone.
In use: "Build-vs-buy on the SSC's tier-2 IT support came out build — the BPO quote was 15% cheaper on labor but added a 6-week SLA response time we can't accept for line-down tickets."
Misuse: comparing only the headline price of the "buy" option against fully loaded internal cost, ignoring control, SLA, and transition risk.

**Operating model** — The combination of org structure, process, governance, and location choices that together determine how work actually gets done, distinct from strategy (what to do) or org chart (who reports to whom) alone.
In use: "The org chart didn't change much, but the operating model did — decisions that used to require a plant GM sign-off now route through the category manager."
Misuse: using "operating model" as a synonym for org chart — the chart is one input to it, not the whole thing.

**Cost bridge (savings bridge)** — A line-by-line reconciliation from a baseline cost figure to a target figure, with each line tied to a named driver.
In use: "The bridge shows $12.7M of run-rate savings across four named drivers — nothing in that number is unattributed."
Misuse: presenting a single "target minus baseline equals savings" number without the line-by-line drivers that let finance verify it.

**Dis-economies of scale (in shared services)** — The point past which further consolidation adds coordination and standardization cost that offsets the labor savings.
In use: "We stopped the SSC scope at payroll/AP/tier-1 IT — pushing Quality in would hit dis-economies, since the underlying processes aren't standardizable."
Misuse: assuming consolidation savings scale linearly with however many functions or sites get folded in.
