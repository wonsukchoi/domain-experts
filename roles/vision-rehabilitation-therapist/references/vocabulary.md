# Vocabulary

### Preferred Retinal Locus (PRL)
The area of retina, outside a central scotoma, that a client with macular disease learns to use as their functional "center" of vision.
**In use:** "Her PRL is superior to the scotoma, so we're training her to fixate slightly upward of where she thinks she's looking."
**Common misuse:** Assuming any off-center glance the client happens to use is "the" PRL — it has to be identified systematically and trained consistently, or the client reverts to an inefficient, inconsistent gaze pattern under stress.

### Acuity reserve
The multiple of magnification above bare acuity-matching that a device needs to supply for sustained, fluent task performance rather than threshold letter identification.
**In use:** "20/200 needs 5x just to match 20/40 print, but reading fluency needs a 2x reserve on top of that — call it 10x."
**Common misuse:** Prescribing the base acuity-matching power (1x reserve) for a sustained reading task and treating the resulting slow, effortful performance as a training problem instead of an underpowered device.

### Diagnostic (clinical) acuity vs. functional vision
Clinical/diagnostic acuity is the Snellen or logMAR chart result under standardized clinic lighting and contrast; functional vision is what the person can actually do with their vision in their own tasks and environments.
**In use:** "Chart says 20/100, but functionally she can't read a stove dial in her own kitchen lighting — that's the assessment we act on."
**Common misuse:** Treating the clinical acuity number as sufficient to plan training or devices without a task-based functional assessment.

### Dignity of risk
The principle that a client has the right to choose activities carrying real risk (independent street crossing, cooking, driving) as part of self-determination, rather than having risk minimized on their behalf by default.
**In use:** "He's choosing to cross that intersection alone knowing the traffic pattern — that's his call to make once he has the skill and the information."
**Common misuse:** Invoked to justify skipping adequate safety training or informed risk disclosure, rather than what it actually means — an informed, negotiated choice after competence is established.

### Adventitious vs. congenital vision loss
Adventitious loss is acquired after early childhood (often in adulthood); congenital loss is present from birth or very early infancy.
**In use:** "He's adventitiously blind at 58 — expect a grief-and-adjustment phase before skills training sticks; she's congenitally blind, so the gap is foundational concept development, not grief."
**Common misuse:** Running the identical intervention sequence for both — congenitally blind clients often need spatial/concept-building work that adventitiously blinded clients, who have a lifetime of visual reference, don't.

### Bioptic telescope
A small telescope mounted in the superior portion of a spectacle lens, used briefly to spot distant detail (signs, signals) rather than worn as continuous correction.
**In use:** "She spots the sign through the bioptic for a second, then drops back to carrier-lens vision for the rest of the drive."
**Common misuse:** Assuming the client looks through the telescope continuously like ordinary glasses — sustained use through the narrow bioptic field would itself be unsafe.

### Sighted guide technique
A specific protocol where the person with vision loss holds the guide's arm just above the elbow and walks a half-step behind, rather than being led by the hand or pushed.
**In use:** "Grip just above my elbow and stay half a step back — I'll narrate doors and steps as we go."
**Common misuse:** Casual hand-holding, pushing from behind, or steering by the shoulders — all less safe and skip the technique that's supposed to be fading toward independent travel, not replacing it long-term.

### Expanded Core Curriculum (ECC)
Nine instructional domains (compensatory/functional academics, O&M, social interaction, independent living, recreation/leisure, career education, assistive technology, sensory/visual efficiency, self-determination) beyond standard academics for students with visual impairments.
**In use:** "O&M is the priority domain this IEP cycle, but self-determination moves to the top once she hits transition-planning age."
**Common misuse:** Treating O&M as the only ECC domain that matters, or running through all nine as equal-weight checklist items instead of prioritizing by the student's current stage.

### Contrast sensitivity
The ability to distinguish an object from its background at reduced contrast — a separate visual function from acuity, tested with charts like Pelli-Robson or Mars rather than a Snellen chart.
**In use:** "Her Pelli-Robson score is well below normal, so this device needs contrast control, not just magnification."
**Common misuse:** Treating a passable Snellen acuity result as ruling out a functional vision problem, when reduced contrast sensitivity alone can make everyday tasks (curbs, stairs, faces) difficult.

### Two-point touch technique
A long-cane technique where the tip alternately taps the ground just outside each foot before that foot lands, clearing the path ahead of each step.
**In use:** "Keep the arc at shoulder width and make sure the tip clears outside the foot before you step onto it."
**Common misuse:** Confusing it with the constant-contact (dragging) technique, which is appropriate for different surfaces and skill levels, not interchangeable by default.

### Eccentric viewing training
Systematic instruction teaching a client with a central scotoma to redirect gaze to use their preferred retinal locus instead of their (non-functional) former center of vision.
**In use:** "We're training her to look slightly to the side of the object rather than straight at it, so the image lands on working retina."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a client will do this automatically or that any compensatory glance counts — it requires identifying a specific, consistent locus and deliberate retraining of a lifetime visual habit.

### Low vision device abandonment
A device dispensed but not used for its intended task after a defined follow-up period, typically used as an outcome/quality signal.
**In use:** "Abandonment at three months usually traces back to the fitting, not the client — check the magnification math and the lighting first."
**Common misuse:** Attributing abandonment to client non-compliance by default, rather than treating it as a signal to re-check the prescribing and training process first.

### Individualized Written Rehabilitation Program (IWRP)
The funding-agency-required goal document specifying measurable rehabilitation objectives, used by state vocational rehabilitation and blindness agencies to justify and track services.
**In use:** "The IWRP goal reads 'cross two named intersections independently in 3 of 3 trials by [date]' — that's what gets billed against."
**Common misuse:** Writing goals in vague, subjective language ("improve confidence") that can't be verified against trial data during a funding audit.

### Structured Discovery
An O&M teaching philosophy (associated with the National Federation of the Blind's training centers) where the client is given minimal step-by-step instruction and instead works through problem-solving to reach a travel objective, versus graduated, sequenced systematic instruction.
**In use:** "We're using structured discovery for the route to the pharmacy — she'll work out the plan herself with prompting, not a taught sequence."
**Common misuse:** Assuming it means "no structure" or unsupervised practice — it's a defined pedagogical method with its own preparation and safety scaffolding, not an absence of teaching.
