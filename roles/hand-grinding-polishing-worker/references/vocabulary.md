# Vocabulary

### Temper drawing (tempering)
A heat-induced softening of hardened steel, occurring when the material is heated (even briefly and locally) to a temperature that alters its heat-treated microstructure, reducing hardness at the affected spot.
**In use:** "Even without visible color change, that much continuous grinding pressure could draw temper right at the contact point — that's why we're checking hardness after."
**Common misuse:** Assuming the absence of visible tempering colors (straw, blue) means no heat-affected softening occurred — visible color typically indicates the temperature already exceeded a threshold well past where some softening begins, so its absence isn't a reliable all-clear.

### Abrasive grit progression
The specified sequence of increasingly fine abrasive grits used in grinding/polishing, where each stage is responsible for removing the scratch pattern left by the previous, coarser stage.
**In use:** "We're not jumping from 220 straight to 600 — that skips removing the 220 scratch pattern properly, and 600 won't clear it efficiently."
**Common misuse:** Skipping an intermediate grit stage to save time — the skipped stage's scratch pattern is too deep for the next, much finer grit to remove within a reasonable time, producing a surface that looks polished on top while defects remain underneath or requiring much more rework than following the sequence in order.

### Cross-contamination (metal finishing)
The transfer of metal particles from one material to another via shared grinding or polishing media, most commonly iron particles embedding into stainless steel and later causing surface rust.
**In use:** "That's cross-contamination rust, not a material defect — this wheel was used on carbon steel before it touched the stainless part."
**Common misuse:** Assuming rust on a stainless steel part indicates a material or manufacturing defect in the steel itself — surface rust from embedded iron particles introduced during finishing is a distinct, well-known, and entirely avoidable cause, separate from any inherent flaw in the stainless material.

### Ra (surface roughness)
A standard measurement of average surface roughness, used to specify and verify a required surface finish quantitatively rather than by visual/tactile impression alone.
**In use:** "Spec calls for 16 Ra — checking with the profilometer, not just feeling if it seems smooth enough."
**Common misuse:** Relying on visual or tactile judgment to assess whether a surface meets its Ra specification — "feels smooth" and "measures at the specified Ra value" aren't reliably the same thing, especially near a specification's boundary.

### Stock removal (hand finishing)
The amount of material removed from a part's surface during grinding or finishing, which must stay within the part's dimensional tolerance allowance.
**In use:** "Stock removal spec is three thousandths per side — checking dimensions periodically so we don't accidentally take off four or five."
**Common misuse:** Estimating stock removal by feel or by how much time has been spent grinding, rather than by direct dimensional measurement — hand grinding's inherent unevenness means time or effort isn't a reliable proxy for actual material removed.

### Dedicated media (grinding/polishing)
Grinding wheels, belts, or compounds reserved exclusively for use on a single metal type, preventing particle transfer between dissimilar metals.
**In use:** "This wheel's dedicated to stainless only — marked and kept separate from the carbon steel bench."
**Common misuse:** Treating media dedication as an unnecessary extra step when a wheel "looks clean" between uses — visible cleanliness doesn't remove embedded particles at a microscopic level, which is what actually causes cross-contamination corrosion.

### Heat-affected zone (grinding)
The region of a workpiece where grinding-generated heat has altered the material's microstructure or properties, which can extend beyond what's visually apparent as discoloration.
**In use:** "Heat-affected zone from aggressive grinding can extend past the visibly discolored area — that's why we're testing a slightly wider region."
**Common misuse:** Assuming the heat-affected zone is limited to exactly the area showing visible discoloration — the zone of actual property change can extend somewhat beyond the visible color change, especially with brief, high-intensity localized heating from fast grinding contact.

### Intermittent contact (grinding technique)
A grinding technique using brief, repeated contact with pauses between passes, allowing heat to dissipate rather than accumulate, as opposed to sustained continuous contact.
**In use:** "Working this in short passes with breaks, not one long continuous grind — keeps the heat from building up in one spot."
**Common misuse:** Treating grinding speed/efficiency as purely a function of contact time and pressure — intermittent contact with cooling pauses, while slower in continuous-contact terms, avoids the heat accumulation that continuous grinding risks on heat-sensitive material.

### Rockwell hardness test
A standardized hardness measurement method used to verify a material's actual hardness at a specific location, providing objective confirmation distinct from visual or dimensional inspection.
**In use:** "Dimensional check passed, but we're still pulling a Rockwell reading on this ground surface before it ships — that's the only way to actually confirm hardness held."
**Common misuse:** Treating a passing dimensional or visual inspection as sufficient evidence that a heat-sensitive part's hardness wasn't affected during finishing — hardness is a distinct material property that requires its own direct test, not inferred from other passing checks.

### Finish-to-tolerance interaction
The relationship between a part's surface finish requirement and its dimensional tolerance, where achieving the specified finish consumes some of the available dimensional allowance.
**In use:** "We can't chase a finer finish than spec here — we're already close to the tolerance limit, and more polishing means more material removed."
**Common misuse:** Treating surface finish and dimensional tolerance as independent goals to each optimize separately — on a part with both a tight finish spec and a tight dimensional tolerance, over-finishing to "make it extra smooth" can consume tolerance the part doesn't have room to spare.
