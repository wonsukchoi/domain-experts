# Red flags

Smell tests a transit safety supervisor or dispatcher catches early. Format for each: what it usually means, the first question to ask, and the data to pull.

## 1. Wheelchair securement consistently completed in under 30 seconds

**Usually means:** at least one component of the 4-point-plus-belt sequence (straps, angle, or the shoulder belt) is being skipped to protect schedule adherence — the full sequence reliably runs closer to 100 seconds.

**First question:** "Walk me through exactly which straps and belts you used on the last three wheelchair boardings."

**Data to pull:** CAD/AVL dwell-time data at stops with a wheelchair boarding flag; any onboard camera footage for the securement stations; comparison against the operator's average vs. the fleet average dwell time for the same boarding type.

## 2. Lift/ramp cycle-test failure noted on the pre-trip but the bus departs on the accessible route anyway

**Usually means:** schedule pressure or a shortage of spare vehicles is overriding the ADA out-of-service requirement — this is a denial-of-service exposure, not a maintenance backlog issue, the moment the bus carries a rider who needed the lift.

**First question:** "Who authorized this bus to run the route after the defect card was filed, and was a substitute or accessible alternative dispatched?"

**Data to pull:** the defect card timestamp vs. pull-out time; dispatch log for substitute-vehicle requests; any ADA complaint filed for that block/date.

## 3. Early departures from timepoints

**Usually means:** an operator managing to on-time-performance by leaving stops ahead of schedule to protect a downstream number — this strands riders who arrived on time and is treated by most agencies as an equal or worse violation than running late.

**First question:** "Pull the timepoint departure log for this operator over the last two weeks — how many stops show a negative (early) variance?"

**Data to pull:** AVL timepoint arrival/departure deltas; passenger complaint log for "bus left early"; comparison of early-departure rate against the agency's on-time-performance policy definition (commonly a −0/+5 minute window).

## 4. A specific paratransit rider shows a rising pattern of trip denials or "no-shows" logged by the carrier

**Usually means:** either a genuine capacity/scheduling constraint that needs a fix, or a pattern-and-practice ADA capacity-constraint violation forming — a rider being turned away in substance while it's logged as scheduling friction.

**First question:** "Show me this rider's trip request vs. completed-trip history for the last quarter, and how each denial or no-show was coded."

**Data to pull:** paratransit scheduling system trip logs; denial/no-show reason codes; comparison of this rider's denial rate against the system-wide denial rate; any ADA complaint on file.

## 5. Following distance and speed unchanged despite an active weather advisory or road-condition alert

**Usually means:** the operator is holding a dry-pavement following gap and speed at a moment stopping distance has roughly tripled or quadrupled — a symptom of chasing on-time performance through a hazard rather than adjusting the plan for it.

**First question:** "What was the target speed and following distance on this segment during the advisory, versus this operator's normal dry-day numbers?"

**Data to pull:** telematics/speed data for the segment during the advisory window; collision/near-miss reports for the same corridor on similar-condition days; the agency's weather-operations policy, if one exists.

## 6. Recurring near-miss or "close call" reports at the same intersection during right or left turns

**Usually means:** the blind zone created by the rear overhang's tail swing at that specific turn geometry is catching pedestrians or cyclists inside the arc — often a geometry problem at that corner more than an individual operator problem.

**First question:** "How many near-miss reports have come from this intersection in the last year, and from how many different operators?"

**Data to pull:** incident/near-miss log by intersection; operator diversity on those reports (one operator vs. many suggests individual training gap vs. intersection design issue); mirror/camera footage if available.

## 7. Passenger complaints cluster on "rudeness" or "escalated an argument" for one operator

**Usually means:** the operator is meeting raised-voice behavior with raised-voice responses instead of running the de-escalation ladder — a trainable gap, not necessarily a character issue, but one that predicts a physical incident if untouched.

**First question:** "Pull this operator's last five passenger-conflict incident reports — at what behavior level did the response escalate instead of de-escalate?"

**Data to pull:** incident reports with behavior-level coding if the agency uses one; any onboard audio/video for flagged incidents; whether the operator has completed CPI or equivalent de-escalation training and when.

## 8. Duty-day hours consistently landing at or just under the legal ceiling

**Usually means:** scheduling is running operators to the edge of the on-duty window or driving cap as routine, not as an occasional necessity — a fatigue-risk pattern that will eventually produce a documented HOS violation or a fatigue-related incident.

**First question:** "Show me this operator's on-duty and driving totals for the last 14 days — how many days landed within 30 minutes of the applicable limit?"

**Data to pull:** duty-day worksheets or ELD-equivalent logs; the applicable regime (FMCSA §395.5 for intercity, agency fatigue-management plan for fixed-route transit); run-cutting/scheduling records for that operator's block.

## 9. Standee counts or boarding refusals trending up on a specific run without a corresponding schedule change

**Usually means:** ridership has outgrown the vehicle size or headway assigned to that run — a capacity-planning gap that shows up first as crush loads and skipped boardings, well before ridership data catches up in the next scheduling cycle.

**First question:** "What's the average and peak passenger count on this run over the last month, against the vehicle's rated capacity?"

**Data to pull:** automatic passenger counter (APC) data by trip; boarding-refusal ("pass-up") reports; comparison of current ridership against the headway/vehicle-size assumptions used when the run was last scheduled.

## 10. Two buses on the same route arriving at a stop together ("bunching") on a repeating basis

**Usually means:** a downstream operator is falling behind (often due to a boarding-heavy stop, a signal pattern, or an under-resourced segment) and the following bus is catching up empty, which then compounds into a long gap behind it — a headway-management failure more than an individual-trip problem.

**First question:** "Where in the route does the lead bus first start losing time relative to the following bus, on a normal day?"

**Data to pull:** AVL headway data for the route by stop; ridership/dwell-time data at the stop where the gap typically opens; whether the route has a documented bunching pattern the schedule hasn't been adjusted to reflect.
