# Vocabulary

Terms generalists flatten together that transit operators and dispatchers keep sharply separate.

## Fixed-route vs. paratransit vs. demand-response

**Fixed-route** service runs a published route and schedule regardless of individual requests. **Paratransit** (specifically ADA complementary paratransit, 49 CFR §§37.121–37.139) is the origin-to-destination, next-day-booking service transit agencies must provide for riders whose disability prevents them from using fixed-route service, within 3/4 mile of a fixed route. **Demand-response** is a broader category of on-request service that may or may not be the ADA-mandated paratransit program.

*In use:* "That denial has to be logged against our complementary paratransit obligation, not just as a demand-response scheduling miss — it's an ADA compliance number."

*Common misuse:* calling any door-to-door or shuttle service "paratransit." Paratransit is a specific federally-mandated program with its own eligibility and response-time rules, not a synonym for any non-fixed-route trip.

## Schedule adherence vs. headway management

**Schedule adherence** measures whether a bus hits its timepoints against a printed schedule (used on routes with longer, less frequent service). **Headway management** measures whether buses are evenly spaced against each other (used on high-frequency routes, where riders care more about "next bus in X minutes" than a specific clock time).

*In use:* "This route runs every 6 minutes at peak — stop coaching operators on schedule adherence and start managing headway, or you'll keep getting bunching."

*Common misuse:* applying schedule-adherence discipline (chasing a printed timepoint) on a high-frequency route where the printed schedule is a planning fiction and headway is what actually matters to riders.

## Recovery time vs. layover

**Recovery time** is the schedule buffer built in at the end of a trip specifically to absorb accumulated delay before the next trip starts — it's a safety and reliability margin. **Layover** is unpaid or paid non-driving time at the end of a run for operator rest/meal breaks, which may or may not overlap with recovery time.

*In use:* "We didn't lose the schedule to the wheelchair stop — the recovery time exists to absorb exactly that, and it still had seven minutes left."

*Common misuse:* treating recovery time as slack to be eliminated for efficiency; cutting it below what accumulated stop-level variability requires turns ordinary delays into missed next-trip departures system-wide.

## Deadhead

Operating a bus with no revenue passengers aboard — between the garage and the start of a route, or between the end of one route and the start of another. Deadhead time and mileage count toward duty time and vehicle wear but not toward ridership or fare revenue.

*In use:* "That run pencils out fine on paper, but 40% of it is deadhead getting to and from the garage — the real revenue-hour cost is a lot higher than the schedule shows."

*Common misuse:* omitting deadhead time when estimating an operator's actual duty-day length, which understates how close a run pushes toward the on-duty ceiling.

## Kneeling bus vs. low-floor bus

A **low-floor bus** has no steps at the front door by vehicle design (floor at or near curb height). A **kneeling bus** (low-floor or not) can hydraulically lower the front entrance further and/or deploy a ramp to reduce the step height for boarding — it's an active mechanism, not a fixed design trait.

*In use:* "The kneel function failed the cycle test — that's a hold even though it's a low-floor bus, because kneeling and the ramp are two separate systems."

*Common misuse:* assuming a low-floor bus doesn't need the kneel/ramp check because the floor is already low — accessibility for a wheelchair user usually still depends on the ramp deploying correctly, independent of floor height.

## WC19 securement

The ANSI-RESNA/SAE J2249 standard for wheelchair tie-down and occupant-restraint systems used in transit vehicles: four-point strap securement plus a separate pelvic (lap) and shoulder belt, with anchorage points rated to defined load limits (commonly referenced near 2,500 lbf).

*In use:* "Anchor three failed the tug test — tag that station out of service, it's not WC19-compliant until it's replaced."

*Common misuse:* treating any four strap points as "compliant" securement regardless of angle, load rating, or whether the separate occupant belt was used — WC19 specifies the belt as part of the system, not an optional add-on.

## Hours-of-service (FMCSA §395.5) vs. agency fatigue-management plan

**FMCSA §395.5** is the federal hours-of-service rule that applies to passenger-carrying commercial motor vehicle drivers — chiefly intercity motorcoach operators — with its own 15-hour on-duty window and 10-hour driving cap. Most **local fixed-route transit** operators are not on Part 395 at all; their duty limits come from the agency's own fatigue-management plan required under the FTA's Public Transportation Agency Safety Plan rule (49 CFR Part 673), combined with the union contract's run-cutting rules.

*In use:* "Don't apply the §395.5 numbers to our fixed-route drivers — pull the agency fatigue-management plan, the caps are different and that's what actually governs run-cutting here."

*Common misuse:* assuming every CDL-P bus driver runs under the same federal hours-of-service rule as a long-haul truck driver; the applicable regime depends on whether the service is intercity (FMCSA) or local fixed-route (agency/FTA), and the caps and structure differ.

## CDL Passenger (P) endorsement vs. School bus (S) endorsement

The **P endorsement** (49 CFR Part 383 Subpart F) is required to operate any vehicle designed to carry 16+ passengers including the driver, covering emergency exits, passenger management, and accessibility equipment. The **S endorsement** is an additional, separate requirement specific to school bus operation (student loading/unloading procedures, railroad-crossing procedures specific to school buses) and is not implied by holding P alone.

*In use:* "He's got the P endorsement from his transit job, but he can't be dispatched on the school contract route without also testing for S."

*Common misuse:* treating the two endorsements as interchangeable because both involve carrying groups of passengers — they test different procedures and neither substitutes for the other.

## Bunching vs. gapping

**Bunching** is two or more buses on the same route arriving at a stop together (a headway failure that usually starts with one bus falling behind and the following bus catching up empty). **Gapping** is the resulting large headway that opens up behind the bunched pair, leaving riders on that segment with a much longer wait than scheduled.

*In use:* "We're not short a bus — we're bunched at Elm and Fifth, which is creating the gap riders are complaining about further down the line."

*Common misuse:* responding to a rider complaint about a long wait (a gapping symptom) by adding a bus to the schedule, when the actual fix is headway-management intervention (holding or short-turning a bus) at the point where bunching starts.

## Tail swing / off-tracking

**Tail swing** is the outward arc the rear overhang of a long vehicle sweeps *opposite* the turn direction in the first portion of a turn, before the vehicle tracks through the arc. This is distinct from **off-tracking**, where a vehicle's rear wheels follow a tighter path than the front wheels through a turn (more relevant to articulated trailers than to a rigid-body transit coach).

*In use:* "That near-miss wasn't off-tracking — it was tail swing catching the cyclist waiting on the inside of the turn before the bus even started tracking right."

*Common misuse:* importing off-tracking intuition from tractor-trailer training (watch the inside of the turn for the trailer cutting in) onto a rigid-body bus, where the relevant hazard in the first instant of the turn is the opposite side.

## Crush load / standee capacity

The passenger count at which a bus is carrying its full seated capacity plus its rated number of standing passengers (**standee capacity**), calculated from floor area per standing passenger under the manufacturer's/agency's capacity rating. **Crush load** describes exceeding that rated standee count, typically at peak periods.

*In use:* "APC data shows this trip running crush load twice a week — that's a scheduling problem, not something the operator can manage by asking people to move back."

*Common misuse:* treating any full-looking bus as "crush load" without checking against the actual rated standee capacity, or pinning the rated standee number on the driver alone to enforce without a schedule/vehicle-size fix behind it.
