# Vocabulary

### Advance
Money paid to an author upon signing, against future royalty earnings — the author keeps it regardless of sales, and it's "earned out" only once royalties owed exceed the amount paid.
**In use:** "This deal doesn't earn out under our expected-case model for 4 years — that's outside our normal 2–3 year target."
**Common misuse:** Treating the advance as simply "what the book is worth" rather than a specific bet against a modeled sales projection that can be right or wrong independently of the book's quality.

### Royalty escalator
A contract clause that increases the author's royalty percentage once sales cross defined unit thresholds (e.g., 10% of list to 5,000 units, 12.5% to 10,000, 15% beyond).
**In use:** "We offered escalated royalties above 10,000 units instead of a higher advance — gives the author real upside without us absorbing more downside risk."
**Common misuse:** Treating escalators as a minor deal-sweetener rather than a genuine tool for shifting risk allocation between publisher and author on the upside case.

### Returns reserve
A percentage of royalty payments a publisher withholds against the likelihood that shipped copies will later be returned unsold, released once the returns window has substantially closed.
**In use:** "This is a pre-order-heavy debut with no sales history — we're holding a higher reserve than our standard backlist rate."
**Common misuse:** Applying a flat reserve percentage across all titles regardless of actual return-risk profile, which either overpays authors on risky titles or underpays them on proven ones.

### BookScan (Nielsen point-of-sale data)
A syndicated retail point-of-sale tracking service covering the large majority of U.S. print book sales, used as the industry-standard source for comp-title benchmarking and real-time sales tracking.
**In use:** "Pull the comp set's first-week BookScan numbers before we size this print run."
**Common misuse:** Treating BookScan figures as complete sales figures rather than a large, representative sample — it undercounts some channels (certain independent and non-traditional retail), which matters for niche categories.

### Sell-through
The rate at which shipped/printed copies actually sell at retail, as opposed to copies simply shipped into the supply chain — the number that reveals real demand versus initial sell-in.
**In use:** "Sell-through at week 3 is only 40% of the first printing — that's below model, hold off on the reprint order."
**Common misuse:** Confusing sell-in (copies shipped to retailers) with sell-through (copies actually purchased by readers) — a strong sell-in number can mask weak real demand until returns arrive.

### Backlist / frontlist
Frontlist is newly published or soon-to-publish titles receiving current marketing focus; backlist is previously published titles still generating ongoing sales, often with minimal ongoing marketing spend.
**In use:** "Backlist carried the P&L again this quarter — frontlist marketing spend is up but frontlist net contribution is flat."
**Common misuse:** Treating backlist as a passive, self-sustaining asset that needs no attention, when repositioning, cover refreshes, or renewed marketing can meaningfully extend a backlist title's sales life.

### Net receipts
The actual revenue a publisher collects per unit sold, after wholesale/retail discounts — distinct from the cover price, which is what the consumer pays.
**In use:** "Net receipts on this format run about 50% of cover price after the standard wholesale discount — model off that, not list price."
**Common misuse:** Building a P&L off cover price instead of net receipts, which overstates the actual revenue the publisher captures per unit by roughly half.

### Remaindering
Selling excess unsold inventory at deep discount (often below cost) to liquidate warehouse space, typically after a title's sales have effectively ended.
**In use:** "We're remaindering the overprinted stock rather than warehousing it another year — it's a sunk cost at this point."
**Common misuse:** Delaying the remaindering decision too long out of reluctance to "give up" on a title, which just adds warehousing cost without changing the outcome.

### Subsidiary rights
Rights to exploit a work in forms or markets beyond the primary print edition — foreign/territory rights, audio, film/TV option, book club, serialization — often sold or licensed separately from the core publishing deal.
**In use:** "Sub-rights income turned this from a marginal acquisition into a solidly profitable one — audio alone covered a third of the advance."
**Common misuse:** Treating sub-rights as a pleasant surprise discovered after the deal closes rather than value that should be estimated and factored into the advance decision itself.

### Comp title
A previously published, comparable book used as the benchmark for projecting a new acquisition's likely sales, chosen for similarity in genre, author platform, and marketing investment.
**In use:** "These three comps match on genre and starred-review profile, but none of them had this author's existing platform — adjust the projection up."
**Common misuse:** Selecting comps that match only superficially (same genre) while ignoring the variables that actually drive sales differences, like platform size or marketing spend level.

### Print run
The quantity of physical copies produced in a single printing, sized against projected demand with awareness of the asymmetric costs of under- versus over-printing.
**In use:** "First printing's set at 11,000 — sell-in plus a buffer, with reprint plates held ready rather than printing to the full pre-order-implied demand."
**Common misuse:** Sizing the print run to the most optimistic demand estimate "just in case," which converts a manageable reprint-timing risk into a larger warehousing and remaindering risk if demand falls short.

### Auction (publishing)
A competitive bidding process among multiple publishers for the rights to a manuscript, typically run by a literary agent, with rounds of bids until a winner is determined.
**In use:** "We're four rounds into the auction and past our ceiling — time to send a final offer and be ready to walk."
**Common misuse:** Treating the highest bid as evidence of the book's true market value rather than recognizing that competitive auctions systematically produce winner's-curse outcomes — the winning bid is often the most optimistic outlier, not the consensus estimate.

### World rights / territory rights
World rights grant the publisher the ability to sell a book in all territories/languages; territory rights are split (e.g., North American rights only), leaving the author's agent free to separately sell UK, foreign-language, or other territory rights.
**In use:** "We're only bidding for North American rights — that lowers our advance number but also caps our sub-rights upside."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a lower advance for territory-limited rights is automatically the better deal without also accounting for the sub-rights income given up by not holding world rights.
