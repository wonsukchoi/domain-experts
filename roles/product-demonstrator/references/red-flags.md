# Red flags

Smell tests a field lead or client should catch before trusting a shift's reported result. Format: what it usually means, the first question to ask, the data to pull.

## 1. Sample-to-sale ratio far outside the category's normal 1:6–15 range with no explanation

**Usually means:** either bad table placement (back aisle, low traffic), a pricing or availability friction at the shelf that the pitch can't overcome, or — if the ratio is unusually *tight* — samples weren't actually being tracked accurately.

**First question:** "Where exactly was the table relative to the product's normal shelf location, and was anything about price or stock different that day?"

**Data to pull:** table location vs. planogram-assigned location, shelf price that day vs. normal price, stock-out timing, hourly sample/sale log rather than the daily total alone.

## 2. Store "nearly sold out" mid-shift

**Usually means:** the store under-ordered against a routine new-item allocation, not that true demand was met and exceeded — the ceiling was never actually tested.

**First question:** "What was the opening stock count, and is that number a typical order or a conservative first-time allocation?"

**Data to pull:** opening stock count, the store's standard reorder point for that SKU, whether a restock was requested and denied mid-shift.

## 3. Recap report is adjectives with no numbers ("huge success," "everyone loved it")

**Usually means:** the rep is protecting the next booking, not reporting the actual result — soft language is what fills the space where a units-vs-baseline number should be.

**First question:** "What were units sold against this store's trailing baseline for that SKU, and what was the sample count?"

**Data to pull:** trailing 4-week baseline velocity for the SKU at that store, the day's register pull, the logged sample count.

## 4. Temperature-log gaps longer than 60 minutes

**Usually means:** compliance corner-cutting under time pressure, not a one-off oversight — the pattern usually shows up across several shifts once checked.

**First question:** "Can I see the temperature log for this shift, with entries at least every 30–60 minutes?"

**Data to pull:** the physical or photographed log, the rep's shift history for the same gap pattern, whether the retailer's own compliance walk flagged anything that week.

## 5. Store manager moved the table location without documentation

**Usually means:** an informal renegotiation on the floor that quietly invalidates whatever the client is trying to measure that day — a slower aisle can cut conversion by multiples on its own.

**First question:** "Is this the location in the signed placement agreement, and if not, who approved the change and when?"

**Data to pull:** the signed placement contract, timestamped setup photo, any communication with the store contact about the move.

## 6. High engagement, flat sales

**Usually means:** samples are being handed out without an actual ask — a friendly table that never delivers the benefit line or the CTA — or there's a price/availability friction at the shelf itself that no pitch fixes.

**First question:** "Walk me through the actual pitch — what gets said between the taste and the shopper walking away?"

**Data to pull:** the pitch script as delivered (shadow a few interactions if possible), shelf price and any recent price change, whether the product is in stock and visible from the table.

## 7. Same store, same demonstrator, booked indefinitely with no lift review

**Usually means:** the booking is running on relationship and habit rather than proven return — nobody has checked in months whether the incremental units still justify the shift cost.

**First question:** "When was the last time we compared this store's post-demo lift against the cost of the shift?"

**Data to pull:** cost per shift at that store over the booking's history, incremental units per shift over the same period, trend direction.

## 8. "Out of stock" flagged at the same store on 3+ demo dates

**Usually means:** the retailer's ordering process, not the demo program, is the actual constraint on growth — no amount of demo scheduling fixes a supply problem.

**First question:** "Has this store's reorder point for the SKU been adjusted since the first stockout was flagged?"

**Data to pull:** stockout dates and times across the booking history, the store's standing order quantity and reorder point, any communication with the buyer about raising it.

## 9. Safety incident not filed same-day (most agency contracts require it within 24 hours)

**Usually means:** the shift ran long, the rep didn't want to flag a problem, or the incident seemed minor at the time — any of which can end the agency's contract at that retailer chain-wide if it surfaces later without a filed report.

**First question:** "Was anything filed with the agency and the store the same day this happened?"

**Data to pull:** the incident log timestamp vs. the event time, the store's own incident report if one exists, the agency's required reporting window for that retailer.

## 10. A coupon or discount is attached to every single sample

**Usually means:** the measured "lift" is largely price-driven and will not persist once the discount stops — the demo is testing price sensitivity, not product appeal.

**First question:** "What would the sold-units number look like without the discount attached?"

**Data to pull:** redemption rate on the coupon, post-demo sales in the weeks after the discount ends, a comparable demo date at the same store without a coupon if one exists.

## 11. "Sold out" reported without a shelf-count check at close

**Usually means:** the number is being estimated from memory rather than counted, which quietly inflates the reported incremental units.

**First question:** "Did anyone physically count remaining stock at close, or is this from the register total alone?"

**Data to pull:** closing shelf photo, register pull, reconciliation between the two.

## 12. Client wants to scale a program off a single pilot day

**Usually means:** a decision-maker is treating one data point — often supply-constrained or weather-affected — as representative, skipping the step of checking it against a category benchmark or a second store.

**First question:** "What would have to be true about this one day for it to represent every store we're about to book?"

**Data to pull:** the pilot's sample-to-sale ratio against the category benchmark, any known anomalies that day (competitor activity, weather, local event), whether a second store or date has been tested.
