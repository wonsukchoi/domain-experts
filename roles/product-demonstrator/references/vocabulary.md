# Vocabulary

Terms generalists conflate that practitioners keep sharply separate. Each: definition, a sentence a practitioner would actually say, and the common misuse.

## Demo vs. activation vs. brand ambassador work

A **demo** (or demonstration) is a short, transactional sampling table tied to one retail location and shift, aimed at same-day trial and a shelf purchase. An **activation** is a larger branded experience — booth infrastructure, staff, often multi-day, at a venue like a festival or trade show — aimed at awareness and lead capture more than an immediate sale. **Brand ambassador** work is ongoing or multi-touch persona-based promotion (social content, event appearances, community presence) without a single transactional moment attached.

*In use:* "This is a demo, not an activation — one table, one shift, we're grading it on units, not on dwell time or photo booth traffic."

*Common misuse:* calling every in-store table a "brand activation" inflates the scope and the expected deliverable; a client budgeting activation-level KPIs (awareness lift, social reach) against a four-hour demo shift is measuring the wrong thing.

## Sample-to-sale ratio

Samples given out divided by units sold above baseline during the same window. The core conversion metric for a sampling event, distinct from raw sample count.

*In use:* "288 samples, 28 incremental units, that's 1:10.3 — normal for a premium beverage, not the standout the recap made it sound like."

*Common misuse:* reporting total samples served as the success metric on its own, with no sales number to divide it against.

## Incremental lift vs. baseline velocity

**Baseline velocity** is what a SKU sells at that location on a normal day with no demo running, usually a trailing multi-week average. **Incremental lift** is the day's actual sales minus that baseline — the only number that isolates what the demo itself caused.

*In use:* "34 sold isn't the number — 34 minus the 6-a-day baseline is the number: 28 incremental units."

*Common misuse:* reporting total units sold that day as if the demo caused all of it, ignoring that some of those units would have sold anyway.

## Sell-through vs. "sold out"

**Sell-through** is the percentage of stocked units actually sold. "**Sold out**" just means the shelf reached zero — it says nothing about whether demand was fully met or the store simply under-ordered.

*In use:* "34 of 40 is 85% sell-through, but the store only ordered 40 — that's a stocking ceiling, not a demand ceiling."

*Common misuse:* treating "sold out" as unambiguous proof of strong demand and using it to justify a big rollout without checking what the opening stock count actually was.

## Certificate of insurance (COI)

A document from an agency's insurer naming the specific retailer as an additional insured, at coverage limits that retailer requires, before any staff can work its floor.

*In use:* "Can't set up until the COI is confirmed on file for this store — a general liability policy that doesn't name this retailer doesn't count."

*Common misuse:* assuming a demonstrator's own general liability coverage, or a COI issued for a different retailer, satisfies the requirement.

## Planogram

The retailer's or client's documented shelf and floor-placement plan — a contractual specification, not a loose suggestion for where signage looks nice.

*In use:* "The planogram has us at the end-cap by the entrance — if the manager moves us to aisle 9, that's outside the contract and needs sign-off, not just showing up there."

*Common misuse:* treating placement as negotiable on the day of the shift because "the manager said it was fine."

## Temperature danger zone

The FDA Food Code range, 41°F–135°F, in which food-safe holding limits apply: no more than roughly 2 hours cumulative in that range before discard, 4 hours as an absolute outer limit.

*In use:* "That batch has been out almost 2 hours — cut it now and start a fresh one rather than pushing toward the 4-hour limit."

*Common misuse:* treating the rule as "it was only out for a little while" instead of tracking cumulative time against the actual threshold.

## End-cap vs. secondary placement

An **end-cap** sits at the end of an aisle facing the main traffic path — the highest-visibility, highest-conversion placement in most stores. **Secondary placement** is any additional display away from the product's normal shelf location, which can still outperform the regular aisle spot but rarely matches an end-cap.

*In use:* "We lost the end-cap to another vendor this week — expect the ratio to come in worse purely from the aisle-9 placement, not the pitch."

*Common misuse:* comparing conversion across two shifts at different placements as if location were held constant.

## Shrinkage vs. waste

**Shrinkage** is product lost to theft or unrecorded loss. **Waste** is product prepared for sampling but not actually served or consumed — over-prepping past demand. The two get reported together but come from different causes and need different fixes.

*In use:* "That's waste, not shrinkage — we cut too many samples for the slow midday hour and had to toss them at the 4-hour mark."

*Common misuse:* lumping all unaccounted product into "shrinkage" on a recap, which hides an over-prepping pattern that a smaller batch size would fix.

## Chain-wide pull

A retailer or agency ending a program at every location of that chain, not just the one where a problem occurred, usually triggered by a compliance or safety violation.

*In use:* "One unreported allergic reaction at store #114 got the whole chain pulled — it wasn't isolated to that store's contract."

*Common misuse:* assuming a bad shift or violation only puts that single location's booking at risk.

## Trial vs. repeat purchase

**Trial** is a first-time purchase prompted by the sample, measurable same day. **Repeat purchase** is whether that customer buys again in the weeks after, usually the number that actually justifies the marketing spend.

*In use:* "Day-of trial at 1:10 is fine, but the rollout decision should wait on the 3- and 6-week repeat-purchase pull, not the pilot day alone."

*Common misuse:* treating a strong trial day as proof the program worked, when trial without repeat purchase means the spend bought a one-time sale and nothing durable.

## Field report / recap

The rep's or field lead's written record of a shift, submitted to the agency and client — the client's only record of what actually happened on the floor.

*In use:* "The recap needs the hourly log and the competitive note, not just the summary line — that's what the client actually reads."

*Common misuse:* filling the recap with subjective color ("great energy, big turnout") instead of the numbers and specific observations that make it useful for a rollout decision.
