# Playbook

Filled operational sequences a demonstrator or field lead actually runs, with structure and example numbers. Starting points to adapt, not scripts to recite.

## 1. Pre-shift setup checklist (with timing)

Arrive 30–45 minutes before the shift starts — a demo that opens late loses the pre-shopping-rush window entirely.

1. **Verify the contract, not the store manager's memory:** confirmed location matches the signed placement (end-cap, aisle number), certificate of insurance on file with that specific retailer, signage present and correct. Mismatch → call the agency coordinator before setup, don't quietly relocate.
2. **Check opening stock on the shelf** and write it down. This is the number that turns "sold out" from a celebration into a diagnosis later.
3. **Set up the food-safety kit:** thermometer calibrated, hand sanitizer visible, gloves on hand, sneeze guard up if serving open food. Start the temperature log with the first reading — cold items must read ≤41°F, hot items ≥135°F at first log and every 30–60 minutes after (FDA Food Code danger-zone rule).
4. **Stage samples for the first hour only.** Over-prepping product that sits past the safe holding window (2 hours in the danger zone, 4 hours cumulative discard limit) is the single most common compliance failure, not contamination from mishandling.
5. **Photograph the table before opening** — the "before" half of the recap's before/after evidence.

## 2. Pitch structure (filled example: cold-brew coffee)

Total pitch, taste to close, should run under 20 seconds for a shopper who stopped but hasn't asked a question.

```
HOOK (0-3 sec, visual/audio, no words needed):
  Pour visible, ice audible, cups laid out in a fan — designed to
  register in peripheral vision at walking pace.

OFFER (as they slow):
  "Cold brew, want to try it?" — question, not a statement, leaves
  an easy no.

BENEFIT LINE (only after they taste, <=8 sec):
  "Cold-steeped 16 hours, half the acidity of regular iced coffee."
  One line. Stop talking.

SILENCE (2-4 sec):
  Let them read the label or say something. Filling this with more
  pitch is the single biggest cause of lost closes after a positive
  taste reaction.

CTA (only if they engage further):
  "It's $4.99, right there on the endcap — today only $1 off with
  the coupon on the table."

OBJECTION HANDLING (only the objection actually raised, not a
pre-loaded list):
  "too expensive"    -> point at the coupon, don't argue the price
  "I don't drink coffee cold" -> "totally fair" + let them go;
                                   don't try to convert a stated no
  "is it strong?"     -> answer literally, then stop
```

## 3. Shift-tracking log (filled example)

Logged at fixed intervals, not reconstructed at the end from memory.

| Time | Samples served (running) | Shelf stock remaining | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| 10:00 (open) | 0 | 40 | Temp log: 38°F |
| 11:00 | 62 | 33 | Morning rush, mostly visual-hook stops |
| 12:00 | 118 | 27 | Temp log: 39°F |
| 13:00 | 161 | 22 | Slower — one competitor sampling table set up two aisles down |
| 14:00 | 203 | 15 | Temp log: 40°F |
| 15:00 | 251 | 8 | Restock requested from backroom — none available |
| 16:00 (close) | 288 | 6 | Final register pull: 34 sold. Temp log: 39°F |

The 13:00 note (competitor activity) and the 15:00 note (no backroom restock) are exactly the two lines a client recap needs and a "huge success" summary tends to drop.

## 4. Escalation protocols

**Out of stock mid-shift:** stop implying availability in the pitch immediately ("while supplies last" only, never "grab one at the register"), log the time stock hit zero and the shelf count at that moment, notify the store contact and the agency coordinator, keep sampling if product remains — sampling with nothing to buy still seeds future trial but must be logged as a demand-ceiling-unknown day, not a sellout success.

**Allergic reaction or safety complaint:** stop service at that table immediately, offer to call store first aid/store manager, do not diagnose or offer remedies, document time/description/what was served in the incident log, notify the agency coordinator same day — this report is what keeps the agency's contract with the retailer, not paperwork for its own sake.

**Hostile or aggressive customer:** disengage, do not argue product claims or matched energy, involve store staff/security if it escalates, log it factually without embellishment in the recap.

**Power/weather failure (outdoor or club-entrance setup):** move to the pre-agreed indoor fallback location if one exists in the contract; if none exists, stop the shift, document the time and reason, and flag it to the coordinator — don't run a compromised setup that could trigger a compliance violation to avoid reporting a lost shift.

## 5. Sample-to-sale benchmark table (stated industry heuristics, not a single study)

| Category | Typical sample-to-sale ratio | Why it differs |
|---|---|---|
| Mainstream packaged food (chips, cookies) | 1:6–8 | Low price, low consideration, impulse-compatible |
| Premium/specialty food & beverage | 1:10–15 | Higher price, needs the taste to overcome price resistance |
| Supplements/nutraceuticals | 1:15–25 | High consideration, often needs a return visit, not a same-day close |
| Small appliances/durable goods demo | 1:20–40+ (demo count, not "samples") | Long consideration cycle; success metric shifts toward leads/coupons captured, not same-day units |

Use this table to judge whether a day's ratio needs an explanation, not as a pass/fail gate — a single day's traffic mix (weekday vs. weekend, store size) moves the ratio on its own.
