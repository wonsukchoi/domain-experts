# Red flags

Signals a senior scaler or a check-scaler catches in the first pass over a ticket or a load.

### Scale ticket doesn't state which log rule was used

- **Usually means:** the scaler defaulted to habit (usually whatever rule the mill's own contracts use) instead of confirming the governing rule for this specific sale — or the ticket was filled out from memory after the fact.
- **First question:** "Which rule does this timber sale contract or purchase agreement specify, and where's that written down?"
- **Data to pull:** the contract's scale-rule clause; the rule stamped on the scale stick used; prior tickets from the same sale for consistency.

### Same load re-scaled under a different rule shows a swing greater than ~20%

- **Usually means:** either the original scale used the wrong rule for this contract, or the log population skews small-diameter, where Doyle vs. Scribner/International divergence is largest.
- **First question:** "What's the diameter distribution on this load — is it small enough that a 20%+ rule-to-rule swing is expected, or is something else off?"
- **Data to pull:** per-log diameter and length from both scale events; the specific rule used each time; the contract clause naming the governing rule.

### Grade assigned looks generous relative to visible defect

- **Usually means:** grading from overall visual impression rather than the clear-face/diameter thresholds in the standard, or pressure (explicit or not) to move a marginal load into a higher-paying grade.
- **First question:** "Walk me through which specific threshold in the grading standard this log meets for the grade assigned."
- **Data to pull:** the applicable grading handbook's threshold table; photos or field notes of the defect in question; the scaler's documented reasoning on the ticket.

### Diameter measured mid-log or at the large end instead of the small end

- **Usually means:** a shortcut on a tapered log, sometimes innocent (crew grabbed the easiest measurement point), sometimes a way to inflate volume on a visibly tapered log.
- **First question:** "Where exactly was this diameter taken — can you point to it on the log?"
- **Data to pull:** re-measurement at the true small end; the taper (diameter difference between ends) to quantify the overstatement if any.

### A conk or other decay indicator is visible but no cull deduction appears on the ticket

- **Usually means:** the decay indicator was missed on the walk-around, or was seen and not translated into a deduction because the scaler wasn't sure how to size it.
- **First question:** "Was the conk (or scar/crack) noted during the walk-around, and if so, why isn't there a Step B deduction on the worksheet?"
- **Data to pull:** field notes from the walk-around; the deduction worksheet; a probe check if the log is still accessible.

### A single flat percentage cull applied across dissimilar defects (e.g., "15% cull" covering both sweep and rot)

- **Usually means:** the scaler collapsed two different deduction methods (diameter-reduction for sweep, percentage-of-section for decay) into one number, which can't be reproduced or audited defect-by-defect.
- **First question:** "Break that 15% into what came from sweep and what came from decay — what's the diameter-reduction calculation for the sweep piece?"
- **Data to pull:** the deduction worksheet with steps separated; re-derive each deduction independently and compare to the bundled figure.

### Truckload scaled as a single aggregate with no per-log breakdown

- **Usually means:** acceptable for some low-value pulpwood contracts, but for sawlog/veneer loads it removes the ability to audit any individual log's grade or deduction, and hides whether one high-value log is being undercounted inside an average.
- **First question:** "Does this contract call for aggregate or per-log scaling, and if per-log, why is this ticket aggregate?"
- **Data to pull:** the contract's scaling-method clause; a physical re-count and per-log re-scale of the load if disputed.

### A scaler's cull rate runs more than roughly 3x the crew or regional average

- **Usually means:** either the scaler is working an unusually defect-heavy tract (check the timber type and history first) or the scaler is biased — high from overcorrection after a prior dispute, or low from pressure to keep volume up for a buyer.
- **First question:** "What's this scaler's cull rate on the last 20 loads compared to the crew average for the same species and tract?"
- **Data to pull:** the scaler's rolling cull-rate history; check-scale results on a sample of the same scaler's recent tickets; tract cruise data predicting expected defect rate.

### Contract is silent on the governing scale rule and a marginal grade or volume dispute is already underway

- **Usually means:** the sale was written without a scale-rule clause (common in informal or verbal-agreement timber sales), and the dispute is really a contract gap, not a scaling error.
- **First question:** "What rule has been used on prior loads from this same sale, and is there an implied course of dealing we can point to?"
- **Data to pull:** all prior tickets from the sale; any correspondence referencing a rule; state default-rule statutes if the sale falls under a jurisdiction with one.

### Peeler-grade call made without measuring the smallest scaling section on the log

- **Usually means:** the diameter used for the grade call was taken at the butt end or an average impression rather than the log's narrowest true cross-section, which is what the buyer is actually contracting for.
- **First question:** "Where's the smallest diameter on this log, and does it still clear the peeler minimum there?"
- **Data to pull:** diameter measurements at both ends and at any visible taper break; the peeler minimum-diameter threshold for the species in question.
