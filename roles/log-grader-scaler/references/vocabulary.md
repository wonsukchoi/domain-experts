# Vocabulary

Terms generalists conflate or use loosely; practitioners keep them sharply separate because a contract or a dispute turns on the distinction.

## Doyle rule vs. Scribner rule vs. International 1/4-inch rule

Three different formulas for estimating board-foot lumber recovery from a round log's diameter and length. **Doyle** is a closed formula, (D−4)²×L÷16, that assumes a fixed 4-inch slab loss regardless of log size. **Scribner** is diagram-derived and tabulated, not a formula, rounding to the nearest 10 board feet in its common "Decimal C" form. **International 1/4-inch** is tabulated per 4-foot section with an explicit 1/4-inch saw-kerf and taper allowance, and tracks actual mill recovery more closely than the other two.

**In use:** "This sale's contract calls for Doyle — don't quote the Scribner number even though it's higher, it's not the payable figure here."

**Common misuse:** treating the three as interchangeable estimates of "the same volume" that should roughly agree. They diverge by 20–50% on small-diameter logs and only converge near 26–30 inches diameter; disagreement between them on a small log is expected, not a sign one measurement is wrong.

## Scaling diameter vs. DBH

**Scaling diameter** is the diameter measured inside bark at the small end of a cut log, the input to every scale-rule formula. **DBH** (diameter at breast height, 4.5 feet above ground) is a standing-tree measurement used for cruising and inventory, taken outside bark. They describe different objects at different life stages of the same tree.

**In use:** "DBH on the cruise sheet said 22 inches, but after bucking, the top log's scaling diameter is only 11 — don't extrapolate log volume from the tree's DBH."

**Common misuse:** using a tree's DBH to estimate the board-foot volume of logs cut from it, skipping the taper and top-diameter losses that scaling diameter is specifically measured to capture.

## Sweep vs. crook

**Sweep** is a gradual curve along a log's length. **Crook** is a sharp bend, usually localized to a shorter section. Both are deducted using the diameter-reduction method, but crook's localized nature often means only a portion of the log's length carries the deduction, while sweep frequently runs the log's full length.

**In use:** "That's crook in the butt 4 feet, not sweep the whole length — deduct diameter only on that section, not the whole log."

**Common misuse:** applying a full-length diameter reduction for a defect that's actually localized crook, over-culling the unaffected portion of the log.

## Cull vs. defect deduction

**Cull** is the portion of a log or load determined to be unmerchantable and excluded from payable volume entirely. **Defect deduction** is a partial reduction applied to an otherwise merchantable log to account for a specific flaw. A heavily culled log can still carry a positive net scale if some merchantable volume remains; "culled" doesn't always mean zero.

**In use:** "It's not fully culled — 30% of the section is culled for rot, the rest scales net after the deduction."

**Common misuse:** using "cull" to mean any deduction at all, which erases the distinction between a log that's rejected outright and one that's merely discounted.

## Grade vs. volume

**Volume** answers how much wood a log contains, in board feet, under a stated rule. **Grade** answers what that wood is worth per unit once processed, based on defect and clear-face criteria independent of volume. A log's volume and grade are measured and reported separately because they answer different economic questions.

**In use:** "It's a big log — 320 board feet — but it's #3 grade on defect, so don't price it like the #1 material at the same MBF count."

**Common misuse:** assuming a larger-diameter or higher-volume log is automatically higher grade; grade tracks defect and clear-face criteria, not size.

## MBF

Thousand board feet — the standard unit for pricing sawlogs and stumpage, distinct from the board foot itself, which is the volume unit. Prices are quoted per MBF ($/MBF), not per board foot, even though the underlying scale is in board feet.

**In use:** "Stumpage on this grade is running $600/MBF this quarter — a 4,200-board-foot load nets $2,520."

**Common misuse:** confusing a $/MBF price with a $/board-foot price by a factor of 1,000 when doing quick mental math on a load value.

## Conk

A visible fungal fruiting body on a log's surface, the external sign of internal decay that has usually been established for years before it fruits. Conks are a lagging indicator, not the full extent of the decay.

**In use:** "There's a conk at the butt — assume decay runs at least 3 feet up from there, don't just cull the exact spot where it's visible."

**Common misuse:** treating a conk as a point defect confined to the exact location observed, understating the decay it indicates.

## Merchantable length vs. total length

**Merchantable length** is the portion of a felled tree or log meeting minimum diameter and defect standards for sale — commonly 8 feet minimum for softwood sawlogs at many mills, with a stated minimum small-end diameter. **Total length** is the full physical length of the piece, including any unmerchantable top or defective sections.

**In use:** "Total stem is 42 feet, but merchantable length only runs to 34 — the top 8 feet doesn't clear minimum diameter."

**Common misuse:** scaling and pricing the full physical length of a stem without checking that the top sections still clear the minimum diameter and defect standard to count as merchantable.

## Stumpage vs. delivered price

**Stumpage** is the price paid to a landowner for standing timber before harvest, logging, and hauling costs. **Delivered price** is what a mill pays for logs arriving at its yard, which already has logging and transport cost built in. The gap between the two is the logger's margin, not a scaling artifact.

**In use:** "Delivered price is $850/MBF, but after logging and haul cost, stumpage on this tract only pencils out to $480/MBF."

**Common misuse:** comparing a stumpage quote directly to a delivered-price quote as if they represent the same point in the supply chain.

## Check scale

An independent re-scale of a sample of logs or loads, performed by a different scaler or agency, used to measure a scaler's or crew's systematic bias against a reference standard — not a re-scale triggered only by a specific dispute.

**In use:** "Check scale on the last 20 loads came back 4% low relative to the reference crew — that's a training issue, not one bad ticket."

**Common misuse:** treating check-scaling as a dispute-resolution tool only, rather than an ongoing calibration process that catches systematic bias before any single load is contested.
