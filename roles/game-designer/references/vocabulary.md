# Game design working vocabulary

Terms a live-ops puzzle-game designer uses precisely and generalists conflate. Format: definition → how a practitioner says it → common misuse.

**Pass rate vs. attempts-to-clear** — Pass rate is the percentage of players who clear a level, usually on the first attempt; attempts-to-clear (mean or median tries per player who eventually clears) measures the same level from an effort angle pass rate can't see.
In use: "Pass rate's fine at 81%, but pull the attempts-to-clear median before we sign off — that's where the frustration would show up."
Misuse: Reporting pass rate as a standalone health metric in a live-ops-lead sign-off review; a level can post an acceptable pass rate while its failing minority carries an attempts-to-clear or abandonment problem the aggregate number can't surface.

**Puzzliness** — A King-coined qualitative rating of how much a level's outcome depends on player skill and decision-making versus luck in the board deal — not a measure of difficulty or fun on its own.
In use: "This level's puzzliness score is low — it's basically a slot pull with a match-3 skin, that's the churn driver, not the win threshold."
Misuse: Using "puzzliness" as a synonym for "difficulty" or "quality" — a level can be highly puzzly and still poorly tuned, or low-puzzliness and still convert well if that's the intended design.

**D1/D7/D30 retention** — The percentage of players who return exactly 1, 7, or 30 days after install (or after a specific level/cohort entry point) — each measures a different failure mode, not points on one continuous decay curve.
In use: "D1's fine at 38%, but D7 dropped to 15% against a 21% baseline — that's a mid-game content problem, not an onboarding one."
Misuse: Treating a D1 dip and a D7 dip as the same diagnosis ("retention is down") — D1 problems live in the first-session hook, D7 problems live in content pacing and fatigue several sessions in, and they need different fixes.

**ARPDAU vs. ARPPU** — ARPDAU (average revenue per daily active user) divides revenue across the whole active player base including non-payers; ARPPU (average revenue per paying user) divides it only across players who paid.
In use: "ARPDAU held flat, but ARPPU jumped 12% — we're not converting more players, we're extracting more from the same payers."
Misuse: Quoting ARPDAU alone as "monetization is up" when payer count actually fell and ARPPU rose — the same ARPDAU can hide a healthier or a more whale-dependent economy depending on which is moving underneath it.

**Juice (game feel)** — The measurable input-to-feedback responsiveness of a control or mechanic, tied to a specific latency window — not a subjective aesthetic label for "polish."
In use: "The cascade doesn't need more juice, it needs the feedback latency under 50ms — right now it's landing at 140ms and reading as unresponsive."
Misuse: Treating "add juice" as a late-cycle art/audio polish task; juice is a latency budget decided at mechanic-design time, and a "make it feel better" note with no latency number attached is unactionable.

**Near-fail** — A specific board-completion state — ending within a defined band of the win threshold (e.g., ≥88% and <100% of target score, or ≤2 moves left) — not any loss that merely "felt close" to the player.
In use: "Check the near-fail band before we touch the offer — if failers are landing at 60% of threshold, this isn't a near-fail problem at all."
Misuse: Calling any narrow loss a "near-fail" without checking the board-completion logs against the defined band — a mis-set trigger band can flag genuinely unclearable attempts as near-fail, which tanks offer conversion and gets misread as a pricing problem.

**Sink vs. faucet** — In a soft-currency economy, a faucet grants currency to players (daily login, level completion, event reward); a sink removes it from circulation (purchases, upgrades, entry fees). Economy health is read from the balance between the two, not from either alone.
In use: "Balance-per-user is up 14% week over week — pull the faucet/sink breakdown, not just the net number, before we touch pricing."
Misuse: Diagnosing an economy problem from the net currency balance trend alone; the same drift can come from a new faucet shipping without a matching sink, or an existing sink getting harder to trigger, and the fix differs in each case.

**Payer concentration ("whale" dependency)** — The share of total IAP revenue generated by the top percentile of paying users (commonly reported at the top 1% or top 5%) — a structural description of the revenue base, not automatically a defect.
In use: "Top 1% is carrying 54% of IAP revenue — that's not necessarily broken, but check if it's trending up or down before deciding anything."
Misuse: Treating any high concentration number as inherently unhealthy without checking the trend — a stable high concentration is a known-risk design choice, a rising one is calcification worth flagging, and a falling one may mean broadening reach or diluting ARPPU.

**Abandonment vs. churn** — Abandonment (voluntary exit) is a player quitting mid-attempt without failing — a comprehension or boredom signal; churn is a player who fails or completes and then doesn't return — a downstream retention signal. They come from different events and diagnose different problems.
In use: "Voluntary mid-level exit is 18% on this one — that's an abandonment problem, not a difficulty problem, even though the pass rate looks fine."
Misuse: Lumping quits and post-fail non-returns into one "players are leaving" bucket — a fail is data about the mechanic, a quit-without-failing is data about whether the player wanted to keep playing at all, and conflating them sends the fix to the wrong system.

**Core loop vs. meta loop** — The core loop is the moment-to-moment mechanic repeated within a level or session (match, swap, clear); the meta loop is the progression layer spanning sessions (map progression, collection, seasonal events) that gives repeating the core loop a reason to continue.
In use: "The core loop's fine — attempts-to-clear tracks with target. The drop-off is in the meta loop, players aren't coming back for the map progression."
Misuse: Retuning core-loop difficulty to fix a retention problem that's actually meta-loop pacing (nothing new to progress toward), or vice versa — a fix aimed at the wrong loop won't move the metric it was meant to.

**Difficulty spike vs. difficulty curve** — A difficulty curve is the intended, gradual escalation of challenge across a level sequence; a difficulty spike is a single level or section that jumps well outside that curve, whether by design (a deliberate "paywall level") or by accident.
In use: "Confirm whether level 120 is an intentional paywall spike before we file it as a broken curve."
Misuse: Treating every high-difficulty outlier as an accidental curve problem to smooth out — some spikes are deliberate monetization design, and smoothing one without checking intent removes a level that was working as designed, while a genuinely accidental spike left in place still reads as broken to players.

**Soft launch** — A limited-market release run before global launch to gather live cohort data (retention, monetization, difficulty curve) under production conditions at controlled scale — not a bug-hunting beta.
In use: "Soft-launch D7 in the Philippines cohort was 24% — before we greenlight global, check whether that population's ARPU profile even transfers."
Misuse: Extrapolating soft-launch metrics directly to global performance without adjusting for the test market's population differences (device mix, ARPU baseline, competitive landscape) — a market is chosen for volume and iteration speed, not demographic representativeness.

**Cohort (cohort-segmented data)** — A group of players defined by a shared starting event (install date, level-reach date, batch version) tracked forward together, so downstream metrics are measured from the same reference point rather than blended across players at different lifecycle stages.
In use: "Pull the 14-day cohort for level 162, not the trailing-30-days blended number — we need everyone measured from the same reference point."
Misuse: Reporting a metric as a flat "current" number without naming the cohort's entry date and window — a blended, non-cohorted number mixes players at different lifecycle stages and can move for reasons unrelated to the thing actually being evaluated.

**IAP vs. IAA cannibalization** — IAP (in-app purchase) and IAA (in-app advertising, e.g., rewarded/interstitial) draw on the same player attention and goodwill — increasing one's frequency can suppress the other, so they need tuning as a joint system, not optimizing independently.
In use: "Raising interstitial frequency bumped IAA revenue 8%, but the near-fail offer's conversion dropped 0.4 points the same week — that's cannibalization, not two unrelated wins."
Misuse: Running IAP and IAA optimization as separate workstreams reporting separate wins — an ad-frequency increase that fatigues players right before the near-fail moment can quietly erode the offer conversion the IAP team is trying to improve, and neither team's dashboard alone shows the interaction.
