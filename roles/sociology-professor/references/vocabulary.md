# Vocabulary

Terms of art in academic sociology that generalists misuse. Format per term: definition, how a practitioner actually uses it, the common misuse.

### Sociological imagination

- **Definition:** C. Wright Mills' (1959) term for the capacity to connect a private trouble to a public issue and the historical structure producing it.
- **In use:** "The final paper prompt requires students to trace their own housing situation back to a structural policy — that's the sociological imagination assignment, not the lecture on it."
- **Common misuse:** treating it as a synonym for "critical thinking" in general, or as a unit to cover rather than a specific analytical move (private trouble → public issue → structure) an assessment has to elicit.

### Public sociology

- **Definition:** Michael Burawoy's (2004 ASA address, published *ASR* 2005) four-part typology of sociological labor — professional (peer-reviewed), critical (reflexive/normative), policy (client-driven), and public (broad-audience) — used to classify where a given piece of work counts.
- **In use:** "The op-ed is public sociology — real, but it's currency three of four for tenure, not a stand-in for the professional record."
- **Common misuse:** using "public sociology" as a blanket term for any non-academic writing, losing the distinction between policy work (has a client) and public work (has a general audience), which matters for how each gets weighted in review.

### Book culture / monograph track

- **Definition:** the norm, strongest in ethnography, historical-comparative, and cultural sociology, that a single-authored book with a disciplinary-series press — not an article count — is the primary tenure unit.
- **In use:** "This is a book-culture subfield — two articles by tenure is normal here if the monograph is under contract with a real series."
- **Common misuse:** assuming article-count benchmarks calibrated to quantitative subfields apply uniformly across the department.

### Salami-slicing

- **Definition:** publishing multiple articles drawn from a single dataset (often one ethnographic or interview study) without disclosing the overlap, inflating apparent output.
- **In use:** "Four papers off one 18-month fieldwork project without a shared-data note — a tenure letter will flag that, not credit it."
- **Common misuse:** treating high article count from a single fieldwork site as straightforwardly good productivity rather than checking for undisclosed overlap first.

### Saturation (qualitative research)

- **Definition:** the point in data collection where additional interviews or field observations stop producing new codes or themes; Guest, Bunce & Johnson (2006) found this often by interview 12 in relatively homogeneous samples.
- **In use:** "We hit saturation around interview 11 — two more added nothing new, so the sample size is defensible at 13."
- **Common misuse:** citing "saturation" as a post-hoc justification for stopping early without a documented check (a coding memo showing when new codes stopped appearing), or applying the ~12 figure to a heterogeneous population where it doesn't hold.

### Thick description

- **Definition:** field-note or ethnographic writing detailed enough to convey the meaning participants themselves attach to an action, not just the observable behavior.
- **In use:** "The field notes need the negotiation over the rent, not just 'tenant and landlord disagreed' — that's the thick description the analysis depends on."
- **Common misuse:** equating "thick description" with simply "long" or "detailed" notes, rather than notes that capture participants' own interpretive frame.

### Desk reject vs. R&R

- **Definition:** a desk reject is an editor's rejection issued before peer review, usually on fit or framing grounds; an R&R (revise and resubmit) is an invitation for a further review round — a real, resource-worthy step, not an acceptance.
- **In use:** "Desk-rejected at AJS in nine days with a scope note — retarget, don't rewrite. The R&R at Social Forces is the real submission — every reviewer point gets a response."
- **Common misuse:** reading a desk reject as a verdict on quality when overall rejection rates at ASR/AJS run near 90%, or treating an R&R as equivalent to acceptance.

### IRB exempt vs. expedited vs. full board

- **Definition:** three tiers of institutional review board oversight keyed to risk and identifiability — exempt (minimal-risk, typically anonymous survey research), expedited (minimal risk but identifiable, or specific categories including many fieldwork designs), full board (greater-than-minimal risk or a vulnerable population).
- **In use:** "This is sustained fieldwork in a named housing development — that's expedited at minimum, not exempt, regardless of pseudonyms in the writeup."
- **Common misuse:** defaulting fieldwork to exempt because "it's not a survey," missing that sustained access and rich contextual detail raise identifiability risk that exempt review wasn't built to cover.

### External letter / outside letter

- **Definition:** a solicited written evaluation from a scholar outside the candidate's institution, used in tenure and promotion review to assess the work's standing in the field.
- **In use:** "We need at least one external letter from an ethnographer, not just quantitative demographers, or the committee has no one qualified to assess the manuscript's method."
- **Common misuse:** treating external letters as interchangeable regardless of the letter-writer's subfield or methodological training relative to the candidate's work.

### Structural vs. individual explanation

- **Definition:** the disciplinary default of locating the cause of a social outcome in institutional arrangements, policy, or historical structure, rather than solely in individual choices or traits.
- **In use:** "The unit teaches the documented disparity as structural — a function of the redlining-era map, not individual preference — that part isn't up for debate; the policy remedy is."
- **Common misuse:** presenting the structural explanation itself as one side of an open debate, when in most stratification topics the structural mechanism is the settled empirical claim, not the contested part.

### Gateway course

- **Definition:** a large, often 100+ seat introductory course (typically Introduction to Sociology) that serves as many students' only exposure to the discipline and commonly the department's primary enrollment driver.
- **In use:** "The 210-seat Intro section is the gateway course — the sociological-imagination assessment there is the one artifact most students in the department will ever produce."
- **Common misuse:** treating gateway-course evaluation swings the same as a small upper-division seminar's, without accounting for the scale, TA-grading structure, and self-selection effects at that enrollment size.
