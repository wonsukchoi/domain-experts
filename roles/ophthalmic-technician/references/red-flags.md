# Red Flags

### IOP >30 mmHg in either eye, on Goldmann or NCT, at any visit
- **Usually means:** acute pressure spike (angle-closure, uveitic glaucoma flare, steroid response) — second most likely is a measurement artifact from poor patient fixation or excessive lid squeezing during applanation.
- **First question:** "Is there pain, redness, or a fixed mid-dilated pupil right now?" — separates a true emergency from a technique artifact.
- **Data to pull:** repeat measurement with a fresh mire alignment, pachymetry if not on file, and a quick slit-lamp check for corneal edema or a shallow chamber before the doctor is called in.

### Visual field fixation losses ≥20%, or FP/FN ≥33%, reported as if it were a valid baseline
- **Usually means:** poor patient instruction on the blind-spot monitor, or genuine unstable fixation from macular disease — second most likely is technician time pressure skipping the instruction step.
- **First question:** "Did the patient understand the blind-spot check before the test started?"
- **Data to pull:** the reliability index printout itself (not just the grayscale), and whether a prior field on this patient had the same failure pattern.

### 2+ mmHg IOP increase visit-over-visit with no pachymetry ever on file
- **Usually means:** the "increase" may be entirely an artifact of never having corrected for corneal thickness — second most likely is a real early pressure trend.
- **First question:** "Has this patient ever had pachymetry?"
- **Data to pull:** pachymetry today, and the full IOP history to see if the trend holds after correction.

### Van Herick angle grade 2 or below with a dilation order already written
- **Usually means:** the dilation order was written before this technician's angle check, or angle status was never assessed — second most likely is a true narrow angle needing gonioscopy before any mydriatic.
- **First question:** "Has this patient had gonioscopy, and does the chart already document angle status?"
- **Data to pull:** prior gonioscopy note if it exists; if not, hold dilation and get the physician's read on the angle first.

### Fellow-eye axial length difference >0.3 mm with no surgical or pathologic history
- **Usually means:** measurement artifact — probe misalignment, corneal touch/compression on ultrasound A-scan, or poor fixation on optical biometry — second most likely is true anisometropia or a staphyloma.
- **First question:** "Was this measured by ultrasound contact or optical (non-contact) biometry?"
- **Data to pull:** re-measurement of both eyes, ideally by optical biometry if the first pass was ultrasound.

### Patient's chief complaint (in their own words) doesn't match the referral or scheduling reason
- **Usually means:** the referral was written from an old note or a different visit's concern — second most likely is a new problem that's developed since the referral was generated.
- **First question:** "What's bothering you today, specifically — when did it start?"
- **Data to pull:** the referral letter or scheduling note side-by-side with today's verbatim complaint; chart both explicitly rather than reconciling them silently.

### New afferent pupillary defect on a patient with no known optic nerve or retinal disease
- **Usually means:** an evolving optic neuropathy or significant asymmetric retinal disease — this is not a routine pretest finding at any acuity level.
- **First question:** "Is there any vision change, eye pain especially with movement, or history of trauma?"
- **Data to pull:** confrontation fields, color vision if available, and flag for immediate physician evaluation rather than finishing the routine sequence first.

### Visual acuity reduced from last visit and pinhole does not improve it
- **Usually means:** a media or retinal/neurologic cause rather than a refractive one — second most likely is an uncorrected recording error (wrong glasses tested, wrong eye).
- **First question:** "Are you wearing the glasses you use for distance right now?"
- **Data to pull:** lensometry of the glasses actually worn, and confirm testing distance/chart calibration before assuming pathology.

### OCT or fundus photo signal/quality score below the device's stated reliability floor, filed as a normal baseline
- **Usually means:** media opacity (cataract, dry eye, poor tear film) or patient fixation/cooperation issues — second most likely is technician acceptance of a fast, low-quality scan under time pressure.
- **First question:** "What was the signal strength, and did the patient blink or move during acquisition?"
- **Data to pull:** the scan's quality metric (not just the printed report), and consider a repeat with artificial tears if dry eye is suspected.

### Patient reports flashes, new floaters, or a curtain/shade over part of vision
- **Usually means:** posterior vitreous detachment with possible retinal tear, or retinal detachment — this is a same-day physician evaluation regardless of how the rest of the pretest looks.
- **First question:** "When did it start, and is it getting worse or spreading?"
- **Data to pull:** dilated fundus exam priority flag to the front of the chart; do not let this wait behind routine testing.

### Technician flags every borderline reading regardless of context
- **Usually means:** overcorrection after a prior missed finding, or unclear escalation criteria — second most likely is genuine clustering of borderline results in one clinic session (less common).
- **First question:** "What specific threshold made this one worth flagging?"
- **Data to pull:** the technician's flag log over the past week/month — if the flag rate is far above the rate of anything actually escalated by the physician, the criteria need recalibration, not more flags.
