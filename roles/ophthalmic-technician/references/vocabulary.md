# Vocabulary

### Tonometry
Measurement of intraocular pressure, most commonly by Goldmann applanation at the slit lamp or by non-contact (air-puff) tonometer for screening.
**In use:** "Goldmann's the reference standard — if the NCT reading looks off, confirm with applanation before charting it."
**Common misuse:** Treating an NCT reading as interchangeable with Goldmann; NCT reads systematically differently at pressure extremes and shouldn't substitute for Goldmann when a management decision hinges on the number.

### Pachymetry
Measurement of central corneal thickness (CCT), used to correct or contextualize an applanation IOP reading.
**In use:** "No pachymetry on file for a glaucoma suspect means the IOP history isn't really an IOP history yet."
**Common misuse:** Treating pachymetry as a one-time formality done at diagnosis rather than a value that changes the interpretation of every IOP reading going forward, especially in patients near a management threshold.

### Applanation
The tonometry method (Goldmann) that measures the force needed to flatten a fixed area of cornea; assumes a standard corneal thickness and rigidity.
**In use:** "Applanation reads high on a thick cornea and low on a thin one — that's exactly why pachymetry pairs with it."
**Common misuse:** Assuming applanation gives "the" pressure rather than a pressure estimate whose accuracy depends on corneal properties it doesn't directly measure.

### Reliability indices
The fixation-loss, false-positive, and false-negative rates reported alongside an automated visual field, indicating whether the test result reflects the patient's true visual field.
**In use:** "Fixation losses were 25% — that field's flagged unreliable, not just 'a little rough.'"
**Common misuse:** Reading only the grayscale defect pattern and skipping the reliability indices, which can make a technically invalid test look like a clinical finding.

### Van Herick technique
A slit-lamp estimate of anterior chamber angle depth, comparing the peripheral corneal thickness to the adjacent anterior chamber depth, graded 1–4.
**In use:** "Van Herick's a grade 2 OD — hold the dilation and get gonioscopy before we proceed."
**Common misuse:** Treating van Herick as equivalent to gonioscopy; it's a fast screening estimate, not a definitive angle assessment, and a borderline van Herick still needs gonioscopy to confirm.

### Gonioscopy
Direct visualization of the anterior chamber angle using a specialized contact lens, the definitive method for angle assessment.
**In use:** "The doctor did gonioscopy last visit and documented an open angle — we don't need to hold dilation on van Herick grade alone today."
**Common misuse:** Assuming gonioscopy is a technician-scope test; it's typically performed or directly supervised by the ophthalmologist, not delegated as a routine pretest step.

### Biometry (axial length)
Measurement of eye length (cornea to fovea) via ultrasound (A-scan) or optical (partial coherence interferometry) methods, the dominant input to IOL power calculation.
**In use:** "The two eyes are 1.7 mm apart on axial length with no history explaining it — re-measure both before this goes into an IOL calc."
**Common misuse:** Accepting a single biometry pass without checking fellow-eye symmetry or keratometry agreement, letting a probe-alignment artifact drive a postoperative refractive surprise.

### Cycloplegia vs. mydriasis
Cycloplegia is pharmacologic paralysis of accommodation (used for refraction in some patients); mydriasis is pupil dilation. Some agents (e.g., tropicamide) do both; others (phenylephrine) are primarily mydriatic without meaningful cycloplegic effect.
**In use:** "We need cycloplegic refraction on this pediatric patient, not just dilation for the fundus exam — different drop, different purpose."
**Common misuse:** Using the terms interchangeably, which matters clinically — a dilated pupil doesn't guarantee accommodation is paralyzed, and vice versa depending on the agent.

### SITA (Swedish Interactive Thresholding Algorithm)
A statistical algorithm used by Humphrey perimeters to estimate visual field sensitivity faster than full threshold testing, available in Standard, Fast, and Faster variants.
**In use:** "Switch her to SITA Faster on the retest — she couldn't sustain attention through a full SITA Standard, and Faster still gives a usable reliability profile."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a faster algorithm is simply "less accurate"; SITA Faster trades some precision for testing time and is often the better choice for a patient whose reliability degrades with fatigue.

### Amsler grid
A grid of lines used for rapid bedside detection of central or paracentral scotomas and metamorphopsia (distortion), most relevant to macular disease.
**In use:** "Patient reports the lines look wavy on the Amsler grid near the center — that's going straight to the doctor before we finish the rest of the workup."
**Common misuse:** Treating a normal Amsler grid as ruling out macular pathology; it's a rough screening tool, not a substitute for OCT or fundus exam.

### Keratometry
Measurement of corneal curvature, used for contact lens fitting, astigmatism assessment, and as an input to IOL power calculation alongside axial length.
**In use:** "Keratometry readings don't agree between the auto-K and the manual K by more than half a diopter — re-check before it goes into the IOL formula."
**Common misuse:** Treating keratometry as a fixed, one-time value; corneal curvature can change with dry eye, contact lens wear, or prior surgery, and a stale keratometry reading silently degrades an IOL calculation.

### Confrontation visual field
A rapid bedside visual field screening test using the examiner's hand or fingers in the periphery, compared against the examiner's own (presumed normal) field.
**In use:** "Confrontation fields are grossly full OU — that doesn't replace a formal Humphrey field if the chief complaint is a specific scotoma."
**Common misuse:** Treating a normal confrontation field as sufficient to rule out a field defect; it only detects gross, dense defects and misses subtle or early ones that automated perimetry catches.

### Exophthalmometry
Measurement of the anterior projection of the globe relative to the lateral orbital rim (e.g., with a Hertel exophthalmometer), used to assess proptosis.
**In use:** "Hertel measurements are 24 mm OD, 19 mm OS with the same base setting — that asymmetry needs to go to the doctor today, not at the next scheduled visit."
**Common misuse:** Comparing measurements taken with different base settings between visits without noting the base value; the reading is only comparable across visits at the same base setting.
