# Vocabulary

### AFIS / IAFIS / NGI
Automated Fingerprint Identification System (state-level), the FBI's legacy Integrated AFIS, and its successor Next Generation Identification — biometric search systems that return a similarity-ranked candidate list for a submitted print.
**In use:** "NGI came back with a 20-candidate list; we haven't disposed any of them yet."
**Common misuse:** treating "AFIS/NGI hit" and "identification" as synonyms — the system returns leads, not findings.

### Ten-print card
A full biometric capture of all ten fingers (rolled and flat), taken at booking or licensing, used for classification, database enrollment, and identity verification — distinct from a latent print, which is a partial, unplanned print recovered from a surface.
**In use:** "The ten-print card came back rejected for quality — rebook the subject before we submit again."
**Common misuse:** using "print" generically for both ten-prints and latents, which obscures that they have different quality standards and different evidentiary weight.

### Latent print
A print recovered from a scene or item, typically partial, often degraded, developed and lifted rather than deliberately captured.
**In use:** "The latent only gave us six usable minutiae — that's inconclusive, not an exclusion."
**Common misuse:** assuming any recovered print is automatically comparison-quality; usable area and clarity vary enormously and drive the disposition.

### ACE-V
Analysis, Comparison, Evaluation, Verification — the standard documented methodology for latent print examination, ending in an independent verification step.
**In use:** "Log the ACE-V steps separately — analysis and comparison notes before you write the evaluation conclusion."
**Common misuse:** treating "verification" as a courtesy sign-off rather than an independent, ideally blind, re-examination.

### Minutiae
The specific ridge-path features (ridge endings, bifurcations, and their relative position) compared between a latent and a known print.
**In use:** "Thirteen corresponding minutiae with consistent ridge counts — that supports identification."
**Common misuse:** citing a minutiae count alone as if a fixed national number legally mandates identification or exclusion — no such fixed standard exists in the U.S. since IAI dropped it in 1973.

### RAP sheet
Colloquial term for a Record of Arrest and Prosecution — an individual's compiled criminal history record drawn from CHRI systems.
**In use:** "Pull the RAP sheet before we respond to the licensing board's request, and check for anything sealed."
**Common misuse:** treating the RAP sheet as a single authoritative document rather than an aggregation that can carry unresolved data-entry errors from multiple contributing agencies.

### CHRI (Criminal History Record Information)
The formal regulatory term (28 CFR Part 20) for the arrest, disposition, and identification data a state repository maintains and discloses under specific statutory rules.
**In use:** "That's CHRI — it goes out under 20.33's licensing exception, not as a general courtesy."
**Common misuse:** using "public record" and "CHRI" interchangeably; CHRI disclosure is purpose-restricted even when parts of it originated from public court proceedings.

### NCIC / III
The National Crime Information Center (want/warrant, missing person, stolen property, and similar files) and the Interstate Identification Index (the pointer system for criminal history across states).
**In use:** "Run it through NCIC first for an active want, then III if we need out-of-state history."
**Common misuse:** conflating the two — NCIC returns active operational flags, III points to where full criminal history records live.

### Validation (records)
The periodic requirement that the agency that entered an NCIC want/warrant/person record confirm it's still accurate and active, on a set schedule (commonly 60–90 days initially, annually thereafter).
**In use:** "This warrant's validation is due Friday — get the detective bureau to confirm before it purges."
**Common misuse:** treating validation as a records-clerk formality rather than an operational deadline that determines whether the entry is even visible to other agencies' queries.

### Expungement vs. sealing
Expungement generally destroys or is treated as destroying a record for most purposes; sealing restricts disclosure while the record continues to exist in the repository. The specific effect is state-statute-dependent.
**In use:** "This one's sealed, not expunged — it still exists, we just can't release it outside the statutory exceptions."
**Common misuse:** using the two terms interchangeably, which leads to either wrongly disclosing a sealed record or wrongly destroying a record that should have been retained.

### CJIS Security Policy
The FBI's Criminal Justice Information Services Security Policy — the access-control, audit-logging, and encryption requirements that govern any system touching CHRI or NCIC data.
**In use:** "Shared logins on the CHRI terminal are a CJIS Security Policy finding waiting to happen — every query needs an individually attributable user."
**Common misuse:** treating it as an IT department's problem rather than a records-bureau operational requirement that shows up in every audit.

### Point standard
The historical practice (largely abandoned in the U.S. since the IAI's 1973 resolution) of requiring a fixed number of corresponding minutiae before calling an identification.
**In use:** "We don't run a legal point standard anymore — we run our own documented sufficiency policy and defend it as a methodology, not a magic number."
**Common misuse:** citing "12 points" or similar as if it were a current legal requirement rather than a historical, agency-specific, or informally referenced practice.

### Candidate list / disposition
The ranked set of possible matches an AFIS/NGI search returns (the candidate list), and the examiner's documented conclusion for each one reviewed (exclude, inconclusive, identification) — the disposition, not the list, is the evidentiary output.
**In use:** "We disposed four of the twenty candidates before we hit a verified identification; the other sixteen were never examined."
**Common misuse:** reporting "the candidate list" as if its existence were itself the finding, rather than the disposition reached after comparison.

### Purge
The automatic removal of an NCIC/III entry when a required validation isn't confirmed by its due date.
**In use:** "The entry purged Tuesday — nobody confirmed it, so it's not visible on a routine query anymore."
**Common misuse:** assuming a purge means the underlying case closed; it usually just means the validation deadline was missed.
