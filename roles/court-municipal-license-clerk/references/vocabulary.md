# Court, Municipal, and License Clerk — Vocabulary

### Docket
The court's official chronological log of filings and actions in a case.
**In use:** "The motion wasn't docketed until the 14th, so the response clock runs from that date, not the filing date."
**Common misuse:** treated as a synonym for "case file" — the docket is the log of events, not the collection of documents itself.

### Pro se
A party representing themselves without an attorney.
**In use:** "This is a pro se filer, so I can explain what the form requires but not what to argue."
**Common misuse:** assumed to mean the filer has no rights to procedural help — pro se filers are still entitled to have the clerk explain form requirements, just not legal strategy.

### Fee waiver
A court-approved exemption from paying filing fees based on financial hardship.
**In use:** "She qualifies for a fee waiver under the categorical path — she's enrolled in Medicaid."
**Common misuse:** treated as automatic for anyone who says they can't afford it — it requires meeting a documented threshold or categorical qualification, not a verbal claim.

### Sealed record
A case record restricted from public access by statute or court order.
**In use:** "That case type is sometimes sealed — check the specific flag before releasing anything."
**Common misuse:** assumed to apply uniformly to an entire case type rather than checked per individual case.

### Deficiency notice
Formal written notice that a filing is missing a required element and cannot be accepted as-is.
**In use:** "I sent one deficiency notice listing all three missing items instead of three separate notices."
**Common misuse:** issued piecemeal, one problem at a time, instead of listing every deficiency found in a single completeness review.

### Indigency threshold
The income or categorical-eligibility line below which a filer qualifies for a fee waiver.
**In use:** "Her income is below the 125%-of-poverty-guideline threshold, so the waiver is granted."
**Common misuse:** applied as a subjective hardship judgment rather than the jurisdiction's specific published threshold.

### Grace period
The window after a credential's expiration during which renewal (not new application) rules still apply.
**In use:** "He's two weeks past the 30-day grace period, so this has to be processed as a new application."
**Common misuse:** assumed to extend indefinitely for good-faith delays — it's a fixed window set by rule.

### Public-access presumption
The default legal principle that court records are open to the public unless specifically exempted.
**In use:** "The public-access presumption applies unless there's a specific seal on this case."
**Common misuse:** inverted to assume records are private by default and must be proven public, rather than the reverse.

### Unauthorized practice of law (UPL)
Giving legal advice or opinion without a law license, which court clerks are prohibited from doing regardless of how confident they are in the answer.
**In use:** "Telling her which form to file for her specific situation would cross into UPL — I can only describe what each form is for."
**Common misuse:** thought to only cover formal legal opinions — even casual "I think you should..." advice from a clerk can constitute UPL.

### Categorical eligibility
Automatic qualification for a benefit (like a fee waiver) based on enrollment in another verified program, bypassing an income calculation.
**In use:** "She qualifies categorically through her SNAP enrollment, so we don't need to run the income calculation."
**Common misuse:** assumed to require the income test to also be run and compared — categorical eligibility is an independent, sufficient path on its own.

### Local rule
A procedural rule specific to a particular court or jurisdiction, supplementing statewide statutes or rules of procedure.
**In use:** "The 5-business-day cure period for fee deficiencies comes from Local Rule 4.2, not the state code."
**Common misuse:** conflated with statewide statute — local rules vary by county/court and must be checked at that level, not assumed uniform.

### Case caption
The heading portion of a filing identifying the court, case number, and parties.
**In use:** "The caption lists the wrong case number — that's a deficiency on its own even if everything else is correct."
**Common misuse:** treated as a formality that doesn't need verification — a caption mismatch can misfile a document into the wrong case entirely.
