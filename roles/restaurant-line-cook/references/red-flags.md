# Red flags

Signals a veteran cook or sous chef catches by walking the line for two minutes, with the first question and the data to pull before deciding anything.

### Entrée tickets running >18 min fire-to-window with no "behind" call logged

**Usually means:** the station is absorbing a slowdown silently instead of flagging it — either out of pride or because the cook hasn't checked print time against plate time in twenty minutes. Second most likely: the pass is firing out of ticket-arrival order instead of by cook time, stacking long items behind short ones.

**First question:** "When did this ticket print, and when did you last check the rail?"

**Data to pull:** KDS/ticket timestamps for the last hour by station; count of "behind" calls logged that shift vs. tickets that ran over target; whether fire order matches protein cook-time sequencing or raw arrival order.

### Station's mise en place prepped at 150%+ of the par sheet

**Usually means:** the cook is prepping to feel ahead rather than to what's actually moving — often after getting caught short once and overcorrecting. Second: the par sheet itself is stale against a menu change or a shifted day-of-week pattern.

**First question:** "Walk me through why this item is prepped this high — what sold through last week?"

**Data to pull:** par sheet vs. actual prep quantities for the last two weeks; sell-through rate per item; waste log for items discarded past FIFO age limits.

### "Behind" only gets called after a guest or manager complaint

**Usually means:** the cook is treating a status call as an admission of failure instead of a heads-up, which is the single most expensive habit on a line — a two-minute gap hidden becomes a table of four all landing late. Second: no clear channel or expectation was ever set for when to call it.

**First question:** "At what point in that ticket did you know you were behind, and why didn't the pass hear about it then?"

**Data to pull:** time between first sign of delay (per KDS) and the first verbal/logged "behind" call; frequency of guest-initiated vs. cook-initiated delay flags over the last month.

### Reach-in or walk-in temp log shows identical readings across multiple checks

**Usually means:** the log is being filled in from memory or copied at the end of shift rather than read live off the probe — the numbers are too consistent to be real fluctuation. Second, less common: a genuinely stable but miscalibrated thermometer.

**First question:** "Show me the actual reading right now, and when was this thermometer last calibrated against an ice bath?"

**Data to pull:** temp log timestamps vs. POS/clock-in timestamps for plausibility; thermometer calibration log; cross-check against a second unit.

### Same dish coming back from two tables with visibly different portions or plating

**Usually means:** a cook is freelancing off the recipe card, most often two-plus hours into a rush when fatigue erodes portion discipline first, or a newer cook was never walked through the station diagram. Second: two cooks covering the same station across a split shift with no shared spec check.

**First question:** "Which cook plated each of these, and did they use the station diagram or go from memory?"

**Data to pull:** POS ticket times matched to which cook was on station; last line-check date for that dish; portion-scale spot-checks over a week.

### The 86 board says an item is available but the POS is still selling it

**Usually means:** the verbal 86 call happened but the physical board or POS block was never updated — a break between the kitchen calling it and the front-of-house system reflecting it. Second: a partial 86 (one protein variant, not the whole dish) got flattened into a blanket "still available."

**First question:** "When was this item actually 86'd in the kitchen, and who was supposed to update the POS?"

**Data to pull:** timestamp of the verbal 86 call vs. POS block timestamp; count of orders taken for the item after the kitchen call; which role owns the POS update step.

### Raw or cooked protein sitting out of refrigeration for approaching 4 cumulative hours

**Usually means:** a station is staging too much product at room temperature for convenience during a rush, or product is cycling in and out of the danger zone (41–135°F) across multiple prep sessions without anyone summing the cumulative time. This is a food-safety violation risk, not a pacing issue.

**First question:** "How long, cumulative, has this product actually been between 41 and 135 degrees today — not just since it came out this time?"

**Data to pull:** temp log entries for that product across the full shift; time-stamps of every in-and-out-of-refrigeration cycle; ServSafe 4-hour cumulative threshold as the hard line, discard past it regardless of appearance.

### One station is chronically "in the weeds" every Friday or Saturday specifically

**Usually means:** the par sheet or staffing plan was built off a weekday or average-week baseline that doesn't hold for peak nights — the station is structurally understaffed or under-prepped for its busiest shift, not just having a bad run of luck. Second: the same cook is always scheduled on the hardest night without support.

**First question:** "Is this par sheet and staffing count built off average covers or off peak-night covers?"

**Data to pull:** covers by day-of-week over 8+ weeks; par sheet basis (average vs. peak); "behind" call frequency by day-of-week; staffing headcount by shift for that station.

### A cook cross-utilizes another station's mise en place without telling that station's cook

**Usually means:** one station ran short and grabbed from a neighbor rather than flagging the pass — solves the immediate ticket but silently creates a second shortage the other cook doesn't know is coming. Second: unclear station boundaries in a small kitchen where cooks share prep.

**First question:** "Did the station you borrowed from know their count just dropped?"

**Data to pull:** par sheet counts for both stations against what was actually fired; any 86 calls that followed within the same shift; whether cross-utilization is a standing house policy or an improvised fix.
