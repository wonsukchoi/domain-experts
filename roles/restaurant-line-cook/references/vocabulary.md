# Vocabulary

Terms of the line that generalists either don't know or use loosely enough to blur a real distinction. Each: definition, a sentence a cook would actually say, and the common misuse.

### Mise en place

Literally "everything in its place" — the complete set of ingredients, tools, and partially prepared components staged and ready before service starts, specific to one station.

**In use:** "My mise is set — I'm ready for doors."

**Common misuse:** treating it as a synonym for "prep" in general (chopping vegetables). Mise en place is the state of readiness itself, including tool placement and mental sequencing — a station can have every ingredient prepped and still not have its mise en place set if the layout isn't right for service speed.

### Brigade de cuisine

Escoffier's station-based kitchen hierarchy — executive chef, sous chef, chef de partie (station chef), commis (station apprentice), expediter — where each named role owns one accountable slice of the kitchen.

**In use:** "We don't run a full brigade here, but the chicken station is still one person's name on every plate that leaves it."

**Common misuse:** small independent kitchens adopting the full French title set for a four-person line. The useful part of the brigade concept is one-station-one-accountable-cook, not the titles.

### Chef de partie vs. commis vs. line cook

**Chef de partie** runs a station with full authority over its output. **Commis** works under a chef de partie, learning the station without final authority over what leaves it. **Line cook** is the general American-kitchen term that usually maps to chef de partie level — running a station solo during service.

**In use:** "She's still commis on garde manger — she plates, but the chef de partie signs off before anything fires."

**Common misuse:** using "line cook" and "sous chef" interchangeably. A sous chef runs the whole kitchen in the chef's absence and sets the par sheet; a line cook executes one station to that spec.

### Expo / expediter (aboyeur)

The role that reads tickets aloud, calls fire timing, coordinates all stations so a table's dishes land together, and is the single point of contact with the floor during service.

**In use:** "Expo's calling entrées in three — get your garnish up now."

**Common misuse:** assuming expo is just relaying tickets. Expo's real job is timing coordination across stations so a four-top's plates arrive simultaneously, which is a harder problem than any single station's cook time.

### 86'd

An item that's out of stock and can no longer be ordered for the rest of service — origin disputed (soda-fountain slang, Prohibition-era code, or rhyming slang), meaning is fixed across kitchens.

**In use:** "86 the halibut — we're out."

**Common misuse:** using it loosely for "we're running low" rather than "cannot be fulfilled at all." Calling 86 too early wastes usable stock; calling it too late risks selling something the kitchen can't deliver — the discipline is precision on the moment it becomes true.

### Behind / in the weeds

**Behind** is a specific, callable status: this station cannot currently hit its ticket times and needs the pass or another station to know. **In the weeds** is the informal, broader term for a station or whole line overwhelmed past normal recovery.

**In use:** "I'm behind on the grill side — six back." vs. "We were in the weeds from 7 to 9, full stop."

**Common misuse:** treating "behind" as an admission that reflects badly on the cook, so it goes uncalled until a manager or guest notices — exactly the failure mode that turns a two-minute gap into a service-wide backup.

### Calling the pass / calling back

The verbal call-and-response discipline where every instruction from expo gets repeated back by the station before execution, and every status update from a station gets acknowledged by expo.

**In use:** "Heard — two chicken, one salmon, no onion."

**Common misuse:** nodding or grunting acknowledgment instead of repeating the actual content back. A nod doesn't catch a mishearing; the repeated words do.

### All day (count)

The aggregate count of a given item across every currently open ticket on a station, tracked continuously rather than reset per new ticket.

**In use:** "All day, four chicken, two salmon, one filet."

**Common misuse:** firing per-ticket instead of tracking all-day counts — a station that fires one chicken breast five separate times because five tickets each called for one, instead of batching the pan once for all five, is slower and more error-prone under fire.

### On the fly

A ticket fired immediately, out of the normal course-pacing sequence, usually because a table is in a hurry or a mistake needs correcting fast.

**In use:** "Fire that entrée on the fly — table's catching a flight."

**Common misuse:** using "on the fly" for anything urgent, including things that are actually the station's own lateness. On the fly is a deliberate pass decision to break course sequencing for a stated reason, not a cook's excuse for having fallen behind.

### Par level

The target quantity of a prepped item a station should hold at any point in service, set by the chef off historical sell-through, not a number the line cook resets independently.

**In use:** "We're at par on salmon, but chicken's already at 80% — starting a backup batch."

**Common misuse:** treating par as a one-time number rather than something that should shift with day-of-week, season, and known events (private parties, local news driving walk-ins) — a stale par sheet is a leading cause of mid-service shortages.

### FIFO (first in, first out)

The rotation discipline where the oldest labeled and dated product is used before newer stock of the same item, enforced by physical placement (oldest in front) not memory.

**In use:** "Pull from the front — that batch is dated yesterday."

**Common misuse:** dating containers but not actually rotating stock placement, which defeats the purpose; the label only works if the oldest product is also physically the easiest one to reach.

### Danger zone / time-temperature control

The 41°F–135°F range (ServSafe) in which bacterial growth accelerates, with a cumulative 4-hour limit on total time food may spend in that range before it must be discarded regardless of appearance or smell.

**In use:** "That chicken's been in and out of the zone three times today — call it, we're over four hours cumulative."

**Common misuse:** resetting the mental clock every time product goes back into the cooler, rather than summing total cumulative time across all the times it left refrigeration that day.

### Line check

The pre-shift and pre-service walkthrough of every station — mise en place against par, temps, tool and knife readiness, plating spec — done by the chef or sous chef, distinct from the cook's own personal setup.

**In use:** "Line check in ten — get your temps logged before he comes through."

**Common misuse:** treating line check as the cook's own prep review. It's a supervisory verification step; a cook who is "ready" and a station that "passes line check" are not automatically the same thing.
