### Out-of-service (OOS) criteria
The published, numeric thresholds and defect definitions (CVSA North American Standard Out-of-Service Criteria) that determine whether a vehicle, driver, or cargo condition must be immediately removed from service, revised annually and effective each April 1.
**In use:** "Stroke measured 2.25 inches against a 2.00-inch OOS threshold for this chamber — that's out of service, not a repair recommendation."
**Common misuse:** Treating OOS as a judgment call about how urgent a repair feels, rather than a fixed threshold that either is or isn't crossed by the measured number.

### North American Standard (NAS) Inspection Levels
The tiered inspection scopes (Level I full driver-and-vehicle exam through Level VI enhanced radiological) that define what is and isn't checked at a given stop.
**In use:** "Hazmat placard visible and the lane has 25 minutes — that's a Level I, not a Level II."
**Common misuse:** Treating the level as a formality chosen after the fact to match how much time was actually spent, rather than a decision made deliberately before approaching the vehicle.

### Pushrod stroke / brake adjustment
The distance an air-brake chamber's pushrod travels when the brake is applied; excessive stroke for the chamber's type and size means the brake can't develop full braking force and is out of adjustment.
**In use:** "This is a Type 30 long-stroke chamber — the threshold is 2.00 inches, not the 1.75 that applies to the standard-stroke version."
**Common misuse:** Reading stroke at rest instead of at full application, or applying a generic threshold instead of the one specific to the chamber's stamped type and size.

### 20%-brakes rule
The CVSA aggregate criterion placing a vehicle or combination out of service when 20% or more of its total brake positions are defective, regardless of how severe any single defective brake is on its own.
**In use:** "Three of ten positions are defective — that's 30%, so the combination is OOS on the aggregate rule even though none of the three readings alone is dramatic."
**Common misuse:** Evaluating each brake position in isolation and stopping once one position is marked defective, without running the aggregate count across the whole vehicle.

### CVSA decal
A sticker issued after a Level I (or Level V-equivalent) inspection finds zero out-of-service violations, valid 3 months from the inspection date.
**In use:** "No decal issued — this inspection found three OOS violations, so it doesn't qualify regardless of what else passed."
**Common misuse:** Treating the decal as proof the vehicle's 12-month DOT annual inspection is current, when it certifies a different, shorter-cycle event entirely.

### DOT annual inspection (49 CFR 396.17)
The federally required periodic inspection, performed by a qualified inspector, that every commercial motor vehicle must pass at least once every 12 months to remain in service.
**In use:** "The annual sticker is dated 14 months ago — that's expired regardless of how recent the CVSA decal is."
**Common misuse:** Confusing the 12-month annual cycle with the 3-month CVSA decal cycle, or assuming a roadside pass substitutes for the periodic annual requirement.

### Placarding threshold (Table 1 / Table 2, 49 CFR 172.504)
The hazmat regulation dividing materials into Table 1 (placard required at any quantity) and Table 2 (placard required once the aggregate gross weight of that hazard class reaches 1,001 lbs).
**In use:** "440 gallons of Class 3 works out to about 3,168 pounds — well over the 1,001-pound Table 2 threshold, so placarding is mandatory."
**Common misuse:** Checking a single container's weight against the threshold instead of summing the aggregate weight across every package of that hazard class on the shipping paper.

### Shipping paper
The hazmat shipment's legal document of record — hazard class, proper shipping name, quantity, and emergency contact — against which the vehicle's placards must be verified.
**In use:** "The placard says Class 8, but the shipping paper lists this load as Class 3 — that's a mismatch, not a paperwork nuance."
**Common misuse:** Treating the shipping paper as a formality to confirm cargo exists, rather than as the controlling document the placard is legally required to match.

### Warning vs. out-of-service disposition
A warning documents a defect that doesn't meet an OOS threshold but should be repaired before it becomes one; an OOS disposition is a legal bar on continued operation until the specific condition is corrected.
**In use:** "The tread is at 5/32 — that's a warning on wear, not OOS; the steer tire at 3/32 is the one that's out of service."
**Common misuse:** Using "warning" as a softer synonym for a defect that is actually OOS, or issuing an OOS order for a condition that hasn't actually crossed the published threshold.

### Vehicle Maintenance BASIC (Safety Measurement System)
One of FMCSA's Behavior Analysis and Safety Improvement Categories (BASICs) under the Compliance, Safety, Accountability (CSA) program, tracking a carrier's roadside violation history related to vehicle maintenance and scoring it as a percentile against other carriers.
**In use:** "This carrier's Vehicle Maintenance BASIC percentile is elevated — that's a risk signal for choosing Level I over a faster level on this stop."
**Common misuse:** Treating a single inspection's outcome as directly changing "the carrier's score" in the moment, rather than understanding it as one data point feeding a rolling percentile calculated over a longer window.

### Fifth wheel / kingpin coupling
The mechanical interface connecting a tractor's fifth wheel to a semitrailer's kingpin; a rated-capacity mismatch or an unlocked/partially seated kingpin is a coupling-device defect independent of brake or tire condition.
**In use:** "The kingpin looks seated but isn't fully locked — that's an OOS coupling defect regardless of how the rest of the rig checks out."
**Common misuse:** Confirming the kingpin is physically present in the fifth wheel and treating that as sufficient, without checking that the locking mechanism is actually engaged.

### Working load limit (WLL) / cargo securement
The rated maximum force a tie-down or securement device can safely apply, which combines with tie-down count to determine whether a specific cargo weight and length is adequately secured under 49 CFR Part 393 Subpart I.
**In use:** "Four tie-downs rated at 5,000 pounds each doesn't clear the aggregate WLL this load's weight requires — that's a securement violation, not a style preference."
**Common misuse:** Judging securement by whether tie-downs are present at all, rather than computing whether the count and rated WLL actually meet the formula for the cargo's specific weight and length.
