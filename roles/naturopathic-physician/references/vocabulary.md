# Naturopathic Physician — Vocabulary

### Therapeutic order
The sequenced hierarchy of intervention (Pizzorno & Murray) from least to most invasive: establish healthy conditions, stimulate self-healing, support weakened systems, correct structure, specific natural substances, specific pharmaceuticals, symptom suppression.
**In use:** "We're at step 3 for the iron deficiency and step 6 for the thyroid dose — that's the PCP's call, not mine."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a philosophy that justifies delaying urgent referral, rather than a sequencing tool that a red flag overrides entirely.

### Vis medicatrix naturae
Latin for "the healing power of nature" — the principle that the body has an inherent capacity to restore and maintain health given the right conditions.
**In use:** "The rash cleared once we removed the trigger food — that's vis medicatrix naturae doing the actual work, not the supplement."
**Common misuse:** Invoked as a reason to withhold conventional workup on a presentation that hasn't been shown to be self-limited.

### Tolle causam
Latin, "treat the cause" — the principle of addressing the underlying driver of a condition rather than only its symptom.
**In use:** "Suppressing the reflux with an antacid doesn't address why she's making excess acid at 2am."
**Common misuse:** Used to justify open-ended investigation into a "root cause" instead of naming a specific, testable one with a re-check date.

### Docere
Latin, "doctor as teacher" — the principle that patient education is a core clinical function, not an add-on.
**In use:** "The visit ran long because docere on the interaction mechanism took ten minutes, not because I was behind schedule."
**Common misuse:** Confused with simply talking more; docere means teaching the specific mechanism that changes patient behavior, not general wellness conversation.

### Functional range
A lab-value threshold associated with symptomatic disease or dysfunction, which may sit inside or outside the lab's own printed statistical reference range.
**In use:** "Ferritin of 22 is inside your lab's normal range, but it's below the functional threshold where deficiency symptoms appear."
**Common misuse:** Extended to every marker without a specific cited threshold, turning "functional range" into a license to treat any number the patient dislikes.

### Wolff-Chaikoff effect
A transient reduction in thyroid hormone synthesis triggered by excess iodine exposure; in autoimmune thyroiditis this suppression can fail to escape and worsen hypothyroidism.
**In use:** "Kelp is contraindicated here — her Hashimoto's means she's at real risk of a Wolff-Chaikoff response, not a supportive one."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a reason iodine is universally dangerous, when it's specifically a risk in excess intake and in autoimmune thyroid disease, not at RDA-level intake in a euthyroid patient.

### Herb-drug interaction
A pharmacologic or pharmacokinetic interaction between a botanical/supplement and a pharmaceutical, via mechanisms including CYP450 induction/inhibition or absorption competition.
**In use:** "St. John's Wort induces CYP3A4 strongly enough to drop oral contraceptive efficacy — that's a real interaction, not a theoretical one."
**Common misuse:** Dismissed as unlikely because the substance is "natural," rather than checked against a named interaction reference.

### Scope of practice
The specific set of clinical actions (ordering labs, prescribing, performing procedures) a given state's naturopathic license legally authorizes.
**In use:** "My license in this state doesn't include prescriptive authority for thyroid hormone, so that stays with your PCP."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to be uniform across states, or assumed to match training rather than the specific license actually held.

### Co-management
An explicit division of clinical responsibility between a naturopathic physician and another prescriber/specialist for the same patient, with each party's decisions stated in writing.
**In use:** "This is a co-managed case — I own the iron repletion, you own the levothyroxine dose."
**Common misuse:** Used loosely to mean "the patient also sees another provider," without a documented statement of who owns which specific decision.

### NPLEX (Naturopathic Physicians Licensing Examinations)
The two-part board examination (NPLEX I: basic sciences; NPLEX II: clinical sciences) required for naturopathic licensure in jurisdictions that recognize the credential.
**In use:** "NPLEX II clinical-science boards, not just the degree, are what most state boards require for licensure."
**Common misuse:** Conflated with a medical board exam of equivalent scope and mandatory postgraduate residency requirement, which NPLEX does not carry.

### CNME (Council on Naturopathic Medical Education)
The accrediting body for naturopathic medical programs in the US and Canada, recognized by the US Department of Education.
**In use:** "Only CNME-accredited program graduates are eligible for licensure in states that require it."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to guarantee a mandatory clinical residency comparable to MD/DO training; naturopathic residency is available but optional and far less universally completed.

### Adaptogen
A category of botanicals (e.g., ashwagandha, rhodiola) claimed to help the body resist non-specific stress; evidence quality varies widely by specific herb and indication.
**In use:** "Ashwagandha has trial-level evidence for cortisol reduction at this dose range — that's a step-5 substitution, not a step-1 lifestyle add."
**Common misuse:** Used as a blanket term implying uniform, strong evidence across every herb in the category, when evidence grade varies substantially herb to herb.

### Detoxification (clinical sense)
In naturopathic practice, protocols intended to support the body's own elimination pathways (hepatic, renal, lymphatic); distinct from the marketing use of "detox" for unproven cleanses.
**In use:** "The support here is targeted at documented hepatic conjugation pathways, not a generic 7-day cleanse."
**Common misuse:** Applied to any restrictive diet or supplement regimen without naming the specific pathway or mechanism being supported.

### IV nutrient therapy
Intravenous administration of vitamins/minerals (e.g., Myers' cocktail), permitted only in states whose naturopathic scope explicitly grants injection therapy.
**In use:** "I'd need the injection-therapy certification and this state's authorization before offering that, not just clinical judgment that it would help."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to be a universally available naturopathic tool rather than a scope-gated one requiring specific state authorization.

### Primum non nocere
Latin, "first, do no harm" — the principle that an intervention's risk must be actively weighed, not assumed low because it's naturopathic.
**In use:** "The interaction risk here outweighs the plausible benefit — primum non nocere means we don't add it."
**Common misuse:** Invoked as if it applies only to pharmaceuticals, when it equally constrains botanical and supplement choices with real interaction risk.
