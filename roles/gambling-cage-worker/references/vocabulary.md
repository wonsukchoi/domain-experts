# Gambling Cage Worker — Vocabulary

### Marker
A short-term credit instrument issued to a patron against an approved credit line, redeemable in chips at the table and repayable in cash or by check.
**In use:** "Patron's asking for a $5,000 marker — he's got $12,000 outstanding already against a $15,000 line, so this one's fine, but flag him close to the ceiling."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a casino "loan" in the consumer sense — it's a specific credit instrument with its own documentation and collection process, not an informal IOU.

### Fill
Chips transferred from the cage to a table's chip tray to replenish it, documented on a fill slip signed by dealer, pit supervisor, and cage worker.
**In use:** "Table 12 needs a fill — $3,000 in green, dealer's confirming the count when it arrives."
**Common misuse:** Confused with a "credit" — a fill moves chips cage-to-table; a credit moves chips table-to-cage. They're opposite directions and both require the same three-signature discipline.

### Credit (cage context)
Chips transferred from a table back to the cage, reducing the table's chip inventory, documented on a credit slip.
**In use:** "Table's running heavy on green — sending a $2,500 credit back to the cage before end of shift."
**Common misuse:** Not to be confused with a "marker" or patron credit line — this "credit" refers to chip movement between table and cage, an entirely different concept sharing the word.

### Drop
The total cash and markers deposited into a table's drop box over a shift, used to calculate table win/loss (hold percentage) independent of the cage's own reconciliation.
**In use:** "Table 8's drop was $22,000 tonight against a $6,500 hold — that's about 30%, worth a second look given the usual range."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a cage-worker concern — the drop and hold-percentage calculation is a table-games/accounting function; the cage worker's reconciliation is a separate, parallel control.

### Hard count
The formal, witnessed process of counting a table's drop box contents (typically in a secured count room, on camera, with multiple counters), producing the official win/loss figure for accounting.
**In use:** "Hard count team is running the box from Table 8 now — we'll have the official drop number in twenty minutes."
**Common misuse:** Confused with the cage's own shift-change bank count — the hard count is table-drop-specific and happens in a separate secured process, not at the cage window.

### Title 31 / CTR (Currency Transaction Report)
The Bank Secrecy Act reporting regime (31 CFR Part 1021) requiring casinos to file a CTR for cash transactions (or aggregated same-day transactions by one patron) exceeding $10,000.
**In use:** "Patron's aggregate cash-out hit $10,500 across two redemptions today — filing the CTR before end of shift."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to apply only to a single transaction over $10,000 — the aggregation requirement (same patron, same gaming day, same direction) is what most compliance gaps miss.

### Structuring
Deliberately breaking a large cash transaction into smaller sub-threshold pieces to avoid triggering a CTR — itself a federal offense under the Bank Secrecy Act regardless of the underlying funds' legitimacy.
**In use:** "Three redemptions today, each just under $10,000, same patron — that pattern gets flagged as possible structuring even though no single one crossed the line."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to require "dirty money" to matter — structuring is illegal based on the act of evading the reporting threshold, independent of whether the funds are legitimate.

### Cage bank
The cage's working cash and chip inventory, reconciled against a book balance (opening balance plus fills, minus credits, minus net payouts) at shift change.
**In use:** "Bank reconciled clean this shift — book and physical count matched to the dollar."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a single number to eyeball — it's a formula-driven reconciliation with specific inputs (fills, credits, redemptions) that each need independent verification, not just a final total comparison.

### Credit limit (patron)
The maximum outstanding marker balance a patron is approved for, set by a credit committee based on financial verification, not negotiable at the cage window.
**In use:** "He's at his limit — cage can't extend it, he'd need to go through credit services for an increase."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to be flexible based on the patron's play level or relationship with staff — the cage worker's job is to enforce the approved limit, not exercise discretion on it.

### Chip-tray count
The physical count of chips in a table's tray, done at intervals and at shift change, cross-checked against expected inventory (opening count plus fills minus credits minus payouts).
**In use:** "Chip-tray count came up $200 light on Table 5 — checking the fill slips before assuming a counting error."
**Common misuse:** Confused with the cage's own bank reconciliation — the tray count is a table-level control that feeds into but is separate from the cage's overall reconciliation.

### Aggregation (Title 31 context)
The requirement to sum a single patron's cash transactions in one direction (all cash-in, or all cash-out) across a single gaming day, not evaluate each transaction in isolation, when determining CTR reporting obligations.
**In use:** "Neither transaction alone hit ten thousand, but aggregated they did — that's still a CTR."
**Common misuse:** Applied across cash-in and cash-out together — Title 31 aggregates each direction independently, not combined.
