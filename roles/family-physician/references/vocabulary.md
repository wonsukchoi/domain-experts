# Vocabulary

Terms of art generalists misuse or flatten.

### Continuity of care
The measurable degree to which a patient sees the same clinician(s) over time, often tracked as a continuity index (e.g., proportion of visits with the usual physician).
**In use:** "Her continuity index dropped to 40% after she started seeing whoever's available same-day — worth pulling her back to a scheduled slot with me."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a soft, unmeasurable "nice to have" rather than a tracked metric that correlates with outcomes and gets actively managed like any other quality measure.

### Panel size
The number of patients formally attributed to a physician's ongoing care, used to plan capacity (commonly 1,500–2,500 per FTE in adult primary care).
**In use:** "At 2,400, this panel is above the sustainable range for one FTE — we need to either close it or add capacity before quality slips."
**Common misuse:** Confused with daily patient volume; panel size is the total roster under a physician's care, not how many are seen in a day.

### Advanced access (open-access) scheduling
A scheduling model that matches daily appointment capacity to daily demand so most patients are offered a same-day or next-day slot, rather than booking weeks out.
**In use:** "We moved to advanced access last quarter — the backlog cleared in about eight weeks once we stopped pre-booking so far ahead."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to mean "no scheduling at all" or double-booking everyone same-day; it's a deliberately balanced capacity model, not the absence of a system.

### Multimorbidity
The presence of two or more chronic conditions in the same patient, each with its own guideline, that must be jointly prioritized rather than treated as independent problems.
**In use:** "This isn't a diabetes visit or a CKD visit — it's a multimorbidity visit, and the CKD trajectory takes priority over the A1c number today."
**Common misuse:** Used interchangeably with "comorbidity," which implies one primary disease with secondary conditions attached, rather than several co-equal conditions competing for the same visit time and treatment budget.

### Undifferentiated illness
A presentation that hasn't yet resolved into a specific diagnosis and may legitimately require time, not more testing, to declare itself.
**In use:** "This is still undifferentiated at two days in — reasonable to recheck in 48 hours rather than image everything today."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a synonym for "unclear, so order more tests," when the correct response is often structured watchful waiting with clear return precautions.

### Hidden agenda
The patient's actual underlying concern, distinct from the stated chief complaint, that a visit isn't complete without surfacing.
**In use:** "The stated complaint was a cough, but the hidden agenda was that her father died of lung cancer last month — that's what the visit was really about."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to mean the patient is being deceptive; it usually reflects a patient who doesn't know how to name the real worry, not one who is withholding it deliberately.

### PCMH (Patient-Centered Medical Home)
An NCQA-recognized practice model built around a coordinated care team, proactive population management, and same-day access, rather than a solo physician handling everything.
**In use:** "Our PCMH recognition means the care coordinator handles the referral tracking, so I'm not the one chasing down whether the nephrology appointment happened."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a facility label or building type rather than a team-based operating model; "home" refers to the care relationship, not a physical clinic.

### Watchful waiting
A deliberate, time-boxed decision to recheck rather than immediately test or treat, used when the cost of waiting is lower than the cost and risk of premature intervention.
**In use:** "Watchful waiting with a 2-week recheck is the right call here — the red flags aren't present, and most of these resolve without imaging."
**Common misuse:** Confused with inaction or neglect; a valid watchful-waiting plan includes explicit return precautions and a scheduled recheck, not silence until the patient calls back.

### Curbside consult
An informal, uncharted question to a specialist colleague to answer a narrow "does this need your clinic" question, distinct from a formal referral.
**In use:** "I curbsided nephrology before referring — they said manage it in primary care for now and re-check in three months."
**Common misuse:** Treated as equivalent to a formal consult that generates a documented specialist opinion; a curbside answer isn't a substitute for a real referral once the question becomes complex enough to need exam and records review.

### E/M coding (Evaluation & Management)
The billing-code system (e.g., 99213 vs. 99214 vs. 99215) that reflects visit complexity, based on either medical decision-making or time spent, not simply visit length.
**In use:** "Two chronic conditions with one being exacerbated and a medication change pushes this to a 99214, not a 99213."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to be purely about how long the visit took; current guidelines let either time or medical-decision-making complexity justify the level, and complexity alone can justify a higher code even in a short visit.

### Problem list
The chart's living, prioritized inventory of a patient's active diagnoses, reconciled at each visit rather than left as an unmanaged running log.
**In use:** "Her problem list still shows 'possible pneumonia' from eight months ago — that needs resolving or removing before it confuses the next covering physician."
**Common misuse:** Left to accumulate indefinitely as a historical record rather than actively curated, which causes a stale, inactive entry to be mistaken for a current active issue.

### Care gap
A specific, trackable overdue element of guideline-recommended care (a screening, a lab, a vaccination) for a given patient, identified through registry review rather than only at the point of an in-person visit.
**In use:** "The registry flagged her as a care gap for colorectal screening — due for the conversation at her next visit regardless of why she's actually coming in."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a checklist item to tack onto a visit already crowded with other concerns, rather than something proactively addressed outside the acute visit via portal outreach or a dedicated nurse visit.

### Shared decision-making
A structured conversation for USPSTF Grade C or I recommendations, where the evidence doesn't clearly favor one path and the patient's values determine the choice.
**In use:** "PSA screening at his age is Grade C — this is a shared decision-making conversation, not a default recommendation either way."
**Common misuse:** Used loosely to describe any conversation where a patient is informed of a decision already made, rather than a genuine deliberation where either choice is a reasonable, guideline-supported default.

### Deprescribing
The deliberate, systematic process of discontinuing or tapering medications that no longer have a clear indication or net benefit, distinct from simply not renewing a refill by oversight.
**In use:** "This is a deprescribing visit — every one of her ten medications gets a documented reason to continue or a taper plan today."
**Common misuse:** Conflated with a refill being denied due to insurance friction; deprescribing is a clinical decision with a documented rationale and taper plan, not an administrative lapse.

### HEDIS measure
A standardized quality metric (Healthcare Effectiveness Data and Information Set) used by payers to score population-level performance, such as percentage of diabetic patients with A1c <8%.
**In use:** "Our HEDIS diabetes-control rate is 68% this quarter — the registry outreach for the other 32% is where the next improvement has to come from."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a measure of any individual encounter's quality, when it's a population-denominator metric meant to drive panel-level outreach, not a per-visit scorecard.
