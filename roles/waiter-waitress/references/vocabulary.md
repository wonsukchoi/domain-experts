# Vocabulary

Terms generalists conflate that career servers keep sharply separate.

## Covers vs. check average vs. PPA

**Covers** are the number of guests served, not tables. **Check average** is total sales divided by number of checks (tables). **PPA** (per-person average) is total sales divided by covers — the number that actually reflects how much each guest spent, since a check average can look healthy while hiding a large party that dragged the number up.

*In use:* "Check average looks fine tonight, but PPA is down — we're turning bigger parties, not selling better per guest."

**Common misuse:** quoting check average as if it measures selling skill per guest; a section full of 6-tops inflates check average without anyone upselling anything.

## Firing vs. punching vs. course timing

**Punching** is entering the order into the POS. **Firing** is the specific act of sending a held course to the kitchen to begin cooking, which may happen well after punching if the course is being timed against another. **Course timing** is the overall discipline of sequencing multiple fires across a table or section so plates land when the guest is actually ready.

*In use:* "Punch the whole table now, but don't fire the entrées — I'll fire when the apps clear."

**Common misuse:** treating punch and fire as the same action; on most POS systems the default behavior sends everything to the kitchen at punch time unless the server actively holds it, which is exactly how autopilot firing causes collisions.

## Tip credit vs. tip pool vs. tip-out

**Tip credit** is the federal (or state) mechanism letting an employer count a portion of tips toward the minimum-wage obligation, paying a lower direct cash wage. **Tip pool** is tips collected and redistributed among a defined group of employees by a set formula. **Tip-out** is the specific transfer a server makes from their own tips (or sales) to support roles like bussers and runners — a subset of, or alternative to, a full pool.

*In use:* "We're not on a full tip pool here, it's tip-out — I keep what I make and pay a set percentage to bussers and runners."

**Common misuse:** using "tip pool" to describe any tip-sharing arrangement; a true pool redistributes by formula across everyone in it, while tip-out is a one-directional payment from the tipped employee outward.

## 80/20/30 rule (dual-jobs rule)

The 2021 DOL rule governing when an employer can apply the tip credit to non-tip-producing "directly supporting" work (rolling silverware, brewing coffee, wiping down stations). The tip credit applies to that supporting work only up to 20% of the workweek, or a continuous stretch no longer than 30 minutes — beyond either threshold, that time must be paid at full minimum wage.

*In use:* "I was on side-work for 45 minutes straight before the shift opened — that's past the 30-minute rule, it should be paid at full minimum, not the tipped rate."

**Common misuse:** assuming any non-serving task automatically disqualifies tip credit for the whole shift; the rule is about the proportion and continuous duration of that specific work, not a blanket ban.

## Comp vs. void vs. 86'd

**Comp** is giving an item away after it was made and rung, usually for service recovery. **Void** is removing an item from a check before or without it being served, typically a punch-in error. **86'd** means the kitchen is out of an item entirely, unrelated to any individual table's situation.

*In use:* "That's a comp, not a void — the dish went out fine, the table waited too long for it."

**Common misuse:** using "comp" and "void" interchangeably in shift reporting, which makes it impossible for management to tell service-recovery cost apart from order-entry error rate.

## Reading the table

The practiced skill of inferring a table's mood, pace preference, and occasion from body language, conversation volume, and ordering pattern, without asking directly. Distinct from a script, which applies the same behavior regardless of signal.

*In use:* "I read the table as wanting to linger — no rushing the check, no upsell pitch, just keep water full."

**Common misuse:** treating "reading the table" as an innate talent that can't be described or taught; in practice it's built from specific, nameable signals (pace of conversation, whether menus stay open, kids at the table) that a server can be coached on.

## Stacking / pacing

**Pacing** is the host's discipline of spreading a server's incoming tables across time so each can be run in sequence. **Stacking** is the failure mode: multiple tables in the same section seated within minutes of each other, guaranteeing simultaneous course-stage collisions later in the meal.

*In use:* "Host stacked my whole section in a seven-minute window — I need to know now so I can call the fire sequence to the kitchen early."

**Common misuse:** blaming the server for a pacing collision that originated at the host stand; the server's job under stacking is damage control (staggered firing, proactive guest communication), not preventing the stack itself.

## Camping

Guest behavior of occupying a table well past the meal's natural end without ordering more or requesting the check, common at high-demand times.

*In use:* "Table 6 is camping — I'll drop the check without being asked, that's the polite nudge here."

**Common misuse:** treating every slow-to-leave table as camping; a table that's still ordering (dessert, another round) is not camping even if it's running long, and rushing it produces the same tip-percentage penalty as any other rushed table.

## Big 9 allergens / allergen matrix

The **Big 9** are the major food allergens the FDA requires be identified on packaged food labels and that most restaurant allergen-training programs (ServSafe Allergens) treat as the disclosure baseline: milk, eggs, fish, shellfish, tree nuts, peanuts, wheat, soybeans, and sesame (added by the FASTER Act, effective January 2023). The **allergen matrix** is the house reference document mapping every menu item to which of these it contains, including hidden sources like stocks and shared fryer oil.

*In use:* "Check the matrix before you tell a table something's nut-free — the vinaigrette has a cashew base that isn't obvious from the menu description."

**Common misuse:** relying on menu descriptions or memory instead of the matrix; cross-contact sources (shared fryers, shared cutting boards, sauce bases) are exactly the things a menu description won't mention.
