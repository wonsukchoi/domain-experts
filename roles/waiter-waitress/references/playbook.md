# Playbook

Filled tables and worked sequences a server can run directly, not descriptions of them.

## Section sizing by service style

| Service style | Tables/server | Covers/server | Table-turn target | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Fine dining, dedicated runner team | 3-4 | 12-16 | 90-120 min | Server plates/pours tableside for some courses; runner carries food, server carries the guest relationship |
| Casual-upscale, no dedicated runner | 4-5 | 16-20 | 60-75 min | Server fires, runs food, and buses own tables during peak |
| Casual/family dining | 5-6 | 20-24 | 45-60 min | Bussers dedicated; server's job is almost pure course-timing and check-drop speed |
| High-volume/diner | 7-8 | 28-32 | 30-40 min | POS auto-fires on order; server's judgment shifts almost entirely to triage, not pacing |

Above the top of a row's covers band, course-timing errors (cold apps, entrée collisions) start rising faster than tip income compensates for — that's the signal a section is oversized for the format, not just busy.

## Course-fire timing windows

| Course | Default fire trigger | Early-fire exception | Kitchen ticket-time target |
|---|---|---|---|
| Appetizer | Immediately on order | — | 8-10 min casual, 12-15 min fine dining |
| Entrée (fast proteins: fish, pasta, salad) | When apps are ~80% cleared from the table | None needed | 10-14 min |
| Entrée (slow proteins: braises, well-done steak, bone-in chicken) | Same trigger, but flagged verbally to expo as "fire early" 3-5 min ahead of the fast-protein tables in the same fire batch | Cook time exceeds 18-20 min | 18-25 min |
| Dessert | After entrée plates are fully cleared and check-ready pacing confirmed with the table | Guest states a time constraint (early movie, meeting) — fire earlier and say so | 6-10 min |

**Kitchen line-capacity rule of thumb:** a single hot line (sauté/grill) runs cleanly at roughly 2 entrée tickets per 5-6 minute window. When a server's own section would push more than that into the same window — because a host stacked tables too close together — stagger the fire across 2-4 extra minutes per held ticket rather than sending all tickets as ordered. A verbal fire call to the expo ("firing 11 and 12 now, holding 14 four minutes, 15 stays on time") is the actual mechanism; the printer alone won't do this.

## Tip-out settlement models

**Model A — percentage of tips collected** (used when the house takes no tip credit, or takes it only on server wages):
- Bussers: 15% of server's tips
- Food runners: 5% of server's tips
- Bartenders (if server rings drinks through the bar): 3-5% of bar sales rung, not of tips

Worked example: server collects $340 in tips on a shift. Tip-out: bussers $51.00 (15%), food runners $17.00 (5%) = $68.00 out, server nets $272.00.

**Model B — percentage of sales** (flat regardless of how the table tipped):
- Bussers: 3-4% of net food sales
- Food runners: 1-2% of net food sales
- Bar: 1% of net food sales if drinks are rung through server

Worked example: server rings $1,900 in net sales. Tip-out: bussers 3.5% = $66.50, runners 1.5% = $28.50, bar 1% = $19.00 = $114.00 out — owed regardless of what the tables actually tipped that night. A server under Model B who gets tipped poorly (say 12% blended instead of the usual 19%) still owes the same $114.00, which is the real mechanism behind why some servers under-invest in a table they've already read as a low tipper — know which model a house runs before assuming a slow table is "not worth the effort."

**FLSA tip-credit reconciliation (federal baseline, verify state law):**
- Federal minimum wage: $7.25/hr. Maximum tip credit: $5.12/hr. Federal tipped direct cash wage: $2.13/hr.
- Employer must ensure direct wage + tips ≥ $7.25/hr for every hour worked, or make up the difference.
- 2021 DOL "80/20/30" dual-jobs rule: tip credit applies only to hours spent on tip-producing work, or on directly supporting work (rolling silverware, refilling condiments) up to 20% of the workweek or a continuous 30 minutes — directly supporting work beyond that threshold must be paid at full minimum wage for those hours.
- Several states (CA, WA, OR, MN, AK, MT, NV among others) require full state minimum wage before tips, with no tip credit at all — always check the state baseline before applying the federal figures above.

Worked example (federal baseline, no state override): 8-hour shift, direct wage $2.13 x 8 = $17.04. Total tips for the shift: $210.00. Required minimum for 8 hours: $7.25 x 8 = $58.00. Actual total pay: $17.04 + $210.00 = $227.04, well above the $58.00 floor — no employer make-up owed. If the same server had only made $30.00 in tips that shift (a dead Tuesday lunch), $17.04 + $30.00 = $47.04 falls short of $58.00, and the employer owes an $10.96 make-up to reach the floor.

## Upsell script bank by table read

| Table read | Default move | Avoid |
|---|---|---|
| Asks "what do you recommend?" | Name one specific second-tier item with a one-line reason ("the branzino — it's lighter, comes off the wood grill tonight") | Defaulting to the most expensive item every time |
| Celebrating an occasion | Offer a specific add-on tied to the occasion (a shareable dessert, a glass of something by-the-glass) once, don't repeat the offer if declined | Repeating the same upsell after a clear decline |
| Clearly price-conscious (asks about portion size, shares a single entrée) | Suggest a shareable app or side instead of pushing a second entrée | Pushing an add-on that reads as ignoring the signal |
| Regular who orders the same thing every visit | Mention a new or seasonal item once, briefly, without pressure | Re-pitching the same special every visit once declined twice
| Large party (8+) | Suggest family-style shareable items to speed pacing and raise check average simultaneously | Taking individual orders for 8 separate entrées when the kitchen fire window can't support it (see fire-timing table above) |

## Allergen disclosure sequence

1. Guest states an allergy or restriction verbally.
2. Confirm out loud, specifically: "no shellfish at all, including the stock in the sauces — got it."
3. Enter the modifier on the POS ticket using the house allergen code, not a free-text note alone.
4. State it again directly to the expo or kitchen manager at fire time, not just at order time: "table 14, seat 2, shellfish allergy, confirmed with the chef."
5. When the dish returns, confirm at the table before setting it down: "this is the version without shellfish, confirmed with the kitchen."

Never substitute step 2-5 with a single "I'll let them know" — that phrase, spoken and not logged, is the pattern behind most service-side allergic-reaction incidents.
