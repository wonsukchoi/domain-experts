# Vocabulary

Terms of art generalists flatten together or misuse; a practitioner keeps them sharply separate.

### Guarantee vs. door deal

A **guarantee** is a fixed fee paid regardless of attendance. A **door deal** (or percentage deal) pays a share of ticket revenue, usually after the venue recoups fixed costs — the band bears attendance risk instead of the venue.

**In use:** "It's a $1,050 door cut at the promoter's projected draw, but the guaranteed date pays $790 no matter what — take the guarantee unless they can show real numbers for this room."

**Common misuse:** treating a promoter's projected draw for a door deal as if it were a guaranteed number, rather than the promoter's best case.

### Split sheet

A signed document, filled out at (or immediately after) a writing session, recording each contributor's percentage ownership of a song's composition — separate from any recording or master-ownership agreement.

**In use:** "We signed the split sheet before we even demoed it — 45/35/20, no ambiguity if this one takes off."

**Common misuse:** assuming a verbal understanding at the session counts as documentation, or waiting to formalize splits until after a song succeeds, when everyone's memory of their own contribution has conveniently grown.

### Mechanical royalty vs. performance royalty

A **mechanical royalty** is paid for the reproduction of a composition (physical copies, downloads, and interactive streams pay a mechanical-equivalent rate). A **performance royalty** is paid when a composition is performed publicly — broadcast, streamed, played live, or used in a venue — and is collected by a PRO, not the mechanical-rights administrator.

**In use:** "The mechanical side comes through the MLC on streaming; the performance side is a separate check from BMI for the same song."

**Common misuse:** assuming one collection society or one royalty check covers both — they're separate rights, separate collectors, separate money.

### PRO (Performing Rights Organization)

An organization (ASCAP, BMI, SESAC in the US) that collects performance royalties on behalf of songwriters and publishers from venues, broadcasters, and streaming services, then distributes them based on reported performances.

**In use:** "Register the song with your PRO the day it's released — royalties from performances before registration are much harder to recover."

**Common misuse:** believing PRO registration alone covers mechanical royalties too, or assuming a PRO tracks every live performance automatically without the venue or artist reporting the setlist.

### Sync license

A license granting the right to synchronize a piece of music with visual media — film, TV, ads, games. Distinct from a mechanical or performance license; typically negotiated directly (or via a sync agency) and paid as a flat fee plus, often, ongoing performance royalties when the media airs.

**In use:** "The sync fee for the ad was $4,000 flat, but it'll also generate a performance royalty from BMI every time the commercial airs on broadcast TV."

**Common misuse:** conflating a sync fee with a mechanical royalty, or assuming a sync placement replaces the need for PRO registration on the underlying composition.

### Rider (technical vs. hospitality)

A **technical rider** specifies stage plot, backline, input list, and monitor requirements. A **hospitality rider** specifies food, drink, and accommodation. Venues and promoters often only read the hospitality rider closely, which is why technical needs must be stated explicitly and early.

**In use:** "Send the technical rider ten days out so the house engineer has time to source the missing DI boxes — the hospitality rider can go with the contract."

**Common misuse:** sending one combined document assuming both get equal attention, or omitting the technical rider on the assumption "they'll just figure it out" at load-in.

### Backline

Amplifiers, drum kits, and other large instrument-adjacent equipment provided by the venue or a rental company rather than carried by the touring act — a major line item in tour budgeting and rider negotiation.

**In use:** "Confirm backline in writing — 'the venue has a drum kit' means nothing until you know if it's a working 5-piece with hardware or three mismatched pieces in a closet."

**Common misuse:** assuming a venue's claimed backline is functional and complete without a specific inventory confirmed in advance.

### Scale

The minimum negotiated wage for a union (typically AFM) session or engagement, covering base pay, doubling fees (extra pay per additional instrument played), and often pension contribution — a floor, not a typical or competitive rate for in-demand players.

**In use:** "That's a scale date — fine for a first call with a new contractor, but not the rate to expect once you're the first-call player."

**Common misuse:** treating scale as a fair market rate for an established, in-demand session player rather than the contractual floor it actually is.

### 360 deal

A recording or management contract in which the label or manager takes a percentage of revenue across multiple streams — recordings, touring, merchandise, publishing — rather than just recorded-music income.

**In use:** "It's a 360 with a 20% cut of touring and merch on top of the usual recording royalty split — ask exactly what services justify the touring and merch percentages specifically."

**Common misuse:** accepting a 360 structure at an early career stage without pricing what the label/manager actually contributes to each of the additional revenue streams they're taking a cut of.

### SPL / dB(A)

**SPL** (sound pressure level) is the measured loudness of sound; **dB(A)** is the A-weighted decibel scale used for occupational noise-exposure standards because it approximates human hearing sensitivity across frequencies. NIOSH sets 85 dB(A) as the 8-hour time-weighted-average exposure limit before hearing damage risk rises materially.

**In use:** "Stage SPL at the drum riser was reading 108 dB(A) — that's a permissible exposure of about 15 minutes before hitting the daily dose, not the whole set."

**Common misuse:** treating "loud" as a subjective feeling rather than a measurable, cumulative exposure with a documented safe limit.

### Deliberate practice vs. woodshedding / noodling

**Deliberate practice** is targeted, effortful repetition of a specific identified weakness at a tempo/difficulty where it can be corrected, per Ericsson's research on expert skill acquisition. **Woodshedding** colloquially refers to focused solo practice generally, but often collapses in practice into **noodling** — playing familiar material for enjoyment or "warm feeling," which builds comfort but not new capability.

**In use:** "That was woodshedding, not deliberate practice — we ran the whole tune five times and never once isolated the bridge transition that keeps failing."

**Common misuse:** logging practice time as evidence of improvement when the content of the time was unstructured run-throughs rather than isolated work on a specific failure point.

### Tessitura vs. range

**Range** is the full span of notes a voice or instrument can physically produce. **Tessitura** is the subset of that range where the voice sits most comfortably and sustains tone quality over a full performance — the working range, not the maximum range.

**In use:** "The top note's technically in her range, but it's above her tessitura for an eight-show week — she'll shred her voice by Thursday if we don't drop the key."

**Common misuse:** picking a key or arrangement based on a singer's maximum range rather than their sustainable tessitura for the length and frequency of the engagement.

### SOVT (semi-occluded vocal tract) exercises

A category of vocal warm-up and rehabilitation technique — straw phonation, lip trills, humming through partial obstruction — that reduces vocal fold collision force while maintaining phonation, used both as a warm-up and as part of vocal health recovery protocols.

**In use:** "Straw phonation for ten minutes before soundcheck, every night of the run — it's the lowest-effort way to protect the folds across eight consecutive shows."

**Common misuse:** treating any vocal warm-up (scales, humming without occlusion) as equivalent to SOVT work, when the mechanism specifically relies on the semi-occlusion to reduce collision force.
