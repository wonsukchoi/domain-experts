# Red flags

Smell tests a shift lead or GM should catch fast, with the first question to ask and the data to pull before assuming a cause.

### Blended pour cost above 24% for a single month, or above 20% for two consecutive months

- **Usually means:** the aggregate number is hiding a category-specific problem — a costing-template error on one recipe, or genuine free-pour drift on one tier — rather than a house-wide issue.
- **First question:** "Break this down by well, call, premium, and beer/wine — where does the overage concentrate?"
- **Data to pull:** category-level theoretical-vs-actual reconciliation for the period; POS recipe-costing setup for the highest-volume item in whichever category carries the largest share of the overage.

### Till short or over by more than ~1% of net cash sales, repeated across 3+ shifts for the same bartender

- **Usually means:** either a counting/reconciliation habit problem, or comps/voids being entered without the required manager sign-off.
- **First question:** "Pull the comp/void log for those three shifts — how many entries are missing a manager initial above the threshold?"
- **Data to pull:** POS Z-reports and comp/void log for the flagged shifts; drawer count sheets; whether the variance direction (short vs. over) is consistent or random.

### One drink category carries a disproportionate share of total cost overage relative to its share of revenue

- **Usually means:** either a POS recipe-costing template that doesn't match the printed spec (common when a menu is updated but the button isn't re-costed), or a specific build that's genuinely hard to standardize (multiple modifiers, muddled ingredients not weighed).
- **First question:** "Does the POS button's costed pour size match what the recipe card actually calls for?"
- **Data to pull:** the printed recipe spec vs. the POS modifier/costing setup for the top 3 sellers in that category; unit sales volume for each, to size the dollar impact of a costing fix versus a training fix.

### Physical bottle count comes in more than one full bottle-equivalent below POS-theoretical usage for a shift

- **Usually means:** free-pour overage compounding across many drinks, an uncounted comp, or — least commonly but not to be dismissed — unrecorded consumption/theft.
- **First question:** "Was this bottle behind a spot-checked well slot, and does the comp log account for any of the gap?"
- **Data to pull:** bottle-weight spot-check result for that well slot; comp log for the shift; whether the same bottle/slot has shown variance on prior shifts with different bartenders (rules out a person-specific cause).

### A guest who was cut off returns to the bar within 20-30 minutes already visibly intoxicated, with no incident record on file

- **Usually means:** the refusal happened but wasn't documented, or the guest was served by a different bartender who wasn't informed of the cutoff.
- **First question:** "Was the cutoff logged and communicated to the rest of the bar, or handled quietly with nothing written down?"
- **Data to pull:** incident/refusal log for the night; POS timestamp of the guest's last recorded drink before the cutoff; any statements from staff who interacted with the guest afterward.

### Bottle-weight spot check shows average pour more than ~0.25oz over the 1.5oz standard, on two or more checks

- **Usually means:** genuine free-pour drift rather than a one-off inconsistent pour; often correlates with pouring heavier for regulars or high-tip tables.
- **First question:** "Is the overage consistent across all drinks that bartender pours, or concentrated on specific guests/tables?"
- **Data to pull:** POS ticket-level pour data (if tracked) cross-referenced with which server/table the drinks went to; repeat the spot check on a different, unannounced shift to rule out a one-time lapse.

### Comp/void entries cluster heavily around one bartender's regulars

- **Usually means:** comps being used informally to build tips or goodwill rather than for legitimate service recovery — sometimes fireable, sometimes just needs the sign-off threshold enforced.
- **First question:** "How many of these comps have a manager initial, and what reason code is attached to each?"
- **Data to pull:** comp log filtered by bartender and by guest, over a full month; reason codes used; whether the same guests appear repeatedly.

### Garnish or ice runs out mid-rush more than once in a month

- **Usually means:** PAR levels were set to an average night's volume instead of the slowest-restock-window volume, or prep isn't happening at the right time in the shift.
- **First question:** "What was the PAR set to, and when in the shift did prep happen versus when the rush actually hit?"
- **Data to pull:** PAR sheet for the item; covers count for the night versus the PAR's assumed volume; time-stamped 86'd-item log if the POS tracks it.

### A newly launched cocktail's ticket times or comp rate are notably higher than the rest of the menu

- **Usually means:** the recipe wasn't standardized before launch — inconsistent build steps slow the bartender down and produce inconsistent drinks that get comped when a guest complains.
- **First question:** "Has every bartender on the schedule actually been walked through this build, or did it launch off a written spec alone?"
- **Data to pull:** average ticket time for that item versus menu average; comp reason codes tied to that item specifically.
