# Vocabulary

Terms generalists conflate that a working bartender keeps sharply separate.

### Pour cost vs. liquor cost vs. beverage cost

**Pour cost** is the cost of the liquor in a specific drink or category as a percentage of that drink's/category's revenue. **Liquor cost** is the same idea applied to the full liquor program over a period (usually monthly), pulled from invoices and inventory counts. **Beverage cost** is the broader number including beer, wine, and non-alcoholic drinks, often blended into one line on a P&L.

**In use:** "Our beverage cost looks fine at 22%, but that's blending a 15% wine cost with a 28% cocktail cost — pull liquor cost alone before you tell me the bar's fine."

**Common misuse:** treating "beverage cost" and "pour cost" as interchangeable — the blended number can hide a specific category running well outside target.

### Free pour vs. jigger pour

**Free pour** is measuring liquor by counting a practiced pour speed/duration rather than a measuring tool — a skill, not a shortcut, when done to a trained standard. **Jigger pour** uses a physical measuring tool (1oz/1.5oz) for every pour, the default on a well slammed with volume.

**In use:** "Free-pour the showcase drinks where the guest is watching; jigger everything on the well tonight, we're three deep in tickets."

**Common misuse:** assuming free pour is inherently less accurate — a trained free pour checked periodically against a jigger can be as consistent as the tool; an untrained or untested free pour is the actual risk, not the method itself.

### Well vs. call vs. premium (top-shelf)

**Well** is the house's default unbranded pour when a guest orders a drink by type, not brand ("a vodka soda"). **Call** is a guest naming a specific mid-tier brand. **Premium/top-shelf** is a named high-end brand or a built craft cocktail priced and costed on its own recipe.

**In use:** "Well vodka's at $0.71/oz, call's at $1.10, premium's at $1.60 — price the tiers off those numbers, not off one blended spirit cost."

**Common misuse:** assuming premium drinks should hit the same pour-cost percentage as well drinks — premium tiers are priced for margin at a lower cost percentage because guests are paying for the name or the craft, not matching cost-plus math to the well.

### Comp vs. void vs. discount

**Comp** removes the charge for an item already made/served, usually for service recovery or hospitality, and should carry a reason code. **Void** removes an item from a ticket before or instead of it being served (a mis-ring). **Discount** reduces price on a still-charged item (happy hour, a promotion) and is a pricing decision, not a service-recovery one.

**In use:** "That's a void, not a comp — the drink never left the rail, it was rung to the wrong table."

**Common misuse:** logging a comp as a void (or vice versa) to avoid the manager sign-off threshold that applies to comps — this is exactly the pattern a till/comp audit is built to catch.

### Dram shop liability

Civil liability, recognized in some form by most US states, that puts an establishment (and sometimes an individual bartender/server) on the hook for harm caused by a patron who was served alcohol while visibly intoxicated. It attaches to the *service decision*, not to the guest's later actions alone.

**In use:** "Document the refusal the same night — specific behaviors, time, what was offered — because if this turns into a dram shop claim, that log is the first thing anyone asks for."

**Common misuse:** treating cutoff decisions as a purely hospitality/etiquette question rather than a liability decision with a documentation requirement; also assuming the rule is identical everywhere — state law varies and some states limit or don't recognize it.

### Visible intoxication vs. BAC

**Visible intoxication** is the observable behavioral standard (speech, coordination, mood, reaction time) that responsible-service training and dram shop law actually turn on. **BAC (blood alcohol concentration)** is a lab/breathalyzer number a bartender has no way to measure or reliably estimate in real time.

**In use:** "I'm not guessing his BAC — he's slurring and he dropped his card twice, that's enough to slow him down."

**Common misuse:** trying to mentally estimate a guest's BAC from drink count and body weight instead of watching for the actual behavioral cues the standard is built around; drink count is a weak proxy for both tolerance and food intake.

### Back bar vs. speed rail

**Back bar** is the display/storage area behind the bartender holding full stock, including slower-moving premium bottles. **Speed rail** is the small, immediately reachable row of well and high-velocity call bottles positioned for the fastest possible pour during a rush.

**In use:** "Restock the speed rail every time you grab from the back bar — don't wait until it runs dry mid-rush."

**Common misuse:** treating the speed rail as just "the front row" rather than a deliberately curated, high-velocity subset restocked continuously — a poorly maintained speed rail is a common hidden cause of slow ticket times during a rush.

### PAR level

The quantity of an item (ice, garnish, a well spirit) a bar stocks to survive to the next scheduled restock without running out, set against the *slowest* restock window of a shift, not the average.

**In use:** "Ice PAR is 180 lbs because the barback can't restock again until after last call — that's the number that matters, not what we use on an average Tuesday."

**Common misuse:** setting PAR to average nightly usage rather than to the worst-case gap between restocks, which is exactly when a shortage actually happens.

### Bottle yield

The number of standard pours a given bottle size produces before waste/spillage factor — a 750ml bottle (25.4oz) yields roughly 16 pours at a clean 1.5oz standard, fewer once spillage and free-pour variance are accounted for.

**In use:** "Theoretical yield on this bottle is 16 pours; we only rang 13 — before assuming a full bottle's worth of theft, check whether it was behind a slot that got spot-checked for overpour."

**Common misuse:** treating theoretical yield as an exact, achievable number every time rather than a target that real-world spillage and pour variance always shave a bit off.

### Last call

The house's final call for alcohol orders before service stops for the night, timed to state or local closing law, not a soft suggestion.

**In use:** "Last call was five minutes ago — I can get you water or close out your tab, but nothing else is going through."

**Common misuse:** treating last call as negotiable for a good tipper or a regular; the timing is a legal cutoff tied to the establishment's license, not a service-discretion moment.

### 86 (a guest or an item)

To refuse further service to a specific guest ("86 the guy in the blue jacket") or to mark an item unavailable ("86 the well gin"). For guests, it's the operational term for a cutoff that's meant to be communicated across the whole bar/floor, not just remembered by one bartender.

**In use:** "He's 86'd for the night — tell the door and the other bartenders, don't let him get served somewhere else in the building."

**Common misuse:** using it only in the moment of refusal without communicating it to the rest of the staff, which is exactly how a cut-off guest gets re-served by someone who wasn't told.
