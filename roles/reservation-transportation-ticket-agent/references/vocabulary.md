# Reservation and Transportation Ticket Agent — Vocabulary

### Fare basis (code)
The alphanumeric code on a ticket identifying exactly which fare bucket, cabin, and rule set the ticket was sold under.
**In use:** "Pull the fare basis before you quote a change fee — the route doesn't tell you the rule, the code does."
**Common misuse:** Assuming all economy tickets on the same flight share one change-fee rule; fare basis varies by booking class, not cabin.

### Oversell / oversold flight
A flight where more confirmed reservations exist than seats available, typically planned around expected no-shows.
**In use:** "We're oversold by four — start the volunteer solicitation before we get anywhere near the gate."
**Common misuse:** Treating any full flight as "oversold" — a flight can be completely full without being oversold if confirmed passengers exactly match seats.

### Involuntary denied boarding (IDB)
A passenger bumped from an oversold flight against their will, after voluntary solicitation failed to fill the gap, triggering statutory compensation.
**In use:** "We ran the volunteer auction and still needed one seat — this is now an IDB case, not a service gesture."
**Common misuse:** Calling any bumped passenger's situation "IDB" even when they volunteered — voluntary bumps are a negotiated exchange, not a statutory compensation event.

### Voluntary denied boarding (VDB)
A passenger who accepts a carrier's offer (cash, credit, rebooking) to give up a confirmed seat on an oversold flight.
**In use:** "Two VDBs at $600 got us most of the way there before we needed an IDB for the last seat."
**Common misuse:** Assuming VDB compensation is capped by the same statutory formula as IDB — VDB compensation is whatever the carrier offers and the passenger accepts, unregulated by the DOT cap.

### IROP (irregular operations)
An airline-industry term for disruptions to planned operations — cancellations, major delays, diversions — requiring passenger rebooking outside the original plan.
**In use:** "This is a weather IROP, so the fare-class waiver applies automatically per policy code WX-1."
**Common misuse:** Using "IROP" loosely for any schedule change, including minor gate changes or routine delays under the carrier's normal on-time threshold.

### Boarding priority (list)
The published, rule-based order in which passengers are protected from involuntary denial, typically by fare class, status tier, and check-in time.
**In use:** "Check the boarding-priority list before selecting anyone for involuntary denial — don't pick based on who's easiest to deal with."
**Common misuse:** Treating boarding priority as agent discretion rather than a fixed, auditable, published order.

### Change fee vs. fare difference
The change fee is a flat penalty for modifying a ticket; the fare difference is the separate amount owed (or refunded) if the new flight's fare differs from the original.
**In use:** "The change fee is $75, but there's also a $120 fare difference since the new flight is a higher bucket — total due is $195."
**Common misuse:** Quoting only the change fee and omitting the fare difference, understating the total cost to the passenger.

### Waiver (fare-rule waiver)
A carrier-authorized exception that suspends a normally applicable fee or restriction, tied to a specific disruption code or policy.
**In use:** "Policy code WX-1 authorizes a same-cabin waiver for this cancellation — no change fee applies."
**Common misuse:** Applying a waiver based on passenger explanation alone, without confirming the disruption code that authorizes it actually exists on the flight.

### GDS (Global Distribution System)
The reservation/inventory platform travel providers and agents use to search, book, and modify itineraries in real time.
**In use:** "Re-run the GDS search now — don't trust a quote from ten minutes ago."
**Common misuse:** Treating a GDS search result as a locked quote rather than a live, moment-in-time snapshot of inventory.

### Standby
A passenger not holding a confirmed seat on a specific flight, waitlisted for any seat that becomes available, ranked by a priority rule.
**In use:** "She's on standby for the 6:40, ranked by her original check-in time, not when she asked to be added to the list."
**Common misuse:** Ranking standby passengers by when they requested standby status rather than the carrier's actual priority formula (often check-in time and fare/status tier).

### Involuntary vs. voluntary change (disruption framing)
An involuntary change is carrier-caused (cancellation, schedule change, oversell); a voluntary change is passenger-initiated for personal reasons.
**In use:** "This is a voluntary change — she just wants an earlier flight, there's no disruption code on this route today."
**Common misuse:** Accepting a passenger's self-description of "involuntary" without checking whether an actual disruption code exists on the flight.

### One-way fare (as compensation basis)
The base fare used to calculate denied-boarding compensation — specifically the one-way fare to the passenger's next stopover or final destination, not the full round-trip price.
**In use:** "Her ticket was round-trip, but compensation is based on the one-way fare to her destination, not half the round-trip total — those aren't always the same number."
**Common misuse:** Dividing a round-trip fare by two and assuming that equals the one-way fare used for compensation; carriers often price one-way and round-trip fares independently.
