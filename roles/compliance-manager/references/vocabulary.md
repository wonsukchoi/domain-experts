# Compliance manager working vocabulary

Resourcing and staffing-model terms a compliance manager uses precisely and a generalist blurs together. Format: definition → how a practitioner says it → common misuse.

**Coverage ratio** — The share of an in-scope population (accounts, transactions, business units) actually tested or monitored in a period, expressed as a percentage against a stated denominator.
In use: "We're at 61% coverage on the top-risk-tier segment this quarter, up from 48% — that's the number that matters, not the raw review count."
Misuse: reporting a raw count of reviews completed without the denominator, which makes progress or regression impossible to judge from one period to the next.

**Span of control (analyst-to-population ratio)** — The number of accounts, relationships, or cases a single analyst can cover without alerts aging past SLA, typically named as a ratio (e.g., 1:200).
In use: "That unit's at 1:340 — well past our 1:200 target, which is why the backlog is growing, not because the analysts are underperforming."
Misuse: treating "the team feels stretched" as sufficient justification for a headcount ask instead of citing the actual ratio against the target.

**Unfunded mandate** — A new regulatory obligation that creates a compliance workload without an automatic corresponding budget or headcount increase; the program owner has to originate the resourcing ask.
In use: "The new state law is an unfunded mandate until we cost the volume increase in headcount-hours and bring that number to the CFO."
Misuse: describing a new regulation only in terms of legal risk without ever translating it into a specific dollar or headcount ask, which is why it doesn't get funded.

**Build vs. buy (compliance tech)** — The decision between developing monitoring/case-management technology in-house versus licensing a vendor platform, compared on total cost of ownership including the headcount needed to work the tool's output.
In use: "Buy wins here on both cost and time-to-coverage — build only pays back if we have an 18-month runway, and this deadline is eight weeks out."
Misuse: comparing licensing cost alone against build cost without including the paired analyst headcount on both sides, which understates the buy path's true cost and overstates its savings.

**Centralized vs. embedded compliance model** — Centralized keeps all compliance staff in one reporting line serving every business unit; embedded places analysts inside business units for context/speed while (properly) keeping compensation and review authority with compliance.
In use: "We're embedding two analysts in consumer lending given its risk score, but their reviews and comp stay solid-line to me, not the business unit head."
Misuse: embedding staff for speed without documenting who controls their compensation and performance review — the model choice is fine, the undocumented authority gap is the failure.

**Dotted-line vs. solid-line reporting** — Solid-line is the reporting relationship that controls compensation, performance review, and hiring/firing authority; dotted-line is an informational or advisory relationship with no such authority.
In use: "The business unit gets a dotted-line voice in this analyst's review, but the solid-line sign-off stays with compliance."
Misuse: treating any reporting relationship named on an org chart as equivalent, when only the solid-line relationship actually controls incentives.

**Alert aging** — The elapsed time between an alert or case being generated and its resolution or escalation, tracked as a distribution rather than an average, since averages hide a long tail of stale cases.
In use: "The average age looks fine at 6 days, but the 90th percentile is 42 days — that tail is where the regulatory exposure sits."
Misuse: reporting only the average alert age, which can look acceptable while a meaningful share of cases sit well past SLA.

**Risk-tiered allocation** — Distributing finite compliance headcount/budget proportional to a business line's risk score (likelihood × impact) rather than evenly or by headcount-per-unit.
In use: "Consumer lending scores highest on our risk matrix, so it gets the analyst reallocation before deposits does, even though deposits has more accounts."
Misuse: allocating headcount by account count or business-unit size alone, which under-resources a smaller but higher-risk unit.

**Regretted attrition** — Departures of employees the organization would have preferred to retain, as distinct from attrition of underperformers, tracked because it signals workload or compensation problems rather than performance management working as intended.
In use: "Regretted attrition in case-handling hit 24% this year — that's a caseload and comp problem, not a talent problem."
Misuse: reporting total attrition without separating regretted from non-regretted departures, which masks a workload-driven exodus as routine turnover.

**Total cost of ownership (TCO)** — The full cost of a technology or staffing decision over its useful life, including implementation, licensing/maintenance, and the headcount required to operate it — not just the sticker price.
In use: "The platform's license is cheap, but TCO including the exception-review analyst is what we compare against building it ourselves."
Misuse: comparing only upfront or license cost between options, ignoring the ongoing headcount or maintenance cost that determines which option is actually cheaper.

**Benchmark rate (industry)** — A comparison figure (headcount ratio, request volume, hotline report rate) drawn from named peer or industry survey data, used to argue a resourcing ask is in line with (or behind) comparable organizations.
In use: "We modeled the volume increase at the midpoint of the IAPP peer benchmark range, not a guess."
Misuse: presenting an internal estimate as if it were externally benchmarked, which weakens the credibility of the ask the moment someone asks for the source.
