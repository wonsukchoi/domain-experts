# Security officer working vocabulary

Terms a guard uses precisely and generalists misuse.

### Shopkeeper's privilege
A statutory or common-law defense letting a merchant or their agent briefly detain a suspected shoplifter without it being false imprisonment, conditioned on reasonable grounds for suspicion, reasonable manner, and reasonable time (commonly bounded around 20–30 minutes before police involvement is required).
**In use:** "We had reasonable grounds and a clean observation chain, but we blew the reasonable-time prong holding her forty minutes before PD showed — that's what the plaintiff's attorney is going to hang the claim on."
**Common misuse:** treating it as a general license to detain anyone suspected of theft for as long as needed, or assuming it covers a physical search — it doesn't cover either without meeting all three prongs.

### Continuous observation
The requirement, baked into most shopkeeper's-privilege case law and retail LP policy, that the same person (or a coordinated team with no gap) watch a suspect uninterrupted from the moment of concealment to the point of exit.
**In use:** "I lost her behind the end-cap for fifteen seconds — chain's broken, we're not stopping her, just documenting."
**Common misuse:** assuming that seeing concealment and later seeing the person outside with the item is enough — a gap anywhere in between defeats the privilege even if the ending looks identical.

### Citizen's arrest
A private person's limited statutory or common-law authority to detain someone for a crime committed in their presence (and in some states, certain felonies not in their presence) — narrower than a peace officer's arrest power and carrying personal liability if wrong.
**In use:** "We can't cite 'probable cause' like the report says — we don't have arrest power, we made a citizen's arrest on a misdemeanor committed in our presence, and the elements are different."
**Common misuse:** using "arrest" and "detention" interchangeably, or believing the guard has the same latitude as a police officer to act on secondhand information.

### Reasonable force (civilian standard)
The tort-law standard governing a private guard's use of force: proportionate to an actual, imminent threat to self or a third party (and, more narrowly and by state, to property), judged by what a similarly situated person would do with the information available at the time.
**In use:** "Value of the merchandise doesn't factor into the force analysis at all — the only question is whether he posed an imminent threat when I made contact."
**Common misuse:** applying a police-style force-continuum framework, or assuming a higher-value theft justifies more force than a lower-value one — the standard is threat-based, not value-based.

### Post orders
The site-specific written document defining a guard's duties, authority, and required responses at a given post — the primary evidence, after the fact, of what a guard was actually authorized to do.
**In use:** "Before I touch this, what do the post orders actually say about unattended packages at this entrance?"
**Common misuse:** treating post orders as a suggestion to be improved on with initiative — deviating from them removes the guard's own liability protection even when the deviation turns out fine.

### Fire watch
A temporary, human-patrol substitute for an impaired fire detection or suppression system, patrolling affected areas at an interval set by the authority having jurisdiction (commonly every 30 minutes) until the system is restored.
**In use:** "Sprinkler's been down since noon — we're on a 30-minute fire watch of the warehouse until the contractor signs off."
**Common misuse:** treating fire watch as optional or reducible to "checking in periodically" — the interval is set by the impairment permit, not by guard discretion, and an unlogged patrol is treated the same as a missed one.

### Type II workplace violence
The OSHA/NIOSH classification for violence where the perpetrator is a customer, client, or patient of the workplace (as opposed to Type I, a criminal with no legitimate business there; Type III, worker-on-worker; Type IV, a personal-relationship assailant).
**In use:** "This is a Type II event — patient in crisis, not an intruder — so we're running the de-escalation protocol, not the active-threat response."
**Common misuse:** applying the same physical-intervention playbook to every violent incident regardless of type — a Type II response in a healthcare setting is built around de-escalation first because the person is usually a patient, not a criminal actor.

### CPTED (Crime Prevention Through Environmental Design)
A design approach using natural surveillance, access control, and territorial reinforcement (lighting, sightlines, landscaping, defined boundaries) to reduce opportunity for crime before it reaches the guard.
**In use:** "Half our loitering complaints in that corner would go away with CPTED fixes — trim the hedge line so there's sightline from the booth, not more patrols."
**Common misuse:** treating CPTED as an architect's problem unrelated to guard work — a guard's post-order recommendations on lighting and sightlines are exactly where CPTED gets applied in practice.

### Deter-detect-delay-respond
The layered physical-security model (used in NFPA 730 and broadly across the industry) describing the sequence a security program is built to execute: deterrence reduces attempts, detection catches what isn't deterred, delay buys time, response resolves it.
**In use:** "Adding cameras alone doesn't fix this — cameras are detection, and we still have no delay layer between the parking lot and the server room door."
**Common misuse:** treating any single layer (usually cameras) as a complete security posture — each layer only works because the others cover its gap.

### Duty of care / negligent security
A property owner's (and by extension a contracted guard company's) legal obligation to take reasonable steps to protect people lawfully on the property from foreseeable harm — the basis for a negligent-security lawsuit when a preventable incident occurs.
**In use:** "Three prior assaults in that stairwell in six months is exactly the foreseeability a negligent-security claim is built on if we don't add lighting or patrol coverage there now."
**Common misuse:** assuming liability only attaches to what the guard personally did wrong — negligent security claims often target staffing levels, lighting, and training decisions made well above the guard's own actions.

### Chain of custody
The documented, unbroken record of who held a piece of evidence (footage, a recovered item, a written statement) from the moment it was collected to whenever it's produced later.
**In use:** "I logged the recovered merchandise into the evidence locker at 15:10 and had the manager countersign — chain of custody's intact if this ever goes to court."
**Common misuse:** treating chain of custody as only a police concern — a guard's own evidence handling (footage exports, recovered property) is scrutinized the same way if a case proceeds.

### Tailgating (piggybacking)
An unauthorized person following an authorized person through an access-controlled door without presenting their own credential.
**In use:** "That's a tailgate alert, not a forced-door alert — two people, one badge swipe — check who the second person was before assuming anything."
**Common misuse:** conflating tailgating with a forced or propped door — the access-control system logs them differently and they call for different responses.

### Vicarious liability
The legal principle that holds an employer (the guard company, or the client if the guard is proprietary) liable for an employee's actions taken within the scope of employment.
**In use:** "The company gets named in this suit too — I was on shift, in uniform, following what I believed were the post orders, which is squarely within scope."
**Common misuse:** assuming liability stops at the individual guard — in nearly every civil claim involving a guard, the employer is named alongside them.
