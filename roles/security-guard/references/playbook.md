# Field playbook — detention, access-control, and fire-watch sequences

Executed process, not description. Three parts: (1) shoplifting/trespass detention sequence, (2) access-control anomaly response, (3) fire-watch and incident-report structure.

## Detention sequence (shoplifting or trespass)

1. **Confirm the observation chain before moving.** For a merchandise-concealment stop: did you personally see the subject approach, select, conceal, and fail to pay, with no break in observation between concealment and the last point of sale? Any break — camera blind spot, subject leaving your sightline, a shift handoff mid-observation — means no physical detention; fall back to a receipt check or a documentation-only report.
2. **Approach only past the last point of sale (or the property line, for trespass).** Detaining before that point (in the aisle, before checkout) removes the shopkeeper's-privilege defense entirely — at that stage there's no evidence of intent not to pay.
3. **State the basis and the request plainly, in public view when possible.** "I'm store security, I need you to come back inside with me" — not an accusation of a crime, not a physical grab. Witnesses and cameras covering the approach protect both parties' accounts later.
4. **Start the clock the moment contact begins.** Note the time in your head or radio it to control. Most jurisdictions' shopkeeper-privilege case law treats detentions that stretch past 20–30 minutes without police en route as sliding toward false imprisonment — the clock is not a formality.
5. **Never search the person or their bag without consent.** A refusal to consent to a search is not evidence of guilt and is not grounds for force; wait for police if a search is needed.
6. **If the subject attempts to leave, do not use force to stop them unless they pose an imminent threat of harm.** Losing recovery of the merchandise is a business cost; using force to enforce a property claim exceeds the civilian reasonable-force standard in most states.
7. **Release or hand off to police at the threshold in the post order** — most sites require calling police immediately upon confirming a detention, not waiting to see if the subject "cooperates."

## Access-control anomaly response

1. **Pull the specific post order for that door/credential before reacting.** A denied badge, a forced-door alarm, or a tailgating alert is a prompt to check one hypothesis (expected contractor, recently terminated employee, known maintenance access) against what the post order says should be true there.
2. **Classify the anomaly before dispatching a response**: benign/expected (delivery, contractor with escort), configuration gap (badge not yet deactivated, wrong door group assigned), or genuine anomaly (no matching explanation).
3. **For a genuine anomaly, respond to the location without assuming intent** — position for observation first (who is there, what are they doing) before any verbal contact.
4. **Log every anomaly, even resolved ones**, with the specific door, time, and resolution — a door propped open twice in one week by "unknown" is a pattern only visible if every instance is logged, not just the one that got escalated.
5. **Escalate to police only when the post order's specific criteria are met** (forced entry, no plausible explanation, subject flees on approach) — not on the guard's personal read of how suspicious it seemed.

## Fire watch (system impairment)

1. **Confirm the impairment and the AHJ-set patrol interval** — commonly every 30 minutes, but always verify against the specific notice or permit for that impairment; intervals below 30 minutes are common for high-occupancy or life-safety-critical areas.
2. **Patrol every affected area on the set interval without exception**, checking for smoke, fire, blocked exits, and any change in the impairment status.
3. **Log each patrol with time and area covered**, on paper or an electronic tour system — an unlogged fire watch is functionally the same as no fire watch if it's ever reviewed.
4. **Carry a means to notify the fire department directly** (radio, phone) — a fire watch exists specifically because the automatic system can't, so the human link can't have a gap either.
5. **Do not stand down until the impairment is formally cleared** by whoever restores the system, documented with a time and a name.

## Incident report structure

Four blocks, in this order, every time:

1. **Setup** — what post order or assignment you were working, what you observed and when, before any contact — establishes what you knew and when you knew it.
2. **Chronological narrative** — first person, timestamped, one sentence per discrete fact. Pair every characterization with the specific act: not "the subject became aggressive" but "the subject stepped toward me and raised his voice, stating [quote]."
3. **Action taken** — what you did (approached, called police, declined to detain, applied force), and the specific fact that authorized it, referencing the six-step chain, an imminent threat, or the applicable post order.
4. **Disposition** — police response (if any), subject's identity if obtained, evidence retained (footage, photos) and where it's stored, and any follow-up owed (report to client, to insurance, to a supervisor).

### Fact vs. conclusion — before/after examples

| Conclusion (unusable alone) | Specific fact (usable) |
|---|---|
| "Subject was uncooperative." | "Subject was asked three times to step aside and did not comply, continuing to walk toward the exit." |
| "Subject seemed intoxicated." | "Subject's speech was slurred, subject swayed while standing, and I detected an odor of an alcoholic beverage from approximately 3 feet away." |
| "The door was left insecure." | "Door 4B was found propped open with a trash can at 22:14; no personnel were present in the loading area at the time." |
| "I felt threatened." | "Subject raised a closed fist to shoulder height and stepped to within 2 feet of me while stating [quote]." |

## Escalation ladder (when multiple posts or alerts compete)

1. Active threat to a person (weapon displayed, violence in progress, medical emergency).
2. Fire alarm or life-safety system activation.
3. Access-control breach in progress (forced door, tailgating alarm with no explanation).
4. In-progress property crime with a subject still on-site.
5. Resolved/cold incident (property already gone, subject already left).
6. Routine administrative check (parking, low-battery alarm, scheduled patrol).

Update the priority read the moment new information arrives — a call coded routine that turns out to involve an injured person jumps to the top immediately.
