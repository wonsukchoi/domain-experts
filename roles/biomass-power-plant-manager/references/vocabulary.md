# Biomass power generation working vocabulary

Terms a plant manager uses precisely. Format: definition → how a practitioner says it → common misuse.

**As-received HHV (higher heating value)** — The calorific value of a fuel measured including its actual moisture content, as opposed to dry-basis HHV which excludes moisture — the correct basis for calculating how much fuel volume is needed for a given energy requirement.
In use: "As-received HHV on this batch is 7,200 Btu/lb at 22% moisture — the dry-basis number doesn't tell us how many tons we actually need to burn."
Misuse: sizing fuel volume or comparing fuel sources using dry-basis HHV, which overstates the usable energy content of a wetter fuel relative to a drier one.

**Ash content (% by weight)** — The proportion of a fuel's mass that remains as non-combustible residue after burning, a direct driver of ash-handling system load and, at high levels, slagging/fouling risk.
In use: "This residue runs 8% ash versus 2% for our normal wood chips — burning it at full substitution volume would triple our daily ash tonnage."
Misuse: evaluating a fuel purely on calorific value or price without checking ash content against the ash-handling system's actual capacity — a cheap, energy-dense fuel with high ash content can still be operationally infeasible at scale.

**Slagging and fouling** — Slagging is the formation of molten or semi-molten ash deposits on furnace surfaces; fouling is the buildup of ash/particulate deposits on heat-exchange surfaces further downstream — both degrade efficiency and can force an outage if severe.
In use: "We're seeing early fouling on the economizer tubes — likely linked to the higher-alkali fuel blend we ran last week."
Misuse: treating slagging/fouling as a generic "dirty fuel" problem rather than tracing it to the specific ash chemistry (alkali/silica content) of the fuel blend that caused it.

**Transport-cost radius** — The geographic distance within which a low-energy-density fuel like biomass remains economical to transport relative to its delivered energy value, a hard constraint on viable fuel sourcing distinct from the origin price alone.
In use: "That supplier's price looks good, but at 150 miles they're outside our economical radius once transport cost per MMBtu is factored in."
Misuse: comparing fuel sources on origin price per ton without normalizing for both calorific value and transport distance to a delivered cost-per-unit-energy basis.

**Fuel portfolio diversification** — Sourcing biomass fuel from multiple suppliers and/or fuel types (within what the combustion system can handle) specifically to reduce the risk of a single source's seasonal unavailability or price spike disrupting plant operation.
In use: "We qualified a second wood-chip supplier and added agricultural residue as a blend option specifically so no single source exceeds 50% of volume."
Misuse: treating "we buy from several vendors" as diversification if those vendors all draw from the same underlying regional supply and would all be affected by the same seasonal shortfall.

**Moisture-derated capacity** — The plant's achievable output when fuel moisture content is higher than the design/normal spec, since wetter fuel carries less usable energy per unit weight and often burns less efficiently.
In use: "At this batch's moisture level, we're moisture-derated to about 91% of nameplate output without over-firing the boiler."
Misuse: assuming nameplate capacity is always achievable regardless of incoming fuel moisture — actual achievable output is fuel-dependent, not a fixed plant constant.

**Spontaneous combustion risk** — The risk that stored biomass fuel self-heats through biological/chemical decomposition to the point of igniting, a real fire hazard distinct from external ignition sources, that increases with storage duration, pile size, and moisture content.
In use: "Pile 3's internal temperature is trending up faster than normal for its age — pulling fuel from it first before it becomes a spontaneous-combustion risk."
Misuse: treating biomass storage as passively stable like coal or a non-biological fuel; biomass actively decomposes in storage in a way that requires active temperature monitoring, not just periodic visual inspection.

**Seasonal availability curve** — The predictable pattern of higher and lower fuel supply volume across the year for a given biomass source (e.g., agricultural residue availability spiking post-harvest, forestry residue tied to logging season), used to plan procurement and storage ahead of known low-availability periods.
In use: "Straw availability drops sharply from March through June — we build inventory in the fall specifically to cover that window."
Misuse: planning procurement as if fuel availability were roughly uniform year-round, then treating a predictable seasonal dip as an unexpected supply shock.

**Fuel blending ratio** — The proportion of two or more fuel types combined in a single burn, used to manage the combined moisture, ash, and calorific characteristics within what the combustion and ash-handling systems can process.
In use: "We're running a 62/38 blend of wood chips to agricultural residue — that keeps combined ash content under the level that overloads the handling system."
Misuse: describing a blend only by cost or volume ratio without checking that the resulting combined ash/moisture/calorific profile actually stays within combustion and ash-system limits.

**Combustion parameter adjustment (air/fuel ratio, residence time)** — Operating settings (excess air, fuel feed rate, residence time in the combustion zone) tuned to the actual fuel batch's moisture and calorific characteristics, as opposed to a single fixed operating recipe.
In use: "Moisture came in higher than the last batch, so we increased excess air and residence time before firing at full rate."
Misuse: running the same combustion settings across fuel batches with materially different moisture or calorific characteristics, then troubleshooting the resulting inefficiency or emissions issue as an equipment problem rather than a parameter-matching one.
