# Vocabulary

### Denier
A unit of linear mass density for fiber, defined as grams per 9,000 meters of fiber length, calculated from the ratio of mass throughput rate to take-up speed.
**In use:** "Denier's calculated at (throughput × 9,000) ÷ take-up speed — that's what determines the actual number, not the hole size alone."
**Common misuse:** Assuming denier is fixed by spinneret hole size alone, without accounting for how take-up speed at a given throughput rate determines the actual resulting denier.

### Draw ratio
The ratio of fiber speed or length after a drawing/stretching step to its speed or length before, which controls molecular orientation and resulting mechanical properties like tenacity and elongation.
**In use:** "Draw ratio's at the top of this polymer's safe range — pushing it further risks breakage, not more strength."
**Common misuse:** Assuming higher draw ratio always improves fiber strength without limit, ignoring the breakage risk threshold specific to the polymer and process.

### Take-up speed
The winding speed at which spun fiber is collected, which combined with polymer throughput rate determines the resulting fiber denier.
**In use:** "Take-up speed needs recalculating for this throughput — reusing the last product's setting will miss the target denier."
**Common misuse:** Setting take-up speed based on a prior product's parameters without recalculating for the current actual throughput rate.

### Spinneret
A plate with precisely sized holes through which molten or dissolved polymer is extruded to form individual fiber filaments.
**In use:** "Check hole 14 on the spinneret — that's the position where the weak filaments keep showing up."
**Common misuse:** Assuming all spinneret holes in a multi-hole spinneret perform identically over time, without checking for individual hole degradation that can produce a localized weak filament within an otherwise acceptable aggregate yarn.

### Tenacity (fiber)
A measure of fiber strength per unit linear density, a key mechanical property target influenced primarily by draw ratio and molecular orientation.
**In use:** "Tenacity's below spec — check draw ratio and orientation before assuming it's a polymer batch issue."
**Common misuse:** Assuming tenacity is determined solely by polymer type, rather than recognizing it as strongly dependent on the actual draw ratio and orientation achieved during processing.

### Quench (fiber spinning)
The cooling process immediately after polymer extrusion from the spinneret, which affects crystallization behavior and the polymer structure available for subsequent drawing.
**In use:** "Quench conditions shifted — expect different final properties even though draw ratio hasn't changed."
**Common misuse:** Assuming draw ratio alone determines final fiber properties, without recognizing that quench conditions independently affect the starting structure that drawing then orients.

### Throughput rate (polymer, spinning)
The mass flow rate of polymer through a spinneret position, a key input, along with take-up speed, to the denier calculation.
**In use:** "Throughput's up to 50 g/min from the spinneret reconfiguration — that changes the take-up speed math."
**Common misuse:** Treating throughput rate as a fixed constant across different production runs or spinneret configurations, rather than verifying it explicitly when calculating required take-up speed for a target denier.

### Multi-filament yarn
A yarn composed of many individual continuous filaments spun simultaneously, whose aggregate properties, like average denier, can mask individual filament defects.
**In use:** "Aggregate denier's fine, but check individual filaments — one weak thread doesn't show up in the average."
**Common misuse:** Relying on aggregate yarn-level measurements alone to verify quality, missing individual filament irregularities that only manifest as localized weak points or downstream processing defects.

### Molecular orientation (fiber)
The degree to which a polymer's molecular chains are aligned along the fiber axis, achieved through drawing, which directly determines mechanical properties.
**In use:** "Same polymer, but different draw ratio means different orientation — and that's what's actually driving the strength difference."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a fiber's mechanical properties are fixed by its polymer chemistry alone, without recognizing that processing, draw ratio specifically, determines the actual molecular orientation and resulting properties.

### Godet
A rotating roller used to control fiber speed and tension at specific points in a spinning/drawing line, often used in pairs to establish a specific draw ratio between them.
**In use:** "Draw ratio's set by the speed ratio between godet 1 and godet 2, not either roller's speed alone."
**Common misuse:** Assuming godet speed settings are independent of each other, rather than recognizing that the ratio between consecutive godet speeds is what actually defines the draw ratio applied to the fiber.
