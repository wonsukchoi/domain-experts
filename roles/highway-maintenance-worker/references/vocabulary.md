### Taper
The tapered sequence of channelizing devices that shifts traffic out of a closed lane, sized by the MUTCD formula for posted speed (L = WS or WS²/60).
**In use:** "Posted's 55, so it's a 660-foot merging taper — twelve foot lane, formula's WS."
**Common misuse:** Treated as "however many cones we usually put out" instead of a speed-driven calculation; a taper undersized for the posted speed doesn't give approaching drivers enough distance to react and merge.

### Buffer space
The clear distance between the end of the taper and the start of the actual work space, sized separately from the taper by a speed-based table (MUTCD Table 6C-2).
**In use:** "Taper's set, but we're short on buffer — pull the work truck back another twenty feet before anyone gets out."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to be included in the taper length calculation or treated as a flexible "few extra cones," when it's a distinct required distance that absorbs a driver's stopping error, not the merge itself.

### Positive protection
Physical barrier (concrete barrier, truck-mounted attenuator) placed between the work space and traffic, as distinct from channelizing devices, which only mark the boundary but don't stop a vehicle.
**In use:** "We've got a TMA staged behind the crew — that's positive protection, the cones are just the boundary."
**Common misuse:** Confusing cones/drums (visual channelization) with positive protection (physical stopping capability) — a taper of cones does not protect a crew from an intrusion the way a barrier or attenuator does.

### Cold mix / cold patch
Bagged or bulk premixed asphalt that requires no heating and is workable down to near-freezing conditions, intended as a temporary repair.
**In use:** "Pavement's at 36 and holding — cold mix today, flag it for hot mix on the next dry day above 50."
**Common misuse:** Left in place indefinitely as if it were a permanent repair, when it's selected specifically because conditions ruled out hot mix, not because it performs as well long-term.

### Hot-mix asphalt (HMA)
Asphalt heated and mixed at a plant, requiring roughly 40°F-plus ambient temperature and prompt compaction to bond and perform as a durable repair.
**In use:** "We're clear above 40 and dry through the shift — this one gets hot mix, we're not coming back to it."
**Common misuse:** Ordered on a marginal-temperature day "because it's the better material," when the temperature window has already ruled it out and the result is a hot-mix patch that fails as fast as cold mix would have, at higher cost.

### Tack coat
A thin asphalt emulsion applied to the vertical faces and base of a patch cavity before placing new material, so the new patch bonds to the existing pavement rather than sitting as a loose plug.
**In use:** "Cavity's clean and dry — tack the faces before we dump the mix, or it'll pop right back out."
**Common misuse:** Skipped on "quick" patches to save time, which is a leading cause of a patch popping loose within weeks regardless of which mix was used.

### Dynamic deflection
The distance a guardrail system is tested (under MASH criteria) to flex laterally when struck, before or without contacting whatever's behind it.
**In use:** "Back of rail to the culvert headwall is only three feet — that's inside this system's rated deflection, we need the stiffened section, not a straight reinstall."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to not matter if the repaired section "looks the same as before," when the relevant number is the rated deflection versus the actual offset to whatever's behind the rail, not visual similarity to the prior installation.

### Clear zone
The roadside width, measured from the edge of the traveled way, that should be free of fixed obstacles or traversable enough for an errant vehicle to recover, sized by AASHTO's Roadside Design Guide based on speed, traffic volume, and roadside slope.
**In use:** "That new sign post is two feet inside the clear zone for this speed — it needs to be behind the guardrail run, not in front of it."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a fixed universal number ("30 feet, always") instead of a value that varies by design speed, ADT, and slope — a clear zone appropriate for a 70 mph highway is far wider than one for a 35 mph local road.

### MASH (Manual for Assessing Safety Hardware)
The current AASHTO crash-test standard that roadside safety hardware (guardrail, end terminals, barriers) must be rated against; supersedes the older NCHRP Report 350 standard.
**In use:** "That terminal's a legacy NCHRP-350 unit — it's not rated to MASH, it needs to come out on the next upgrade pass."
**Common misuse:** Assuming any guardrail hardware that "looks standard" is current-rated, when older installed hardware may predate MASH and not carry its performance rating.

### Pavement temperature
The temperature of the road surface itself, read from a sensor or infrared gun, which governs deicing chemical effectiveness and asphalt workability — and which can differ substantially from air temperature.
**In use:** "Air's reading 28, but the bridge deck sensor's at 19 — that's a different chemical rate than the rest of the route."
**Common misuse:** Substituting the truck cab's air-temperature reading for an actual pavement reading, missing colder microclimates (bridge decks, shaded curves) that need a different chemical or rate.

### Retroreflectivity
The measured brightness of a sign's sheeting when illuminated by headlights at night, which degrades over the sheeting's service life independent of visible daytime condition.
**In use:** "It still looks fine in daylight, but it's past its rated sheeting life — pull a retroreflectometer reading before we decide it's fine."
**Common misuse:** Judged by daytime visual appearance alone; a sign can look clean and legible in daylight while its nighttime retroreflectivity has already dropped below the usable threshold.

### Anti-icing vs. deicing
Anti-icing applies chemical (typically brine) before precipitation to prevent snow/ice from bonding to the pavement; deicing applies chemical after bonding has already occurred, requiring far more material and time to break the bond.
**In use:** "Storm's forecast for tonight — get the brine down this afternoon, that's anti-icing; waiting till tomorrow morning makes it a deicing job at triple the material."
**Common misuse:** Treated as interchangeable terms for "putting salt down," when the timing relative to the storm changes both the chemical strategy and the material quantity needed.

### Chip seal
A pavement-preservation treatment (asphalt emulsion plus aggregate chips, rolled in) applied to an intact surface to extend its life, distinct from a patch, which fills a localized failure.
**In use:** "This segment's not potholed, it's just weathering — that's a chip seal candidate, not a patching job."
**Common misuse:** Confused with patching or resurfacing; chip seal is preventive on sound pavement, not a repair for existing structural failure.
