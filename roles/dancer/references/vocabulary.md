# Vocabulary

### Turnout
Outward rotation of the legs from the hip joint, used to increase the visible range of motion available to the side and to open the line of the leg to the audience.
**In use:** "Her turnout looks like 180 on stage, but her actual hip rotation is closer to 60 — the rest is coming from somewhere it shouldn't."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a foot position (how far the toes point out) rather than a hip-joint range that the feet merely display; "fixing" it by forcing the front foot line instead of working the hip is exactly the substitution that causes injury.

### Marking
Walking or lightly performing a phrase at reduced energy and often reduced range, to conserve the body while still rehearsing spacing, counts, and transitions.
**In use:** "Let's mark the lifts today and save full-out for Thursday's run."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to be a lesser or lazy version of rehearsing, rather than a deliberate load-management tool that, used well, is what makes a full-length run possible without injury by opening night.

### Full-out
Performing a phrase at full performance energy, range, and speed, as it would be danced in front of an audience.
**In use:** "We've marked this three times — let's go full-out once before we move on."
**Common misuse:** Used as the only standard for "real" rehearsing, ignoring that a role rehearsed exclusively full-out burns recovery capacity faster than one balanced with marking.

### Corps de ballet
The ensemble of dancers who perform in unison, as distinct from soloists and principals; the group whose job is collective precision rather than individual distinction.
**In use:** "The corps needs to land that arabesque within a count of each other, not just each dancer's own timing."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a junior or lesser role rather than a distinct skill — unison precision across a line of 16–24 dancers is a harder technical problem than an individual solo in several respects.

### Rehearsal director / ballet master (or mistress)
The staff member who runs day-to-day rehearsals, gives corrections, and maintains the choreography's fidelity to the original intent, distinct from the artistic director who sets overall programming and casting.
**In use:** "Take that correction to the rehearsal director before the next run, not to the choreographer directly, unless they're in the room."
**Common misuse:** Conflated with the artistic director; the two roles have different authority — one over daily execution, the other over programming, casting, and company direction.

### Artistic director (AD)
The company's senior creative and casting authority, who sets programming, approves casting, and answers for the company's overall identity.
**In use:** "That casting decision came down from the AD, not the rehearsal director."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to make every casting call purely on technical merit observed in the room, when injury history, understudy depth, and program balance across a night's bill routinely factor in.

### Session-RPE / training load
A method of quantifying a training session's demand as duration multiplied by a rating of perceived exertion (typically 0–10), summed across a day or week into arbitrary units (AU).
**In use:** "Her load this week came in at 1,080 AU — that's a 30% jump from last week's 830."
**Common misuse:** Treated as an objective physiological measurement; it's a self-reported, relative tool for tracking a dancer's own week-to-week trend, not a number comparable across different dancers.

### Acute:chronic workload ratio (ACWR)
The ratio of a dancer's recent (acute, e.g. one week) training load to their longer-term (chronic, e.g. four-week average) load, used as a directional signal for injury risk when the ratio spikes.
**In use:** "The ramp puts her at roughly 1.3 acute-to-chronic this week — inside the usual caution band."
**Common misuse:** Applied as a hard pass/fail threshold rather than the directional, individualized signal the underlying sports-science literature actually supports — and the literature itself has documented limitations worth knowing before leaning on it too hard.

### RED-S (Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport)
A medical condition where energy intake doesn't cover training energy demand, with effects including menstrual dysfunction, low bone density, and elevated injury risk.
**In use:** "Missed cycles plus this drop in weight — that's a RED-S conversation with the company physician, not a coaching note."
**Common misuse:** Mistaken for a simple weight or body-composition issue to be addressed through diet coaching alone, delaying the medical evaluation it actually requires.

### Épaulement
The use of the head, neck, and shoulders in coordination with the body's line to add expressive shading to a position or step.
**In use:** "The step is right, but there's no épaulement — it reads flat from the house."
**Common misuse:** Treated as decorative flourish rather than as integral to whether a position reads as intentional and finished from the audience's distance.

### Spotting
A technique for turning that fixes the eyes on a point and whips the head around faster than the body to prevent dizziness and maintain orientation.
**In use:** "The turns are falling off axis because the spot is late, not because the turnout is wrong."
**Common misuse:** Diagnosed as a balance or core-strength problem when the actual fault is timing of the head whip relative to the body's rotation.

### Partnering / pas de deux
Choreographed work performed by two dancers together, typically involving lifts, supported balances, and shared weight transfer, requiring explicit rehearsed communication distinct from solo technique.
**In use:** "We need a partnering-only rehearsal before we run this full cast — the counts aren't locked between the two of them yet."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to be an extension of each dancer's individual technique rather than its own skill set built on communication and trust that has to be separately rehearsed.

### Understudy / cover
A dancer rehearsed and prepared to perform a role without being the first-cast choice, providing depth against injury or scheduling conflict.
**In use:** "She's covering the lead — if [name] can't do Saturday, she goes on with two days' notice."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a consolation assignment rather than as company-critical infrastructure; being asked to cover a role is often itself a signal of trust from the rehearsal director.

### Sickling
A fault where the foot rolls inward or outward at the ankle instead of maintaining a straight line from the shin through the pointed foot, most visible in relevé or in the air during a jump.
**In use:** "She's sickling on the landing — that's an ankle-sprain risk, not just an aesthetic note."
**Common misuse:** Dismissed as a purely cosmetic line issue rather than flagged as a mechanical fault with real injury implications, especially on landings.
