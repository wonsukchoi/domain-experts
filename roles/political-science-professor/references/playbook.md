# Playbook

Filled templates and step sequences, not descriptions of them. Defaults, not laws — deviate consciously and note why in the record you keep.

## 1. Backward-designed syllabus (Wiggins & McTighe template, filled)

Course: Introduction to American Government, 200-level, 14-week term. Build the assessment column before the activities column.

| Learning objective (Bloom's level) | Assessment producing evidence | Weight |
|---|---|---|
| Explain the separation-of-powers design and identify a real case where it produced gridlock (Understand/Apply) | Week 4 short-answer exam item + case memo (2 pp.) | 15% |
| Analyze how a redistricting map changes partisan outcomes without changing vote share (Analyze) | Week 9 problem set — student redraws a district using provided precinct data | 20% |
| Evaluate a public-opinion poll for what it actually supports vs. what a headline claims (Evaluate) | Week 11 poll critique (1 pp., graded against a 4-criterion rubric) | 15% |
| Construct an argument for or against a specific institutional reform, representing the strongest opposing case (Create) | Final paper (8–10 pp.) | 35% |
| Recall core constitutional text and case names (Remember) | Weekly low-stakes quizzes (dropped lowest 2) | 15% |

Rule: if an objective has no row in the assessment column, cut the objective or add the assessment — an uncounted objective is a slide, not a course requirement.

## 2. Tenure/reappointment dossier — evidence structure

Departments vary; the shape below is a common stated pattern, not a universal rule — confirm the actual weighting in the department's written tenure document before building the file around it.

| Category | Typical R1 weight | Typical teaching-focused weight | Evidence to include |
|---|---|---|---|
| Research | ~40% | ~20% | Publication log (venue, date submitted/accepted, tier); citation count with a disciplinary-norm comparator, not a raw number; grant activity; work-in-progress with expected timeline |
| Teaching | ~40% | ~60% | Evaluation summary with response rates stated per term (never bare averages); item-level breakdown for any term flagged as an outlier; peer observation letters; syllabi showing objective-to-assessment mapping |

## 3. Grade dispute — steps in order

1. Student submits the dispute in writing, citing the specific rubric criterion they believe was misapplied — not "I deserved a better grade."
2. Re-grade against the rubric's descriptor language only, blind to the original score if possible (cover it before re-reading the work).
3. If the re-grade criterion-by-criterion matches the original, the grade stands — respond in writing quoting the descriptor language that supports each criterion score.
4. If a criterion was misapplied, correct that criterion's score only — do not adjust the overall grade beyond what the corrected criterion changes, and do not adjust other students' identical-pattern work retroactively unless a systematic misapplication is found, in which case re-grade the affected criterion for the whole class.
5. If the student escalates past the instructor, the rubric and the two written responses (original delivery, dispute response) are the file — no new arguments introduced at that stage carry more weight than what's already on the record.

## 4. Settled-vs-open classroom protocol (Hess & McAvoy)

Before teaching any topic likely to draw a bias complaint, classify it:

- **Settled empirical question** (e.g., partisan gerrymandering is measurable and occurs; a given policy's historical vote-share effect is documented) → teach the finding as settled, cite the empirical literature, do not manufacture "both sides" of a measurement.
- **Open normative or policy question** (e.g., whether a state should adopt an independent redistricting commission) → "best practice" model: assign readings representing the strongest form of at least two defensible positions, do not disclose a personal preference, structure at least one assignment (memo, debate, problem set) that requires the student to construct the strongest case for a position they don't hold.
- **Mixed** (a settled empirical finding embedded in a contested policy debate — this is the common case) → teach the empirical component as settled, then explicitly mark the transition to the open normative component ("the data on X is not in dispute; what to do about it is").

Documentation to keep contemporaneously for any topic classified as open: the reading list with source balance noted, and the assignment prompt showing the strongest-opposing-case requirement. This is the record an AAUP-standard review actually checks.

## 5. IRB determination flow (survey/interview research)

1. Does the study collect data from or about living individuals through interaction or intervention, or use identifiable private information? No → not human-subjects research, no IRB filing needed (document this determination in writing anyway).
2. Yes → is the data anonymous or de-identified at collection, and is the topic minimal-risk (standard political attitudes/behavior survey, no protected-class-triggering questions, no vulnerable population)? Yes → file for **exempt** determination under 45 CFR 46.104, category 2 (educational tests, surveys, interviews, or observation of public behavior).
3. Identifiable data, or minimal-risk but not anonymous (e.g., recorded elite interviews with named officials)? → file for **expedited** review.
4. Vulnerable population (minors, prisoners, cognitively impaired subjects) or greater-than-minimal risk → **full board** review; budget 4–8 weeks before data collection can start.
5. Set a calendar reminder for the continuing-review date before the first participant is recruited — data collected after a lapsed continuing review is typically unusable and may require a reportable protocol deviation.

## 6. Journal submission triage

| Outcome | Typical timeline | Action |
|---|---|---|
| Desk reject, generic (no reviewer comments) | Days to 2 weeks | Fit problem — re-target a different subfield or tier within 2–4 weeks; do not revise the manuscript itself yet |
| Desk reject, editor cites a specific framing issue | Days to 2 weeks | Fix the named issue only, submit to a different venue — don't resubmit to the same editor without the framing change |
| Reject after review | 2–4 months | Read reviewer comments for a design flaw vs. a presentation flaw; a design flaw (identification strategy, missing robustness check) needs new work before any resubmission anywhere |
| R&R | 2–4 months to receive | Treat as the real submission; a point-by-point response memo addressing every reviewer comment is mandatory, not optional, even for comments you push back on |
| Reject with encouragement to resubmit elsewhere | 2–4 months | Equivalent to a fit-problem desk reject — re-target, don't take literally as "resubmit here later" |
