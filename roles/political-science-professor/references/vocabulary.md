# Vocabulary

Terms of art in academic political science that generalists misuse. Format per term: definition, how a practitioner actually uses it, the common misuse.

### Tenure clock / probationary period

- **Definition:** the fixed number of years (AAUP caps it at seven) a faculty member holds a tenure-track appointment before a mandatory up-or-out decision.
- **In use:** "I'm year three of a six-year clock here — the third-year review this spring is the one that actually predicts the outcome."
- **Common misuse:** treating every year of the clock as equally consequential; the mid-clock review is disproportionately predictive and gets treated casually by junior faculty who assume it's a formality.

### R&R (revise and resubmit)

- **Definition:** a journal editorial decision inviting a revised version of a manuscript for a further round of review — not an acceptance, but a real, resource-worthy invitation.
- **In use:** "It's an R&R at the Journal of Politics with two reviewers wanting a robustness check on the identification strategy — that's a green light, not a rejection with homework."
- **Common misuse:** conflating R&R with rejection (demoralizing, and it stalls a paper that's actually close) or treating it as equivalent to acceptance (it isn't — a non-responsive revision gets rejected at the second round).

### Desk reject

- **Definition:** an editor's rejection issued without sending the manuscript to peer reviewers, usually on fit or framing grounds.
- **In use:** "Desk-rejected at APSR in eight days with a two-line note about scope — that's a retarget, not a rewrite."
- **Common misuse:** reading a desk reject as a verdict on the paper's quality; it's frequently a venue-fit signal decided before any reviewer saw the work.

### Backward design

- **Definition:** a course-design method that starts from the assessment evidence a mastered objective would produce, then builds instruction to produce that evidence — rather than starting from content to cover.
- **In use:** "I wrote the final paper prompt before I wrote a single lecture — the prompt is what tells me what week 9 actually needs to teach."
- **Common misuse:** building a syllabus as a content list ("we'll cover federalism, then Congress, then the courts") with assessments added afterward to match whatever got covered — the objective-to-assessment link ends up backward or missing.

### Academic freedom vs. extramural speech

- **Definition:** academic freedom (AAUP 1940 Statement) protects teaching and research judgment within one's discipline in the classroom and scholarship; extramural speech (public commentary as a citizen, off campus) is protected separately and more narrowly, tied to accuracy and not claiming to speak for the institution.
- **In use:** "The classroom complaint is an academic-freedom question — did I represent the open question fairly. The op-ed is an extramural-speech question — different standard, different committee."
- **Common misuse:** treating the two as one protection; a professor's public political statement and their in-class pedagogical choice are evaluated under different standards and different processes.

### Course release / buyout

- **Definition:** a reduction in normal teaching load for a term, funded either by the department (release) or by a grant paying the department to cover the cost (buyout).
- **In use:** "I bought out one course this fall with the NSF grant — that's the difference between finishing the manuscript and not."
- **Common misuse:** assuming a release is unconditional time off; most carry an implicit expectation (a grant deliverable, a specific manuscript) that gets checked at the next review.

### Impact factor vs. disciplinary citation norm

- **Definition:** impact factor is a journal-level average citation metric; disciplinary citation norm is what a "normal" citation count looks like for a given subfield and career stage in political science specifically — which is lower and slower than in the natural sciences.
- **In use:** "Twelve citations three years post-publication is solid for a comparative-politics article at this career stage — it would look thin by a biology standard, which isn't the standard here."
- **Common misuse:** importing citation benchmarks from other disciplines (or using bare impact factor as a stand-in for article quality) in a hiring or tenure discussion.

### Settled vs. open question (classroom pedagogy)

- **Definition:** a settled question has a discipline-wide empirical consensus (an election result, a measured effect); an open question is a genuine normative or policy disagreement without a consensus answer, per Hess & McAvoy's framework.
- **In use:** "That gerrymandering's measurable is settled — I teach it as fact. Whether a state should adopt an independent commission to fix it is open — that's where I run the best-practice balance."
- **Common misuse:** applying "present both sides" to a settled empirical finding (manufacturing false controversy) or presenting a genuinely open normative question as though the discipline had already resolved it.

### Response rate (student evaluations)

- **Definition:** the fraction of enrolled students who completed the end-of-term evaluation; the denominator that determines whether the average is a meaningful sample or a self-selected fragment.
- **In use:** "3.6 out of 5 on a 38% response rate isn't comparable to 4.3 on an 83% response rate last term — before anything else, fix the denominator problem."
- **Common misuse:** comparing raw averages across terms or sections without checking whether the response rates are even in the same range.

### IRB exempt vs. expedited vs. full board

- **Definition:** three tiers of institutional review board oversight for human-subjects research, keyed to risk level and identifiability — exempt (minimal risk, often anonymous survey/interview research), expedited (minimal risk but identifiable, or specific low-risk categories), full board (greater than minimal risk or a vulnerable population).
- **In use:** "It's an anonymous attitudes survey with no protected-class screening — that's exempt category 2, should clear in a couple of weeks."
- **Common misuse:** assuming any survey automatically qualifies for exempt status; identifiability, vulnerable populations, and sensitive topics (even in a survey) can push it to expedited or full review.

### Service creep

- **Definition:** the gradual accumulation of committee and administrative commitments that individually seem reasonable but collectively consume the time a tenure case actually needs.
- **In use:** "I said yes to three things this year that each looked like an hour a month and turned out to be a day a week combined — that's the creep."
- **Common misuse:** noticing it only after the writing block is already gone, rather than capping commitments against the tenure-clock position up front.

### Adjunctification / contingent faculty

- **Definition:** the structural shift of undergraduate teaching load toward non-tenure-track instructors (adjuncts, visiting faculty, lecturers) with less job security and typically no research expectation.
- **In use:** "Three of the four intro sections this year are staffed by contingent faculty — that changes who's actually exposed when a controversial-topic complaint lands."
- **Common misuse:** treating contingent-faculty staffing as a neutral scheduling fact rather than a factor in who bears complaint and grievance risk with the least institutional standing to contest it.
