# Red Flags

Clinical decompensation signals a paramedic checks for on every reassessment. Format per flag: what it usually means, the first question to ask, the data to pull.

### GCS drop of 2 or more points between assessments

- **Usually means:** expanding intracranial hemorrhage or rising ICP; second most likely is hypoxia or a new airway compromise dragging all three GCS components down together.
- **First question:** "What were the individual eye/verbal/motor components at each check, not just the sum — did one component drop, or all three?"
- **Data to pull:** timestamped GCS components from both assessments, SpO2 and EtCO2 at both points, pupil exam.

### Widening pulse pressure with bradycardia (Cushing's triad, third component: irregular respirations)

- **Usually means:** rising intracranial pressure approaching herniation; this is a late, not early, sign — treat as urgent once seen, don't wait for all three components to appear together.
- **First question:** "What was the pulse pressure and heart rate 5–10 minutes ago, and is the respiratory pattern actually irregular or just faster?"
- **Data to pull:** serial BP/HR trend, respiratory pattern description, GCS trend, mechanism.

### Respiratory rate outside 10–29/min in an adult trauma patient

- **Usually means:** either primary respiratory failure (chest injury, airway obstruction) or a compensatory response to shock/metabolic acidosis; a rate >29 with normal-appearing perfusion is often early compensated shock, not a respiratory-only problem.
- **First question:** "Is the rate change compensating for something else — check perfusion and mental status before treating it as an isolated airway problem."
- **Data to pull:** SpO2, EtCO2 waveform and number, chest exam (breath sounds, symmetry, subcutaneous air), CDC field-triage Step 1 status.

### Capillary refill >2 seconds or absent radial pulse with a normal-looking systolic BP

- **Usually means:** compensated hemorrhagic shock — systolic BP is one of the last vitals to fall because of peripheral vasoconstriction; by the time it drops, 30%+ of circulating volume is often already lost.
- **First question:** "Where's the bleeding, and has the source been controlled, or just hidden by clothing/positioning?"
- **Data to pull:** full exposure exam, pelvis/long-bone stability check, HR trend (tachycardia usually precedes the BP drop), skin color/temperature.

### Unequal or sluggish pupils

- **Usually means:** unilateral expanding intracranial mass effect (epidural/subdural hematoma) compressing cranial nerve III on the affected side; second most likely is direct orbital trauma, which should be visibly obvious on exam.
- **First question:** "Is there visible orbital trauma that explains this locally, or is the rest of the face uninjured?"
- **Data to pull:** pupil size/reactivity both sides, GCS trend, mechanism and point of impact.

### Tracheal deviation, absent unilateral breath sounds, JVD, and hypotension together

- **Usually means:** tension pneumothorax — this is the classic four-finding cluster, though tracheal deviation is a late and inconsistently present sign; don't wait for all four before acting on strong suspicion from the first three.
- **First question:** "Which side has the penetrating wound or the rib fracture, and does that side have breath sounds at all?"
- **Data to pull:** bilateral breath-sound comparison, SpO2 trend, BP trend, mechanism (penetrating chest trauma, blunt chest trauma with positive-pressure ventilation).

### A "walking wounded" green-tagged patient with any penetrating truncal wound

- **Usually means:** the START walking gate over-triaged this patient to Minor because ambulatory status was checked before the injury survey; penetrating truncal trauma carries a meaningfully higher deterioration risk than the ambulatory gate assumes.
- **First question:** "Has this patient's chest/abdomen/back actually been exposed and examined, or was the green tag assigned from the walking gate alone?"
- **Data to pull:** exposure exam findings, time since tagging, vitals at time of tagging vs. now.

### Anaphylaxis symptoms recurring 5–15 minutes after the first epinephrine dose

- **Usually means:** either an inadequate first dose/absorption (IM into fat rather than muscle) or a biphasic reaction beginning early; second most likely is a missed second allergen exposure.
- **First question:** "Was the first dose given IM into the anterolateral thigh, and how long ago exactly?"
- **Data to pull:** time and site of first dose, current airway/stridor/hives status, blood pressure trend.

### Last-known-well time reported with low confidence ("sometime this morning")

- **Usually means:** the actual eligible window for thrombolysis or thrombectomy may already be closer to expiring than the story suggests, or the reported time is an approximation nobody actually witnessed.
- **First question:** "Who is the single best witness to the patient's normal baseline, and what's the most specific time they can give — not an estimate, the actual last direct observation?"
- **Data to pull:** witness contact information for the receiving team, timestamp of the 911 call relative to the reported LKW, any video/phone-log evidence of the actual onset.

### Scene time exceeding 10 minutes on a Step 1/2 trauma patient with no active extrication

- **Usually means:** task-fixation on an on-scene procedure (IV attempts, splinting) that could be done en route; second most likely is a genuinely difficult extrication that should be logged as the reason, not absorbed silently into "scene time."
- **First question:** "What specifically is still being done on scene that can't be done in the moving unit?"
- **Data to pull:** ePCR timestamps (dispatch, on-scene, patient contact, wheels-rolling), extrication complexity notes if applicable.

### Heart rate exceeding systolic BP (adult/older adult)

- **Usually means:** early compensated shock — this was added as a 2021 CDC field-triage physiologic criterion precisely because it flags decompensation earlier than an isolated low SBP reading does.
- **First question:** "What's the actual numeric HR and SBP pair, not just 'vitals are stable' — is HR genuinely above SBP?"
- **Data to pull:** paired HR/SBP at each vital-sign check, trend direction, mechanism.

### Pediatric respiratory rate <15 or >45/min at a multi-casualty scene

- **Usually means:** impending respiratory failure — in children, respiratory arrest typically precedes cardiac arrest, so this rate band is the JumpSTART cutoff precisely because it catches the reversible phase.
- **First question:** "Has this child been rechecked since the initial tag, and is the rate trending toward or away from the 15–45 band?"
- **Data to pull:** serial respiratory rate, work-of-breathing observation (retractions, grunting, nasal flaring), pulse check.
