# Vocabulary

Working vocabulary that generalists reliably misuse. Format per term: definition, how a paramedic actually uses it, the common misuse.

### Load and go vs. stay and play

- **Definition:** "Load and go" (scoop and run) prioritizes minimizing scene time and treating en route; "stay and play" prioritizes completing interventions on scene before transport.
- **In use:** "This one's load and go — GCS is dropping and the trauma center's 14 minutes out, we can start the second IV rolling."
- **Common misuse:** treating one as universally correct. Cardiac arrest with a shockable rhythm often benefits from staying and running the algorithm on scene; a deteriorating trauma patient rarely does. The choice is patient- and condition-specific, not a house style.

### Standing order vs. online medical control

- **Definition:** a standing order is a protocol pre-authorized by the medical director that a paramedic can act on without calling in; online medical control is a live physician (or designee) contacted by radio/phone for anything outside the standing orders.
- **In use:** "Fentanyl 1 mcg/kg is a standing order for this pain scenario; a second dose past the protocol's ceiling needs online control on the line first."
- **Common misuse:** assuming "paramedic scope" is a fixed national list. Scope is defined by the local medical director's protocols within the state's practice act — the same paramedic can have different standing orders in two adjacent counties.

### Index of suspicion

- **Definition:** the working estimate of how likely a serious-but-not-yet-obvious condition is present, based on mechanism and history rather than current exam findings alone.
- **In use:** "Vitals look fine, but a 12-foot fall keeps my suspicion for intra-abdominal bleeding high enough to trend vitals every 5 minutes instead of every 15."
- **Common misuse:** letting "suspicion" become a synonym for "definitely has it" and over-treating, or the opposite — dismissing suspicion the moment one reassuring vital sign appears. It's a reason to reassess more often, not a diagnosis.

### Compensated vs. decompensated shock

- **Definition:** compensated shock is inadequate tissue perfusion with the body's reflexes (vasoconstriction, tachycardia) still maintaining a near-normal blood pressure; decompensated shock is perfusion failure severe enough that blood pressure itself has dropped.
- **In use:** "Cap refill's 3 seconds and he's tachycardic at 128, but BP's still 108/70 — that's compensated, not stable. Don't wait for the number to drop before treating it as shock."
- **Common misuse:** using "BP is normal" as reassurance that shock isn't present. By the time hypotension shows up, compensation has already failed — the earlier signs (tachycardia, delayed cap refill, narrowing pulse pressure, anxiety/altered mentation) are the actionable ones.

### Significant mechanism of injury (MOI)

- **Definition:** a mechanism statistically associated with occult serious injury even when the current exam is reassuring (falls from height, high-speed MVC with intrusion, ejection, high-energy transfer).
- **In use:** "MOI alone doesn't put him at a trauma center by Step 1 or 2 — but the fall height puts him there under Step 3 even with normal current vitals."
- **Common misuse:** treating MOI and physiologic criteria as interchangeable, or as a hierarchy where MOI must be checked first. CDC field triage runs physiologic criteria first precisely because they're the stronger signal when present; MOI is the fallback question, not the lead one.

### Golden hour vs. platinum 10 minutes

- **Definition:** the golden hour is the conventional first-60-minutes-post-injury window in which definitive trauma care matters most; the platinum 10 minutes is the derived convention that scene time for a serious trauma patient should be roughly 10 minutes or less, protecting the golden hour budget for transport and hospital care.
- **In use:** "We're at minute 9 on scene — package now, finish the second IV en route, we're spending golden-hour minutes we don't get back."
- **Common misuse:** treating "platinum 10" as a hard rule that overrides patient safety (e.g., rushing an unsecured airway to hit the number) or, at the other extreme, dismissing it as outdated because some studies now question its evidence base — it remains a useful scene-time discipline heuristic, not a literal stopwatch mandate.

### Triage tag (START/JumpSTART category)

- **Definition:** a point-in-time classification (Immediate/Delayed/Minor/Deceased or Expectant) assigned during mass-casualty triage to sequence treatment and transport resources.
- **In use:** "He was Delayed at initial triage, but he's re-tagged Immediate now — GCS dropped and he stopped following commands."
- **Common misuse:** treating a tag as a permanent record rather than a snapshot. The tag should change every time the patient is reassessed; an unchanged tag after 20 minutes on a critical patient usually means nobody rechecked, not that the patient is stable.

### GCS component notation (E/V/M)

- **Definition:** Glasgow Coma Scale is reported as three sub-scores — Eye opening (1–4), Verbal response (1–5), Motor response (1–6) — that sum to a 3–15 total.
- **In use:** "Total's the same, 10, but the components flipped — motor got worse and verbal got better. That's not the same patient trajectory as a flat 10."
- **Common misuse:** reporting only the sum. Two patients can share a GCS of 10 with opposite and clinically distinct component patterns; the sum alone erases the signal that would catch a real change.

### CDC field-triage "Step" (1 through 4)

- **Definition:** the four sequential decision tiers in the CDC's national field-triage guideline — physiologic (Step 1), anatomic (Step 2), mechanism (Step 3), and special considerations (Step 4) — each checked in order, with a positive at any step ending the search.
- **In use:** "He's Step 2 anatomic — flail chest — so trauma center's decided regardless of what his vitals happen to look like right now."
- **Common misuse:** running the steps out of order, or treating a Step 3/4 mechanism-only positive with the same urgency framing as a Step 1 physiologic positive — the guideline itself treats later steps as lower-certainty and appropriately routes them through a "consider/contact medical control" framing rather than an automatic transport mandate.

### MIST vs. SBAR handoff

- **Definition:** MIST (Mechanism, Injuries, Signs, Treatment) is the conventional trauma handoff structure; SBAR (Situation, Background, Assessment, Recommendation) is the general medical/clinical handoff structure borrowed from broader healthcare communication.
- **In use:** "It's a medical call, not trauma — give the trauma nurse the short version, but lead the stroke team with SBAR so the recommendation lands clearly."
- **Common misuse:** defaulting to one format regardless of call type, or omitting the "recommendation"/"treatment" line — the whole point of a structured handoff is that the receiving team gets the action-relevant piece without having to extract it from a narrative.

### Epinephrine concentration (1:1000 vs. 1:10,000)

- **Definition:** the same drug labeled by two different dilution ratios historically tied to route — 1:1000 (1 mg/mL) for IM use in anaphylaxis, 1:10,000 (0.1 mg/mL) for IV/IO use in cardiac arrest.
- **In use:** "Confirm it's the 1:10,000 pushed IV, not the 1:1000 auto-injector concentration — different box, different math."
- **Common misuse:** assuming "epinephrine" is a single dose regardless of context. Modern labeling increasingly states mg/mL directly to reduce this exact error, but the ratio notation is still common enough in practice and literature that misreading it remains the most cited prehospital epinephrine error.

### Last known well (LKW)

- **Definition:** the most recent time a patient was confirmed to be at their neurologic baseline, used as the clock start for stroke-treatment eligibility windows rather than the time symptoms were discovered.
- **In use:** "Discovery time was 0830, but the daughter says she talked to her at 0715 and she was normal then — LKW is 0715, not 0830."
- **Common misuse:** using "time found" or "time of 911 call" as a stand-in for LKW. The gap between LKW and discovery can be the difference between a patient qualifying for IV thrombolysis and missing the window entirely.
