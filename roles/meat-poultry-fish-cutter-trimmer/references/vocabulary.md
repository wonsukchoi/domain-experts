# Vocabulary

### Critical Control Point (CCP)
A specific point in a process, defined in the plant's HACCP plan, where a control is applied and monitored to prevent, eliminate, or reduce a food-safety hazard to an acceptable level.
**In use:** "Chilling is a CCP on this line — a temperature reading out of range there triggers the plan's corrective action, not a judgment call."
**Common misuse:** Treating any point where a hazard *could* occur as automatically a CCP — CCPs are the specific points the plan actually designates, and an operator's own hazard assessment at a non-CCP station still has to follow whatever the plan actually specifies for that point, not improvise a CCP-style response.

### Yield
The percentage of finished, trimmed product weight relative to incoming raw product weight, tracked against a customer or plant specification.
**In use:** "Yield came in at 77% against an 80% floor — that's outside normal variance and needs a root cause, not just a log entry."
**Common misuse:** Treating a below-target yield as always a quality failure and an above-target (over-yield) result as always good — over-yield often means under-trimming relative to spec, which is its own out-of-spec condition, not a win.

### Cumulative time-temperature exposure
The total elapsed time a product has spent above a specified temperature threshold across every processing station it has passed through, distinct from any single station's dwell time or reading.
**In use:** "This station's reading alone looks borderline, but cumulative exposure across the whole line is still well within our 30-minute allowance."
**Common misuse:** Evaluating a temperature reading in isolation at a single station without checking the running total across prior stations — bacterial growth risk tracks total exposure time, not any one station's number.

### Corrective action
The specific, documented response required by a HACCP plan when a CCP's monitoring value falls outside its critical limit, distinct from any informal fix an operator might otherwise apply.
**In use:** "The plan's corrective action for a chilling deviation is hold, verify chiller function, and recheck cumulative exposure — that's what we log, not just 'fixed it.'"
**Common misuse:** Applying a technically reasonable but undocumented fix instead of the plan's specified corrective action — an audit checks the record against the plan's required procedure, not whether the outcome was ultimately fine.

### Critical limit
The maximum or minimum value (a specific temperature, time, or measurement) that a CCP's monitored parameter must stay within to be considered controlled.
**In use:** "Forty degrees is our critical limit for this CCP — a reading above that triggers the corrective action regardless of how close it is."
**Common misuse:** Treating a critical limit as a soft target that a slightly-over reading can be waved through informally — the limit is the defined boundary the plan is built around, and any breach requires the documented response regardless of magnitude.

### Cross-contact / cross-contamination
The unintended transfer of a pathogen or allergen from one product or surface to another, typically via shared equipment, surfaces, or personnel movement between zones.
**In use:** "That knife's designated for raw poultry only — cross-contact risk is exactly why it doesn't touch the ready-to-eat line, even for a quick job."
**Common misuse:** Treating cross-contact risk as visible or detectable at the time it happens — the transfer itself produces no observable defect, which is why the segregation control has to be procedural and exceptionless rather than judgment-based.

### Foreign material control
The layered set of detection methods (visual inspection, metal detection, X-ray) used to catch unintended physical contaminants in a food product before it ships.
**In use:** "Metal detector caught something on this lot — we're stopping to find the source, not just pulling the piece and running the line again."
**Common misuse:** Treating any one detection layer as sufficient on its own or treating a reject as resolved once the flagged piece is removed — each layer catches different failure modes, and an unresolved root cause behind one reject risks a repeat event the same layer (or a different one) may not catch again.

### Allergen segregation
Dedicated equipment, tools, or zones reserved for a specific allergen-containing product, kept separate from other products to prevent unintended allergen transfer.
**In use:** "This cutting station is peanut-free zone only — we don't run a peanut-containing product through it even for one batch."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a quick clean between products is equivalent to allergen segregation — allergen cross-contact risk is why dedicated equipment/zones exist in the first place; a "clean and switch" approach is a different, lower-assurance control than true segregation.

### Chain of custody / traceability
The documented record following a specific lot of product from receiving through processing to shipping, enabling a recall or investigation to trace back to its origin.
**In use:** "If this lot ever needs tracing, the receiving date, supplier lot number, and every station log tie back to this batch number."
**Common misuse:** Treating traceability documentation as bureaucratic overhead disconnected from the actual product flow — it's what allows a specific problem (a contaminated lot, a supplier issue) to be isolated and recalled rather than triggering a much broader, more disruptive investigation.

### USDA/FSIS inspection
Ongoing, mandatory federal inspection of meat, poultry, and egg processing facilities under the Food Safety and Inspection Service, distinct from a periodic third-party audit.
**In use:** "The FSIS inspector's on the floor daily — our logs need to be current and accurate at any given moment, not just ready for a scheduled audit."
**Common misuse:** Treating FSIS presence as an occasional event to prepare for, like a scheduled audit — inspection is continuous, and recordkeeping has to be accurate in real time, not reconstructed or backfilled when an inspector asks.
