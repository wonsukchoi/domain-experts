# Red flags — signals a session is going sideways

Smell tests for mediation and arbitration sessions. Format per flag: signal, most likely cause, the first question a veteran asks, and what to pull to check it.

### A negotiator repeats "I don't have authority past $X" more than once in the same round
- **Usually means:** a genuine authority cap the principal hasn't disclosed, or a stalling tactic to extract a mediator's proposal without moving the party's own number again.
- **First question:** "Is that your personal limit, or does someone outside this room need to approve anything past it?"
- **Data to pull:** the intake notes on stated settlement authority, and whether the actual decision-maker (insurer, board, GC) is present or reachable by phone.

### A party's BATNA/WATNA math ignores its own litigation costs or assumes a 100% win probability
- **Usually means:** counsel hasn't run the expected-value arithmetic out loud, or is deliberately inflating the client's confidence to hold a position.
- **First question:** "Walk me through the probability weighting on each outcome — where does the 100% come from?"
- **Data to pull:** counsel's own case assessment memo if one exists, or run the expected-value calculation together in caucus.

### Joint session runs past 90 minutes without new information on the table
- **Usually means:** positional entrenchment — the parties are restating arguments for each other's benefit, not exchanging anything that changes the analysis.
- **First question:** "Has anything been said in the last 30 minutes that either side didn't already know?"
- **Data to pull:** the session agenda and clock; if the answer is no, break to caucus immediately.

### A caucus disclosure is directly contradicted by the same party in joint session
- **Usually means:** the settlement authority in the room is lower than represented, or the true decision-maker isn't present and is second-guessing by phone.
- **First question:** "Who else needs to sign off on any number we discuss today?"
- **Data to pull:** the intake authority statement versus what's said live; confirm by asking directly whether anyone off-site must approve.

### A party in binding arbitration requests an off-record conversation with the arbitrator alone
- **Usually means:** an attempt at ex parte contact — sometimes innocent (a scheduling question), sometimes an attempt to influence the merits outside the record.
- **First question:** "Is this a scheduling/logistics matter, or does it touch the substance of the case?"
- **Data to pull:** the applicable rules' ex parte provisions (AAA/JAMS both prohibit substantive ex parte contact); document the request and notify the other side regardless of the answer.

### A wide settlement gap collapses to zero within one round, in a high-stakes case
- **Usually means:** either a genuine breakthrough (rare this fast) or one party capitulating out of fatigue, pressure, or a decision-maker who isn't really weighing the numbers.
- **First question:** "Before we write anything down — does this number reflect what you actually think the case is worth, or what you want to be done with today?"
- **Data to pull:** the party's own BATNA/WATNA notes from earlier caucus rounds; compare the final number against the ZOPA estimated at intake.

### The person doing all the talking is counsel, and the principal hasn't spoken all session
- **Usually means:** real settlement authority may not be in the room, or the principal is deferring so completely that any agreement risks being disowned later.
- **First question:** directly to the principal — "does this number reflect your own read of the case, separate from your lawyer's?"
- **Data to pull:** confirm the principal's title/role and authority level against the intake form.

### A tentative deal reopens with "subject to" language after both sides thought it was settled
- **Usually means:** the represented authority was lower than stated, or a second approver (insurer, board, spouse) hasn't actually signed off.
- **First question:** "Who is the 'subject to' — is there someone we haven't accounted for in this negotiation?"
- **Data to pull:** the term sheet draft and the authority chain confirmed at intake versus who actually needs to sign.

### An arbitration award is drafted without addressing one or more submitted claims or counterclaims
- **Usually means:** an oversight in drafting, or a deliberate choice to duck a weak finding — either way it creates a completeness defect.
- **First question:** "Does the draft award have a finding, however brief, on every claim in the demand and every counterclaim in the answer?"
- **Data to pull:** the demand, the answer, and a checklist matching each claim to a paragraph in the draft award.

### A party discloses a real bottom-line number in caucus, then asks the mediator not to reveal it, then raises their demand in the next round
- **Usually means:** the party is using the mediator to gather intelligence on the other side's likely floor rather than negotiating in good faith.
- **First question:** "That number moved away from where we started — what changed since we last spoke?"
- **Data to pull:** the caucus notes timeline; if the pattern repeats twice, name it directly to the party in caucus.

### Mediator's fee or cost split is left unresolved until the very end of the session
- **Usually means:** a low-priority housekeeping item, but one that can reopen an otherwise-settled dispute when a party balks at the split after the substantive deal is done.
- **First question:** "Has the fee split been confirmed with both sides before we finalize the substantive terms?"
- **Data to pull:** the engagement letter or retainer agreement's fee-allocation clause, confirmed at the start of the session, not the end.
