# Working vocabulary — terms a generalist misuses

Format per entry: definition, a sentence a practitioner would actually say, and the common misuse.

### BATNA vs. WATNA
BATNA (Best Alternative to a Negotiated Agreement) is the value of your best available path if this negotiation fails. WATNA (Worst Alternative) is the value of your worst realistic path. Both are needed — a party anchored only on their BATNA ignores the downside risk that should be pulling their number toward settlement.
**In use:** "Your BATNA on a full verdict is $840,000, but your WATNA is a defense verdict at $0 — the number that should move you is the probability-weighted average of both, not the best case."
**Common misuse:** treating BATNA as a single confident number instead of a probability-weighted range, and never running the WATNA calculation at all.

### ZOPA (Zone of Possible Agreement) vs. bracket
ZOPA is the actual overlap between one side's floor and the other's ceiling — it exists or doesn't, independent of what either party has said aloud. A bracket is a mediator's *technique* — a hypothetical conditional midpoint offered to test movement — not the same thing as the ZOPA itself.
**In use:** "We won't know the real ZOPA until both sides have tested their numbers in caucus; the bracket is just how we get them to reveal it."
**Common misuse:** proposing a bracket before either side has moved, mistaking a technique for surfacing the ZOPA with a shortcut that skips the movement needed to find it.

### Caucus vs. joint session
Caucus is a private, confidential meeting between the mediator and one side alone. Joint session includes all parties and counsel together. Which one to use for a given issue is a judgment call, not a fixed order.
**In use:** "Non-monetary terms like the reference letter go in joint session — neither side wants to look unreasonable in front of the other; the dollar figure stays in caucus."
**Common misuse:** running an entire mediation in one mode only — all joint session locks in positional posturing; all caucus loses the momentum joint problem-solving can create on non-monetary issues.

### Caucus vs. ex parte contact (arbitration)
Caucus is a normal, sanctioned part of mediation. In binding arbitration, a private conversation between the arbitrator and one side alone is ex parte contact — prohibited outside narrow logistical exceptions, because the arbitrator is the decision-maker, not a facilitator.
**In use:** "That scheduling question is fine to take one-on-one; anything touching the merits needs both sides on the call, or it's an ex parte problem."
**Common misuse:** assuming the mediator's caucus norms carry over to arbitration — they don't, because the arbitrator's neutrality standard is different from a mediator's.

### Binding vs. non-binding arbitration
Binding arbitration produces an award enforceable in court with only narrow grounds for judicial review (FAA §10). Non-binding arbitration produces an advisory decision either side can reject and proceed to litigation or binding arbitration.
**In use:** "The clause says binding — once I issue this award, neither side gets a second bite except on the fraud/partiality/exceeded-powers grounds in §10."
**Common misuse:** assuming "arbitration" always means binding; many contracts and court-annexed programs specify non-binding arbitration as an early-neutral-evaluation step, not a final resolution.

### Vacatur vs. appeal
Vacatur is the narrow FAA §10 process for a court to set aside an arbitration award for procedural defect (fraud, evident partiality, misconduct, exceeding powers) — it is not a review of whether the arbitrator got the merits right. An appeal, by contrast, reviews the substance of a decision.
**In use:** "You can move to vacate for evident partiality if I had an undisclosed conflict — you can't vacate just because you think I weighed the evidence wrong."
**Common misuse:** treating a losing arbitration outcome as automatically appealable on the merits, the way a trial court judgment would be.

### Mediation privilege vs. general confidentiality
Mediation privilege is a legal rule (state Uniform Mediation Act statutes, or a contractual equivalent) making mediation communications inadmissible in later proceedings. General confidentiality is the practical, ethical norm the mediator enforces session to session — the two overlap but the privilege is what actually keeps a caucus disclosure out of a later trial.
**In use:** "Even though we didn't settle, nothing said in today's caucus can come into evidence at trial — that's the privilege, not just my promise to keep it quiet."
**Common misuse:** assuming a verbal "this is confidential" from the mediator has the same legal force as the jurisdiction's mediation-privilege statute; check whether the statute actually applies before relying on it.

### Facilitative vs. evaluative vs. transformative mediation (the Riskin Grid)
Facilitative: the mediator manages process, not content, and never opines on the merits. Evaluative: the mediator offers a assessment of likely outcomes if asked and qualified to do so. Transformative: the goal shifts from resolving this dispute to restoring the parties' own capacity to negotiate future ones.
**In use:** "Both sides asked for my read on the liability exposure, so I'm moving evaluative for this one issue only, then back to facilitative for the dollar negotiation."
**Common misuse:** a mediator staying evaluative as a default style rather than a deliberate, consent-based shift — or a party assuming any mediator will render an opinion on request.

### Med-arb
A hybrid process: the same neutral mediates first, and if issues remain unresolved, converts to binding arbitrator on those issues — using authority the parties granted in advance.
**In use:** "We're doing med-arb on the valuation dispute — whatever's left unresolved after mediation, I decide as arbitrator under the same engagement letter."
**Common misuse:** treating med-arb as risk-free; a party who was candid in caucus, expecting mediation-style confidentiality, may find those same disclosures informing the arbitrator's later binding decision unless the engagement letter walls them off explicitly.

### High-low agreement
A pre-arbitration or pre-trial agreement where both sides set a floor and ceiling on the award regardless of the actual verdict — the plaintiff is guaranteed at least the floor, the defendant is capped at the ceiling.
**In use:** "They agreed to a $200,000–$600,000 high-low before the hearing, so even a defense finding pays $200,000 and even a runaway award caps at $600,000."
**Common misuse:** confusing a high-low agreement (a private risk-management contract between the parties) with the arbitrator's award itself — the arbitrator typically isn't told the high-low numbers and rules as if they don't exist.

### Settlement authority vs. apparent authority
Settlement authority is the actual power to bind a party to a number. Apparent authority is what the other side reasonably believes that person has, based on how they've been presented — the two can diverge, and only actual authority matters when a deal later needs enforcing.
**In use:** "Confirm before we start whether the VP on this call has actual authority up to $500,000, or just apparent authority that her GC will later override."
**Common misuse:** assuming a title (VP, partner, adjuster) implies actual authority at whatever number gets discussed — always confirm the number, not just the title.

### Impasse vs. deadlock
Impasse is a stall that a technique (bracket, reframe, information exchange) can usually break. Deadlock is a stall that persists because the parties' actual interests, not just their positions, are irreconcilable — no technique fixes a genuine deadlock.
**In use:** "This isn't deadlock yet — we haven't tried a bracket or tested whether the real interest is the money or the apology."
**Common misuse:** declaring deadlock and ending the session after one failed technique, without diagnosing whether the block is informational or positional first.

### Single negotiating text
One document both sides redline in turn, replacing dueling counter-drafts, used for multi-issue disputes stalled on drafting rather than dollars.
**In use:** "We're circulating one term sheet from here forward — no more competing redlines chasing each other."
**Common misuse:** introducing a single negotiating text before the parties agree on the broad shape of a deal — it works to finish a deal, not to discover whether one exists.

### Functus officio
Latin for "having performed the office" — once an arbitrator issues a final award, authority over the case ends; the arbitrator generally cannot revisit or modify the decision except for narrow clerical corrections.
**In use:** "I'm functus officio on this award — a correction to the interest calculation is within scope, reopening the liability finding is not."
**Common misuse:** assuming an arbitrator can informally reconsider a ruling after the award issues, the way a mediator might revisit a term sheet before signatures.

### Reasoned award vs. standard award
A standard award states the outcome only. A reasoned award explains the basis for each finding against each claim. Rules often make reasoned awards optional unless a party requests one before the hearing closes.
**In use:** "Neither side asked for a reasoned award in advance, but I'm writing one anyway — it holds up better if this ever faces a vacatur motion."
**Common misuse:** assuming a bare/standard award and a reasoned award carry the same weight on review — a reasoned award, addressing every claim, is materially harder to challenge as incomplete or as exceeding the arbitrator's powers.
