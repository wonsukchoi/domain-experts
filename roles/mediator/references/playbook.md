# Playbook — running the session

Filled templates for the process itself: agenda structures by dispute type, caucus scripts, and the two standard impasse-breaking sequences with worked numbers. Adapt the numbers to the case; keep the structure.

## Agenda templates by dispute type

**One-day commercial mediation (single-issue dollar dispute, ~$500k–$1M in controversy):**

| Time | Block |
|---|---|
| 9:00–9:45 | Joint opening: mediator's ground rules, each counsel's summary (10 min each), no rebuttal |
| 9:45–10:30 | Caucus 1 — plaintiff: intake, confirm settlement authority in the room, BATNA math |
| 10:30–11:15 | Caucus 2 — defendant: intake, confirm authority, WATNA math |
| 11:15–12:30 | Shuttle round 1 — first moves off opening numbers |
| 12:30–1:00 | Working lunch, caucus continues informally |
| 1:00–3:00 | Shuttle rounds 2–3 — reciprocal moves, narrow the gap |
| 3:00–3:30 | Bracket or mediator's proposal (only once each side has moved twice independently) |
| 3:30–4:30 | Close the gap, draft term sheet live in the room |
| 4:30–5:00 | Signatures, fee split, next-steps for formal agreement |

**Multi-day employment mediation (discrimination/wrongful-termination claim, non-monetary terms in play — reference, neutral statement, reinstatement):**
Add a 30-minute joint session on day 2 morning specifically for non-monetary terms — these often resolve faster in joint session because neither side wants to look unreasonable refusing a reference letter in front of the other, unlike dollar figures, which resolve faster in caucus.

**Family/divorce mediation (ongoing co-parenting relationship):**
Shorter caucus blocks (15–20 min, not 20–40) and more joint time — the transformative goal is restoring the parties' capacity to negotiate the next disagreement (school choice, holiday schedule) without a mediator, so working the substance jointly, with the mediator managing tone rather than shuttling numbers, is itself part of the deliverable.

**Binding arbitration hearing (record-closing checklist before drafting the award):**
1. Confirm both sides rested and the record is formally closed — post-hearing briefs, if any, filed and both sides copied.
2. Confirm every claim and counterclaim in the demand and answer has a corresponding finding drafted — an award silent on a submitted claim is a completeness defect that invites a vacatur motion for exceeding or failing to exercise granted powers.
3. Draft the reasoned award addressing each claim with the evidentiary basis, even where the rules only require a "standard" (bare) award — the reasoned version survives review better and most commercial parties expect it.
4. Circulate for signature and issue inside the rules' deadline (AAA Commercial Rules: 30 days from close of hearing unless extended by agreement).

## Caucus scripts

**Opening a first caucus (plaintiff side):**
"Everything you tell me in here stays in here unless you tell me I can share it — I'll always ask first. Walk me through the case the way you'd tell it to a judge, and then the way you'd tell it to a friend over dinner — I want both versions, because the second one usually has the number that actually matters to you."

**Permission-to-share script (before crossing any number to the other room):**
"You told me $260,000 is your real floor. Can I tell them you're prepared to move that low, or do you want me to test their number first without disclosing yours?" — never assume consent from an earlier general "you can negotiate for us."

**Reality-testing a stated bottom line against case exposure:**
"Your counsel put a 35% chance on a full verdict, 40% on partial, 25% on defense — I ran that as an expected value and it comes out under $300,000 net of your own costs. You're telling me your floor is $500,000. Help me understand the gap — is there something in that number besides the litigation math?" This surfaces either (a) a non-monetary interest the math doesn't capture, or (b) a floor that won't survive contact with the other side's actual movement.

**Reciprocity script after a concession (to the side that just moved):**
"They moved $100,000. Before I take anything back to them, what's the smallest move you're comfortable making that still counts as a real answer to that?" — prevents a one-sided cascade where one party keeps conceding into silence.

## Impasse-breaking sequences (with numbers)

**Bracket, sequenced correctly.** Gap: plaintiff at $500,000, defendant at $300,000 after two independent moves each.
1. Caucus plaintiff: "Would you move to $375,000 if they moved to $375,000?" — a conditional bracket, not a real offer, framed as a question about a hypothetical midpoint.
2. Caucus defendant: same question, same number.
3. If both answer conditionally yes (as in the worked example in SKILL.md: plaintiff yes-at-$400k, defendant yes-at-$350k), the mediator now has a live $50,000 gap to close with one more round of direct moves — not a bracket restated, a genuine final round.
4. If either side rejects the bracket outright, do not repeat the same number — that reads as the mediator's number, not theirs, and repetition burns credibility. Go back to independent moves for at least one more round before trying a bracket again at a different midpoint.

**Mediator's proposal (used when brackets fail or time is running out).** Format: a single number presented simultaneously (by phone or in separate rooms within minutes of each other) with the frame "this is my number, not either side's — you may each accept or reject it confidentially, and I will not tell the other side which of you rejected it if it fails." This preserves face for whichever side says no. Use only after both bracket attempts and at least three rounds of independent movement have failed — offered earlier, it substitutes the mediator's judgment for the parties' negotiation instead of unlocking one.

**Doubling (for a party stuck on a position that contradicts their own stated interest).** Mediator sits beside the party (not across, as counsel would) and voices the case *for* moving, in the party's own language: "If I were you, I'd be worried that turning this down means eighteen more months of legal fees eating into whatever I win — and I might still lose." Used sparingly, only in caucus, only when the party's own words earlier in the session support the reframe — inventing a rationale the party never expressed reads as pressure, not reflection.

**Single negotiating text (for multi-issue disputes stalled on drafting, not dollars).** Mediator drafts one term sheet covering all open issues at once and circulates it to both sides for redline, rather than negotiating issue by issue — useful once the parties agree on the broad shape of a deal but keep reopening settled issues while negotiating the next one. Each round returns exactly one document with tracked changes, never two competing drafts.
