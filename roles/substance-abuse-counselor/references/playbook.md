# Level-of-care, relapse prevention, and consent playbook

Filled worksheets for the three recurring counselor tasks: ASAM dimension assessment, individualized relapse prevention planning, and 42 CFR Part 2 consent scoping.

## 1. ASAM (or equivalent) six-dimension level-of-care worksheet

| Dimension | Assessment question | Finding | Indicated level of care signal |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1. Acute intoxication/withdrawal potential | Is medical detox/monitoring needed now? | No current withdrawal symptoms; consistent MAT | No signal for medical detox |
| 2. Biomedical conditions/complications | Any medical comorbidity affecting treatment? | Stable | No signal for medical level of care |
| 3. Emotional/behavioral/cognitive conditions | Co-occurring mental health symptoms present? | Appropriate distress/regret, no acute symptoms | No signal for psychiatric level of care |
| 4. Readiness to change | Is the client engaged and motivated? | High — prompt voluntary disclosure, continued attendance | Supports continuing current level, not escalation |
| 5. Relapse/continued use potential | Is the use pattern isolated or escalating? | Isolated, tied to specific identifiable trigger | Does not indicate need for more restrictive setting |
| 6. Recovery environment | Is the living/social environment supportive or high-risk? | Stable, trigger was situational not environmental | Does not indicate need for residential removal from environment |

Rule: complete all six dimensions explicitly, in writing, before recommending a level of care — a level-of-care decision driven by bed availability or insurance authorization instead of this worksheet is the single most common documentation gap that undermines an appeal.

## 2. Individualized relapse prevention plan template

```
Trigger category: <person / place / emotional state / physiological cue>
Specific trigger: <exact description, from the client's actual history — not generic>
Warning signs preceding use (client-specific): <specific behaviors/thoughts the client has
  identified in their own pattern>
Coping response (specific, rehearsed): <exact action — a person to call, a place to go,
  a script to use, not "use coping skills">
Support contact: <specific name/number the client will call>
Plan reviewed/updated: <date, what changed>
```

Example entry (from the worked example):
```
Trigger category: Person
Specific trigger: Encountering former using partner, particularly at the gas station on
  [street], a location on client's regular commute route
Warning signs: Client reports a specific physical sensation (tightness in chest) and urge
  to "just say hi" as the first warning sign before craving escalates
Coping response: Alternate route avoiding that gas station; if encountered anyway, use
  rehearsed exit script ("I have somewhere to be") and leave within 30 seconds; call
  sponsor [name] within 10 minutes of the encounter regardless of outcome
Support contact: [Sponsor name/number]
Plan reviewed: [date] — added after lapse event, previous plan didn't address this specific
  location
```

Rule: never accept "identify triggers, use coping skills" as the plan's content — every entry needs the client's own specific history mapped to a specific, rehearsed, actionable response.

## 3. 42 CFR Part 2 consent scoping worksheet

```
Who is receiving the disclosure: <specific named individual/entity, not a category>
What specific information is being disclosed: <scoped — e.g. "attendance and progress
  summary," not "full chart">
Purpose of disclosure: <specific reason>
Consent obtained: <date, form used>
Consent expiration/revocation terms: <specific date or event-based expiration>
Re-disclosure prohibition notice included: <yes — required language that recipient cannot
  further disclose without separate consent>
```

Rule: a general medical release, a general mental-health release, or an implied "she's coordinating my care so it's fine" assumption does not satisfy Part 2 — every disclosure needs its own scoped, written, revocable consent, including disclosures to other treating providers.

## 4. Lapse / relapse / treatment-failure classification worksheet

| Pattern | Definition | Clinical response |
|---|---|---|
| Lapse | Single, isolated use event, promptly disclosed, followed by re-engagement | Process the event, reinforce/update relapse prevention plan, no automatic level-of-care change |
| Relapse | Sustained return to the prior use pattern over days/weeks | Reassess level of care via full ASAM review; may indicate need for more intensive setting |
| Treatment failure | Repeated relapse despite appropriate level of care and adherence to plan | Reassess treatment modality entirely — may need different level of care, different therapeutic approach, or MAT reassessment with prescriber |

Rule: classify before reacting — an isolated lapse treated as treatment failure (unnecessary escalation, punitive discharge) and a genuine treatment-failure pattern treated as a manageable lapse (continued ineffective care) are both classification errors with real clinical cost.
