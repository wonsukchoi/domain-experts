# Red flags — signals a substance abuse counselor notices instantly

Smell tests with thresholds. Any one alone can be manageable within the current plan; two or more together on the same client warrants an immediate reassessment.

### A use event is classified as "relapse" or "treatment failure" without checking whether it's actually an isolated lapse
- **Usually means:** the response may be escalating (level-of-care step-up, punitive discharge) disproportionately to what the actual pattern warrants, damaging the therapeutic alliance and potentially disrupting otherwise-effective treatment.
- **First question:** "Was this a single isolated use event promptly disclosed and followed by continued engagement, or a sustained pattern over days/weeks?"
- **Data to pull:** lapse/relapse/treatment-failure classification worksheet (see `references/playbook.md` §4), attendance and disclosure timeline.

### A level-of-care recommendation is made based on bed availability or insurance authorization rather than a documented ASAM-dimension assessment
- **Usually means:** the placement may not reflect actual clinical need, risking either under-treatment (client sent to a less intensive setting than needed) or unnecessary cost/disruption (over-placement).
- **First question:** "Has each of the six ASAM dimensions been assessed and documented independent of what's currently available or authorized?"
- **Data to pull:** ASAM six-dimension worksheet (see `references/playbook.md` §1), current documentation supporting the recommended level.

### Withdrawal risk hasn't been assessed for a client using alcohol or benzodiazepines
- **Usually means:** a same-day medical safety issue (seizure or delirium tremens risk) may be present and requires immediate medical evaluation, distinct from and prior to broader treatment planning.
- **First question:** "Has withdrawal risk specific to this substance been assessed today, separate from the general intake process?"
- **Data to pull:** substance use history and last use timing, current withdrawal symptom presentation, medical monitoring availability.

### SUD treatment information is about to be shared with another provider, family member, or employer under a general medical or mental-health release
- **Usually means:** this likely doesn't satisfy 42 CFR Part 2's specific consent requirement, creating a confidentiality violation regardless of good intentions.
- **First question:** "Is there a Part 2-specific, scoped, written consent for this exact disclosure, or is this being covered by a general release?"
- **Data to pull:** consent scoping worksheet (see `references/playbook.md` §3), the specific release document being relied on.

### A client on MAT is being counseled toward an abstinence timeline that contradicts or isn't coordinated with the prescriber's plan
- **Usually means:** the counselor may be substituting personal treatment philosophy for the prescriber's medical judgment, which isn't the counselor's role and can undermine evidence-based care.
- **First question:** "Has this timeline been discussed with and confirmed by the prescribing provider, or is this the counselor's independent recommendation?"
- **Data to pull:** MAT coordination notes with the prescriber, treatment plan's stated medication goals and timeline.

### A relapse prevention plan uses generic language ("identify triggers, use coping skills") without client-specific detail
- **Usually means:** the plan is unlikely to function in an actual moment of risk, since it doesn't specify what the client's real triggers are or what concrete action to take.
- **First question:** "What are this specific client's documented triggers, and what specific, rehearsed response is mapped to each?"
- **Data to pull:** individualized relapse prevention plan template (see `references/playbook.md` §2), client's use history and previously identified trigger patterns.

### A client's use disclosure was met with a confrontational or "in denial" framing rather than a motivational-interviewing-consistent response
- **Usually means:** this risks rupturing the therapeutic alliance that promotes future honest disclosure, which is itself a protective factor against undetected escalating use.
- **First question:** "How was the disclosure responded to in the moment, and does the documentation reflect a collaborative rather than confrontational tone?"
- **Data to pull:** session notes from the disclosure, client's subsequent engagement pattern (did disclosure lead to continued honesty or withdrawal from treatment).

### A client is discharged from treatment immediately after a single use event with no documented ASAM reassessment
- **Usually means:** discharge may be a punitive response to a lapse rather than a clinically justified decision based on the actual dimensions of risk and readiness.
- **First question:** "What does the ASAM-dimension reassessment show, and does it actually support discharge, or is this a reflexive response to the use event itself?"
- **Data to pull:** ASAM six-dimension worksheet completed for this specific event, documentation of the discharge rationale.
