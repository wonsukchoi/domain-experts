# Substance abuse counseling working vocabulary

Terms a substance abuse counselor uses precisely and a generalist blurs together. Format: definition → how a practitioner says it → common misuse.

**Lapse vs. relapse vs. treatment failure** — A lapse is a single, isolated use event, promptly disclosed and followed by re-engagement; a relapse is a sustained return to the prior use pattern; treatment failure is a pattern of repeated relapse despite appropriate level of care and adherence.
In use: "This is a lapse, not a relapse — one use event, disclosed voluntarily, followed by continued attendance and MAT adherence."
Misuse: treating any use event as automatically a "relapse" requiring the same escalated response, when the specific pattern (isolated vs. sustained, disclosed vs. hidden) determines which clinical response actually fits.

**ASAM Criteria (level-of-care dimensions)** — The American Society of Addiction Medicine's multidimensional framework assessing withdrawal risk, biomedical conditions, emotional/behavioral/cognitive status, readiness to change, relapse potential, and recovery environment to determine the clinically appropriate treatment intensity.
In use: "All six ASAM dimensions point to continuing at the current IOP level — nothing here indicates a need for residential."
Misuse: using "ASAM level" loosely to mean whatever level is available or authorized, rather than the specific clinical conclusion the six-dimension assessment actually supports.

**42 CFR Part 2** — Federal regulations governing confidentiality of substance use disorder patient records, imposing stricter consent and re-disclosure requirements than general HIPAA, including a prohibition on further disclosure by the recipient without separate consent.
In use: "This needs a Part 2-specific consent, not the general HIPAA release — Part 2's re-disclosure restriction is stricter."
Misuse: assuming a general HIPAA authorization or a mental-health-only release automatically covers SUD treatment information — Part 2 requires its own specific, scoped consent regardless of what other releases are on file.

**Medication-assisted treatment (MAT)** — The use of FDA-approved medications (e.g., buprenorphine, methadone, naltrexone) in combination with counseling and behavioral therapies to treat substance use disorders, considered evidence-based standard of care, particularly for opioid use disorder.
In use: "Continuing buprenorphine after this lapse is standard practice — stopping it would raise overdose risk, not lower it."
Misuse: treating MAT as a temporary crutch to be phased toward abstinence on a fixed timeline set by the counselor, rather than a medical decision made collaboratively with the prescriber based on the individual's clinical course.

**Dual diagnosis (co-occurring disorders)** — The presence of both a substance use disorder and a mental health condition (e.g., depression, PTSD) in the same individual, requiring coordinated rather than sequential or siloed treatment.
In use: "Given the co-occurring PTSD symptoms, this needs a coordinated dual-diagnosis treatment plan, not SUD treatment alone with a mental health referral tacked on."
Misuse: treating the substance use disorder in isolation and deferring mental health symptoms to "after sobriety is established," when integrated treatment is typically the more effective approach for genuinely co-occurring presentations.

**Withdrawal risk (substance-specific)** — The medical danger associated with stopping a specific substance, varying significantly by substance: alcohol and benzodiazepine withdrawal carry seizure and delirium tremens risk requiring medical monitoring, while opioid withdrawal is typically medically uncomfortable but rarely life-threatening.
In use: "This is alcohol withdrawal risk — that's a same-day medical monitoring question, different urgency than the opioid withdrawal we'd assess for a different client."
Misuse: applying a uniform withdrawal risk assessment across all substances, missing that alcohol/benzodiazepine withdrawal carries a materially higher acute medical danger than opioid withdrawal.

**Relapse prevention plan** — An individualized document mapping a client's specific documented triggers (people, places, emotional states, physiological cues) to specific, rehearsed coping responses, distinct from generic psychoeducational material about triggers and coping in general.
In use: "The plan names the specific gas station and the specific exit script — that's what makes it usable in the actual moment, not a generic 'avoid triggers' reminder."
Misuse: providing a generic relapse-prevention worksheet without populating it with the client's own specific trigger history and matched responses, producing a document that reads as clinically thorough but won't function when actually needed.

**Motivational interviewing (MI)** — A collaborative, client-centered counseling approach designed to strengthen a client's own motivation for change, avoiding confrontational or "you're in denial" framing in favor of exploring ambivalence.
In use: "We're using an MI-consistent response to the disclosure — reflecting back the client's own stated regret rather than confronting them about the use."
Misuse: treating any directive or confrontational approach as interchangeable with MI — MI specifically avoids confrontation in favor of eliciting the client's own reasons for change, which is a distinct clinical technique, not just a general tone of kindness.

**Readiness to change** — A specific ASAM dimension and clinical construct assessing a client's current motivation and engagement level, often informed by frameworks like the stages of change model (precontemplation, contemplation, preparation, action, maintenance).
In use: "Voluntary, prompt disclosure of the use event is itself evidence of high readiness to change — that's a specific clinical data point, not just a general impression."
Misuse: assuming readiness to change is static or assumed from a client's diagnosis or history, rather than actively assessed and documented as its own dimension at each relevant clinical decision point.

**Recovery environment** — An ASAM dimension assessing the client's living situation, social network, and environmental stressors/supports relevant to sustaining recovery, distinct from the individual's internal psychological or biomedical presentation.
In use: "The trigger was a specific, isolated encounter, not evidence the overall recovery environment is unsafe — that distinction matters for the placement decision."
Misuse: conflating a single situational trigger with a broader unsafe recovery environment, potentially leading to an unnecessary residential-level recommendation when the actual living/social environment remains stable and supportive.
