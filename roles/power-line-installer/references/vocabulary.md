# Vocabulary

Terms generalists conflate that a lineworker keeps sharply separate, because the difference changes what's actually authorized or protected against.

## Minimum approach distance (MAD) vs. NESC clearance

**MAD** is the distance a qualified worker's body or a conductive tool must maintain from an energized part *while working*, set by OSHA 1910.269 by voltage class. **NESC clearance** is the design minimum distance built into permanent construction (conductor-to-ground, conductor-to-structure) under the National Electrical Safety Code. NESC clearances are usually larger than MAD, but they answer "how was this built," not "how close may I work."

**In use:** "The NESC clearance on this line is fine — that's not the question. What's the MAD for a 25kV class conductor, because that's what governs where my bucket can sit."

**Common misuse:** citing a structure's built clearance as if it were the operating limit for how close a worker may approach during live work — the two numbers come from different standards answering different questions.

## De-energized vs. isolated vs. grounded

**De-energized** means no voltage is being supplied to the conductor from its normal source. **Isolated** means switching has disconnected the conductor from all known sources. **Grounded** means temporary protective grounds have been applied to create a bonded equipotential zone. A conductor can be de-energized and isolated and still not be grounded — and an ungrounded, isolated, de-energized conductor is not yet safe to work.

**In use:** "The circuit's isolated and tested de-energized, but we haven't grounded it yet — nobody touches it until the ground sets are on."

**Common misuse:** treating "de-energized" as the finish line, when it's the second of three required conditions before work begins.

## Equipotential zone vs. "grounded"

**Grounded**, used loosely, can mean anything from a single ground rod to a fully bracketed set. An **equipotential zone** specifically describes a bonded area where a worker, the conductor, and nearby conductive structures are all held at the same electrical potential, so a fault current has no potential difference to drive through the worker's body. A single ground connection at one end of a long de-energized section does not create an equipotential zone at a distant work position.

**In use:** "One ground set at the pole doesn't put me in an equipotential zone at this work position — I need the bracket sets on both sides of where I'm actually standing."

**Common misuse:** assuming any grounding at all on a circuit protects any worker anywhere on that circuit, rather than only within the bonded zone the grounds actually create.

## Arc flash vs. arc blast

**Arc flash** is the light, heat, and incident energy (measured in cal/cm²) radiated from an arcing fault — the burn hazard PPE categories are rated against. **Arc blast** is the pressure wave and physical projectile hazard (molten metal, shrapnel, structural fragments) from the same event — no PPE category rating protects against arc blast's physical force. The two happen in the same event but require different mitigations.

**In use:** "PPE category 4 covers the arc-flash burn exposure at this incident energy, but the arc-blast pressure at this fault current is why we're also increasing standoff distance, not just upgrading the suit."

**Common misuse:** treating a higher PPE category as protection against the blast/pressure component of an arcing fault, which cal/cm²-rated clothing does not address.

## Incident energy vs. available fault current

**Available fault current** is the maximum current a fault at a given point could draw from the system. **Incident energy** is the thermal energy (cal/cm²) that would reach a worker at a specific distance, given that fault current *and* the time it takes upstream protection to clear it. The same fault current with a faster clearing time produces far less incident energy than with a slow one.

**In use:** "Fault current here is high, but the recloser clears it in 3 cycles, so incident energy is actually moderate — don't assume high fault current means high PPE category without checking clearing time."

**Common misuse:** selecting PPE category from fault current alone, skipping the clearing-time half of the calculation.

## Qualified employee vs. journeyman

**Qualified employee** is an OSHA 1910.269-defined status: demonstrated skills and knowledge for the specific task and equipment, verified by the employer, task by task. **Journeyman** is a trade-credential/tenure designation (completed apprenticeship). A journeyman is not automatically "qualified" for every task under 1910.269 — qualification is task- and equipment-specific and must be verifiable, not assumed from rank.

**In use:** "He's a journeyman, but he's never been qualified on this utility's specific switching procedure — get him briefed and signed off before he's on this crew's rotation for it."

**Common misuse:** treating trade tenure or journeyman status as automatic proof of task-specific qualification under the regulatory definition.

## Switching order vs. clearance/hold tag

**Switching order** is the sequence of device operations (open/close specific switches, reclosers, breakers) issued by the system operator to reach a target circuit configuration. **Clearance (or hold) tag** is the authorization artifact that gives a specific crew the right to work a specific isolated section — it's what's released back to the operator when work is done. The switching order changes the circuit; the tag controls who's allowed to touch it while it's changed.

**In use:** "The switching order's complete and the circuit's isolated, but nobody's released a clearance tag to us yet — we don't have the equipment until that tag's in hand."

**Common misuse:** starting work once the switching is heard to be complete, without confirming the tag/clearance authorizing this specific crew has actually been issued.

## Mutual assistance vs. contract crew

**Mutual assistance** (EEI framework) is utility-to-utility crew sharing during declared emergencies, under reciprocal agreements and cost-recovery terms, with the visiting crew briefed onto the host utility's procedures. A **contract crew** is a commercially engaged crew, potentially from any vendor, under the contracting utility's standard procedures regardless of emergency status. The distinction matters for which procedures, insurance, and command structure govern on site.

**In use:** "These are mutual-assistance crews from three states over — they get the full host-procedure briefing before dispatch, same as if they were a contract crew, but the cost-recovery paperwork is different."

**Common misuse:** assuming mutual-aid crews arrive already trained on the host utility's specific grounding and switching procedures, skipping the briefing step because "they're all lineworkers."
