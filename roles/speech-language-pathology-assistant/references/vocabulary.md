# Vocabulary

Terms of art generalists reliably misuse. Format per term: definition, how a practitioner actually uses it, the common misuse.

### Plan of care (treatment plan)

- **Definition:** the SLP-authored document specifying goals, targets, cueing hierarchy, criteria for advancement, and any task-specific supervision requirements the assistant must follow exactly.
- **In use:** "The plan says independent-level, 3 consecutive sessions at 80% — I don't get to round that up because today looked good."
- **Common misuse:** treating the plan as a rough guideline the assistant can adapt session-to-session based on how the client seems to be doing — it's a document only the SLP modifies.

### Cueing hierarchy

- **Definition:** the ordered set of support levels — independent, minimal, moderate, maximal — used to describe how much help produced a given response.
- **In use:** "18 out of 20, but that's at minimal cue, not independent — doesn't count toward the independent-mastery line yet."
- **Common misuse:** reporting a bare accuracy percentage without the cue level, which makes the number impossible to compare across sessions or interpret correctly.

### Mastery criterion / advancement criterion

- **Definition:** the specific, SLP-set threshold (e.g., ≥80% accuracy, independent cue, 3 consecutive sessions) that must be met before a goal can be considered ready to advance.
- **In use:** "One session at 90% independent doesn't meet criterion — we need two more consecutive sessions at that level first."
- **Common misuse:** conflating "a good session" or a rising trend with "criterion met" — the criterion is a specific, multi-session, cue-level-specific rule, not a vibe.

### Generalization vs. acquisition phase

- **Definition:** acquisition is producing the target correctly in structured drill; generalization is producing it in less-structured, functional, or conversational contexts.
- **In use:** "Drill's at 90% independent, but the conversational probe is 45% — she's acquired it, not generalized it yet."
- **Common misuse:** assuming drill mastery automatically implies the skill shows up in real conversation — the two are measured and often diverge substantially.

### Direct vs. indirect supervision

- **Definition:** direct supervision means the SLP observes the assistant's work in real time (in person or synchronous telepractice); indirect supervision covers activities like chart review, case conferencing, or delayed feedback that don't require real-time observation.
- **In use:** "This task needs direct supervision per the plan — I need you in the room, not just reviewing the note afterward."
- **Common misuse:** treating any SLP involvement (a phone call, a signed note) as satisfying a "direct supervision" requirement when the task specifically calls for real-time, in-person observation.

### Scope of practice (assistant-specific)

- **Definition:** the ASHA- and state-defined boundary of what an SLPA may do — implementing a documented plan — versus what's reserved to the supervising SLP: assessment, diagnosis, plan authorship/modification, and discharge.
- **In use:** "Interpreting that screening result is outside my scope — I'll get you the raw scores and you make the call."
- **Common misuse:** assuming scope is about task difficulty ("I've done this a hundred times, I can handle it") rather than a fixed legal/credentialing boundary that doesn't move with experience.

### Standardized vs. criterion-referenced screening

- **Definition:** standardized tools are normed against a reference population with fixed administration rules; criterion-referenced tools measure performance against a specific skill criterion rather than a norm group.
- **In use:** "This is a standardized screener — I have to follow the exact administration script, I can't paraphrase the prompts."
- **Common misuse:** administering any instrument the assistant finds useful rather than only the specific ones the supervising SLP has designated and trained them to use.

### Dysphagia / aspiration risk

- **Definition:** dysphagia is difficulty swallowing; aspiration risk is the danger of food, liquid, or secretions entering the airway, which can cause pneumonia or death.
- **In use:** "Feeding tasks on this plan require you in the room — aspiration risk means there's no margin for 'it's been fine before.'"
- **Common misuse:** treating feeding/swallowing tasks like any other drill activity that can run under standard indirect supervision, when the governing guidance narrows assistant involvement specifically because the failure mode is severe and irreversible.

### Caseload ratio (assistant-to-SLP)

- **Definition:** the maximum number of SLPAs one supervising SLP may oversee at a time, set by state licensure regulation and varying by jurisdiction.
- **In use:** "Adding this referral would put you at four assistants — that's over the cap in this state, we need to reassign before intake."
- **Common misuse:** treating the ratio as an informal guideline that can flex when things get busy, rather than a hard regulatory ceiling that risks both parties' licensure if exceeded.

### Co-signature / countersignature

- **Definition:** the supervising SLP's formal review and sign-off on an assistant's documentation, required before the note is considered part of the official clinical record.
- **In use:** "This note isn't final until it's countersigned — don't send it to the file cabinet or billing yet."
- **Common misuse:** treating a note as complete once the assistant has written and filed it, skipping the SLP review step that both compliance and reimbursement rules require.

### C-SLPA (Certified Speech-Language Pathology Assistant)

- **Definition:** ASHA's national certification for speech-language pathology assistants, distinct from and not a pathway to CCC-SLP (the fully licensed SLP credential).
- **In use:** "I'm a C-SLPA, not a CF — this credential doesn't convert into an SLP license no matter how many hours I log."
- **Common misuse:** confusing the C-SLPA credential with the Clinical Fellowship (CF) year that a graduate SLP completes on the way to full licensure — the CF is a temporary, supervised step toward independent SLP practice; the SLPA role has no such conversion path.

### Clinical Fellowship (CF)

- **Definition:** the supervised post-graduate year a licensed SLP-in-training completes to earn full independent certification (CCC-SLP); distinct from the assistant role entirely.
- **In use:** "She's a CF, not an SLPA — she already has her master's and license, she's finishing her fellowship year toward full certification."
- **Common misuse:** using "assistant" loosely to describe both roles — a CF is a licensed clinician on a path to full autonomy; an SLPA is a permanent, supervised, non-independent role.
