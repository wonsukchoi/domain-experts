# Vocabulary

### Two-midnight rule
CMS policy that inpatient admission is generally appropriate when the physician expects the patient's care to span two midnights, documented at the time of the decision; shorter expected stays default to observation status.
**In use:** "She'll likely turn around in a day — call it observation and revisit if she's still here tomorrow night."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a length-of-stay label applied retroactively at discharge, instead of a prospective expectation documented at admission — an auditor evaluates the documented expectation at the time, not how things actually turned out.

### Observation status
A billing/regulatory status for patients who need hospital-level monitoring or treatment but aren't expected to need care crossing two midnights; clinically the care delivered can be identical to inpatient care.
**In use:** "He's observation, not inpatient — same monitoring, just make sure the note supports why we don't expect him past tomorrow night."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to mean "less sick" or "lower acuity" — it's a payment/audit category, not a clinical severity marker, and patients can be quite sick on observation status.

### Curbside consult
An informal, verbal question to a specialist about a case, without a formal bedside evaluation or billable note.
**In use:** "Let me curbside GI before we order the scope — might save the patient a whole extra day."
**Common misuse:** Used for questions that actually need the specialist to lay eyes on the patient and take documented responsibility (surgical timing, a liability-bearing management change) — a curbside answer isn't a substitute for a formal consult when the decision needs the specialist's name attached to it.

### Code status
The documented, patient-directed instruction for resuscitation efforts (full code, DNR, DNI, and combinations), distinct from goals-of-care more broadly.
**In use:** "Confirm code status on admission, don't inherit it from the last visit — it may have changed."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a one-time admission checkbox rather than something revisited when the clinical picture materially changes, especially after a new life-limiting diagnosis emerges mid-stay.

### LACE index
A four-component score (Length of stay, Acuity, Comorbidity via Charlson index, ED visits in 6 months) predicting 30-day readmission or death risk after discharge.
**In use:** "LACE came out to 11 — bump her to the high-risk follow-up pathway, not the routine two-week visit."
**Common misuse:** Calculated once on day one and never recomputed as length of stay changes — a score frozen at the admission-day estimate understates risk for a stay that runs longer than expected.

### HOSPITAL score
A validated readmission-risk score using hemoglobin at discharge, discharge from an oncology service, sodium level, presence of a procedure during the stay, admission type, number of hospitalizations in the prior year, and length of stay.
**In use:** "HOSPITAL score agrees with LACE here — both flag her high risk, that's not a coincidence given the low sodium and prior admissions."
**Common misuse:** Used interchangeably with LACE without checking whether they actually agree — they weight different factors and can disagree, in which case the disagreement itself is the signal worth a second look, not something to average away.

### Padua Prediction Score
An 11-item risk score for venous thromboembolism in hospitalized medical patients, used to decide between pharmacologic and mechanical VTE prophylaxis.
**In use:** "Padua's a 5 with the active cancer and reduced mobility — she needs pharmacologic prophylaxis, not just SCDs."
**Common misuse:** Applied only at admission and never revisited — a patient who becomes bed-bound mid-stay after being ambulatory on admission needs the score redone, not left at the original number.

### I-PASS
A structured handoff mnemonic (Illness severity, Patient summary, Action list, Situation awareness, Synthesis by receiver) shown to reduce handoff-related medical errors.
**In use:** "Give me the I-PASS on bed 12 before you head out — I especially need the action list and the call parameters."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a verbal-only ritual — the "synthesis by receiver" step (the incoming physician repeating the plan back) is the part most often skipped, and it's the step that actually catches misunderstandings.

### Case-mix index (CMI)
A hospital-level average of the relative weights of its discharged patients' diagnosis-related groups (DRGs), used as a proxy for average patient complexity and tied directly to reimbursement.
**In use:** "The documentation didn't capture the acute-on-chronic component, so this discharge undercoded and pulled CMI down."
**Common misuse:** Conflated with clinical acuity as experienced at the bedside — CMI reflects what was documented and coded, not necessarily how sick the patient actually was; under-documentation lowers CMI without the patient having been any less complex.

### Clinical documentation improvement (CDI) query
A formal, non-leading request from a documentation specialist asking a physician to clarify or specify a diagnosis already implied by clinical data, to ensure the chart supports accurate coding.
**In use:** "CDI queried whether the AKI was acute-on-chronic — worth confirming, it changes the DRG."
**Common misuse:** Dismissed as billing bureaucracy disconnected from patient care — the query is usually pointing at a real documentation gap (e.g., "acute kidney injury" implied by labs but never explicitly written), and closing it also makes the next physician's read of the chart more accurate, not just the bill.

### Rapid response team (RRT)
A pre-defined team activated by specific vital-sign or clinical triggers to evaluate a deteriorating non-ICU patient before a full cardiac arrest occurs.
**In use:** "Her respiratory rate's been climbing for two hours — call the RRT now, don't wait for a number that triggers automatically."
**Common misuse:** Reserved mentally for only the hard vital-sign trigger thresholds, when the intent of the system is to allow activation on clinical gestalt alone, before the numbers cross a line.

### Discharge summary
The formal document summarizing the hospital course, transmitted to the outpatient physician, intended as the primary information bridge between inpatient and outpatient care.
**In use:** "The summary needs to go out within 48 hours — the follow-up visit is useless if the PCP hasn't seen it yet."
**Common misuse:** Treated as complete once dictated and signed — if it isn't actually routed to and received by the correct outpatient provider before the follow-up appointment, it hasn't functioned as a handoff regardless of when it was signed.

### Dry weight
A patient's estimated euvolemic body weight, used as the target for diuresis in volume-overload conditions like heart failure.
**In use:** "She's 1 kilo above her documented dry weight — not there yet, hold discharge another day."
**Common misuse:** Approximated from "feels better" or "vitals normal" rather than compared against a specific prior documented number — without a stated target, "improving" and "at goal" get treated as the same thing.
