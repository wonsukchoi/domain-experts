# Art therapy working vocabulary

Terms an art therapist uses precisely and a generalist blurs together. Format: definition → how a practitioner says it → common misuse.

**Art as therapy** — Edith Kramer's framework holding that the art-making process itself, independent of any verbal interpretation, is the therapeutic mechanism (via sublimation and containment of affect).
In use: "With a client this nonverbal, we're working art-as-therapy — the making itself is doing the work, not a verbal decoding of the image after."
Misuse: treating "art as therapy" as a synonym for any art activity done in a clinical setting, rather than a specific theoretical stance about where the therapeutic action lives.

**Art psychotherapy** — Margaret Naumburg's framework holding that the image is a symbolic communication to be brought into verbal, often psychoanalytically-informed, processing between client and therapist.
In use: "She's ready for art psychotherapy now — we can start talking through what this recurring image means to her, not just making it."
Misuse: assuming every client benefits from verbal interpretation of their art regardless of readiness or verbal capacity — pushing this onto a client who needs art-as-therapy process work instead can shut down the modality's actual value for them.

**Expressive Therapies Continuum (ETC)** — Kagin and Lusebrink's model organizing media and interventions along kinesthetic/sensory, perceptual/affective, and cognitive/symbolic levels, used to match materials to a client's current processing capacity.
In use: "The ETC says she's working at the kinesthetic/sensory level right now — that's why we're staying with clay instead of moving to a symbolic drawing task."
Misuse: treating the ETC as a fixed developmental ladder to be climbed once and left behind, rather than a level clients move between session to session depending on state.

**Media control continuum** — The ordering of art materials from resistive (pencil, collage) to fluid (wet paint, clay), where more fluid materials lower psychological control and can surface affect faster.
In use: "We're staying resistive for another session or two before I introduce anything fluid — she hasn't shown me she can regulate through a wet-paint session yet."
Misuse: choosing media by convenience or the therapist's own preference rather than deliberately matching it to the client's demonstrated regulation capacity.

**Formal Elements Art Therapy Scale (FEATS)** — Gantt and Tabone's validated rating scale scoring formal properties of a drawing (space, energy, line quality, problem-solving, realism) on a 0–5 scale, typically paired with the PPAT directive.
In use: "Problem-Solving Approach moved from a 1 to a 4 on the FEATS — that's the strongest signal in this reassessment."
Misuse: substituting a gut impression of "looks better" for an actual FEATS score, which forfeits the comparability the instrument exists to provide.

**Person Picking an Apple from a Tree (PPAT)** — A standardized drawing directive (draw a person picking an apple from a tree) used as a repeatable baseline for FEATS scoring across sessions.
In use: "We'll re-administer the PPAT at termination with the same media as the baseline so the FEATS comparison is valid."
Misuse: changing the media or directive wording between baseline and reassessment, which invalidates the score comparison even if both drawings look reasonable.

**Directive vs. open studio** — A directive session assigns a specific prompt or theme; an open-studio session offers materials with no assigned topic, prioritizing client autonomy and rapport.
In use: "This week's open studio — I want her choosing the material and subject herself, given how much control's been taken from her at home."
Misuse: assuming directive sessions are always "more clinical" and open studio is just free time — open studio is a deliberate choice for goals like autonomy-building, not the absence of a plan.

**Diagnostic Drawing Series (DDS)** — Barry Cohen's structured three-drawing protocol (free drawing, a tree, and an expression-of-feeling drawing) used in some diagnostic and research settings.
In use: "The DDS protocol was administered as part of the intake battery, alongside the verbal history."
Misuse: treating the DDS's three drawings as sufficient on their own for a diagnosis, rather than as one component of a larger diagnostic battery.

**Containment** — The art object's capacity to hold overwhelming affect within a bounded, external form, giving a client a way to externalize distress without it staying purely internal or becoming dysregulating in the room.
In use: "The clay figure gave him a way to contain the anger physically instead of it building with no outlet in the session."
Misuse: assuming any art-making automatically produces containment — fluid, unstructured media with a client lacking regulation capacity can do the opposite and release more than the session can hold.

**Third hand** — Kramer's term for the therapist's careful, minimal intervention in a client's art-making process (helping technically without imposing content or taking over authorship of the piece).
In use: "I used a third-hand intervention there — steadied the brush for the stroke he was struggling with, but the image and its content stayed entirely his."
Misuse: using "third hand" to justify directing the content or symbolism of a client's piece, rather than strictly technical, authorship-preserving assistance.

**Sublimation** — A psychoanalytic concept, foundational to Kramer's art-as-therapy framework, describing the channeling of an unacceptable or overwhelming impulse into a socially acceptable, creative act.
In use: "The aggressive imagery in the painting looks like sublimation of the anger he can't express directly yet — the art is carrying what words can't."
Misuse: labeling any emotionally intense artwork as "sublimation" without regard to whether the process actually transformed and contained the impulse, versus simply depicting it.

**ATR / ATR-BC / ATCS** — Credentials issued by the Art Therapy Credentials Board: Registered Art Therapist (ATR), Board Certified (ATR-BC, requiring an exam), and Art Therapy Certified Supervisor (ATCS).
In use: "She's ATR-BC, so she's cleared to supervise toward the ATR for the associate on our team."
Misuse: treating "art therapist" as a self-applied title independent of these credentialing requirements, or assuming any mental health license (LPC, LCSW) alone confers art therapy scope of practice.
