# Carpenter Helper — Vocabulary

### King stud
The full-height stud on either side of a rough opening, running from bottom plate to top plate, that the header bears against.
**In use:** "King studs are already up on both sides of the window — go ahead and cut the jacks to length."
**Common misuse:** Generalists use "king stud" and "jack stud" interchangeably; a king stud runs full height, a jack (trimmer) stud is cut short to support the header underneath it. Mixing them up in a conversation about load usually means the load path isn't understood, not just the vocabulary.

### Jack stud (trimmer)
A shorter stud, cut to the rough-opening height, nailed to the inside face of a king stud to help support the header.
**In use:** "Two jacks on the door, this header's heavy enough to want the extra support."
**Common misuse:** Assuming any stud next to an opening is a jack stud — a cripple stud (above a header or below a sill) looks similar but carries a different load and sits in a different position.

### Cripple stud
A short stud above a header or below a rough sill, filling the space between the header/sill and the top/bottom plate, maintaining stud spacing through the opening.
**In use:** "Cripples above the header still need to land on 16" centers, not just fill the gap."
**Common misuse:** Treating cripples as filler that doesn't need to hit layout — they carry the same spacing requirement as full-height studs for sheathing nailing patterns.

### Crown
The slight lengthwise bow every piece of solid lumber has; sighted by eye or with a straightedge, oriented consistently (up in joists/rafters) so the structure settles predictably instead of randomly.
**In use:** "Crown's up on all of these, sight down the edge before you cut if you're not sure which way."
**Common misuse:** Ignoring crown on studs because "it's not a joist" — crowned wall studs still telegraph through drywall as a visible wave if not oriented and culled consistently.

### Rough opening (R.O.)
The framed opening size for a door or window — larger than the actual unit size to allow for the jamb, shims, and leveling.
**In use:** "R.O. on the plan is 38x82 for a 36x80 door — don't frame it to the door's actual size."
**Common misuse:** Framing to the finished door/window dimension instead of the plan's called-out rough opening, which is always slightly larger and includes shim allowance.

### Story pole
A vertical layout stick, marked once for a given wall height with the positions of plates, header heights, and window/door rough-opening heights, used to transfer identical vertical layout to multiple walls without re-measuring each one.
**In use:** "Use the story pole for the header height on wall 3, don't re-measure off the plan every time."
**Common misuse:** Treating the story pole as optional on a repetitive job — skipping it is how header heights drift by 1/4" to 1/2" wall to wall on a job with a dozen similar openings.

### Sequential-trip (single-actuation) trigger
A nail gun trigger mode requiring the safety contact tip to be pressed against the work and the trigger pulled, in that order, for every shot — the tool won't fire from trigger-first contact.
**In use:** "Switch to sequential before you toe-nail, contact-trip on that angle is how people put a nail through a hand."
**Common misuse:** Assuming trigger mode is a tool preference rather than a task-specific safety setting that should change with the work, not stay fixed for the day.

### Contact-trip (bump/bounce) trigger
A nail gun trigger mode that fires whenever the contact tip is depressed while the trigger is held back, allowing rapid repeat firing by "bouncing" the tool across the work.
**In use:** "Contact-trip's fine for this sheet on the ground, nobody's hands are near it."
**Common misuse:** Leaving a gun on contact-trip as a default because it's faster, without switching back for close-in or off-angle work where an unintended double-fire is likelier.
### On-center (o.c.)
The spacing convention for framing members measured from the center of one member to the center of the next, most commonly 16" or 24" for wall studs.
**In use:** "This wall's 16 o.c. everywhere except the 24 o.c. run on the gable end, check the plan before you lay it out."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a job is uniformly 16" o.c. throughout without checking — plans commonly mix spacing by wall type or engineered load, and a helper who lays out on assumption instead of the plan will be wrong somewhere.

### Plumb, level, square
Three distinct checks: plumb is vertical (a wall standing straight up), level is horizontal (a plate or sill flat side to side), square is a 90° corner relationship between two members.
**In use:** "It's plumb and level, but check it for square before we sheathe — the diagonals aren't matching yet."
**Common misuse:** Treating the three as one general "is it straight" check — a wall can be perfectly plumb and level while being out of square, and each failure mode needs a different fix.

### Sistering
Fastening a new piece of lumber alongside an existing, damaged, or undersized framing member to add strength without full replacement.
**In use:** "That joist has a check running through it — sister it rather than pull it, we don't want to disturb the subfloor above."
**Common misuse:** Using sistering as a default fix for anything found wrong in existing framing, without confirming with the carpenter or an engineer whether the specific damage/undersizing actually calls for sistering versus full replacement.

### Waste factor
The percentage added to a material take-off's exact linear-footage or square-footage total to account for cutoffs, defects, and errors, so the crew doesn't run short mid-wall.
**In use:** "Take-off says 288 square feet of sheathing, order at 10% waste — that's 10 sheets, not 9."
**Common misuse:** Applying waste factor inconsistently or rounding down "to save material," which produces the exact short-mid-wall scenario the factor exists to prevent.

### Toe-nailing
Driving a fastener at an angle through the face or edge of one framing member into another, typically used where a straight (face) nailing angle isn't accessible — such as securing a stud to a plate from the side.
**In use:** "Toe-nail that stud, we can't face-nail it from this side once the panel's stood."
**Common misuse:** Using contact-trip nail gun mode for toe-nailing because it's a familiar motion — the off-angle, close-in nature of toe-nailing is exactly the situation sequential-trip exists for.

### Formwork
Temporary molds (typically plywood/lumber panels braced and shored) that hold poured concrete in its designed shape until it cures enough to be self-supporting.
**In use:** "Formwork's braced and inspected, but we're not stripping it until the mix design's strength window is met, not just because it's been three days."
**Common misuse:** Treating formwork strip timing as a fixed day-count rule of thumb rather than a strength-based decision tied to the specific mix design and site conditions.
