# Railroad Conductor and Yardmaster — Vocabulary

### Blue flag / blue signal protection
A physical flag (day) or light (night), or blue signal per 49 CFR 218 Subpart B, placed on a track or car to protect workers on, under, or between equipment — removable only by the employee(s) who placed it, with no exception for rank or schedule pressure.
**In use:** "Blue's up on Track 4 — nothing moves onto that track till Torres pulls his own flag."
**Common misuse:** Treating the protection as removable on a supervisor's or dispatcher's verbal say-so, when the rule specifically reserves removal to the crew that placed it.

### Consist
The ordered list of every car in a train, with reporting marks, weight, and hazmat placement — the live document a crew, dispatcher, or first responder relies on to know exactly what's coupled and where.
**In use:** "Pull the consist before you call it in — I need the exact car and position, not 'somewhere in the middle.'"
**Common misuse:** Treating the consist as a billing or paperwork artifact updated after the fact, rather than a safety document that has to match what's physically coupled at all times.

### Switch list / work order
The yardmaster's plan assigning inbound cars to classification tracks by destination block and specifying the pull order for building an outbound train.
**In use:** "Switch list says Block C goes down first — don't pull B ahead of it."
**Common misuse:** Treating an issued switch list as fixed rather than something rebuilt from the yard's current state whenever a hot car, bad-order, or missed connection changes the plan.

### Block / blocking
A group of cars for the same destination or set-out point, coupled together and moved as a unit within the train.
**In use:** "Keep that block together — don't split Town B cars across two cuts."
**Common misuse:** Using "block" and "cut" interchangeably — a block is defined by destination, a cut is whatever's coupled together for a given switching move, and the two don't always line up.

### Buffer car
A non-placarded car required to separate certain hazmat cars from the locomotive or from other incompatible placarded cars, per 49 CFR 174.85.
**In use:** "Buffer car goes between the engine and the first PIH car — no exceptions on that spacing."
**Common misuse:** Assuming any car satisfies the buffer requirement, when a buffer car must itself be non-placarded and correctly positioned relative to both the locomotive and the hazmat car it's separating.

### Set-out
Removing one or more cars from a train at an intermediate point rather than the final terminal.
**In use:** "Four cars set out at Town A — brakes were cut out on the terminal test."
**Common misuse:** Using "set-out" as a synonym for "bad order," when a set-out can happen for any reason — destination, priority, a defect — not only a mechanical one.

### Point protection
The practice of a crew member maintaining direct sightline or radio contact with the leading end of a shoving movement in order to call stops.
**In use:** "I want point protection the whole shove — if you lose sight of the leading car, call the stop."
**Common misuse:** Treating an initial car count as sufficient point protection, rather than maintaining continuous contact for the entire move.

### Shoving movement
A train or cut moving with the locomotive pushing rather than pulling, requiring continuous communication because the crew in the cab has no direct view of the leading end.
**In use:** "We're shoving three cars into Track 9 — stay on the radio the whole way."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a short shove needs less communication discipline than a long one — the stop-on-loss-of-contact rule doesn't scale down with distance.

### Hump yard / bowl track
A classification yard where cars are pushed over a rise (the hump) and gravity-rolled into bowl (classification) tracks sorted by destination.
**In use:** "That car's rolling wrong off the hump — check the retarder setting before the next cut."
**Common misuse:** Assuming every classification yard is a hump yard; many are flat-switched by locomotive, which changes both the equipment involved and the sequencing math.

### Job briefing
The required conversation among crew members before a task — and again after any plan change — covering the specific move, hazards, and protection in place.
**In use:** "New plan, new briefing — we added two cars to that cut, let's go over it again before we move."
**Common misuse:** Treating the job briefing as a one-time formality at shift start rather than something repeated whenever the actual plan changes.

### Operative brakes / brake effectiveness
The percentage of cars in a train with functioning power brakes, established at the initial terminal air brake test, with a regulatory minimum (commonly cited at 85%) below which train movement is restricted.
**In use:** "We're at ninety-one percent operative — legal to go, but those four cut-out cars come off at the first point we can do it."
**Common misuse:** Treating "the brakes worked at the last stop" as equivalent to a documented percentage from the actual initial terminal test.

### PIH (Poison Inhalation Hazard)
A hazmat classification for materials that pose an inhalation hazard if released, subject to stricter placement and buffer-car rules than general placarded freight.
**In use:** "That's a PIH car — it needs its own buffer, not just whatever buffer the neighbor placarded car already has."
**Common misuse:** Treating all placarded hazmat cars as interchangeable for spacing purposes, when PIH commodities carry separation requirements stricter than other hazard classes.

### Double handling / re-switching
Pulling and reclassifying a block of cars more than once because the original build order didn't anticipate what was needed at set-out.
**In use:** "We're double-handling Block A again because it got buried under C — that's a switch-list problem, not a crew problem."
**Common misuse:** Attributing repeated re-switching to a slow crew, when it's usually a symptom of a block plan that didn't follow reverse-set-out order.

### Reporting marks
The alphanumeric code (owning road's initials plus a number) stenciled on a car — the unique identifier used in the consist, in Umler, and on every switch list reference.
**In use:** "Give me the reporting marks on that tank car, not just 'the one with the yellow placard.'"
**Common misuse:** Identifying a car by visual description or current position alone in a report, when reporting marks are the only identifier that survives a re-switch or a track reassignment.
