### Adverse impact
A selection process that produces a substantially different outcome rate for one protected-class group compared to another, regardless of whether the process appears neutral or intent to discriminate exists.
**In use:** "The interview-to-offer stage shows adverse impact against female candidates even though the interview questions are identical for everyone."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a process that looks neutral in design can't produce adverse impact, or that adverse impact requires proof of discriminatory intent.

### Four-fifths rule (80% rule)
A screening test comparing the selection rate of one group to another as a ratio; a ratio below 80% is generally treated as an indicator of potential adverse impact.
**In use:** "Female selection rate is 50% of the male rate — that trips the four-fifths rule."
**Common misuse:** Treating the four-fifths rule as a definitive legal conclusion on its own, rather than a screening test that should be followed by a statistical significance test when sample size allows.

### Statistical significance test (standard deviation / Z-test)
A more rigorous test than the four-fifths rule, checking whether an observed selection-rate disparity is unlikely to have occurred by chance, typically requiring a Z-score beyond a threshold (commonly ±2.0 or ±1.96) to be considered significant.
**In use:** "The Z-score came in at −1.89 — just under the standard significance threshold, so this is borderline, not conclusive."
**Common misuse:** Skipping this test when sample sizes are large enough to make it meaningful, relying on the four-fifths rule alone.

### Availability analysis
The process of determining the percentage of qualified, available individuals from a protected-class group in the relevant labor market and internal workforce, used as the basis for setting affirmative action placement goals.
**In use:** "Availability analysis for this job group shows 22% of the qualified external labor market is female, versus 12% current incumbency."
**Common misuse:** Setting a placement goal from a general diversity target instead of the specific external labor-market and internal-pool availability data for that job group.

### Placement goal
A benchmark an affirmative action plan sets for a job group, derived from availability analysis, that an employer works toward through documented good-faith recruitment and outreach efforts.
**In use:** "The placement goal here is a target for outreach efforts, not a number tied to any individual hiring decision."
**Common misuse:** Treating a placement goal as a quota that justifies or requires a specific hiring outcome for an individual candidate.

### Quota
A rigid numerical hiring or promotion requirement — explicitly distinguished from a placement goal, and generally not a lawful basis for an individual employment decision.
**In use:** "Basing this specific hire on hitting a number would cross from goal into quota territory — that's a legal risk."
**Common misuse:** Conflating a placement goal (a good-faith-effort benchmark) with a quota (a binding numerical requirement) in practice, even when the affirmative action plan's language correctly distinguishes them.

### Affirmative action plan (AAP)
The written plan a federal contractor is required to maintain under OFCCP regulations, documenting workforce analysis, availability analysis, placement goals, and good-faith-effort actions.
**In use:** "The AAP needs updated availability analysis and placement goals before this year's OFCCP submission."
**Common misuse:** Treating the AAP as a static document rather than one requiring periodic updates as workforce composition and labor-market data change.

### OFCCP (Office of Federal Contract Compliance Programs)
The federal agency responsible for enforcing affirmative action and non-discrimination requirements for federal contractors and subcontractors.
**In use:** "OFCCP scheduled an audit — we need the complete AAP package ready, not piecemeal responses."
**Common misuse:** Confusing OFCCP's contractor-specific affirmative action requirements with the EEOC's broader anti-discrimination enforcement, which applies more generally and doesn't require an AAP.

### Compensation equity analysis
A statistical analysis (typically regression-based) comparing pay across protected-class groups while controlling for legitimate, job-related factors, to identify any statistically significant unexplained gap.
**In use:** "The regression controls for tenure, performance, and level — the remaining gap after that is what we investigate further."
**Common misuse:** Reporting a raw, unadjusted pay gap as evidence of discrimination without running the controlled analysis, or dismissing a controlled analysis's significant finding without investigating it.

### EEO-1 report
The annual report required from employers meeting certain size thresholds (and federal contractors at a lower threshold), categorizing the workforce by race/ethnicity, sex, and standardized job category.
**In use:** "Job titles need to map correctly to EEO-1 categories before we run adverse-impact analysis using this data."
**Common misuse:** Using company-specific job titles directly in EEO-1 categorization instead of mapping them to the standardized federal job categories, which skews both the report and any analysis built on it.
