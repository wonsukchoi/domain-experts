# Vocabulary

Terms generalists conflate that a locomotive engineer keeps sharply separate, because the difference changes what was actually authorized, verified, or physically at risk.

## Absolute signal vs. permissive (intermediate) signal

**Absolute signal** displays no number plate and, at Stop, cannot be passed under any circumstance without direct verbal authority from the dispatcher. **Permissive (intermediate) signal** displays a number plate and, at Stop, can be passed at restricted speed after coming to a full stop, without additional authority. The visual aspect at the signal head can look identical between the two; the number plate is what actually determines what's permitted.

**In use:** "That's a numbered signal at Stop — full stop, then restricted speed on into the block. If it didn't have the plate, we'd need the dispatcher on the radio before we move an inch."

**Common misuse:** reading the color/aspect and assuming the authority, without checking for the number plate that actually distinguishes the two.

## Restricted speed vs. "going slow"

**Restricted speed** is a specific, numeric operating requirement: not exceeding roughly 20 mph, and prepared to stop within half the range of vision, short of a train, obstruction, or a switch not properly lined. **"Going slow"** is the layperson's shorthand that assumes any reduced speed satisfies the rule.

**In use:** "We're at restricted speed through here — that's not just slower, it means I need to be able to stop inside half of what I can actually see, every foot of this curve."

**Common misuse:** treating any speed reduction as compliant with restricted speed, rather than checking it against the specific stopping-within-half-the-sight-distance requirement.

## Buff force vs. draft force

**Buff force** is the compressive force at a coupling when cars are being pushed together (train bunching, such as during a brake application with slack running in). **Draft force** is the tensile force when cars are being pulled apart (train stretching, such as under power or during a run-out). Both are the same coupler system under opposite loading, and a train can transition rapidly between the two at a grade change or a throttle/brake transition.

**In use:** "We went from draft to buff right at the sag — that's exactly where a run-in does its damage, not out on the flat stretch."

**Common misuse:** describing any rough coupling event as "slack action" without distinguishing whether it was a buff (run-in) or draft (run-out) condition, which drives different corrective sequencing.

## Run-in vs. run-out

**Run-in** is slack closing (cars bunching together), typically from braking or descending into a sag. **Run-out** is slack extending (cars pulling apart), typically from accelerating or cresting into an ascending grade. Both draw on the same total free-slack budget in the train, and managing one without regard to the other just shifts where the abrupt transition happens.

**In use:** "That whip we felt was the run-out stretching back out after the run-in bunched us up at the bottom — the sequencing through the sag is what needs fixing, not just the braking at the bottom."

**Common misuse:** treating a single slack event in isolation instead of recognizing it as one half of a bunch-then-stretch (or stretch-then-bunch) cycle across a grade transition.

## Minimum reduction vs. full-service application vs. emergency application

**Minimum reduction** is the smallest mandated initial brake pipe pressure reduction (commonly cited around 6–8 psi) that reliably sets the train brakes without a large air loss. **Full-service application** is a larger, graduated reduction approaching the maximum useful brake pipe drop before hitting the emergency threshold. **Emergency application** dumps brake pipe pressure to zero at maximum rate for the fastest possible (but least controlled) stop. These are three distinct points on one continuum, not "light brakes" vs. "hard brakes."

**In use:** "Start with a minimum reduction here — we don't need full service yet, and emergency is the last resort, not the second step after minimum doesn't feel like enough."

**Common misuse:** skipping from minimum reduction straight to a much harder application without an intermediate graduated step, or treating "emergency" as just a bigger version of full service rather than a qualitatively different, less controllable action.

## Dynamic brake vs. air (train) brake

**Dynamic brake** uses the locomotive's traction motors as generators to retard the train, dissipating energy as heat through resistor grids, without drawing down brake pipe air. **Air (train) brake** applies friction braking at every car via the brake pipe, and is the only brake that acts on the cars themselves rather than just the locomotive. Dynamic brake has thermal/grid limits on long descents; air brake has recharge-time limits between applications.

**In use:** "Dynamics are fading on this grade — I need to bring in some air before we lose retarding force altogether, not wait until the grid cuts out."

**Common misuse:** treating dynamic brake capacity as unlimited on an extended descending grade, ignoring that sustained heavy use can fade or hit a control system limit.

## Independent brake vs. automatic brake

**Independent brake** controls the locomotive's own brakes only, used for coupling, slack control, and holding the locomotive on a grade without affecting the train. **Automatic brake** (the train brake valve) controls the entire train's brakes via the brake pipe. Using the independent brake when a train-wide application is needed (or the reverse) doesn't produce the intended slack or stopping behavior.

**In use:** "Set the independent to hold us here while I couple up — I don't want the automatic bleeding pressure off the whole train for this."

**Common misuse:** assuming "the brakes" is one system, when independent and automatic act on different parts of the train and are used for different purposes.

## PTC enforcement vs. PTC advisory

**PTC enforcement** is an automatic penalty brake application triggered when the system determines the train will violate its authorized speed or signal-derived limit without engineer action. **PTC advisory** is display information (upcoming restrictions, target speed) shown to the engineer with no automatic braking action. Reacting to the advisory is the intended use of the system; reaching enforcement means the advisory was missed or acted on too late.

**In use:** "The advisory's been counting down for the last half mile — I want to be braking well before it ever gets to an enforcement."

**Common misuse:** treating the absence of an enforcement event as proof that train handling was fine, rather than recognizing that staying ahead of the advisory is the actual target.

## Ruling grade vs. any grade segment

**Ruling grade** is the steepest sustained grade on a subdivision or route segment that determines the maximum tonnage a given locomotive consist can handle over that route. **Any grade segment** is simply a stretch of track with a given slope, which may or may not be the ruling grade for tonnage purposes even if it's steep in absolute terms.

**In use:** "This grade's steep, but it's short — the ruling grade for tonnage purposes on this sub is the one twelve miles up, and that's what set today's power assignment."

**Common misuse:** assuming the steepest-looking or most memorable grade on a route is automatically the ruling grade, rather than checking which sustained grade actually constrains tonnage.

## Timetable speed vs. authorized speed vs. restricted speed

**Timetable speed** is the maximum speed published for a segment of track under normal conditions. **Authorized speed** is what's actually permitted right now, accounting for any temporary speed restriction, signal indication, or PTC-enforced limit that may be lower than timetable speed. **Restricted speed** is the specific stop-within-half-the-sight-distance rule that can override both when in effect. All three can apply to the same stretch of track at different times, and the most restrictive of whichever apply governs.

**In use:** "Timetable's 60 through here, but there's a slow order down to 30, and the signal's calling for restricted into the siding — the signal wins, not the timetable number."

**Common misuse:** operating to the timetable speed as a default without checking whether a temporary restriction or a signal indication has lowered the actually-authorized speed for that specific movement.
