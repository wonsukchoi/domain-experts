# Red flags

Smell tests a route worker catches on the first pass through a checklist or a handoff conversation. Format for each: what it usually means, the first question to ask, and the data to pull before deciding whether to correct on the spot or escalate.

## 1. A grease point gets a partial shot at a visit it isn't due for ("topping off to be safe")

**Usually means:** the worker is treating grease as a harmless margin rather than a bounded quantity — the actual risk is churning heat and seal blow-by from cumulative over-lubrication, not under-greasing.

**First question:** "Is this point actually due per the corrected interval, or is this an unscheduled add?"

**Data to pull:** last logged relube date and quantity for the point, corrected duty-cycle interval, running total of unscheduled adds over the last quarter.

## 2. Housing/bearing temperature reads more than 15°C (27°F) above that point's own 5-visit baseline

**Usually means:** an active change in condition (lubrication breakdown, contamination, developing mechanical distress) rather than ordinary seasonal or load variation, which moves a whole line's temperatures together rather than one point relative to its own history.

**First question:** "What has this specific point read on the last five visits, not what does it read today in isolation?"

**Data to pull:** the point's own logged temperature trend, recent relube history (over- or under-serviced), ambient temperature for the same visits.

## 3. Belt deflection-force reading is off spec while the sheave grooves and belt condition look clean

**Usually means:** tension has simply drifted since the last service — a straightforward retension, not a component problem.

**First question:** "What did the force gauge read at correct deflection, against this belt section's spec range?"

**Data to pull:** current deflection and force reading, belt section's manufacturer force chart, date of last retension.

## 4. The same checklist deviation appears on three consecutive route visits despite being corrected each time

**Usually means:** the correction is treating a symptom of a mechanical root cause (misalignment, wear, a loosening mount) that's outside the route's scope to fix, not a maintenance chore that keeps recurring by chance.

**First question:** "Has this exact line item — same point, same deviation — shown up on the last two visits too?"

**Data to pull:** the last three route sheets for that asset, whether the same corrective action was logged each time.

## 5. A filter is changed at the calendar interval regardless of its differential-pressure reading

**Usually means:** the schedule is running on the manual's default rather than actual loading conditions — a filter can hit its rated ΔP well before or after the calendar date depending on contamination load.

**First question:** "What was the ΔP reading at change-out, and how does it compare to the manufacturer's rated change point?"

**Data to pull:** ΔP reading at the last three change-outs, rated change-out spec, contamination environment notes.

## 6. A sight glass shows a level drop between visits with no logged top-off

**Usually means:** a leak or seal issue consuming oil between services, not normal consumption — normal top-off needs are small and consistent, not variable.

**First question:** "How much dropped, and was anything added between the last two visits that would explain it?"

**Data to pull:** logged level readings for the last three visits, any top-offs recorded, visible leak points on the housing.

## 7. A checklist line item is marked complete with no reading, gauge value, or measurement logged next to it

**Usually means:** the item was skipped or eyeballed rather than actually measured — this erases the baseline the next visit's comparison depends on.

**First question:** "What was the actual number taken for this line, not just the pass/fail mark?"

**Data to pull:** the route sheet's raw values for the last several visits; if blank or identical every time, that's the tell.

## 8. A new sound is present that wasn't there on the last visit, layered on top of the machine's usual running noise

**Usually means:** an active mechanical change — this is exactly the kind of finding that's outside the route worker's diagnostic scope but squarely inside their detection scope.

**First question:** "Is this a new sound layered on the normal hum, or does the whole machine just sound different today (which points to a process/load change, not the machine)?"

**Data to pull:** location and description of the sound, when it was first noticed, whether load or process conditions changed recently.

## 9. PM schedule compliance percentage is healthy while specific high-criticality points are chronically deferred

**Usually means:** the route is prioritizing easy, low-criticality points to protect the headline compliance number rather than the assets that actually matter — the percentage is hiding the composition.

**First question:** "Which specific points make up the missed or deferred percentage, and what's their criticality?"

**Data to pull:** the deferred-item list broken out by asset criticality, not just the aggregate compliance rate.

## 10. A belt or filter fails reactively (breaks, clogs solid) despite being on a periodic wear-inspection route

**Usually means:** the inspection is happening on schedule but not catching the actual leading indicator — either the inspection method (visual only, no force/ΔP reading) or the interval itself needs revisiting.

**First question:** "What was logged at the last scheduled inspection of this exact point, and did it show any early sign that got missed or dismissed?"

**Data to pull:** the last two inspection logs for that point, the failed component itself if available, whether the interval matches this asset's actual duty cycle.
