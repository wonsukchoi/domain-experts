# Vocabulary

Terms generalists reliably misuse in pediatrics. Format per term: definition, how a practitioner actually uses it, the common misuse.

## Well-appearing

- **Definition:** a clinical gestalt assessment — alert, consolable, adequately perfused, interactive for age — used as one input among several, not a standalone risk stratifier.
- **In use:** "He's well-appearing, but at 37 days that doesn't change the workup — appearance isn't a low-risk criterion below 60 days, the labs are."
- **Common misuse:** treating "well-appearing" as sufficient to skip age-mandated workup in young infants. It is a real and useful sign at 2 years old and nearly irrelevant to ruling out serious bacterial infection at 3 weeks old.

## Growth faltering (formerly "failure to thrive")

- **Definition:** a trajectory diagnosis — weight (or weight-for-length) crossing down through major percentile lines over time — not a single low data point.
- **In use:** "One 15th-percentile weight check isn't faltering by itself; he's dropped from the 60th to the 15th over three visits, that's the actual finding."
- **Common misuse:** flagging a child who has always tracked at a low percentile as "failure to thrive," while missing a child who dropped from a high percentile to a merely-average one, which is the real trajectory red flag.

## Percentile vs. Z-score

- **Definition:** the percentile is where a measurement ranks among a reference population; the Z-score is how many standard deviations from the mean it sits. Percentiles compress at the extremes (there's a huge Z-score range between the 0.1st and 1st percentile that "percentile" alone doesn't show).
- **In use:** "He's off the growth chart on percentile, but the Z-score shows he's actually moved from −2.5 to −3.1 — that's a meaningful further drop the percentile chart can't display."
- **Common misuse:** reading "off the chart" as a single undifferentiated category instead of tracking the Z-score trend for children at the extremes, where percentile alone stops being informative.

## Developmental surveillance vs. developmental screening

- **Definition:** surveillance is the ongoing, informal observation and history-taking done at every visit; screening is administering a standardized, validated instrument (ASQ-3, M-CHAT-R/F) at specific scheduled visits.
- **In use:** "Surveillance today was reassuring, but he's due for the formal ASQ-3 at this visit regardless — that's not optional just because he looks on-track."
- **Common misuse:** treating a reassuring informal impression as equivalent to having done the standardized screen, and skipping the tool at the scheduled visit because "he seems fine."

## Acute otitis media (AOM) vs. otitis media with effusion (OME)

- **Definition:** AOM requires acute signs/symptoms plus middle-ear effusion; OME is fluid behind the eardrum without acute infection signs — often a resolution phase after AOM.
- **In use:** "That's OME, not AOM — no antibiotics; it clears in most cases within 3 months and warrants a recheck, not treatment."
- **Common misuse:** prescribing antibiotics for OME because the eardrum "still looks abnormal" post-treatment, when the guideline explicitly excludes OME from antibiotic treatment.

## Serious bacterial infection (SBI)

- **Definition:** an umbrella term (in febrile-infant literature) covering urinary tract infection, bacteremia, and bacterial meningitis collectively — not synonymous with sepsis.
- **In use:** "UTI accounts for the large majority of SBI in this age band — that's why urine is the first and most likely-to-be-positive test, not blood or CSF."
- **Common misuse:** using "SBI" and "sepsis" interchangeably, which overstates the acuity of the much more common (and less immediately dangerous) UTI cases within the SBI umbrella.

## Simple vs. complex febrile seizure

- **Definition:** simple — generalized, <15 minutes, doesn't recur within 24 hours, in a child 6 months–5 years; complex — focal, prolonged (>15 min), or recurs within 24 hours.
- **In use:** "That was a simple febrile seizure — no EEG, no neuroimaging indicated; the workup is for the fever source, not the seizure itself."
- **Common misuse:** ordering EEG or neuroimaging for a simple febrile seizure "to be thorough" — the guideline explicitly discourages this because it doesn't change management and adds cost, sedation risk, and caregiver alarm for no benefit.

## Innocent (functional) heart murmur

- **Definition:** a murmur produced by normal blood flow through a structurally normal heart — soft, systolic, no radiation to the back, varies or disappears with position change.
- **In use:** "Grade 2, vibratory, softens when he sits up — that's a Still's murmur, innocent; no echo needed unless something else in the exam changes."
- **Common misuse:** ordering an echocardiogram for every murmur regardless of characteristics, when the classic innocent-murmur features (soft, positional, no other cardiac exam findings) are enough to observe without imaging.

## Presumptive vs. participatory vaccine framing

- **Definition:** presumptive framing announces the vaccines as a default ("he's due for..."); participatory framing opens it as a decision ("what do you want to do about vaccines today?"). Studied outcome: presumptive framing produces measurably higher acceptance on the first pass.
- **In use:** "Lead with presumptive framing on routine visits — save the participatory, exploratory conversation for when resistance actually shows up, not before."
- **Common misuse:** defaulting to participatory framing for every family "to be respectful," which in practice invites more hesitancy than it resolves, and burns the visit's time budget on families who would have accepted a presumptive announcement.

## Catch-up / catch-down growth

- **Definition:** an expected trajectory shift toward a child's genetically determined percentile after a perinatal condition resolves (e.g., a growth-restricted-at-birth infant tracking upward toward familial percentile) — a normal phenomenon, distinguishable from pathological faltering by its context and direction.
- **In use:** "She was IUGR at birth — this upward percentile crossing over the first 6 months is catch-up growth, not a new endocrine workup."
- **Common misuse:** using "catch-up growth" as a reflexive explanation for any percentile crossing without confirming there's an actual perinatal history that predicts it — the label explains a crossing, it doesn't excuse skipping the check for one.
