# Red Flags

Smell tests that separate routine pediatric presentations from the rare dangerous ones. Format per flag: what it usually means, the first question to ask, the data to pull.

### Fever ≥38.0°C rectal in an infant <90 days old, however "well-appearing"

- **Usually means:** age-inappropriate reassurance — appearance does not rule out serious bacterial infection (UTI, bacteremia) at this age; it means the workup is age-gated, not appearance-gated.
- **First question:** "What is the exact age in days, and was the temperature measured rectally?"
- **Data to pull:** exact age in days, UA + urine culture, blood culture, CBC/ANC, CRP or procalcitonin per age band.

### Growth curve crossing ≥2 major percentile lines over consecutive visits

- **Usually means:** intake problem (volume, technique, formula concentration), less commonly GI, endocrine, or psychosocial (neglect, food insecurity) — rarely a single benign cause.
- **First question:** "What does a typical day of intake actually look like, in ounces/calories, not in the caregiver's summary of it?"
- **Data to pull:** weight-for-length or BMI trend across all prior visits, feeding log, prior labs if any were drawn.

### Bilious (green) vomiting in an infant

- **Usually means:** surgical emergency until proven otherwise — malrotation with volvulus, obstruction — not "just reflux" or "just a stomach bug."
- **First question:** "Is the vomit truly green, or yellow — and how long has it been going on?"
- **Data to pull:** immediate surgical consult, abdominal X-ray/upper GI series; do not defer for outpatient follow-up.

### Bruising pattern inconsistent with developmental stage

- **Usually means:** most bruising in mobile toddlers is accidental and shin/forehead-located; bruising on a pre-mobile infant, or patterned/clustered bruising (fingertip marks, loop marks, torso/ear/neck), raises non-accidental trauma.
- **First question:** "Is this child developmentally capable of causing this injury to themselves in the way described?"
- **Data to pull:** full skin exam, skeletal survey if under 2 years old, retinal exam if head injury suspected, social work consult.

### Fever ≥5 days plus rash, conjunctival injection, or extremity changes

- **Usually means:** Kawasaki disease until the criteria are formally ruled out — missed diagnosis carries coronary artery aneurysm risk.
- **First question:** "Has the fever been continuous for 5 full days, and are at least 4 of the 5 classic criteria present (rash, conjunctivitis, oral changes, extremity changes, cervical adenopathy)?"
- **Data to pull:** echocardiogram, CBC, CRP/ESR, urinalysis (sterile pyuria supports the diagnosis).

### Toxic appearance at any temperature, including afebrile

- **Usually means:** the exam overrides the vital signs — a toxic-appearing child with a normal or low temperature is not lower risk than a toxic-appearing febrile one.
- **First question:** "Independent of the number on the thermometer, is this child interactive, feeding, and consolable right now?"
- **Data to pull:** full sepsis workup regardless of temperature reading; do not let a normal or borderline temperature delay escalation.

### Weight loss >10% of birth weight, or failure to regain birth weight by 2 weeks of life

- **Usually means:** feeding/intake problem (latch, supply, technique) in the large majority of cases; occasionally underlying illness.
- **First question:** "How many wet and dirty diapers in the last 24 hours, and what does a feed actually look like?"
- **Data to pull:** weight on the same scale as birth weight, feeding observation, lactation consult referral, bilirubin if jaundiced.

### Limp with fever

- **Usually means:** septic arthritis or osteomyelitis until ruled out — a limping febrile child is a different differential than an afebrile limping child (toxic synovitis, trauma).
- **First question:** "Can the child bear weight at all, and where exactly does it hurt on exam?"
- **Data to pull:** ESR, CRP, CBC, plain films, joint ultrasound if hip is suspected — Kocher criteria to stratify septic arthritis risk.

### Head circumference crossing percentiles after infancy

- **Usually means:** acquired macrocephaly (hydrocephalus, subdural collection) if crossing upward; acquired microcephaly (injury, severe neglect, metabolic) if crossing downward — either is more concerning than a congenitally large or small head that has always tracked its own percentile.
- **First question:** "Has this always tracked around this percentile, or is this a new crossing?"
- **Data to pull:** serial head circumference measurements plotted against the same chart, neuroimaging if the crossing is new and significant.

### Recurrent visits for the same complaint (≥3) without a settled diagnosis

- **Usually means:** either a diagnosis is being missed because the differential anchored too early, or the underlying driver is psychosocial/functional and hasn't been named as such.
- **First question:** "What has changed or not changed between each of these visits, and has anyone looked at all three notes side by side?"
- **Data to pull:** full visit history reviewed together, not visit-by-visit; consider a broader differential re-set or a functional-symptom conversation.

### Caregiver becomes non-responsive to all preventive-care outreach

- **Usually means:** disengagement from care, not necessarily a settled refusal — often follows a single confrontational vaccine or screening conversation that damaged the relationship.
- **First question:** "What happened at or since the last visit that might explain the drop-off?"
- **Data to pull:** visit history and documented conversation notes around the point contact stopped; consider a lower-friction re-engagement (phone outreach, non-vaccine visit reason) before reintroducing the original topic.
