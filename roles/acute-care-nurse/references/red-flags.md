# Red flags

### A vital sign assessed only as a single snapshot rather than compared against the shift's trend
- **Usually means:** an early, developing deterioration pattern may be missed until it becomes a much more severe single-reading event.
- **First question:** has this reading been compared against this patient's trend over the shift, or assessed only in isolation?
- **Data to pull:** the patient's vital sign/early warning score chart across the shift.

### A post-operative finding judged against generic "normal vitals" rather than the specific expected recovery timeline
- **Usually means:** a finding that's actually concerning for this time point in recovery may be dismissed as routine, or a genuinely expected finding may be over-escalated.
- **First question:** where is this patient in the expected recovery timeline for their specific procedure, and does this finding match or diverge from that timeline?
- **Data to pull:** the procedure-specific expected recovery timeline, the patient's actual post-op hour/day and current findings.

### A more urgent patient need on the assignment addressed only after completing lower-priority scheduled tasks
- **Usually means:** overall patient safety across the assignment may have been worse than if priority had been reallocated to the more urgent need.
- **First question:** was the nurse aware of the more urgent need, and if so, why wasn't the task order reprioritized?
- **Data to pull:** the timing of the urgent signal, the actual task sequence followed.

### Analgesia administered on a fixed schedule without assessing current sedation/respiratory response first
- **Usually means:** risk of oversedation/respiratory depression if the patient's current status doesn't support the scheduled dose.
- **First question:** was sedation/respiratory status assessed immediately before this administration, or was the dose given strictly per schedule?
- **Data to pull:** sedation scale and respiratory rate at time of administration, the medication administration record.

### A discharge completed based on paperwork completion without verifying patient/family understanding
- **Usually means:** the patient/family may not actually be able to safely execute the post-discharge care plan at home.
- **First question:** did the patient/family demonstrate understanding via teach-back, or was understanding assumed from form completion?
- **Data to pull:** the discharge education record, any teach-back verification documented.

### An escalation made with only the current vital sign value, not the trend leading up to it
- **Usually means:** the receiving physician/team may not have the full picture needed to judge urgency accurately.
- **First question:** does the escalation communication include the trend data (multiple readings over time), or only the single current value?
- **Data to pull:** the escalation communication record, the underlying trend data available at the time.

### A patient's early warning score rising over multiple readings without an escalation being made
- **Usually means:** a developing deterioration may continue unaddressed past the point where earlier, simpler intervention was possible.
- **First question:** at what point did the trend begin rising, and why wasn't escalation triggered earlier in that trend?
- **Data to pull:** the full trend history, the escalation timing relative to the trend's onset.

### A nurse's patient assignment showing consistently rising acuity without additional support requested
- **Usually means:** the nurse may be unable to adequately trend-monitor and respond across all assigned patients given the current acuity level.
- **First question:** does the current patient ratio and acuity level still allow adequate trend monitoring across the full assignment?
- **Data to pull:** current patient ratio, acuity trend across the assignment, any support requested.
