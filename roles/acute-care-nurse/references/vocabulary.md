# Vocabulary

### Early warning score (e.g., NEWS2)
A standardized composite score derived from multiple vital signs, used to trend a patient's overall clinical trajectory over time rather than assess any single reading in isolation.
**In use:** "NEWS2 climbed from 2 to 5 across the shift — escalating based on that trend, not just the current reading."
**Common misuse:** Treating the early warning score as only meaningful at a single point in time — its real value is in the trend across multiple readings, which reveals deterioration earlier than any single snapshot would.

### Trend charting
The practice of recording and comparing a patient's vital signs/scores across multiple time points during a shift, rather than assessing each reading independently.
**In use:** "Trend charting shows a steady rise across three readings — that's a different picture than any one reading alone."
**Common misuse:** Charting vital signs without actually comparing across time points — the charting itself doesn't add value unless the trend is actively reviewed and acted on.

### Recovery timeline (procedure-specific)
The expected trajectory of vital signs and clinical findings for a specific procedure or condition at a specific point in time after it, used to judge whether a finding is expected or concerning.
**In use:** "Fever at 36 hours doesn't fit this procedure's recovery timeline, which expects resolution by 24-36 hours — flagging it."
**Common misuse:** Judging a post-operative finding against a generic "normal vitals" reference instead of the specific procedure's expected recovery timeline — the same reading can be routine at one time point and concerning at another.

### Teach-back
A discharge education verification method where the patient/family explains or demonstrates the care plan back in their own words, confirming actual understanding rather than assumed comprehension.
**In use:** "Had the patient teach back the wound care steps before discharge — caught a gap in the dressing-change sequence."
**Common misuse:** Treating discharge education as complete once instructions have been given and the discharge form signed — without teach-back, actual patient understanding hasn't been verified.

### Re-triage (assignment-wide)
Continuously re-prioritizing tasks across a nurse's full patient assignment based on real-time acuity signals, rather than working strictly through an originally scheduled task order.
**In use:** "Re-triaged the assignment when Room 412's trend started climbing — moved that reassessment ahead of the routine med pass."
**Common misuse:** Treating the shift's task schedule as fixed regardless of new acuity signals — the schedule reflects conditions at shift start, not a commitment to follow regardless of what changes.

### Analgesia titration
Adjusting pain medication dosing/timing based on the patient's current, real-time response (pain scale, sedation level, respiratory status) rather than administering strictly per a fixed schedule.
**In use:** "Titrated based on the current sedation scale reading before this dose, not just because it was scheduled."
**Common misuse:** Administering scheduled analgesia without checking current sedation/respiratory status first — this misses the actual purpose of titration, which is matching dose to current patient response.

### Sedation scale
A standardized assessment of a patient's current level of sedation, used alongside pain scales to judge whether additional analgesia is safe to administer.
**In use:** "Sedation scale showed the patient was already drowsy — held the next dose and reassessed rather than giving it per schedule."
**Common misuse:** Assessing pain level without also checking the sedation scale — pain management decisions require both, since treating pain without checking sedation risks oversedation/respiratory depression.

### Patient ratio (acuity-adjusted)
The number of patients assigned to a nurse, considered in relation to the acuity/complexity of those patients rather than as a flat headcount.
**In use:** "Ratio is technically within range, but acuity across this assignment has climbed — flagging for additional support."
**Common misuse:** Treating patient ratio as adequate purely by headcount, without accounting for whether the current acuity mix still allows adequate trend-monitoring across all assigned patients.

### Rapid response escalation
A formal request for additional clinical support/assessment triggered by a concerning trend or threshold, communicated with the specific supporting data rather than a general concern.
**In use:** "Called rapid response with the full trend — temp and heart rate climbing over 8 hours, NEWS2 from 2 to 5 — not just today's number."
**Common misuse:** Escalating with only the current snapshot value rather than the trend data that shows why the current value is concerning — the trend is often what makes the case for urgency.
