# Vocabulary

### Cavity pressure
The actual pressure the material experiences inside the mold/die cavity during fill and pack, distinct from the machine-set injection pressure measured at the cylinder or barrel.
**In use:** "Cavity pressure sensor shows we're running twelve to sixteen hundred psi in the mold, even though the machine's set to two thousand — that gap is what's causing the intermittent fill issue."
**Common misuse:** Treating machine-set pressure as equivalent to what the material actually experiences in the cavity — pressure drop through the nozzle, runner, and gate means the two numbers are routinely different, and only cavity-side measurement (where equipped) or careful part inspection reveals the actual condition.

### Shot sleeve fill percentage (die casting)
The ratio of the metered metal dose to the shot sleeve's total volume, a critical parameter for preventing air entrapment during the injection stroke.
**In use:** "Fill's running sixty percent against a seventy percent minimum — that's exactly the kind of gap that traps air ahead of the metal front."
**Common misuse:** Treating shot sleeve fill as a minor metering detail rather than a direct porosity root cause — insufficient fill is one of the most common, well-characterized causes of gas porosity in die casting, and it's frequently invisible until X-ray or a functional failure reveals it.

### Short shot
A casting/molding defect where the cavity doesn't completely fill, leaving a portion of the part incomplete or missing material.
**In use:** "That corner's a short shot — cavity didn't fully pack before the gate froze off."
**Common misuse:** Adjusting shot size or pressure upward to fix a short shot without checking whether the increase pushes the process into flash territory instead — the two defects are often opposite ends of the same adjustable range.

### Flash
A casting/molding defect where excess material escapes the cavity at the parting line or another gap, creating a thin, unwanted extension on the finished part.
**In use:** "We fixed the short shot, but now we've got flash on the parting line — that adjustment went too far the other direction."
**Common misuse:** Treating flash and short shot as unrelated defects requiring separate, independent fixes — they're frequently linked through the same underlying pressure/volume relationship, and a fix for one needs checking against the other.

### Porosity (gas porosity)
Internal voids or gas pockets trapped within a cast or molded part during solidification, typically invisible from the exterior and requiring X-ray, CT scan, or machining to detect.
**In use:** "Visually this part's perfect, but it's a pressure-critical application — we're X-ray sampling before it ships, because porosity wouldn't show any other way."
**Common misuse:** Treating a visually and dimensionally acceptable part as equivalent to a structurally sound one — porosity's defining characteristic is that it's typically undetectable without a specific internal inspection method.

### Weld line (knit line)
A visible or structurally weaker line on a molded/cast part where two flow fronts of material meet and fuse during fill, whose location is set by gate and part geometry.
**In use:** "That weld line's in the same spot every cycle — that's a gate location issue, not something we can process our way out of."
**Common misuse:** Attempting to eliminate a weld line entirely through process parameter adjustment alone — weld line location is fundamentally determined by gate placement and part geometry, and process parameters can only influence its strength/appearance within limits the tooling design sets.

### First-article inspection
A full dimensional, visual, and (where required) internal inspection of the first part or parts produced from a new or changed tool, validating the tool and process together before production quantity begins.
**In use:** "New die's in — first article goes through full dimensional check plus X-ray before we run production quantity."
**Common misuse:** Treating a visual/dimensional check alone as sufficient first-article verification for a critical application — first-article inspection needs to match the part's actual required inspection scope, including internal checks where the application demands them.

### Cooling time
The portion of a molding/casting cycle allowing the part to solidify sufficiently before ejection, distinct from total cycle time.
**In use:** "We shaved cooling time to cut cycle time, but we're checking dimensions after full cool-down, not just right off the machine, before we call that a win."
**Common misuse:** Verifying a cooling-time reduction only by checking the part immediately after ejection — a part can appear dimensionally fine while still hot and only reveal warpage once it reaches full room-temperature equilibrium, well after leaving the machine.

### Melt temperature (actual vs. set)
The actual temperature of molten material at the point of injection, which can differ from the barrel's set temperature due to shear heating, residence time, and heat loss through the flow path.
**In use:** "Barrel's set to spec, but actual melt temp at the nozzle can run higher from shear heating — that's worth checking if we're seeing degradation."
**Common misuse:** Assuming barrel set temperature equals actual melt temperature at the point of injection — various factors along the flow path can shift actual temperature away from the programmed setpoint.

### Process sheet (traveler)
The documented specification for a specific job's process parameters — pressure, speed, temperature, cooling time, shot size — that the machine should be set to for that part.
**In use:** "Pulling the process sheet before we start — don't want to run this job on whatever the machine was last set to."
**Common misuse:** Trusting the machine's current settings as correct without confirming them against the specific job's process sheet — settings from a prior job can persist on the machine and don't automatically match the new job's requirements.
