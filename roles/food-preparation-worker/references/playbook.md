# Playbook

Filled worksheets a prep worker actually runs during a shift, with real structures and plausible numbers — adapt the quantities, keep the math.

## 1. Prep list / pull sheet (filled example — Saturday lunch, 180 covers)

| Item | Portion | Mix % | Orders | +10% buffer | Batch/case size | Cases/batches to pull |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Chicken sandwich (bnls skinless breast, 92% yield) | 6 oz EP | 30% | 54 | 60 → 22.5 lb EP | 10 lb AP/case | 3 cases (30 lb AP → 27.6 lb EP → 73 portions) |
| House slaw (green cabbage + carrot, 78%/81% yield) | 3 oz EP | 55% | 99 | 109 → 20.4 lb EP | 5 lb prepped batch | 5 batches |
| Marinara base (roma tomato, 94% yield after coring/rough dice) | 2 oz per plate use | n/a — batch for the week | 7-day batch | n/a | 8 qt batch | 1 batch covers ~5 service days at current draw |
| Diced onion (89% yield) | 0.75 oz avg per ticket | cross-menu | 180 tickets | 198 → 9.3 lb EP | 3 lb prepped batch | 4 batches |

**Reading the sheet:** the "orders" column always starts from the forecast, never from a par level alone — par tells you what's already on hand, forecast tells you what's needed. Net to prep = buffered need − on-hand.

| Ingredient | Prep applied | Yield % | Source |
|---|---|---|---|
| Boneless skinless chicken breast | Trim tenders/silverskin, portion | 92% | House yield card, cross-checked against *The Book of Yields* |
| Whole chicken | Fabricate to 8-piece | ~70% | *The Book of Yields* |
| Green cabbage | Core, shred | 78% | *The Book of Yields* |
| Carrot | Peel, julienne | 81% | *The Book of Yields* |
| Yellow onion | Peel, dice | 89% | *The Book of Yields* |
| Roma tomato | Core, rough dice | 94% | *The Book of Yields* |
| Russet potato | Peel, cut for fries | 81% | *The Book of Yields* |
| Beef tenderloin, whole (PSMO) | Trim chain/silverskin, portion | 83% | *The Book of Yields* |

**Worked conversion (potato, filled):** Line needs 40 lb EP fries cut for the weekend. AP required = 40 ÷ 0.81 = 49.4 lb → round up to 50 lb, ordered in 50 lb sacks → pull 1 sack. If a delivery's potatoes run unusually large-eyed and trim comes in closer to 75% yield, recompute before the next order (50 lb AP × 0.75 = 37.5 lb EP, a 2.5 lb shortfall against the 40 lb need) and flag purchasing rather than silently absorbing the gap through longer prep hours.

## 3. TCS labeling & shelf-life table

| Category | Default shelf life at ≤41°F | Label fields |
|---|---|---|
| Raw cut poultry | 24–48 hrs | Prepped date/time, discard date/time, "RAW POULTRY" |
| Raw cut seafood | 24 hrs | Prepped date/time, discard date/time, "RAW SEAFOOD" |
| Raw ground/cut red meat | 24–48 hrs | Prepped date/time, discard date/time, "RAW MEAT" |
| Cut leafy greens/produce | 3–4 days | Prepped date/time, discard date/time |
| Cooked sauces/stocks, cooled properly | 7 days | Prepped date/time, discard date/time |
| Cooked proteins, cooled properly | 3–4 days | Prepped date/time, discard date/time |
| Any allergen-containing prepped item | per category above | + allergen flag (e.g., "CONTAINS: TREE NUT") |

**Rule:** day of prep counts as day 1. A marinara base prepped Monday at 10am with a 7-day shelf life discards Monday of the following week at 10am, not "sometime next Monday." Shorter house or local health-department limits always override this table — check before assuming 7 days on anything unfamiliar.

## 4. Cooling & hot-holding log (filled example)

| Item | Start temp | 2-hr check | Target (≤2 hrs) | 6-hr check | Target (≤6 hrs total) | Result |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Marinara base, 8 qt | 195°F | 68°F @ 12:05pm | ≤70°F by 2 hrs | 39°F @ 4:05pm | ≤41°F by 6 hrs total | Pass — into walk-in, labeled |
| Braised short rib, 6 qt | 190°F | 74°F @ 12:10pm | ≤70°F by 2 hrs | — | — | **Fail at 2-hr check** — over 70°F, re-ice bath immediately or discard per house policy |

**Rule:** miss the 2-hour checkpoint (135°F→70°F) and the whole cooling run is invalid regardless of what the 6-hour number reads later — restart with an ice bath/shallow-pan split, or discard. Never let a missed early checkpoint get "made up" by a good final number.

## 5. Allergen-station protocol (filled)

- **Dedicated station:** one board (labeled, not color-coded with the raw-protein set), one knife set, one set of prep containers, physically separated from raw-protein and produce boards — not just "the clean end of the same table."
- **Sequence when the station is shared with non-allergen prep during a slow period:** allergen items first on a freshly sanitized station, never last. Prep the tree-nut pesto before the regular basil pesto on the same equipment, not after.
- **Labeling:** every allergen-containing batch gets "CONTAINS: [allergen]" in addition to the standard prepped/discard dates — the Big 9 (milk, egg, wheat, soy, peanut, tree nut, fish, shellfish, sesame) covers current U.S. federal labeling scope.
- **Cross-contact check before starting:** station wiped and sanitized, gloves changed, cutting board flipped/swapped — not just wiped with the same rag used on the previous item.

## 6. Batch-marinating schedule (fallback preference order)

1. **Marinate under refrigeration, in a covered non-reactive container, portioned to the day's need** — the default.
2. **If tomorrow's batch must go in today because of walk-in space or staffing**, hold at ≤41°F and shorten the discard window accordingly (a marinade start today doesn't reset a 24-hour raw-protein clock that started at fabrication).
3. **Never room-temperature marinate** "to save time," even briefly — this is a danger-zone violation the moment it starts, not a risk that accrues gradually.
4. **Reserve unmarinated portions before any batch touches raw protein** if a future non-marinated use is anticipated — once marinade contacts raw protein, that marinade is done; it doesn't get reused as a fresh dip or serving sauce, and doesn't get "boiled down to be safe" as a workaround unless the house recipe explicitly calls for a cooked-through reduction step.
