# Vocabulary

Terms generalists conflate that a prep worker keeps sharply separate. Each: definition, a sentence a practitioner would actually say, and the common misuse.

## AP vs. EP weight

**AP** (as-purchased) is the weight of the ingredient as it arrives — bone, peel, trim, and all. **EP** (edible portion) is the usable weight after trimming, peeling, or portioning — what actually reaches the plate. Yield% is EP ÷ AP.

*Practitioner usage:* "We need 30 lb EP for the sandwich program — at 92% yield that's 33 lb AP, so pull four 10-lb cases, not three."

*Common misuse:* ordering AP weight equal to the EP need, which silently underbuys by exactly the trim loss every time.

## Yield percentage

The ratio of edible portion to as-purchased weight for a specific ingredient and cut, sourced from a house yield card or a published reference. It varies by ingredient, cut, and sometimes supplier — it is not a universal constant.

*Practitioner usage:* "This delivery's onions are trimming closer to 84% than our card's 89% — recheck before we order the same case count next week."

*Common misuse:* treating one ingredient's yield% as fixed forever, or applying one item's yield% to a different cut of the same ingredient (whole chicken at ~70% is not the same number as boneless skinless breast at ~92%).

## Par level vs. prep list

**Par level** is the target on-hand quantity a station should never fall below, generally sized off average or historical demand. The **prep list** is the specific quantity to produce for tomorrow's shift, driven by the covers forecast — the two agree only when tomorrow's demand matches the historical average the par was built on.

*Practitioner usage:* "Par on diced onion is 3 lb, but we've got a 220-cover Saturday forecast tomorrow — the prep list calls for 4 batches regardless of what par says is 'normal.'"

*Common misuse:* using par level alone to set tomorrow's prep quantity, which ignores day-of-week and event-driven demand swings the forecast would have caught.

## FIFO vs. FEFO

**FIFO** (first-in, first-out) rotates stock by the order it was received or prepped. **FEFO** (first-expired, first-out) rotates by actual discard date, which is usually the same as FIFO but diverges when a shorter-shelf-life item arrives after a longer one, or a receiving error mislabels something.

*Practitioner usage:* "Normally FIFO and FEFO agree, but this batch of seafood came in after the poultry and expires first — pull it front regardless of arrival order."

*Common misuse:* rotating strictly by shelf position (FIFO) without checking dates, which can put a shorter-dated item behind a longer-dated one if receiving order and expiration don't line up.

## TCS food

**Time/temperature control for safety** food — the current FDA Food Code term (replacing the older "potentially hazardous food," PHF) for items that support rapid bacterial growth if held in the danger zone: most proteins, cut produce, cooked grains and legumes, and dairy.

*Practitioner usage:* "That's TCS — it gets a discard date and a cold-holding check, the dry rub next to it doesn't."

*Common misuse:* applying the same handling rules to non-TCS shelf-stable items (dry goods, whole uncut produce) as to TCS items, which wastes labeling effort where it isn't needed and can mask which items actually carry risk.

## Danger zone

The temperature range, 41°F–135°F under the FDA Food Code, where bacterial growth on TCS food accelerates fastest. Cumulative time in this range — not one continuous stretch — is what counts against the 4-hour (or documented 6-hour) limit.

*Practitioner usage:* "It's been out twice today for a total of ninety minutes plus the forty minutes from this morning — that's cumulative, not reset by putting it back in the walk-in."

*Common misuse:* assuming the clock resets every time the item goes back into the cooler, when the limit is cumulative time out of range across the whole day.

## Mise en place

Literally "everything in its place" — the practice of having every cut, portion, and component staged and ready before service starts, distinct from the cooking itself.

*Practitioner usage:* "Mise is walked and signed off by 4:30 — if it's not staged by then, the sous chef needs to know before doors, not during the first ticket."

*Common misuse:* treating mise en place as a synonym for "prep in general" rather than the specific pre-service readiness check tied to a start-of-service deadline.

## Cross-contact vs. cross-contamination

**Cross-contact** specifically means an allergen transferring to a food that shouldn't contain it, even in trace amounts, via shared equipment or surfaces. **Cross-contamination** is the broader food-safety term for any harmful transfer (bacteria, pathogens) between foods or surfaces, allergen or not.

*Practitioner usage:* "That's a cross-contact risk, not just cross-contamination — the almond flour dust doesn't have to touch the food to matter, it just has to be on the same surface."

*Common misuse:* using "cross-contamination" for allergen incidents, which understates the zero-tolerance standard allergens require versus general sanitation risk.

## Cold chain / cold-holding

**Cold chain** is the unbroken sequence of refrigeration from receiving through storage, prep, and service. **Cold-holding** specifically refers to the temperature discipline (≤41°F) during active service or staging, the point in the chain most vulnerable to drift.

*Practitioner usage:* "The cold chain was fine until cold-holding on the line — that reach-in's been running at 44°F all shift."

*Common misuse:* checking walk-in temperatures as a proxy for the whole cold chain while ignoring line-side cold-holding units, which drift more often and faster.

## HACCP / CCP

**HACCP** (Hazard Analysis and Critical Control Points) is the overall food-safety system built around identifying where hazards can enter a process. A **CCP** (critical control point) is a specific step in that process — cooking temperature, cooling time, cold-holding — where a control limit must be met or the hazard isn't prevented.

*Practitioner usage:* "Cooling is a CCP for us — miss the 2-hour checkpoint and the whole batch fails the plan, not just gets a note."

*Common misuse:* treating HACCP as a binder that gets filled out after the fact rather than a live set of checkpoints that changes what a prep worker does in the moment (restart a cooling run, discard a batch) when a limit is missed.

## Batch cooking / batch prep

Producing a shelf-stable-for-its-category quantity of a component (sauce base, marinade, cooked grain) ahead of need, sized to a shelf-life window rather than a single day's demand — distinct from cooking to order.

*Practitioner usage:* "Marinara's batched at 8 quarts to cover five service days at current draw — that's a batch decision, not a per-ticket one."

*Common misuse:* sizing a batch to "use up the ingredient" or convenience of container size rather than to the item's actual shelf life and draw rate, which produces either waste or a batch that expires before it's used.

## Prep list vs. pull sheet

The **prep list** specifies what to produce (cut, portion, batch) from raw or partially processed ingredients. The **pull sheet** specifies what to retrieve from existing storage (walk-in, dry storage) to meet par or the prep list's raw-material need. They're sequential, not interchangeable documents.

*Practitioner usage:* "Pull sheet says we've got 12 lb of yesterday's diced onion on hand — subtract that from today's prep list before cutting more."

*Common misuse:* building the prep list without first checking the pull sheet/on-hand count, which double-produces an item already covered by existing stock.

## Sanitizing vs. cleaning

**Cleaning** removes visible soil and debris. **Sanitizing** reduces pathogens to a safe level using a chemical or heat method, and only works on a surface that's already been cleaned — sanitizer applied to a soiled surface doesn't sanitize it.

*Practitioner usage:* "It looks clean, but has it actually been through the sanitizer step, or just wiped?"

*Common misuse:* treating a visibly clean surface as sanitized, or treating a sanitized-smelling surface as clean if food debris was never actually removed first.

## Shelf life vs. discard date

**Shelf life** is the general duration a category of food remains safe under stated conditions (e.g., "raw poultry, 24–48 hours at ≤41°F"). **Discard date** is that shelf life applied to one specific batch's actual prep timestamp — a fact about that batch, not a category generality.

*Practitioner usage:* "Shelf life on this category is two days, but this batch's discard date is Sunday 2:15pm specifically, because it was prepped Friday 2:15pm."

*Common misuse:* labeling by category default without doing the specific date math for that batch's actual prep time, which produces labels that are directionally right but wrong by hours when it matters at a shift change.

## Covers forecast

The predicted number of guests (covers) for a service period, broken down by item mix% to project demand per menu item — the input every prep quantity should trace back to.

*Practitioner usage:* "Forecast's 240 covers tomorrow at last month's Friday mix — that's what tonight's prep list is built from, not tonight's actual count."

*Common misuse:* substituting today's actual covers or last week's prep quantity for tomorrow's forecast, which ignores known variance (day-of-week, weather, local events) the forecast is supposed to capture.
