# Red flags

Smell tests a lead prep worker or sous chef catches walking the line before service. Format for each: what it usually means, the first question to ask, and the data to pull.

## 1. Prep quantities match "what we always make" despite a forecast swing over 15%

**Usually means:** the prep list is being built from habit or yesterday's batch size instead of tomorrow's covers forecast — the gap surfaces as either a mid-service 86 or a large throwaway at close.

**First question:** "What's tomorrow's forecast and mix%, and does this quantity trace back to that math?"

**Data to pull:** covers forecast and item mix% for the shift in question; the prep list's stated quantity; the buffer % applied (or not applied).

## 2. A TCS item has no date label, or a handwritten label missing a discard date

**Usually means:** labeling got pushed to "the end of the shift" and skipped under time pressure, or whoever prepped it didn't know the house shelf-life default for that category.

**First question:** "When was this actually made, and what's the discard time using our shelf-life table?"

**Data to pull:** prep schedule/timestamps for the shift; the house TCS shelf-life table; whether this is a recurring gap for one prep worker or one station.

## 3. Cumulative time-out-of-temperature on a batch is untracked

**Usually means:** the batch moved between prep, holding, and re-prep without anyone logging when it left ≤41°F/≥135°F, so nobody can say whether it's inside the 4-hour (or documented 6-hour) cumulative limit.

**First question:** "Walk me through every point this batch left refrigeration or hot-holding today, with times."

**Data to pull:** cooling/hot-holding log for the batch; probe-thermometer spot checks; whether a time-as-a-public-health-control plan is in place and being logged against.

## 4. A cutting board or knife is shared across raw protein and produce without a visible wash/sanitize step

**Usually means:** time pressure during a rush pushed someone to skip the swap-and-sanitize step between ingredient classes, betting the visible surface looked clean enough.

**First question:** "Show me the last sanitize step on this board before this item went on it."

**Data to pull:** station assignment sheet (which board/color is assigned to which ingredient class); sanitizer log or test-strip check; whether this happened during a known rush window.

## 5. Yield% used for ordering hasn't been rechecked against a recent delivery

**Usually means:** the house yield card is stale relative to a supplier or seasonal change in trim, and every AP-to-EP conversion built on it is now quietly wrong in the same direction.

**First question:** "When did we last actually weigh EP against AP on this item, and does it still match the card?"

**Data to pull:** most recent trim-out weigh test if one exists; delivery specs/supplier changes in the last quarter; whether shortfalls or excess trim waste have been logged for this item recently.

## 6. An allergen-declared item was prepped on a shared station with no separation step

**Usually means:** the dedicated allergen station was occupied, busy, or skipped under rush pressure, and the item was prepped "at the clean end" of a shared table instead.

**First question:** "Was this made on the dedicated allergen station, and if not, what separation step happened before and after?"

**Data to pull:** station log for the shift; board/tool assignment; whether the item's label carries the required allergen flag.

## 7. Marinade is reused after contact with raw protein

**Usually means:** someone treated the marinade as a finishing sauce or dip to avoid waste, without recognizing that contact with raw protein ends its life as anything but a fully-cooked reduction called for by the recipe.

**First question:** "Did this marinade touch raw protein, and if so, why is it back in circulation?"

**Data to pull:** batch log for the marinade; recipe spec on whether a cooked-reduction step is actually called for; whether a fresh, unmarinated portion was reserved before contact.

## 8. FIFO is violated in the walk-in — newer product in front of older

**Usually means:** rotation during a busy receiving day got skipped, or a receiving error mislabeled a shorter-dated item as if it were the older stock.

**First question:** "What are the prep/receipt dates on the front-shelf item versus what's behind it?"

**Data to pull:** date labels on both items; receiving log for the day in question; whether this is a one-off or a pattern for a specific shelf/station.

## 9. A batch's cooling check relies on "it felt cool enough" instead of a probe reading

**Usually means:** the 2-hour and 6-hour cooling checkpoints are being estimated rather than measured, which invalidates the cooling record even if the food happens to be safe.

**First question:** "What did the probe actually read at the 2-hour checkpoint, logged at what time?"

**Data to pull:** cooling log entries with timestamps and temps; whether a probe thermometer was available and calibrated at the station.

## 10. Same item thrown out at close repeatedly with no adjustment to the prep list

**Usually means:** the forecast multiplier for that item is set too high and nobody has fed the waste pattern back into the prep math — this is a recurring cost being treated as a one-off each time.

**First question:** "How many days out of the last two weeks did we throw this out at close, and by how much?"

**Data to pull:** waste log by item and date; the prep list's stated quantity for those days; the forecast those quantities were supposedly based on.

## 11. Same item 86'd mid-service repeatedly on the same day-of-week

**Usually means:** the opposite of #10 — the forecast multiplier is set too low for that day-of-week pattern, and the shortfall is being absorbed as "bad luck" instead of a math correction.

**First question:** "Is this the third Friday in a row we've run out of this by 7pm?"

**Data to pull:** 86-board log by item, date, and time; covers forecast accuracy for that day-of-week over recent weeks; whether the buffer % applied is appropriate for this item's variance.

## 12. Raw poultry or seafood prepped on the same shift as ready-to-eat items with no station reset between

**Usually means:** the prep sequence wasn't planned to put raw work at the end (or on a fully separate station), so a ready-to-eat item got prepped in a space that still carried raw-protein risk.

**First question:** "What order did today's prep happen in, and was the station reset between the raw and ready-to-eat runs?"

**Data to pull:** prep sequence/schedule for the shift; station sanitize log between tasks; which board/tools were used for each.
