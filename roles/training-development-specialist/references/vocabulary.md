# Training and Development Specialist working vocabulary

Terms a specialist uses precisely and generalists conflate. Format: definition → how a practitioner says it → common misuse.

**Terminal objective vs. enabling objective** — A terminal objective states the overall condition/behavior/criterion the whole course must produce; enabling objectives are the smaller, gradable sub-behaviors (usually one per module or chunk) that build toward it.
In use: "The terminal objective is the full 3-tier decision under time pressure; EO2 just covers correctly identifying what documentation a tier-2 case requires."
Misuse: writing only a terminal objective and treating each module's content outline as if it were an enabling objective, when it's actually just a topic list with no gradable behavior attached.

**Needs assessment vs. needs analysis (Rossett's three levels)** — The fieldwork (interviews, task analysis, performance data) that establishes organizational, task, and person-level gap data, distinct from simply accepting a stakeholder's description of what training is needed.
In use: "We haven't done the needs assessment yet — right now all we have is the VP's description of the problem."
Misuse: treating a single stakeholder conversation as the needs assessment, skipping the task-level (correct-performance) and person-level (actual gap size) data gathering.

**Action mapping** — Cathy Moore's process of deriving training content from a business goal through required on-job behaviors and practice activities, working backward to the minimum information needed — as opposed to starting from a topic and adding content.
In use: "Action-mapped this back from the escalation-rate goal — turns out only two of the eight 'best practices' the SME listed actually change the target behavior."
Misuse: using "action mapping" loosely to mean any content-planning exercise, when its defining feature is starting from a measurable business goal and observable behaviors, not from a list of topics or knowledge points.

**ADDIE vs. SAM** — ADDIE (Analyze, Design, Develop, Implement, Evaluate) is a linear, gated instructional-design process with sign-off between phases; SAM (Successive Approximation Model) is an iterative prototype-review-revise cycle that surfaces objective or format mistakes earlier, at the cost of less phase-by-phase documentation.
In use: "This is compliance-critical, so we're running full ADDIE with documented sign-off at each phase, not a SAM sprint."
Misuse: assuming ADDIE is simply "the standard process" that should govern every project regardless of stakes or timeline, rather than a choice made against the project's regulatory and timeline requirements.

**SCORM vs. xAPI (Tin Can API)** — SCORM is the older LMS packaging/tracking standard (1.2 or 2004) reporting coarse completion and score data to the LMS itself; xAPI reports granular interaction statements (individual choices, simulation steps, even off-platform activity) to a Learning Record Store (LRS).
In use: "SCORM will give us pass/fail on the module; if we need branch-choice-level data for the L2 evaluation, that's xAPI to an LRS, not SCORM."
Misuse: assuming any LMS-tracked course automatically produces the granular interaction data needed for a Level 2 evaluation instrument — SCORM alone typically only gives completion status and an overall score, not per-item or per-branch response data.

**Completion status (LMS)** — The field an LMS reports when a learner finishes a course, which can be configured to trigger on merely viewing all content or on passing a scored assessment — the trigger condition, not the word "completed," determines what the metric actually measures.
In use: "Completion here is configured to trigger on viewed-all-slides, so a 92% completion rate tells us nothing about whether they passed the knowledge check."
Misuse: treating "completed" as synonymous with "learned" or "can perform the task," when the underlying trigger may be nothing more than navigating to the last screen.

**Kirkpatrick Level 3 (behavior) vs. Level 2 (learning)** — Level 2 measures whether the learner can demonstrate the target knowledge/skill in the training environment (a scored assessment); Level 3 measures whether that behavior actually shows up on the job afterward, typically observed with a delay (30-90 days).
In use: "She passed the Level 2 knowledge check at 9/10, but the Level 3 observation at day 30 showed she's still escalating cases that don't need it — something's not transferring."
Misuse: reporting a strong Level 2 pass rate as evidence the training "worked," when Level 2 only confirms the learner could perform in the assessment environment, not that the behavior transferred to the job.

**Chunking (working-memory limit)** — Organizing content into groups of roughly 4-7 discrete items per unit, based on limits on how much new information can be held in working memory at once, rather than however a source document happens to be structured.
In use: "The SME's policy doc lists 14 exceptions in one paragraph — we're chunking that into three enabling objectives of about five each."
Misuse: treating chunking as just "breaking up long text into shorter paragraphs" for readability, rather than a limit on the number of new discrete items a learner can process and retain per unit.

**Forgetting curve / spaced reinforcement** — The empirically observed rapid decay of unreinforced learning (roughly two-thirds gone within a day), used to justify building spaced retrieval-practice or job-aid backup into a course design rather than relying on a single session.
In use: "Given the forgetting-curve decay, we're adding a 5-question spaced-retrieval email at day 3, day 10, and day 30 instead of assuming the single session sticks."
Misuse: citing "the forgetting curve" as a vague justification for "more training" in general, rather than as the specific reason to design spaced reinforcement or job-aid support into the original build.

**Bloom's Taxonomy level** — The cognitive complexity an objective and its assessment item are pitched at (remember, understand, apply, analyze, evaluate, create), used to check that an assessment actually tests the level the objective claims to require.
In use: "The objective calls for 'apply' — correctly classify a scenario — so a multiple-choice 'name the three tiers' question is testing the wrong level; it needs a scenario-classification item."
Misuse: writing an objective with an "apply" or "analyze" verb but assessing it with a recall-level (multiple-choice fact) item, which measures a lower cognitive level than the objective specifies.

**Instructional fidelity** — How closely a practice activity or delivery format mirrors the real conditions and stakes of the actual on-job task, as opposed to how polished or content-complete the material looks.
In use: "The eLearning branch logic covers the decision rule, but it has zero instructional fidelity for the live-customer-delivery part — that's what the role-play drill is for."
Misuse: judging a module's quality by production values (graphics, narration polish) rather than by how well its practice activity matches the real task's conditions.

**Delivery format (ILT / eLearning / blended)** — The mode content is delivered in, chosen against the objective's required practice fidelity, audience size/distribution, timeline, and per-learner cost — not a default house style or "whatever we built last time."
In use: "This objective set is blended: eLearning for the decision-rule knowledge, ILT role-play for the live-delivery component — not one or the other for the whole course."
Misuse: selecting a single delivery format for an entire course regardless of how the underlying objectives differ in required fidelity, rather than mixing formats per objective.

**Job aid** — A standalone reference (checklist, decision table, quick card) designed to be consulted at the moment of task performance, distinct from training content, which is meant to be internalized and recalled without a reference in hand.
In use: "For the sub-tier documentation list, a laminated job aid at the register solves it — that's not something we need learners to memorize."
Misuse: building a full training module to convey information that's actually better delivered as a point-of-use reference, or conversely, relying on a job aid for a skill that genuinely needs to be internalized (e.g., a live de-escalation behavior that can't be looked up mid-conversation).
