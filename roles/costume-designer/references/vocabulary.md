# Costume Designer — Vocabulary

### Costume plot
The master tracking document listing every costume piece, which actor/character uses it, when it's worn, and where it goes before and after each scene or change.
**In use:** "The plot still shows Roxie's Look 3 entering from stage left — that changed last week, get it updated before the dresser runs it wrong."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a one-time document finished at the top of the process, rather than a living record that must be revised every time blocking or a build decision changes.

### Quick-rig
Construction or modification specifically engineered to speed up a costume change — swapping buttons for magnets or Velcro, adding a hidden zipper, or building a convertible underlayer.
**In use:** "This gown needs a quick-rig — magnetic closures under the boning, not the house zip, or we'll never hit an 11-second change."
**Common misuse:** Applied reflexively to every change flagged as "fast" rather than reserved for changes that actually failed a timed test — over-rigging burns shop hours on garments that didn't need it.

### Cutter/draper
The shop role that translates a design sketch into an actual pattern, either by flat-pattern drafting or draping fabric directly on a form or the actor's body.
**In use:** "Send the sketch to the cutter/draper with the fabric swatch attached — they need the drape weight to pick a pattern approach."
**Common misuse:** Conflated with a stitcher — the cutter/draper builds the pattern and often cuts the fabric; the stitcher executes the sewing from that pattern.

### First hand
The senior stitcher in a costume shop who assembles the most complex or highest-priority garments and often supervises other stitchers on a build.
**In use:** "Put the beaded gown with the first hand — that's not a piece to hand to a junior stitcher this close to tech."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to be an assistant-level title because of the word "first" in a generic sense, rather than the senior execution role it actually is.

### Wardrobe supervisor
The person who runs the costume department once a show is in performance — managing the running crew, maintenance, laundry, and repairs, distinct from the designer who conceives the looks.
**In use:** "Once we open, the wardrobe supervisor owns the plot — the designer isn't in the building every night."
**Common misuse:** Confused with the costume designer's role during the design and build process, when the wardrobe supervisor's authority is specifically over the show's run, not the original creative decisions.

### Dyer/painter (breakdown artist)
The shop specialist who dyes fabric to a target color and "breaks down" (distresses, ages, or paints) a costume to look worn, dirty, or period-accurate.
**In use:** "That coat needs a breakdown pass — grease at the cuffs, dust on the hem — before it goes on stage as a laborer's costume."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a cosmetic afterthought scheduled at the end of the build, rather than a process that needs its own shop-hour allocation and test swatches before committing a full piece.

### Tech table
The rehearsal stage (typically during technical rehearsals) where lighting, sound, and costume elements are checked together under actual performance conditions for the first time.
**In use:** "Hold the cut on that fabric until we see it at the tech table — shop light isn't telling us the truth about that color under the wash."
**Common misuse:** Skipped or treated as optional for costume approval, when it's the actual approval gate — shop-light approval is provisional, not final.

### Dye lot / bolt
The specific production batch of dyed or woven fabric; pieces cut from different lots or bolts of the "same" color or fabric can show a visible mismatch under certain lighting even when swatch cards look identical.
**In use:** "Check the bolt number on the vest fabric against the jacket — if they're different lots, we need a side-by-side under stage wash before committing."
**Common misuse:** Assumed that matching a color name or swatch code guarantees a match; lot-to-lot variation is common even within the same nominal color.

### Understudy/swing track
The costume assignments built and fitted for an actor's cover (understudy or swing), which must function within the same plot and change timing as the principal's track.
**In use:** "The swing track for three ensemble roles needs its own fitting slot — don't assume it inherits the principal's fit."
**Common misuse:** Deprioritized on the build schedule as a "just in case" item, then left unfinished or unfitted close to opening when it's actually a same-plot obligation, not an optional extra.

### Build vs. rent vs. buy
The per-garment sourcing decision between constructing a costume from scratch in the shop, sourcing it from a costume rental house, or purchasing a ready-made or off-the-rack piece and altering it.
**In use:** "Look 2 has no timing constraint and a house has our size in stock — that's a rent, not a build."
**Common misuse:** Decided once for an entire show ("we're a build house this season") rather than per garment against timing, budget, and fit constraints — a rental house's no-alteration contract terms alone can force a build regardless of house policy.

### Contingency line
The reserved portion of a costume budget set aside for fit problems, timing failures, or late fixes discovered during fittings and tech, as distinct from funding for new scope.
**In use:** "That's a scope addition, not a contingency spend — it needs its own line, the contingency is for the fixes we haven't found yet."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a flexible pool for adding pieces the designer wants late in the process, leaving nothing available when an actual late-discovered fit or timing problem needs funding.

### Breakdown
The process of aging, distressing, or otherwise altering a costume's finish (fading, staining, tearing, dust) to match a character's circumstances, as distinct from the garment's original construction.
**In use:** "This is a new-off-the-bolt fabric — it needs a breakdown pass before it reads as three years of wear."
**Common misuse:** Confused with alteration (fit changes) — breakdown is a finish/surface treatment applied after construction, done by the dyer/painter, not a fit adjustment done by the cutter/draper.
