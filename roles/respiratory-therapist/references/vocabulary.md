# Respiratory Therapist — Vocabulary

### Rapid shallow breathing index (RSBI)
Respiratory rate divided by tidal volume in liters, calculated at the end of a spontaneous breathing trial; a value under 105 is the most widely cited single predictor of successful weaning.
**In use:** "RSBI came out to 68 at the end of the trial — well under 105, patient's a good candidate for extubation."
**Common misuse:** Treating RSBI as sufficient on its own — it was derived and validated as one input alongside the bedside exam, and an anxious or heavily sedated patient can produce a misleading number in either direction.

### P/F ratio (PaO2/FiO2)
The ratio of arterial oxygen partial pressure to the fraction of inspired oxygen, used to grade the severity of hypoxemia and stage ARDS under the Berlin definition (mild 200–300, moderate 100–200, severe <100).
**In use:** "P/F is 105 on 0.6 FiO2 — that's moderate ARDS, not mild, and changes how aggressively we titrate PEEP."
**Common misuse:** Calculating it without confirming the FiO2 actually being delivered (versus the number dialed in on a leaky or high-flow-diluted device), which produces a P/F ratio that doesn't reflect the patient's real oxygenation status.

### PEEP (positive end-expiratory pressure)
The pressure maintained in the airway at the end of exhalation, set by the ventilator to keep alveoli open and improve oxygenation.
**In use:** "We're following the ARDSNet lower table — at FiO2 0.6 that pairs with a PEEP of 10."
**Common misuse:** Assuming the displayed PEEP number equals the total pressure in the lung at end-exhalation — in obstructive disease with air trapping, the true (auto-)PEEP can be substantially higher than the set value.

### Auto-PEEP (intrinsic PEEP / breath stacking)
Unintended, unmeasured positive pressure remaining in the alveoli at end-exhalation because the next breath begins before the previous one fully exhales, common in obstructive lung disease or high respiratory rates.
**In use:** "Peak pressure's climbing but plateau's flat and now he's hypotensive — let's run an expiratory hold and check for auto-PEEP before reaching for fluids."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a hemodynamic problem on the ventilator is cardiac or volume-related without first ruling out auto-PEEP, which reduces venous return by a completely different mechanism and requires a ventilator change, not a fluid bolus, as the fix.

### Plateau pressure (Pplat)
The pressure measured during a brief pause at end-inspiration with no gas flow, reflecting alveolar distending pressure and lung/chest-wall compliance, distinct from the peak pressure that includes airway resistance.
**In use:** "Peak's high but plateau's only 24 — that's a resistance problem, not a compliance problem, so I'm looking at the tubing and airway, not the tidal volume."
**Common misuse:** Reading peak pressure and plateau pressure as interchangeable — a high peak with a normal plateau points to airway resistance (bronchospasm, secretions, kinked tube), while a high plateau points to a lung or chest-wall compliance problem, and they call for different fixes.

### Predicted body weight (PBW)
A height- and sex-based formula for estimating lung size, used to calculate lung-protective tidal volume instead of actual body weight.
**In use:** "PBW comes out to 73 kg from his height — that's what the 6 mL/kg tidal volume target is based on, not his actual 140 kg."
**Common misuse:** Substituting actual body weight for PBW when calculating tidal volume, especially in obese patients, which can nearly double the intended lung-protective volume and drive over-distention injury.

### Permissive hypercapnia
A deliberate strategy of tolerating an elevated PaCO2 and lower blood pH to keep tidal volume and plateau pressure within lung-protective limits, rather than increasing ventilation to normalize CO2.
**In use:** "pH's at 7.27 but we're prioritizing keeping plateau under 30 — that's permissive hypercapnia, not a problem to fix by cranking up the rate."
**Common misuse:** Treating any elevated CO2 as an emergency requiring immediate correction — within an accepted pH floor (commonly cited around 7.15–7.20, per local protocol), the elevated CO2 is the intended trade-off, not a sign the strategy has failed.

### Spontaneous breathing trial (SBT)
A timed period (typically 30–120 minutes) during which a ventilated patient breathes with minimal ventilator support (T-piece, low CPAP, or low pressure support) to test readiness for extubation.
**In use:** "He's on hour one of the SBT, tolerating it well — RSBI at the end will tell us if we're pulling the tube today."
**Common misuse:** Confusing "tolerating pressure support" with "passing an SBT" — pressure support can do enough of the work of breathing to mask a patient who isn't actually ready, which is why the trial uses minimal, not moderate, support.

### Cuff leak test
A pre-extubation check that deflates the endotracheal tube cuff and measures the exhaled tidal volume around the tube, used to screen for laryngeal edema that could cause post-extubation stridor.
**In use:** "Leak volume came back at 80 mL on a 400 mL tidal volume — that's under our 110 mL threshold, so we're pre-treating with steroids before pulling the tube."
**Common misuse:** Skipping the test in a low-risk patient and also in a high-risk one — the test isn't needed for every extubation, but omitting it in a patient with a traumatic or prolonged intubation removes the one screen that predicts a specific, preventable failure mode.

### Dead space (physiologic)
The portion of each breath that doesn't participate in gas exchange (either anatomic — conducting airways — or alveolar, from ventilated but underperfused lung units).
**In use:** "Dropping the tidal volume that much shrinks the alveolar-ventilation fraction because the anatomic dead space stays fixed — expect the CO2 to climb a bit."
**Common misuse:** Assuming minute ventilation and alveolar ventilation move together proportionally — as tidal volume shrinks toward the fixed anatomic dead space, a larger fraction of every breath stops contributing to gas exchange, so CO2 can rise even when minute ventilation is held constant.

### Ventilator dyssynchrony
A mismatch between the patient's own respiratory effort/timing and the ventilator's delivered breath (trigger, flow, or cycling mismatch), visible on the pressure or flow waveform.
**In use:** "Waveform's showing double-triggering — he's dyssynchronous with the current inspiratory time, not agitated for no reason."
**Common misuse:** Treating dyssynchrony as a sedation problem to be solved with more sedative before checking whether a ventilator-setting mismatch (trigger sensitivity, flow rate, inspiratory time) is the actual cause.

### Hypoxic drive (the "knocked-out drive" model)
The older, oversimplified explanation for oxygen-induced hypercapnia in COPD, attributing it to suppression of a hypoxia-driven respiratory drive; current understanding attributes most of the effect to worsened ventilation/perfusion mismatch and the Haldane effect.
**In use:** "It's not really that we knocked out his drive to breathe — releasing hypoxic vasoconstriction worsened his V/Q mismatch, that's the bigger driver of the CO2 rise."
**Common misuse:** Citing "hypoxic drive" as the primary mechanism and therefore starving an acutely hypoxic COPD patient of needed oxygen — regardless of mechanism, acute severe hypoxia is treated first, with CO2 managed afterward.

### Static compliance
A measure of lung and chest-wall distensibility calculated as tidal volume divided by driving pressure (plateau pressure minus PEEP), reflecting the elastic properties of the respiratory system at no-flow conditions.
**In use:** "Compliance dropped from 40 to 20 mL/cmH2O overnight — that's worsening lung mechanics, not a tubing problem."
**Common misuse:** Confusing static compliance (measured at a pause, no flow) with dynamic compliance (measured during flow, which also reflects airway resistance) — a falling dynamic compliance with stable static compliance points to a resistance problem, not a true lung-stiffness change.
