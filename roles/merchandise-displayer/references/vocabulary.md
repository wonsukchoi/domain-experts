# Merchandise Displayer — Vocabulary

### Capture rate
The percentage of passing foot traffic that enters the store or engages with a display, computed as entries ÷ passing traffic over the same time window.
**In use:** "Concept B's capture rate is 10%, up from 8% on concept A — that's a real lift, not just more people walking by."
**Common misuse:** Treating raw entry-count increases as proof a display worked, without checking whether passing traffic also changed — a busier week can inflate entries with no display improvement at all.

### Buy zone
The 57–63 inch shelf-height band (roughly shoulder-to-eye level) that generates disproportionately higher sales than shelf space above or below it.
**In use:** "Move the promoted SKU into the buy zone before the reset — it's currently sitting in the stoop zone at 24 inches."
**Common misuse:** Assuming "eye level" means the very top of a fixture; the buy zone is lower and narrower than most people's intuition.

### Reach zone
Shelf space from roughly 30–57 inches, below the buy zone — accessible without bending or stretching, but converting at a lower rate than the buy zone itself.
**In use:** "Secondary items go in the reach zone; the buy zone is reserved for what we most need to move."
**Common misuse:** Conflating reach zone with buy zone — they're adjacent but perform differently, and treating all "accessible" shelf space as equally valuable understates the buy zone's premium.

### Planogram
A diagram specifying exact SKU placement on a fixture, used as the standard staff restock against.
**In use:** "We're auditing planogram compliance in the seasonal aisle this week — last check it was at 78%."
**Common misuse:** Treating a planogram as a one-time setup instruction rather than an ongoing compliance standard that needs periodic re-auditing.

### Planogram compliance
The percentage of SKUs on a fixture that match their specified planogram location, measured by physical audit.
**In use:** "Compliance dropped below 85% on the endcap zone — that's our trigger for a targeted re-set."
**Common misuse:** Reporting compliance at the whole-store level when the real signal is zone-by-zone — an 85% storewide average can hide one zone at 40% and the rest at 95%.

### Adjacency
The deliberate placement of a complementary item near a primary item to prompt an additional purchase, judged by whether it sits within the shopper's physical reach.
**In use:** "Put the belts in adjacency with the pants fixture — within reach, not across the aisle."
**Common misuse:** Placing a "related" display anywhere in the same department and calling it adjacency; true adjacency requires arm's-reach proximity to actually lift attachment rate.

### Attachment rate
The percentage of primary-item purchases that also include a cross-merchandised complementary item.
**In use:** "Attachment rate on the belt display dropped after we moved it away from the pants fixture."
**Common misuse:** Expecting attachment rate to hold steady regardless of physical placement — it's highly sensitive to proximity, not just product pairing logic.

### Endcap
The fixture at the end of an aisle, typically used for promotional or seasonal groupings and requiring more setup time than a standard shelf due to signage and multi-SKU coordination.
**In use:** "Budget 45 minutes per endcap for this reset, not the 15 we use for standard shelves."
**Common misuse:** Estimating endcap reset time the same as a standard shelf — endcaps routinely take 2–3x longer due to signage swaps and promotional groupings.

### Reset
A scheduled, store-wide or zone-specific re-merchandising event, typically tied to a season or promotional calendar and executed inside a fixed labor-hour/closure-window budget.
**In use:** "The fall reset is scheduled for next Tuesday overnight — crew of 3, 7-hour window."
**Common misuse:** Treating "reset" as informal ongoing restocking; a reset specifically means a planned, comprehensive re-merchandising event against a plan, not routine shelf tidying.

### Sell-through
The percentage of stocked units of a SKU that have sold within a given period — a buyer's metric, but one that informs which SKUs a displayer prioritizes for buy-zone placement.
**In use:** "The buyer flagged this SKU's sell-through is lagging — that's why it's getting the buy-zone slot this reset."
**Common misuse:** Treating sell-through as a displayer-owned metric; it's tracked and interpreted by the buyer, and the displayer's job is to act on what the buyer flags, not independently diagnose sell-through problems.

### Visual merchandising
The discipline of presenting merchandise — fixture layout, windows, signage, lighting — to maximize sales of an already-selected assortment.
**In use:** "Visual merchandising decides how the fall line is shown; the buyer already decided what's in it."
**Common misuse:** Using "visual merchandising" and "buying" interchangeably — they're sequential, distinct functions with different accountabilities (what to stock vs. how to present it).

### Foot traffic
The count of people passing a location (storefront, department, window), measured independently of whether they enter — the denominator in capture rate.
**In use:** "Passing traffic was actually down 4% during the new concept's test window, which means the entry-count increase understates its real effect."
**Common misuse:** Using entry counts alone as a traffic proxy — entries are a function of both passing traffic and the display's capture rate, and conflating the two hides which one actually changed.
