# Red flags

Smell tests a dispatcher, base owner, or senior driver catches early. Format for each: what it usually means, the first question to ask, and the data to pull before anyone reacts.

### 1. Shift utilization below ~35% with no positioning-strategy change logged

**Usually means:** the driver is cruising for street hails through a stretch of the shift where app-matching or feeder-point staging would convert deadhead miles into revenue miles, and nobody has adjusted the approach.

**First question:** "What fraction of tonight's miles had a passenger aboard, and what changed about where you were driving during the low stretch?"

**Data to pull:** odometer start/end and per-trip mileage log for the shift; timestamps of drop-offs versus next pickup to locate the deadhead gaps.

### 2. Metered fare charged on a route matching a filed flat-rate zone (or the reverse)

**Usually means:** the driver assumed the flat rate applies symmetrically in both directions, or forgot to check the filed tariff before starting the meter.

**First question:** "Does the filed flat-rate tariff cover this exact origin, destination, and direction — and if so, why did the meter run instead?"

**Data to pull:** the local flat-rate tariff filing; the trip receipt showing which fare type was charged; any passenger complaint tied to the trip.

### 3. Partition, camera, or GPS logging disabled or obstructed

**Usually means:** the driver traded a real safety and evidentiary control for a marginal convenience (faster cash handling, perceived passenger comfort) without weighing what it removes in a dispute or incident.

**First question:** "When was this last confirmed active, and what's the operational reason it's off right now?"

**Data to pull:** vehicle equipment inspection log; GPS/camera uptime log for the shift; any incident reports where the equipment would have been the record of what happened.

### 4. Passenger requests a destination change to an isolated area late at night, unlogged

**Usually means:** the trip has drifted outside what dispatch or the app has on record, which matters if anything goes wrong afterward — there's no longer a matched, logged endpoint for the ride.

**First question:** "Was the destination change relayed to dispatch or logged in the app, and does the driver have a read on why the passenger wants that specific location?"

**Data to pull:** original versus final destination on the trip record; dispatch communication log for the trip; time and location of the change relative to pickup.

### 5. Same low-demand zone or hour cruised repeatedly despite a documented dead pattern

**Usually means:** the driver is running on habit or an outdated mental map of where fares are, rather than current dispatch/demand data for that time and place.

**First question:** "What's this zone's actual average wait-to-hail time at this hour over the last month, and why is it still the default move?"

**Data to pull:** trailing 30-day fare pickup density by zone and hour; the driver's own utilization trend during that recurring stretch.

### 6. Driver reports a shift's profitability using gross fares only

**Usually means:** fuel and lease cost against total miles driven (including deadhead) haven't been netted out, so the reported number overstates real profit, sometimes substantially.

**First question:** "What were total miles driven, and what did fuel and the lease actually cost against that — not just against the fares taken?"

**Data to pull:** shift odometer total; fuel receipts or estimated consumption; lease/gate fee for the shift; gross fares plus tips.

### 7. Repeated passenger complaints about being overcharged on a specific route

**Usually means:** either a real flat-rate-vs-metered error happening on that route, or a passenger misunderstanding a legitimate flat rate as an overcharge — both need the filed tariff pulled to settle.

**First question:** "Does this route match a filed flat rate, and what fare type does the trip record show was actually charged?"

**Data to pull:** the local flat-rate tariff filing for that route; a sample of recent trip receipts on that route; complaint text and dates.

### 8. Vehicle inspection or driver license/permit renewal lapsed

**Usually means:** a renewal deadline was missed or tracked informally rather than against the actual licensing-authority calendar, putting every trip during the lapse at legal and insurance risk.

**First question:** "What's the exact expiration date on file with the licensing authority, and when was the renewal actually filed?"

**Data to pull:** licensing authority's renewal record; vehicle inspection certificate date; any trips logged after the lapse date.

### 9. Driver declines a string of short-distance hails or fares

**Usually means:** the driver is optimizing for trip length in a way that crosses into a refusal pattern, which is both a lost-utilization problem (short trips still count as revenue miles) and a regulatory complaint risk in most licensing regimes.

**First question:** "What's the pattern in the declined trips — distance, zone, time of day — and is this a documented refusal rate the licensing authority would flag?"

**Data to pull:** trip-decline log with stated reasons; local rule on refusal/discrimination complaints; the driver's utilization trend to see whether declining short trips is actually helping or hurting it.

### 10. A driver treats every late-night hail as routine after a string of uneventful ones

**Usually means:** the hail-acceptance safety screen has quietly stopped running because nothing's gone wrong yet, not because the risk factors have actually changed.

**First question:** "Walk me through the last hail you accepted after midnight — hour, location, destination-before-doors-locked, passenger presentation — was the screen actually applied?"

**Data to pull:** trip log timestamps and locations for late-night pickups; any near-miss or incident reports from the same driver or zone.
