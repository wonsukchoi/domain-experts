# Driver/Sales Worker — Vocabulary

### Facing
One unit-width of shelf allocated to a single SKU, stacked front-to-back; a SKU's total shelf presence is measured in facing count, not linear inches alone.
**In use:** "We're down to one facing on Double-Chip — that's the one losing volume, not Original."
**Common misuse:** Treating facing count and linear shelf space as interchangeable when depth (units stacked front-to-back) differs by SKU, which changes actual on-shelf capacity at the same facing count.

### Planogram
The retailer's or category captain's diagram specifying exact shelf position, facing count, and adjacency for every SKU in a category, refreshed at each reset.
**In use:** "Check the current planogram before you assume last cycle's facings are still accurate."
**Common misuse:** Assuming the physical shelf matches the planogram on file without checking — stores frequently drift from the documented plan between formal resets.

### Share of shelf
A SKU's or vendor's facings divided by total facings in the category set at that account.
**In use:** "We're at 15% share of shelf here but 34% share of the category dollars — that gap is the argument for more space, not less."
**Common misuse:** Treating share of shelf as a fixed entitlement rather than a negotiable position that should track share of category sales.

### Space-to-sales curve
The documented, concave relationship between facing count and unit sales for a given SKU or line — each additional facing adds less volume than the last.
**In use:** "Don't assume a facing cut costs an even third of volume — pull the curve and use the marginal number."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a linear relationship (losing one of three facings costs exactly a third of sales) instead of checking the account's actual marginal-facing contribution.

### Par level
The target on-hand/on-order quantity for a SKU at an account, sized to expected sell-through and shelf capacity between visits.
**In use:** "Par's coming down from 8 cases to 7 now that we're at two facings instead of three."
**Common misuse:** Setting par once and carrying it forward indefinitely instead of recalculating it whenever facing count, seasonality, or sell-through shifts.

### Stale / code date
Product past its manufacturer-assigned freshness or sell-by date, pulled from the shelf and credited back to the account (or written off) rather than sold.
**In use:** "Pull anything within three days of code before it becomes a stale credit instead of a sale."
**Common misuse:** Treating a stale credit purely as a bookkeeping entry rather than a signal that the order or par level is miscalibrated for that account's actual sell-through.

### Net sales (as a commission base)
Gross delivered value minus stale, return, and other credits — the figure most DSD commission structures are actually calculated against, not gross delivered value.
**In use:** "Gross looked fine this week, but the stale credit ate into net — that's what my commission's based on."
**Common misuse:** Tracking only gross delivered revenue as a personal performance measure and being surprised when commission comes in lower than expected.

### Cost-per-stop
The fully loaded cost (drive time, service time, fuel share, vehicle cost) of servicing one account visit, independent of what that stop sells.
**In use:** "This stop's order barely clears three times its cost-per-stop — it's a candidate to fold into another day."
**Common misuse:** Evaluating a route only by total revenue or total stops, without checking whether any individual stop's revenue clears its own service cost.

### Revenue-per-stop
Average order value at a given account, compared against that account's cost-per-stop to judge whether the stop is still worth its place on the route.
**In use:** "Revenue-per-stop's been trending down here for three cycles — worth a route-frequency review before it's a bigger problem."
**Common misuse:** Confusing a high-revenue stop with a profitable one without checking the cost side of the same calculation.

### Cut-in
The process of getting a new SKU authorized and added to an account's planogram, distinct from simply pitching the account on carrying it.
**In use:** "Product's a good fit, but it still needs a formal cut-in before it can go on the shelf."
**Common misuse:** Treating a verbal agreement from a store manager as equivalent to an authorized cut-in, when the chain's category buyer may not have approved it.

### Slotting fee
A payment from a vendor to a retailer for guaranteed shelf placement, typically negotiated at the account or chain level, not by the route rep.
**In use:** "That endcap isn't free — it's carrying a slotting fee the district negotiated for the promo period."
**Common misuse:** Assuming any favorable placement was earned through the rep's relationship alone, when it may be a paid arrangement with its own term and renewal condition.

### Backhaul / reverse logistics
The return flow of stale product, damaged goods, or returnable equipment (trays, racks) from the account back to the warehouse or plant.
**In use:** "Log the stale pull now — it rides back on the same trip as backhaul, not as a separate pickup."
**Common misuse:** Treating stale pulls and equipment returns as an afterthought at the end of a route rather than a routine part of each stop.

### Fill rate
The percentage of an order actually delivered against what was ordered, tracked at the account or SKU level.
**In use:** "Fill rate's been under 95% on this SKU for three cycles straight — that's a warehouse allocation problem, not my ordering."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a fill-rate shortfall reflects the rep's own order-writing rather than checking whether the shortfall originates upstream at the warehouse or plant.

### OTIF (on-time, in-full)
A combined delivery-performance metric — did the delivery arrive on the scheduled window and complete the full ordered quantity — commonly used in chain vendor scorecards.
**In use:** "Chain's OTIF scorecard dinged us last month — on-time was fine, it was the in-full side that missed."
**Common misuse:** Treating on-time and in-full as a single pass/fail outcome instead of diagnosing which half actually failed.

### Deadhead (route context)
Time or distance spent traveling between stops or back to the depot with no service being performed — the DSD-route analogue of empty miles between fares.
**In use:** "Resequencing these two stops cuts fifteen minutes of deadhead between them."
**Common misuse:** Treating deadhead as unavoidable overhead rather than a sequencing variable that a route review can actually reduce.

### Scan-based trading (SBT)
An arrangement where the retailer doesn't take ownership of (or pay for) inventory until it scans at the register, shifting inventory-carrying risk to the vendor/rep rather than the store.
**In use:** "This account's on scan-based trading — we don't get paid until it rings up, so overordering here is a direct cost to us, not the store."
**Common misuse:** Assuming payment terms are the same as a standard invoiced account when a chain has actually moved that account to scan-based trading.
