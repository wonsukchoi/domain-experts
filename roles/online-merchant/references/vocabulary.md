### Net contribution margin (per SKU)
The profit remaining per unit after subtracting cost of goods, payment processing fees, fulfillment/shipping costs, and expected return costs from the retail price.
**In use:** "Net contribution margin came out to $33.38 per unit, well below the 60% gross margin headline figure."
**Common misuse:** Reporting gross margin (retail price minus COGS only) as if it represents true profitability, omitting payment fees, shipping, and expected return costs.

### Chargeback ratio
The percentage of a merchant's transactions that result in a chargeback (a forced reversal initiated by the cardholder's bank), monitored by card networks against specific compliance thresholds.
**In use:** "Chargeback ratio is climbing toward the network's monitoring threshold — we need to identify the root cause now."
**Common misuse:** Treating chargeback ratio purely as a cost metric rather than a compliance threshold that can trigger fines or processing termination if exceeded.

### Customer acquisition cost (CAC)
The average cost of acquiring one new paying customer through a specific marketing channel, including ad spend and related costs, divided by the number of customers acquired.
**In use:** "CAC on this paid social channel is $28 per first-time customer."
**Common misuse:** Comparing CAC directly against first-order profit margin without factoring in that channel's actual retention and repeat-purchase behavior.

### Lifetime value (LTV)
The total expected contribution margin a customer generates over a defined period (often 12 months or longer), including their first purchase and any expected repeat purchases.
**In use:** "12-month LTV contribution for this channel's customers is $49.74, once the 35% reorder rate is factored in."
**Common misuse:** Using a single blended LTV figure across all acquisition channels, when retention and repeat-purchase behavior can differ significantly by channel.

### CAC:LTV ratio
The ratio of a customer's lifetime value to the cost of acquiring them, used as a standard measure of whether an acquisition channel is a sound investment.
**In use:** "CAC:LTV ratio of 1.78x is below the commonly cited 3:1 healthy threshold — this channel needs optimization before scaling further."
**Common misuse:** Scaling ad spend in a channel based on positive first-order margin alone, without checking whether the CAC:LTV ratio actually clears a healthy threshold.

### Marketplace fee (referral fee / fulfillment fee)
Fees charged by a marketplace platform (e.g., Amazon) for selling through it — a referral fee (percentage of sale price) and, if using the platform's fulfillment service, a separate fulfillment fee.
**In use:** "Marketplace referral and fulfillment fees together take about 25% off the top before we even get to our own costs."
**Common misuse:** Comparing marketplace revenue to owned-store revenue without netting out the marketplace's specific fee structure, overstating the marketplace channel's relative attractiveness.

### Return processing cost
The full cost of handling a returned item, including return shipping, restocking or inspection labor, and any markdown or write-off risk if the item can't be resold at full price.
**In use:** "Return processing cost for this apparel category is about $18 per returned unit once you include restocking labor and markdown risk on damaged returns."
**Common misuse:** Accounting only for the return shipping label cost and omitting restocking labor and markdown/write-off risk, understating the true cost of returns.

### Stockout cost
The cost of not having inventory available to fulfill a sale, including the immediate lost sale and the risk of long-term customer loyalty loss to a competitor.
**In use:** "Stockout cost isn't just the lost sale today — it's also the risk this customer buys from a competitor next time."
**Common misuse:** Treating stockout cost as limited to the immediate lost transaction, ignoring the potential longer-term customer retention impact.

### Overstock / holding cost
The cost of carrying excess inventory, including tied-up working capital, storage costs, and the risk of eventual markdown or write-off for slow-moving stock.
**In use:** "Holding cost on this overstocked SKU is eating into margin every month it sits unsold."
**Common misuse:** Setting reorder points to aggressively avoid stockouts without weighing the offsetting overstock/holding cost, leading to chronic excess inventory.

### Reorder point
The inventory level at which a new purchase order is triggered, calculated by weighing the cost of a potential stockout against the cost of holding additional inventory.
**In use:** "Reorder point needs to balance the stockout risk during lead time against how much capital we're willing to tie up in safety stock."
**Common misuse:** Setting reorder points based only on minimizing stockout risk, without factoring in the holding cost of the resulting safety stock level.
