# Customs and Border Protection Officer — Vocabulary

### Primary inspection
The first-line screening every traveler or shipment passes through — document/manifest check plus a brief interview, under a tight time budget.
**In use:** "Primary flagged the manifest description as generic, but nothing else triggered — I let it clear rather than referring on one weak indicator."
**Common misuse:** Generalists assume primary inspection is a thorough check; it's a fast triage filter designed to catch obvious issues and route ambiguous cases onward, not to resolve every question itself.

### Secondary inspection
Extended screening after a primary referral, with more time and tools (interview, database cross-check, imaging, physical search).
**In use:** "Two indicators clustered, so I referred to secondary — full tier given the imaging request."
**Common misuse:** Treating a secondary referral as a presumption of guilt rather than a resource-allocation decision to apply more scrutiny to a borderline case.

### Admissibility
The legal standard a traveler must affirmatively establish to be permitted entry; the burden sits with the traveler, not the officer.
**In use:** "He couldn't establish admissibility on the visa category claimed, so I referred him to the immigration judge process rather than denying entry myself."
**Common misuse:** Assuming the officer must prove inadmissibility "beyond a reasonable doubt" the way a prosecutor would in criminal court — the burden and standard are inverted from that expectation.

### Manifest
The shipper/carrier's declared description of cargo contents, quantity, and value submitted before or at entry.
**In use:** "The manifest said 'parts,' but the HTS code filed was for finished electronics — that mismatch is what triggered the referral."
**Common misuse:** Treating manifest data as verified fact rather than a self-reported claim that inspection exists specifically to test.

### Structuring
Deliberately splitting or scaling transactions to keep each one under a reporting or scrutiny threshold.
**In use:** "Four entries in sixty days, each just under the formal-entry threshold, through four different carriers — that's a structuring pattern, not four coincidences."
**Common misuse:** Looking only at a single transaction for evidence of structuring; the pattern only becomes visible across multiple transactions over a review window.

### Chain of custody
The unbroken, documented record of who held seized evidence from the moment of seizure through final disposition.
**In use:** "The seizure itself was solid, but there's a four-hour gap in the custody log — that's the vulnerability, not the underlying finding."
**Common misuse:** Treating chain-of-custody documentation as administrative paperwork completed after the real work (the seizure) is done, rather than as an equally load-bearing part of the case.

### Reasonable care / risk-based targeting
The framework (used by both CBP and importers under the parallel "reasonable care" standard on the trade side) that inspection and enforcement resources are allocated by estimated risk, not applied uniformly to every transaction.
**In use:** "The targeting score put this shipment in the top decile for this port, which is why it got extra scrutiny even though nothing was obviously wrong on the surface."
**Common misuse:** Treating a risk score as a verdict rather than a prioritization input to be combined with the officer's own read of the specific case.

### Contraband
Goods whose importation, possession, or transport is itself prohibited or restricted, distinct from goods that are legal but merely undeclared or misvalued.
**In use:** "The counterfeit goods are contraband under the trademark statute — separate issue from the undervaluation, which is a valuation/duty violation, not a contraband one."
**Common misuse:** Conflating "undeclared or misdescribed" with "contraband" — an undervalued but otherwise legal shipment is a different violation category with different legal authority and consequences than a shipment containing prohibited goods.

### Secondary referral threshold
The cumulative risk-indicator level (however a given port/system defines it) at which a case moves from primary clearance to secondary inspection.
**In use:** "This case scored a 4 against our port's referral threshold of 3 — straightforward call once the pattern indicator was counted."
**Common misuse:** Assuming the threshold is a fixed, universal number across all ports and cargo types; it varies by port, cargo category, and current risk environment, and specific operational thresholds are not public.

### Compounding vs. additive indicators
The distinction between independent risk factors that simply add up versus a pattern across multiple transactions that should weigh more heavily than the sum of isolated instances.
**In use:** "One near-threshold entry is a weak indicator; four near-threshold entries from the same importer across different carriers is a compounding one — I weighted it as such, not just tallied four points."
**Common misuse:** Scoring a repeated pattern the same as four unrelated single indicators, which understates how much more diagnostic a confirmed pattern is than isolated occurrences.

### Watchlist hit — biometric vs. name-match
The distinction between a confirmed identity match (biometric, document-verified) and a probabilistic name/demographic-similarity match, which carry very different reliability.
**In use:** "It's a name-match hit, not biometric — I'm treating it as one input pending confirmation, not an automatic secondary referral on its own."
**Common misuse:** Treating any "watchlist hit" alert as equally reliable regardless of match type, when name-match hits have a materially higher false-positive rate than biometric confirmation.

### Formal vs. informal entry
The distinction (on the trade/cargo side) between shipments requiring a full customs entry filing versus those qualifying for simplified processing under a value threshold.
**In use:** "The declared value sits just under the formal-entry threshold, which is itself worth noting given this importer's pattern."
**Common misuse:** Assuming the threshold exists purely for administrative convenience rather than recognizing it as a documented target for deliberate under-declaration.
