# Red flags

### Blow count exceeds predicted range by 2x+ at a depth well above the expected bearing stratum
- **Usually means:** an obstruction (boulder, cobble lens, old foundation debris, buried utility) rather than the design bearing layer being reached early.
- **First question:** does the boring log at this pile location show anything at this depth, or is the nearest boring more than the spec's allowable offset away?
- **Data to pull:** the site boring log and geologic profile nearest this pile, plus records of any obstructions hit on adjacent piles in the same footing/pier group.

### Blow count stays below the bearing-graph floor at planned tip elevation
- **Usually means:** the pile has not reached the required nominal resistance yet — the plan elevation was an estimate, not a guarantee, from the original borings.
- **First question:** is the hammer actually delivering rated energy (stroke/energy readout), or is this a hammer-performance problem masquerading as a soil problem?
- **Data to pull:** saximeter stroke log (or hydraulic hammer energy readout) for the last 10–20 ft of drive, compared to the energy assumed in the wave-equation analysis.

### Ram stroke (diesel hammer) reads below roughly 85% of rated stroke for more than a few feet of drive
- **Usually means:** cold hammer, incorrect fuel-pump setting, worn piston rings/cylinder, or a degraded cushion absorbing energy before it reaches the pile.
- **First question:** when was this hammer last serviced, and has the cushion been in service longer than the manufacturer's blow-count interval?
- **Data to pull:** hammer maintenance log, cushion installation date and blow count, saximeter trace for the shift.

### Restrike blow count comes in lower than end-of-drive blow count
- **Usually means:** relaxation — common in dense fine sand, silt, or weathered/fractured rock, where apparent driving resistance was temporarily elevated by pore-pressure or stress-relief effects that dissipate after driving stops.
- **First question:** what does the boring log say about grain size and density at the pile tip elevation — is this a soil type with a documented relaxation history on similar sites?
- **Data to pull:** boring log soil classification at tip elevation, restrike blow count vs. end-of-drive blow count, any PDA/CAPWAP data from the restrike.

### Restrike blow count comes in much higher than end-of-drive blow count
- **Usually means:** setup — common in saturated clay and clayey silt as excess pore pressure generated during driving dissipates and shear strength regains.
- **First question:** how long was the wait between end-of-drive and restrike, and does it match the geotechnical engineer's specified minimum for this soil profile?
- **Data to pull:** restrike wait time, soil classification and plasticity data at tip elevation, comparison of restrike result to the original bearing-graph criterion.

### Heave on an adjacent, previously driven pile exceeding roughly 0.25–0.5 in. (or the project's stated tolerance)
- **Usually means:** full-displacement pile driving (closed-end pipe, precast concrete) in a tight group is pushing soil and lifting piles already placed, most common at center-to-center spacing under about 3 pile diameters.
- **First question:** was this pile driven per the specified group sequence (center-out or engineer-specified order), or out of sequence for installation convenience?
- **Data to pull:** pre- and post-drive elevation survey on all piles in the group, actual driving sequence log against the specified sequence.

### Sudden drop in blow count after steady penetration, with no change in visible soil conditions
- **Usually means:** the pile has broken (concrete pile cracking, steel pile buckling or splice failure) and is now advancing with reduced resistance because it's no longer transferring load properly — or it has punched into a void or very soft seam.
- **First question:** does the drop correlate with a known splice or joint location in the pile, or with a specific depth flagged as questionable on the boring log?
- **Data to pull:** pile driving log around the drop, PDA data if available (a broken pile shows a characteristic signal), splice location and inspection record.

### Hammer cushion or pile cushion visibly charred, compressed, or past the manufacturer's blow-count replacement interval
- **Usually means:** the force-time pulse delivered to the pile has changed from what the wave-equation analysis assumed, so blow-count criteria established against the original cushion are no longer directly valid.
- **First question:** how many blows are on this cushion, and does it match or exceed the interval in the hammer manufacturer's cushion guidance?
- **Data to pull:** cushion installation date/blow count, visual condition against manufacturer photos/spec, any recent unexplained change in blow count that coincides with cushion age.

### Pile "running" — rapid, near-freefall penetration through several feet with minimal hammer input
- **Usually means:** a void, extremely soft layer, or a previously undetected utility/cavity beneath the expected stratum.
- **First question:** does the boring log show anything at this depth, and is there a records check needed for buried structures or utilities in this area?
- **Data to pull:** boring log, utility locate records, driving log immediately before and after the running section.

### CAPWAP-derived capacity from a dynamically tested pile deviates more than roughly 20% from a static load test on the same site
- **Usually means:** the wave-equation soil-resistance-distribution assumption (skin friction vs. end bearing split, damping factors) used in the original analysis doesn't match actual site conditions.
- **First question:** does the deviation direction (over- or under-predicting) suggest the same correction should apply to remaining piles, or is this pile an outlier (obstruction, unusual local soil)?
- **Data to pull:** CAPWAP report, static load test report, original wave-equation analysis assumptions for comparison.

### Pile deviates from plumb or specified batter by more than roughly 2% of pile length
- **Usually means:** leads not properly set/plumbed before driving started, an obstruction deflecting the tip, or ground movement from an adjacent pile mid-drive.
- **First question:** was the deviation present from the first few feet (leads/setup issue) or did it develop partway through the drive (obstruction or ground movement)?
- **Data to pull:** inclinometer or transit readings taken during drive at intervals, driving log for any anomaly noted at the point deviation likely started.

### Concrete pile head shows spalling, cracking, or "brooming" during driving
- **Usually means:** driving stress exceeding the pile's compression or tension limit for its concrete strength and prestress level, often from a hammer oversized for the pile or a degraded/missing pile cushion.
- **First question:** what driving stress does the PDA (if monitoring) show against the pile's rated compression/tension limits, and is the pile cushion present and within spec thickness?
- **Data to pull:** PDA stress trace if available, pile cushion condition and thickness, hammer energy setting relative to the pile's rated driving-stress capacity.
