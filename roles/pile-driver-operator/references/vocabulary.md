# Vocabulary

### Refusal
The blow count (or set) at which the specification says driving stops — typically a stated blows-per-inch threshold (e.g., 10 blows for the last inch) rather than a fixed depth.
**In use:** "We hit refusal at 22 ft, three feet shallower than the boring predicted — flag it before we pour the cap."
**Common misuse:** Treating any high blow count as refusal. A high blow count from an obstruction well above the expected bearing stratum isn't refusal on the design bearing layer — it's an obstruction that needs its own diagnosis.

### Set
The pile's net penetration per blow (or per group of blows), the raw field measurement blow count is derived from — usually recorded in inches per blow or blows per foot/inch.
**In use:** "Log the set every foot down to 40 ft, then every inch once we're inside the bearing-graph range."
**Common misuse:** Confusing "set" (penetration per blow, a field measurement) with "setup" (a soil strength-gain phenomenon in clay) — they sound alike but mean unrelated things.

### Bearing graph
The plot, output by a wave-equation analysis, of blow count versus nominal (ultimate) pile driving resistance for a specific hammer, cushion, pile, and assumed soil-resistance distribution.
**In use:** "36 blows a foot is below the bearing graph's floor for 600 kips — this pile isn't there yet."
**Common misuse:** Applying a bearing graph or blow-count number from a different hammer, pile section, or job as if it transfers — it's specific to the exact combination it was run for.

### Stroke
The height a diesel hammer's ram rises on each blow, read with a saximeter; the direct measure of energy actually being delivered to the pile on a given blow.
**In use:** "Stroke's down to 7.5 ft on a hammer rated for 10 — check the fuel setting before we blame the soil."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a hammer is always delivering its rated (nameplate) energy. Actual stroke varies with fuel setting, temperature, and hammer condition, and wave-equation analyses should be run against the realistic field stroke, not the catalog maximum.

### Saximeter
A field instrument (mechanical or electronic) that reads a diesel hammer's ram stroke height during driving, the operator's primary real-time check on delivered energy.
**In use:** "Saximeter's been steady at 8 ft all afternoon — the hammer's fine, the low blow count is real."
**Common misuse:** Treating saximeter readings as optional paperwork rather than the diagnostic that separates a hammer problem from a soil problem.

### CAPWAP
Case Pile Wave Analysis Program — a signal-matching analysis run on PDA-collected strain/acceleration data that produces an estimated static capacity, soil-resistance distribution, and driving-stress values for a specific blow or restrike.
**In use:** "CAPWAP on the restrike came back at 640 kips, within 15% of the load test — the criteria holds for the rest of the group."
**Common misuse:** Treating a CAPWAP number as exactly equal to true static capacity rather than an engineering estimate with a typical accuracy band (commonly cited around ±10–20% against a static load test).

### PDA (Pile Driving Analyzer)
Strain and acceleration transducers mounted near the pile head, recording force and velocity for each hammer blow in real time during initial drive or restrike; the raw data CAPWAP analyzes.
**In use:** "Put the PDA on Pile 7 for the restrike instead of Pile 9 — that's the one with the open capacity question."
**Common misuse:** Assuming PDA monitoring alone (without a CAPWAP analysis run on the data) delivers a capacity number — the transducers record force and velocity; capacity comes from the signal-matching analysis afterward.

### Wave equation analysis
A one-dimensional stress-wave model of the hammer-cushion-pile-soil system (run in software such as GRLWEAP) used to predict the bearing graph and driving stresses before or during a job.
**In use:** "The wave equation run says 6 to 9 blows an inch gets us to 300 tons with this hammer — anything under that, keep driving."
**Common misuse:** Confusing a wave equation analysis (a predictive model run before/during driving) with a dynamic pile-driving formula like the Engineering News formula (an empirical rule of thumb applied directly to the field blow count) — the two are different tools with very different reliability.

### Setup
The gain in a pile's static capacity over time after driving stops, most pronounced in saturated clay and clayey silt, as excess pore pressure generated by driving dissipates and shear strength regains.
**In use:** "This site's clay setup runs 20–40% over the first week per the geotech report — don't call final capacity off the end-of-drive number alone."
**Common misuse:** Assuming setup applies universally to any soil, or skipping the restrike because the end-of-drive number already looks acceptable — setup is a soil-specific phenomenon, not a general safety margin.

### Relaxation
The loss of apparent pile capacity over time after driving stops, seen in some dense fine sands, silts, and weathered or fractured rock, as temporarily elevated pore pressure or stress effects dissipate.
**In use:** "We're in weathered shale here — schedule the restrike specifically to check for relaxation, not just to confirm the end-of-drive number."
**Common misuse:** Assuming time after driving only ever helps capacity (as in clay setup) — in relaxation-prone soils, waiting and restriking can reveal the pile carries less than the end-of-drive blow count suggested.

### Cushion
An energy-absorbing/transmitting material between the hammer ram (or helmet) and the pile — a hammer cushion for the ram, and a separate pile cushion (commonly plywood) for precast concrete pile heads — that shapes the force pulse delivered to the pile and protects the pile head from spalling.
**In use:** "Cushion's at 4,000 blows and starting to char — swap it before we start the capacity-critical piles."
**Common misuse:** Treating the cushion as a consumable that doesn't affect the engineering — a degraded cushion changes the driving-stress and energy-transfer characteristics the bearing graph was calculated against.

### Restrike
Reapplying hammer blows to an already-driven pile after a wait period, to measure how blow count (and therefore estimated capacity) has changed due to setup or relaxation.
**In use:** "Restrike's scheduled for 48 hours out on the clay piles, immediately on the sand piles where relaxation's the concern."
**Common misuse:** Treating restrike timing as a fixed universal number (e.g., always "24 hours") rather than a soil-specific decision the geotechnical engineer sets based on the profile at each pile.

### Splice
A field connection joining two pile sections end-to-end (full-penetration weld for steel, mechanical or cast connectors for concrete) to extend a pile beyond the length originally ordered.
**In use:** "Three feet short — we're splicing a section rather than pulling it and reordering a longer pile."
**Common misuse:** Treating a splice as a routine cut-and-weld with no schedule or inspection impact — a structural splice on a capacity-critical pile typically requires its own inspection and, on some contracts, non-destructive testing before driving resumes.

### Heave
Upward or lateral ground and pile movement caused by driving a full-displacement pile (closed-end pipe, precast concrete) near already-installed piles, most significant at tight spacing.
**In use:** "Survey showed 0.4 in. of heave on Pile 3 after we drove Pile 4 next to it — re-check it before we call the group done."
**Common misuse:** Assuming heave is a cosmetic or negligible effect — heaved piles can lose bond/capacity along the shaft and, if not caught and corrected (re-driving), leave a pile short of its design resistance.

### Driving shoe
A steel point or reinforcing fitting attached to a pile tip (common on precast concrete and timber piles, sometimes H-piles) to protect the tip and aid penetration through dense or obstructed ground.
**In use:** "Site's got old foundation debris at 12 ft on the borings — spec a reinforced driving shoe on these piles before we start."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a driving shoe is needed (or unnecessary) by default rather than as a response to a specific obstruction risk identified in the geotechnical report.
