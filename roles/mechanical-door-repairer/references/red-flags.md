# Red flags

Signals a technician should catch in the first few minutes on site, before touching a spring or clearing a complaint. Each: what it usually means, the first question to ask, and the data to pull.

### Door fails the balance test — drops or shoots more than 1–2 in. when released at half-open

- **Usually means:** spring weak, under-wound, mismatched wire gauge from a prior repair, or one spring already broken and the other compensating.
- **First question:** "When was the last time these springs were replaced or adjusted, and by whom?"
- **Data to pull:** spring wire diameter and inside diameter (measured, not assumed), wind direction, door weight/manufacturer spec, opener strain history (breaker trips, overheating complaints).

### Third spring failure on the same door within 18 months

- **Usually means:** cycle-life mismatch — a standard-duty spring installed on a door running well above residential cycle frequency — not a defective part or bad luck.
- **First question:** "How many times a day does this door actually cycle, and how many days a year is it in use?"
- **Data to pull:** access log or opener cycle counter if available; facility interview estimate if not; compare cycles/year against the installed spring's rated cycle count.

### Photo-eye indicator light flickering or intermittently unlit

- **Usually means:** misalignment, a loose bracket, or a wiring/connector fault degrading the entrapment-protection beam — the sensor may be passing intermittently rather than reliably.
- **First question:** "Has anyone noticed the door reversing unpredictably, or closing without stopping on an obstruction?"
- **Data to pull:** beam alignment measurement at both jambs, mounting height (should be at or below roughly 6 in. from floor), continuity/voltage check at the receiver.

### Reversal-force setting has been increased to stop "nuisance" reversals

- **Usually means:** someone tuned the entrapment-protection force threshold up to quiet complaints, which weakens or defeats the force-sensing safety system rather than fixing what triggered it.
- **First question:** "What was actually triggering the reversal before the force setting was changed?"
- **Data to pull:** current force-gauge reading vs. the setting that reverses reliably on a standard test object; adjustment-screw wear pattern suggesting repeated manual tuning.

### Winding bar on the truck is a single bar, a screwdriver, or shows visible bending/wear at the tip

- **Usually means:** a corner has been cut on winding procedure at some point, on this job or a prior one — improvised or undersized bars are the leading cause of serious torsion-spring injuries in the trade.
- **First question:** "Are both matched winding bars for this spring's cone diameter on hand before we start?"
- **Data to pull:** bar diameter vs. spring cone bar-hole diameter; visual inspection for bending, which indicates the bar has already been overstressed once.

### Only one spring replaced in a two-spring system after a single failure

- **Usually means:** the surviving spring is the same age and cycle count as the one that just failed and is close behind it on the fatigue curve; a same-day or same-month return call on the "good" spring is the predictable next event, not a coincidence.
- **First question:** "Do we know the install date and approximate cycle count on the spring that didn't break?"
- **Data to pull:** install records if available; if absent, treat both springs as end-of-life together and quote the pair.

### Vertical track out of plumb by more than roughly 1/4 in. over its height

- **Usually means:** rollers binding and loading the spring/cable asymmetrically — this shows up later as premature cable, bearing, or roller failure rather than as an immediate complaint, so it's easy to miss on a callback for a different symptom.
- **First question:** "Has the door been dragging, binding, or making noise at a specific point in travel rather than throughout?"
- **Data to pull:** plumb measurement top-to-bottom on both vertical tracks, roller condition/flat-spot check, cable tension comparison side to side.

### Commercial/industrial door install using residential-duty photo-eyes and springs

- **Usually means:** the job was quoted or installed off a generic parts list without classifying actual use (cycles/day, gate class if applicable) — residential-duty entrapment protection and springs are frequently undersized for a use case that looks similar but cycles far more often.
- **First question:** "What's the actual daily cycle count on this door, and does the hardware on it match a commercial/industrial duty rating or a residential one?"
- **Data to pull:** installed parts list vs. manufacturer's commercial/industrial specification sheet; facility cycles/day estimate; ASTM F2200 gate class if it's an automated gate rather than a sectional door.

### Opener breaker trips repeatedly or motor housing runs noticeably hot to the touch

- **Usually means:** the opener is compensating for a balance problem — lifting door weight the spring should be carrying — rather than a motor or electrical fault in isolation.
- **First question:** "Has the door's balance been checked with the opener disconnected recently?"
- **Data to pull:** balance-test result with opener disconnected; opener amperage draw during a cycle if a clamp meter is available, compared to nameplate rating.
