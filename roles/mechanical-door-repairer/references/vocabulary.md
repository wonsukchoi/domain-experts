# Vocabulary

Terms generalists conflate or use loosely. Each: definition, a sentence a practitioner would actually say, and the common misuse.

### Torsion spring vs. extension spring

**Torsion spring** mounts horizontally above the door on a shaft, stores energy by being wound (twisted) around its axis, and lifts via cable drums on either end. **Extension spring** mounts along the horizontal tracks and stores energy by being stretched lengthwise as the door closes. Torsion systems are more common on garage doors built or upgraded since roughly the 1990s and are generally considered the safer geometry when a safety cable is fitted, since the spring stays contained near the shaft rather than flying the length of the track if it breaks.

*In use:* "This one's still on extension springs with no safety cables — that's the first thing I'm flagging, not the noise he called about."

*Common misuse:* calling any garage door spring a "torsion spring" regardless of type, or assuming extension springs are simply an older, functionally identical version rather than a different failure-mode profile that changes what safety hardware matters.

### Wind (turn) vs. spring rate

A **wind** or **turn** is one full rotation applied to the spring during installation; the total wind count sets how much energy is stored and how much lift the spring delivers. **Spring rate** (or torque per turn) is the amount of torque added per additional turn, set by wire diameter, coil diameter, and length — two springs wound to the same turn count can deliver very different lift if their rate differs.

*In use:* "Same turn count as the old one won't matter if the wire gauge's off — match the rate, not just the winds."

*Common misuse:* treating "count the turns on the old spring and match it" as sufficient for a replacement without confirming wire diameter and coil diameter also match.

### Cycle vs. cycle life

A **cycle** is one full open-and-close of the door. **Cycle life** is the number of cycles a spring, cable, or roller is rated to survive before expected fatigue failure, published by the manufacturer independent of calendar time.

*In use:* "Don't quote him a warranty in years — quote cycle life, then translate it to years using his actual traffic."

*Common misuse:* stating a spring's expected lifespan in years without reference to usage frequency, which is only valid at the specific cycles/day the rating assumes (commonly a low residential frequency).

### Balance vs. tension

**Balance** describes whether the door, disconnected from the opener, holds its position through the travel range — the outcome that matters. **Tension** describes how tightly the spring is wound — an input that produces balance only when set correctly for that specific door's weight and drum. A correctly-tensioned spring on the wrong door, or a spring wound to the wrong count for its wire gauge, is not balanced.

*In use:* "Tension's not the problem, it's balanced fine at half-open — the noise is coming from a roller, not the spring."

*Common misuse:* using "the springs need more tension" as a diagnosis before running the actual balance test, when the real issue is often elsewhere in the system.

### UL 325 vs. ASTM F2200

**UL 325** is the safety standard covering door, gate, and window *operators* — the electromechanical systems, including entrapment-protection requirements for garage door and gate operators. **ASTM F2200** is the construction and classification standard for automated *vehicular gates themselves* (materials, gate classes I through IV by use case), a separate document from the operator standard that installs on that gate.

*In use:* "The gate's built to F2200 Class III spec, but I still need to verify the operator's UL 325 entrapment protection separately — they're not the same checklist."

*Common misuse:* treating either standard as covering both the gate/door hardware and the operator, when compliance with one says nothing about compliance with the other.

### Entrapment protection (Type 1 vs. Type 2)

**Type 1** entrapment protection is an external sensing device — most commonly a monitored photo-eye pair mounted low on the jambs. **Type 2** is inherent protection built into the operator itself, typically force or motor-current sensing that detects resistance and reverses the door. U.S. residential garage door operators have required both, independently, since 1993 — one alone is not considered sufficient.

*In use:* "Photo-eye's aligned fine, that's Type 1 covered — now let's actually test the force reversal, that's Type 2, and it's a separate check."

*Common misuse:* verifying only the photo-eye (visibly present, easy to check) and assuming that satisfies the entrapment-protection requirement, without separately testing force/motor-current reversal.

### High-cycle spring

A torsion spring manufactured (heavier wire, different heat treatment, or design) to survive materially more open/close cycles than a standard-duty spring — commonly available in ratings well above the standard tier, sold at a modest cost premium for doors with above-residential usage.

*In use:* "At his cycle count the standard spring pays for itself in replacement visits inside a year — quote the high-cycle set."

*Common misuse:* defaulting to high-cycle springs on every commercial job regardless of actual measured cycles/day, when a low-traffic commercial door annualizes cheaper on standard-duty hardware.

### Drum (cable drum)

The grooved cylinder mounted at each end of the torsion shaft that the lift cable winds onto as the door closes and pays out as it opens. Drum diameter changes how much cable travels per shaft rotation, which changes how many turns a given spring needs to lift the same door.

*In use:* "Different drum diameter than the last one I did this size door on — don't copy the wind count from memory, check the chart for this drum."

*Common misuse:* assuming drum diameter is standardized across manufacturers or door heights and reusing a remembered wind count without checking.

### Winding bar

A solid steel rod, sized to match a specific spring cone's bar-hole diameter, used in matched pairs to wind or unwind torsion springs by hand under full control at each step.

*In use:* "Neither of these bars is a match for this cone — we're not starting until the right pair's on the truck."

*Common misuse:* substituting a screwdriver, a single bar, or a power tool to save time; any of these removes the controlled, incremental release path the procedure depends on.

### Track plumb

The vertical alignment of the door's side tracks relative to true vertical, measured over the full track height and expressed as an offset (e.g., within roughly 1/4 in. over the height).

*In use:* "Track's out by half an inch top to bottom — that's loading the rollers unevenly, fix that before we touch the spring tension."

*Common misuse:* treating track alignment as a cosmetic or noise issue disconnected from spring and cable wear, rather than a contributor to premature hardware failure elsewhere in the system.

### Force setting (opener)

The adjustable threshold on an automatic operator controlling how much resistance the door can encounter before the motor stops or reverses — set to satisfy entrapment-protection requirements, not to make the door "feel strong."

*In use:* "It's reversing on the weatherstrip catching, not on a person — the fix is the weatherstrip, not turning this dial up."

*Common misuse:* raising the force setting to eliminate reversals triggered by a mechanical issue (binding track, worn roller, weatherstrip drag) instead of fixing that issue, which quietly raises the force a real obstruction would have to withstand before the door stops.

### Cost per cycle

An annualized cost figure (spring/hardware cost ÷ rated or expected life) used to compare hardware options across doors with different usage rates on a common basis, rather than comparing sticker prices alone.

*In use:* "Sticker price's higher, but cost per cycle is a fraction of standard-duty at his traffic — that's the number that belongs in the quote."

*Common misuse:* comparing two spring options on installed price alone without normalizing for how many cycles each is rated to deliver.
