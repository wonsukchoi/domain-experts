# Vocabulary

Terms generalists conflate or use loosely that practitioners keep sharply separate. Each: definition, how a practitioner actually uses it, and the common misuse.

### Breakdown, intermediate, and finish rolling

**Breakdown** rolling (typically 250–320°F) does the bulk of density gain and usually runs vibratory. **Intermediate** rolling (roughly 200–250°F) continues density gain, often with a pneumatic-tire roller for its kneading action. **Finish** rolling (160–200°F, always static) removes roller marks and adds the last small increment of density — it is not a second chance to fix a density shortfall.

**In use:** "We're short on this section — don't send the finish roller back over it again, that's not a breakdown problem anymore, the window's closed."

**Common misuse:** treating all three stages as interchangeable "more rolling," and sending a static finish roller back over a section that's actually short on breakdown-stage density, which adds surface marks without recovering density.

### Thermal segregation vs. aggregate segregation

**Thermal segregation** is a temperature differential across or along the mat, usually from haul/delivery variance, that produces uneven density even with a correct roller pattern. **Aggregate segregation** is uneven distribution of coarse vs. fine aggregate within the mix itself, visible as streaking, that no amount of rolling fixes because the material gradation is wrong at that spot.

**In use:** "That streak is coarse and open-textured, not just cool — that's aggregate segregation from the auger starving on one side, not a temperature problem."

**Common misuse:** diagnosing every visible mat inconsistency as "cold" and reaching for the thermal camera, when a streaky, open-textured band is actually a gradation problem that traces back to the truck-to-hopper transfer or auger flow, not delivery temperature.

### %Gmm (percent of maximum theoretical density) vs. relative compaction (% Proctor)

**%Gmm** compares in-place density to the mix's maximum theoretical density (zero air voids) — the metric used for hot-mix asphalt paving specs. **Relative compaction (% Proctor)** compares in-place density to a lab-determined maximum dry density for a soil or aggregate material — the metric used for subgrade and trench-backfill specs. They are not interchangeable numbers even though both are expressed as a percentage.

**In use:** "Mainline's spec'd at 91% Gmm minimum on the asphalt; the subgrade underneath it was a separate 95% Modified Proctor requirement — don't quote one number when someone asks about the other."

**Common misuse:** citing a soil compaction percentage (Proctor-based) when discussing asphalt mat density, or vice versa — the two use different reference maximums and are not comparable.

### Hot-side vs. cold-side joint rolling

**Hot-side rolling** compacts a longitudinal joint from the just-placed, still-hot lane, with the roller overlapping onto the already-compacted, cooler lane. **Cold-side rolling** — the older, now-discouraged practice — starts from the already-compacted lane and works toward the new one, wasting compactive effort on material that's already set.

**In use:** "Roll it hot-side — start on the new lane and lap 6 inches over onto the old one, not the other way around."

**Common misuse:** defaulting to cold-side rolling out of habit because it "feels" like protecting the finished lane, when it actually under-compacts the joint itself, which is where most longitudinal cracking starts.

### NMAS (nominal maximum aggregate size)

The largest aggregate sieve size at which more than 10% of the mix is retained — a mix-design property that sets the practical minimum compacted lift thickness (roughly 3–4× NMAS), not a spec an operator can work around with more roller passes.

**In use:** "This is a 9.5 mil mix — NMAS caps us at maybe 1.25 inches minimum lift, we can't run it at 3/4 inch no matter how careful the screed is."

**Common misuse:** treating lift thickness purely as a tonnage/schedule decision without checking it against the mix's NMAS, leading to a lift that cools too fast or crushes aggregate under the roller.

### Test strip vs. process control (routine) density testing

A **test strip** is a one-time (or per-condition-change) exercise to determine and lock the rolling pattern. **Process control testing** is the ongoing, routine density verification at a defined frequency across the job to confirm the locked pattern is still delivering spec density. Skipping the test strip doesn't just skip a formality — it means every subsequent process-control reading is being compared against a pattern nobody actually validated.

**In use:** "We haven't pulled a strip on this mix yet today — don't trust the routine readings as pass/fail until we have one."

**Common misuse:** running routine process-control density checks without ever having established a validated pattern via a test strip, then being surprised when density is inconsistent across the day.

### Screed float vs. screed steering

A paver screed **floats** — it self-levels in response to towpoint angle and the head of material in front of it. It is not **steered** directly like a blade; a direct crank input at a specific spot creates a wave in the mat that propagates for a hundred feet or more before it damps out.

**In use:** "Don't crank the screed to fix that dip — bump the towpoint angle a hair and let it float back to grade over the next 50 feet."

**Common misuse:** reacting to a visible grade defect with an immediate, localized crank adjustment, which fixes the symptom at that spot but creates a new, larger defect downstream as the screed re-equilibrates.

### Lift vs. course

A **lift** is one physical layer of material placed and compacted in a single pass of the paver or fill operation. A **course** (e.g., base course, binder course, surface course) is a functional layer in the pavement structure that may be built from one or more lifts, and each course typically uses a different mix design or material spec.

**In use:** "The binder course here is going down in a single 3-inch lift; on the last job it needed two lifts because the base course wasn't graded tight enough to place it in one."

**Common misuse:** using "lift" and "course" interchangeably, which causes confusion when a course legitimately requires multiple lifts (e.g., a thick base course that exceeds one lift's maximum workable thickness).

### Rated effective lift depth (compaction equipment)

The maximum loose-material thickness a specific compactor can adequately densify in one pass, set by its weight, vibration/impact energy, and the soil type — not a universal number across compactor classes or materials.

**In use:** "This walk-behind plate is rated for maybe 8 inches loose in granular backfill — don't stack a foot of material in one lift and expect the bottom half to compact."

**Common misuse:** assuming a compactor's rated depth from one job (e.g., a heavy reversible plate on granular fill) applies to a different compactor or a cohesive soil, where the same lift thickness will leave the bottom under-compacted.

### Aggregate crushing / degradation under the roller

Physical breakdown of individual aggregate particles under roller pressure, most likely when a lift is too thin relative to aggregate size or when a mix is rolled too aggressively at too-high temperature — distinct from normal density gain, which compacts the void structure without breaking the aggregate itself.

**In use:** "We're seeing fines on the surface that weren't in the mix design — that's crushing, not just fines migrating; the lift's too thin for this NMAS."

**Common misuse:** treating any surface fines or texture change as normal finish-rolling polish, missing that it's actually aggregate breakdown caused by an undersized lift or excessive vibratory amplitude.
