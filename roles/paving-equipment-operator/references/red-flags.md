# Red flags

Smell tests a veteran paving or compaction operator catches early in a shift. Format for each: what it usually means, the first question to ask, and the data to pull before changing anything.

### Mat surface temperature within 15°F of the breakdown floor (e.g., 240°F spec, reading 250–255°F) and the roller crew is more than one paver-length back

**Usually means:** the roller crew has fallen behind pace — a truck delay, a paver stop, or the crew simply drifting back to work a joint or edge longer than planned.

**First question:** "How far back is breakdown right now, and is that distance closing or opening?"

**Data to pull:** current thermal-camera or IR-gun reading at the breakdown roller's position; paver speed and roller spacing over the last 15 minutes; time since last truck delivery.

### Thermal profile shows a differential greater than ~25°F across the mat width

**Usually means:** one or more trucks in the haul string are running significantly cooler than the rest — long haul distance, uncovered load, or a dispatch gap — not a roller or screed problem.

**First question:** "Pull the last five trucks' delivery temperatures at the hopper — is one truck the outlier, or is the whole string trending down?"

**Data to pull:** truck-by-truck hopper temperatures for the shift; haul distance/time per truck; tarping status; plant-reported load-out temperature.

### Density reading more than 2 points below mainline within 6 in. of a longitudinal joint

**Usually means:** the joint was rolled cold-side instead of hot-side, the overlap was too narrow (<6 in.), or the second lane wasn't placed before the first lane's edge cooled past the workable window.

**First question:** "Was this joint rolled hot-side, and how long was the gap between the two lanes going down?"

**Data to pull:** timestamp of first-lane placement vs. second-lane placement at that station; roller pass log for the joint; overlap distance used.

### Test-strip pattern is being run on a lift or mix that's changed since the strip was pulled

**Usually means:** the crew is reusing a pattern validated for a different lift thickness, NMAS, or ambient condition — density gain per pass shifts with all three, so a pattern that hit diminishing returns at 8 passes on a 1.5-in. lift may need a different pass count on a 2.5-in. lift.

**First question:** "What lift and mix was the current pattern actually validated against, and does today's match?"

**Data to pull:** test-strip record (date, lift, mix, ambient temp, locked pattern); today's job ticket for lift thickness and mix design; current ambient temperature vs. the strip's conditions.

### A truck gap or paver stop longer than roughly 10 minutes on a thin lift (<2 in.) in cool weather (<50°F)

**Usually means:** the section paved just before the stop is at risk of dropping below the breakdown temperature floor before the roller reaches it, especially on thin lifts that cool fast.

**First question:** "How long was the mat down before the stop, and what's the estimated temperature now versus the breakdown floor?"

**Data to pull:** stop duration; ambient temperature and wind; lift thickness; last known mat temperature reading before the stop; ambient-condition cooling-rate estimate for that lift.

### Density gauge readings improving less than ~0.5 points per two-pass increment, but the crew keeps rolling

**Usually means:** the pattern has already reached its point of diminishing return — continued rolling is either wasted effort or, below the finish-rolling floor, active damage (checking, roller marks) with no density benefit.

**First question:** "What's the marginal density gain over the last two passes, and is the mat still above the finish-rolling temperature floor?"

**Data to pull:** density readings by pass count for this section; current mat temperature; the locked test-strip pattern for comparison.

### Compacted lift thickness on the job ticket is less than 3× the mix's NMAS

**Usually means:** someone thinned the lift to stretch tonnage or shorten haul cycles without adjusting for the mix design's minimum workable thickness.

**First question:** "What's the NMAS on today's mix, and does the lift thickness on the ticket clear the 3× minimum?"

**Data to pull:** mix design NMAS from the plant ticket; specified and actual lift thickness; any aggregate-crushing or excessive-checking reports from the roller crew.

### Trench-backfill lift placed thicker than the compactor's rated effective depth

**Usually means:** the crew stacked two lifts' worth of loose material into one pass to save time, betting the compactor's energy reaches the bottom — it usually doesn't, especially with a light walk-behind plate.

**First question:** "What's this compactor's rated effective lift depth, and how much loose material is actually in this lift?"

**Data to pull:** compactor spec sheet (rated lift depth by soil type); loose lift depth as placed; density test result for this lift specifically (not a blended reading across multiple lifts).

### A density test is skipped or postponed "because the section looked fine"

**Usually means:** schedule pressure is overriding the QC plan, and the section in question — often one that experienced a delay, thermal event, or pattern deviation — is exactly the one most likely to fail if tested.

**First question:** "Is this the section that had the truck gap/cold streak/pattern change earlier — why is it the one being skipped?"

**Data to pull:** QC test log against the planned test frequency; any logged delays, thermal flags, or pattern deviations for that station.
