# Red Flags — Smell Tests a Climate Policy Analyst Catches Instantly

Each entry: what the smell usually means, the first question to ask, and what to actually pull. None of these are automatic verdicts — they're triggers for a check that takes minutes and routinely changes the recommendation.

---

## 1. A regulatory impact analysis (RIA) with no discount-rate sensitivity shown

The RIA reports one benefit-cost ratio at one discount rate, with no range.

**Usually means:** the agency ran the number that supports its preferred conclusion and didn't disclose how sensitive it is, or nobody on the team understood that OMB Circular A-4's 2023 revision made a single-rate result look dated and challengeable.

**First question:** "What's the benefit-cost ratio at the top and bottom of the current federal discount-rate range?"

**Data to pull:** the RIA's Section 4 (or equivalent) input assumptions table; recompute at 1.5%, 2%, and 2.5% if the underlying tons and per-ton values are disclosed. If they aren't disclosed, that's the bigger finding.

---

## 2. A vehicle or power-plant RIA that monetizes only CO2e

Draft benefit-cost table has one row: greenhouse gas benefit.

**Usually means:** an omission, not a judgment call — EPA and most state RIA guidance require monetizing co-emitted criteria pollutants (NOx, SO2, PM2.5) for combustion-reduction rules, and skipping them typically understates total benefit by 30-60% or more for urban mobile-source rules.

**First question:** "Where's the co-pollutant benefit-per-ton table, and why isn't it in this draft?"

**Data to pull:** the underlying emissions-factor model run (MOVES, AP-42, or equivalent) — co-pollutant tons are usually already computed for the emissions inventory even when they weren't monetized.

---

## 3. An offset program claiming >90% additionality with no third-party audit

Program materials assert nearly all credited tons are additional (would not have happened anyway), sourced only from the registry's own methodology documentation.

**Usually means:** self-reported additionality without independent verification — the 2023 investigation into Verra's rainforest credits found the large majority of a large audited sample were not real, additional reductions, and that pattern is not unique to one registry.

**First question:** "Has an independent academic or third-party auditor (not paid by the project developer) reviewed this specific project type's additionality rate?"

**Data to pull:** Berkeley Carbon Trading Project publications or equivalent independent reviews for the same credit type and vintage; compare the registry's claimed rate to the independently estimated rate.

---

## 4. A state ZEV-mandate timeline with no litigation buffer

Fiscal note or implementation plan assumes the rule binds on the statute's stated effective date.

**Usually means:** the plan didn't account for the near-certain legal challenge to the underlying Clean Air Act Section 209 waiver or equivalent state authority — these have historically taken years to resolve.

**First question:** "What's the plan if the waiver approval or the rule itself is enjoined for 18-36 months?"

**Data to pull:** the waiver's docket status at EPA (or the equivalent authorizing action) and the litigation history of comparable prior waivers.

---

## 5. A bill described as "climate policy" moving through budget reconciliation with a pure emissions standard inside it

A reconciliation bill draft includes a provision that sets an emissions limit or performance standard directly, not a tax, fee, or spending item.

**Usually means:** the provision will not survive a Byrd Rule challenge — its primary effect is regulatory/policy, not budgetary, even if it has some fiscal footprint — and will likely be stripped by the parliamentarian before floor consideration.

**First question:** "What is this provision's budgetary effect, and is it merely incidental to its regulatory effect?"

**Data to pull:** CBO's scoring memo or note for the provision, and precedent rulings on comparable provisions in prior reconciliation bills.

---

## 6. A public comment letter that only restates opposition to the rule's goal

Comment argues the rule is bad policy, expensive, or unnecessary, with no citation to a specific number, assumption, or methodology in the docket.

**Usually means:** the letter will get a form-response in the agency's Response to Comments document and create no appealable record — agencies are not required to substantively respond to policy disagreement, only to comments that identify a specific factual or methodological issue.

**First question:** "Which specific number, assumption, or omitted analysis in the docket does this comment challenge?"

**Data to pull:** the RIA's assumptions table and compare against the comment's claims line by line; if no line item is challenged, the comment needs a rewrite before filing.

---

## 7. A "social cost of carbon" figure quoted without a discount rate or vintage year attached

A memo or press release states "the social cost of carbon is $190/ton" with no further qualification.

**Usually means:** the number was copied from a headline without the discount rate (2%, 1.5%, or 2.5%) or the emissions year (SC-CO2 rises over time — the 2030 figure differs from the 2020 figure) that make it meaningful, and it will not survive a technical challenge.

**First question:** "At what discount rate and for what emission year is this number?"

**Data to pull:** the EPA 2023 SC-GHG report's full table, not the single headline figure most often cited.

---

## 8. A cap-and-trade proposal with unlimited or loosely capped offset use

Draft bill allows covered entities to meet more than ~10-15% of their compliance obligation with offsets, with no quality tiering.

**Usually means:** the allowance price signal is being substantially diluted by offset supply of uncertain integrity — California's program caps offset use specifically because uncapped offset use was identified as a price-suppression and integrity risk in earlier program designs.

**First question:** "What share of compliance can be met with offsets, and is offset quality tiered by project type?"

**Data to pull:** the offset-use cap provision in the draft text and compare to RGGI's and California's current caps as a base rate.

---

## 9. A comparison of "current policy" to "no policy" instead of the realistic baseline

An analysis compares a proposed rule's benefits against zero action, when other rules already in the pipeline would achieve part of the same reduction anyway.

**Usually means:** double-counting — claiming credit for emissions reductions that an already-adopted rule (a prior model-year standard, an existing RPS) would have delivered regardless, inflating the marginal benefit of the new proposal.

**First question:** "What does the baseline already assume is happening without this rule?"

**Data to pull:** the baseline emissions projection's underlying assumption set and check it against other currently-binding rules covering the same source category.

---

## 10. A jurisdiction's NDC target cited as though it were a legal ceiling on emissions

A memo treats a country's or state's Paris Agreement NDC number as an enforceable cap, with penalties implied for missing it.

**Usually means:** conflating an international diplomatic commitment with domestic legal enforceability — NDCs carry no binding penalty mechanism under the Paris Agreement; enforceability, if any, comes entirely from domestic implementing law.

**First question:** "What domestic statute or rule actually requires meeting this number, and what's the consequence for missing it?"

**Data to pull:** the domestic implementing legislation or executive action, if any exists, rather than the NDC submission itself.
