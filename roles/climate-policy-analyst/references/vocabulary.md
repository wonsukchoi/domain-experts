# Working Vocabulary — Terms Generalists Misuse

Format per term: definition, a sentence the way a practitioner actually uses it, and the common misuse to avoid.

---

**Social cost of carbon (SCC / SC-GHG)**
The monetized damage from emitting one additional ton of a greenhouse gas, expressed in dollars per ton, dependent on the discount rate and the emission year used to calculate it.
*Usage:* "SC-CO2 at 2% for 2030 emissions is roughly $230/ton; at 2.5% it's closer to $190 — the memo needs both, not a bare number."
*Misuse:* quoting a single SCC figure as if it were a fixed physical constant rather than a modeled output that moves with the discount rate assumption and the emission year — a number without those two qualifiers is not yet meaningful.

**Carbon price vs. social cost of carbon**
A carbon price is a policy instrument (tax rate or allowance price) actually charged to emitters. The social cost of carbon is an estimate of external damage, used to evaluate whether a policy's benefits justify its costs — the two numbers do not have to match, and rarely do in practice.
*Usage:* "RGGI's allowance price is around $15-20/ton; that's not a judgment that carbon only costs $18 in damage, it's what the market cleared at given the current cap."
*Misuse:* treating a low observed carbon price as evidence the SCC is overstated, or vice versa — one is a market-clearing price under a specific cap or tax design, the other is a damage estimate; conflating them erases the entire point of a benefit-cost test.

**Additionality**
The requirement that an emissions reduction or removal (especially for offset credits) would not have happened without the specific incentive of the credit — a project that would have proceeded anyway generates no real additional benefit.
*Usage:* "The avoided-deforestation claim fails additionality if the tract was never actually threatened with clearing in the baseline scenario."
*Misuse:* treating registry certification as proof of additionality. Certification confirms a methodology was followed; it does not independently verify the counterfactual baseline was realistic.

**Leakage**
Emissions reductions in one location or sector that are offset by increased emissions elsewhere, because the activity moved rather than stopped (a protected forest tract's logging demand shifts to an unprotected tract nearby).
*Usage:* "Estimated leakage on this REDD+ project is 20-40%, so the net credited tons should be discounted accordingly, not just the gross avoided-deforestation figure."
*Misuse:* ignoring leakage entirely, or treating it as a fixed 0% when a project's methodology doesn't actually model where displaced activity goes.

**NDC (Nationally Determined Contribution)**
A country's self-set emissions target submitted under the Paris Agreement — a diplomatic commitment, not a domestically enforceable legal obligation on its own.
*Usage:* "The NDC target only binds anything once it's translated into a domestic rule or statute — right now it's a number in a UN submission, not a compliance obligation."
*Misuse:* citing an NDC miss as though it triggers a legal or financial penalty. The Paris Agreement has no binding enforcement mechanism; consequences, if any, are reputational or come from separate domestic law.

**Major questions doctrine**
The post-*West Virginia v. EPA* legal standard requiring clear, specific congressional authorization before a federal agency can decide a question of vast economic or political significance, even under a broadly worded statute.
*Usage:* "A system-wide generation-shifting requirement is exactly the kind of major question that needs explicit statutory authorization now, not a plausible reading of 'best system of emission reduction.'"
*Misuse:* treating every ambitious federal climate rule as automatically vulnerable — the doctrine applies to rules with especially large economic/political stakes and novel statutory readings, not to routine standard-setting well within an agency's traditional authority.

**Benefit-cost ratio (BCR)**
Total monetized benefits divided by total costs for a proposed policy; a ratio above 1.0 indicates benefits exceed costs at the assumptions used.
*Usage:* "BCR moved from 0.68 to 1.08 once co-pollutant benefits were included — same policy, corrected accounting."
*Misuse:* treating a single-point BCR as dispositive without checking which assumptions (discount rate, benefit-per-ton values, baseline) it depends on — a BCR near 1.0 is a coin flip that a different reasonable assumption set can flip either way, and that needs to be said explicitly.

**Cap-and-trade vs. carbon tax**
Cap-and-trade fixes the quantity of emissions (via a declining cap) and lets the market set the price through allowance trading; a carbon tax fixes the price and lets the quantity respond to it.
*Usage:* "If the legislature needs quantity certainty tied to a statutory target, cap-and-trade is the right instrument family even though a tax is administratively simpler."
*Misuse:* treating the two as interchangeable "carbon pricing," full stop — they trade off price certainty against quantity certainty, and that tradeoff should drive instrument choice, not administrative habit or which one passed last time.

**Co-benefits (co-pollutant benefits)**
Non-climate benefits — usually public-health benefits from reduced criteria-pollutant emissions (NOx, SO2, PM2.5) — that accompany a greenhouse-gas-reduction policy because the same combustion activity is being reduced.
*Usage:* "Co-pollutant benefits are $5.58M/year of the total $15.08M — leaving them out isn't conservative, it's incomplete."
*Misuse:* treating co-benefits as a secondary talking point rather than a required line item in the same benefit-cost accounting used for the primary climate benefit — or the opposite error, leaning on co-benefits so heavily that the climate case evaporates if the co-benefit assumptions are challenged.

**Byrd Rule**
The Senate reconciliation rule barring provisions whose budgetary effect is "merely incidental" to their policy purpose — used to strip regulatory provisions (like direct emissions standards) from bills intended to pass with a simple majority.
*Usage:* "A direct emissions performance standard won't survive the Byrd Rule in reconciliation; a tax credit or fee achieving the same behavioral effect will."
*Misuse:* assuming any climate-related provision can ride in a reconciliation bill because reconciliation is "budget-related" — the test is the individual provision's primary effect, not the bill's overall subject.

**Baseline (regulatory baseline)**
The projected future without the proposed policy, against which the policy's incremental costs and benefits are measured — not a projection against zero emissions or the present-day status quo held constant.
*Usage:* "The baseline already assumes the federal fuel-economy standard's next phase-in, so don't credit this state rule for reductions that standard would deliver anyway."
*Misuse:* comparing a new policy's effect against zero action or against today's emissions level, rather than against what would happen anyway absent the new policy — this double-counts reductions already locked in by other rules.

**Endangerment finding**
An EPA administrative determination (first issued in 2009 following *Massachusetts v. EPA*) that a pollutant's atmospheric concentration endangers public health or welfare — the legal predicate required before EPA can regulate that pollutant as a criteria or hazardous pollutant under the Clean Air Act.
*Usage:* "Any new EPA greenhouse gas rule still rests on the 2009 endangerment finding — challenge that finding and the whole regulatory chain built on it is exposed."
*Misuse:* treating the endangerment finding as settled and irrelevant to current strategy — it remains the load-bearing legal predicate for GHG regulation under the Act and is a recurring target of legal challenges.

**Section 209 waiver**
The Clean Air Act provision allowing California (and states that adopt California's standards) to set vehicle emissions standards stricter than federal ones, subject to EPA approval — the legal basis for state ZEV mandates.
*Usage:* "The mandate doesn't actually bind fleet purchasers until the waiver clears EPA review and survives the near-certain court challenge — build that lag into the compliance timeline."
*Misuse:* treating a state ZEV mandate's statutory effective date as the actual binding date, ignoring the waiver-approval and litigation timeline that has historically added years before the rule takes effect.
