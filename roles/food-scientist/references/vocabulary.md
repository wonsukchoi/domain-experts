# Food Scientist — Vocabulary

### Q10 (temperature coefficient)
The factor by which a reaction rate increases for every 10°C rise in temperature; used to extrapolate accelerated-test results back to storage-temperature shelf life.
**In use:** "This is a moisture-migration failure, so we're using a Q10 of 1.8, not the 3.0 we'd use for lipid oxidation."
**Common misuse:** Treating Q10 as a universal constant (usually defaulting to 2 or 3) instead of a mechanism-specific value that differs sharply between chemical and physical degradation modes.

### Water activity (aw)
The ratio of water vapor pressure in a food to that of pure water at the same temperature — the "free" water available for microbial growth and chemical reactions, distinct from total moisture content.
**In use:** "Total moisture barely changed, but aw crossed 0.85 — that's enough to support mold growth even though the product still tastes fine."
**Common misuse:** Confusing water activity with moisture percentage; two products can have identical moisture content and very different water activity depending on how the water is bound.

### Critical control point (CCP)
A step in a process where a control action can be applied that is essential to prevent, eliminate, or reduce a food-safety hazard to an acceptable level — distinct from any step where contamination could merely occur.
**In use:** "Metal detection is a CCP because we have a measurable limit and a monitoring method; the open-room mixing step isn't, because there's no equivalent control action there."
**Common misuse:** Labeling every step where a hazard is theoretically possible as a CCP, producing a monitoring plan that's unworkable and dilutes attention from the steps that actually matter.

### Critical limit
The maximum or minimum value of a measurable parameter (temperature, time, pH, aw) that must be met at a CCP to control the hazard.
**In use:** "The critical limit at the bake step is an internal temperature of 165°F for at least 15 seconds — anything short of that and the batch gets held."
**Common misuse:** Setting a critical limit as a target/goal rather than a hard pass/fail boundary that triggers an automatic corrective action if missed.

### Triangle test
A forced-choice sensory test where panelists are given three samples (two identical, one different) and must identify the odd one out, used to establish whether a detectable difference exists at all.
**In use:** "The triangle test came back significant at day 14 — that's our failure point for the accelerated study, regardless of whether panelists said they liked or disliked the change."
**Common misuse:** Treating a non-significant triangle test as proof the product is "fine," when a small panel or a subtle difference can produce a false negative — a null result isn't the same as a validated pass.

### Hedonic scale
A liking-based sensory scale (commonly 1-9, "dislike extremely" to "like extremely") measuring consumer preference, not detectability of a difference.
**In use:** "Liking score held at 6.8 through day 21, but that doesn't override the triangle-test failure at day 14 — different and disliked are separate questions."
**Common misuse:** Using a stable hedonic score to justify extending a shelf-life claim past a difference-test failure point.

### Accelerated shelf-life test (ASLT)
A study design that stores product at elevated temperature (or humidity) to speed up degradation, then extrapolates back to normal storage conditions using a kinetic model.
**In use:** "The ASLT gives us a provisional number fast, but we still need the real-time control to confirm the extrapolation before we commit to the full claimed shelf life."
**Common misuse:** Treating the accelerated-test extrapolation as final without ever running or completing a real-time confirmation study.

### Arrhenius equation
The kinetic model relating reaction rate to temperature via activation energy; Q10 is a simplified, temperature-range-specific shortcut derived from it.
**In use:** "Q10 is fine for a quick estimate, but if this product ships to both cold and tropical climates, we should fit the full Arrhenius model instead of assuming a constant Q10 across that whole range."
**Common misuse:** Assuming Q10 stays constant across a wide temperature range, when activation energy effects mean the true acceleration factor can shift at temperature extremes.

### Hazard analysis
The systematic identification of biological, chemical, physical, and allergen hazards at each process step, and the assessment of their likelihood and severity — the step that precedes and justifies CCP determination.
**In use:** "We can't finalize the CCP list until the hazard analysis is done — otherwise we're guessing which steps matter."
**Common misuse:** Skipping straight to naming CCPs based on intuition or a template from a similar product, without a documented likelihood/severity assessment specific to this process.

### Allergen cross-contact
Unintentional transfer of an allergen from one product or surface to another (as opposed to cross-contamination, a broader food-safety term covering pathogens too).
**In use:** "The cleaning validation swab confirmed cross-contact risk is below the ELISA detection threshold after the changeover procedure."
**Common misuse:** Using "cross-contamination" and "cross-contact" interchangeably; regulators and allergen-labeling rules treat allergen cross-contact as a specific, separately-governed risk category.

### Shelf-life safety margin
The gap deliberately built between the tested failure point and the printed shelf-life date, to absorb lot-to-lot and product-to-product variability.
**In use:** "Failure point was 34 days; we're printing 25 to leave a safety margin for the variability we haven't characterized yet."
**Common misuse:** Printing the tested failure date itself as the shelf-life claim, leaving zero margin for real-world variability the lab study didn't capture.

### Descriptive analysis (QDA)
A trained-panel sensory method that quantifies the intensity of specific attributes (e.g., "staleness," "off-flavor") rather than just detecting whether a difference exists.
**In use:** "The QDA panel scored staleness at 2.1 on day 0 and 6.4 at the failure point — that's the specific attribute driving the triangle-test result."
**Common misuse:** Running a QDA panel and reporting only the overall "different/not different" conclusion, discarding the attribute-level detail that explains *why* the product failed and which Q10 range applies.

### Corrective action
The predefined response taken when a critical limit is not met at a CCP — distinct from a general troubleshooting step, because it must be documented and specific to the HACCP plan.
**In use:** "Corrective action for a missed metal-detector calibration check is to isolate all product since the last verified check, not just the current batch."
**Common misuse:** Treating "investigate and fix the equipment" as sufficient corrective action, without also addressing the disposition of product made during the uncontrolled period.
