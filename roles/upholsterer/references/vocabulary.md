# Vocabulary

### Pattern repeat
The vertical (and sometimes horizontal) distance after which a fabric's printed or woven pattern repeats itself.
**In use:** "27" repeat on this fabric — that's what's driving the yardage up, not the panel size itself."
**Common misuse:** Estimating yardage from finished piece dimensions alone, ignoring that each cut piece may consume a full repeat length of fabric regardless of the piece's own actual size.

### ILD (Indentation Load Deflection)
A foam firmness rating measuring the force required to compress the foam a specified percentage, independent of the foam's density.
**In use:** "Same density foam, but the 35 ILD feels noticeably firmer under you than the 25 ILD."
**Common misuse:** Treating firmness (ILD) and density as the same property, when a foam can be firm-feeling (high ILD) yet low density (poor durability), or the reverse.

### Foam density
The weight of foam per cubic foot, correlating with durability and resistance to breaking down over time, independent of how firm the foam feels.
**In use:** "This is 1.8 lb density — should hold up under daily use far better than the 1.2 lb foam it's replacing."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a firmer-feeling foam sample is automatically higher density or more durable, when firmness and density are independent measurements.

### 8-way hand-tied (suspension)
A spring suspension system where coil springs are tied to each other and to the frame in eight directions for support and sag resistance — generally the most durable seating suspension type.
**In use:** "This piece is 8-way hand-tied — that's why it's held its shape for twenty years."
**Common misuse:** Assuming any "spring suspension" description refers to this system, when sinuous/zigzag and drop-in coil systems are meaningfully different in construction and sag resistance.

### Sinuous springs (zigzag springs)
A suspension system using continuous S-shaped wire springs across the frame, generally less expensive and less sag-resistant over time than 8-way hand-tied.
**In use:** "Customer's original quote called for hand-tied — swapping to sinuous changes the durability picture and needs to be disclosed."
**Common misuse:** Treating it as functionally equivalent to hand-tied springs when substituting one for the other without disclosure.

### Nap / pile direction
The directional lay of fibers in fabrics like velvet or corduroy, which changes the fabric's apparent color and sheen depending on viewing angle.
**In use:** "Check nap direction on every panel before cutting — mismatched panels will look like different colors under lamp light."
**Common misuse:** Cutting panels without checking that nap direction runs consistently across all visible pieces, producing a visible mismatch even from the same fabric roll.

### Railroaded fabric
Fabric woven or printed so its pattern runs horizontally along the bolt rather than vertically, allowing it to be applied across a wide piece (like a sofa back) without seams.
**In use:** "This fabric's railroaded — can run it across the back in one piece instead of piecing it."
**Common misuse:** Assuming any fabric can be applied "railroaded" regardless of its actual weave or print orientation, risking an upside-down or sideways pattern on the finished piece.

### Deck
The platform — often webbing, burlap, or plywood — beneath the seat cushions that provides the base support structure.
**In use:** "Deck's sagging under the webbing — that needs to be addressed before the new cushions go on, not just covered."
**Common misuse:** Assuming the deck is purely cosmetic and hidden, and doesn't need the same tension and repair attention as visible upholstery.

### Webbing
Interwoven strips of jute or synthetic material stretched across a frame to provide base support beneath springs or foam.
**In use:** "Webbing's stretched loose from years of use — re-stretch it before adding new padding on top."
**Common misuse:** Assuming webbing tension is a one-time setting that doesn't loosen or need re-stretching over the life of a reupholstery job.

### Center-out tacking sequence
The practice of stapling or tacking fabric starting from the center of a panel and working outward in a defined pattern, to distribute tension evenly.
**In use:** "Started at center and worked out to the corners — that's what keeps this panel smooth long-term."
**Common misuse:** Tacking corner-to-corner or edge-first, which can trap uneven tension into the fabric that shows up later as wrinkles or localized stress.
