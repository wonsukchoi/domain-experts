# Vocabulary

Terms generalists flatten together that a packing-line grader keeps sharply separate.

### Grade vs. standard vs. spec

**Grade** is the tier a specific lot earns (US Fancy, US No. 1, Grade AA). **Standard** is the published rule set a grade is measured against (USDA 7 CFR 51 series, the Egg-Grading Manual). **Spec** is a specific buyer's contract requirement, which may sit inside, at, or tighter than the legal standard.

**In use:** "We're grading to spec, not to standard — this customer's contract is two points tighter than the legal Fancy minimum."

**Common misuse:** treating "meets the standard" and "meets the customer's spec" as the same statement; a lot can legally be Fancy and still fail a specific buyer's contract.

### Defect vs. damage vs. serious damage vs. decay

**Defect** is the umbrella term for anything short of the ideal piece. **Damage** is a defect that meaningfully affects appearance, shipping quality, or edibility. **Serious damage** is damage severe enough to seriously affect those qualities — a narrower, tighter-tolerance sub-bucket. **Decay** (rot) is its own smallest, tightest sub-bucket because it's progressive and can spread to neighboring fruit in storage.

**In use:** "Total defect rate is fine at 6%, but two of those are decay against a 1% decay allowance — that's the actual failure."

**Common misuse:** reporting one blended "defect %" and missing that a narrower sub-allowance (serious damage or decay) has already been exceeded on its own.

### Ac / Re (accept number / reject number)

The two thresholds from an acceptance-sampling plan (e.g., ANSI/ASQ Z1.4): if the count of defective units found in the sample is at or below Ac, the lot is accepted; at or above Re, it's rejected at that sample. The two numbers are set together from the lot size and target AQL (acceptable quality level) — they aren't independently chosen.

**In use:** "We pulled the 125-count sample for this lot size — Ac is 7, Re is 8, and we came in at 11, so this fails, full stop."

**Common misuse:** treating any number between Ac and Re as "borderline pass" — in most standard tables Re = Ac + 1, so there is no ambiguous middle count; the ambiguity, if any, is in whether to re-sample, not in reading the same sample differently.

### Lot vs. sublot vs. field run

A **lot** is the full quantity being graded or sampled as one unit (a bin, a truckload, a day's pack). A **sublot** is a portion of a lot split out for separate handling (one bin from a mixed load). **Field run** is unsorted, ungraded product straight off the harvest, before any sizing or defect culling has happened.

**In use:** "Don't sample the field run and report it as the packed grade — sample after the sorter, once it's actually the lot going out the door."

**Common misuse:** quoting a field-run defect estimate as if it describes the finished, packed-out lot; the whole point of the line is that the two numbers are different.

### Haugh unit

A calculated measure of interior egg quality derived from egg weight and thick-albumen height, used to separate Grade AA (≥72) from Grade A (≥60) from Grade B (below 60); measured by candling or a specialized meter, not by looking at the shell.

**In use:** "Shell looks clean, but Haugh came in at 54 — that's a B regardless of exterior appearance."

**Common misuse:** assuming exterior cleanliness or shell color determines grade; Haugh unit and air-cell depth (interior/freshness measures) drive the AA/A/B tier, not shell appearance.

### NIR sorting vs. optical (camera) sorting

**Optical/camera sorting** uses visible-light imaging to grade size, shape, and external color. **NIR (near-infrared) sorting** shines infrared light through the product to read internal attributes — sugar content (Brix-correlated), density, sometimes early internal breakdown — that a camera can't see through the skin.

**In use:** "The camera pass rate looks great, but we're shipping this variety on NIR sugar reading too, since color alone doesn't predict ripeness for it."

**Common misuse:** calling any automated sorter "optical" as a catch-all; NIR and camera sorting solve different problems and a line often runs both in sequence.

### Culls vs. seconds vs. juice grade

**Culls** are pieces pulled out of the pack-out stream for any reason. **Seconds** are culls still sound enough to sell at a lower grade/price point (not necessarily processed). **Juice grade** (or processing grade) is the lowest commercial tier — sound enough for juice/processing but not for fresh sale in any form.

**In use:** "That bruising cull is a second, not juice grade — it'll still move at the No. 1 price if we route it right."

**Common misuse:** treating all culls as equally low-value; a cosmetic cull and a decay cull have very different downstream value and, for decay, different food-safety handling requirements entirely.

### Chargeback vs. claim

A **claim** is a buyer's formal assertion that a shipment failed to meet grade/spec, usually with its own inspection evidence. A **chargeback** is the resulting deduction from payment (or additional invoice) the buyer applies once the claim is accepted or negotiated. A claim can exist without a chargeback (denied or reduced); a chargeback shouldn't exist without an underlying claim and evidence.

**In use:** "We're disputing the claim before it becomes a chargeback — their receiving inspection sample size doesn't match what our contract specifies."

**Common misuse:** treating the two as interchangeable, or accepting a chargeback without first checking whether the claim's own sampling was valid.

### Traceability vs. traceback

**Traceability** is the standing recordkeeping capability — lot-to-grower-to-harvest-day linkage maintained on every lot as a matter of course. **Traceback** is the specific act of using those records to trace a particular problem (contamination event, customer complaint) to its source after the fact.

**In use:** "Traceability is why the traceback on this complaint took twenty minutes instead of a week — the lot log already had the grower and harvest date attached."

**Common misuse:** only building traceability records reactively, after an incident prompts a traceback — by then the linking data for that lot is usually gone or reconstructed from memory.

### Physiological disorder vs. decay vs. mechanical damage

**Physiological disorder** (e.g., bitter pit, watercore, scald) is a quality defect from the plant's own growing/storage conditions, not an organism or an impact. **Decay** is defect caused by a pathogen (fungal/bacterial rot) and is progressive/contagious in storage. **Mechanical damage** (bruising, punctures) comes from handling and doesn't spread to neighboring fruit the way decay does.

**In use:** "That's watercore, not decay — it won't spread to the rest of the bin, but it will affect storage life, so it still needs its own disposition."

**Common misuse:** lumping all interior quality issues under "decay," which triggers unnecessary cross-contamination handling for a physiological defect that poses no spread risk to adjacent fruit.
