# Red flags

Smell tests a counter veteran catches before ringing up the sale. Format for each: what it usually means, the first question to ask, and the data to pull.

## 1. Customer names a part and insists "I already know what's wrong"

**Usually means:** a symptom has been self-diagnosed into a part, often the wrong one — batteries, grounds, and belts routinely masquerade as alternator failures, for example. Selling the named part without a question ships a guess.

**First question:** "What's it doing — is this a confirmed test result or is that your best guess from the symptom?"

**Data to pull:** none from the catalog side; this is a conversation check. If the answer is symptom-only and the part is expensive or labor-intensive to install, suggest a load/parasitic-draw test (or the shop's equivalent) before the sale, not after a return.

## 2. Part number returns "superseded" more than once in the chain

**Usually means:** the requested number is old enough that the design has changed at least twice; price, stock, or even fitment on the intermediate numbers is unreliable.

**First question:** "What's the final active number, and does anything about the part change across the chain — connector, bolt pattern, included hardware?"

**Data to pull:** full supersession chain to the terminal active SKU; vendor bulletin or catalog note on what changed at each supersession step, if any is flagged.

## 3. Comeback rate on one SKU or category rises above ~3% of units sold in a quarter

**Usually means:** either a fitment/quality issue with that specific part, or an install issue at one or more accounts buying it — the two look identical in the return count and need to be separated.

**First question:** "Are the comebacks concentrated at one account, or spread across many?"

**Data to pull:** comeback count by SKU by account for the quarter; vendor's defect-rate benchmark for the category if available; whether returned units show a consistent failure mode.

## 4. Vendor's aftermarket catalog and the OE application catalog disagree on fitment

**Usually means:** the aftermarket listing is a "universal fit" or broad-application entry that technically bolts on but isn't application-correct — common on brackets, sensors, and electrical connectors where the mechanical fit is close enough to install but the tolerance or pinout is wrong.

**First question:** "Does the OE catalog list this exact number for this VIN, or just something with the same bolt pattern?"

**Data to pull:** OE-decoded application for the VIN in question; the aftermarket vendor's fitment notes/exceptions field, which often lists the caveat in fine print.

## 5. Customer requests "the cheapest one" on a safety-critical system

**Usually means:** price sensitivity is real, but an unqualified economy-tier request on brakes, steering, or airbag components needs a spoken tradeoff, not a silent downgrade — liability and comeback risk both concentrate here.

**First question:** "Cheapest that's still rated for this application, or cheapest full stop — want me to walk you through what changes between tiers?"

**Data to pull:** tier comparison (OE/premium/mid/economy) with the actual spec difference, not just price, for that specific part category.

## 6. Core charge disputes at the register

**Usually means:** either the customer's old core doesn't match what was sold (wrong core, damaged beyond core-eligible condition, or missing a required component), or the counter person quoted core value without inspecting the unit.

**First question:** "Do we have the actual old unit here to inspect, or is this a credit request without the core in hand?"

**Data to pull:** core policy for that part category (condition requirements, core value); whether the core was physically inspected at time of sale or credited on trust.

## 7. Special-order or non-stock part quoted as "in stock" to close the sale

**Usually means:** pressure to close the ticket led to overstating availability — this becomes a broken promise the moment the shop schedules the vehicle around it.

**First question:** "Is this actually on our shelf right now, or a DC transfer / special order — what's the real ETA?"

**Data to pull:** current on-hand quantity at the local location specifically, not network-wide availability; vendor lead time if it's a special order.

## 8. New or seasonal staff quoting application-sensitive parts by year/make/model only

**Usually means:** the VIN-decode step is being skipped, usually from unfamiliarity with which categories need it, not laziness — but the comeback exposure is the same either way.

**First question:** "Did you pull the VIN on this one, or go off year/make/model?"

**Data to pull:** that staffer's comeback rate on application-sensitive categories versus the counter average; whether onboarding covered which part categories require VIN-level ID.

## 9. Same vehicle (by VIN or plate) returns for the same repair category within 90 days

**Usually means:** either the wrong part was sold the first time, the install was faulty, or the original diagnosis was wrong and a different component is actually failing — all three need to be ruled out before selling the same category again.

**First question:** "What part number and what shop did the first repair — and do we know if it was installed by them or by the customer?"

**Data to pull:** prior sale history on that VIN; whether the same shop or a different one is bringing it back; any warranty claim already filed on the first part.

## 10. Staff-initiated price override outside the account's matrix tier

**Usually means:** usually a legitimate accommodation (price-match, loyalty gesture) but occasionally margin leakage or an undisclosed side arrangement — the pattern matters more than any single override.

**First question:** "What's the business reason for this override, and is it a one-time exception or should this account's tier actually change?"

**Data to pull:** override frequency and dollar total by staff member and by account over the quarter; whether overridden accounts show comeback or payment patterns that would justify a tier change instead of repeated one-off overrides.
