# Vocabulary

Terms generalists conflate that parts-counter professionals keep sharply separate. Each: definition, how a practitioner actually uses it, and the common misuse.

## OE vs. OEM vs. aftermarket vs. remanufactured

**OE** (original equipment) is the exact part that came on the vehicle from the factory, carrying the vehicle manufacturer's part number. **OEM** (original equipment manufacturer) is the company that made that OE part for the vehicle maker — often sold separately under its own brand at a lower price than the dealer's OE-branded box, same part. **Aftermarket** is any part made by a company with no OE supply relationship, ranging from OE-equivalent quality to economy-grade. **Remanufactured (reman)** is a used core rebuilt to spec, sold at a discount to new with a core charge attached.

*In use:* "Customer wants OE pricing but this is an OEM part, same factory, no dealer markup — quote that first."

**Common misuse:** treating "OEM" and "aftermarket" as opposites; an OEM part is technically aftermarket-channel once sold outside the dealer, just made by the original supplier.

## Fitment vs. interchange

**Fitment** is whether a specific part physically and functionally matches a specific vehicle's application. **Interchange** (or cross-reference) is the practice of finding a different part number — from another brand or another vehicle application — that fits the same application as the one requested.

*In use:* "The interchange says this Ford part crosses to the same casting as the GM number — confirm fitment against the VIN before I sell it as a match."

**Common misuse:** assuming an interchange listing guarantees fitment; interchange means "shares a base design or is documented as compatible," not "verified against every trim variant."

## Supersession (superseded part number)

A part number the manufacturer has formally replaced with a new number, due to a design change, a numbering-system change, or a discontinuation with a direct replacement.

*In use:* "That number's dead — three supersessions deep, current active is the R2 version, and the R2 has a different connector than the original."

**Common misuse:** treating a supersession as a pure renumbering with no functional change; some are, some aren't, and the catalog note (not the assumption) tells you which.

## VIN decode vs. RPO/option code

**VIN decode** extracts the base application (year, make, model, engine, drivetrain) encoded in the vehicle identification number. **RPO/option codes** (Regular Production Option, GM's term; other makers have equivalents) identify factory-installed option packages — trailering, handling, brake upgrades — that a VIN alone often does not disambiguate.

*In use:* "VIN gets us to base 1500, 5.3L — RPO sticker in the glovebox is what tells us if this one has the trailering package's bigger rotors."

**Common misuse:** assuming a VIN decode alone is sufficient for every application-sensitive part; several vehicle families require the option-code layer on top of the VIN for brakes, suspension, and towing-related components.

## Core charge / core return

A refundable deposit charged on a remanufactured part (alternator, starter, caliper, axle) to guarantee the customer's old unit — the "core" — comes back to the rebuilder; credited when the old unit is returned in acceptable condition.

*In use:* "Core's $45 on this alternator — bring the old one back in rebuildable shape and it's a full credit, cracked housing and it's a partial."

**Common misuse:** crediting a core without physically inspecting it, or assuming core value is fixed regardless of the returned unit's condition — most core policies grade condition and credit accordingly.

## ACES / PIES

**ACES** (Aftermarket Catalog Exchange Standard) is the data format the aftermarket industry uses to describe which parts fit which vehicle applications. **PIES** (Product Information Exchange Standard) is the companion format for product attributes, pricing, and content (images, descriptions). Together they're what most modern parts-catalog software is built on underneath the storefront.

*In use:* "The fitment's wrong on the display — check whether it's a display bug or the ACES data feed itself is bad, because a bad feed means every retailer using that vendor's data has the same wrong listing."

**Common misuse:** assuming a catalog display error is a one-store problem rather than checking whether it traces to the underlying vendor data feed shared across retailers.

## Will-call vs. delivery vs. DC transfer

**Will-call** is counter pickup of an in-stock local item. **Delivery** is the store's own driver bringing a part to a commercial account. **DC transfer** is moving stock from a distribution center to the local store because the part isn't on the local shelf.

*In use:* "It's not local — DC transfer, same-day cutoff was noon so this one's tomorrow morning, not tonight."

**Common misuse:** quoting DC-transfer stock as "in stock" to the customer; the honest distinction (and real ETA) is what keeps a commercial account's schedule intact.

## Matrix pricing

A cost-plus pricing structure for commercial accounts where markup percentage is set by account tier (volume, payment history, comeback rate) rather than a flat retail list price.

*In use:* "This account's Tier B — matrix is cost plus 28%, not list, and not the Tier A rate that account down the street gets."

**Common misuse:** applying one account's negotiated tier to another account informally, or treating matrix pricing as arbitrary rather than tied to documented volume/payment/comeback criteria.

## Comeback

A part that a customer or shop brings back because it failed, didn't fit, or didn't fix the original problem — distinct from a simple return of an unused, unopened part.

*In use:* "That's our third comeback on that caliper SKU this month — pull the numbers before we reorder more."

**Common misuse:** lumping comebacks in with routine returns in tracking; a comeback signals a fitment, quality, or diagnosis problem worth investigating, while a routine return usually doesn't.

## Special order (non-stock)

A part not carried in local or DC inventory, ordered directly from the manufacturer or a specialty distributor, typically with a longer lead time and often non-returnable once ordered.

*In use:* "That's special order — two-week lead time from the manufacturer, and it's non-cancelable once I place it, so confirm the fitment twice before I do."

**Common misuse:** treating special-order lead times as flexible or the order as freely cancelable; many vendors bill on order placement, not delivery.

## New old stock (NOS)

An original, unused part manufactured under an older part number or for a discontinued model, still in its original packaging, as opposed to a reproduction or aftermarket equivalent.

*In use:* "That's true NOS, factory box, not a repro — priced accordingly, and there's exactly one left."

**Common misuse:** using "NOS" loosely for any old-looking or hard-to-find part; it specifically means unused factory-original stock, not a used take-off part or a modern reproduction.
