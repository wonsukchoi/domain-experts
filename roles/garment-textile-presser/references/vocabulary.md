# Vocabulary

### Glazing (fabric pressing defect)
A shiny, flattened appearance on fabric caused by excess heat or pressure during pressing, particularly visible on dark or synthetic materials, and generally not reversible once it occurs.
**In use:** "That sheen along the lapel is glazing from over-pressing — once it's set, there's no undoing it."
**Common misuse:** Assuming glazing can be corrected with additional pressing or steaming — glazing is fiber damage from excess heat/pressure, and further pressing typically worsens rather than corrects it.

### Heat-set crease
A crease or fold pattern permanently fixed into fabric by the combination of heat, pressure, and (for some fibers) moisture during pressing, which becomes very difficult to remove or reposition once formed.
**In use:** "Positioning has to be right before we press — a heat-set crease in the wrong spot isn't something we can just press out afterward."
**Common misuse:** Treating garment positioning as something to check after pressing rather than before — once heat and pressure set a crease, correcting its location typically requires extensive rework or isn't fully possible, making pre-press verification the actual control point.

### Dwell time (pressing)
The duration heat and pressure are applied to a garment during a press cycle, one of several parameters (along with temperature, pressure, and moisture) that together determine the pressing outcome for a specific fiber type.
**In use:** "Same temperature, but this fiber needs a shorter dwell time — six seconds instead of eight, or we risk over-pressing."
**Common misuse:** Treating dwell time as independent from the other pressing parameters — temperature, pressure, moisture, and dwell time interact together, and a setting safe at one dwell time can become damaging at a longer one even with unchanged temperature and pressure.

### Fiber content (pressing parameter basis)
The actual fiber composition of a garment (cotton, polyester, wool, blends), which determines the correct pressing parameters — distinct from and more reliable than garment type or category as a basis for parameter selection.
**In use:** "Checking the fiber content tag before we press this one — it's labeled as a different fabric than what our standard blend parameters assume."
**Common misuse:** Setting press parameters based on garment type (jacket, shirt) or general appearance rather than confirmed fiber content — the same garment category can be made from meaningfully different fiber compositions requiring different press parameters.

### Press form / buck
The shaped fixture a garment is positioned on during pressing, whose alignment with the garment determines where heat and pressure are actually applied.
**In use:** "Checking alignment on the buck before we press — that's what determines whether the crease lands where it's supposed to."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a garment is correctly positioned on the press form simply because it's loaded and the press cycle can begin — misalignment can be present without being obvious at a glance, and verification before pressing is the actual control point.

### Under-pressing
Insufficient heat, pressure, dwell time, or moisture applied during pressing, resulting in an unfinished, wrinkled, or insufficiently creased appearance — distinct from over-pressing's opposite failure mode.
**In use:** "This one's under-pressed, not damaged — needs another pass at the correct parameters, not a fix for fabric damage."
**Common misuse:** Assuming caution (using lower parameters "to be safe") always produces an acceptable result — under-pressing is itself a real, distinct defect requiring the press to actually reach its correct parameter window, not just avoid the over-pressing failure mode.

### Steam moisture level (pressing)
The amount of steam/moisture introduced during pressing, which must match the fabric type and intended outcome — too little fails to properly set the press, too much over-relaxes fibers or causes visible water spotting.
**In use:** "Light steam for this synthetic — heavy steam would over-relax the fibers and we'd lose the crease definition we're trying to set."
**Common misuse:** Using a single default steam setting regardless of fabric type — different fibers and finishes require different moisture levels to achieve the intended pressing result without over- or under-relaxing the material.

### Fiber-specific parameter reference chart
A documented reference matching press parameters (temperature, steam, pressure, dwell time) to specific fiber types, used to select correct settings rather than relying on a single standard or memory.
**In use:** "Pulling the reference chart for one hundred percent polyester — that's forty degrees lower than our standard blend setting."
**Common misuse:** Relying on memorized "standard" settings for a general garment category rather than consulting the actual fiber-specific reference — different fibers within the same general garment type can require meaningfully different parameters, and a memorized standard doesn't account for that variation.
