# Vocabulary

### Predicate device
A legally marketed device to which a new 510(k) submission claims substantial equivalence.
**In use:** "K210987 is our predicate — same sensor chemistry, same read interval, same intended use."
**Common misuse:** treating "predicate" as "closest competitor product" rather than the specific legally cleared device cited in the submission, with its own indications-for-use statement that must actually match.

### Substantial equivalence
The FDA 510(k) standard: the new device has the same intended use and either the same technological characteristics as the predicate, or different characteristics that don't raise new questions of safety or effectiveness.
**In use:** "The sensor material differs, but it doesn't raise a new question of safety — we can still argue substantial equivalence with a bridging biocompatibility study."
**Common misuse:** assuming "substantially equivalent" means "nearly identical" — the standard explicitly tolerates technological differences as long as they're adequately supported.

### Additional Information (AI) request
FDA's formal request for more data during a 510(k) review, which pauses (holds) the review clock until answered.
**In use:** "We got an AI request on the biocompatibility data — clock's on hold until we respond."
**Common misuse:** treating an AI request as optional feedback rather than a hold on the review; the submission doesn't move forward, complete or not, until it's answered.

### Refuse to Accept (RTA)
FDA's administrative completeness screen applied before a 510(k) enters substantive review; missing one required element results in rejection, not a pause.
**In use:** "Run the RTA checklist before we file — a missing test report resets us to zero, not just delays us."
**Common misuse:** confusing RTA rejection (administrative, resets the clock) with an AI request during review (substantive, pauses the clock).

### De Novo pathway
A pathway for novel, low-to-moderate-risk devices with no valid predicate, creating a new device classification and often a new predicate for future submissions.
**In use:** "There's no predicate for this indication, so we're filing De Novo instead of forcing a weak 510(k) comparison."
**Common misuse:** treating De Novo as a fallback for a "close enough" predicate — it's specifically for devices with no legitimate predicate at all.

### Letter to file
An internal manufacturer record documenting a device change assessed as not requiring a new regulatory submission.
**In use:** "We documented this as a letter to file — the change doesn't affect the sterilization validation or the cleared performance claims."
**Common misuse:** using it as a default low-effort option for any change, rather than the documented outcome of running the change through FDA's decision tree.

### MARD (mean absolute relative difference)
A standard accuracy metric for continuous glucose monitors, comparing sensor readings to a reference method across the measurement range.
**In use:** "Our MARD came in at 8.9%, under the predicate's cleared 9.2% — that supports our accuracy claim."
**Common misuse:** quoting a single overall MARD figure without checking performance across the full glucose range (hypoglycemic range MARD is often materially worse and is what FDA scrutinizes most).

### Special controls
Device-type-specific requirements (performance standards, guidance documents, post-market surveillance) that, combined with general controls, provide FDA's basis for reasonable assurance of safety and effectiveness for a Class II device.
**In use:** "This device type has special controls under 21 CFR 862.1355 — our test plan has to hit every one of them, not just the ones we think matter."
**Common misuse:** treating special controls as suggestions rather than the specific, citable requirements a 510(k) will be checked against.

### Indications for use
The formal statement of what a device is cleared to do, for whom, and under what conditions — the actual boundary of the regulatory clearance.
**In use:** "Marketing wants to say 'monitors for early diabetes signs' — that's outside our indications for use, which stop at 'general wellness trend monitoring.'"
**Common misuse:** treating marketing copy or common usage as equivalent to the indications for use, which is a specific legal document, not a product description.

### 510(k) summary
The public document FDA posts describing a cleared device's intended use, predicate comparison, and testing performed.
**In use:** "Pull the 510(k) summary for the predicate before we finalize our comparison table."
**Common misuse:** assuming the summary contains the full technical data submitted — it's a public abstract, not the complete submission FDA reviewed.

### Notified Body
An EU-designated organization authorized to assess conformity of medical devices under MDR, issuing the CE certificate.
**In use:** "Our Notified Body flagged the clinical evaluation report as insufficient on long-term data — that's a different gap than anything FDA raised."
**Common misuse:** assuming a Notified Body's role is equivalent to FDA's — MDR has no predicate-based clearance concept, and Notified Body review is a conformity assessment against different technical requirements entirely.

### Post-market surveillance (PMS) plan
The documented system for actively and passively monitoring a device's real-world performance and safety after clearance.
**In use:** "The PMS plan needs an update — the new indication changes what counts as a reportable event."
**Common misuse:** treating PMS as reactive complaint-handling only, rather than a plan that must be actively updated whenever the label, risk profile, or regulatory region changes.

### Pre-submission (Q-Sub)
A formal, non-binding meeting request to get FDA feedback on a regulatory strategy or study design before formal submission.
**In use:** "We're taking the classification question to a Q-Sub before we spend six months on the wrong pathway."
**Common misuse:** skipping it under time pressure on genuinely novel questions, then discovering the contested position during a substantive review — much more expensive than the meeting would have been.
