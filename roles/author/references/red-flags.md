# Red Flags — Smell Tests in Drafts, Queries, and Contracts

Each entry: what the smell usually means, the first question to ask, and what to actually look at. None are automatic verdicts — they're triggers for a specific check that takes minutes and often changes the decision.

---

## 1. Contract has a flat hardcover royalty rate (no escalator past 5,000/10,000 copies)

**Usually means:** boilerplate that was never negotiated, or a smaller press with genuinely thinner margins than a major imprint — either way, a missed opportunity to capture upside if the book sells past initial expectations.

**First question:** "What rate applies past 5,000 copies, and past 10,000?"

**Data to pull:** the royalty schedule clause itself; comparable recent deals for similarly positioned debuts (Publishers Marketplace deal announcements).

---

## 2. Ebook royalty offered below 25% of net receipts

**Usually means:** outdated boilerplate copied from an old template, or the publisher testing what an unrepresented or first-time author will accept without pushback.

**First question:** "Is 25% of net the standard here, or is this a legacy/small-press rate specific to this contract?"

**Data to pull:** the ebook royalty clause; the Authors Guild Model Trade Book Contract's current recommended terms.

---

## 3. Option clause with no response deadline and no genre/length limit

**Usually means:** the publisher is hedging on the author's next book at zero cost to itself — "any future work" with no clock can tie up a career's next project indefinitely.

**First question:** "What response window, and what exactly counts as 'next work'?"

**Data to pull:** the option clause text; how other agented deals in this genre scope the same clause.

---

## 4. Reversion-of-rights clause with no numeric trigger

**Usually means:** rights can sit unused — book unavailable in any format, zero promotion, zero sales — without ever legally reverting to the author, because "out of print" and "publisher's discretion" were never defined with a number.

**First question:** "What specific unit-sales threshold or time period triggers reversion, in writing?"

**Data to pull:** the reversion clause text; whether "out of print" is defined at all (print-only definitions are now mostly meaningless once ebooks/POD exist).

---

## 5. 20+ queries sent, all form rejections, zero partial or full requests

**Usually means:** the problem lives in the opening pages or the query letter itself, not the underlying premise — premise-level rejections tend to arrive with slightly more personalization or a specific "not right for my list" note.

**First question:** "Am I opening in the right scene, with a concrete stake on the page?"

**Data to pull:** the submission tracking log (see artifacts.md); the manuscript's first five pages held up against recent comp-title openings.

---

## 6. Revision-letter response drafted before a full reread of the manuscript

**Usually means:** the response addresses the editor's notes locally, chapter by chapter, instead of catching that one note (e.g., pacing) recurs in three other places the letter didn't flag.

**First question:** "Does this specific note show up anywhere else in the manuscript the letter didn't mention?"

**Data to pull:** a fresh scene-by-scene read-through list, done after the letter but before drafting the response memo.

---

## 7. Self-published title priced at a category extreme with no test

**Usually means:** price was set from anxiety (going low to "compete") or ego (going high to signal quality) rather than from what the category's actual bestsellers charge.

**First question:** "What do the top 15-20 titles in this exact category price at, right now?"

**Data to pull:** current bestseller list price points in the specific subcategory, not the general category.

---

## 8. Work-for-hire or ghostwriting agreement silent on credit line and promotional use of name

**Usually means:** byline, cover credit ("with" vs. "as told to" vs. no credit at all), and whether the ghostwriter's name can be used in the client's own promotion were never actually agreed — discovered only after publication, when it's unfixable.

**First question:** "Does the contract state the exact credit line, verbatim, as it will appear on the cover and title page?"

**Data to pull:** the contract's credit/attribution clause; whether it distinguishes cover credit from copyright ownership (these are separate and are often conflated).

---

## 9. Beta reader feedback is entirely subjective with no located diagnostic

**Usually means:** the notes ("loved it," "didn't like the ending") won't be actionable before submission, because they describe a reaction without a location in the text.

**First question:** "Where exactly did you stop being able to predict what happens next, or start skimming?"

**Data to pull:** chapter-by-chapter engagement notes, not a single end-of-book reaction.

---

## 10. Query cites zero comp titles, or comps are decades old

**Usually means:** either the author hasn't researched where the book sits in the current market, or is avoiding comps out of fear of inviting comparison — both read to an agent as "I don't know how this sells."

**First question:** "What are two titles published in the last 3-5 years that a reader of this book also read?"

**Data to pull:** recent category bestseller lists; the agent's own recent sales (via Publishers Marketplace) to confirm the comp is relevant to *this* agent, not just the genre generally.
