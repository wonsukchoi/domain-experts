# Author Artifacts — Templates With Example Content

Real work products, filled in with plausible examples. Copy the structure, replace the content. The example project throughout is *Low Tide*, a fictional debut literary-thriller novel (85,000 words).

---

## 1. Query letter

A query is three paragraphs, one page, no attachments unless requested. Its only job is to get a full-manuscript request.

```
Dear [Agent name],

I'm querying you because you represent [comp author]'s work and mentioned
wanting "quiet suspense with a literary voice" in a recent interview —
LOW TIDE is a complete 85,000-word literary thriller that should appeal
to readers of Celeste Ng's LITTLE FIRES EVERYWHERE and Tana French's
THE SEARCHER.

When marine biologist Ren Okafor returns to her hometown to settle her
estranged mother's estate, she finds the tide charts her mother kept for
thirty years — logs that place her missing father at the harbor on the
night he supposedly drowned at sea, thirty miles away. Reconstructing
her mother's last months means reckoning with what her mother knew, how
long she kept it, and what it cost the family to let a lie set like
concrete. Ren has six days before the house sells and the evidence goes
with it.

I'm a [relevant credential: e.g., "graduate of X program" / "essays in Y,
Z"] and this is my second completed novel. The manuscript is complete
and available upon request; thank you for your time and consideration.

[Name, contact info]
```

What makes this work: it names why this agent specifically, states word count and genre in the first paragraph, gives two comps published within the last few years, and the plot paragraph contains a concrete stake and deadline (six days) — not a summary of the whole book.

## 2. Nonfiction book proposal — outline with what goes in each section

Fiction sells on a finished manuscript; nonfiction typically sells on a proposal before the book is fully written.

```
# Book Proposal: [Working Title]

## Overview (1-2 pages)
The argument in one paragraph, the reader's takeaway in one sentence,
and why this book, why you, why now. Name the gap in existing
literature this book fills — not "there's nothing like this," but
"X and Y cover the theory; this covers the practitioner's version."

## Author platform
Concrete, current numbers: newsletter subscribers, speaking engagements
booked in the next 12 months, prior press, professional credential
that makes you the person to write this. Platform claims without
numbers ("large following") read as inflated; give the actual count
and the date it was measured.

## Comparable titles (4-6 titles)
Title, author, publisher, year, and — where findable — sales
performance or list placement. State how this book is similar and
where it differs; "nothing like it" is a red flag to an editor, not
a selling point (see red-flags.md).

## Chapter-by-chapter outline
One paragraph per chapter: what claim or scene it delivers, not a
restatement of the chapter title. A chapter outline that could be
inferred from the table of contents alone hasn't done its job.

## Sample chapter(s)
One or two chapters, fully written, representative of the book's
actual voice and rigor — not the easiest chapter to write.

## Marketing and timeline
Realistic promotional assets you control (newsletter, speaking
calendar, media contacts) and a completion date for the remaining
manuscript.
```

## 3. Synopsis (for agent/editor submission, 1-2 pages, present tense)

A synopsis reveals the ending. It is not the query letter, longer.

```
LOW TIDE — Synopsis

Ren Okafor, a marine biologist, returns to her hometown of Cardigan
Point to close her late mother's estate. Sorting the house, she finds
thirty years of her mother's handwritten tide logs — and one entry,
the night her father supposedly drowned thirty miles offshore, that
places the tide at dead slack at the harbor, not the open water where
his boat was found.

[... plot continues through present tense, beat by beat, revealing
every major turn including the ending ...]

In the final chapter, Ren confronts her mother's closest friend, who
confirms the father did not drown — he left, and the mother spent
three decades maintaining the story rather than the truth, believing
the lie was smaller than what the town would do to a man who walked
out. Ren decides not to correct the record publicly; she keeps her
mother's tide logs instead of selling them with the house.
```

The tell of a synopsis that isn't doing its job: it stops before the ending "to avoid spoilers." An agent reading a synopsis needs to see that the ending works and earns its setup — withholding it defeats the document's purpose.

## 4. Revision-letter response memo

When an editor sends a developmental letter (often 3-8 pages covering the whole manuscript), respond with a structured memo before touching prose — this is the deliverable that gets discussed on a call, not a redlined manuscript.

```
# Response to Editorial Letter — LOW TIDE — [date]

## Notes I'm implementing as written
- Act 2 sags between chapters 14-19 (five chapters, no plot movement):
  cutting chapter 17 entirely, moving its one necessary beat (Ren finds
  the second tide log) to the end of chapter 15.
- Mother's motivation underexplained until chapter 22: adding one scene
  in chapter 8 (flashback, ~1,200 words) establishing what the town did
  to the family that left, before Ren's father does.

## Notes I'm implementing with a modification
- Note: "readers won't buy Ren keeping the secret at the end." Agreed
  the current ending under-motivates it. Rather than having Ren reveal
  the truth publicly (suggested fix), I'm adding a scene where Ren
  learns what public exposure actually did to another family in town
  in a parallel case — giving her decision a concrete, witnessed cost
  instead of an abstract one.

## Notes I'm not implementing, with reasoning
- Note: "cut the tide-chart procedural detail, it slows pacing."
  Keeping it: three beta readers and the sample-chapter feedback both
  cited the specificity of the tide mechanics as what makes the
  reveal feel earned rather than convenient. Trimming to roughly half
  the current length instead of removing it.

## Timeline
Revised draft in 6 weeks (by [date]); flagging now if that doesn't work
against the schedule.
```

Reading this log after ~20-25 queries: form rejects with no partials/fulls requested point at the opening pages; a full requested then rejected with a *specific, repeated* craft note (here: pacing in the same chapters flagged twice) is a structural revision signal, not a "wrong agent" signal.
