# Working Vocabulary — Terms Generalists Misuse

Format per term: definition, a sentence the way a practitioner actually uses it, and the common misuse to avoid.

---

**Advance**
Money paid to the author before or during writing/publication, recoupable against future royalties — not a fee for services rendered.
*Usage:* "The advance is $18k; I won't see another check until royalties earned pass that number."
*Misuse:* treating the advance size as a proxy for the book's expected quality or success — it reflects the publisher's sales projection and negotiating leverage, not a verdict on the manuscript.

**Royalty escalator**
A tiered royalty rate that increases at defined unit-sales thresholds (e.g., 10% of list for the first 5,000 copies, 12.5% for the next 5,000, 15% thereafter).
*Usage:* "We're at 6,143 copies to earn out because the escalator kicks in at 5,000."
*Misuse:* assuming a single flat royalty percentage applies at all sales volumes — a missing escalator is itself a negotiation point, not a neutral default.

**Net receipts vs. list price**
List price is the cover price; net receipts is what the publisher actually collects after retailer/distributor cuts (often ~65-70% of list for ebooks). Ebook royalties are typically a percentage of net receipts, not list.
*Usage:* "25% of net on a $13.99 ebook is around $2.45 a copy, not $3.50 — that's the net-vs-list gap."
*Misuse:* comparing an ebook royalty percentage directly to a print royalty percentage without checking which base (net or list) each applies to — the numbers aren't comparable until converted to a per-unit dollar amount.

**Option clause**
A contract clause giving the publisher first (or exclusive) right to consider the author's next work, usually before it can be shown elsewhere.
*Usage:* "The option clause needs a genre limit and a 45-day response deadline, or the next book can sit unanswered indefinitely."
*Misuse:* treating any option clause as standard and unremarkable — the danger is entirely in the *scope* (how broadly "next work" is defined) and whether a response deadline exists at all.

**Reversion of rights**
The contractual return of publishing rights to the author, typically triggered when a book goes out of print or falls below a defined sales threshold.
*Usage:* "Rights revert if net sales drop below 150 copies in any 12-month period and the book is unavailable in every format."
*Misuse:* accepting "publisher's sole discretion" or an undefined "out of print" as the trigger — without a number and a format condition, reversion may never actually happen even for a commercially dead title.

**Comp titles (comps)**
Recently published books cited in a pitch to signal category, audience, and market position — ideally with real, checkable sales traction.
*Usage:* "Comps are Ng's LITTLE FIRES EVERYWHERE and French's THE SEARCHER — same tonal register, similar reader."
*Misuse:* citing decades-old bestsellers or claiming "nothing like this exists" — both read as a failure to research the current market rather than as evidence of originality.

**On submission**
The period after an agent has sent the manuscript to a curated list of editors and is awaiting responses, prior to any offer.
*Usage:* "We've been on submission for six weeks; two editors have it at acquisitions committee."
*Misuse:* confusing "on submission" (agented, targeted editor list) with the querying stage (unagented, seeking representation) — they involve different gatekeepers and different diagnostics for a stall.

**Earn-out**
The point at which royalties owed exceed the advance already paid, after which the author receives additional royalty checks.
*Usage:* "At 6,143 hardcover copies we hit earn-out; anything past that is a new check."
*Misuse:* assuming most books reach this point — a large share of traditionally published titles never earn out, which is why contract terms beyond the advance number matter.

**Subsidiary rights**
Rights beyond the primary print/ebook edition — audio, foreign translation, film/TV option, serial excerpt — each separately negotiable and often reverting or splitting differently than primary rights.
*Usage:* "Foreign rights stay with the agency at a 20% commission; audio went to the publisher this time."
*Misuse:* assuming all rights bundle identically with the main deal — audio and foreign are frequently negotiated and sold on separate timelines with separate splits.

**Work-for-hire**
An arrangement (common in ghostwriting) where the commissioning party owns copyright from creation, rather than the writer — as opposed to a licensing deal where the writer retains copyright and grants rights.
*Usage:* "This is work-for-hire, so the client owns it outright; my credit line and rate are the only things I'm negotiating."
*Misuse:* assuming a freelance or ghostwriting contract without the words "work-for-hire" still gives the commissioner ownership by default — absent that clause, copyright defaults to the writer under US law.

**Revise-and-resubmit (R&R)**
A response where an agent or editor declines the current draft but explicitly invites a resubmission after specific revisions.
*Usage:* "It's an R&R with three concrete notes on the ending — worth another pass before I query further."
*Misuse:* treating every request for "some changes" as an R&R — a real R&R names specific, addressable notes and invites a resubmission; a vague "maybe try revising" is usually a soft pass.

**Platform (author platform)**
An author's pre-existing, measurable reach to potential readers — newsletter subscribers, speaking engagements, media presence, professional credential — cited with real current numbers in nonfiction proposals especially.
*Usage:* "Platform: 4,200 newsletter subscribers as of last month, two prior bylines in [outlet]."
*Misuse:* describing platform in adjectives ("a strong following") instead of a number and a measurement date — editors evaluating a nonfiction proposal are pricing the platform, and vague claims price at zero.

**Developmental edit vs. copyedit vs. proofread**
Three distinct editorial passes at increasing granularity: developmental (structure, plot, character, pacing — whole-manuscript level), copyedit (sentence-level grammar, consistency, style-guide conformance), proofread (final catch of typos/formatting after layout).
*Usage:* "We're still in developmental — no point copyediting chapter 14 if it might get cut."
*Misuse:* requesting or performing a copyedit before the developmental pass is settled, which wastes line-level work on text likely to be cut or restructured.

**Deep POV (point of view)**
A close narrative mode where the narration reads entirely through the viewpoint character's perception and voice, without authorial framing ("she thought," "she felt") interrupting it.
*Usage:* "Pull the filter words — 'she noticed the door was open' becomes 'the door stood open' — that's deep POV."
*Misuse:* treating deep POV as simply "first person" or "close third" — it's a stricter discipline about eliminating narrative distance within either of those, and can be done badly in both.

**Beat sheet**
A structural outline naming a story's major turning points (inciting incident, midpoint, low point, climax) at approximate percentage-of-length markers, used to diagnose or plan pacing.
*Usage:* "Checked the draft against a beat sheet — the midpoint reversal is landing at 70%, not 50%, which is why act two feels long."
*Misuse:* using a beat sheet as a fill-in-the-blank template to generate plot rather than as a diagnostic against an existing draft — this produces mechanically-paced stories with no distinct voice.
