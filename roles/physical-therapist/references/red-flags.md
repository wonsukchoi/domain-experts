# Physical Therapist — Red Flags

### Saddle anesthesia, new bowel/bladder dysfunction, or bilateral progressive leg weakness with low back pain
- **Usually means:** Cauda equina syndrome — a surgical emergency; less commonly a severe central disc herniation without full cauda equina presentation.
- **First question:** "Have you noticed any numbness around your groin or inner thighs, or any change in your ability to control urination or bowel movements?"
- **Data to pull:** Onset timing of bowel/bladder symptoms relative to pain onset; perianal sensation on exam if within scope; immediate same-day physician/ED referral, do not proceed with PT evaluation.

### New, severe back or neck pain following significant trauma in a patient over 65, or any trauma in a patient on long-term corticosteroids
- **Usually means:** Vertebral fracture — osteoporotic compression fracture or trauma-related fracture.
- **First question:** "Did this start after a fall, accident, or other injury, and are you taking or have you recently taken steroid medications?"
- **Data to pull:** Mechanism and force of injury; steroid history and duration; imaging status — refer for imaging before manual treatment if fracture is suspected and not yet ruled out.

### Unexplained weight loss, night pain that isn't relieved by position change, or history of cancer with new musculoskeletal pain
- **Usually means:** Metastatic disease or primary malignancy presenting as musculoskeletal pain.
- **First question:** "Have you lost weight without trying, and does the pain wake you at night or stay the same no matter how you're lying?"
- **Data to pull:** Weight-loss amount and timeframe; cancer history (personal or strongly relevant); whether pain has any mechanical pattern (better/worse with position, activity) — true mechanical pain typically varies with position, night pain that doesn't is a stronger flag.

### Sudden, severe headache described as "the worst of my life," or neurologic symptoms with neck pain (vision change, slurred speech, facial droop)
- **Usually means:** Vertebral artery dissection or other cerebrovascular event, especially in the context of recent neck manipulation history or trauma.
- **First question:** "Did the headache come on suddenly and severely, and have you noticed any changes in your vision, speech, or one side of your face or body?"
- **Data to pull:** Onset speed and severity; cranial nerve screen; immediate emergency referral, do not perform cervical manual techniques.

### Fever, chills, or recent infection with new spine or joint pain
- **Usually means:** Spinal infection (discitis, osteomyelitis) or septic joint.
- **First question:** "Have you had any fever, chills, or been treated for an infection recently, especially IV drug use, recent surgery, or a weakened immune system?"
- **Data to pull:** Temperature if available; recent infection, surgery, or immunosuppression history; refer for medical workup before proceeding.

### Chest pain, shortness of breath, or pain that doesn't change with movement or position, especially in a patient with cardiac risk factors
- **Usually means:** Cardiac or pulmonary pathology masquerading as musculoskeletal chest or upper-back pain.
- **First question:** "Does the pain change at all when you move, breathe deeply, or change position, or does it stay exactly the same regardless of what you do?"
- **Data to pull:** Cardiac risk factors; pain reproducibility with palpation or movement (reproducible pain is more likely musculoskeletal); refer for medical evaluation if pain is non-mechanical.

### Progressive, unremitting weakness in a specific myotomal or peripheral-nerve distribution that worsens visit over visit
- **Usually means:** Progressive nerve root compression or peripheral nerve injury requiring surgical evaluation, not a typical resolving radiculopathy.
- **First question:** "Is the weakness the same as last visit, better, or getting worse?"
- **Data to pull:** Manual muscle test grades at each visit in the affected myotome, tracked over time; refer back to physician if strength is declining rather than stable or improving.

### Two consecutive reassessments showing outcome-measure change below MCID despite documented adherence to the plan of care
- **Usually means:** Wrong working diagnosis, an undiagnosed comorbidity, non-mechanical pain generator, or a psychosocial barrier (fear-avoidance, secondary gain) not yet addressed.
- **First question:** "What's changed, or not changed, since we started — and is there anything getting in the way of doing the home program?"
- **Data to pull:** Adherence log; outcome-measure scores at each interval side by side; screen for yellow-flag psychosocial factors; consider re-referral to the physician for a second look.
