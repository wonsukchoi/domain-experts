# Physical Therapist — Vocabulary

### MCID (Minimal Clinically Important Difference)
The smallest change in an outcome measure that patients or clinicians would consider meaningful, distinct from the smaller amount of change that could be due to measurement error alone.
**In use:** "The ODI moved 12 points, which clears the MCID, so this is real functional progress, not noise."
**Common misuse:** Treating any numeric improvement as clinically meaningful — a 3-point shift on an instrument with a 10-point MCID is statistically indistinguishable from test-retest error and shouldn't be reported as progress.

### MDC (Minimal Detectable Change)
The smallest amount of change in a measurement that exceeds the measurement's own error/noise floor — a statistical concept distinct from, though related to, the MCID.
**In use:** "The strength difference between visits is within the dynamometer's MDC, so I'm not confident that's a real change yet."
**Common misuse:** Conflating MDC (statistical detectability) with MCID (clinical meaningfulness) — a change can clear the MDC threshold and still be clinically trivial, or in rare cases be meaningful without clearing a conservative MDC.

### Myotome
The group of muscles primarily innervated by a single spinal nerve root, used to localize a suspected nerve root lesion via manual muscle testing.
**In use:** "Weakness isolated to the L5 myotome — big toe extension and ankle dorsiflexion — points to an L5 nerve root issue, not a general leg weakness."
**Common misuse:** Treating any weak muscle as myotomal evidence without checking whether the weakness pattern actually matches a single nerve root's distribution versus a peripheral nerve or muscular cause.

### Dermatome
The area of skin primarily innervated by sensory fibers from a single spinal nerve root, used alongside myotomal testing to localize a nerve root lesion.
**In use:** "Numbness follows the L4 dermatome down the anterior-medial shin, consistent with the myotomal weakness we found."
**Common misuse:** Expecting dermatomal boundaries to be sharp lines — there's substantial overlap between adjacent dermatomes, so a finding at a boundary is less diagnostic than one clearly centered in a single dermatome.

### Yellow flag
A psychosocial risk factor (fear-avoidance beliefs, catastrophizing, depression, low expectation of recovery, workplace dissatisfaction) associated with delayed recovery or transition to chronic pain, distinct from a "red flag" medical emergency indicator.
**In use:** "No red flags on screening, but strong fear-avoidance yellow flags — she's avoiding all bending because she's convinced it will 're-injure' her spine."
**Common misuse:** Confusing yellow flags with red flags and either over-referring a yellow-flag patient to the ED, or ignoring yellow flags entirely because "there's no medical emergency" — yellow flags predict outcome and change the treatment approach even without urgent referral.

### Centralization
A phenomenon in which distal (further from the spine) pain or symptoms move proximally (toward the spine) in response to repeated movement testing, generally considered a favorable prognostic sign in spinal conditions.
**In use:** "Repeated extension centralized his leg pain back up into the low back over 10 reps — good prognostic sign, that's the direction we'll load."
**Common misuse:** Assuming any change in symptom location during testing counts as centralization — true centralization is a specific, reproducible, lasting proximal shift, not a transient or peripheralizing change (which is an unfavorable sign, the opposite finding).

### Special test (orthopedic)
A specific manual maneuver designed to stress or reproduce symptoms from a particular anatomical structure, used as part of a cluster of findings rather than a standalone diagnostic.
**In use:** "One positive special test in isolation doesn't confirm a meniscus tear — I'm clustering it with joint line tenderness and effusion before I'm confident."
**Common misuse:** Treating a single positive special test as diagnostic on its own — most orthopedic special tests have modest individual sensitivity/specificity and are validated as part of clusters, not standalone rule-in/rule-out tools.

### Plan of care
The formal documented set of goals, frequency, duration, and interventions established at evaluation and updated at reassessment, required for insurance authorization and medical necessity justification.
**In use:** "The plan of care specifies 2x/week for 6 weeks with goals tied to the ODI — that's what justifies the continued authorization request."
**Common misuse:** Treating the plan of care as paperwork written once and never revisited — it should be actively updated when goals are met, unmet, or when the working diagnosis changes.

### Direct access
State-specific regulatory permission allowing patients to see a physical therapist without a physician referral, with variation by state in duration and scope of unsupervised treatment allowed.
**In use:** "This state allows direct access for evaluation and up to 30 days of treatment before a physician referral is required."
**Common misuse:** Assuming direct-access rules are uniform nationally — they vary significantly by state in duration, restrictions (e.g., some states exclude certain interventions), and whether they apply to all payers (some payers require a referral regardless of state PT practice law).

### Manual muscle test (MMT) grade
A 0–5 ordinal scale for grading muscle strength during a resisted manual exam, with half-grades sometimes used for finer discrimination in the middle range.
**In use:** "Hip abductors graded 4-/5 — moves through full range against gravity with some resistance, but breaks with maximal manual resistance."
**Common misuse:** Relying on MMT to discriminate strength differences at grade 4 and above, where the scale's resolution is coarse — a hand-held dynamometer gives a quantitative, more sensitive measurement for tracking change in stronger muscles.

### Functional outcome measure
A standardized, validated instrument (patient-reported or performance-based) that quantifies a patient's functional status in a given condition area, used to track change over an episode of care.
**In use:** "We're using a condition-specific outcome measure at eval, mid-point, and discharge so the payer sees quantified progress, not just narrative notes."
**Common misuse:** Switching outcome measures mid-episode of care (e.g., changing instruments between visits) — this breaks the ability to compare scores directly and makes MCID comparisons invalid.

### Maximum medical improvement (MMI)
The point at which a patient's condition has stabilized and further skilled intervention is not expected to produce additional meaningful functional gain, distinct from the patient being asymptomatic.
**In use:** "He's at MMI — plateaued for two reassessment cycles despite good adherence — time to transition to a home program rather than continue skilled visits."
**Common misuse:** Equating MMI with "fully recovered" — a patient can reach MMI with residual symptoms or limitations that simply aren't expected to improve further with continued skilled PT, which is a distinct clinical and billing determination from full resolution.
