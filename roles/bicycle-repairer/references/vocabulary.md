# Vocabulary

Terms generalists conflate or misuse, kept sharply separate by a practitioner. Each: definition, how it's actually used in a sentence, and the common misuse to avoid.

## Chain elongation vs. "chain stretch"

**Elongation** is the measurable increase in effective chain length caused by wear at the pin-and-bushing interfaces, expressed as a percentage. Steel doesn't actually stretch; the metal wears away at each articulating joint, and the accumulated clearance reads as length. "Chain stretch" is the common name for the same measurement.

*Practitioner usage:* "It's reading 0.89% elongation on the CC-3.2 — that's past the cassette-safe line, not just a chain that's a little loose."

*Common misuse:* assuming the chain literally stretches like a spring, which leads people to think a chain can be "un-stretched" or that elongation is reversible — it isn't; the worn material is gone.

## Torque spec vs. "snug" / "hand-tight"

**Torque spec** is the manufacturer's numeric Nm value for a specific fastener, measured with a calibrated tool. "Snug" or "hand-tight" are feel-based approximations with no numeric floor or ceiling, and no way to verify after the fact.

*Practitioner usage:* "The frame decal says 5–6 Nm for the seatpost clamp — torque it to 5.5 and log it, don't snug it and call it done."

*Common misuse:* treating "snug" as an acceptable substitute for a carbon torque spec because it's worked on steel for years; carbon has no forgiving middle zone the way steel does.

## Carbon assembly (friction) paste vs. grease

**Carbon assembly paste** contains abrasive particles that raise the friction coefficient at a clamp interface, so a given torque value holds more securely. **Grease** lowers friction, which is correct for threads and bearings but wrong for a carbon clamp face.

*Practitioner usage:* "Don't grease that seatpost clamp — carbon paste, or it'll slip at spec torque and someone will crank it past the crush point trying to stop the slip."

*Common misuse:* using leftover bearing or thread grease on a carbon clamp interface because it's already on the bench, which quietly encourages overtightening to compensate for the lost friction.

## DOT fluid vs. mineral oil

**DOT fluid** (commonly DOT 4 or DOT 5.1) is glycol-based, hygroscopic, and used by SRAM, Avid, Hayes, and Formula hydraulic disc brakes. **Mineral oil** is non-hygroscopic and used by Shimano hydraulic disc brakes. The two are not interchangeable and use different seal compounds.

*Practitioner usage:* "That's a Shimano system — mineral oil kit only, and don't reach for the DOT syringe we just used on the SRAM bike."

*Common misuse:* assuming "hydraulic brake fluid" is one product across brands, or topping off a low reservoir with whatever bleed kit is already open on the bench.

## Spoke tension vs. trueness

**Tension** is the measured pulling force on each spoke, read with a tensiometer and converted to kgf or Newtons via a chart. **Trueness** is the wheel's lateral and radial straightness on the stand. A wheel can look true with badly uneven tension, which shows up as recurring untrueness later.

*Practitioner usage:* "It's true on the stand, but the tension's uneven side to side — true it and check tension together, or it'll be back out in a month."

*Common misuse:* truing to visual straightness alone and treating a wheel that "looks right" as finished, without a tension reading confirming the fix will hold.

## Wheel dish

The offset of the rim relative to the hub's centerline, required on rear wheels (and disc-brake front wheels) because the hub isn't symmetric — the drive side (or rotor side) carries meaningfully higher spoke tension than the opposite side to keep the rim centered.

*Practitioner usage:* "Non-drive side is reading about 55% of drive-side tension — that's within the expected dish ratio for this hub, don't chase it toward equal tension."

*Common misuse:* trying to equalize tension across both sides of a dished wheel, which pulls the rim off-center rather than fixing anything.

## Bleed (brake) — generic term vs. brand-specific procedure

**Bleeding** removes air from a hydraulic brake system, but the mechanical procedure differs by brand — SRAM's syringe-to-syringe "Bleeding Edge" method versus Shimano's funnel-and-gravity method are not interchangeable steps with different fluids swapped in.

*Practitioner usage:* "It's a Bleeding Edge system, not a funnel bleed — different port, different sequence, not just a different fluid."

*Common misuse:* applying one brand's bleed sequence to another brand's brake because "it's all just bleeding," which leaves air trapped in a path the wrong procedure doesn't address.

## Hook wear (cassette/chainring) vs. general "worn out"

**Hook wear** is a specific, visually identifiable tooth profile — teeth deformed into a shark-fin or hook shape by prolonged contact with an elongated chain — distinct from generic wear statements that don't specify a mechanism.

*Practitioner usage:* "The 11T and 12T cogs are hooked — that's not just old, that's from riding the chain past its wear window."

*Common misuse:* calling a cassette "worn out" without checking for the hook profile specifically, which is the tell that a chain-only replacement will fail rather than hold.

## Headset preload vs. stem clamp torque

**Preload** is the compression setting that removes play from the headset bearings, adjusted via the top cap bolt with the stem loose. **Stem clamp torque** is the separate value that locks the stem to the steerer tube once preload is set. Confusing the two leads to fixing headset play by overtightening the stem clamp instead.

*Practitioner usage:* "Loosen the stem clamp bolts first, set preload at the top cap until the play's gone, then torque the stem clamp to spec — don't skip straight to cranking the stem."

*Common misuse:* tightening the stem clamp bolts to eliminate headset play, which can crush a carbon steerer without fixing the actual bearing preload.

## Cross-chaining

Running the chain at an extreme angle — large chainring to largest cassette cog, or small chainring to smallest cog — which increases lateral chain wear and drivetrain noise beyond what straight-line gear combinations produce.

*Practitioner usage:* "This customer rides big-ring/big-cog constantly — that's why the elongation rate is running faster than the mileage would suggest."

*Common misuse:* dismissing cross-chaining as only a noise or shifting-quality issue, when it also measurably accelerates the wear-rate math driving the chain-replacement threshold.

## Freehub vs. freewheel

A **freehub** is the ratcheting mechanism built into the rear hub body, with a separate cassette sliding onto it. A **freewheel** is a self-contained ratcheting unit that threads directly onto a hub without a cassette body. Servicing and replacement procedures differ completely between the two.

*Practitioner usage:* "This is an older freewheel hub, not a freehub — the cassette-removal tool in the wall rack won't work here."

*Common misuse:* using "cassette" and "freewheel" interchangeably, which leads to pulling the wrong tool and, on a freewheel, risking cross-threading the hub.
