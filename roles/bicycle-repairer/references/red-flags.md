# Red flags

Smell tests a senior bicycle repairer catches at intake or mid-teardown, before the bike goes back on the stand for a second time. Format for each: what it usually means, the first question to ask, and the data to pull before quoting or releasing the bike.

## 1. Chain elongation reads ≥0.75% on an 11/12/13-speed drivetrain

**Usually means:** the cassette cogs have already begun reshaping to match the worn chain — a new chain alone will not mesh cleanly with hooked teeth.

**First question:** "When was this drivetrain last checked, and at what reading?"

**Data to pull:** service log elongation history and mileage intervals between checks; visual hook-wear inspection of the 11T–13T cogs under a loupe; chainring wear as a separate check, since it typically lags cassette wear.

## 2. A newly installed chain skips under load within days

**Usually means:** the chain was replaced without measuring cassette wear first, and the cogs were already past the point a fresh chain can mesh with.

**First question:** "Was cassette wear checked and logged before this chain went on, or was it a straight swap?"

**Data to pull:** the elongation reading (if any) taken before the swap; cog hook-wear inspection now; whether the shop's own service log shows a prior reading that should have flagged full-drivetrain replacement.

## 3. A carbon component creaks after being "properly tightened"

**Usually means:** missing or insufficient carbon assembly paste at the clamp interface, not under-tightening — cranking the bolt down further on a dry interface risks crush damage without fixing the actual cause.

**First question:** "Was carbon assembly paste used at this interface, and to what Nm was it torqued?"

**Data to pull:** any torque-wrench record on file for this component; the frame/post/bar manufacturer's stamped or printed spec; visual inspection of the clamp faces for a dry, shiny (unpasted) contact surface.

## 4. Torque wrench absent from the work order on a carbon interface

**Usually means:** the fastener was tightened by feel, which on carbon is a binary risk — either under-tight (slip under load) or over-tight (silent fiber crush that fails later without warning).

**First question:** "What Nm was this torqued to, and with what tool?"

**Data to pull:** the component's stamped torque spec; re-torque the fastener with a calibrated click-type wrench and log the value before release.

## 5. Brake lever pumps up and firms after several squeezes

**Usually means:** air in the hydraulic line, not fluid contamination — the classic air-vs-boil distinction, and the two have different fixes.

**First question:** "Does the lever firm up after multiple pumps, or is it mushy from the very first squeeze after hard braking?"

**Data to pull:** fluid color and clarity at the reservoir; date of last bleed; whether the softness followed a specific hard-braking or long-descent event (points toward boiled fluid instead of simple air).

## 6. Brake lever mushy from the first pump right after a hard descent

**Usually means:** fluid has been pushed past its boiling point (common after sustained braking on a long descent with old or contaminated fluid), not simple air intrusion — a standard bleed won't fix a fluid that boils at a lower temperature than fresh fluid.

**First question:** "How old is the fluid in this system, and when was it last fully changed rather than topped off?"

**Data to pull:** fluid age/color; DOT fluid's known moisture-absorption behavior over time if applicable; recommend a full fluid change, not a top-off bleed.

## 7. Discolored or burnt-smelling brake fluid at the reservoir

**Usually means:** fluid contamination or heat degradation — its boiling point has likely dropped below a safe margin for hard use.

**First question:** "Has this system had a full fluid change, or only top-offs, since it was new?"

**Data to pull:** fluid color against a fresh reference sample; system age and brake-pad wear history (frequent hard braking accelerates fluid heat exposure); schedule a full bench bleed, not a partial one.

## 8. Two different hydraulic fluid families serviced at the same bench station same day

**Usually means:** cross-contamination risk from a shared funnel, syringe, or rag — a residual drop of DOT fluid in a mineral-oil system (or the reverse) swells the wrong seal compound.

**First question:** "Were separate kits used for the DOT job and the mineral-oil job today?"

**Data to pull:** which tools were used on which job; if shared, inspect both systems' seals for early swelling or a soft lever developing over the following weeks, and flag both for recheck.

## 9. Wheel goes out of true again within days of a truing job

**Usually means:** the wheel was trued to lateral straightness without correcting spoke tension — an uneven-tension wheel looks straight on the stand and moves again as the looser spokes fatigue and settle faster than the tight ones.

**First question:** "Were tension readings taken and logged, or was this trued by eye/twang alone?"

**Data to pull:** spoke tension readings against the wheel's expected drive-side/non-drive-side band; any spoke reading outside roughly ±10% of its side's average; check for a fatigued spoke nearing failure rather than just a loose one.

## 10. Customer declines a recommended repair on a safety-critical system

**Usually means:** the liability doesn't leave with the bike when the recommendation is declined verbally — it needs to be documented, not just remembered.

**First question:** "Has the specific finding and the decline been written on the work order, with a signature or timestamp?"

**Data to pull:** the measured finding driving the recommendation (Nm, elongation %, tension reading, fluid condition); confirm the work order carries the finding and the decline in writing before the bike is released.

## 11. Cassette or chainring shows shark-fin (hooked) tooth profile on visual inspection

**Usually means:** wear has progressed well past a chain-only replacement window, regardless of what the chain-wear indicator alone shows on a chain that hasn't been checked recently.

**First question:** "What's the current elongation reading, and how long since it was last checked?"

**Data to pull:** current chain wear percentage; mileage since the last logged check; whether the wear-rate acceleration (elongation risen faster between the last two checks than the two before that) confirms the visual finding.
