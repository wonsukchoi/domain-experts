# First-Line Supervisor of Office and Administrative Support — Vocabulary

### Minimum coverage
The smallest number of people who must be present at any given moment for the team's function to operate — an instant-in-time requirement, not an average or a daily total.
**In use:** "We hit minimum coverage on the daily total, but not during the noon window — that's the actual gap."
**Common misuse:** Treating it as a sum (total scheduled hours for the day or week) rather than a floor that must hold at every point in time.

### FLSA executive exemption
The Fair Labor Standards Act test determining whether a supervisory employee is exempt from overtime pay, based on primary duty, regular direction of two or more employees, and genuine input into personnel decisions.
**In use:** "Her title says supervisor, but she doesn't meet the executive exemption's duties test — she's non-exempt and owed overtime for these weeks."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a "supervisor" or "lead" job title automatically means the person is exempt from overtime.

### Primary duty test
The FLSA standard asking what an employee's main, most important job function actually is in practice — not what the job description says.
**In use:** "Primary duty test comes down to how she actually spends her week, not what her offer letter said."
**Common misuse:** Relying on the written job description alone instead of the employee's actual day-to-day time allocation.

### Progressive discipline
A structured sequence of increasingly serious consequences for repeated or escalating performance/conduct issues, designed to give an employee notice and a chance to correct course before termination.
**In use:** "This is his second occurrence in 90 days — that puts him at the written-warning tier, not verbal."
**Common misuse:** Treating the tiers as optional suggestions rather than a consistency requirement — skipping a tier for one employee while applying it to another for the same infraction.

### Disparate treatment
Treating similarly situated employees differently based on a protected characteristic (or in a way that looks like it), which can support a discrimination claim even without explicit discriminatory intent.
**In use:** "If we write up Employee A for lateness but let Employee B's identical lateness slide, that's a disparate-treatment exposure regardless of why we did it."
**Common misuse:** Assuming intent matters more than pattern — inconsistent enforcement itself is the legal risk, not just deliberate discrimination.

### Cross-training matrix
A table mapping which team members are trained (primary or backup) on which task types, used to spot single points of failure and plan training priorities.
**In use:** "The matrix shows Task A only has one backup — that's our next cross-training priority."
**Common misuse:** Building the matrix once and never updating it as people are trained, hired, or leave, so it stops reflecting actual coverage.

### Single point of failure
A task or function covered by only one trained person, such that their absence leaves nothing to fall back on.
**In use:** "We've got a single point of failure on invoice processing — Cole's the only one who knows that system."
**Common misuse:** Treating "we have a backup" as automatically meaning the single-point-of-failure risk is resolved, without checking if the backup is actually current/trained.

### Voluntary overtime
Extra hours an employee agrees to work, as opposed to mandatory overtime the employer requires regardless of employee preference.
**In use:** "Let's offer voluntary OT first before we consider mandating it."
**Common misuse:** Skipping straight to mandatory overtime or outside coverage without first checking who might volunteer.

### Backfill
Temporarily covering a role or task normally done by an absent employee, using another team member, cross-trained staff, or outside coverage.
**In use:** "We need a backfill for Tuesday's shift — Alvarez is out."
**Common misuse:** Using "backfill" to mean permanently replacing a departed employee, rather than temporary coverage for an absence.

### Capacity problem
A gap between the work a team is expected to handle and what its current staffing can actually deliver, regardless of how the existing hours are scheduled.
**In use:** "This isn't a scheduling issue anymore — after two adjustments, it's a capacity problem and needs a headcount conversation."
**Common misuse:** Continuing to treat a persistent backlog as solvable through better scheduling alone, when the real issue is not enough total capacity.

### Shift-boundary gap
A coverage shortfall that occurs specifically at the start/end of a shift, a break window, or right after an unplanned absence — not visible when only checking total scheduled hours.
**In use:** "The daily total looked fine, but there's a shift-boundary gap during the lunch rotation."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a schedule with adequate total hours has no gaps, without checking the specific windows where headcount dips.

### Documented coaching
A written, dated, specific record of a performance conversation, kept as part of an employee's file, distinct from an informal or undocumented chat.
**In use:** "That was documented coaching, not just a friendly reminder — it's in his file with today's date."
**Common misuse:** Treating a verbal conversation as equivalent to documented coaching when nothing was actually written down.
