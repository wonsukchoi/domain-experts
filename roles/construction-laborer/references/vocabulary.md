# Vocabulary

Terms generalists blur that a practitioner keeps sharply separate — each with the definition, a sentence a practitioner would actually say, and the common misuse.

### Competent person

An individual designated by the employer who is capable of identifying existing and predictable hazards and has authorization to take prompt corrective action — a specific OSHA-defined role with documented authority, not just an experienced worker.

*In use:* "Get the competent person to re-classify this before anyone steps back in — I can call it Type C, but my read isn't the one that goes on the log."

*Common misuse:* treating any senior laborer's judgment as equivalent to the competent person's documented sign-off, or assuming the title is informal seniority rather than a specific designated, authorized role.

### Soil classification (Type A / B / C)

A same-day determination of soil stability based on unconfined compressive strength and a set of exclusionary conditions (seepage, vibration, fissuring, prior disturbance) that can override a favorable strength test — Type A most stable, Type C least.

*In use:* "The geotech report calls this Type A, but we've got seepage at the base today — that's Type C as of this morning, report or no report."

*Common misuse:* relying on an old lab classification instead of reclassifying against current site conditions, since the exclusionary conditions are evaluated day-of, not once at the start of the job.

### Protective system (sloping, benching, shoring, shielding)

The four recognized categories of trench protection: sloping (cutting the wall back at an angle), benching (stepped cuts), shoring (support installed against the wall, e.g., hydraulic jacks or timber), and shielding (a rated box or trench shield workers stay inside). Selection depends on soil classification, depth, and available footprint.

*In use:* "We don't have room to slope past the gas main, so we're going to a box instead — that's shielding, not shoring, since nobody's touching the wall directly."

*Common misuse:* using "shoring" as a catch-all for any protective system, including shielding and sloping, which blurs which system is actually rated for the site's classification and depth.

### Spoil pile / spoil setback

Excavated material piled at the surface; the setback is the minimum required distance (at least 2 feet under OSHA) between the pile and the excavation edge, because the pile's weight is a surcharge load on the wall.

*In use:* "Move that spoil back another foot — it's inside the 2-foot line and it's sitting right over where we're about to shore."

*Common misuse:* treating the setback as a walkway-clearance convention rather than a load-bearing safety distance, which is why it's the first thing that gets ignored when space is tight.

### Signal person (qualified)

The individual designated to direct a crane or heavy-equipment operator using a pre-agreed signal set, qualified either by a documented practical assessment or a recognized certification — a specific, singular designation for the lift, not "whoever's standing closest."

*In use:* "I'm the signal person for this lift — nobody else waves at that operator until I hand it off."

*Common misuse:* letting more than one worker informally signal the same operator during a lift, which is how a load moves on a gesture the operator misreads as a command.

### Standard hand signals (ASME B30.5 set)

A fixed, standardized set of gestures (hoist, lower, stop, emergency stop, boom up/down, travel, dog everything, and others) used to direct crane and equipment operators, treated as the primary communication channel with radio as a pre-arranged backup.

*In use:* "Radio cut out mid-lift — good thing we agreed on hand signals first, operator never lost the stop signal."

*Common misuse:* defaulting to radio or shouted instructions as the main channel and treating hand signals as a formality, which removes the one channel that doesn't depend on a working device.

### Exclusion zone / swing radius

The physical area around an operating crane or equipment (including the boom's swing arc and the load's travel path) that must be kept clear of personnel and staged material not directly involved in the lift.

*In use:* "Swing radius runs to that rebar stake — hold everything on the far side until the load's set down."

*Common misuse:* assuming the exclusion zone is fixed for the whole shift rather than re-checking it whenever the boom angle, load, or ground position changes.

### Focus Four hazards

OSHA's four leading categories of construction fatalities — falls, struck-by, caught-in/between, and electrocution — used as a checklist for scanning a multi-trade site rather than a single-hazard mindset.

*In use:* "Before I set foot in that zone, that's caught-in/between from the trench, struck-by from the crane, and electrocution from the rough-in panel, all in the same twenty feet."

*Common misuse:* treating hazard awareness as one category at a time (just watching for the trench, say) instead of scanning for all four whenever moving through an unfamiliar zone.

### Utility locate / call-before-you-dig

The process of having underground utilities marked (paint, flags, or stakes) before excavation begins, establishing a boundary that constrains where a protective system's footprint — especially a slope's required offset — can actually be built.

*In use:* "The locate puts that gas line four feet off our wall — that's why sloping's not an option on this side, doesn't matter what the depth table says in isolation."

*Common misuse:* treating the locate marking as advisory information rather than a hard boundary that can eliminate sloping as a viable protective-system option entirely.

### Caught-in/between

The Focus Four hazard category covering trench collapses, cave-ins, and being pinned between equipment and a fixed object — distinct from struck-by (moving object hits a stationary worker) even though both can occur in the same excavation scenario.

*In use:* "That's a caught-in/between exposure, not struck-by — if that wall lets go, nothing's moving toward him, the ground itself is the hazard."

*Common misuse:* using "struck-by" and "caught-in/between" interchangeably when describing an excavation incident, which matters for identifying the correct control (protective system versus exclusion zone).

### Hazard-increasing event

Any condition change — rainfall, new vibration source, nearby excavation starting, a load passing overhead — that voids a prior inspection or clearance and requires re-verification before work continues.

*In use:* "It rained an hour ago, so that clearance from this morning doesn't count anymore — get the competent person back out before anyone goes back in."

*Common misuse:* treating the morning's inspection or classification as good for the whole shift regardless of what's changed on-site since, instead of re-triggering a check on each qualifying event.

### Dog everything (hold)

The hand signal indicating all crane motion stops and the load remains exactly where it is, suspended — distinct from "stop," which halts a specific motion but doesn't necessarily mean the operator holds position indefinitely.

*In use:* "Dog it there — I need everyone clear before we set that beam down, don't lower on your own judgment."

*Common misuse:* treating "stop" and "dog everything" as the same signal; "stop" can precede another motion resuming quickly, while "dog everything" is a deliberate hold until further signal.
