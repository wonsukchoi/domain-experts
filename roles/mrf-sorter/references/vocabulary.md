# Vocabulary

Terms generalists conflate or misuse when discussing MRF sorting-line work; sorters keep them separate because the classification and the response — quality pull versus safety stop — differ by which term actually applies.

### Bale purity (grade)

The percentage of a baled commodity that meets the target material specification, expressed against a buyer contract's price-tier bands rather than as a simple average quality score.

**In use:** "We're at 91% on this PET bale — that's the $300/ton band, not the $380/ton band we normally hit at 96%."

**Common misuse:** treating a purity percentage as a smoothly linear cost, when contract bands make it a step function — a few points crossing a tier boundary changes the price on the entire bale's tonnage, not just the out-of-spec fraction.

### Outthrows / prohibitives

ISRI (Institute of Scrap Recycling Industries) terminology for material in a bale that doesn't belong: "outthrows" are the wrong grade of the same general material (e.g., colored paper in a white-paper bale); "prohibitives" are materials that actively damage processing or product (e.g., wax-coated paper, plastic film in a paper bale).

**In use:** "The buyer's spec caps prohibitives at 1% — outthrows have a looser tolerance, but prohibitives don't, because those are the ones that damage the paper mill's process."

**Common misuse:** using "contamination" as a catch-all when the two categories carry different tolerances and different downstream consequences in the buyer's grading.

### Positive sort vs. negative sort

**Positive sort** is pulling the target material off the line (e.g., picking PET bottles out of a mixed stream). **Negative sort** is pulling contamination off the line while target material stays and rides through (e.g., pulling non-fiber items off a paper line). The station's default action — pull it or leave it — is opposite between the two.

**In use:** "This is a negative-sort station on the fiber line — if you're not sure what an item is, leave it, don't pull it, since pulling the wrong thing here removes good material instead of contamination."

**Common misuse:** applying a positive-sort mindset (pull what you recognize) at a negative-sort station, which inverts the intended action and actively removes recoverable material.

### Nip point / pinch point

The specific location on a conveyor or piece of processing equipment (a pulley, a roller, a star-screen shaft) where two surfaces come together and can trap a hand or clothing — the fixed hazard location that a picking station's reach distance and guard rail are engineered against.

**In use:** "That item's past the nip point guard line — let it go to the next station, don't lean past the rail for it."

**Common misuse:** treating "nip point" as a general term for "somewhere on the machine that's dangerous," when it refers to a specific, identifiable trap location with a defined guarding distance around it.

### Line-stop cord (E-stop pull cord)

An emergency-stop cable running the length of a sorting line that any sorter can pull to halt the belt immediately — the primary hazard-response tool, intended to be used proactively on any line-stop-classified item, not held back as a last resort.

**In use:** "Swollen battery at station 4 — cord's already pulled, we'll sort out next steps once the belt's stopped."

**Common misuse:** treating a line-stop pull as an escalation of last resort that should be avoided if possible, when the correct posture is to pull it immediately on any qualifying hazard and accept the brief production cost.

### Thermal runaway

The self-accelerating heat-generating chemical reaction inside a damaged, defective, or improperly handled lithium-ion cell that can progress to fire or explosion — the specific mechanism behind battery-related MRF fires, distinct from an external ignition source.

**In use:** "We don't wait for visible smoke — a swollen case is already a thermal-runaway indicator, and that's the immediate-stop trigger."

**Common misuse:** assuming a battery is only dangerous once it's visibly smoking or on fire, when swelling, heat, or a hissing sound are earlier and equally actionable signs of the same process.

### Residue rate

The percentage of total material entering a MRF's process that ends up landfilled rather than recovered as a marketable commodity, driven by both contamination in the input stream and material lost during sorting.

**In use:** "Our residue rate ticked up this month — check whether that's more contamination coming in or a station running under its normal pick accuracy."

**Common misuse:** treating residue rate purely as a program-level (coordinator) metric with no station-level driver, when a specific station's coverage gaps or accuracy dips are a direct, traceable contributor.

### Star screen (disc screen)

A mechanical screen made of rotating discs that separates material by size and shape ahead of or alongside hand-sorting — typically used to pull 2D material (paper, film) away from 3D material (containers) before it reaches a picking station.

**In use:** "Check the star screen spacing before blaming the pickers — if it's mis-set, containers are riding through to the fiber line regardless of how well the station is picking."

**Common misuse:** assuming all separation happens at the hand-sort stations, when mechanical pre-sorting equipment like a star screen does much of the coarse separation before a sorter ever sees the material.

### Positive-pressure hazard item

A sealed container capable of holding pressurized contents (propane cylinders, aerosol cans, CO2 cartridges) that poses a rupture or explosion risk if compressed, punctured, or exposed to heat — classified separately from battery hazards because the failure mechanism and the required handling differ.

**In use:** "That's a full propane cylinder, not an empty one — treat it as an immediate stop, not a routine metals pull."

**Common misuse:** lumping all "hazardous items" into one response protocol, when a pressure vessel and a damaged battery require different isolation methods and pose different failure modes.

### Reach-distance discipline

The practice of confining physical pulls to a station's engineered reach zone relative to the nearest nip point, rather than leaning, stepping, or reaching across a guard rail to extend effective reach.

**In use:** "That was a good pull, but it was past your reach line — let items like that go to the next station instead of leaning over the rail."

**Common misuse:** treating reach-distance limits as a suggestion that can be stretched for a valuable item, when the distance is set specifically to keep contact with the nip point physically impossible from a normal working position.

### Contamination (quality) vs. hazardous item (safety)

Two categories requiring different responses: contamination is a material that lowers bale purity and grade but poses no immediate danger (glass, film, wrong-resin plastic); a hazardous item poses an injury, fire, or explosion risk (batteries, pressure vessels, sharps) independent of its effect on purity.

**In use:** "Don't log that swollen battery as a contamination pull — it's a safety event, log it as a line-stop with a time and station."

**Common misuse:** logging or reporting hazardous-item pulls the same way as routine contamination pulls, which hides the fire/injury-risk trend inside a quality-metric report where nobody is looking for it.
