# Community Health Worker — Vocabulary

### SDOH (Social Determinants of Health)
The non-clinical conditions — housing, food security, transportation, income, safety — that shape health outcomes as much as or more than clinical care.
**In use:** "Her A1C isn't moving because the SDOH screen flagged food insecurity — she can't afford consistent meals, let alone diabetic-appropriate ones."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a single checkbox ("SDOH: yes/no") instead of a multi-domain screen where each domain needs its own referral and follow-up.

### Closed-loop referral
A referral tracked through to confirmed outcome (appointment attended, resource received) rather than considered complete once made.
**In use:** "That's not a closed-loop referral yet — we made the call, but nobody's confirmed she actually got in."
**Common misuse:** Counting "referral made" and "referral completed" as the same metric, which hides the actual failure point (referral sent but never scheduled, or scheduled but never attended).

### Warm handoff
A referral made through a direct, personal introduction (a call together, an accompanied visit) rather than handing over a phone number or flyer.
**In use:** "Cold referral to that clinic gets maybe a third of clients in the door — do a warm handoff instead."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to mean "a friendly phone call," when the defining feature is the direct introduction between client and receiving resource, not the tone.

### Teach-back
A comprehension-check method where the client demonstrates or restates instructions in their own words, rather than answering "does that make sense?"
**In use:** "Don't just ask if she understood the medication schedule — do teach-back and have her walk you through tomorrow morning."
**Common misuse:** Confused with simply repeating the instruction back verbatim, which tests memory, not understanding — real teach-back requires the client to apply it to their own situation.

### Scope of practice
The defined boundary of tasks a CHW is trained and authorized to do, beyond which anything must be referred to a licensed clinician or social worker.
**In use:** "That's outside my scope — I can screen for depression symptoms, but I can't diagnose or treat it."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a soft guideline flexible to the relationship, when it's a hard boundary that protects both the client and the CHW's role integrity.

### Health literacy
A person's capacity to obtain, process, and act on basic health information — distinct from general literacy or intelligence.
**In use:** "It's not that he doesn't care about his blood pressure — the health literacy gap is that 'sodium' on a label doesn't register as 'salt' to him."
**Common misuse:** Equated with reading ability alone, ignoring numeracy, verbal comprehension, and system-navigation skills that also fall under health literacy.

### Peer-based trust
Rapport built on shared lived experience or community membership rather than clinical or institutional authority.
**In use:** "She opened up to the CHW about the eviction notice in a way she never would with the hospital case manager."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to be automatic because a CHW is "from the community," when it must still be earned visit by visit and can be lost quickly.

### Mandated reporting
The legal obligation to report suspected abuse, neglect, or imminent danger to authorities, which overrides normal confidentiality.
**In use:** "Once she disclosed the child wasn't being fed, this became a mandated-reporting situation, not a coaching conversation."
**Common misuse:** Treated as discretionary or something to weigh against preserving the relationship — it is a legal floor, not a judgment call.

### Motivational interviewing (MI)
A counseling style that draws out a client's own reasons for change rather than telling them what to do, often structured around OARS (open questions, affirmations, reflections, summaries).
**In use:** "Don't lecture him about the diet — use MI, ask what he's noticed about how he feels after eating out."
**Common misuse:** Reduced to reciting the OARS acronym mechanically without genuinely adapting to what the client says, which reads as scripted and undermines trust.

### Caseload
The set of clients formally assigned to a CHW for ongoing screening, referral, and follow-up.
**In use:** "My caseload is 40 right now, but only about half are in an active referral cycle at any given time."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a static headcount rather than a mix of active-crisis, active-referral, and maintenance-phase clients requiring very different contact frequencies.

### Barrier
A specific, concrete obstacle preventing a client from following through on a health-related action — not a general statement of hardship.
**In use:** "The barrier isn't 'poverty' — it's specifically that the pharmacy closes before her shift ends."
**Common misuse:** Named too broadly to act on ("financial barriers") instead of narrowed to something a referral can actually address ("can't afford the $40 copay specifically").

### Community health worker core competencies
The standardized skill domains (e.g., outreach, cultural mediation, care coordination, health education, advocacy) defined by frameworks like the C3 Project, used to structure CHW training and scope.
**In use:** "Care coordination is one of our core competencies, but diagnosis is explicitly not — that's the line."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to be identical across every CHW program, when core-competency frameworks are adapted locally and scope varies by employer and state.
