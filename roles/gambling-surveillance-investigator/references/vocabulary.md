# Gambling Surveillance Officer and Gambling Investigator — Vocabulary

### Past-posting
Adding a chip to a wager after the outcome is already known (e.g., after a card is exposed or a dice roll settles), so the "bet" only existed once the result favored it.
**In use:** "The extra $500 landed on the spot 2.3 seconds after the card was exposed — that's a past-post, not a dealer miscount."
**Common misuse:** Lumped together with any payout discrepancy, when the defining feature is specifically that the wager amount changed *after* the result was known, not just that the payout was wrong.

### Pinching
Removing a chip from a losing wager before the dealer registers the loss, the mirror-image technique to past-posting.
**In use:** "Watch the losing spot, not just the winning one — pinching shows up as chips disappearing, not appearing."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to require the same review technique as past-posting; pinching needs the officer watching the losing side of the layout, which trainees often skip because attention defaults to where the payout happens.

### Hole-carding
Legally viewing a dealer's face-down card through an imperfect dealing procedure (an exposed edge, a consistent tell), a form of advantage play, not cheating.
**In use:** "This isn't a marked-card case — the player's read is coming from the dealer's hole-card exposure procedure, which is a game-protection fix, not a security escalation."
**Common misuse:** Treated as inherently suspicious or accusation-worthy, when it's legal exploitation of a procedural flaw; the correct response is fixing the dealer's technique, not confronting the player.

### Shuffle tracking
Legal advantage play that follows a cluster of favorable cards through an imperfect shuffle to bet more when that cluster is likely to come into play.
**In use:** "The bet spread correlates with shoe position, not with the count directly — this reads as shuffle tracking, not straightforward card counting."
**Common misuse:** Conflated with card counting; the evidence needed to demonstrate each is different (shoe-position correlation versus running-count correlation), and pulling the wrong data wastes review time.

### Marker
A credit instrument extended to a player at the table or cage, repayable in a defined window, distinct from a cash wager.
**In use:** "The marker was signed and countersigned before play started — check the credit slip's timestamp against the camera, not just the dollar amount."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to be purely a credit/finance matter outside surveillance's scope, when marker issuance and repayment timing is itself part of the control trail surveillance verifies.

### Drop
The total cash and markers deposited into a table's drop box over a shift, the raw input figure used to calculate win/loss against theoretical.
**In use:** "The drop was $12,450 for the shift — that number by itself doesn't tell you anything about integrity, only volume."
**Common misuse:** Treated as synonymous with revenue or win; drop is gross cash-in, not the table's actual result, which also accounts for fills, credits, and the closing tray count.

### Soft count / hard count
Soft count is the currency (paper money) count from drop boxes; hard count is the coin/token count from slot drop, both done under a documented multi-person protocol and recorded on camera.
**In use:** "The soft count room feed has to run continuously during the count, no exceptions — that's the one camera assignment that never gets deprioritized."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to be a back-office bookkeeping task outside surveillance's real interest, when the count room is one of the highest-value camera assignments precisely because large uncounted cash is briefly concentrated there.

### Reportable event
A defined incident type or dollar threshold (commonly around $2,500 in state minimum internal control standards) that triggers a mandatory surveillance log entry and extended footage retention, regardless of whether wrongdoing is confirmed.
**In use:** "This dispute crosses the reportable-event threshold — log it and flag the footage for extended retention now, before anyone determines whether it's actually a problem."
**Common misuse:** Treated as discretionary ("I'll log it if it turns out to matter"), when the threshold is a mechanical trigger independent of the officer's read on the incident's seriousness.

### Structuring
Deliberately breaking cash transactions into amounts under the $10,000 Currency Transaction Report threshold to avoid triggering the report.
**In use:** "Three cage transactions of $4,000 each within an hour, same patron — that's structuring, and it's a same-day notification to compliance, not a wait-and-see."
**Common misuse:** Confused with simply having several transactions in a day; the pattern (amounts clustered just under the threshold, spread across windows or times specifically to stay under it) is what makes it structuring rather than ordinary play.

### Watch list / known-cheat database
A shared database (historically the Griffin Investigations "black book," now largely biometric systems like Biometrica) of individuals previously identified as cheats or flagged advantage players.
**In use:** "We got a plausible match against the watch list — confirm quietly via ID at the cage before anyone approaches the player."
**Common misuse:** Treated as proof on its own; a facial-recognition match is a lead requiring confirmation, not a confirmed identity that justifies an immediate floor confrontation.

### Chip tray inventory / table par
The documented starting ("par") chip value in a dealer's tray at table opening, reconciled against fills, credits, and the closing count.
**In use:** "The tray opened at par of $8,000 — any variance from that has to trace through the fills and credits before it's a real discrepancy."
**Common misuse:** Skipped as a formality; without a verified opening par, a closing-count discrepancy can't be attributed to anything specific.

### Eye in the sky
The historical term for a casino's overhead surveillance vantage (originally a physical catwalk, now a dome camera network), still used informally for the surveillance function generally.
**In use:** "Eye in the sky caught the whole exchange — pull PIT3-11 for the primary angle."
**Common misuse:** Used as if it referred to a single all-seeing camera, when actual coverage is a matrix of specific, individually-logged camera positions with real gaps and angles that have to be checked, not assumed.

### Retention window
The default period (commonly a 7-day baseline under state minimum internal control standards) recorded footage is kept before automatic overwrite, extendable only via an active flag.
**In use:** "That table's footage rolls off in two days — flag it for extended retention now, we'll finish the report next week."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to reset or pause automatically when an incident is "important," when in practice it requires an explicit officer action to extend before the standard cycle purges it.
