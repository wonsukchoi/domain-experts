# Vocabulary

Terms generalists conflate or misapply. Each: definition, a sentence a practitioner would actually say, and the common misuse.

## Food cost % vs. prime cost %

**Food cost %** is cost of goods sold ÷ food sales for a period or a single item. **Prime cost %** is (food cost + labor cost) ÷ total revenue — the combined number that predicts whether the operation can survive, because the two lines trade off against each other constantly.

*In use:* "Food cost looks fine at 29%, but labor crept to 34% covering it — prime cost is 63% and that's the number the owner needs to see, not the 29%."

*Common misuse:* reporting food cost % alone as "how the kitchen is doing" — a kitchen can hit its food-cost target by pushing labor-intensive scratch prep that blows the labor line, and prime cost is the only view that catches it.

## AP vs. EP cost

**AP (as-purchased) cost** is the invoice price for the ingredient in the form it arrives. **EP (edible portion) cost** is what that ingredient actually costs per usable pound after trim, cooking loss, or bone/skin removal — EP cost = AP cost ÷ yield %.

*In use:* "The fillet's $11.20 a pound AP, but at 88% yield after pin-boning it's really $12.73 EP — that's the number that goes on the costing card."

*Common misuse:* costing a recipe off the invoice price directly, which understates true cost on anything with meaningful trim or cooking loss (whole fish, bone-in proteins, trimmed produce) and quietly erodes margin nobody can trace.

## Yield percentage

The ratio of usable product after trim/cooking to the original purchased weight — a fixed-enough number per product and prep method that it belongs on the costing card, re-tested when the vendor, cut, or grade changes.

*In use:* "New purveyor's chicken breasts are trimming heavier — yield dropped from 92% to 86%, so the plate cost went up even though the invoice price didn't move."

*Common misuse:* treating yield as a constant that never needs re-testing, so a vendor switch or grade downgrade silently inflates EP cost while the costing card still shows the old number.

## Menu engineering: Star / Plowhorse / Puzzle / Dog

The four-quadrant classification (Kasavana & Smith) of a menu item by popularity (mix %) against a fair-share threshold and contribution margin against the category average. **Star**: popular + high margin. **Plowhorse**: popular + low margin. **Puzzle**: unpopular + high margin. **Dog**: unpopular + low margin.

*In use:* "The short rib's a Star — don't touch it. The chicken parm's our biggest Plowhorse; it earns its spot on volume, not margin, so we fix the recipe cost, not the price."

*Common misuse:* classifying by sales volume alone and calling the top-seller a "Star" regardless of margin — a high-volume, low-margin item is a Plowhorse, and treating it like a Star (protecting it from any cost engineering) leaves money on the table every single ticket.

## Contribution margin (CM)

Menu price minus plate (food) cost for a single item — the dollar amount, not the percentage, that item contributes toward labor and overhead once ingredients are paid for.

*In use:* "Salmon's food cost % looks worse than the risotto, but its CM dollars are actually higher — we sell fewer of them, but each one carries more toward the light bill."

*Common misuse:* comparing items purely on food-cost % and concluding the lower-percentage item is "more profitable" — a $4 appetizer at 20% food cost contributes $3.20; a $25 entrée at 32% food cost contributes $17.00. CM dollars, not the percentage, decide which item actually pays the bills.

## Temperature danger zone / 2-hour–4-hour rule

The 40°F–140°F range in which bacteria multiply fastest (FDA Food Code). The 2-hour/4-hour rule: food in the danger zone under 2 cumulative hours is safe to use or re-chill; 2–4 hours, use immediately (no re-chilling); over 4 cumulative hours, discard — no exception for cost.

*In use:* "That tray's been out since prep started, call it 3 hours — use it in the next ticket or it's gone, we're not re-chilling it."

*Common misuse:* resetting the clock every time a product goes back in the cooler — the 2/4-hour count is cumulative time in the danger zone across the product's life, not per instance out of refrigeration.

## FIFO / par level

**FIFO** (first in, first out) is rotating stock so the oldest received product is used first, enforced by date-labeling on arrival. **Par level** is the min/max quantity that should be on hand for an ingredient, set against actual usage velocity, not convenience.

*In use:* "Par on heavy cream is 1 to 3 cases — we're ordering to that number, not to whatever felt right last week, and every new case goes behind what's already labeled."

*Common misuse:* setting par levels once and never revisiting them as the menu or covers change, so the kitchen either runs out mid-service or carries excess inventory that ages past its usable window.

## Sales per labor hour (SPLH)

Total sales for a period divided by total labor hours worked in that period — the scheduling anchor used to size a shift against forecasted volume rather than habit.

*In use:* "Forecast says 180 covers Friday — at our $32 SPLH target that's roughly 40 labor hours for the kitchen, so we're overstaffed by one line cook on the current schedule."

*Common misuse:* scheduling to a fixed headcount every week regardless of forecast, which is the single most common driver of labor-cost (and therefore prime-cost) drift.

## 86'd

Kitchen shorthand for an item that's out of stock or off the menu for the shift — communicated immediately to the front of house, not discovered mid-service by a server placing an order that can't be filled.

*In use:* "We're 86 the halibut as of now — last portion just went out."

*Common misuse:* treating an item that's 86'd more than twice in a month as simply "popular" rather than as a par-level or vendor-schedule problem that needs fixing, not just restating.
