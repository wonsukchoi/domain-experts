# Correctional Officer — Vocabulary

### Classification override
A documented deviation from the score produced by the objective classification instrument, applied when a specific fact outside the instrument's scoring factors (a threat statement, an undocumented enemy conflict) warrants a different custody level or special-management flag.
**In use:** "The instrument scored him medium, but I'm writing an override for the victim-threat statement — special management, not a custody-level change."
**Common misuse:** treating "override" as a synonym for "ignore the score" rather than a documented, justified deviation that still requires a supervisor countersignature in most policies.

### Some evidence standard
The minimum evidentiary bar for a prison disciplinary hearing finding, established in *Superintendent v. Hill* (1985) — lower than preponderance or beyond-reasonable-doubt, but still requires the finding to be traceable to specific evidence in the record.
**In use:** "The report doesn't meet 'some evidence' — there's a conclusion but nothing in the record that supports it."
**Common misuse:** assuming "some evidence" means any officer statement automatically suffices — a bare conclusion with zero supporting facts still fails this standard on appeal.

### Use-of-force continuum
The documented range of response levels (presence, verbal, soft-hands, hard-hands, less-lethal, lethal) an officer can apply, matched to the threat level presented, not a mandatory sequence to climb through from the bottom every time.
**In use:** "The threat level at first contact justified entering at hard-hands — we don't have to document a verbal-only attempt that had no realistic chance of working."
**Common misuse:** treating the continuum as a checklist requiring every lower tier to be attempted first regardless of the actual threat presented.

### PREA (Prison Rape Elimination Act)
Federal law and national standards (28 CFR Part 115) requiring facilities to prevent, detect, and respond to sexual abuse/harassment in custody, including mandatory screening, reporting, and investigation protocols.
**In use:** "That's a PREA-relevant disclosure — it needs a documented housing response, not just a log entry."
**Common misuse:** treating a PREA disclosure as fully handled once logged, without a documented follow-up action (housing change, investigation referral).

### Objective jail classification instrument
A point-based scoring tool using defined, documented factors (charge severity, prior record, institutional history, etc.) to assign a custody level, designed to reduce the bias inherent in unstructured officer judgment.
**In use:** "Run the instrument first — then tell me what it missed that justifies your override."
**Common misuse:** treating the instrument's score as immutable and refusing to override even when a specific documented safety fact clearly warrants it — the instrument is a floor for consistency, not a ceiling on judgment.

### Transition-point risk window
The specific, predictable events (booking, adverse court outcome, housing reassignment, disciplinary placement) associated with elevated suicide risk in custody, used to trigger rescreening rather than relying on a single intake screen.
**In use:** "He's got a segregation placement today — that's a rescreen trigger, not optional."
**Common misuse:** treating the intake screen as sufficient coverage for the entire length of stay, missing the risk spikes at later transition points.

### Special-management flag
A housing/contact restriction applied on top of (not instead of) the classification score, triggered by a specific safety fact (victim threat, enemy conflict, PREA vulnerability) rather than by the point total.
**In use:** "Custody level stays medium — the flag is what changes his phone and mail restrictions, not the housing tier."
**Common misuse:** conflating a special-management flag with a full custody-level reclassification — they're separate decisions that happen to be recorded together.

### Contraband search pattern randomization
Deliberately varying search timing, target selection, and method to prevent a population from learning and evading a predictable schedule.
**In use:** "We're pulling cells by tip plus a random sample this week, not the same roster order as last month."
**Common misuse:** assuming any regular search schedule has deterrent value regardless of predictability — a fixed pattern loses most of its recovery value after the population learns it.

### De-escalation
Verbal and positional tactics intended to reduce the intensity of a developing incident before force becomes necessary — a tool used when time and circumstance allow, not a universal substitute for force in an active-threat situation.
**In use:** "There wasn't time or distance for de-escalation once he lunged — that's a first-contact force decision, not a failure to de-escalate."
**Common misuse:** treating any use of force as evidence that de-escalation "should have" been tried, without accounting for whether the threat presented allowed the time for it.

### Institutional behavior history
An inmate's documented record of conduct within a correctional facility (infractions, violence, compliance), distinct from their pre-incarceration criminal record, and one of the classification instrument's scoring factors.
**In use:** "No institutional history to score yet — this is his first time in this system, so that factor comes in at zero, not a penalty."
**Common misuse:** conflating a zero institutional-history score with a positive safety indicator — it may simply mean no data exists yet, not that the inmate is low-risk.
