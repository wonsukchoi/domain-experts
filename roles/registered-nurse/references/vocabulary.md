# Vocabulary

### NEWS2 (National Early Warning Score 2)
An aggregate score (0–20) built from six vital-sign parameters plus a supplemental-oxygen flag, used to trigger review and escalation thresholds.
**In use:** "She was a NEWS2 of 4 at 0900, now she's 14 — that's not a gradual climb, that's collapse."
**Common misuse:** Treating the aggregate total as the only trigger and missing that any single parameter scoring 3 mandates urgent review on its own, even with a low total.

### MEWS (Modified Early Warning Score)
An earlier, simpler precursor to NEWS2 using similar vital-sign bands but different point values and thresholds; some facilities still use it.
**In use:** "This unit still runs MEWS, not NEWS2 — don't import the NEWS2 cutoffs, the numbers don't map 1:1."
**Common misuse:** Assuming MEWS and NEWS2 point values and thresholds are interchangeable across facilities — they use different scales for the same parameters.

### SBAR (Situation-Background-Assessment-Recommendation)
A structured communication format for escalating a change in patient condition or handing off care.
**In use:** "Give me the SBAR before you call — I don't want to hear the story, I want the ask."
**Common misuse:** Delivering Situation and Assessment but skipping a concrete Recommendation, leaving the listener to guess what action is wanted.

### Failure to rescue
A death or serious harm following a complication that a timely recognition and intervention could plausibly have prevented — a specific outcome measure, not a general synonym for medical error.
**In use:** "That wasn't a medication error, it was failure to rescue — the tachycardia was documented for three hours before anyone acted on it."
**Common misuse:** Using it loosely for any bad outcome, including ones with no preceding recognized complication to have rescued from.

### Scope of practice
The legally defined boundary, set by the state board of nursing, of what an RN may independently assess, decide, and do versus what requires a physician/APRN order or judgment.
**In use:** "I can tell you what I'm seeing, but I can't order the CT — that's outside scope."
**Common misuse:** Treating scope of practice as a competence or confidence question ("I'm sure enough to act") rather than a legal boundary independent of how correct the RN's read turns out to be.

### Rapid response team (RRT)
A pre-arrest escalation resource, typically activated by an early-warning-score threshold or defined clinical criteria, distinct from a code team.
**In use:** "Her NEWS2 hit 14 — that's an RRT criterion on its own, call it before she arrests, not after."
**Common misuse:** Reserving RRT activation mentally for near-code situations only, when the entire design intent is to intervene well before that point.

### Delegation (five rights)
The NCSBN framework — right task, right circumstance, right person, right direction/communication, right supervision/evaluation — governing what an RN may hand to unlicensed or lower-scope staff.
**In use:** "Right circumstance is the one that fails here — she's four hours post-op and trending, this isn't a delegate-the-vitals patient right now."
**Common misuse:** Treating delegation as transferring accountability along with the task, rather than the RN retaining responsibility for interpreting and acting on the result.

### Rights of medication administration
The classic five — right patient, right drug, right dose, right route, right time — with many facility policies extending to right documentation and right reason/response.
**In use:** "We're behind — protect right time and right documentation first, those are the ones that actually hurt someone if skipped."
**Common misuse:** Treating all five (or more) rights as equally flexible under time pressure, when lateness on time-critical drugs (insulin, anticoagulants, sepsis antibiotics) is itself the adverse event.

### Acuity
A real-time measure of a patient's instability and the intensity of nursing care currently required — not a fixed property of the diagnosis.
**In use:** "Her diagnosis hasn't changed, but her acuity has — she needs eyes on her every 15 minutes now."
**Common misuse:** Conflating acuity with how severe a diagnosis sounds on paper, rather than the patient's current, moment-to-moment stability.

### ABCs (Airway, Breathing, Circulation)
The acute-triage sequence for prioritizing which patient or which problem gets addressed first.
**In use:** "Airway and breathing come before I deal with her pain request — ABCs, not who called first."
**Common misuse:** Substituting Maslow's hierarchy of needs as a prioritization tool — Maslow describes motivation, not acute clinical triage, and was never designed to rank instability.

### Titration
Adjusting a medication dose or infusion rate against a specific measured target and a defined reassessment interval (e.g., oxygen flow to a target SpO2, insulin drip to a target glucose).
**In use:** "I'm titrating the O2 to keep her at 92% or above, rechecking every 15 minutes."
**Common misuse:** Adjusting a rate or dose without a stated target and reassessment interval — that's guessing, not titrating.

### Standing order / protocol
A pre-approved order set that authorizes a specific nursing action under specific, named trigger conditions, without a new physician order each time.
**In use:** "The standing order covers oxygen up to 4L to keep her above 92% — past that, I need a call."
**Common misuse:** Treating a standing order as blanket permission for the general category of action, rather than checking that this specific situation matches the order's stated trigger conditions.

### Subtle cues
Non-vital-sign observations (patient's affect, family concern, "not himself" reports) that frequently precede measurable score changes.
**In use:** "Nothing on the monitor yet, but his wife says he's not himself — I'm rechecking him now, not at the next scheduled round."
**Common misuse:** Dismissing subtle cues as unquantifiable and therefore not actionable until an objective score confirms them.
