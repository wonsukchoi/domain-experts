# Vocabulary

Terms generalists use loosely that a supervisor keeps sharply separate, because the distinction changes what gets staffed, scored, or reported.

## Credit vs. room count

**Credit** is a workload-weighted unit — a checkout typically counts as a full credit (1.0), a stayover as a half credit (0.5) — used to convert a mix of room types into a single staffing number. **Room count** is just how many physical rooms exist in a section, with no weighting for the type of clean each needs.

**In use:** "That section shows 20 rooms, but it's 14 checkouts — that's 14 credits, not 10. Staff it like the heavier load it actually is."

**Common misuse:** assigning sections by room count alone, which understaffs a checkout-heavy section and overstaffs a stayover-heavy one even though both "have 20 rooms."

## Stayover vs. checkout clean

**Stayover** service is for a guest continuing their stay — a lighter touch (make beds, restock, empty trash). **Checkout clean** is the full clean required before a room can be resold to a new arrival, done after a guest departs.

**In use:** "We're not behind on stayovers, we're behind on checkouts — that's the number that actually blocks tonight's arrivals."

**Common misuse:** treating all "unclean rooms" as one undifferentiated backlog instead of recognizing that checkout backlog blocks revenue and stayover backlog mostly blocks guest satisfaction.

## PAR level

The standard quantity of linen, amenities, or supplies a property keeps on hand relative to its room or unit count — commonly stated as a multiple (e.g., "3 PAR" of sheets means enough sets for three full changes across the property).

**In use:** "We're down to 1.5 PAR on queen sheets because of the linen service delay — that's below the 2 PAR floor where we'd start rationing stayover changes."

**Common misuse:** treating PAR as a one-time purchasing decision instead of a level that needs monitoring against actual usage and vendor lead time.

## Turn / same-day turn

The process of taking a room from a departing guest's checkout to fully ready for a new arrival within the same day — the operational deadline that makes checkout-credit work non-discretionary.

**In use:** "We can't promise a 2pm turn on that room if housekeeping doesn't get to it until 1:30 — tell front desk to hold the early check-in request."

**Common misuse:** assuming "turn" just means "clean the room," without the same-day deadline that makes it the highest-priority category of work.

## GHS / SDS

**GHS** (Globally Harmonized System) is the labeling standard requiring a product identifier, pictogram, and hazard statement on chemical containers, including secondary containers like spray bottles. **SDS** (Safety Data Sheet) is the detailed per-product document covering hazards, handling, dilution, and first aid, which OSHA's Hazard Communication Standard (29 CFR 1910.1200) requires to be accessible to employees.

**In use:** "Pull the SDS before you argue about dilution ratio — don't guess from the bottle color."

**Common misuse:** treating the SDS binder as a compliance-audit prop rather than the actual reference for dilution and PPE decisions on the floor.

## Contact time / dwell time

The minimum period a disinfectant must remain visibly wet on a surface to achieve its labeled kill claim — stated on the product label, commonly in the range of 1–10 minutes depending on the product and target pathogen.

**In use:** "This is a 10-minute-contact disinfectant — a quick wipe-and-move doesn't meet the label claim, whatever the room looks like."

**Common misuse:** judging disinfection by visual cleanliness or speed of the wipe, when the label's contact time is the actual efficacy requirement.

## Cross-contamination / color-coding

**Cross-contamination** is the transfer of pathogens or residue from one surface or zone to another via a shared tool (cloth, mop head). **Color-coding** is the control — assigning tool colors to zones (e.g., red for restrooms) so a tool never moves between zones it shouldn't.

**In use:** "That cloth came out of the restroom cart — it doesn't touch the breakroom counter, full stop, color system or not."

**Common misuse:** treating color-coding as a tidiness or organization system rather than an infection-control control that should never be improvised around.

## OOO vs. OOS

**OOO** (out of order) is a room or unit taken offline for maintenance reasons unrelated to cleaning — broken fixture, HVAC failure. **OOS** (out of service) is specifically a housekeeping/inspection hold — a critical-fail item, an incomplete clean, or a compliance issue.

**In use:** "That room's OOS for the smoke detector, not OOO — housekeeping owns getting it back, not engineering."

**Common misuse:** using the two interchangeably in reporting, which misroutes the fix to the wrong department and hides which holds are actually housekeeping's responsibility to clear.

## Walked guest

A guest with a confirmed reservation who cannot be accommodated at check-in (commonly because a promised room isn't ready or oversold) and is relocated to another property, typically at the original property's cost plus a goodwill gesture.

**In use:** "If we sell those six rooms before they clear full clean, we're one late arrival away from walking someone — that's a $250–400 direct cost plus the review it generates."

**Common misuse:** treating "walked guest" as purely a front-desk/revenue-management problem, when a housekeeping capacity shortfall not communicated upstream is frequently the root cause.

## CIMS / CIMS-GB

**CIMS** (Cleaning Industry Management Standard, ISSA) is the accreditation framework assessing a cleaning organization's quality-management system — not just whether rooms look clean, but whether the process producing that outcome is documented and repeatable. **CIMS-GB** adds the Green Building module covering sustainable products and practices.

**In use:** "We passed the inspection today, but CIMS certification asks whether we'd pass it consistently without this particular inspector — that's a process question, not a one-day score."

**Common misuse:** conflating a single clean inspection pass with the organizational-process certification CIMS actually measures.

## Blitz clean / quick-clean

An abbreviated service pass covering only the highest-visibility items (trash, towels, quick surface wipe) — used deliberately for opt-out/eco-refresh programs or genuine emergencies, distinct from a full stayover or checkout clean.

**In use:** "That room's on the reuse program — it's a blitz today, not a full stayover, and it's coded at the lighter credit."

**Common misuse:** using a blitz clean to quietly absorb a staffing shortfall on rooms that were never opted into the lighter-service program, which understates the actual service gap in the day's records.

## Productivity standard vs. inspection score

**Productivity standard** measures output against time (credits or square feet per hour). **Inspection score** measures the quality of that output against a checklist, independent of how fast it was produced. The two are tracked separately because a rising productivity number can be masking a falling inspection score for several days before the connection is obvious.

**In use:** "Productivity's up 8% this week, but don't call it a win until we see this week's inspection scores — check both before reporting either."

**Common misuse:** reporting productivity gains as an unqualified success without checking the corresponding quality metric in the same period.
