# Vocabulary

Terms of art generalists misuse in a field-based environmental science program.

### Extramural utterance

- **Definition:** AAUP's term for a faculty member's public statement made as a citizen (op-ed, testimony, social media) rather than in their institutional teaching or research role — protected by academic freedom unless it demonstrates unfitness for the position, a distinct and higher bar than "the university disagrees with it."
- **In use:** "That op-ed on the pipeline permit was an extramural utterance, not a classroom statement — the department doesn't get to discipline it as if it were course content."
- **Common misuse:** conflating a professor's public political statement with what they teach in class, then applying classroom-content standards (evidentiary disclosure, confidence bounds) to a citizen's op-ed, or the reverse — treating a classroom claim as protected personal speech because it happens to be politically salient.

### Indirect costs / F&A rate

- **Definition:** the negotiated percentage a university adds to a grant's direct costs to recover overhead (facilities, administration) — applied to modified total direct costs (MTDC), not the full award.
- **In use:** "We're at 52% F&A on MTDC, so that $18,000 equipment purchase doesn't get the overhead tax — only the $161,500 MTDC base does."
- **Common misuse:** applying the F&A rate to the entire direct-cost total instead of the MTDC base, which overstates the indirect-cost line and can make an otherwise-fundable budget look like it exceeds the sponsor's ceiling.

### Field season

- **Definition:** the fixed, typically seasonal window (often ~10–16 weeks) during which primary field data collection for a project is physically possible — set by biology, weather, or access, not by the researcher's calendar.
- **In use:** "That's 1.5 completed field seasons of new data, not two calendar years of work — the publication cadence follows the season count, not the hire date."
- **Common misuse:** measuring a field-based researcher's productivity against calendar time elapsed since hire instead of field seasons completed, which systematically undercredits early-career output.

### IACUC vs. IRB

- **Definition:** IACUC (Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee) reviews protocols involving live vertebrate animals; IRB (Institutional Review Board) reviews protocols involving human subjects. A single project can require both, separately.
- **In use:** "The fish tagging needs IACUC; the resident-survey component needs its own IRB submission — they don't cover each other."
- **Common misuse:** assuming environmental fieldwork is automatically exempt from both because it "isn't medical research," or assuming one committee's approval covers a design element that belongs to the other's jurisdiction.

### Job hazard analysis (JHA)

- **Definition:** a written, task-by-task breakdown of a field activity's specific hazards and the control measure for each — distinct from a general safety orientation, which covers the site but not the task sequence.
- **In use:** "The JHA for the stream-wading exercise lists slip/fall, cold-water immersion, and biological exposure, each with its own control — that's what gets filed with the safety office, not the general course syllabus safety paragraph."
- **Common misuse:** treating a generic "wear closed-toe shoes and bring water" safety note as equivalent to a JHA; a JHA is task-specific and precedes the activity in writing, not a paragraph added to the syllabus template.

### Scholarship of application / integration (Boyer)

- **Definition:** Boyer's typology extends "scholarship" beyond discovery (original research) to integration (synthesizing across fields), application (using knowledge to address real problems), and teaching — all potentially creditable in review, if the institution's guidelines say so.
- **In use:** "The land-management partnership report counts as scholarship of application under our department's guideline — it's not service just because it's not a journal article."
- **Common misuse:** defaulting to counting only discovery-scholarship (peer-reviewed papers) in a review, which systematically undercounts interdisciplinary and applied environmental science work unless the department's written guideline explicitly credits the other categories.

### Soft money vs. hard money

- **Definition:** hard money is salary and infrastructure guaranteed by the institution's base budget; soft money is funding contingent on external grants, renewed on the funder's schedule, not the institution's.
- **In use:** "Her summer salary and the grad student's stipend are both soft money — if the renewal isn't funded, that's not a hypothetical, the lab stops running in month 37."
- **Common misuse:** treating a soft-money-funded position as equivalent in job security to a hard-money line when planning multi-year commitments (a grad student's timeline, a long-term monitoring site).

### Broader impacts vs. intellectual merit

- **Definition:** NSF's two mandatory review criteria — intellectual merit is the scientific significance and rigor of the proposed work; broader impacts is the proposal's benefit to society (education, workforce, public data, underrepresented-group participation) beyond the science itself.
- **In use:** "The intellectual merit section is strong, but broader impacts is one sentence about 'outreach' — that's the section that sinks otherwise-fundable proposals."
- **Common misuse:** treating broader impacts as a pro forma paragraph rather than a criterion reviewers score with the same rigor as intellectual merit, or conflating "will publish results" (which is intellectual merit's job) with a genuine societal-benefit plan.

### Data management plan (DMP)

- **Definition:** a required component of most federal proposals specifying how data will be formatted, documented, stored, shared, and for how long after the project ends.
- **In use:** "The DMP commits us to depositing the sonde data in the state's environmental data portal within 12 months of collection — that's a real deliverable the program officer will check on the next report."
- **Common misuse:** writing the DMP as boilerplate copied from a prior proposal without matching it to the actual data types and repository this project will use, which becomes a compliance gap the sponsor can flag at reporting time.

### Citizen science / community-based participatory research

- **Definition:** citizen science involves trained volunteers in data collection under a researcher-designed protocol; community-based participatory research goes further, involving the community in defining the research question itself, not just executing the protocol.
- **In use:** "We're running citizen science for the water-quality monitoring — volunteers collect samples on our protocol — but the environmental-justice siting study is community-based participatory, since the residents helped set the research question."
- **Common misuse:** using the terms interchangeably; a funder or IRB reviewing a "participatory" proposal expects evidence of community input into the question and design, not just volunteer labor on a researcher-set protocol.

### Field station

- **Definition:** a dedicated, often remote, institutional facility providing housing, lab space, and equipment access for extended field research and courses — a fixed asset with its own budget, staffing, and scheduling calendar, distinct from a day-trip field site.
- **In use:** "Booking the field station for the six-week course means reserving it against the other three PIs who also run summer programs there — that's a scheduling negotiation, not a given."
- **Common misuse:** assuming field-station access is unlimited or free because it's an institutional resource; most stations run internal cost-recovery charges (bed nights, lab fees) that belong in the grant budget.

### Scientific collecting permit

- **Definition:** a state or federal agency's authorization to collect specified species or materials from a jurisdiction's land or waters for research, distinct from general land-access permission.
- **In use:** "The land-access agreement covers the property; we still need the state wildlife agency's collecting permit before anyone touches a fish."
- **Common misuse:** treating landowner permission as sufficient for specimen collection — the two permissions are independent and both required, and the collecting permit's agency processing time (commonly 60–90 days) is the actual field-season bottleneck, not the land access.

### Course release / buyout

- **Definition:** reducing a faculty member's teaching load for a term, funded either by the department (a release, usually unpaid to the grant) or by charging the cost of a replacement instructor to a grant (a buyout, a real budget line).
- **In use:** "The grant is funding a one-course buyout next spring so she can finish the field season's analysis — that's a specific line in the budget, not a courtesy the department extends for free."
- **Common misuse:** assuming a course release is automatically available or free; a grant-funded buyout has to be budgeted explicitly and approved by the department, since it commits the department to covering that course another way.
