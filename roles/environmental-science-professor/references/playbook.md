# Playbook

Filled templates and thresholds, not descriptions of them. Defaults, not laws — deviate consciously and document why.

## 1. Field exercise go/no-go safety plan

A written plan filed before the semester starts, with numeric thresholds, not narrative judgment calls.

**Weather thresholds (stream/forest field-methods course, example):**

| Condition | Threshold | Action |
|---|---|---|
| Lightning | ≥30% probability within 10 miles per NOAA forecast, or thunder heard within 30 seconds of a visible flash | Cancel outdoor portion; move to indoor backup lab exercise |
| Heat index | ≥103°F (NWS "danger" category) | Cancel or move start time to before 9am with mandatory water/shade breaks every 20 min |
| Stream stage | Above the site's posted "wading unsafe" gauge height (site-specific — pull from USGS real-time gauge before departure) | Cancel water-entry portion; bank-only sampling or reschedule |
| Wind | Sustained ≥25 mph in forested terrain (falling-branch risk) | Relocate to open-field or indoor site |

**Ratios and roster limits:**
- 1 instructor or TA per 12 students in the field, minimum 2 supervising adults per group regardless of size (no solo-instructor field trips).
- Any student with a disclosed medical condition affecting heat/exertion tolerance gets a written accommodation plan on file before the first field day, cross-referenced against that day's threshold check.

**Documentation:** the go/no-go call is logged in writing (date, forecast pulled, threshold compared, decision, who made it) every time — a verbal call with no record is the pattern that fails a liability review after an incident, whether or not the call itself was correct.

## 2. Federal grant budget — filled example

NSF 3-year proposal, $420,000 total requested, standard university F&A (indirect cost) rate of 52% on modified total direct costs (MTDC; excludes equipment, tuition, and subcontract amounts over $25k).

| Line item | Year 1 | Year 2 | Year 3 | Total |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| PI summer salary (1 month/yr) | $11,000 | $11,330 | $11,670 | $34,000 |
| Grad student stipend + tuition | $34,000 | $35,000 | $36,000 | $105,000 |
| Field equipment (sonde, 1x, Yr1 only) | $18,000 | — | — | $18,000 |
| Supplies/consumables | $4,000 | $4,000 | $4,000 | $12,000 |
| Travel (conference + field site) | $3,500 | $3,500 | $3,500 | $10,500 |
| **Direct costs subtotal** | $70,500 | $53,830 | $55,170 | $179,500 |
| MTDC base (subtract $18,000 equipment from Yr1) | $52,500 | $53,830 | $55,170 | $161,500 |
| F&A @ 52% of MTDC | $27,300 | $27,992 | $28,688 | $83,980 |
| **Total** | $97,800 | $81,822 | $83,858 | **$263,480** |

If the target is a round $420,000 ceiling, this budget has headroom — the gap gets allocated to a second grad student or an additional field season of equipment, not padded uniformly across every line, which reads as unjustified to a panel. Every line traces to a named deliverable in the project narrative; a reviewer who can't map a budget line to a task in the workplan flags it as unjustified.

**Broader impacts vs. intellectual merit:** NSF requires both criteria addressed with equal specificity. Intellectual merit is the science question and why it advances the field; broader impacts is what non-scientists gain (K-12 partnership, public data portal, workforce training) — a one-sentence "will also do outreach" broader-impacts paragraph is the single most common reason a technically strong proposal scores poorly.

## 3. Tenure-case normalization worksheet

Build the actual comparison set before judging a count:

1. Pull the department's own prior tenure files for candidates in field-based (not bench-lab) subfields; record papers-at-year-3 or papers-at-year-6 for each.
2. Compute mean and range for that subset specifically — not the whole department.
3. Note the candidate's completed field-season count (not calendar years since hire) as of the review date.
4. Note whether the department's written P&T guideline assigns an explicit weight to external funding; if none exists, this is itself worth flagging to the committee as a gap, since interdisciplinary fields lean on funding as a signal more than single-discipline fields do.
5. Present the comparison in the review memo as a table (field-based mean/range vs. candidate), not as prose asserting the candidate is "on track" without the numbers shown.

## 4. IRB / IACUC / permit routing decision tree

1. Does the project involve identifiable living human subjects (surveys, interviews, observation of behavior, collection of self-reported data) in any component? → **Yes at any scale → submit IRB.** Environmental sampling alone does not trigger IRB; adding one interview question does.
2. Does the project involve live vertebrate animals (capture, tagging, handling, even non-invasive observation protocols in some institutional policies)? → **IACUC.**
3. Is fieldwork on state or federal land involving collection of specimens (plant, soil, water beyond incidental sampling, wildlife)? → **State natural-resource agency scientific collecting permit**, applied for on the agency's own timeline — most run 60–90 days minimum processing.
4. Is fieldwork on tribal land or land under a co-management agreement? → **Direct consultation with the relevant tribal government's natural-resources office before any permit application** — this is a separate, prior step, not satisfied by a state permit alone.
5. Is fieldwork on private land? → **Signed landowner access agreement on file**, renewed each season it's used, naming exactly what activity is permitted.

Any "no" answer that later turns into a "yes" (a water-quality study that adds a resident-interview component mid-project) re-triggers the relevant step — the review requirement is set by the most sensitive component present, checked at every design change, not just at the original submission.
