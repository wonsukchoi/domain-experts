# Physical Therapist Assistant — Vocabulary

### Plan of care
The PT's written evaluation, goals, and treatment parameters that govern every PTA session for that patient.
**In use:** "I can't add a new exercise today — that's not in the plan of care, I need to check with the PT first."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a loose guideline the PTA can adapt freely; it's the legal and clinical boundary of what a PTA can do without PT involvement.

### Direct supervision
A supervision level requiring the PT to be on-site and immediately available during the PTA's treatment session.
**In use:** "This payer requires direct supervision for this code, so the PT needs to be in the building today."
**Common misuse:** Confused with "general supervision" (PT reachable by phone/electronic means, not necessarily on-site) — the two have materially different legal requirements and mixing them up is a compliance issue, not a technicality.

### General supervision
A supervision level requiring the PT to be available for consultation (often by phone) but not physically on-site.
**In use:** "Under general supervision, I can treat here at the satellite clinic as long as the PT is reachable."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to mean "no supervision needed" — the PT is still legally responsible and must be reachable, not simply informed after the fact.

### Progression parameters
The specific range and criteria (e.g., resistance increments, rep targets, pain thresholds) a PT sets for how a PTA advances an exercise.
**In use:** "The plan's progression parameters say 2-5 lb increments contingent on 3x10 at low pain — today's step is 3 lb, not the max."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a single fixed rule to always apply the same way, rather than a range the PTA exercises judgment within.

### Minimal clinically important difference (MCID)
The smallest change in an outcome measure considered clinically meaningful, distinguishing real change from measurement noise.
**In use:** "The functional scale moved 12 points, which is above this instrument's MCID, so that's real progress, not noise."
**Common misuse:** Confused with statistical significance — a change can be statistically detectable but still below the MCID, meaning it isn't clinically meaningful.

### Re-evaluation
A formal PT assessment at a scheduled interval (or triggered by a specific event) that can modify, continue, or end the plan of care.
**In use:** "Reaching the resistance ceiling triggers a re-evaluation before we continue past it."
**Common misuse:** Treated as optional or as something the PTA can substitute with their own judgment about whether the plan still fits.

### Scope of practice
The set of activities a PTA is legally authorized to perform, distinct from what a PT or other clinician is authorized to do.
**In use:** "Modifying the plan itself is outside my scope of practice — I can execute and progress within it, not redesign it."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a matter of individual competence ("I could do this if I wanted to") rather than a legal boundary independent of skill level.

### Functional goal
A treatment goal stated in terms of a real-world activity the patient can or can't do, as opposed to an impairment measurement alone.
**In use:** "The functional goal is climbing a flight of stairs without a handrail — that's what we're tracking, not just knee flexion degrees."
**Common misuse:** Conflated with impairment-level measures (strength grade, ROM) — those are evidence for the functional goal, not the goal itself.

### Home exercise program (HEP)
A set of exercises the patient performs independently between supervised sessions, typically issued and modified by the PT.
**In use:** "Adherence to the HEP has been inconsistent — that's worth flagging at the next PT check-in."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to be something the PTA can freely add to or change during a session without PT sign-off.

### Adherence
The degree to which a patient follows the prescribed plan (in-session effort, home program compliance).
**In use:** "Two missed goals with good adherence is different from two missed goals with a skipped home program — the note should say which."
**Common misuse:** Used interchangeably with "progress" — a patient can be fully adherent and still plateau, or non-adherent and still show some change, and the note should distinguish the two.

### Discharge criteria
The PT-set conditions (functional goals met, plateau at maximum medical improvement) that determine when treatment ends.
**In use:** "Discharge criteria aren't met yet — the functional goal is close but not confirmed across two consecutive visits."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a PTA decision; discharge is a PT-level clinical decision even when the PTA is the one who observes the criteria appear to be met.

### Objective measure
A standardized, reproducible measurement (goniometry, an outcome-measure score, rep count at a given resistance) as distinct from subjective patient report.
**In use:** "Pain report lagged, but the objective measure — the outcome score — showed real improvement."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to always outrank subjective report in every case; pain that diverges sharply from objective findings can itself be a finding worth flagging, not simply overridden.

### Regression (of an exercise)
Stepping an exercise back to a lower resistance, fewer reps, or an easier variation, typically when a patient can't meet the current parameters.
**In use:** "Patient couldn't complete the set at 13 lb today with acceptable pain — holding at 13, not regressing, since this is the first missed session."
**Common misuse:** Applied reflexively at the first sign of difficulty, when the plan may specify holding at the current level before stepping back — check the plan's actual regression trigger, not the PTA's instinct.
