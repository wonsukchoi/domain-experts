# Vocabulary

Terms generalists conflate that a certificated mechanic keeps sharply separate.

## Airworthiness Directive (AD) vs. Service Bulletin (SB)

An **AD**, issued by the FAA under 14 CFR Part 39, is legally mandatory — non-compliance grounds the aircraft regardless of anyone's preference. A **Service Bulletin** is the manufacturer's recommendation, not automatically mandatory unless an AD specifically incorporates it by reference.

**In use:** "The SB is a good idea and we'll schedule it at the next check, but it's the AD referencing it that's actually mandatory — don't conflate 'manufacturer recommends' with 'FAA requires.'"

**Common misuse:** treating a manufacturer SB as equally binding as an AD, or assuming an AD's compliance time is negotiable because "the manufacturer's own bulletin gave more time."

## MEL vs. CDL

The **Minimum Equipment List** governs dispatch with an inoperative *system or component* (e.g., an APU generator). The **Configuration Deviation List** governs dispatch with a missing *external part* (e.g., a wingtip fairing, an access panel) and often carries a performance penalty applied to takeoff/landing data.

**In use:** "That's a missing fairing, not a broken system — check the CDL, not the MEL, and apply the airspeed/fuel-burn penalty it specifies."

**Common misuse:** looking for a missing physical part in the MEL, or applying MEL deferral-day logic (Category A/B/C/D) to a CDL item, which doesn't use that structure.

## Return to service vs. approved for return to service

**Return to service** is the general act of releasing an aircraft after maintenance. **"Approved for return to service"** is the specific legal phrase required in the 14 CFR 43.9 statement — its presence (or a materially equivalent statement) is what makes the log entry legally complete, not just "signed off."

**In use:** "The entry's got a signature, but it doesn't actually say 'approved for return to service' anywhere — that's a paperwork defect, fix it before this aircraft flies again."

**Common misuse:** assuming any signed log entry satisfies §43.9 regardless of its wording.

## Major vs. minor repair/alteration

A **minor** repair/alteration can be approved by the performing mechanic's own return-to-service signature. A **major** one (per 14 CFR 43 Appendix A's definitions — appreciably affecting weight, balance, structural strength, performance, or flight characteristics) requires FAA Form 337 and sign-off by someone with the authority to approve major work (an IA, a repair station, or the FAA directly).

**In use:** "That's not a minor patch — it's load-bearing skin repair, appreciably affecting structural strength, so it's a 337, not a logbook line."

**Common misuse:** classifying a repair as "minor" because it seems small in scope, when the Appendix A test is about the *effect* on structure/performance, not the size of the patch.

## Hard time vs. on-condition vs. condition monitored

**Hard time** components are replaced or overhauled at a fixed hour/cycle limit regardless of condition. **On-condition** components stay in service as long as they pass a recurring inspection or test against a defined standard. **Condition monitored** components have no fixed limit or scheduled check at all — they run to failure, with fleet-wide failure data monitored to catch trends.

**In use:** "That component's on-condition, not hard-time — we don't pull it at a fixed hour number, we pull it when the inspection criteria say it's out of limits."

**Common misuse:** applying a fixed replacement interval to an on-condition part "to be safe," which burns serviceable life for no airworthiness benefit, or the reverse — leaving a hard-time part in past its limit because it "looks fine."

## Alternative Means of Compliance (AMOC)

An FAA-approved method of satisfying an AD's intent other than the AD's literally stated action — used when the stated action doesn't fit a particular configuration, or a better fix exists. An AMOC must be specifically approved (by the FAA or its delegate) for that aircraft/operator; it is not a mechanic's own substitution.

**In use:** "The AD's stated inspection doesn't apply cleanly to this modified airframe — we need an AMOC approved for this tail number, not an improvised version of the AD's procedure."

**Common misuse:** treating "we did something equivalent" as automatically acceptable without the actual FAA/delegate approval on file.

## Inspection Authorization (IA) vs. A&P

**A&P** (Airframe and Powerplant) is the base mechanic certificate authorizing maintenance and minor repairs/alterations with a return-to-service signature. **Inspection Authorization** is an additional privilege (14 CFR §65.91, requiring 3 years of recent A&P experience plus a separate exam) required to perform and approve annual inspections and major repairs/alterations.

**In use:** "I can sign off the repair as an A&P, but the annual inspection sign-off needs an IA — that's not me on this one."

**Common misuse:** assuming any experienced A&P can sign an annual inspection or a Form 337 — both specifically require the IA privilege, not just seniority.

## Required Inspection Item (RII)

A maintenance task designated (by the operator's manual, per 14 CFR 121/135 requirements) as requiring an independent second inspection beyond the mechanic who performed the work — because an undetected error would be especially consequential (e.g., flight control rigging).

**In use:** "That's an RII — I can't be my own second inspector on it, get someone else to sign the buyback."

**Common misuse:** treating a thorough self-check as satisfying an RII requirement; the point of RII is a genuinely independent second set of eyes, not a more careful first one.

## No Fault Found (NFF) vs. Cannot Duplicate (CND)

**NFF** means the component was tested (often on a bench or with dedicated test equipment) and found within specification. **CND** means the reported symptom simply didn't reproduce during the ground check — a much weaker finding, since intermittent faults routinely fail to show up on the ground.

**In use:** "This came back CND twice, not NFF — nobody's actually tested the component off the aircraft yet, so we haven't ruled anything out."

**Common misuse:** logging a CND result as if it were NFF, which closes the discrepancy on paper without any actual verification that the fault is gone.

## 100-hour vs. annual vs. progressive inspection

The **100-hour** inspection (14 CFR §91.409(b)) is required for aircraft used commercially "for hire." The **annual** inspection (§91.409(a)) is required every 12 calendar months regardless of hours flown, on every certificated aircraft. **Progressive inspection** is an FAA-approved alternative that breaks the same total inspection scope into smaller recurring segments instead of one large annual event, keeping the aircraft available more continuously.

**In use:** "This one's on a progressive program, so there's no single big annual event — the same scope is just spread across the rotation, and we're due for segment 4 this week."

**Common misuse:** assuming a 100-hour inspection satisfies the annual requirement (it doesn't — the annual's 12-calendar-month clock runs independently of hours), or assuming progressive inspection means "less thorough" rather than "differently scheduled."

## Squawk vs. discrepancy

A **squawk** is the informal, often pilot-originated report of a symptom ("APU generator flickering on start"). A **discrepancy** is the same issue once it's formally entered in the maintenance log with the required 43.9/43.11 fields — the point at which it becomes a legally tracked item with a required disposition.

**In use:** "Get the squawk turned into a proper discrepancy entry before shift change — a verbal 'yeah it's flickering' isn't in the record."

**Common misuse:** treating a verbally-reported squawk as already "in the system" once someone's aware of it, when nothing legally exists until it's logged.
