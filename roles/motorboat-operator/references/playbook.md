# Motorboat Operator — Playbook

## Capacity-reconciliation worksheet (filled example)

Check this before accepting or boarding any group larger than the license's uninspected threshold, and any time a load changes (charter size, added gear, a fuel top-off before a long trip).

| Line item | Source | Value |
|---|---|---|
| COI max persons (passengers + crew) | Certificate of Inspection | 19 (17 pax + 2 crew) |
| Assumed weight per person | USCG NVIC 08-11 standard | 185 lb |
| **Certificated persons load (tested condition)** | 19 × 185 lb | **3,515 lb** |
| Requested persons aboard today | Booking / manifest | 24 (22 pax + 2 crew) |
| **Requested persons load** | 24 × 185 lb | **4,440 lb** |
| **Persons / weight over tested condition** | 24 − 19 = 5 persons; 4,440 − 3,515 | **5 persons / 925 lb over** |
| Builder structural/flotation max weight | Capacity plate | 10,200 lb |
| Fuel + gear aboard | Fuel log / gear manifest | 1,200 lb |
| Flotation margin remaining at requested load | 10,200 − 1,200 − 4,440 | 4,560 lb (looks fine — not the controlling number) |

**Rule applied:** the COI's persons number is the enforceable ceiling regardless of remaining flotation margin. Any requested load exceeding certificated persons is declined or split, never approved on the strength of leftover weight margin. A recurring higher-headcount demand is a case for a new simplified stability test to amend the COI, not for running over the existing one.

## Following-sea / bar-crossing handling sequence (filled)

Use when the route includes a following or quartering sea, or a bar/inlet crossing with breaking waves overtaking the vessel from astern.

| Step | Action | Why |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Estimate wave period and compare implied wavelength to the hull's waterline length | If wavelength is on the order of the hull length or greater, the whole hull can be lifted by a single wave — surf-riding risk, not a large-vessel concern |
| 2 | Avoid running dead before the sea (0° off the stern) | A pure stern-to run loses steerage exactly as the wave face lifts the stern and reduces rudder immersion |
| 3 | Quarter the wave — hold heading roughly 20-30° off dead astern | Keeps enough rudder authority to correct yaw without taking the wave broadside |
| 4 | Adjust speed to avoid matching or being overtaken by wave celerity | Speed close to wave speed is the surf-riding/broach condition; too slow lets breaking crests board over the transom |
| 5 | If broach risk builds despite quartering, reduce speed further and consider a drogue/sea anchor astern | A drogue resists the stern's tendency to slew broadside as a wave passes under it |
| 6 | At a bar or inlet crossing, time the transit to the wave set (cross behind a group of larger breakers, not into one) | Bar crossings concentrate breaking-wave energy in shallow water — timing matters more than boat handling alone |
| 7 | Brief passengers to stay low and seated, away from the stern/transom, before entering the following-sea leg | Limits injury and MOB exposure if a wave does board or the vessel takes unexpected heel |

## Pre-departure passenger safety briefing script (filled)

Delivered verbally to all passengers before getting underway, every trip, logged in the trip manifest.

> "Before we get underway: life jackets are in the bin under the port bench and in the rack by the helm — everyone should know where theirs is. If we ever need to leave the boat, this open deck aft is our muster point — that's where you gather, no exceptions. If someone goes in the water, don't jump in after them — shout 'man overboard,' point continuously at them, and let the crew maneuver the boat. Fire extinguisher is mounted here [point], first-aid kit is under the helm seat. Please stay seated while we're underway in any chop, and keep clear of the stern rail. Any questions before we cast off?"

**Logged as:** "Pre-departure safety briefing given, [time], POB [count] — PFD location, muster point, MOB procedure, extinguisher/first-aid location covered."

## Cold-water man-overboard response timeline (filled)

Based on the 1-10-1 cold-water immersion principle — response is budgeted against this clock, not against how alert the person in the water appears.

| Elapsed time | Physiological stage | Operator action in that window |
|---|---|---|
| 0-1 min | Cold-shock response — gasp reflex, hyperventilation, risk of water inhalation | Shout "man overboard," assign one crew/passenger to point continuously at the person, begin turn immediately |
| 1-10 min | Functional movement still possible but degrading — this is the window for self-rescue or a thrown flotation device | Maneuver alongside on the leeward/downwind side, deploy throwable, prepare to assist — do not expect the person to swim far or long |
| 10 min onward | Muscle failure — swimming and grip strength typically gone even for a strong swimmer | Recovery must be active (boat alongside, ladder/sling, crew-assisted lift) — passive "they'll swim to us" plans fail past this point |
| Up to ~1 hr | Hypothermia progression toward unconsciousness (varies with water temperature and person) | Recovery and warming take priority over continuing the trip; treat as a medical emergency, not a delay |

Treat the 10-minute mark as the operational deadline for recovery method, not the 1-hour mark — a plan that assumes an hour of usable swimming time is planning against the wrong stage of the timeline.
