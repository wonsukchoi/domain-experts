# Vocabulary

Terms generalists conflate that small-craft operators keep sharply separate. Each: definition, how a practitioner actually uses it in a sentence, and the common misuse to avoid.

### OUPV vs. Master license

**OUPV** (Operator of Uninspected Passenger Vessels, the "six-pack" license) authorizes carrying up to 6 passengers for hire on an uninspected vessel under 100 GT. **Master** (25/50/100 GT) is a higher credential tied to a specific tonnage endorsement, required once a vessel is inspected and/or carries more than 6 passengers for hire.

*In use:* "I've got my six-pack, but this booking needs a Master endorsement and an inspected boat — I can't just run it under OUPV."

**Common misuse:** treating OUPV as a general "small boat license" that scales up with experience rather than a hard 6-passenger ceiling tied to vessel inspection status.

### Six-pack vs. inspected small passenger vessel (COI)

A **six-pack** operation is an uninspected vessel carrying up to 6 passengers for hire under an OUPV license. An **inspected small passenger vessel** carries more than 6 passengers for hire, falls under 46 CFR Subchapter T, and must hold a Certificate of Inspection (COI).

*In use:* "The moment we sell a seventh seat, we're not a six-pack trip anymore — we need the COI vessel for that booking."

**Common misuse:** assuming the six-passenger line is a soft guideline rather than the exact threshold that changes which federal inspection regime applies.

### COI (Certificate of Inspection) vs. stability letter

The **COI** is the USCG document certifying an inspected vessel's max persons, required equipment, and route/service restrictions. The **stability letter** (or stability booklet, on larger small-passenger vessels) documents the underlying stability calculation the COI's persons number is based on.

*In use:* "COI says 19 persons max — that number came off the stability letter's simplified test, not off the deck-space count."

**Common misuse:** treating the COI's persons number as derived from available deck or seating space rather than from the stability test behind it.

### Simplified stability test vs. full inclining experiment

The **simplified stability test** (46 CFR Subchapter S) is the abbreviated heel/stability check used for most small passenger vessels to set the certificated max-persons figure. A **full inclining experiment** is the more rigorous test (weight-shift and heel-angle measurement across a matrix of conditions) typically reserved for larger or more complex vessels.

*In use:* "This 34-footer only ever had the simplified test — if we want a materially higher persons count, that's a new test, not a re-read of the old one."

**Common misuse:** assuming any stability review, however abbreviated, validates a higher passenger count than what the original simplified test actually covered.

### Assumed average weight per person (185 lb)

The standardized per-person weight figure (raised from 160 lb to 185 lb under USCG guidance in 2011) used to calculate a vessel's certificated persons load during the stability test.

*In use:* "Certificated load is 19 persons times 185 — that's 3,515 pounds, and that's the number the test actually validated."

**Common misuse:** using an outdated 160 lb figure, or an actual average passenger weight the operator estimates by eye, instead of the standardized figure the COI's number was built on.

### Following sea vs. quartering sea vs. head sea

A **following sea** approaches from directly astern. A **quartering sea** approaches at an angle off the stern (commonly described as 20-30° off dead astern). A **head sea** approaches from ahead.

*In use:* "We're taking this as a quartering sea, not dead astern — keeps the rudder biting instead of losing steerage on the wave face."

**Common misuse:** treating "following sea" and "quartering sea" as interchangeable handling problems, when a dead-astern approach is specifically the one that loses steerage as the stern is lifted.

### Broach vs. surf-riding vs. pitchpole

**Surf-riding** is a small craft being carried forward on a wave face at close to the wave's own speed. A **broach** is the resulting sudden, uncommanded yaw that turns the vessel beam-to-the-sea, risking capsize. A **pitchpole** is a vessel flipping end-over-end, typically from burying the bow into a wave trough or the face ahead.

*In use:* "She started surf-riding on that last set — bow went light, and she broached to port before I could correct it."

**Common misuse:** using "broach" loosely for any loss of control, rather than specifically the beam-on yaw that follows surf-riding.

### Planing hull vs. displacement hull

A **planing hull** rises and rides on top of its own bow wave at speed, common on small motorboats. A **displacement hull** always pushes through the water, displacing its own weight in water regardless of speed, common on larger vessels and slower workboats.

*In use:* "At speed on a following sea, a planing hull's stern lift and reduced rudder immersion are the whole broach risk — a displacement hull just doesn't get picked up by a single wave that way."

**Common misuse:** applying large-vessel (displacement-hull) following-sea intuition to a planing hull, which behaves differently once it's up on plane.

### Float plan vs. passage plan

A **float plan** is a shore-filed document (vessel description, POB, route, and expected return/check-in time) meant to trigger outside notice if a small craft is overdue. A **passage plan** is the operator's own navigational plan for the trip, not filed with anyone ashore.

*In use:* "Float plan's with my sister — she calls the Coast Guard if I haven't checked in by 8:30."

**Common misuse:** treating a passage plan (a route sketch) as equivalent safety coverage to a float plan (an externally triggered overdue-notice system).

### MOB (man overboard) and the 1-10-1 principle

**MOB** is a person overboard requiring immediate recovery. The **1-10-1 principle** describes cold-water immersion stages: roughly 1 minute of cold-shock breathing response, 10 minutes of functional movement before muscle failure, 1 hour before hypothermia-driven unconsciousness.

*In use:* "We're past the ten-minute mark — this is an active-recovery situation now, not a 'toss a line and wait' situation."

**Common misuse:** planning MOB response around the 1-hour hypothermia stage instead of the much shorter 10-minute functional-movement window that actually limits self-rescue.

### POB (persons on board) vs. COI max persons

**POB** is the actual count of everyone aboard right now — passengers and crew. **COI max persons** is the certificated ceiling that count must stay under.

*In use:* "POB is 24 today against a COI max of 19 — that's not a maybe, that's over."

**Common misuse:** counting only paying passengers toward the limit and forgetting crew count toward the same certificated persons ceiling.

### Bar crossing

Transiting the shallow, often breaking-wave area where a river or inlet meets open water — a location where wave energy concentrates and small-craft capsizes disproportionately cluster.

*In use:* "We're timing the bar crossing behind that last set of breakers, not into the middle of it."

**Common misuse:** treating a bar crossing as routine transit rather than a distinct, higher-risk maneuver requiring timing judgment independent of the rest of the passage.

### Freeboard vs. reserve buoyancy

**Freeboard** is the vertical distance from the waterline to the working deck at a given loading condition. **Reserve buoyancy** is the vessel's remaining ability to displace more water (via hull volume above the waterline) before swamping.

*In use:* "Freeboard's already down two inches with this load — that's eating into the reserve buoyancy we'd want if a wave comes over the transom."

**Common misuse:** treating freeboard as a fixed hull characteristic rather than a number that changes with every load the vessel actually carries that day.

### Uninspected vs. inspected vessel

An **uninspected vessel** carries 6 or fewer passengers for hire and is not subject to a USCG Certificate of Inspection. An **inspected vessel** carries more than 6 passengers for hire (or otherwise falls under an inspection subchapter) and must hold a current COI.

*In use:* "We run this boat uninspected on weekdays at six-pack capacity, but the weekend charter needs the inspected boat with the COI."

**Common misuse:** assuming "inspected" is only about vessel condition rather than a specific regulatory category triggered by passenger count and vessel class.
