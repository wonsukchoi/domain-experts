# Vocabulary

Terms a short-order cook uses precisely that generalists flatten into something looser.

### 86'd

An item pulled from availability for the rest of the shift — for any reason, not only "we ran out." A cook can 86 something for time (not enough rush minutes left to safely finish a slow item), for quality (a par-held batch past its window), or for stock.

**In use:** "86 the hash browns — not out, but we won't clear the rest of the rush on what's left without stalling tickets."

**Common misuse:** assuming 86 always means literally zero units remain; it's a threshold decision, not a stock-count fact.

### Hash brown modifiers (scattered, smothered, covered, chunked, diced, peppered, capped, topped, country, all the way)

A short, fixed vocabulary — popularized on diner counters and formalized at chains like Waffle House — where each word is a precise cooking instruction, not a menu flourish: scattered (spread thin on the griddle), smothered (onions cooked in), covered (cheese melted on), chunked (ham added), diced (tomato added), peppered (jalapeños added), capped (mushrooms added), topped (chili added), country (sausage gravy added), all the way (all of the above).

**In use:** "Ticket wants it smothered and covered — onions go in before the cheese, not after."

**Common misuse:** treating the modifiers as ingredient tags on an order slip rather than a sequencing instruction that changes when something goes on the griddle relative to the rest of the batch.

### Over easy / over medium / over hard vs. sunny side up

Precise, non-interchangeable doneness terms describing flip and yolk state, not a general sense of "not too runny." Sunny side up never flips; over easy/medium/hard all flip once, differing only in how long the second side cooks and how set the yolk ends up.

**In use:** "Ticket says over medium, not over easy — that's an extra 15–20 seconds on the flip side, it's not the same batch."

**Common misuse:** a server relaying "kind of runny" or "not too runny" as if it maps directly to one of the four standard terms, when it doesn't specify flip behavior at all.

### Fire vs. prep

Fire is starting the active cook of an item timed against a specific ticket; prep is advance work — par-cooking, chopping, portioning — done before or between tickets, independent of any one order.

**In use:** "Prep another bacon batch now during the lull so we don't have to fire it raw mid-rush later."

**Common misuse:** telling a new cook to "fire the bacon" when the intent is "prep it" — the two words point to different food-safety and timing models (fired items are tracked against a ticket clock; prepped items are tracked against a hold clock).

### Par / par level

The quantity of a par-cooked, hold-safe item kept ready at a given point in the shift, tied to a specific partial-cook-and-hold method — not just "the amount we usually have on hand."

**In use:** "We're under par on sausage with half the rush left — start a fresh batch now, not at zero."

**Common misuse:** applying par logic to eggs, which can't be safely par-cooked and held without losing the texture that is the ordered product; par only applies to items proven safe and acceptable to hold.

### Danger zone vs. hot holding

The danger zone is the 41–135°F range where TCS food can't sit for more than a bounded cumulative time (2 hours before it must be used or refrigerated, 4 hours before it must be discarded). Hot holding means keeping food at or above 135°F, which resets that clock.

**In use:** "That batch has been sitting at 128 — that's danger zone, not hot holding, get it back over 135 or toss it."

**Common misuse:** treating anything that feels warm as "hot held," without checking whether it's actually cleared 135°F on a thermometer.

### Consumer advisory

The regulatory disclosure (FDA Food Code §3-603.11) required on a menu when raw or undercooked animal food is offered to order — over easy eggs, an under-well burger. The posting obligation belongs to the establishment's menu, not to the cook individually, but the cook still has to recognize which orders trigger it and keep them off standard batch groupings.

**In use:** "That's an advisory item — pull it out of the batch, it can't ride with the standard over-easy group."

**Common misuse:** cooks treating it as their own liability paperwork rather than a menu-level requirement they need to recognize operationally, which leads either to ignoring it or over-applying it to items that don't need it.

### Flattop vs. grill

The flattop (or griddle) is a solid flat cooking surface; a grill uses grates over direct flame, producing char marks the flattop can't. A short-order cook's primary tool is the flattop — fat management, zone-heat behavior, and doneness cues are all different from grate cooking.

**In use:** "Put it on the flattop, not the grill — we want an even sear, not char lines, on a short-order burger."

**Common misuse:** using "grill" generically for the whole station, which obscures that the technique being described (zone temperature control, fat pooling and clearing) only applies to a flat surface.

### In the weeds

Being critically behind — not just busy — to the point that recovery needs structural help (another set of hands, an 86 call, cutting a menu section) rather than just working faster.

**In use:** "I'm in the weeds — someone needs to run the toaster and call tickets for the next ten minutes."

**Common misuse:** applying it to any moderately busy stretch; a practitioner reserves it for the point where ticket times are visibly climbing and speed alone won't fix it.

### Call it / up

"Call it" means to verbally repeat back a ticket's items when firing, to confirm what's being cooked. "Up" means a plate is actually finished and ready to be picked up — never said before plating is complete.

**In use:** "Two over easy, side bacon, up!"

**Common misuse:** using "up" as a heads-up that something is close to done rather than actually done, which gets a plate pulled from the window before it's finished.
