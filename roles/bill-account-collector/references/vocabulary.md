# Bill and Account Collector — Vocabulary

### Right-party contact (RPC)
Confirmed verbal or written verification that the person on the line is actually the debtor (or an authorized party), before any account or balance details are disclosed.
**In use:** "We had an RPC on this account today, but the debtor asked for a callback before discussing the balance."
**Common misuse:** Treating "someone answered the phone" as the same as RPC — an unverified household member picking up is not right-party contact, and disclosing balance details to them is a third-party-disclosure violation.

### Mini-Miranda
The required FDCPA disclosure that the call is an attempt to collect a debt and any information obtained will be used for that purpose, given at the start of collection communications.
**In use:** "The recording confirms the mini-Miranda was given in the first fifteen seconds of the call."
**Common misuse:** Assuming this disclosure is a one-time formality rather than a requirement on collection communications generally — omitting it is itself a compliance gap independent of anything else said on the call.

### Validation notice
The required written notice, generally sent within 5 days of the initial communication, stating the debt amount, creditor name, and the consumer's right to dispute within 30 days.
**In use:** "The validation notice went out on the 3rd; the 30-day dispute window runs through early next month."
**Common misuse:** Confusing the validation notice's 30-day window with a payment deadline — it's a dispute window, not a "pay by" date, and continuing standard collection contact during the window is generally fine unless a dispute is actually filed.

### Time-barred debt
Debt for which the state statute of limitations on filing a lawsuit to collect has expired — still owed, but not legally enforceable through litigation.
**In use:** "This account is time-barred under the state's four-year SOL; we can still ask for payment, but any language implying we could sue would be a false representation."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a time-barred debt can't be collected at all — collection by request/negotiation remains legal in most states, but the collector must not represent or imply that legal action is available.

### 7-in-7 rule
Regulation F's limitation on call frequency: no more than 7 calls to a debtor about a specific debt within 7 consecutive days, and no call within 7 days after having had a telephone conversation about that same debt.
**In use:** "We're at 5 calls on this debt this week — one more discretionary attempt before we hit the internal caution threshold."
**Common misuse:** Applying the 7-in-7 limit per debtor rather than per debt — a debtor with two separate delinquent accounts gets a separate 7-call allowance for each debt, not a combined one.

### Cease-and-desist
A written request from the debtor that the collector stop contacting them; after receipt, further contact is restricted to narrow statutory exceptions.
**In use:** "The cease-and-desist came in yesterday — no more calls, only a written notice confirming we've stopped, unless we're notifying of a specific legal action."
**Common misuse:** Believing a cease-and-desist eliminates the debt or the obligation to pay — it only restricts the collector's contact methods, not the underlying debt.

### Charge-off
An accounting action by the original creditor writing the debt off as a loss for its own books, which does not eliminate the debtor's legal obligation to pay and often precedes sale to a third-party collector.
**In use:** "This account charged off at 180 days and was sold to our agency at that point."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a charged-off debt is forgiven or uncollectible — charge-off is an internal accounting classification for the original creditor, not debt forgiveness.

### Skip tracing
The process of locating updated contact information for a debtor who cannot be reached at the information on file.
**In use:** "We ran skip tracing after three failed contact attempts at the number on file and found a current employer through a public-records search."
**Common misuse:** Treating skip tracing as unlimited license to contact third parties for information — FDCPA restricts what a collector can say when contacting third parties for location information (no mention of the debt itself, generally one contact per third party absent specific triggers).

### Promise-to-pay (PTP)
A debtor's stated commitment to make a payment by a specific date and amount, tracked as a pipeline metric distinct from an actual received payment.
**In use:** "We booked a PTP for the 15th, but PTP-to-actual-payment conversion on first-time promises tends to run well under 100%, so don't count it as recovered yet."
**Common misuse:** Reporting PTPs as equivalent to collected revenue — a PTP is a forecast, not a recovery, until the payment actually posts.

### FDCPA vs. state mini-FDCPA
The federal Fair Debt Collection Practices Act sets a compliance floor; many states layer additional, sometimes stricter, requirements through their own statutes ("mini-FDCPA" laws).
**In use:** "Federal FDCPA allows contact until 9pm, but this state's mini-FDCPA cuts it off at 8pm — the stricter state rule controls."
**Common misuse:** Assuming FDCPA compliance alone is sufficient everywhere — the applicable rule is whichever is stricter between federal and the debtor's state law.

### First-party vs. third-party collector
A first-party collector works accounts still owned by the original creditor (often under a different compliance framework in some contexts); a third-party collector works accounts owned by someone else or collects on another's behalf, and is squarely covered by the FDCPA.
**In use:** "As a third-party agency working purchased debt, every FDCPA requirement applies to us directly."
**Common misuse:** Assuming first-party collectors are exempt from all debt-collection-conduct rules — many states and the CFPB apply similar unfair-practice standards to first-party collection even where the federal FDCPA's technical scope is narrower.
