# Vocabulary

### Blind hem stitch
A hand stitch technique designed to secure a hem while remaining invisible from the garment's outside surface, achieved by catching only a small amount of the outer fabric with each stitch.
**In use:** "Blind hem on this trouser — the whole point is invisibility from outside, so tension and needle size both matter for keeping it that way."
**Common misuse:** Treating any hem stitch as interchangeable with a blind hem — a blind hem's specific technique (catching minimal outer fabric) is what makes it invisible, and substituting a different stitch type undermines that specific purpose even if it holds the hem in place.

### Whip stitch
A hand stitch used primarily for edge-binding or reinforcement, passing thread over an edge in a spiral pattern, distinct in purpose from an invisible finishing stitch.
**In use:** "Whip stitching this raw edge to reinforce and bind it — that's a different job than the blind hem we used on the trouser cuff."
**Common misuse:** Using a whip stitch where an invisible finish was actually needed, or vice versa — each stitch type serves a distinct structural or aesthetic purpose, and selecting based on convenience rather than actual purpose produces a mismatched result.

### Bar tack
A short, dense reinforcement stitch applied at a point of concentrated stress (the top of a buttonhole, a pocket corner) to prevent tearing or stress-related failure at that specific location.
**In use:** "Bar tack at the buttonhole ends — that's not decorative, it's what keeps the fabric from tearing outward from that stress point."
**Common misuse:** Treating a bar tack as an optional decorative flourish rather than a functional reinforcement — skipping it at an actual stress-concentration point creates a hidden weak point that fails under normal use, even though the surrounding stitching looks complete.

### Stitch consistency (tension/spacing)
The uniformity of tension, stitch length, and spacing maintained throughout a hand-sewn seam, which — unlike machine sewing — depends entirely on the sewer's deliberate technique control.
**In use:** "Keeping tension consistent through this whole seam by feel — there's no machine setting doing that for us."
**Common misuse:** Assuming hand-sewing quality is primarily about individual stitch technique rather than consistency across the full seam — a single inconsistent section creates both a visible defect and a functional weak point, regardless of how well the rest of the seam was executed.

### Needle-to-fabric matching
Selecting a needle size and type appropriate to a specific fabric's weight and weave, since too large a needle creates permanent visible holes in fine or tightly woven material.
**In use:** "Testing a 70/10 on this scrap before committing — a 90/14 would leave visible holes in a weave this tight."
**Common misuse:** Using a single general-purpose needle size regardless of fabric type — a needle appropriate for heavier fabric can create permanent, unfixable holes when used on fine or tightly woven material.

### Stress-concentration point (garment/textile)
A specific location in a garment or textile item where mechanical stress concentrates during normal use (a buttonhole corner, a pocket seam junction), requiring specific reinforcement rather than standard stitching.
**In use:** "That's a stress-concentration point — regular stitching there isn't enough, it needs a bar tack or equivalent reinforcement."
**Common misuse:** Applying the same stitching approach to a stress-concentration point as to a standard, low-stress seam — these specific locations experience disproportionate mechanical load in normal use and require correspondingly disproportionate reinforcement.

### Load-bearing vs. visible/aesthetic classification (hand-sewn element)
The categorization of a hand-sewn element by its primary function — structural/load-bearing, purely visible/aesthetic, or both — used to determine which stitch technique is actually appropriate.
**In use:** "This is both visible and load-bearing — the technique needs to satisfy strength requirements without looking like a heavy-duty reinforcement stitch."
**Common misuse:** Selecting a stitch technique optimized for only one dimension (appearance or strength) without checking whether the element actually requires both — an element serving a dual function needs a technique that satisfies both requirements, not one optimized for a single purpose.

### Slip stitch
A hand stitch used to invisibly close an opening (like a lining seam or a stuffed item's final closure), sliding the needle through the fold of fabric to hide the thread.
**In use:** "Slip stitching the lining closed here — invisible from both sides once it's done correctly."
**Common misuse:** Confusing a slip stitch's purpose (invisibly closing an opening) with a blind hem's purpose (invisibly securing a folded hem edge) — both aim for invisibility but are executed differently and suited to different structural situations.
