# Red flags

### Bilious (green) vomiting in an infant under 1 month old
- **Usually means:** Malrotation with midgut volvulus until proven otherwise; less urgently, duodenal or jejunal atresia.
- **First question:** "How many hours has the vomiting been bilious, and is the abdomen distended or benign?"
- **Data to pull:** Emergent upper GI contrast series — not a plain abdominal film alone, and not a trial of feeds.

### Bell stage II NEC with lactate climbing above 4 mmol/L or platelets dropping >50% within 12 hours
- **Usually means:** Transmural bowel necrosis evolving toward perforation, even before free air appears on film.
- **First question:** "Has the exam changed in the last 6 hours, independent of whether the film has changed?"
- **Data to pull:** Serial abdominal films every 6–8 hours plus the trend of platelets, lactate, and base deficit — never a single film or lab in isolation.

### Recurrent intussusception within 24 hours of reduction, or any intussusception in a child outside the 3-month–5-year band
- **Usually means:** A pathologic lead point (Meckel's diverticulum, polyp, lymphoma) rather than idiopathic ileocolic intussusception.
- **First question:** "Is this child outside the classic age band, or has this recurred more than once?"
- **Data to pull:** Repeat ultrasound specifically for a lead point; consider surgical exploration if recurrence is repeated.

### Acute scrotal pain with a horizontal lie or high-riding testicle, more than 6 hours from onset
- **Usually means:** Testicular torsion with salvage odds already declining by the hour.
- **First question:** "Can definitive treatment happen within the next hour, or should this go straight to exploration without waiting on imaging?"
- **Data to pull:** Doppler ultrasound only if it adds no more than ~60–90 minutes of delay; otherwise proceed to scrotal exploration on clinical grounds.

### Non-bilious projectile vomiting in a 3–6-week-old with a palpable "olive" mass or hypochloremic, hypokalemic metabolic alkalosis
- **Usually means:** Hypertrophic pyloric stenosis.
- **First question:** "What's the chloride and bicarbonate, and has correction started before anesthesia is even scheduled?"
- **Data to pull:** Basic metabolic panel and pyloric ultrasound (muscle thickness ≥3mm, canal length ≥15mm).

### Gastroschisis bowel that is matted, thickened, or foreshortened ("peel") rather than pink and free at delivery
- **Usually means:** Chemical peel from prolonged amniotic fluid exposure, possibly with an underlying atresia — not a straightforward primary-closure candidate.
- **First question:** "Is there a silo and staged-reduction plan instead of forcing closure tonight?"
- **Data to pull:** Intraoperative bowel exam; bladder pressure or peak inspiratory pressure trend if a reduction is attempted.

### CDH neonate with oxygenation index climbing above roughly 25–40 despite optimized ventilation
- **Usually means:** Pulmonary hypertension or hypoplasia severe enough to need ECMO evaluation before any repair is considered.
- **First question:** "Is this infant an ECMO candidate, and has repair been deliberately deferred until after decannulation or stabilization?"
- **Data to pull:** Serial oxygenation index, pre- and post-ductal saturations, echo for pulmonary hypertension severity.

### Post-appendectomy fever and rising WBC on postoperative day 5–7 after a perforated appendix
- **Usually means:** Intra-abdominal abscess.
- **First question:** "Was the appendix perforated at the index operation, and has imaging been done before restarting broad antibiotics blindly?"
- **Data to pull:** Cross-sectional imaging (ultrasound first-line in children), not just repeat labs.

### Conjugated bilirubin not clearly trending down by 3 months post-Kasai portoenterostomy
- **Usually means:** Inadequate bile drainage or progressing cirrhosis, with the transplant-evaluation window narrowing.
- **First question:** "What's the trend of conjugated bilirubin at 3 and 6 months, not just the pre-operative number?"
- **Data to pull:** Serial bilirubin fractionation; liver transplant referral per center protocol.

### Neonatal or infant inguinal hernia with acute irritability, vomiting, and a firm, tender groin bulge
- **Usually means:** Incarceration — risk is meaningfully higher in premature and younger infants than the adult-hernia mental model suggests.
- **First question:** "Can this be reduced now under sedation, or does it need emergent OR?"
- **Data to pull:** Timed manual reduction attempt; time since onset of irritability.

### Former preemie under roughly 60 weeks postmenstrual age presenting for elective surgery
- **Usually means:** Elevated post-anesthesia apnea risk independent of how "term-looking" or old in chronological weeks the infant now appears.
- **First question:** "What's the postmenstrual age, not the chronological age since birth?"
- **Data to pull:** Birth gestational age plus current chronological age to compute PMA; plan for overnight apnea monitoring if below threshold.
