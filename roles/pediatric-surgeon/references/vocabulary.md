# Vocabulary

### Malrotation
Abnormal position and fixation of the bowel from incomplete rotation around the superior mesenteric axis during fetal development, which predisposes to midgut volvulus but is not itself the acute event.
**In use:** "This is malrotation with volvulus until the UGI proves otherwise."
**Common misuse:** Treated as interchangeable with "volvulus." Malrotation is the anatomic predisposition; volvulus is the acute twist that causes ischemia. A patient can carry malrotation for years without ever volvulizing.

### Ladd's procedure
The operation for malrotation: counterclockwise detorsion, division of Ladd's bands, widening of the mesenteric root by separating the duodenum and cecum, and placement of the bowel in a nonrotated (not anatomically normal) position, with incidental appendectomy.
**In use:** "We did a Ladd's, not a true anatomic correction — the bowel sits nonrotated, not normal."
**Common misuse:** Described as "fixing" the malrotation, implying the bowel is returned to normal rotation. It never is — it's placed in the widest, most mechanically stable nonrotated configuration.

### Bell staging
A three-stage clinical and radiographic classification (I suspected, II definite, III advanced, each with A/B subdivisions) for necrotizing enterocolitis that drives management from observation to laparotomy.
**In use:** "She's Bell IIB with portal venous gas — we're trending labs q6h before committing to the OR."
**Common misuse:** Read as a single-timepoint label rather than a trajectory. A stage II infant deteriorating over hours is a materially different clinical problem from one stable at stage II for two days, even though both carry the same label at the moment of documentation.

### Primary peritoneal drainage
Bedside placement of a drain into the peritoneal cavity, used as either a temporizing bridge or definitive treatment for NEC or spontaneous intestinal perforation in extremely low birth weight infants.
**In use:** "At 620 grams, we're draining first, not opening the abdomen tonight."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to always be a bridge to laparotomy. In many extremely low birth weight infants it is the definitive treatment, and no laparotomy follows — outcomes in trial data are comparable to laparotomy in that population.

### Spontaneous intestinal perforation (SIP)
An isolated bowel perforation, typically of the terminal ileum, occurring in extremely premature infants without the ischemic/inflammatory process that defines NEC.
**In use:** "Pneumoperitoneum without preceding pneumatosis points more toward SIP than NEC."
**Common misuse:** Lumped together with NEC as the same disease. SIP has different pathophysiology, earlier typical onset (often first week of life versus NEC's later onset), and often a better prognosis than NEC-related perforation.

### Pediatric Appendicitis Score (PAS)
A 10-point clinical scoring system combining history, exam findings, and labs to stratify appendicitis probability in children.
**In use:** "PAS of 8 — we're not waiting on a CT, straight to the OR."
**Common misuse:** Applied as a rigid stand-alone diagnostic rule rather than a triage aid. A low score in a child with a strongly localized exam still warrants imaging or surgical consultation, not automatic discharge.

### Oxygenation index (OI)
A ventilator-derived measure — (FiO2 × mean airway pressure × 100) / PaO2 — used to grade respiratory failure severity and time ECMO evaluation, notably in congenital diaphragmatic hernia.
**In use:** "OI trending to 35 despite optimized vent settings — time to start the ECMO conversation."
**Common misuse:** Read as a single snapshot instead of a trend. A transient spike during a procedure or suctioning isn't the same signal as a sustained climb over hours.

### Silo (gastroschisis)
A pre-formed, spring-loaded silastic sac used to house eviscerated bowel outside the abdomen, allowing gradual gravity-assisted reduction over days before delayed fascial closure.
**In use:** "Bowel's too edematous for primary closure tonight — bedside silo, staged reduction over the next five to seven days."
**Common misuse:** Treated as evidence of a failed or incomplete operation rather than a deliberate, often preferred strategy for bowel-to-abdominal-domain mismatch.

### Kasai portoenterostomy
Surgical creation of a bowel conduit to the porta hepatis to restore bile flow in biliary atresia, most effective when performed before roughly 60 days of life.
**In use:** "We're pushing to get the Kasai done before 8 weeks — every week of delay measurably drops native-liver survival."
**Common misuse:** Described as a "cure" for biliary atresia. It is a bridge that may still end in transplant; success is measured by delayed transplant-free survival, not resolution of the underlying disease.

### Damage control laparotomy
An abbreviated operation — control contamination or bleeding, leave bowel in discontinuity or the abdomen open — that defers definitive reconstruction until the patient's physiology improves.
**In use:** "Second look in 24 to 48 hours, not a definitive anastomosis tonight — she's acidotic and cold."
**Common misuse:** Seen as an incomplete or failed operation rather than the correct choice when finishing definitively on the table would kill the patient before it helps them.

### Contralateral groin exploration
Surgical exploration of the clinically unaffected groin during a unilateral inguinal hernia repair, to check for an occult patent processus vaginalis.
**In use:** "She's 4 months old — we're doing a contralateral peek given the age-based occult-hernia rate."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a default in every child regardless of age. Current practice increasingly reserves it for younger infants and higher pretest-probability groups rather than applying it routinely in older children, where a second incision often isn't worth the marginal yield.

### Postmenstrual age (PMA)
Gestational age at birth plus chronological age since birth — the relevant clock for risk stratification of former premature infants, distinct from chronological age alone.
**In use:** "He's 3 weeks old chronologically but only 54 weeks PMA — he's still getting admitted for apnea monitoring after the hernia repair."
**Common misuse:** Risk assessed off chronological age alone, missing that a "term-looking" former preemie is still physiologically premature by the postmenstrual clock.
