# Forensic Science Technician — Vocabulary

### Locard's Exchange Principle
The premise that any physical contact between two surfaces/objects transfers material between them, however small.
**In use:** "Locard's principle says contact happened somewhere — the question is whether our search recovered it."
**Common misuse:** Treating a clean scan of a scene as proof no contact occurred, when it only proves the search didn't recover trace, not that none exists.

### Chain of custody
The unbroken, documented record of everyone who has had physical possession of an item of evidence, from collection through final disposition.
**In use:** "The custody log has to start at the moment of collection, not when it reaches the property room."
**Common misuse:** Treating lab intake as the start of the chain, leaving the scene-to-lab transport period undocumented.

### Presumptive test
A rapid, non-specific field or preliminary lab test indicating a substance *might* be present, subject to false positives.
**In use:** "Kastle-Meyer came back positive, so this swab gets priority for the confirmatory ABAcard test."
**Common misuse:** Reporting a presumptive-positive result as a confirmed finding without running the confirmatory test.

### Confirmatory test
A more specific downstream test that can support a report-ready conclusion, run only after a presumptive test screens an item in.
**In use:** "We don't write 'blood' in the report until the confirmatory result is back."
**Common misuse:** Skipping straight from presumptive to conclusion under time pressure.

### ACE-V (Analysis, Comparison, Evaluation, Verification)
The four-stage methodology for latent print (and some other pattern) examination: analyze the unknown, compare to the known, evaluate the match, then have a second examiner independently verify.
**In use:** "The comparison passed evaluation, but it still needs an independent ACE-V verification before it goes in the report."
**Common misuse:** Treating the first examiner's evaluation as final without the independent verification step, or having the "independent" verifier see the first examiner's conclusion beforehand.

### Class characteristics vs. individual characteristics
Class characteristics are shared by a group of objects (fiber type, shoe tread pattern from a given manufacturer); individual characteristics are unique to one specific object (accidental wear marks on that one shoe).
**In use:** "The tread pattern is a class characteristic — the nick in the third lug is what makes this individualizing."
**Common misuse:** Reporting a class-characteristic match ("consistent with a size 10 boot") in language that implies individualization.

### Elimination sample
A reference sample from a known, legitimate contributor (victim, first responder, resident) collected to distinguish their trace from an unknown contributor's.
**In use:** "Get the first responder's elimination swab now — we can't resolve the mixture without it later."
**Common misuse:** Treating elimination samples as optional or collecting them only after a mixture proves ambiguous, when the contributor may no longer be reachable.

### Linear sequential unmasking (LSU)
A workflow that sequences an examiner's exposure to case information so the technical comparison is completed before biasing context (confession, suspect history) is revealed.
**In use:** "Run this print comparison LSU-style — the case narrative doesn't get read until the comparison is signed off."
**Common misuse:** Assuming an experienced examiner is immune to contextual bias and skipping the unmasking protocol.

### Stochastic threshold
The peak-height level below which allelic dropout/dropin in a DNA electropherogram becomes likely, making low-level signal unreliable for straightforward interpretation.
**In use:** "Below the stochastic threshold we need probabilistic genotyping, not a simple binary allele call."
**Common misuse:** Interpreting a low-template mixture with the same manual method used for a clean, high-template single-source sample.

### Probabilistic genotyping
Software-based statistical modeling (e.g., STRmix) used to interpret complex or low-template DNA mixtures and produce a likelihood ratio rather than a simple inclusion/exclusion call.
**In use:** "The mixture had three contributors, so this went through probabilistic genotyping, not manual interpretation."
**Common misuse:** Treating the software's likelihood ratio output as a black-box number without documenting the interpretation parameters (thresholds, contributor number assumption) that fed it.

### Area of convergence / area of origin
In bloodstain pattern analysis, the point or region in three-dimensional space where the back-projected trajectories of multiple stains intersect, indicating where the blood source was located at impact.
**In use:** "Three stains converge in this region — that's a defensible area of origin, not just one stain's angle."
**Common misuse:** Calling a single stain's calculated angle an "area of origin," when one stain only constrains a line of possible trajectory, not a point.

### Inconclusive
A formal examination outcome stating the evidence doesn't support a match, exclusion, or any conclusion, distinct from either a positive or negative finding.
**In use:** "The partial print didn't have enough minutiae for a comparison — the finding is inconclusive, not a low-confidence match."
**Common misuse:** Treating "inconclusive" as a failure to report rather than a legitimate, defensible outcome in its own right.

### Contamination
The unintended introduction of extraneous material (DNA, trace, print) into a sample after collection, from personnel, tools, or cross-item contact.
**In use:** "The negative control came back with signal — that batch has a contamination problem we have to resolve before reporting any case sample from it."
**Common misuse:** Conflating contamination (unintended, lab/scene-process-driven) with legitimate secondary transfer that occurred before evidence was ever collected.

### Latent print
A print left by natural skin secretions (sweat, oils) that is not visible to the naked eye without development (powder, fuming, chemical).
**In use:** "We cyanoacrylate-fumed the doorframe and got a usable latent print."
**Common misuse:** Using "latent" to mean any print, including visible patent prints (e.g., in blood) or plastic impression prints (e.g., in wax), which use different processing methods.
