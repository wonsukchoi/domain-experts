# Psychiatric Aide Red Flags

### Patient skips 2+ consecutive scheduled activities/meals they normally attend
- **Usually means:** an early mood or motivation shift (depression worsening, medication side effect) before it's visible any other way; second most likely is a physical complaint the patient hasn't voiced yet.
- **First question:** "Has anything else changed today — appetite, sleep, what they said at the last check-in?"
- **Data to pull:** the patient's own attendance/intake log for the past 3–5 days, compared to today, not a general "is this normal for psych patients" comparison.

### Any statement with hopeless, self-harm, or "won't be a problem much longer" content, regardless of tone
- **Usually means:** could be passing, could be active ideation — that distinction is the nurse's assessment to make, not the aide's to pre-judge based on delivery calmness.
- **First question:** "What exactly did they say, word for word, and what were they doing right before?"
- **Data to pull:** none needed to report it — report first, document the verbatim quote and time immediately after.

### One aide is the only staff member a patient will engage with
- **Usually means:** genuine rapport (fine on its own) or the early stage of splitting/favoritism that erodes team consistency; second most likely is that other staff aren't engaging that patient enough and the "only one" pattern is a coverage gap, not a boundary issue.
- **First question:** "Is this patient engaging normally with everyone, or actively refusing other staff?"
- **Data to pull:** the activity/engagement log across all assigned staff for that patient over the past week.

### Patient requests contact information, a gift, or after-discharge contact from a specific staff member
- **Usually means:** boundary testing, which is common and not automatically alarming on its own — but an unreported instance normalizes escalation.
- **First question:** "Has this happened before, with this patient or with me specifically?"
- **Data to pull:** prior shift handoff notes for any earlier version of the same behavior.

### A restraint or seclusion event runs past its scheduled re-evaluation checkpoint
- **Usually means:** the team lost track of the clock during a busy shift, not a deliberate extension — but it's a compliance and safety finding either way.
- **First question:** "What time did the face-to-face/order-renewal deadline actually fall, and did it happen?"
- **Data to pull:** the restraint monitoring flowsheet with initiation time and the required evaluation timestamp.

### An aide reports feeling like they need to "manage" a patient's care plan or advocate against a specific staff decision
- **Usually means:** genuine empathy that has drifted past the aide's scope — the aide is functioning as the patient's advocate rather than a consistent member of the care team.
- **First question:** "What specifically do you disagree with, and have you raised it with the nurse directly instead of with the patient?"
- **Data to pull:** none — this is a supervision conversation, not a chart review.

### A new admission is markedly calmer or more compliant than expected within the first 24 hours
- **Usually means:** genuinely adjusting well (possible), or masking distress to be released faster, or an early medication effect suppressing outward signs the team hasn't calibrated to yet.
- **First question:** "Have they eaten, slept, and spoken normally, or just been quiet and cooperative?"
- **Data to pull:** admission notes and the first 24-hour vitals/ADL log, not just today's mood observation.

### Same patient triggers a restraint/Code response more than once in a week
- **Usually means:** the current de-escalation/engagement approach isn't working for this specific patient and needs a team-level review, not just "try harder" on the next shift; second most likely is an unaddressed environmental trigger (roommate conflict, a specific time of day, a specific staff interaction).
- **First question:** "What was different or the same about the lead-up each time?"
- **Data to pull:** the incident reports for each event side by side, looking for a shared trigger.

### A patient's ADL follow-through drops (hygiene, grooming, eating) without any verbal complaint
- **Usually means:** an early sign of worsening mood or cognition that hasn't surfaced in conversation yet — ADL follow-through is often the first thing to slip, before the patient says anything is wrong.
- **First question:** "When did I last see them shower/change clothes on their own, without a prompt?"
- **Data to pull:** the ADL checklist over the past 3–5 days.

### Aide is asked (by a peer, or feels pressure) to skip or shortcut a scheduled Q15/Q30 round
- **Usually means:** the unit is short-staffed and rounds are being deprioritized under time pressure — the single most common root cause of a falsified or skipped observation finding.
- **First question:** "Is this a one-time coverage gap tonight, or has this been happening regularly?"
- **Data to pull:** rounds-completion log for the unit over the past two weeks, not just tonight's shift.

### A patient begins declining structured recreational activities in favor of isolating in their room
- **Usually means:** withdrawal that precedes a mood or behavioral shift; second most likely is a conflict with another patient or staff member the patient hasn't named yet.
- **First question:** "Did something happen right before they stopped coming, or has it been gradual?"
- **Data to pull:** activity attendance log plus any recent roommate/patient-pairing changes.
