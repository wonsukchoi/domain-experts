# Psychiatric Aide Vocabulary

### Milieu
The structured social and physical environment of the unit — schedule, activities, staff-patient interaction patterns — treated as a therapeutic tool in its own right, not just the setting where treatment happens.
**In use:** "The milieu's been off all week since the schedule got disrupted by the census surge — that's probably as much of why incidents are up as anything about the patients themselves."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a synonym for "the ward" or "the building" rather than the active, manageable variable it actually is — as if calm or conflict on a unit is purely a function of who's admitted, not how the day is structured.

### 1:1 (one-to-one observation)
Continuous, arm's-length observation of a single patient by a dedicated staff member, ordered for active safety risk (suicide, self-harm, elopement).
**In use:** "K.'s on 1:1 — I don't leave that room, including for my own bathroom break, until someone relieves me directly."
**Common misuse:** Treated as interchangeable with Q15 checks ("I'll just check on her every 15 minutes since I'm slammed") — 1:1 means continuous, uninterrupted, arm's-length presence, not a more frequent version of interval rounding.

### Elopement
A patient leaving the unit or facility without authorization — distinct from a discharge, an escorted outing, or a pass.
**In use:** "New admit's flagged elopement risk — watch the exit doors when the cart comes through, that's the moment it usually happens."
**Common misuse:** Used loosely for any patient wandering within the unit; elopement specifically means leaving the secured area, which is a different response (Code/security notification) than redirecting someone pacing the hallway.

### Redirection
A verbal or environmental technique that shifts a patient's attention or location away from an escalating situation, without confronting the content of what's distressing them.
**In use:** "Arguing wasn't working, so I redirected him to the pool table instead — the mood broke in about five minutes."
**Common misuse:** Confused with distraction-as-dismissal ("just ignore what they're saying and change the subject") — effective redirection acknowledges the patient's state before offering the alternative, it doesn't talk over it.

### Chemical restraint
Medication given specifically to control behavior rather than to treat a diagnosed condition, distinguished from a legitimately prescribed PRN for symptom management.
**In use:** "That wasn't a chemical restraint, it was his standing PRN for anxiety, given at the dose and interval the order specifies."
**Common misuse:** Aides sometimes use the term loosely for any PRN medication given during an agitated moment; the distinction is intent and clinical justification, which is a nursing/prescriber determination — an aide reports the behavior, not the classification.

### Baseline
A specific patient's own usual pattern of behavior, mood, appetite, and engagement — the actual comparison point for whether something is a change worth reporting.
**In use:** "He's always quiet at breakfast, so quiet alone isn't his baseline shift — the baseline shift was skipping the card game, which he never does."
**Common misuse:** Replaced with a generic idea of "normal for a psych patient," which erases the individual comparison that actually makes an observation useful.

### Splitting
A dynamic (often associated with certain personality presentations but not exclusive to them) where a patient treats different staff members as "all good" or "all bad," which can fracture team consistency if staff don't communicate.
**In use:** "She told me the night shift 'never lets her do anything' — that's the second time this week I've heard a version of that, worth flagging at handoff."
**Common misuse:** Used as a diagnostic label ("she's just splitting") that dismisses the patient's actual complaint instead of checking whether the team's rules really are being applied inconsistently.

### Least restrictive intervention
The principle that a response to escalation should use the smallest, least confining measure that's actually safe — verbal de-escalation before seclusion, seclusion before physical restraint.
**In use:** "We went to a quiet room before restraint because separation alone was enough — least restrictive that actually worked."
**Common misuse:** Treated as "always try verbal first no matter what," even when danger is already imminent and the least-restrictive-that-works option is the safety response, not another attempt at conversation.

### Community meeting
A scheduled, structured group gathering of patients and staff (distinct from group therapy) focused on unit logistics, shared concerns, and social functioning.
**In use:** "Bring that roommate conflict up at community meeting tomorrow instead of letting it simmer — that's exactly what the meeting's for."
**Common misuse:** Confused with a clinical therapy group; an aide facilitates attendance and participation in a community meeting but doesn't lead the clinical content of a therapy group, which is staff/clinician-led.

### Code (or equivalent facility term for an emergency behavioral response)
A facility-wide call for an immediate team response to an acute safety event — patient violence, elopement attempt, or medical emergency on the unit.
**In use:** "Called a Code when he started throwing furniture — wasn't a moment for de-escalation alone anymore."
**Common misuse:** Called reflexively for any loud or disruptive but non-dangerous behavior, which both desensitizes the team to real Codes and pulls staff away from other patients unnecessarily.

### ADL (activities of daily living)
Basic self-care tasks — bathing, dressing, grooming, eating, toileting — that an aide assists with and that double as a window for behavioral observation.
**In use:** "His ADL follow-through's dropped this week — hasn't showered without a prompt in three days, and that's new for him."
**Common misuse:** Treated purely as a task list to check off, missing that ADL follow-through is itself one of the earliest and most reliable behavioral indicators an aide has access to.

### Scope of practice
The specific set of tasks a role is authorized to perform — for a psychiatric aide, direct care and observation, explicitly excluding medication administration, clinical assessment, or care-plan decisions.
**In use:** "I'm not going to tell you if that's the right dose — that's outside what I can weigh in on, I'll get the nurse."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a formality rather than a real boundary — an aide who starts offering clinical opinions ("I don't think you need that medication") is operating outside scope even when well-intentioned, and it undermines the clinical team's authority with the patient.
