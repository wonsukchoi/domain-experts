### CFM50 / ACH50
The airflow (in cubic feet per minute) a blower door must move to change house pressure by 50 Pascals relative to outdoors, and that same result expressed as air changes per hour at that pressure.
**In use:** "House came in at 3,200 CFM50, which works out to 13.33 ACH50 against a 14,400 cubic-foot volume."
**Common misuse:** Treating CFM50 as directly comparable across houses of different sizes without converting to ACH50 (which normalizes for volume) — a 3,200 CFM50 reading means something different in a 900 ft² house than an 1,800 ft² one.

### Combustion appliance zone (CAZ)
The room or connected space containing a fuel-burning appliance and its air supply, tested as a unit because pressure changes anywhere in the zone affect the appliance's ability to vent safely.
**In use:** "The CAZ includes the utility closet and the adjoining laundry room since the door between them is undercut and doesn't seal."
**Common misuse:** Testing only the room the appliance physically sits in, missing a connected space (like an undercut-door laundry room) that's part of the same pressure zone.

### Worst-case depressurization
A combustion-safety test condition created by running every exhaust device that could pull air out of the CAZ simultaneously (bath fans, kitchen exhaust, clothes dryer) with interior doors closed, to find the most negative pressure the appliance could realistically face.
**In use:** "Idle reading was -1 Pa, but worst case with the dryer and both bath fans running came in at -7 Pa."
**Common misuse:** Reporting the idle or as-found CAZ pressure as the safety finding instead of staging worst-case conditions, which misses backdraft risk that only appears under realistic simultaneous appliance operation.

### Spillage test
A test that checks whether combustion byproducts are venting properly or spilling into the living space, typically using a mirror, smoke pencil, or match at the draft hood under worst-case conditions.
**In use:** "Spillage test at the water heater draft hood showed smoke pulling back into the room and never clearing — that's a fail."
**Common misuse:** Running the spillage test under idle conditions instead of worst-case depressurization, or calling a brief initial spill that self-clears within the test window the same as a spill that persists throughout.

### Savings-to-investment ratio (SIR)
The present value of a measure's projected savings over its service life, divided by its installed cost, using a program-specified discount rate; the standard cost-effectiveness metric for weatherization and utility rebate programs.
**In use:** "Insulation savings look real, but SIR comes out to 0.90 once you discount over its 20-year life — it doesn't clear the program's 1.0 cutoff."
**Common misuse:** Substituting simple payback (years to recoup cost, ignoring service life and discounting) for SIR, or computing SIR without a stated service life and discount rate, both of which produce a number that isn't actually SIR.

### Duct leakage to outside
The portion of a duct system's total air leakage that exits the conditioned envelope entirely (into an attic, crawlspace, or garage), as distinct from leakage within conditioned space.
**In use:** "Total leakage is 220 CFM25, but only 140 of that is to outside — that's the figure that matters for the code threshold."
**Common misuse:** Reporting total duct leakage alone and comparing it against a to-outside threshold, or vice versa — the two figures answer different questions and the sequencing decision depends on the to-outside number specifically.

### N-factor
A climate- and building-specific conversion factor used to estimate natural (weather-driven) infiltration airflow from a blower door's CFM50 result.
**In use:** "Using an N-factor of 17 for this climate zone, the CFM50 reduction translates to about 75 CFM of actual natural infiltration reduction."
**Common misuse:** Applying a single generic N-factor across all climate zones and building heights, when the factor is meant to vary with local climate severity and building exposure.

### Health-and-safety measure
A retrofit item required to correct a life-safety or occupant-health condition (combustion backdraft, inadequate ventilation, moisture/mold risk), funded regardless of whether it independently clears a program's SIR cutoff.
**In use:** "The make-up air fix is a health-and-safety measure — it goes in the scope whether or not it has its own SIR."
**Common misuse:** Applying the SIR cutoff to a health-and-safety item and excluding it from the funded scope when it fails, when program rules exempt this category from the cost-effectiveness test entirely.

### Weather normalization
Adjusting metered utility consumption to account for a specific year's actual weather (heating/cooling degree-days) so it can be compared against a typical year or against a projected post-retrofit result on an apples-to-apples basis.
**In use:** "The 950 therms is already weather-normalized against the local station's degree-days, not the raw metered total from an unusually mild winter."
**Common misuse:** Comparing a raw (non-normalized) pre-retrofit year against a raw post-retrofit year and attributing the entire difference to the retrofit, when part of the change is just a difference in how cold or hot each year actually was.

### ASHRAE 62.2 ventilation threshold
The minimum mechanical or natural ventilation rate a dwelling needs based on floor area and bedroom count, used as the trigger for adding mechanical ventilation when air-sealing work tightens a house past what natural infiltration alone can supply.
**In use:** "Post-work ACH50 of 8 stays above the ventilation trigger for this house, so no mechanical ventilation measure is required in the scope."
**Common misuse:** Treating a tight blower-door result as an unqualified win without checking whether the tightening crossed the ventilation-adequacy threshold for that specific floor area and occupancy.

### Priority list (PL) audit
A DOE-defined energy audit method that assigns a standard, pre-ranked measure sequence to a dwelling based on housing type and climate, used when the building matches a "similar dwelling units without unusual energy-consuming characteristics" profile rather than requiring a full computerized audit.
**In use:** "This is a standard single-family ranch with no unusual systems, so it qualifies for the regional Priority List instead of a full NEAT audit."
**Common misuse:** Applying a Priority List to a building with an atypical system (e.g., an unusual heating fuel or an addition with different construction) where the standard ranking no longer reflects that building's actual cost-effectiveness order.

### Backdrafting
A condition where combustion byproducts (including carbon monoxide) are pulled back down a vent or flue into the living space instead of exhausting outdoors, typically caused by negative pressure in the combustion appliance zone.
**In use:** "The worst-case test confirmed backdrafting at the water heater — that has to clear before any air sealing happens in that zone."
**Common misuse:** Assuming backdrafting only happens with strong wind or extreme conditions, rather than testing for it under normal, staged worst-case operating conditions that occur routinely in daily use.

### Blower-door-directed air sealing
Air sealing performed while the blower door is running (creating a pressure differential that makes leaks detectable by hand, smoke pencil, or thermal camera), as opposed to sealing based on a static visual inspection alone.
**In use:** "Sealing was blower-door directed, so the crew could feel and confirm each leak closing in real time rather than guessing from the walkthrough notes."
**Common misuse:** Treating "air sealing was performed" as sufficient documentation without specifying whether it was blower-door directed, which materially affects whether the largest leaks were actually the ones addressed.
