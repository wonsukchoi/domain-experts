# Red flags

Smell tests a top hand catches before they become an incident report. Format for each: what it usually means, the first question to ask, and the data to pull.

## 1. Casing/tubing pressure bleeds to zero and the crew moves straight to breakout

**Usually means:** the crew is treating an instantaneous bleed as proof of a dead well instead of running the observation/build-back check — the single most common precursor to breaking a connection on a well that isn't actually dead.

**First question:** "Did we hold the bleed and watch it for ten-plus minutes, or did we bleed once and go?"

**Data to pull:** the bleed log with timestamps, whether pressure was re-checked after the hold period, and the well's gas-migration history (any prior build-back events on this lease).

## 2. Company representative's work order says "confirmed dead" with no bleed data attached

**Usually means:** "confirmed dead" is a production report from days ago, not a same-day physical check — trapped pressure can develop in the interim even on a well that genuinely was dead when last checked.

**First question:** "When was the well last physically bled and observed, and by whom?"

**Data to pull:** date/time of the last verified bleed-and-hold, days since shut-in, and whether the well has gas-bearing zones with a migration history.

## 3. Crew reports "it's coming, just needs a little more" on a stuck pull

**Usually means:** hookload is already at or past a safe overpull fraction of the string's rated yield, and the crew is normalizing continued force instead of stopping to check the number.

**First question:** "What's current hookload against the string's rated pull, in numbers, right now?"

**Data to pull:** weight indicator reading, string's calculated body yield strength (grade, wall thickness), and the job's pre-set overpull limit.

## 4. BOP function test skipped or logged as "same as last week"

**Usually means:** time pressure on the job is winning over a hard safety step — the stack's actual function on today's job is unverified regardless of last week's result.

**First question:** "Show me today's function-test log with today's date and today's crew's initials."

**Data to pull:** function-test log, date of last full pressure test (vs. function test), and stack pressure rating against the job's anticipated pressure.

## 5. Fresh tubing tally disagrees with the well file by more than ~15–20 ft

**Usually means:** either a joint-count error in the fresh tally, or the well file is stale from an undocumented re-completion or tubing change — either way, setting a plug or perforating off either number without reconciling risks the wrong depth.

**First question:** "Has anyone recounted the tally, and is there a collar or gamma-ray log to tie depth to independently?"

**Data to pull:** the stand-by-stand tally sheet, the well file's last completion date, and any available correlation log.

## 6. Fishing job running past its third day on what started as a one-day pull

**Usually means:** sunk-cost thinking is driving continued fishing attempts past the point the interval's value justifies the cost — nobody has explicitly named the stopping threshold.

**First question:** "What's the cumulative cost of this fishing job so far, and what's the zone actually worth if we get it back?"

**Data to pull:** day-rate cost accrued, estimated remaining fishing time and cost, and the interval's production/reserve value.

## 7. Personnel standing near a line, tong, or traveling block under load "because it's a quick check"

**Usually means:** normal-feeling routine is eroding the line-of-fire discipline that matters most exactly in unremarkable moments — most struck-by incidents happen on ordinary pulls, not unusual ones.

**First question:** "Why is anyone standing there while that line is loaded?"

**Data to pull:** the crew's line-of-fire briefing/JSA for the task, and whether the position violates a standing hold on the job.

## 8. Swab run recovers far more or far less fluid than the well's known productivity predicts

**Usually means:** either an uncontrolled influx (too much recovery, too fast) or a mechanical problem — plugged perforations, stuck swab cups, wrong fluid level assumption (too little recovery).

**First question:** "How does this run's recovered volume compare to the last three runs on this well?"

**Data to pull:** per-run recovered volume and fluid-level log, swab speed, and any gas or unexpected fluid noted at surface.

## 9. Bullhead attempted on a zone with no established injectivity

**Usually means:** the crew chose the faster kill method without confirming the formation will actually take fluid — pumping into a zone that won't take it raises surface pressure instead of controlling the well.

**First question:** "Has this zone ever taken an injection or kill fluid before, and at what rate/pressure?"

**Data to pull:** prior injectivity data (any completion or workover record showing fluid taken), current pump pressure trend during the attempt, and whether lubricate-and-bleed was considered instead.

## 10. Kill fluid weight ordered without a documented SICP/SITP and TVD behind the number

**Usually means:** the kill weight was estimated or copied from a prior job instead of calculated from today's readings — an under-weighted kill fluid won't control the well, an over-weighted one risks breaking down the formation.

**First question:** "Walk me through the kill-weight calculation — what SICP, what TVD, what starting fluid weight?"

**Data to pull:** the shut-in pressure reading with timestamp, TVD from the well file, and the arithmetic behind the ordered fluid weight.
