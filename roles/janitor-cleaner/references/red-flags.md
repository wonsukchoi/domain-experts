# Red flags

Smell tests a veteran custodian or facilities manager catches on a walkthrough. Format for each: what it usually means, the first question to ask, and the data to pull before anyone decides anything.

## 1. Restroom "smells clean" but the air stings the eyes

**Usually means:** disinfectant applied over-strength (not measured against the label ratio), or two incompatible chemical residues meeting on the same surface — most dangerously a bleach-based product over ammonia-based residue, which produces chloramine gas rather than just a strong smell.

**First question:** "What products were used here tonight, in what order, and were they diluted with a portion cup or poured by eye?"

**Data to pull:** the chemical log/checklist for that room, the dilution ratio on each product's label vs. what was actually used, and whether a dispensing/metering system exists or everything is poured by hand.

## 2. Same spot generates repeated slip complaints despite regular mopping

**Usually means:** mop water or rinse step is leaving a thin film residue (wrong dilution, dirty mop water reused across too many rooms, or no rinse step at all), or the floor is reopened to traffic before the wet-mop dwell/dry time has passed.

**First question:** "How many rooms does one mop-bucket fill cover before the water's changed, and how long after mopping is the area reopened?"

**Data to pull:** mop-water change frequency vs. manufacturer guidance (commonly every 3–4 rooms or when visibly soiled), floor-dry timing logs, and whether wet-floor signage stays up until the floor is confirmed dry rather than a fixed number of minutes.

## 3. Floor finish looks cloudy or "milky" after a recoat

**Usually means:** finish applied over a still-damp floor, over-diluted finish, or incompatible finish/sealer layering (a different manufacturer's chemistry applied over an existing system without compatibility testing).

**First question:** "Was the floor confirmed dry before this coat went down, and is this the same finish product as the layer underneath?"

**Data to pull:** floor-care log for product/lot used on the last several recoats, ambient humidity/dry-time at application, and a small test-patch compatibility check before the next full recoat.

## 4. Burnisher leaves visible swirl marks that get worse with each pass

**Usually means:** pad grit mismatched to the finish's hardness (too aggressive a pad on a soft finish, or too fine a pad failing to remove enough soil), or built-up wax/finish never stripped before an incompatible finish was layered on top.

**First question:** "What pad grade is loaded, and when was this floor last fully stripped versus just recoated?"

**Data to pull:** pad spec vs. finish manufacturer's recommended pairing, strip/recoat history for the zone, burnisher RPM setting against the pad's rated speed.

## 5. Chemical bottles in the closet are decanted into unlabeled or handwritten-label containers

**Usually means:** an OSHA HazCom (1910.1200) violation in progress — nobody working from that container knows the actual product, dilution, or SDS, and a mixing incident (acid + bleach, ammonia + bleach) is one wrong grab away.

**First question:** "Can you tell me what's in this bottle and its SDS without opening it?"

**Data to pull:** SDS binder/app completeness against every product physically on carts and in the closet, and whether the facility has metered dispensing that would make decanting unnecessary in the first place.

## 6. A biohazard/bodily-fluid cleanup is logged at under 10 minutes total

**Usually means:** the disinfectant's labeled dwell time (commonly 5–10 minutes minimum, kept wet) wasn't actually observed — the protocol was performed cosmetically, not to the label's efficacy claim.

**First question:** "Walk me through the timeline — when did the disinfectant go down, and when was it wiped?"

**Data to pull:** the incident log timestamp vs. the specific disinfectant's labeled dwell time; whether the area was re-inspected before signage came down.

## 7. Same restroom or supply point is repeatedly out of stock at inspection

**Usually means:** either the rotation/staffing plan under-budgets resupply time, or par levels (how much stock is kept on the cart vs. in the closet) were set without checking actual daily consumption.

**First question:** "What's the resupply frequency built into tonight's route for this room, and when was it last actually restocked?"

**Data to pull:** consumption rate per fixture/day, cart par-level vs. route frequency, and whether the shortfall clusters on specific nights (staffing gap) or specific rooms (par-level miscalculation).

## 8. Staffing sits well above the APPA square-feet-per-custodian benchmark for the promised service level

**Usually means:** the client contract promises a service level (e.g., nightly full clean) that the staffing level can't mathematically deliver — the gap shows up either as burnout/turnover or as silently skipped tasks nobody's tracking.

**First question:** "What service level was sold, and what's the actual sq-ft-per-custodian ratio against the APPA benchmark for that level?"

**Data to pull:** contracted sq ft and service-level tier, current headcount and shift hours, and a rotation log showing what's actually getting skipped versus what the contract assumes is happening nightly.

## 9. "Green" or GS-42 cleaning-program claims with no metered dilution equipment on site

**Usually means:** the certification or marketing claim isn't backed by the dilution-control and HEPA-filtration practices GS-42 actually requires — a compliance-on-paper gap that surfaces badly in an audit or client walkthrough.

**First question:** "Show me the dispensing system and the vacuum filtration spec this program is certified against."

**Data to pull:** GS-42 (or equivalent) certification scope document, dispensing equipment inventory, vacuum HEPA rating spec sheets.

## 10. A sharps object or needle turns up outside a clinical setting

**Usually means:** either an unreported medical use on-site (diabetic self-injection, illicit drug use in a restroom) that facilities should know the pattern of, or a delivery/contractor left something behind — either way it needs sharps-specific handling, not the general biohazard-bag protocol.

**First question:** "Where exactly, and has this location had a sharps find before?"

**Data to pull:** location-specific incident history (a repeat location changes the response — signage, lighting, patrol frequency — not just the cleanup), sharps-container disposal chain confirmation, and whether facilities/security needs a same-night notification rather than a next-day log entry.
