# Vocabulary

Terms generalists conflate that practitioners keep sharply separate. Each: definition, how a practitioner actually uses it in a sentence, and the common misuse.

## Dwell time / contact time

The minimum period a disinfectant must remain visibly wet on a surface for its EPA-registered kill claim to apply — not how long it takes to wipe something up. Commonly 1–10 minutes depending on product and pathogen claim, stated on the label.

**In use:** "That's a 10-minute dwell disinfectant — if we wipe it at three minutes, we didn't disinfect, we just relocated the residue."

**Common misuse:** treating "I sprayed it and wiped it" as equivalent to disinfecting, regardless of how long the surface actually stayed wet.

## Dilution ratio

The label-specified proportion of concentrate to water (e.g., 1:64 means 1 part product to 64 parts water), the only correct basis for mixing a chemical without a metered dispenser.

**In use:** "This is a 1:256 quat — that's about half an ounce per gallon, not a capful."

**Common misuse:** eyeballing "a good glug" or judging concentration by smell/color instead of measuring against the labeled ratio, which drifts toward over-concentration as a bottle empties.

## Burnish vs. buff vs. polish

**Burnish** is a high-speed (1,000–2,000 RPM) dry or spray pass that melts and levels the top of an existing floor finish to restore gloss without adding material. **Buff/spray-buff** is the lower-speed version of the same idea, sometimes with a light finish application. **Polish** in commercial floor care usually just means "make it shiny" and isn't a defined process — practitioners avoid the word because it hides which actual procedure is meant.

**In use:** "Burnish it tonight, don't recoat — the finish underneath is fine, it just lost gloss."

**Common misuse:** using "polish" to mean any of the above, which leaves the crew guessing whether chemical goes down or not.

## Strip vs. recoat vs. deep scrub

**Strip** removes all existing finish layers down to the substrate with a stripping chemical, done when the finish itself has failed (yellowing, embedded soil, delamination). **Recoat** applies one or more new finish layers on top of an intact existing finish, done as routine maintenance. **Deep scrub** removes surface soil and the top layer or two of finish without going to bare substrate — a middle option between burnish and full strip.

**In use:** "We don't need a strip, we need a deep scrub and two coats — the base finish is still sound."

**Common misuse:** calling any aggressive floor-machine pass a "strip," which overstates the chemical and labor cost being proposed and can lead to unnecessary full strips on floors a deep scrub would have fixed.

## Universal precautions

The OSHA 1910.1030 practice of treating all blood and certain body fluids as if known to be infectious, applied by default rather than case-by-case judgment about how likely a given spill is to actually be hazardous.

**In use:** "We don't know what that stain is, so it's universal precautions — gloves, disinfectant, the full protocol — regardless of how likely it is to be coffee."

**Common misuse:** applying "common sense" instead — deciding a stain "is probably nothing" and skipping PPE or protocol based on that guess, which is the exact judgment universal precautions exists to remove.

## Regulated waste vs. biohazard bag vs. general trash

**Regulated waste** is the OSHA/state-defined category covering blood-contaminated materials that require special handling and disposal, not just visible packaging. A **biohazard bag** (red, labeled) is the container used to hold it pending pickup by a licensed regulated-waste hauler. **General trash** is everything else, and it is never a legal destination for regulated waste even in small quantities.

**In use:** "That's regulated waste once gloves and absorbent touch blood — it goes in the red bag and the biohazard hold locker, not the dumpster."

**Common misuse:** assuming a "small amount" of contaminated material is fine in general trash, or assuming any bagged-up cleaning waste is automatically "biohazard" when it wasn't actually exposed to a regulated fluid.

## Zone cleaning vs. team cleaning vs. deep-clean rotation

**Zone cleaning** assigns one worker full responsibility for a defined area (all task types within that zone). **Team cleaning** assigns each worker one task type (vacuuming, restrooms, trash) across the whole building. A **deep-clean rotation** is neither of those — it's a schedule that spreads lower-frequency tasks (full carpet extraction, edge dusting, full floor strips) across a multi-week or multi-month cycle regardless of which staffing model is used day to day.

**In use:** "We run team cleaning nightly, but carpet extraction is on a deep-clean rotation — Zone 2 gets it the second Tuesday of the month."

**Common misuse:** treating "rotation" and "zone" as synonyms — a rotation is about frequency over time, a zone is about area ownership; a building can run either staffing model with or without a rotation layered on top.

## CIMS vs. Green Seal / GS-42

**CIMS** (Cleaning Industry Management Standard, ISSA) certifies an organization's overall management system — quality systems, training, HR practices — for cleaning operations. **Green Seal GS-42** certifies that a cleaning *service's* practices (chemical selection, dilution control, equipment specs like HEPA filtration) meet a specific environmental/health standard. They audit different things and a claim of one doesn't imply the other.

**In use:** "We're CIMS-certified as a company, but this specific building's program hasn't gone through GS-42 — that's a separate scope."

**Common misuse:** citing either certification as a blanket "we're green/quality-certified" claim without specifying which standard covers which practice.

## PPE (personal protective equipment) — level, not binary

PPE selection scales to the actual hazard (glove-only for routine chemical handling vs. gloves + eye protection + gown for a splash-risk biohazard cleanup), not a single fixed kit worn the same way for every task.

**In use:** "Gloves only for the mop water, but add eye protection and a gown for this one — it's on a stair edge and there's splash risk."

**Common misuse:** either under-protecting (bare-handed on an unidentified spill) or overcorrecting (full-suiting for a plain water spill), both of which come from treating PPE as one fixed kit instead of a hazard-matched decision.

## Cross-contamination (color-coded microfiber)

The transfer of contaminants between areas via shared cleaning tools — most commonly a cloth or mop used in a restroom, then reused in a food-service or general area without a dedicated, color-coded system to prevent it.

**In use:** "Red cloths don't leave the restroom cart, period — that's what stops a restroom wipe-down from cross-contaminating the break room counter."

**Common misuse:** treating color-coding as a nice-to-have organizational habit rather than the actual control that prevents pathogen transfer between zones.

## Production rate

The sq-ft-per-hour (or units-per-hour) benchmark a task should take under normal conditions, used to plan shift capacity — not a measure of how hard or fast a specific person is working on a given night.

**In use:** "At a 4,000 sq ft/hour damp-mop rate, this corridor is a three-hour task regardless of who's assigned to it."

**Common misuse:** using production rates to judge an individual worker's effort or speed rather than to plan capacity and staffing — a rate that's consistently missed points to task scope, equipment, or staffing, not automatically to the person.

## HEPA filtration rating

A specific, measurable standard (≥99.97% particle capture at 0.3 microns) — not a marketing claim that a vacuum "has a filter" or "is good for allergies."

**In use:** "Check the spec sheet — is this actually HEPA-rated at 99.97/0.3, or just a filtered vacuum being marketed as HEPA?"

**Common misuse:** accepting "HEPA-style" or "HEPA-like" language on equipment as equivalent to a certified HEPA filtration spec.

## SDS (Safety Data Sheet)

The current, GHS-aligned document required for every chemical on-site, replacing the older MSDS (Material Safety Data Sheet) format — it must be accessible at the point of use, not filed away, and it's the authoritative source for dilution, PPE, and incompatibility information, not the bottle's marketing label.

**In use:** "Before we combine anything, check the SDS incompatibility section — the label alone won't tell you what it reacts with."

**Common misuse:** treating "MSDS" and "SDS" as different documents that need separate filing, or relying on a product's front label instead of the SDS for hazard and incompatibility information.
