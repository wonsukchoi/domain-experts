# Playbook

Filled operational references for commercial/institutional janitorial work: route sequencing, chemical dilution, floor-care cycles, and the bodily-fluid spill protocol. These are starting templates to adapt to a specific building, not scripts to follow blind.

## 1. Nightly route template (zone-based, high-to-low, dry-before-wet)

**Building example:** 3-story office, 52,000 sq ft, solo overnight crew, 450 productive minutes.

| Order | Zone | Task type | Why this order |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Floor 3 (top) | Trash pull, dust/high-dust | Start at the top; tracked debris and dust settle downward through the rest of the shift, not against it |
| 2 | Floor 3 | Vacuum carpet (dry) | Dry task before any wet task in the same room — wet-mop overspray on a still-dusty floor just relocates dirt |
| 3 | Floor 2 | Trash pull, dust/high-dust | Same logic, one floor down |
| 4 | Floor 2 | Vacuum carpet (dry) | |
| 5 | Floor 1 corridors/breakrooms | Dust-mop hard floor (dry) | Dry before wet, same room |
| 6 | Floor 1 corridors/breakrooms | Damp-mop hard floor (wet) | Wet task last in this zone so nobody re-tracks a drying floor |
| 7 | Restrooms, all floors | Full clean + resupply | Scheduled after dry/dust work elsewhere so restroom traffic isn't re-contaminating a just-cleaned corridor |
| 8 | Lobby | Burnish (if scheduled) or spot-mop | Last, because it's the first thing occupants see arriving — freshest finish at open |
| 9 | Cart restock, log entry, walkthrough | — | Always last; a skipped walkthrough is how a missed spill becomes tomorrow's complaint |

**Rotation overlay:** carpet vacuum splits the building into 4 zones (~10,000 sq ft each); one zone gets a full vacuum per night on a 4-night cycle, so every zone is fully vacuumed roughly weekly even though nightly capacity can't cover 32,000 sq ft of carpet in one shift. Trash, restrooms, and main hard-floor corridors are never on rotation — they run every night regardless of what else gets cut.

## 2. Chemical dilution quick reference

Ratios are label-typical, not universal — always defer to the specific product's SDS and label when they differ from the table.

| Category | Typical dilution | Dwell/contact time | Surface notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| General-purpose neutral cleaner | 1:64 (2 oz/gal) | none required | Safe on sealed floors, most hard surfaces; over-dilution reads as "not clean," under-dilution leaves tacky residue |
| Glass/window cleaner | Ready-to-use, no dilution | none | Never substitute a diluted general-purpose cleaner — streaking |
| Disinfectant (quaternary ammonium) | 1:128 to 1:256 | 10 min wet-contact (label-dependent) | Must stay visibly wet for the full dwell time to meet the EPA-registered kill claim — wiping dry early voids the claim |
| Disinfectant (bleach-based) | 1:10 to 1:64 depending on claim | 1–10 min | **Never combine with ammonia-based product residue** — generates toxic chloramine gas; flush/rinse surface first if switching product families |
| Degreaser | 1:10 to 1:40 | 3–5 min | Kitchen/break-room hoods, entry mats; test on sealed wood or vinyl composition tile (VCT) finish before full application |
| Acid-based restroom bowl cleaner | Ready-to-use or 1:8 | 5–10 min | **Never on natural stone (marble, terrazzo, travertine)** — etches the surface irreversibly; stone gets a neutral-pH cleaner only |
| Floor stripper | 1:4 to 1:1 (per product, cool water) | 5–10 min, kept wet, never allowed to dry on the floor | Full-strength or near it; drying on the floor re-hardens the old finish into the surface |

**Rule of thumb for anything not on a metered dispenser:** measure with a portion cup against the label ratio every time, not "by feel" — feel drifts toward over-concentration as a bottle empties and someone tries to make the last inch "count."

## 3. Floor-care cycle: burnish vs. strip decision

| Indicator | Burnish/recoat | Full strip |
|---|---|---|
| Gloss dulled evenly, no visible scuff embedded in finish | Yes — burnish restores gloss without adding chemical load | No |
| Black heel marks lift with a burnish pad | Yes — burnish/scrub-and-recoat | No |
| Black heel marks remain after burnishing (embedded in finish layers) | No | Yes — finish has absorbed soil past what a surface pass removes |
| Visible yellowing or cloudy/milky buildup across multiple coats | No | Yes |
| Edge delamination (finish lifting/peeling at wall lines or seams) | No | Yes |
| Finish has had 6–10 recoats since last strip with no single defect above, but overall clarity is declining | Recoat again, but flag for strip within 1–2 cycles | Not yet, but schedule it |

**Typical cycle (heavy-traffic commercial corridor/lobby):** initial finish application 4–6 thin coats; burnish or spray-buff 1–3x/week to maintain gloss between recoats; light recoat (1 coat) every 1–3 months depending on traffic; full strip and reapplication when the table above calls for it — commonly every 6–18 months in high-traffic zones, longer in low-traffic areas. Calendar-only stripping (e.g., "always strip every 6 months regardless of condition") wastes labor and chemical on a floor the burnish cycle was already handling.

## 4. Bodily-fluid / biohazard spill protocol

Applies to any fluid of unknown or confirmed bodily origin (blood, vomit, urine, feces) per OSHA 1910.1030 universal precautions.

1. **Classify.** Known bodily fluid or unknown-origin fluid with any ambiguity → treat as bodily fluid. Confidently identified non-bodily (spilled beverage, tracked rainwater, a labeled chemical spill) → standard spill cleanup, not this protocol.
2. **Contain.** Cordon the area from all approach angles with wet-floor signage or cones before touching anything; do not leave the area unattended once cordoned until cleared.
3. **PPE.** Minimum: nitrile gloves. Add eye protection and a fluid-resistant gown/apron if there's any splash risk (a fall down stairs, a larger-volume spill, an elevated surface).
4. **Absorb and contain waste.** Solidifying absorbent for liquid volume; scoop/collect into a red biohazard bag — never a general trash liner.
5. **Disinfect.** Apply an EPA-registered disinfectant with an HIV/HBV-effective claim (EPA List B/D/E) at the labeled dilution; keep the surface visibly wet for the full labeled dwell time (commonly 1–10 minutes) — this is the step most often shortcut under time pressure and the one that actually matters for the efficacy claim.
6. **Rinse and re-inspect.** Wipe/rinse residue, confirm the surface is dry and non-slip before removing signage and reopening the area.
7. **Dispose correctly.** Bag and seal contaminated PPE and cleanup materials, label as regulated waste, and route to the facility's designated biohazard/regulated-waste stream — never general dumpster trash. A facility without a regulated medical-waste contract still needs a documented interim hold location and next-business-day escalation to the facilities manager, not a decision to bin it.
8. **Log.** Every incident gets a written entry: location, time, what was found, protocol steps taken, disposal routing, and any recommended follow-up (tenant notification, first-aid referral). This is required documentation, not optional paperwork — it's what protects the janitor and the employer if an exposure claim or inspection follows.

## 5. Restroom service frequency (high-traffic facility)

| Facility type | Full clean cycle | Mid-shift check |
|---|---|---|
| Standard office building | Once per overnight shift | Not required |
| Retail, transit hub, stadium (open hours) | Every 4–6 hours during open hours | Visual check every 2 hours, log initialed on posted card |
| Airport, arena event day | Every 2 hours | Continuous rotation, dedicated attendant |

A posted, initialed inspection card in the restroom isn't bureaucracy — it's the artifact that answers "when was this last checked" without anyone having to ask, and it's usually the first thing a facilities audit or a complaint investigation asks to see.
