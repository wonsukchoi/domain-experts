# School Psychologist — Vocabulary

### Dual discrepancy
A student showing both a level discrepancy (current skill below peer benchmark) and a rate discrepancy (growth rate below typical) relative to peers, used as evidence for Specific Learning Disability eligibility under RTI models.
**In use:** "The dual discrepancy is there — he's 26 words behind on level and growing at a third of the typical rate."
**Common misuse:** Treating a level gap alone (student is behind but growing normally) as sufficient for an SLD finding — that's often an instructional-timing or curriculum-mismatch issue, not necessarily a disability.

### Response to Intervention (RTI)
A multi-tiered instructional framework (Tier 1 core, Tier 2 targeted, Tier 3 intensive) used both to intervene early and, in many states, to gather eligibility data by measuring whether a student responds adequately to increasingly intensive, evidence-based instruction.
**In use:** "We need two more data points before I can say this is a Tier 3 non-response, not just a slow start."
**Common misuse:** Treating any non-response as automatically diagnostic without checking whether the intervention was delivered with fidelity.

### Fidelity (intervention fidelity)
The degree to which an intervention was delivered as designed — session count, duration, and adherence to the protocol.
**In use:** "Fidelity was only 60% — half the sessions were led by a rotating aide instead of the trained interventionist, so I can't trust this as a true non-response."
**Common misuse:** Assuming fidelity because a service was "on the schedule," without checking session logs or observing delivery.

### Exclusionary factors
The IDEA-specified list of causes (lack of appropriate instruction, vision/hearing/motor impairment, intellectual disability, emotional disturbance, cultural/economic disadvantage, limited English proficiency) that must be ruled out as the *primary* cause of low achievement before an SLD or other disability finding is defensible.
**In use:** "We've documented each exclusionary factor separately — this isn't a checkbox, it's the section that holds up in a hearing."
**Common misuse:** Treating the presence of an exclusionary factor as automatically disqualifying, or its absence-from-discussion as equivalent to having ruled it out.

### Functional Behavior Assessment (FBA)
A structured process to determine the function (purpose) a behavior serves for a student — escape/avoidance, attention, access to a tangible, or sensory stimulation — using indirect (interview, rating scale) and direct (ABC observation) data.
**In use:** "The FBA points to escape — every occurrence follows an independent task above her current skill level."
**Common misuse:** Skipping direct observation and inferring function from interview alone, which frequently misidentifies the actual function.

### ABC observation (Antecedent-Behavior-Consequence)
Direct observational data collection recording what happened immediately before a target behavior, the behavior itself, and what happened immediately after — used to identify the behavior's function empirically.
**In use:** "Three ABC observations all show the same pattern: task assigned, leaves seat, gets a break via redirection."
**Common misuse:** Recording only the behavior and consequence without the antecedent, which loses the information needed to identify the trigger.

### Behavior Intervention Plan (BIP)
A written plan, built from FBA findings, that teaches and reinforces a replacement behavior serving the same function as the problem behavior, alongside environmental/instructional changes.
**In use:** "The BIP has to include a break card — otherwise we're just punishing escape-seeking without giving her an acceptable way to get the same outcome."
**Common misuse:** Writing a BIP that only lists consequences for the problem behavior, with no replacement behavior addressing the underlying function.

### Manifestation determination review (MDR)
A required meeting, triggered when a student with an IEP faces a disciplinary placement change exceeding 10 cumulative school days, to determine whether the behavior was caused by or directly related to the disability.
**In use:** "We're at 11 cumulative days this year — the MDR has to happen before any further removal."
**Common misuse:** Only tracking days from the current incident instead of the cumulative count across the school year.

### Least Restrictive Environment (LRE)
The IDEA requirement that students with disabilities be educated with non-disabled peers to the maximum extent appropriate, with removal to a more restrictive setting justified only after documenting that supplementary aids and services in the less restrictive setting were tried and insufficient.
**In use:** "LRE means we document what we tried in the general-ed classroom before this placement conversation, not just that general ed 'wasn't working.'"
**Common misuse:** Treating LRE as a preference to weigh against convenience rather than a legal presumption that shifts the burden onto justifying a more restrictive placement.

### Curriculum-based measurement (CBM)
Brief, standardized, repeatable probes (e.g., one-minute oral reading fluency) used to track a student's progress toward a goal at a set frequency, generating the data points used for both instructional decisions and eligibility determinations.
**In use:** "CBM shows a flat trend over the last three data points — time to revisit the intervention before the next scheduled review."
**Common misuse:** Using a single CBM score as if it were a stable trait measure instead of one data point in a trend that needs multiple points to interpret.

### Multidisciplinary team (MDT)
The group — school psychologist, special education teacher, general education teacher, parent, and relevant specialists — that collectively makes the eligibility determination; IDEA requires a team decision, not a psychologist's unilateral call.
**In use:** "The MDT reviewed the data together and reached the eligibility conclusion — it's not my call alone to make."
**Common misuse:** Presenting testing results as a predetermined conclusion rather than data for the team to weigh collectively.

### PREPaRE model
NASP's crisis-prevention and -response curriculum (Prevent/Prepare, Reaffirm physical health and safety, Evaluate psychological trauma risk, Provide/Respond, Examine effectiveness) used to structure school crisis triage and response.
**In use:** "We're following PREPaRE — right now we're in the Evaluate step, triaging who needs individual follow-up."
**Common misuse:** Jumping straight to individual clinical intervention in the acute window instead of first triaging exposure and risk level across the affected group.

### Specific Learning Disability (SLD)
An IDEA eligibility category for a disorder in one or more basic psychological processes involved in understanding or using language, manifesting as an imperfect ability to listen, think, speak, read, write, spell, or do mathematical calculations — determined via discrepancy, RTI, or Patterns of Strengths and Weaknesses models depending on state.
**In use:** "This is an SLD-in-reading eligibility question, not an intellectual disability question — the discrepancy is specific to one domain."
**Common misuse:** Treating SLD as a single universal criterion set, when the actual eligibility model (discrepancy vs. RTI vs. PSW) varies by state and changes what evidence is required.
