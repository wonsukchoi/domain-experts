# Vocabulary

### S-twist and Z-twist
The two possible directions of twist in a yarn, named for the visual direction the twist follows compared to the middle stroke of the letters S or Z, whose combination when plying multiple yarns together determines ply stability.
**In use:** "Both singles need to be Z-twist for this construction, with the ply twist going S-direction — that's what makes it a stable, balanced yarn."
**Common misuse:** Assuming any twist direction combination will produce a stable ply — standard balanced construction requires a specific relationship (typically same-direction singles with opposite-direction ply twist), and deviating from it produces an unstable yarn that can actively untwist under tension.

### Twist liveliness (ply stability)
A yarn's tendency to untwist, kink, or snarl when tension is released, used as a diagnostic test for whether a ply's twist direction combination is actually stable and balanced.
**In use:** "Released tension on the sample and checked for kinking — that's the twist liveliness test confirming whether this ply combination is actually stable."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a ply yarn is correctly constructed just because it holds together under tension during processing — twist instability often only becomes apparent when tension is released, which is why a specific liveliness test (not just processing behavior) is the actual verification method.

### Relative tension consistency (parallel ends)
The degree of tension uniformity between multiple yarn ends being wound or twisted together simultaneously, distinct from each individual end's tension measured against a general acceptable range.
**In use:** "Each end reads within our general tension range individually, but they're not consistent relative to each other — that's still a real defect."
**Common misuse:** Verifying only that each parallel end's tension falls within an acceptable standalone range, without checking consistency between the ends — a yarn made from ends with different (but each individually acceptable) tensions still ends up with an inconsistent result, since relative variance between ends matters independently of each one's absolute value.

### Package build
The physical characteristics (density, traverse pattern) of how yarn is wound onto a package, which determine whether it will unwind cleanly at the next processing stage.
**In use:** "Package build's inconsistent in this section — that's going to cause snagging when this unwinds at the next machine."
**Common misuse:** Treating package build quality as only relevant to storage or handling at the current stage — build quality is actually a functional requirement for the next process stage's ability to unwind the yarn cleanly.

### Draw ratio
The ratio of output speed to input speed in a fiber drawing/drafting process, determining how much a fiber strand is thinned or attenuated, which must remain consistent along the strand's length.
**In use:** "Average draw ratio's on target, but we're seeing variation along the strand — that's producing slubs even though the overall number looks right."
**Common misuse:** Confirming only that the average draw ratio matches the target specification, without checking for localized variation along the strand's length — an average-correct draw ratio can still coexist with significant thick/thin spots if the ratio fluctuates during processing.

### Slub / thin spot (yarn defect)
An inconsistency in yarn diameter along its length — a thicker section (slub) or a thinner section — typically caused by draw ratio variation or uneven fiber feeding during the drawing/drafting process.
**In use:** "That's a slub, not a fiber quality issue — traces back to a momentary draw ratio fluctuation during processing."
**Common misuse:** Attributing diameter inconsistencies to raw fiber quality without checking process consistency (draw ratio, tension) during the drawing/drafting stage — process-driven diameter variation is a common, distinct cause from raw material quality issues.

### Plying
The process of twisting two or more singles yarns together to form a multi-ply yarn, requiring correct twist direction combination between the singles and the plying twist for a stable result.
**In use:** "Plying two Z-twist singles with an S-direction ply twist — that specific combination is what gives us a balanced, non-lively yarn."
**Common misuse:** Treating plying as simply "twisting yarns together" without regard to the specific twist direction relationship required — the combination itself determines whether the resulting yarn is stable or prone to untwisting.

### Yarn evenness testing
Instrumented measurement of yarn diameter consistency along its length, used to detect slubs, thin spots, and other diameter irregularities beyond what visual inspection alone would reliably catch.
**In use:** "Evenness tester picked up variation we wouldn't have caught by eye — that's what confirms draw ratio consistency, not just a visual pass."
**Common misuse:** Relying on visual inspection alone to judge yarn diameter consistency — subtle but functionally significant diameter variation can be difficult to detect visually and requires instrumented testing to reliably confirm.

### Traverse pattern
The path a yarn guide follows as it winds yarn onto a package, determining how evenly and consistently the yarn layers build up, directly affecting downstream unwinding behavior.
**In use:** "Traverse pattern's drifted on this package — that inconsistency is what's going to cause tangling when it unwinds at the next station."
**Common misuse:** Treating traverse pattern as a minor mechanical detail rather than a direct determinant of downstream processability — an inconsistent traverse pattern produces a package that looks acceptable but doesn't unwind cleanly.
