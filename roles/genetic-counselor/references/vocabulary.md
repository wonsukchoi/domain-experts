# Genetic Counselor — Vocabulary

### Consultand
The person being counseled or evaluated, regardless of whether they are the one with symptoms.
**In use:** "The consultand herself is unaffected, but her prior risk is driven by her mother's obligate-carrier status."
**Common misuse:** Used interchangeably with "proband" — the proband is the first affected family member who brought the condition to clinical attention; the consultand is whoever is currently being counseled, and they're often not the same person.

### Obligate carrier
A person who, given the pedigree structure and inheritance pattern, must carry the variant with probability 1 — not estimated, deduced.
**In use:** "Her mother is an obligate carrier because two of her sons are affected and no other explanation fits an X-linked recessive pattern."
**Common misuse:** Treating obligate-carrier status as a strong-but-uncertain estimate rather than a logical certainty that anchors the rest of the calculation.

### Prior probability (pedigree-based)
The risk estimate derived purely from family structure and inheritance pattern, before any test result or additional observed evidence is incorporated.
**In use:** "Her prior risk was 50% based on her mother's carrier status alone, before we factored in her three unaffected sons."
**Common misuse:** Confusing the prior with the final answer, or skipping straight to a posterior without stating what prior it started from.

### Posterior probability
The updated risk estimate after conditional evidence (test results, unaffected relatives, biomarkers) has been incorporated via Bayes' theorem.
**In use:** "The posterior carrier risk dropped from 50% to about 11% after accounting for her three unaffected sons."
**Common misuse:** Reporting the posterior as if it were a fresh, standalone measurement rather than a mathematically derived update to the prior.

### Penetrance
The probability that a person carrying a pathogenic variant will actually develop the associated condition, often age-dependent.
**In use:** "This variant has about 70% penetrance by age 60, so being unaffected at 45 is real but incomplete reassurance."
**Common misuse:** Treating penetrance as a fixed, all-ages number rather than a curve that changes what "unaffected so far" means at different ages.

### Residual risk
The risk that remains after a negative test result, accounting for the test's imperfect sensitivity or scope.
**In use:** "A negative panel doesn't take her risk to zero — the residual risk, given the panel's detection rate, is still meaningful."
**Common misuse:** Equating "negative result" with "risk eliminated," especially when no known familial variant was being specifically ruled out.

### Non-directive counseling
A practice discipline of providing full information and naming tradeoffs without steering the consultand toward a particular decision.
**In use:** "Being non-directive doesn't mean refusing to discuss the tradeoffs — it means not picking the outcome for her."
**Common misuse:** Interpreted as withholding professional judgment or declining to engage substantively when asked a direct question.

### Variant of uncertain significance (VUS)
A genetic finding that current evidence cannot confidently classify as either pathogenic or benign.
**In use:** "This is a VUS — it's not yet strong enough evidence in either direction to change her surgical decision."
**Common misuse:** Treated by patients or clinicians as "probably pathogenic" (driving unwarranted intervention) or "probably benign" (driving false reassurance), when it means neither.

### Cascade testing
Testing offered to at-risk biological relatives of someone found to carry a pathogenic variant, working outward through the family.
**In use:** "Once her result came back positive, we discussed a cascade-testing plan starting with her two sisters."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to happen automatically once a proband tests positive, when in practice it depends entirely on the proband's own disclosure to relatives.

### GINA (Genetic Information Nondiscrimination Act)
US federal law prohibiting genetic-information-based discrimination in health insurance and employment — does not cover life, disability, or long-term-care insurance.
**In use:** "GINA protects her health coverage, but I want to flag it doesn't extend to the life insurance policy she's shopping for."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to be comprehensive genetic-discrimination protection across all insurance types, leading to under-informed testing decisions.

### Natural-frequency framing
Expressing a probability as a frequency ("1 in 9") rather than a percentage ("11%"), which most people reason about more accurately.
**In use:** "I switched from '11% carrier risk' to 'about 1 in 9' once it was clear she was tracking the frequency framing better."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a cosmetic communication choice rather than a substantive accuracy tool — the two framings carry identical information but produce different comprehension rates.

### Trio vs. duo sequencing
Sequencing that includes both biological parents plus the patient (trio) versus only one parent (duo), which affects confidence in de novo variant calls.
**In use:** "This was duo sequencing, so we can't confirm de novo status the way we could with a trio."
**Common misuse:** De novo status from duo sequencing treated with the same confidence as trio-confirmed de novo, missing the case where the untested parent is actually the carrier.

### Actionability
Whether a genetic finding, if confirmed, would change clinical management — surveillance, prophylactic intervention, or reproductive planning.
**In use:** "The variant is Likely Pathogenic and actionable — it changes her surveillance schedule starting now."
**Common misuse:** Conflated with "clinically significant" in a general sense; a Pathogenic classification for a condition with no available intervention is significant but may not be actionable in the surveillance/treatment sense.
