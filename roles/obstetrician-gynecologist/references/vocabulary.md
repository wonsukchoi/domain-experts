# Obstetrician-Gynecologist — Vocabulary

### Gravida/Para (G/P notation, e.g., G3P1021)
Shorthand for pregnancy history: gravida = total pregnancies including current; para is usually expanded as term/preterm/abortions/living children.
**In use:** "She's a G3P1021 — one prior term delivery, no preterm, one loss, one living child, this is pregnancy number three."
**Common misuse:** Treating "para" as a single number equal to live births; a patient with two miscarriages and one living child from a twin delivery is not "para 1" in the expanded notation, and glossing over the digits loses obstetric history that changes risk stratification.

### TOLAC vs. VBAC
TOLAC (trial of labor after cesarean) is the attempt; VBAC (vaginal birth after cesarean) is the successful outcome of that attempt.
**In use:** "She's a good TOLAC candidate; whether it ends as a VBAC or a repeat cesarean, we won't know until labor plays out."
**Common misuse:** Using the terms interchangeably in counseling, which implies a guaranteed outcome ("we're doing a VBAC") before labor has even started — patients need to consent to the trial, not a promised result.

### Risk of Malignancy Index (RMI)
A composite score (ultrasound feature score × menopausal status × CA-125) used to triage adnexal masses for referral to gynecologic oncology.
**In use:** "Her RMI comes out to 1,890 — that's well above the 200 threshold, so this goes to gyn-onc, not a general OR list."
**Common misuse:** Treating an elevated CA-125 alone as sufficient to refer or reassure — CA-125 is one of three multiplied factors, and a high value with a benign-scoring ultrasound in a premenopausal patient (where fibroids and endometriosis routinely elevate it) can still yield a low RMI.

### Cervical insufficiency (formerly "incompetent cervix")
Painless cervical dilation in the second trimester leading to pregnancy loss or extreme prematurity, absent contractions or labor.
**In use:** "Her history of a painless second-trimester loss at 20 weeks is consistent with cervical insufficiency — she's a cerclage candidate this pregnancy."
**Common misuse:** Applying the diagnosis to any preterm birth or dilation found in labor; the defining feature is the absence of contractions, not simply early dilation.

### Endometrial hyperplasia with atypia (now often termed EIN — endometrial intraepithelial neoplasia)
A precancerous endometrial lesion with meaningfully higher progression risk to carcinoma than hyperplasia without atypia.
**In use:** "This is EIN, not simple hyperplasia — she needs a hysterectomy discussion, not just progestin therapy, given her age and fertility completion."
**Common misuse:** Lumping all "hyperplasia" results together in counseling; the atypia/EIN distinction is the single biggest driver of whether medical management or surgery is the right next step.

### PPROM vs. PROM
PROM is rupture of membranes before labor onset at term; PPROM is the same event before 37 weeks.
**In use:** "This is PPROM at 32 weeks — the management is latency antibiotics and steroids, completely different from term PROM management."
**Common misuse:** Using "PROM" generically to describe any ruptured membranes regardless of gestational age, which erases the distinction that actually drives the management plan (expectant latency management vs. expedited delivery).

### Bishop score
A 0-13 point cervical-favorability score (dilation, effacement, station, consistency, position) used to select an induction method.
**In use:** "Bishop is 4 — starting with a ripening agent before oxytocin, not going straight to Pitocin."
**Common misuse:** Using the Bishop score as a gate on whether induction is offered at all, rather than what it actually determines — the method chosen to induce, not whether induction is appropriate.

### FIGO stage
A surgical-pathologic staging system (not purely clinical) for gynecologic cancers, updated periodically — endometrial cancer staging was substantially revised in 2023 to incorporate histology, LVSI, and molecular subtype.
**In use:** "This is FIGO Stage IAmPOLEmut under the 2023 system — the molecular subtype actually downstages what would otherwise look higher-risk on anatomy alone."
**Common misuse:** Quoting a FIGO stage from imaging or exam alone before surgical staging is complete, or applying pre-2023 stage definitions without checking whether the molecular modifier changes the assigned stage.

### Discriminatory zone (hCG)
The quantitative hCG threshold above which an intrauterine pregnancy should be visible on transvaginal ultrasound if one exists.
**In use:** "hCG is well above the discriminatory zone with no IUP seen — this is an ectopic until proven otherwise."
**Common misuse:** Treating the discriminatory zone as a fixed universal number rather than a threshold that varies somewhat by equipment and operator, and using a single hCG value instead of a serial trend to make the call in an ambiguous early pregnancy.

### Carpenter-Coustan criteria
The glucose thresholds (fasting ≥95, 1h ≥180, 2h ≥155, 3h ≥140 mg/dL) applied to the 100g 3-hour OGTT to diagnose gestational diabetes.
**In use:** "Two of four values are abnormal by Carpenter-Coustan — that's a GDM diagnosis, not borderline."
**Common misuse:** Diagnosing GDM off a single abnormal value on the 3-hour test, or confusing the Carpenter-Coustan thresholds with the different National Diabetes Data Group cutoffs, which changes who gets labeled GDM.

### Adnexal mass
A mass arising from the ovary, fallopian tube, or surrounding tissue — deliberately nonspecific until characterized.
**In use:** "We have an adnexal mass on imaging; the RMI and morphology will tell us whether this stays with general OB-GYN or goes to oncology."
**Common misuse:** Using "adnexal mass" and "ovarian cyst" interchangeably in patient communication before imaging has actually localized and characterized it — a tubo-ovarian abscess and a corpus luteum cyst are both "adnexal masses" with entirely different management.

### Climacteric
The multi-year physiologic transition encompassing perimenopause through early postmenopause, broader than menopause itself (the single point of final menstrual period).
**In use:** "She's climacteric, not yet twelve months amenorrheic — we treat the vasomotor symptoms now without waiting for the menopause label to apply."
**Common misuse:** Using "menopause" to describe the whole transition, which leads to withholding symptom treatment until the retrospective 12-month diagnosis is met, when the climacteric symptoms warranting treatment often start years earlier.

### Nulligravid vs. nulliparous
Nulligravid means never pregnant; nulliparous means never carried a pregnancy past the point of viability (a patient can be gravid but still nulliparous after a first-trimester loss).
**In use:** "She's gravida 2, para 0 — two pregnancies, both losses before viability, so she's nulliparous but not nulligravid."
**Common misuse:** Using the terms interchangeably in a chart or consult, which erases a distinction that matters directly for obstetric risk counseling (a nulliparous patient with a prior loss carries a different history than a truly nulligravid one).
