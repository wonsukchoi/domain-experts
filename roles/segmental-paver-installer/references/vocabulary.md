# Vocabulary

### Interlock
The mechanical resistance to lateral and vertical unit movement created by paver shape, pattern, joint sand, and edge confinement working together — not any single element alone.
**In use:** "We lost interlock on that curve because the restraint spacing doubled from 12 to 24 inches — the pattern alone can't hold it."
**Common misuse:** Treating "interlocking pavers" as a product name that guarantees interlock regardless of installation — the unit shape only enables interlock; base, bedding, joint sand, and edge restraint have to deliver it.

### Confinement
The lateral restraint (edge restraint plus surrounding soil/hardscape) that keeps the paver field from spreading under load over time.
**In use:** "This border needs steel edge restraint, not plastic — it's taking direct wheel loads on every turn."
**Common misuse:** Assuming any edge restraint product is interchangeable regardless of load — plastic restraint is adequate for pedestrian areas but under-specs for driveway turning radii.

### Proof-roll
A field check where a loaded vehicle or wheelbarrow is driven/rolled over the finished base to visually confirm no rutting, pumping, or deflection before the next layer goes down.
**In use:** "Base failed the proof-roll on the north end — we've got a soft spot, not just a compaction miss."
**Common misuse:** Skipping it because "the compactor ran over it enough times" — pass count doesn't substitute for a load check, especially over variable subgrade.

### CBR (California Bearing Ratio)
A standardized measure of subgrade strength used to size base thickness; expressed as a percentage, lower means weaker soil requiring more base.
**In use:** "This clay's testing around CBR 3 — we're going to 8 inches of base, not the 4 we'd use on sand."
**Common misuse:** Guessing subgrade strength by soil appearance alone and skipping any test or reference-table check, then discovering the mismatch only after rutting appears.

### Screed rails
Temporary guides (pipe or aluminum rail) set at the target bedding-sand depth, used to drag a straight edge across and produce a uniform sand layer before laying.
**In use:** "Pull the rails once you've screeded this bay — don't walk the sand before we set units."
**Common misuse:** Screeding freehand "by eye" on a small job to save setup time, producing an uneven bed depth that shows up as a wavy finished surface.

### Working the joint
The process of vibrating/compacting after joint sand is swept in, so the sand fully settles to the base of the joint rather than sitting loose at the surface.
**In use:** "Run the plate again after sweeping — that joint sand hasn't worked all the way down yet."
**Common misuse:** Treating sweeping alone as sufficient and skipping the post-sweep compaction pass, leaving joints under-filled and prone to washout.

### Soldier course
A border row of pavers set perpendicular to the field pattern's running direction, typically laid first to establish a clean, straight edge line.
**In use:** "Set the soldier course to the string line before you start the herringbone field — everything keys off that edge."
**Common misuse:** Laying the field pattern first "because it's faster" and fitting the border last, which produces inconsistent cut widths along the edge.

### Segmental (as in segmental pavement)
Describes any paving system built from discrete, individually placed units (pavers, brick, block) rather than a continuous poured/monolithic surface — the defining trait is that each unit can move slightly and independently, which is a feature, not a defect.
**In use:** "It's a segmental system — a little unit-to-unit movement under load is normal and part of how it distributes stress."
**Common misuse:** Applying monolithic-concrete thinking (expecting zero movement, treating any hairline joint shift as a defect) to a system designed around distributed, independent unit movement.

### Bedding sand
The 1" leveling layer of coarse concrete sand between the compacted base and the paver units — never compacted before laying, only after, together with the units.
**In use:** "Don't run the plate over the bedding sand before we lay — that defeats the whole point of the layer."
**Common misuse:** Confusing bedding sand with joint sand (they're different products with different gradations and different jobs) or compacting it prematurely.

### Geotextile (separation fabric)
A woven or non-woven synthetic fabric placed between a weak/fine-grained subgrade and the base aggregate to prevent fines from migrating (pumping) up into base voids under load.
**In use:** "Clay subgrade here — we're adding geotextile under the base, or we'll get pumping within a season."
**Common misuse:** Treating it as optional on any subgrade, or substituting it for adequate base thickness rather than using it alongside the thickness spec.

### Polymeric sand
A joint-filling sand blended with a polymer binder that hardens when activated (typically by water) to resist washout and weed growth, distinct from plain sand used for bedding.
**In use:** "Confirm this bag's activation method before we mist it — some formulations haze if you overwater."
**Common misuse:** Assuming all brands activate and cure identically, or using it in a permeable-pavement joint where an open-graded chip is required instead.

### Efflorescence
A white, powdery mineral deposit on the paver face caused by soluble salts leaching to the surface as moisture evaporates — a cosmetic condition, not a structural defect.
**In use:** "That haze is efflorescence, it'll weather off over a season or two — it's not a sign the base failed."
**Common misuse:** Treating any white surface discoloration as a structural or install defect without checking whether it's efflorescence, which resolves on its own with weathering and traffic.
