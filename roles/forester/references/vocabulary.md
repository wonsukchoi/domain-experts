# Forester — Vocabulary

### Basal area
The cross-sectional area of a tree's stem at breast height (4.5 ft), summed across trees and expressed per acre (sq ft/ac) as the standard stocking/density metric.
**In use:** "This stand's running 140 square feet of basal area — that's above the upper stocking line, it needs a thinning."
**Common misuse:** Treating stems-per-acre as an equivalent density measure; two stands with the same tree count can have very different basal area depending on diameter distribution.

### Site index
The expected height of dominant/codominant trees at a reference age (commonly 25 or 50 years, species-dependent) — the standard measure of a site's growth potential, and the lookup key for yield-table projections.
**In use:** "Site index 70 base age 25 for loblolly — that puts this tract in the upper productivity range for the region."
**Common misuse:** Measuring it from a suppressed, forked, or damaged tree instead of a dominant/codominant, undamaged stem, which understates or overstates true site quality.

### Mean annual increment (MAI)
Total stand volume divided by stand age — the average annual growth rate since establishment.
**In use:** "MAI's still climbing at age 28, so we haven't hit biological rotation age yet."
**Common misuse:** Confusing MAI with current growth rate; MAI is a cumulative average, not this year's increment (that's PAI).

### Periodic annual increment (PAI)
The volume growth rate over a recent, specific period (e.g., the last 5 years) — the stand's current growth rate, as opposed to MAI's lifetime average.
**In use:** "PAI dropped below MAI last measurement — that's the culmination point, biological rotation age is here."
**Common misuse:** Reading a single PAI measurement as decisive; a temporary drought or damage year can depress PAI without the stand having truly reached culmination.

### Land Expectation Value (LEV) / Faustmann formula
The present value of an infinite series of identical future rotations on the same land, used to find the financially optimal rotation age and to value bare forestland.
**In use:** "LEV peaks at age 25 for this site and discount rate — waiting to 32 for more volume is actually losing money in present-value terms."
**Common misuse:** Presenting a rotation-age recommendation as "optimal" without disclosing which discount rate and stumpage price were assumed — LEV is highly sensitive to both.

### Basal area factor (BAF) / prism cruising
A fixed multiplier used in variable-radius point sampling: the surveyor counts trees that appear larger than the prism's angle ("in" trees) at each point, and in-count × BAF gives basal area per acre at that point.
**In use:** "We ran a BAF-10 prism — mean in-count was 12 trees per point, so basal area's 120 square feet an acre."
**Common misuse:** Using one BAF across a stand with widely varying density, which over- or under-samples different density strata and inflates total error.

### Stumpage
The price paid for standing timber, before harvest, transport, or processing costs — the value a landowner actually receives, as opposed to delivered or mill-gate log prices.
**In use:** "Stumpage on this sawtimber is running $310 a thousand board feet — that's the number that goes in the landowner's check."
**Common misuse:** Quoting a delivered or mill-gate price as if it were stumpage; the gap between them is harvest, transport, and mill margin, and it's substantial.

### Log rule (Doyle, Scribner, International 1/4-inch)
A formula converting a log's diameter and length into an estimated board-foot volume; different rules give different (sometimes 20%+ different) answers for the same log.
**In use:** "Cruise was compiled on Doyle — the mill scales on International, so don't expect the tallies to match without a conversion."
**Common misuse:** Comparing a cruise volume to a mill scale (or to another cruise) without confirming both used the same log rule.

### Silvicultural system
The planned sequence of harvest and regeneration treatments (clearcut, shelterwood, selection) that determines how a stand regenerates and what age/species structure results.
**In use:** "We're running a shelterwood here — leave 40% canopy until the oak regen's established, then remove the overstory."
**Common misuse:** Choosing a system for its visual/aesthetic effect on the landowner rather than matching it to the regeneration species' shade tolerance.

### Shade tolerance
A species' ability to establish and grow under a forest canopy, ranging from intolerant (needs full sun) to tolerant (thrives in shade) — the primary biological driver of silvicultural-system choice.
**In use:** "Loblolly's shade-intolerant — clearcut it and it regenerates fine; shade it with a shelterwood too long and you lose the pine to hardwood competition."
**Common misuse:** Assuming shade tolerance is a fixed trait independent of site and competition, when it interacts with soil moisture and nutrient availability.

### Stocking guide
A chart, specific to a species and region, showing the basal-area-per-acre range (lower to upper stocking line) associated with full site occupancy without over- or under-crowding.
**In use:** "We're sitting right on the upper stocking line — one more growing season and we're into competition-driven mortality."
**Common misuse:** Applying a stocking guide built for one species or region to a different species/site combination.

### Regeneration survey
A post-harvest field check, typically at 1-5 years, counting established seedlings per acre against a minimum stocking threshold to confirm the stand is regenerating successfully.
**In use:** "Regen survey at year 3 came back under the 400 stems-per-acre threshold — we've got a competing-vegetation problem, not a seed-source problem."
**Common misuse:** Treating a regeneration survey as optional paperwork rather than the check that determines whether site prep or interplanting is needed before the window to fix it closes.

### Rotation age (biological vs. financial)
The age at which a stand is harvested; biological rotation maximizes total wood volume grown (MAI culmination), financial rotation maximizes discounted value (LEV), and the two are rarely the same age.
**In use:** "Biological rotation's out around 33 for this site, but financial rotation's already passed at 25 — client needs to decide which one they're optimizing for."
**Common misuse:** Using "rotation age" without specifying which definition, letting the listener assume whichever one matches their own priority.

### Thinning (from below / crop-tree)
An intermediate harvest removing selected trees to reduce competition on the remaining stand; "from below" removes the smallest/suppressed trees, "crop-tree" selects and releases specific high-value final-crop trees.
**In use:** "This is a crop-tree thin — we're marking and releasing the 60 best-formed stems per acre, not just pulling the smallest trees."
**Common misuse:** Specifying a thinning by tree count or percentage removed instead of a residual basal-area target, which leaves the marking crew no enforceable density standard.
