# Court Reporter — Vocabulary

### Realtime
Live steno-to-text translation delivered to a screen (judge's bench, CART display, connected laptops) as testimony happens, using a case-specific CAT dictionary to resolve steno strokes into words.
**In use:** "We're running realtime for the judge on this one, so the dictionary needs the expert witnesses' names loaded before we start."
**Common misuse:** Treated as interchangeable with "the transcript" — realtime is a live, uncorrected feed; the transcript is the proofread, certified deliverable produced afterward. Realtime accuracy and transcript accuracy are scored differently and serve different purposes.

### CART (Communication Access Realtime Translation)
Realtime captioning delivered specifically for a deaf or hard-of-hearing participant's access needs, distinct from a courtroom realtime feed serving the judge or attorneys.
**In use:** "This is a CART assignment for a hard-of-hearing juror, not a standard realtime feed — the display and pacing requirements are different."
**Common misuse:** Conflated with general courtroom realtime — CART has its own accuracy and delivery conventions tied to accessibility law (ADA), not just litigation-support practice.

### Untranslated / brief conflict
A steno stroke combination that the CAT dictionary doesn't resolve to a single confident word, rendering as a raw phonetic outline in the feed or draft instead of readable text.
**In use:** "That's an untranslate on the witness's maiden name — flagging it for the dictionary rather than guessing."
**Common misuse:** Treated as equivalent in risk to a substantive (resolved-but-wrong) error. An untranslate is self-flagging and low-risk; a wrong word that reads as valid text is the higher-risk category.

### Substantive vs. non-substantive error
The QC-triage distinction between a transcript discrepancy that changes meaning (wrong word, omission that reverses or alters testimony) and one that's visibly broken and doesn't (an untranslated outline).
**In use:** "We're at zero substantive errors and forty-seven non-substantive — certify it, and I'll clear the non-substantive backlog into the dictionary."
**Common misuse:** Reported as a single blended accuracy percentage instead of two separate counts — a high blended score can mask a small number of meaning-changing errors that are the only ones that actually matter for certification.

### Errata sheet
A post-transcript document under FRCP 30(e) or a state analog where a deponent proposes changes to their deposition testimony, with a stated reason, appended to (not replacing) the original transcript.
**In use:** "The errata sheet reverses her answer on question 14 — that's going to counsel before I process it, not straight into the file."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to overwrite the original answer. It doesn't — both the original testimony and the correction remain part of the record, and courts weigh both.

### Read-back
A verbatim recitation of previously recorded testimony from the steno notes, delivered on request from the bench or an attorney during a live proceeding.
**In use:** "Judge asked for a read-back of the last answer — pulling it from the notes, not from memory."
**Common misuse:** Treated as an opportunity to summarize or clean up what was said. A read-back's value is that it's exactly what the notes show, including hesitations or incomplete sentences.

### Steno conflict
Two or more different words or phrases that stroke identically on the steno machine, requiring the CAT dictionary or the reporter's real-time judgment to disambiguate by context.
**In use:** "'Tire' and 'tier' are a steno conflict in this theory — I'm resolving by context each time until I decide which one to default in the case dictionary."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to be a rare edge case rather than a routine, ongoing part of the craft — every steno theory has known conflicts, and managing them is continuous, not a one-time setup problem.

### Certification statement
The signed attestation, appended to a completed transcript, that it is a true and accurate record of the proceeding — the formal gate marking the transcript as complete and usable in the case.
**In use:** "Substantive count is at zero and the audio cross-check is done — I can sign the certification today."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a formality added after the fact rather than a professional and, in most jurisdictions, legal attestation with consequences for inaccuracy — reporters can face sanctions or licensure action for a knowingly false certification.

### Backup audio
An independent digital audio recording of the proceeding, synced to the steno notes by timestamp, retained as a second, unedited record used to resolve contested or ambiguous passages.
**In use:** "The notes are unclear on that phrase — pulling the backup audio at the 19-minute mark to confirm before I correct it."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a backup in the sense of "rarely needed." In practice it's used routinely during QC passes on any transcript with substantive-error risk, not just as a last resort.

### CAT (Computer-Aided Transcription) dictionary
The reporter's personal or case-specific database mapping steno strokes to words, built and maintained continuously — the mechanism that resolves most strokes automatically and flags the rest as conflicts.
**In use:** "I loaded all the expert witnesses' names into the case dictionary before the deposition so they wouldn't come through as untranslates."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to be a fixed, one-time setup. An unmaintained dictionary is the single largest driver of a rising untranslated rate over time, independent of the reporter's underlying skill.

### RPR (Registered Professional Reporter)
NCRA's baseline national certification, requiring passage of a three-speed skills exam (Literary, Jury Charge, Q&A/Testimony) at minimum accuracy thresholds, plus a written knowledge test.
**In use:** "She's RPR-certified, so she's cleared the national baseline speed and accuracy exam — CRR is a separate credential on top of that."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to be the same credential as state licensure. Many states require a separate CSR (Certified Shorthand Reporter) exam or license in addition to, or instead of, NCRA's national certifications.

### CRR (Certified Realtime Reporter)
A credential on top of RPR specifically for realtime delivery, requiring a higher accuracy threshold on a timed realtime test with no post-session editing allowed.
**In use:** "This assignment needs CRR, not just RPR — the judge wants a live feed, not just a post-session transcript."
**Common misuse:** Assumed automatically implied by RPR certification. RPR certifies general stenographic competency; CRR specifically certifies the ability to deliver an accurate uncorrected realtime feed, a distinct and harder skill.
