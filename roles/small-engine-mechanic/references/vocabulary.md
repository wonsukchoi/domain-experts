# Vocabulary

Terms generalists conflate or misuse. Each: definition, how a practitioner actually uses it, and the common misuse.

### Oil-mix ratio
The ratio of gasoline to 2-stroke oil (e.g., 50:1) that provides an engine's only lubrication, since 2-stroke designs have no separate crankcase oil sump the way a 4-stroke does.

**In use:** "This is a 50:1 saw — that's 2.6 oz of oil per gallon, not a capful, and not the same bottle rated for a different fuel quantity."

**Common misuse:** eyeballing a "one bottle per tank" approach without checking the bottle's rated ratio against the actual fuel volume mixed, producing an unintentional rich or lean mix.

### Diaphragm carburetor
A carburetor design, common on handheld 2-stroke tools, that uses a flexible fuel-pump diaphragm and a metering diaphragm/lever instead of a float bowl — allowing the tool to run in any orientation.

**In use:** "It's a diaphragm carb, so there's no float level to check — we're measuring metering lever height instead."

**Common misuse:** treating "float level" as a universal carburetor spec and attempting to apply it to a diaphragm carb, which has no float at all.

### Metering lever height
The diaphragm-carburetor spec analogous to float level: how far the metering lever sits relative to the carb body's gasket surface, controlling fuel delivery into the metering chamber.

**In use:** "Lever's sitting proud of the gasket surface — that's rich of spec, recheck it after reassembly."

**Common misuse:** skipping the check entirely on the assumption that diaphragm carbs don't need adjustment, when an out-of-spec lever height directly causes flooding or a lean bog.

### Float level
On float-type carburetors (common on 4-stroke Briggs & Stratton and Tecumseh engines), the fuel level maintained in the bowl, set by the float arm and measured against a factory spec with the carb inverted or with a gauge tool.

**In use:** "Float's set high — that's your fuel weeping from the vent at idle, not a jet problem."

**Common misuse:** re-jetting to chase a flooding or hesitation symptom that's actually caused by an incorrect float level or a worn needle/seat, which no jet size will fix.

### Idle circuit (fixed/idle jet)
The smallest fixed fuel-metering passage in a small-engine carburetor, controlling idle and low-speed fuel delivery — the first passage to clog with ethanol-fuel varnish.

**In use:** "Fuel looks fine but it won't idle after sitting all winter — check the idle jet before you assume something bigger."

**Common misuse:** assuming clear-looking fuel rules out varnish, when deposits can already be lodged in the passage before the bulk fuel itself shows any discoloration.

### Spark arrestor screen
A fine mesh screen inside the muffler, required on equipment used in off-road or forestry settings, that traps sparks but also traps carbon and can choke exhaust flow when clogged.

**In use:** "It's running hot and losing power under load — pull the spark arrestor screen and check it before you touch the carb again."

**Common misuse:** overlooking the screen as an exhaust-flow restriction and re-diagnosing the carburetor or ignition repeatedly for a symptom the screen is actually causing.

### Phase 2 / Phase 3 emissions standards
EPA small nonroad spark-ignition engine emissions rules — Phase 2 (finalized 1997, phased in through the 2000s) and Phase 3 (40 CFR Part 1054, effective 2011–2012) — that tightened HC+NOx limits and added evaporative controls, pushing manufacturers toward leaner stock carburetor calibration and catalytic mufflers.

**In use:** "This is a post-2011 unit — that catalytic muffler is a real failure point that didn't exist on the pre-Phase-3 version of the same engine."

**Common misuse:** assuming all small engines of a given displacement share the same tuning tolerances and failure points regardless of manufacture year.

### Ethanol phase separation
The process by which ethanol-blended gasoline absorbs ambient moisture and separates into an ethanol-water layer and a gasoline layer when stored too long, degrading combustion and corroding some fuel-system components.

**In use:** "This sat since October with E10 in the tank — check for a cloudy layer or separation before you assume it's just old gas."

**Common misuse:** treating all "old gas" complaints identically, when ethanol-blend fuel degrades and separates on a materially faster timeline than ethanol-free fuel.

### Two-stroke vs. four-stroke lubrication
Two-stroke engines mix oil directly into the fuel for lubrication, since they have no separate oil sump; four-stroke engines carry a dedicated oil reservoir and run on straight gasoline.

**In use:** "Don't mix oil into the fuel on this one — it's the 4-stroke mower, not the 2-stroke trimmer."

**Common misuse:** mixing 2-stroke oil into a 4-stroke engine's fuel tank, or running a 2-stroke on straight gas — producing fouling in one direction and catastrophic lubrication failure in the other.

### Seasonal parts stocking
The practice of ordering high-turnover parts (carb kits and belts for spring mower season; shear pins and scraper bars for winter snowblower season) 60–90 days ahead of the season's demand spike, rather than against the current bench's volume.

**In use:** "Order the mower carb kits and belts in January — by March the bench is already backed up and the distributor's back-ordered too."

**Common misuse:** stocking reactively to the current slow season's low bench volume, missing the lead time needed before the opposite season's rush.

### Shear pin
A deliberately weak pin connecting a snowblower auger to its drive shaft, designed to break before the auger gearbox or shaft does when the auger strikes a hidden obstacle.

**In use:** "Don't upgrade it to a hardened bolt — the shear pin is supposed to break instead of the gearbox."

**Common misuse:** replacing a broken shear pin with a standard hardware-store bolt of similar size, which can be stronger than spec and transfers the impact load to the gearbox instead of absorbing it.

### Compression release
A small mechanical valve on some small engines (chainsaws, some mowers) that bleeds cylinder pressure during starting to ease pull-cord effort, distinct from an actual low-compression fault.

**In use:** "That's the compression release doing its job on the pull — don't read a light pull-cord feel alone as low compression."

**Common misuse:** diagnosing a hard-starting complaint as a compression problem without first checking whether the equipment has a compression-release feature that's supposed to make the pull feel lighter.
