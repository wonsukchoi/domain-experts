# Red flags

Smell tests a roof bolter or foreman catches during a cut, a pull-test review, or a shift-change walk. Format for each: what it usually means, the first question to ask, and the data to pull before signing off.

## 1. Roof sounds hollow/drummy over more than a few feet of bar-sounding

**Usually means:** bed separation forming above the immediate roof layer — a parting the plan's CMRR and bolt length may not fully span at that specific station, even if adjacent rock has been solid all shift.

**First question:** "Where exactly does the hollow response start and stop, and has this stretch been pull-tested yet?"

**Data to pull:** station-to-station extent of the drummy response; most recent pull-test results for that heading; the plan's mapped CMRR and bolt length for this horizon.

## 2. Pull test or SEPT result below roughly 12 tons/ft of resin encapsulation despite the bolt clearing its minimum tension requirement

**Usually means:** the rock's actual anchorage capacity has fallen below the range the plan's CMRR assumed for this heading — a rock-mass signal, not a hardware defect, and one that a tension-only check misses entirely.

**First question:** "What's the tons-per-foot anchorage number, not just the raw pull load, and how does it compare to this horizon's typical range?"

**Data to pull:** pull-test load and encapsulation length (to compute tons/ft); the mine's historical anchorage range for that horizon; the plan's CMRR assumption for the affected heading.

## 3. Bolt spins freely, or torques up with unusually low resistance, after the resin's rated gel time has elapsed

**Usually means:** resin didn't fully mix — spin time exceeded gel time, cartridge was past shelf life, hole was wet, or ambient/hole temperature shortened gel time below what the crew planned for.

**First question:** "What cartridge type went in this hole, and was spin time actually tracked against its rated gel time for today's temperature?"

**Data to pull:** cartridge type and lot/expiration date; recorded or estimated spin time for that hole; ambient and hole temperature; any water observed during drilling.

## 4. ATRS outby edge positioned more than 5 ft from the last row of permanent support

**Usually means:** setback creep under time pressure — the crew treated a fixed geometric limit as adjustable for one cycle because the prior row "was basically right there."

**First question:** "What's the measured distance from the ATRS crossmember to the last bolted row, right now?"

**Data to pull:** a direct tape measurement of the setback; the shift's cycle log for a pattern of repeated creep versus a one-time lapse.

## 5. Methane reading at or above 1.0% at the face with electrical equipment still energized

**Usually means:** either the reading was missed at the required check point (before ATRS positioning, after a ventilation change) or equipment wasn't de-energized once the threshold was crossed — both are stop-work conditions, not a paperwork gap to close later.

**First question:** "When was methane last checked at this face, and what was the reading?"

**Data to pull:** methane monitor log/readout; ventilation survey for the section; timestamp of the last equipment de-energization action if any.

## 6. Visible slips, cutter roof, or a step at the rib line near a bolted heading

**Usually means:** stress concentration at the rib — often a precursor to roof or rib failure that a bolt pattern designed for the roof alone doesn't address; frequently shows up before a drummy sound does.

**First question:** "Is this new since the last shift's walk-through, or has it been logged and watched before?"

**Data to pull:** prior shift's ground-condition log for this entry; rib-bolt or rib-mesh plan status if the approved plan includes rib support; photos or measurements of the slip/step if tracked over time.

## 7. Pattern spacing loosened from the approved plan's spacing to make up cycle time

**Usually means:** schedule pressure overriding the plan without an authorized deviation — this is a violation regardless of whether the loosened pattern happens to hold that shift, because the plan's spacing is a floor set by engineering, not a target the crew can renegotiate informally.

**First question:** "Who authorized the pattern change, and is it documented against the plan section it modifies?"

**Data to pull:** the approved roof control plan's specified pattern for that heading; the shift's actual bolt spacing as installed; any deviation authorization on file.

## 8. Torque wrench or tension gauge readings erratic or inconsistent between consecutive holes in similar-looking rock

**Usually means:** either tool miscalibration or genuinely variable rock conditions being masked by "similar-looking" roof — both need to be ruled out before trusting any single reading from that tool that shift.

**First question:** "Has this wrench/gauge been checked against a known reference load today, and do the erratic readings track with any visible change in the rock?"

**Data to pull:** tool calibration log; a comparison of readings across holes plotted against station location.

## 9. Entry width exceeds 20 ft with only a standard bolt-only pattern installed

**Usually means:** the wide-opening requirement (combination bolts plus conventional support, 30 CFR 75.222(b)(3)) was missed, either because the opening's actual width wasn't measured against the plan's threshold or because an intersection/turnout was treated like a standard entry.

**First question:** "What's the measured width of this opening, and does the installed support match what the plan specifies above 20 ft?"

**Data to pull:** actual measured opening width; the approved plan's wide-opening support specification; installed support type and count for that opening.

## 10. Extended cut depth used in a heading with no prior CMRR history or pull-test data for that specific horizon

**Usually means:** the crew carried forward an extended-cut approval from an adjacent, previously mapped heading without confirming it applies to this one — extended cut is earned per-horizon by roof-fall history and CMRR, not inherited by proximity.

**First question:** "Has this specific heading been pull-tested or CMRR-mapped, or is the extended-cut approval coming from the panel next door?"

**Data to pull:** the plan's extended-cut authorization and the specific headings/horizons it covers; any pull-test or CMRR data collected for the current heading.

## 11. Respirable dust sampling on the bolter operator trending toward or above the 50 µg/m³ full-shift PEL

**Usually means:** drilling into higher-quartz strata than the section's dust-control plan assumed, water spray or dust collector on the bolting machine underperforming, or a ventilation deficiency at the face — roof bolter drilling is consistently one of the highest respirable-dust-generating tasks on a section.

**First question:** "What does the most recent personal sampling result show against the 50 µg/m³ PEL and 25 µg/m³ action level, and has the dust-control system (water pressure, collector, ventilation) been checked since?"

**Data to pull:** most recent respirable dust sampling results for that operator/position; water spray pressure and dust collector function log; ventilation quantity at the face.
