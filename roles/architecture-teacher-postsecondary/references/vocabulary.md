# Working Vocabulary — Terms Generalists Misuse

Format per term: definition, a sentence the way a practitioner actually uses it, and the common misuse to avoid.

---

**Desk crit**
An informal, one-on-one or small-group review of a student's work in progress at their desk or pin-up space, the primary vehicle for ongoing design feedback between formal reviews.
*Usage:* "The egress problem was flagged at the week 7 desk crit — it's in the log, this isn't a surprise at final review."
*Misuse:* treating desk crits as optional or purely conversational; skipping the log means a later grade or code flag has no documented trail, which is what makes a grade appeal defensible or not.

**Pin-up**
A review format where students post drawings, models, or boards on a wall for group discussion, typically less formal than a final jury but more structured than a desk crit.
*Usage:* "Bring the plan and one section to Thursday's pin-up — we're checking constraint response, not polish."
*Misuse:* treating a pin-up as a dress rehearsal for the final rendering rather than a working review of the plan and reasoning.

**Jury / final review**
A formal end-of-project critique, typically with outside licensed architects or faculty from other studios, evaluating the completed project against the brief's stated criteria.
*Usage:* "State the grading basis to the jury before they start — constraint response and reasoning first, formal resolution second — or they'll default to scoring on visual impact alone."
*Misuse:* assuming outside jurors share the studio's stated rubric weighting by default; without stating it, jurors commonly default to formal/visual impressions.

**Charrette**
An intensive, time-boxed design exercise (hours to a few days), used to generate rapid design alternatives rather than a fully resolved project.
*Usage:* "We ran a two-day charrette to generate massing options before committing to one for the semester-long development."
*Misuse:* grading a charrette output against the same resolution standard as a semester project — the format is explicitly for speed and volume of ideas, not finish.

**Program (the program)**
The stated functional and spatial requirements a design has to satisfy — room types, areas, adjacencies, occupancy — distinct from "curriculum" or "syllabus," which the word can be confused with outside the discipline.
*Usage:* "The program calls for a 1,400 sf reading room; if the design delivers 1,000 sf, that's a program-compliance issue, not a formal critique."
*Misuse:* treating program requirements as negotiable in the same way formal choices are — program area and adjacency requirements are typically as fixed as code, not open territory.

**Parti**
The central organizing idea or diagrammatic concept underlying a design — the core move the whole scheme is built around, usually expressible as a simple diagram or single sentence.
*Usage:* "The parti is 'light from the north, mass to the south' — every iteration since week 5 traces back to that."
*Misuse:* using "parti" to mean the finished design or the rendering; it refers specifically to the underlying organizing concept, which can and should survive multiple formal iterations.

**Common path of travel**
The portion of an egress path where occupants must travel before two independent means of egress become available — a specific code-defined distance limit, not a general description of "how far to the exit."
*Usage:* "Common path measured 95 ft against a 75 ft limit — that's a hard-stop redesign item, not a note."
*Misuse:* confusing common path of travel with total travel distance to an exit; they're measured differently and have different code limits, and conflating them produces a wrong compliance check.

**Occupant load**
The calculated maximum number of people a space is designed to hold, derived from net or gross floor area divided by a code-specified occupant-load factor for that use — not an estimate of expected attendance.
*Usage:* "Occupant load came out to 28 at the 50 sf/occupant factor — that's what determines whether one exit is enough, not how many people we expect to actually show up."
*Misuse:* treating occupant load as a soft planning estimate rather than the fixed number that egress-width and exit-count requirements are calculated from.

**Code minimum vs. design intent**
The distinction between the least a design must do to satisfy a code requirement, and the design decision made within or beyond that minimum — a critique conflating the two either treats the minimum as a design goal or treats a design choice as if it were code-mandated.
*Usage:* "The stair meets code minimum width; whether it should be wider for the building's actual traffic pattern is a design decision, not a compliance question."
*Misuse:* citing "code requires X" for a choice that was actually a stylistic or programmatic decision made above the code minimum, which misleads a student about what's actually fixed.

**Preliminary (schematic) vs. final constraint check**
The distinction between an early-phase check of a design's rough compliance with fixed requirements and the full check expected before a project is graded as complete — an unresolved item flagged at schematic phase is expected, the same item unresolved at final review is not.
*Usage:* "The mezzanine egress was flagged as open at the schematic check — it's a real finding now that it's still unresolved at final review."
*Misuse:* treating every open constraint flag the same regardless of which phase it surfaces in; an item open at schematic phase is a normal work-in-progress note, the same item open at final review is a hard-stop failure.
