# Red Flags — Smell Tests a Studio Instructor Catches Instantly

Each entry: what the smell usually means, the first question to ask, and what to actually pull. None are automatic verdicts — they're triggers for a specific check that routinely changes the response.

---

## 1. Jury scores cluster within a 5-point band across a formally diverse studio

**Usually means:** the rubric collapsed onto a single aesthetic-taste axis instead of scoring the stated separate criteria (concept reasoning, constraint response, formal resolution) independently — a diverse set of projects scoring nearly identically is a sign the differentiating criteria weren't actually applied.

**First question:** "If I only had the rubric's constraint-response column, would these scores still cluster this tightly?"

**Data to pull:** each juror's sub-scores by rubric component, not just the total, and whether the spread widens when the components are looked at separately.

---

## 2. A student's rendering quality rises each review while the iteration log goes empty

**Usually means:** the student has learned to perform at the final crit rather than develop and document design reasoning — or is producing renderings without an underlying revised plan, which a desk-crit record would have caught earlier.

**First question:** "Walk me through the last three iterations that led to this plan — not the render, the plan."

**Data to pull:** the desk-crit log for the weeks between reviews, and whether the plan actually changed in ways that trace to a stated design question.

---

## 3. No code citation appears on a final pin-up for 3+ consecutive studio projects

**Usually means:** code compliance is being taught as a checklist appended after the design is finished rather than integrated at the concept phase — a pattern that produces exactly the kind of late-discovered hard-stop failure seen when egress or occupant load isn't checked until final review.

**First question:** "At what week in the process was code checked, not just when it's shown on the board?"

**Data to pull:** the studio's milestone schedule, specifically whether a constraint-check milestone exists before final review or only at it.

---

## 4. A precedent set in a studio brief is 10+ years old with no currency note

**Usually means:** the brief was reused from a prior semester without updating the technical detail (glazing spec, material assembly, adopted code edition) behind the cited precedent, even if the formal/conceptual lesson still holds.

**First question:** "Which part of this precedent are we teaching — the concept, or the construction detail — and has the detail dated out?"

**Data to pull:** the precedent's original completion date against the current adopted code edition and any material/energy-code changes since.

---

## 5. An outside jury panel splits sharply on the same project (e.g., one juror fails it, another scores it near the top)

**Usually means:** one juror is scoring against the brief's stated fixed requirements (code, structural, program) and the other against personal formal/stylistic preference — treating the two scores as measuring the same thing and averaging them obscures which is which.

**First question:** "Which specific criterion is each juror's score actually tracking?"

**Data to pull:** each juror's written comments mapped against the brief's stated fixed-vs-open criteria list.

---

## 6. A student's design changes fundamentally between the last desk crit and final review with no logged reasoning

**Usually means:** either a late panic-driven overhaul with no time to test it, or contradictory desk-crit feedback that pushed the student to abandon a previously-approved direction without discussion.

**First question:** "What in the last desk crit prompted this change, and did we discuss it before you made it?"

**Data to pull:** the desk-crit log's last entry against the final submission, and whether the pivot is explained anywhere in writing.

---

## 7. A grade appeal cites "the professor doesn't like my style"

**Usually means:** either a legitimate taste-imposition happened and the critique record will show it, or the student is deflecting from an unaddressed functional or code flaw that was clearly named at the time — the written critique record settles which.

**First question:** "Pull up the critique note — did it name a specific flaw, or was it phrased as a preference?"

**Data to pull:** the written critique note or rubric comment from the review in question, checked for whether it named an objective criterion or a stated opinion.

---

## 8. A structural or mechanical consultant flags an issue at final review that wasn't raised at schematic phase

**Usually means:** the studio's constraint-checking milestone before final review was skipped, arrived too late in the term to allow redesign, or the consultant wasn't looped in until the final pin-up.

**First question:** "When was the consultant review scheduled relative to the schematic milestone, and did it happen on time?"

**Data to pull:** the studio's milestone calendar against the actual date of consultant review.

---

## 9. Desk-crit attendance or engagement drops sharply after one public critique

**Usually means:** the critique crossed from rigorous to demoralizing in its delivery, not necessarily in its content — a technically correct critique delivered without a stated next step can read as a verdict rather than feedback.

**First question:** "Did that critique pair the flaw with a specific next iteration step, or just name the problem?"

**Data to pull:** the critique note's language from that session, checked for whether it included an actionable next step alongside the named flaw.
