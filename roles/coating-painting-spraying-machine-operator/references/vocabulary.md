# Vocabulary

### Dry film thickness (DFT)
The actual thickness of a cured/dried coating layer, the functional specification that determines a coating's protective/performance properties.
**In use:** "DFT gauge reads 74 microns against a 75-micron target — that's the number that actually matters, not how it looked going on wet."
**Common misuse:** Judging coating adequacy by visual coverage or wet appearance rather than measuring actual DFT, or calculating it from WFT and percent volume solids, against the specification.

### Wet film thickness (WFT)
The thickness of a coating as applied, before solvent evaporation or cure, which has to be calculated from the target DFT and the coating's percent volume solids.
**In use:** "WFT gauge shows 150 microns going on — that's what's needed to hit 75 microns dry at this coating's solids content."
**Common misuse:** Applying wet film thickness numerically equal to the target DFT, without accounting for the volume lost to solvent evaporation.

### Percent volume solids
The percentage of a coating's wet volume that remains as solid film after solvent or water evaporates, used to calculate the wet film thickness needed to achieve a target dry film thickness.
**In use:** "This coating's 50% volume solids — half the wet film thickness ends up as final dry film, the rest evaporates."
**Common misuse:** Treating wet and dry film thickness as approximately equal, ignoring that percent volume solids, often well under 100%, determines how much of the applied wet coating actually remains as final dry film.

### Flash-off time
The time allowed for solvent evaporation between coats, or before a coating enters cure, necessary to prevent trapped solvent from causing defects.
**In use:** "Give it the full flash time before the next coat — cutting it short is how you get solvent popping later."
**Common misuse:** Shortening flash-off time to increase production throughput, trapping solvent in a way that causes defects only visible after cure, not at the time of the next coat.

### Transfer efficiency
The percentage of sprayed coating material that actually lands on and adheres to the part being coated, as opposed to overspray or waste.
**In use:** "Switching to HVLP bumped transfer efficiency up significantly — that's real material cost and VOC savings per part."
**Common misuse:** Treating spray application parameters as a matter of technique preference alone, without considering how they affect transfer efficiency's real material cost and emissions impact.

### HVLP (High Volume Low Pressure)
A spray application method using high air volume at low pressure, generally achieving higher transfer efficiency than conventional air spray.
**In use:** "HVLP's the standard here specifically for the transfer efficiency gain over conventional spray."
**Common misuse:** Assuming all spray application methods achieve similar transfer efficiency, when method choice materially affects material waste and emissions per part.

### Electrostatic coating
A spray application method that electrically charges coating particles so they're attracted to a grounded part, typically achieving very high transfer efficiency.
**In use:** "Electrostatic setup here still needs DFT verification — higher baseline efficiency doesn't mean the thickness check goes away."
**Common misuse:** Assuming electrostatic application eliminates the need to verify transfer efficiency or DFT, when it still requires the same verification even at a generally higher baseline efficiency.

### Cure schedule (coating)
The specified time-at-temperature profile a coating requires to fully crosslink or harden and achieve its final performance properties.
**In use:** "Cure schedule's 30 minutes at 350°F — confirmed the part itself held that, not just the oven."
**Common misuse:** Verifying cure completion by oven setpoint and elapsed time rather than confirming actual part temperature reached and held the required cure temperature for the specified duration.

### Surface profile (coating adhesion)
The measured roughness or texture created on a substrate surface, often via abrasive blasting, to promote mechanical adhesion of a coating.
**In use:** "Profile depth checks out per spec — that's what the coating's actually going to grip onto."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a visually clean surface has adequate profile for coating adhesion, without measuring actual surface roughness against the specification.

### Blistering / solvent popping (coating defect)
Coating defects caused by trapped solvent vapor expanding during cure, often from insufficient flash-off time between coats.
**In use:** "That's solvent popping — points back to a short flash time between coats, not a bad batch of paint."
**Common misuse:** Attributing a blistering defect to the coating material itself or the cure process alone, without checking whether inadequate flash-off time between prior coats was the actual root cause.
