# Vocabulary

### Consignment
An arrangement where the artist retains legal ownership of a work while a gallery holds and sells it on the artist's behalf, remitting an agreed split after sale.
**In use:** "The gallery has six pieces on consignment through March — none of that's a sale until they report it."
**Common misuse:** Generalists treat delivering work to a gallery as equivalent to selling it to the gallery; legally and financially they're opposite arrangements, and conflating them is why artists lose track of unpaid, unsold inventory.

### Sell-through rate
The percentage of exhibited works actually sold, used as the pricing signal instead of gross revenue.
**In use:** "We sold 12 of 14 — 86% sell-through — that's what justifies the increase, not the $96k total."
**Common misuse:** Treated as interchangeable with total revenue; a show that grosses more because prices were raised, not because more works sold, is not the same signal.

### VARA (Visual Artists Rights Act)
US federal moral-rights statute (17 U.S.C. §106A) giving attribution and integrity rights on single works or limited editions of 200 or fewer, for the artist's lifetime only.
**In use:** "The commission contract has a VARA waiver — I want to carve out at least the right of attribution before I sign."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to be an unwaivable, permanent right covering all artwork; it's narrow in scope, commonly and legally waived in writing, and doesn't survive the artist's death.

### Droit de suite / Artist's Resale Right (ARR)
A resale royalty paid to the artist (or estate) on secondary-market sales through an art market professional, standard in the UK/EU (sliding scale from 4% down to 0.25%, capped) but not established in current US federal law.
**In use:** "That resale went through a UK auction house, so ARR applies — my agent should be chasing that royalty."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to apply automatically to any resale anywhere, including US private sales, where no comparable federal right currently exists.

### Edition
A defined, numbered set of identical or near-identical works (prints, casts, photographs) produced from a single matrix or mold, with the edition size fixed and disclosed at the outset.
**In use:** "It's an edition of 50, numbered 4/50 — I can't print a 51st without breaking the edition's value proposition."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a soft cap that can be quietly exceeded if demand shows up later; doing so is considered fraud on collectors who bought based on the stated scarcity.

### Certificate of authenticity (COA)
A signed document accompanying a work (especially editions) attesting to its authorship, medium, edition number, and date.
**In use:** "Ship the COA with the print — a collector buying a $600 edition piece expects paperwork, not just the object."
**Common misuse:** Confused with a copyright transfer; a COA authenticates the object, it does not grant any reproduction or licensing rights.

### Primary market / secondary market
Primary market: work sold for the first time, typically through the artist or their gallery. Secondary market: resale of previously owned work, typically through auction houses or dealers, with the artist usually receiving no direct proceeds (absent ARR).
**In use:** "That auction result is a secondary-market price — it tells us demand exists, but none of that money comes to the artist."
**Common misuse:** A strong secondary-market (auction) result is assumed to directly benefit the artist financially; outside ARR jurisdictions, it usually doesn't, though it can support primary-market price increases.

### Hobby-loss rule
The IRS presumption under 26 U.S.C. §183 that an activity is a business (not a hobby, whose losses aren't deductible) if it shows a profit in 3 of the last 5 tax years, rebuttable via a nine-factor test otherwise.
**In use:** "We're building the record now — separate account, business plan, time logs — so we can win the nine-factor test if audited before year three."
**Common misuse:** Treated as an absolute rule ("no profit in 3 of 5 years means it's a hobby"); it's a rebuttable presumption, and artists with genuine business conduct win audits before hitting that threshold.

### Commission (as in commissioned work)
A specific work created to a client's specification and budget, agreed before production, distinct from work made speculatively for a show.
**In use:** "The commission contract needs a scope-change clause — public art commissions run over spec more often than not."
**Common misuse:** Priced the same as a comparable existing work off the price list; commissions carry approval-cycle risk, revision requests, and often a VARA waiver, and should be priced and contracted differently.

### Studio practice
The artist's ongoing production system — schedule, materials workflow, documentation habits — independent of any single show or sale.
**In use:** "My studio practice is built around a six-week cycle per painting; a gallery asking for eighteen new works in three months is asking me to break that."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a synonym for "having a studio" (the physical space) rather than the production system and its throughput constraints, which is what actually limits how fast an artist can meet gallery or commission demand.

### Exclusivity (gallery representation)
A contractual restriction on where else the artist can sell or exhibit, which can be scoped by geography, medium, or both.
**In use:** "I'll agree to metro exclusivity on paintings, but sculpture and editions stay open for other galleries."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to be all-or-nothing; exclusivity is negotiable in scope (geography, medium, price tier) and unscoped exclusivity from an unproven gallery is a one-sided term.

### Placement
Where a work ends up after a sale — a private collection, a museum, a notable institutional collection — which affects the artist's market credibility independent of the sale price.
**In use:** "That sale was modest, but the placement — a museum's permanent collection — does more for the market than three private sales at a higher price."
**Common misuse:** Ignored in favor of price alone; strong placements compound into future pricing power and institutional interest in ways a private sale of the same dollar value doesn't.

### Fee calculator (nonprofit exhibition fees)
A benchmarking method (the W.A.G.E. Certification model is the reference point) that ties an artist's exhibition/loan fee request to the hosting institution's operating budget rather than a flat or arbitrary number.
**In use:** "Given their budget tier, the fee-calculator benchmark puts a solo show in this range — that's the number I'm anchoring the ask to."
**Common misuse:** Skipped entirely, with artists either asking for nothing ("exposure") or an arbitrary number with no institutional benchmark behind it, weakening the negotiating position either way.
