# Red Flags

### Gallery sale report shows a discount >10% off retail with no prior approval
- **Usually means:** the gallery is closing sales by cutting price without consulting the artist, training collectors to expect discounts, then it means the gallery's own cash-flow pressure is being solved by discounting the artist's asset.
- **First question:** "Was this discount approved in writing before the sale, per the consignment agreement?"
- **Data to pull:** the signed consignment agreement's discount-approval clause and the full sale report history for the last two quarters.

### No sale report or payment 45+ days after a fair or show closes
- **Usually means:** the gallery is short on cash and using consigned proceeds as working capital, then it means the gallery is in real financial distress.
- **First question:** "Can I see the itemized sale report and expected remittance date in writing?"
- **Data to pull:** the consignment agreement's payment-timeline clause and any public signals (other artists' complaints, closed locations, unpaid vendor disputes).

### A collector or acquaintance mentions a sale the gallery never reported
- **Usually means:** an unreported sale (accidental clerical miss, or deliberate), then it means outright misappropriation of consigned proceeds.
- **First question:** "Can you send the sale report and payment for that specific piece dated [date]?"
- **Data to pull:** the inventory sheet entry for that piece and the gallery's complete sale-report log since the last reconciliation.

### Gallery proposes global, all-media exclusivity with no minimum sales or promotional commitment in return
- **Usually means:** the gallery wants to lock up upside without taking on obligation, then it means the gallery hasn't thought through what it's actually offering beyond a wall.
- **First question:** "What does the gallery commit to — minimum annual sales, art fair placements, press outreach — in exchange for this exclusivity?"
- **Data to pull:** the gallery's exhibition history and art-fair booth list for its represented artists over the last 2 years.

### A commission or public-art contract's VARA clause is a blanket waiver with no carve-out
- **Usually means:** boilerplate drafted by the commissioning party's counsel to foreclose any future dispute, then it means the commissioner anticipates wanting to alter, relocate, or remove the work later.
- **First question:** "Under what specific conditions would you need to alter or relocate this work after installation?"
- **Data to pull:** the commissioning institution's facilities/maintenance history with other public art (removed, altered, or relocated pieces).

### A licensing offer is a flat one-time fee for "all rights, worldwide, in perpetuity"
- **Usually means:** the licensee is buying long-term reuse value at a short-term price, then it means the licensee expects the image to have significant future commercial life it isn't paying for.
- **First question:** "What is the anticipated print run, media spend, or usage window, and why does a perpetual buyout make sense for that scope?"
- **Data to pull:** comparable licensing fees for similar usage scope from a licensing agent or the Artists Rights Society/VAGA fee schedules.

### Sell-through drops below 50% two shows in a row at the same gallery, prices unchanged
- **Usually means:** demand softening independent of the artist's work quality (gallery's collector base eroding, poor placement, weak marketing), then it means the market itself has cooled on this body of work.
- **First question:** "What changed in the collector list, marketing spend, or placement between the two shows?"
- **Data to pull:** the gallery's collector-outreach list and marketing activity log for both shows, compared side by side.

### A grant panel or juror rejection cites "unclear artistic vision" more than once across different bodies of work
- **Usually means:** the artist statement is leading with abstraction/theme language instead of concrete process and subject, then it means the actual work is inconsistent in method across submissions.
- **First question:** "Does the statement name the material process and a specific number (scale, duration, materials count) in its first two sentences?"
- **Data to pull:** the last 3 rejected statements side by side against the strong-example pattern in `references/playbook.md`.

### IRS or accountant flags the practice hasn't shown a profit in 3 of the last 5 years
- **Usually means:** legitimate early-career losses that need better documentation, then it means the practice risks reclassification as a hobby under 26 U.S.C. §183.
- **First question:** "Is there a separate business bank account, a written business plan, and time/expense records supporting the nine-factor test?"
- **Data to pull:** Treas. Reg. §1.183-2(b) nine factors checked against the artist's actual records (bank account separation, marketing spend, time logs, prior professional experience).

### A gallery closes or files bankruptcy while holding consigned inventory
- **Usually means:** consigned work is legally protected as the artist's trust property if a signed consignment agreement exists, then without one it means the work is at real risk of being swept into the gallery's general creditor estate.
- **First question:** "Do I have a signed consignment agreement and proof-of-insurance for every piece currently at this gallery?"
- **Data to pull:** the inventory tracking sheet cross-referenced against every signed consignment agreement on file for that gallery.
