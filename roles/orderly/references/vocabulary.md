# Vocabulary

Terms generalists reliably misuse. Format per term: definition, how a practitioner actually uses it, the common misuse.

### Safe Working Load (SWL)

- **Definition:** the manufacturer-rated maximum weight a piece of equipment (wheelchair, stretcher, lift sling) is certified to carry, including anything attached to the patient — not just the patient's body weight alone.
- **In use:** "He's 227 on the scale, but with the IV pole and his bag that's close enough to the 250 SWL that I'm getting the bariatric chair instead."
- **Common misuse:** treating SWL as a pass/fail line against bare patient weight, ignoring that IV poles, oxygen tanks, and belongings travel with the patient and eat into the margin.

### Assist level (assist x1 / assist x2 / total or dependent)

- **Definition:** a documented classification of how much physical help a patient needs to move or transfer — one helper, two helpers, or full mechanical/staff-dependent lift.
- **In use:** "Chart says assist x1 from three days ago, but she got fentanyl twenty minutes ago — I'm treating this as assist x2 until I recheck her standing BP."
- **Common misuse:** reading the documented assist level as a permanent fact about the patient rather than a snapshot that needs reassessment against anything that's changed since it was written.

### Gait belt

- **Definition:** a strap worn around a patient's waist used to stabilize and guide movement during a walk or transfer — a control point, not a lifting handle.
- **In use:** "Gait belt's on, but she's still assist x1 — I'm walking beside her, not hauling her up by the belt."
- **Common misuse:** using the belt to physically lift a patient's body weight, which it isn't rated for, instead of using it purely for balance and directional control.

### Team lift / 2-person assist

- **Definition:** a coordinated transfer performed by two staff members using a pre-agreed count and roles, not simply two people present in the room.
- **In use:** "On three — one, two, three — lift and pivot together, not whoever moves first."
- **Common misuse:** counting a second person's presence as sufficient without an actual coordinated count/cue, which produces exactly the mistimed, uneven lift the second person was there to prevent.

### Mechanical/Hoyer lift

- **Definition:** a powered or hydraulic device that fully supports a patient's weight via a sling during a transfer, used for total-assist or high-risk transfers.
- **In use:** "Sling's rated for 400 — he's 340 with the O2 tank, that clears it, but check the sling size fits his frame before we hook it up."
- **Common misuse:** treating "we have a lift" as automatically safe without checking that the specific sling size and weight rating match the specific patient.

### Terminal clean / turnover

- **Definition:** the complete disinfection and restocking process for a room between occupants, following a defined surface order and disinfectant contact time — distinct from a routine daily wipe-down.
- **In use:** "That's not a terminal clean, that's a quick wipe — high-touch surfaces first, then dwell time, then floor, every time a patient leaves."
- **Common misuse:** conflating a fast surface wipe with the full turnover sequence, especially under pressure to free the bed quickly.

### Dwell time / wet-contact time

- **Definition:** the manufacturer-specified minimum duration a disinfectant must remain visibly wet on a surface to achieve its labeled kill claim.
- **In use:** "Label says 5 minutes wet-contact — leave it, don't wipe it dry at minute two just because it looks clean."
- **Common misuse:** wiping a surface dry as soon as it looks clean, which stops the disinfectant from working regardless of which product was used.

### Transmission-based precautions (contact / droplet / airborne)

- **Definition:** three distinct isolation categories, each with a different required PPE set and, for airborne, a fit-tested respirator and negative-pressure room — not interchangeable.
- **In use:** "It's airborne, not contact — I need the N95 fit-checked, not just a surgical mask and gloves."
- **Common misuse:** applying one generic "isolation PPE" routine to all three categories, which under-protects for airborne and over-protects (wasting PPE and time) for simple contact precautions.

### Two-identifier verification

- **Definition:** confirming patient identity using two independent identifiers (typically name and date of birth) checked against the wristband, not against location or memory.
- **In use:** "Name and date of birth, please — I know I've moved you before, but I check every time."
- **Common misuse:** substituting room number, bed number, or personal recognition of a familiar patient for an actual wristband/verbal check.

### Body mechanics

- **Definition:** technique for positioning one's own body (base of support, keeping the load close, using legs not back) to reduce strain during a permitted manual task.
- **In use:** "Good body mechanics gets you through the tasks that are actually within manual-lift limits — it doesn't make an over-limit lift safe."
- **Common misuse:** treating correct body mechanics as a substitute for a mechanical lift or second person, rather than as a technique that only applies within already-safe lift limits.

### Chain of custody (postmortem transport)

- **Definition:** the documented, signed handoff trail confirming who transported a deceased patient, when, and with what identity verification, at each transfer point.
- **In use:** "Signature at the unit handoff, signature again at the morgue intake — one signature isn't chain of custody, it's half of it."
- **Common misuse:** treating a single sign-off (often rushed) as sufficient instead of completing the verification and signature at both ends of the transport.

### Orthostatic risk

- **Definition:** the risk of a significant blood-pressure drop and resulting dizziness or fall when a patient moves from lying/sitting to standing, often heightened by sedating medication, dehydration, or prolonged bed rest.
- **In use:** "He got pain meds twenty minutes ago — I'm checking his BP sitting and standing before we do the transfer, not assuming he's fine because he walked fine yesterday."
- **Common misuse:** relying on yesterday's mobility status instead of checking for today's specific risk factors (new medication, recent procedure, reported lightheadedness) before a stand-up transfer.
