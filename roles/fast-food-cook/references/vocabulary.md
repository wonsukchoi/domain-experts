# Vocabulary

Terms a fast food cook uses precisely that generalists flatten into something looser.

### Build card

The station's exact assembly sequence — ingredient order, portion sizes, timing — printed as a picture-and-step reference, not a recipe a cook is meant to interpret. A recipe describes an outcome and leaves technique flexible; a build card specifies the sequence itself as the spec being audited.

**In use:** "The build card says sauce goes on before the lettuce — that's not a suggestion, that's what the photo-match audit checks."

**Common misuse:** treating "build card" and "recipe" as interchangeable, which misses that the card's value is the fixed sequence, not the ingredient list.

### Hold-timer / quality-hold window

The fixed duration after cooking (not after bagging) during which an item stays sellable by spec, distinct from the food-safety cumulative-time limit. A fry can be food-safety-legal at 135°F for hours under TCS rules and still be past its 7-minute quality-hold window at the same time — both are true simultaneously.

**In use:** "That basket is food-safety fine at 135, but it's past the quality-hold timer — different rule, still gets dumped."

**Common misuse:** assuming the hold-timer and the food-safety danger-zone clock are the same threshold; they're set by different concerns and the hold-timer is almost always shorter.

### TPM (total polar materials)

The percentage of breakdown compounds in fryer oil, measured with a handheld meter, used as the oil-change trigger instead of visual color or a fixed hours-of-use schedule.

**In use:** "Oil's at 22% TPM — schedule the change for the lull at 2, don't wait for it to look dirty."

**Common misuse:** judging oil quality by color or smell alone; oil can look fine and already be past the TPM threshold, especially after frying breaded items.

### Speed of service / total time

The timestamped interval from a customer's arrival (menu board) to their departure (window), captured automatically by the POS/drive-thru timer system and rolled into a franchise scorecard — not a subjective sense of how fast the shift felt.

**In use:** "Total time's averaging 178 seconds this rush — under target, but that basket gap cost us two cars at over 300."

**Common misuse:** treating "we were slammed" as equivalent to a speed-of-service miss; the scorecard only counts the measured number, not the perceived intensity of the rush.

### 86'd

An item pulled from availability, whether for stock, time, or quality — the same term as in full-service kitchens, but in a fast food station it's more often triggered by a hold-timer or spec-compliance issue (an ingredient defect, a broken portion tool) than by running out.

**In use:** "86 the chicken sandwich — breading machine's down, we can't hold spec on it right now."

**Common misuse:** assuming 86 always means zero stock; here it frequently means "we can't currently meet the build spec," which is a different condition than an empty bin.

### Clamshell grill

A two-sided grill that cooks both faces of a patty simultaneously under a heated top platen, on a fixed timed cycle — removing the flip-timing decision a single-surface griddle requires entirely.

**In use:** "Ninety seconds on the clamshell, both sides done at once — there's no flip to time."

**Common misuse:** describing clamshell cooking as "grilling like a flattop but faster," which misses that the entire flip-judgment skill set doesn't apply — the cycle time is fixed by the machine, not read off the product.

### Scorecard

The franchisor's periodic compliance and performance rating for a location, combining speed-of-service data, spec-compliance audits (secret shopper, photo-match), and food-safety findings into one number that affects the franchisee directly (renewal terms, incentive pay, sometimes franchise standing).

**In use:** "A serve-past-timer violation dings the scorecard harder than one slow car — that's why we don't override the timer to hit total-time."

**Common misuse:** treating the scorecard as a single speed metric; it weighs compliance and safety findings at least as heavily as time, often more.

### Portion scoop / spec weight

A calibrated tool that delivers a fixed weight or count of product (a fry scoop, a patty press, a squeeze-pattern guide) so that portioning doesn't depend on a trained eye — the tool enforces the build card's weight tolerance directly.

**In use:** "Use the scoop, don't eyeball the fries — that's the spec weight, not a guideline."

**Common misuse:** treating the scoop as a convenience tool rather than the actual compliance mechanism; skipping it and eyeballing a "close enough" portion is a spec violation even if the weight comes out nearly identical.

### Bump / bump bar

To send a completed item's ticket forward on the kitchen video/ticket system, signaling the next station that a component is ready — distinct from "up," which means the entire order is complete and ready to hand off.

**In use:** "Bump the fries, sandwich still has ninety seconds on the grill."

**Common misuse:** using "bump" and "up" interchangeably; bumping a component forward is not the same signal as the full order being ready at the window.

### Exception (logged)

A written, timestamped record of any deviation from spec — a timer override, an equipment fault, an ingredient substitution — required specifically because an unlogged deviation is indistinguishable from an unnoticed compliance failure during a later audit.

**In use:** "If the shift lead wants that basket served, it gets logged as an exception with a reason — it doesn't just happen quietly."

**Common misuse:** treating a verbal heads-up to the next shift as equivalent to a logged exception; only the written, timestamped version holds up when a waste count or compliance audit asks what happened.
