# Vocabulary

Working vocabulary that generalists reliably misuse. Format per term: definition, how a practitioner actually uses it, the common misuse.

### Biostratigraphy

- **Definition:** dating and correlating rock sequences using the documented stratigraphic ranges of fossil taxa (index fossils and biozones), as opposed to numerical (radiometric) dating.
- **In use:** "We're within the ammonite zone here, so call it a relative age unless we've got a bracketing ash bed — the zone alone gives us maybe half a million years of resolution, not a number."
- **Common misuse:** treating a biozone assignment as a precise numerical age, when it's a relative-order tool whose absolute duration can span hundreds of thousands of years and needs radiometric anchoring for a true date.

### Taphonomy

- **Definition:** the study of everything that happens to an organism between death and discovery — decay, transport, burial, and diagenesis — that filters and biases what actually becomes a fossil.
- **In use:** "That bonebed's size-sorted and current-aligned — this is a transported assemblage, not a mass-death event in place; the biological story has to account for that before we say anything about the population."
- **Common misuse:** using "taphonomy" loosely to mean "fossilization in general," rather than treating it as the specific bias-correction step that has to happen before any biological or chronological claim is trusted.

### Holotype

- **Definition:** the single physical specimen formally designated in the original description as the definitive reference point for a species' name, per ICZN rules.
- **In use:** "The new material doesn't match the holotype's diagnostic characters closely enough — either it's a distinct species or the holotype's too incomplete to diagnose against, and that's a different problem."
- **Common misuse:** treating any well-preserved or complete specimen as "the type," when only the one specimen formally designated in the original publication holds that status — later, better specimens are referred material, not the type.

### Cladistics / phylogenetic systematics

- **Definition:** a method for inferring evolutionary relationships from shared derived characters (synapomorphies) using parsimony or model-based analysis of a character-taxon matrix, producing a tree (cladogram) with support values at each node.
- **In use:** "That's a shared derived character supporting the clade, not just a shared primitive one — check whether it's actually a synapomorphy before it goes in the matrix."
- **Common misuse:** conflating "primitive vs. advanced" with the actual distinction (plesiomorphy vs. apomorphy), and reading an unsupported or low-support node as a settled evolutionary relationship rather than the current best hypothesis.

### Signor-Lipps effect

- **Definition:** the sampling artifact in which the observed last (or first) occurrence of a taxon in the fossil record predates (or postdates) its true extinction (or origination), because incomplete preservation means the true endpoint is rarely captured exactly.
- **In use:** "The apparent stepwise decline before the boundary is probably Signor-Lipps — sampling density thins out right where the taxa look like they're dropping off one by one."
- **Common misuse:** reading a gradual decline in observed last occurrences near a boundary as direct evidence of a gradual biological extinction, without checking whether sampling density itself declines across the same interval.

### Lagerstätte

- **Definition:** a fossil deposit with exceptional preservation quality or quantity — either conservation Lagerstätten (soft tissue preserved, e.g. Burgess Shale) or concentration Lagerstätten (unusually dense accumulations).
- **In use:** "This isn't a typical bonebed preservation window — soft-tissue impressions like this put it in Lagerstätte territory, and the sampling logic for typical assemblages doesn't apply."
- **Common misuse:** applying the term to any productive or well-known fossil site, rather than reserving it for genuinely exceptional preservation that changes what inferences are even possible from the material.

### CA-ID-TIMS

- **Definition:** Chemical Abrasion – Isotope Dilution – Thermal Ionization Mass Spectrometry, the highest-precision U-Pb zircon dating method currently in routine use, typically resolving ages to a few parts in 10,000 (roughly ±0.02–0.03 Myr on Cretaceous-age material).
- **In use:** "We need CA-ID-TIMS on this ash bed if the age is going into a global-correlation argument — an LA-ICP-MS spot date won't have the precision to settle the bracket."
- **Common misuse:** treating any U-Pb date, regardless of method (LA-ICP-MS spot dating is an order of magnitude less precise), as interchangeable precision with a CA-ID-TIMS result.

### GSSP (Global Boundary Stratotype Section and Point)

- **Definition:** the internationally ratified physical reference point ("golden spike") in a specific outcrop that formally defines the lower boundary of a chronostratigraphic stage, approved by the International Commission on Stratigraphy.
- **In use:** "Until the ICS ratifies a GSSP for that stage boundary, treat the boundary age as provisional — right now it's a working numerical estimate, not a formally fixed point."
- **Common misuse:** treating any well-studied or frequently cited outcrop as "the" boundary reference, when only the one ICS-ratified section and point carries that formal status.

### Ontogeny (in a taxonomic context)

- **Definition:** the sequence of morphological change an individual organism undergoes from juvenile to adult; a major source of within-species morphological variation that can mimic a taxonomically diagnostic difference.
- **In use:** "Before we call this a new genus, plot it against the growth series — if the character shift tracks smoothly with size, it's ontogeny, not a new taxon."
- **Common misuse:** describing a juvenile or subadult specimen's morphology as diagnostic of a new species without checking whether it falls on the known growth trajectory of an already-described close relative.

### Nomen dubium

- **Definition:** a formally published name whose type material is too incomplete or non-diagnostic to be reliably distinguished from other taxa, leaving the name unusable for confident identification of new material.
- **In use:** "That genus is a nomen dubium — the holotype's a single worn tooth with no diagnostic characters, so we can't refer this new, better material to it with confidence."
- **Common misuse:** treating a nomen dubium as simply "an old or outdated name," rather than understanding it as a specific formal status that blocks confident taxonomic assignment regardless of how well-known the name is.

### Synonymy (senior vs. junior synonym)

- **Definition:** when two or more names refer to the same taxon, ICZN priority rules make the first validly published name the senior synonym (the valid name), and later ones junior synonyms (invalid, but tracked).
- **In use:** "Turns out our 'new' genus is a junior synonym of a name published eight years earlier — priority rules mean the older name stands, no matter how much better the newer description is."
- **Common misuse:** assuming the more detailed, recent, or more famous description is automatically the valid name; priority under the ICZN runs on publication date, not description quality.

### Index fossil

- **Definition:** a taxon used to define or recognize a biozone because it is widespread, evolved and went extinct relatively rapidly (short stratigraphic range), and is easily identified — properties that make it useful for correlation.
- **In use:** "That brachiopod isn't a good index fossil for this interval — it's facies-controlled and long-ranging, so its presence tells us about environment more than age."
- **Common misuse:** treating any commonly found or well-known fossil as a reliable index fossil, without checking that it actually has the short range, wide geographic distribution, and facies-independence the role requires.

### Reworking

- **Definition:** the erosion, transport, and redeposition of a fossil (or a dated mineral grain) from its original stratigraphic context into a younger bed, so its apparent stratigraphic position no longer reflects its true age.
- **In use:** "That tooth's rounded and came out of a channel-lag deposit cutting the mudstone — it's reworked, and it can't be used to date the horizon it was found in."
- **Common misuse:** assuming a specimen's stratigraphic position always reflects its true age of deposition, without checking taphonomic signatures (abrasion, sorting, depositional context) that flag transport from an older source bed.
