# Red flags

Smell tests a lead finisher catches during the pour or in the first weeks after, before a defect becomes a callback.

## 1. Trowel marks still show a wet sheen underneath them

**Usually means:** finishing started before bleed water fully left the surface — the trowel is sealing water in rather than closing a dry surface, setting up blistering or delamination that won't show until the slab dries days later.

**First question:** "Did we check that the surface holds a ~1/4-in indentation under foot pressure before we floated this section, or did we go by the clock?"

**Data to pull:** placement time for this section vs. float/trowel start time; ambient and concrete temperature at placement; whether any other section poured the same day (cooler or warmer) shows the same issue.

## 2. Blisters or hollow-sounding spots appear hours after finishing

**Usually means:** trapped bleed water or air under a prematurely closed (often power-troweled) surface — the layer above the trapped moisture has debonded from the slab beneath it.

**First question:** "When did power troweling start on this section relative to when the bleed sheen disappeared?"

**Data to pull:** finishing timeline for the section; whether the mix is air-entrained (more prone to blistering under early hard troweling); ambient conditions (blistering is more common in warm, breezy weather where the surface crusts while the interior is still bleeding).

## 3. Point-of-placement air content reads below spec on an exterior freeze-thaw slab (e.g., 4% against a 6% target)

**Usually means:** air was lost in transit, pumping, or over-vibration/over-troweling, or the batch itself was off — any of which leaves the slab under-protected against freeze-thaw scaling for its service life.

**First question:** "What did the air meter read at the truck versus at the point of placement, and did we pump or vibrate this load more than usual?"

**Data to pull:** batch ticket air content vs. field air-meter reading (ASTM C231); pump/vibration log for this load; whether this slab is actually exposed to freeze-thaw and deicers or was over-specified.

## 4. Random, map-pattern cracking appears within weeks on a slab with correctly spaced control joints

**Usually means:** joints were cut too late — after shrinkage cracking had already initiated — or cut too shallow (less than ~1/4 slab thickness), so they never actually relieved the stress they were placed to relieve.

**First question:** "How many hours after finishing were these joints actually cut, and what depth did the saw run?"

**Data to pull:** finish-time vs. cut-time log for the section; saw depth setting used; weather conditions on pour day (hot/windy conditions compress the safe cutting window well below the conventional 12–24 hours).

## 5. Saw crew scheduled 24+ hours after finishing "to be safe"

**Usually means:** the cutting window is being planned around avoiding raveling (cutting too early) without checking the other edge of the window — shrinkage cracking initiating before the saw arrives, which can happen in well under 12 hours in hot or windy conditions.

**First question:** "What's the forecast evaporation rate for this pour, and does the saw schedule account for it or just default to next-day?"

**Data to pull:** ACI 305 evaporation-rate inputs for pour day; any hairline cracking visible before the saw crew's scheduled arrival; historical cut-time vs. cracking-incident correlation on past pours at this site.

## 6. Surface dusting or light scaling shows up the first winter on an exterior slab

**Usually means:** insufficient air entrainment for the exposure, inadequate curing (surface never reached the strength/density the mix design assumed), or finishing too wet — excess water worked into the surface layer, raising the local w/c ratio right where freeze-thaw stress concentrates.

**First question:** "What was the air content at placement, and how was this slab cured — compound, wet cure, or nothing beyond 'it looked dry'?"

**Data to pull:** air-meter readings from the original pour; curing method and duration used; whether deicing salts were applied before the slab's first full winter (salt use in year one substantially raises scaling risk on marginal air content).

## 7. Diagonal, shallow cracks appear within hours of a hot, windy pour

**Usually means:** plastic-shrinkage cracking — evaporation rate exceeded the concrete's bleed rate before finishing, drying and shrinking the surface while the slab was still plastic.

**First question:** "Did we run the evaporation-rate calculation for today's conditions before placement, and was fogging or retarder actually deployed, or just discussed?"

**Data to pull:** ACI 305 nomograph inputs actually measured on pour day (not just the morning forecast); timing of any fog spray or retarder application relative to placement; wind speed and direction across the slab (cracking often concentrates on the windward edge).

## 8. Slab poured with overnight lows forecast near freezing and no protection plan on file

**Usually means:** the pour was treated as routine because the daytime placement temperature felt fine, ignoring that ACI 306's cold-weather trigger is about sustained air temperature, not the temperature at the moment of placement.

**First question:** "What's the forecast low for the next three nights, and what's the plan to hold concrete temperature above the minimum for this section's thickness?"

**Data to pull:** forecast temperature trend for 72+ hours post-pour; section thickness (thinner sections need a higher minimum hold temperature and are more vulnerable, faster); insulation/blanket plan and whether it's sized to the forecast low, not the forecast high.

## 9. Power trowel finish used on exterior flatwork exposed to foot or vehicle traffic in wet/icy conditions

**Usually means:** the finish was chosen for appearance or speed without weighing the slip-resistance tradeoff a dense, hard-troweled exterior surface creates in freeze-thaw or wet conditions.

**First question:** "Was this finish specified for looks, or was slip resistance in wet/icy conditions actually considered against the traffic this surface sees?"

**Data to pull:** the finish spec called for in the contract documents; actual traffic type and conditions (pedestrian vs. vehicle, exposure to rain/ice); whether a broom or other texture finish was an option that got dropped for schedule reasons.
