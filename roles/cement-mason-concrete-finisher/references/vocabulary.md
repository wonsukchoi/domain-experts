# Vocabulary

Terms generalists conflate that a finisher keeps sharply separate, because the difference changes what you do next.

## Bleed water vs. plastic shrinkage

**Bleed water** is the mix water that rises to the surface as heavier solids settle after placement — a normal part of curing that finishing must wait out. **Plastic shrinkage (cracking)** happens when surface water evaporates faster than bleed water can replace it, shrinking the still-plastic surface while the interior hasn't set — a defect caused by evaporation outrunning bleed, not by bleed water itself.

*In use:* "The sheen you're seeing is bleed water — leave it. What we're watching for is the evaporation rate outrunning it, which is a different problem with a different fix."

**Common misuse:** treating any surface water as a problem to trowel away immediately, which is exactly the mistake that traps bleed water and causes blistering.

## Initial set vs. final set

**Initial set** (ASTM C403) is the point the concrete can no longer be worked as a fluid — roughly when finishing operations should begin. **Final set** is when the concrete has enough rigidity to bear light loads and can be considered no longer plastic. The gap between them is the finishing window, and it isn't fixed — it moves with temperature.

*In use:* "Initial set on this pour is running fast — we're at maybe two hours instead of the usual four, so get the float crew moving now, not at the scheduled time."

**Common misuse:** using "set" as if it's one moment instead of a range, then being surprised the finishing window closed faster or slower than "usual."

## Control joint vs. construction joint vs. isolation joint

**Control joint** is a planned weakened plane (tooled or saw-cut) that gives shrinkage cracking a place to happen on purpose. **Construction joint** is where one placement ends and the next begins, needed for practical reasons (end of a pour day, section too large to place at once), not for crack control. **Isolation joint** fully separates a slab from an adjacent fixed element (column, wall, footing) so the two can move independently.

*In use:* "That crack ran straight through what should've been a control joint — check whether it was actually cut, or whether that line is just a construction joint from yesterday's pour."

**Common misuse:** assuming any visible line in a slab is doing crack-control work; a construction or isolation joint placed for a different reason doesn't substitute for a properly spaced control joint.

## Air-entrained air vs. entrapped air

**Entrained air** is millions of microscopic, uniformly distributed bubbles deliberately created by an admixture to give freezing pore water somewhere to expand into. **Entrapped air** is larger, irregular voids from inadequate consolidation — it doesn't protect against freeze-thaw and instead weakens the concrete.

*In use:* "The air meter's reading 6%, but if half of that is entrapped voids from under-vibrating the pour, we don't actually have the freeze-thaw protection the mix design assumes."

**Common misuse:** reading a passing air-content number and assuming the protection is real, without checking whether it's entrained (designed-in) or entrapped (a consolidation defect that happens to add up to a similar percentage).

## Curing vs. drying

**Curing** is maintaining adequate moisture and temperature so hydration (the chemical reaction that builds strength) continues over time. **Drying** is moisture simply leaving the slab, which can happen with or without adequate curing and tells you nothing about whether strength gain is on track.

*In use:* "It looks dry because the surface moisture evaporated overnight — that's drying, not curing. We still owe this slab three more days under the compound."

**Common misuse:** treating a dry-looking surface as a finished slab and pulling curing measures early, which caps strength gain invisibly.

## Floating vs. troweling

**Floating** (bull float or hand float) embeds aggregate just below the surface and brings enough cream up to allow subsequent finishing — it's a leveling and preparation step, done once bleed water has left. **Troweling** densifies and smooths that surface afterward, often in multiple passes with progressively harder pressure — it's a separate operation with its own timing, later and drier than floating.

*In use:* "We floated at 9, but don't start hard troweling until the surface won't take a fingerprint — that's usually another 30–45 minutes behind the float, not right after it."

**Common misuse:** treating float and trowel as one continuous motion on one timer, instead of two operations separated by the surface reaching a further stage of stiffness.

## Water-cement ratio (w/c) vs. slump

**Water-cement ratio** is the mix-design number that governs strength and durability — lower w/c generally means higher strength and better freeze-thaw resistance. **Slump** is a field test of workability/consistency at the truck; it correlates with water content but isn't the same measurement, and adding water at the site to "fix" a low slump raises w/c and can silently drop the mix below its design strength.

*In use:* "Don't add water to loosen this load for pumping — that's a w/c problem now, not a slump problem. Call for a superplasticizer dose instead."

**Common misuse:** treating slump as purely a placement-convenience number and adding water on-site to hit a target slump, which quietly compromises the strength the mix design was built around.

## Cold joint

An unintended, weak bonding plane where fresh concrete is placed against concrete that has already begun to set — as opposed to a construction joint, which is planned and properly prepared (roughened, sometimes bonding agent applied). A cold joint happens when placement stalls (truck delay, pump breakdown, undersized crew) past the point the prior layer can still bond monolithically.

*In use:* "We lost forty minutes to the pump jamming — that seam is probably a cold joint now, not a clean lift line. Flag it for the inspector."

**Common misuse:** calling any visible seam in a slab a "cold joint" when it may be a properly prepared and planned construction joint; the distinction is whether the joint was planned and prepped, or an accident of timing.

## Efflorescence vs. scaling vs. spalling

**Efflorescence** is a white, powdery salt deposit from soluble salts carried to the surface by moisture movement — a cosmetic/moisture issue, not structural. **Scaling** is the loss of a thin surface layer (often from inadequate air entrainment or finishing while too wet, combined with freeze-thaw or deicer exposure) — a durability defect. **Spalling** is a larger, deeper fragment breaking away, often from corroding embedded reinforcement or a more severe underlying defect.

*In use:* "That white haze is efflorescence — moisture-related, not a strength issue. The flaking a few feet over, where you can see aggregate exposed, is scaling, and that's an air-content or finishing-timing question."

**Common misuse:** lumping all three under "the concrete is deteriorating" without distinguishing cosmetic (efflorescence) from durability (scaling) from potentially structural (spalling), which leads to the wrong fix being proposed.
