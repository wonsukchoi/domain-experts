# Advertising Sales Agent — Vocabulary

### Avails
The remaining unsold inventory (impressions, spots, or slots) for a given format and time period.
**In use:** "Pull avails on video pre-roll before you quote anything — I don't want to oversell it again."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a fixed number that doesn't need rechecking; avails change daily as other deals close.

### Sellout rate / sell-through rate
The percentage of a format's available inventory for a period that has already been sold.
**In use:** "Homepage is running 92% sell-through this month — hold the rate, don't discount it."
**Common misuse:** Confused with revenue performance; a format can be 100% sold out and still be underpriced relative to demand.

### Make-good
Free additional inventory owed to a client when a guaranteed delivery falls short of the committed amount.
**In use:** "We owe Riverside Motors a 240,000-impression make-good from the shortfall on their last flight."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a minor administrative note rather than a real revenue liability that displaces future sellable inventory.

### Remnant inventory
Unsold ad space left over after premium/scarce formats are booked, typically sold at a steep discount or through programmatic channels.
**In use:** "Push the extra budget into remnant ROS — it needs the volume more than homepage does."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to be low-value or low-quality by default; remnant just means low-demand-relative-to-supply, not necessarily low audience quality.

### Insertion order (IO)
The contract specifying the agreed placements, rates, flight dates, and delivery guarantees for a campaign.
**In use:** "Get the discount rationale written into the IO, not just agreed verbally."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a formality signed after the terms are settled, rather than the document that has to precisely match what was actually negotiated (including guarantee type).

### CPM (cost per mille / cost per thousand impressions)
The price charged per 1,000 ad impressions delivered.
**In use:** "We're quoting ROS at $4.83 CPM, well under the $6 card rate, to move the remnant volume."
**Common misuse:** Compared directly across formats with very different audience attention/quality without noting the difference is priced in for a reason.

### CPC (cost per click)
The price charged per click generated by an ad, rather than per impression delivered.
**In use:** "This client wants CPC pricing on the search placements since they only care about click volume."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to be a better deal for the buyer by default; CPC shifts delivery risk onto the seller (a low-CTR placement earns less per impression) and is priced accordingly.

### Flight
The scheduled start and end dates of a campaign's delivery period.
**In use:** "The flight is four weeks — guarantee total impressions across it, not a weekly minimum."
**Common misuse:** Confused with the contract's signature date or invoicing period, which can differ from when the ads actually run.

### Guarantee (impression guarantee)
A contractual commitment to deliver a specific number of impressions, triggering a make-good obligation if underdelivered.
**In use:** "Only guarantee the flight total — a per-week guarantee removes our ability to catch up a slow day."
**Common misuse:** Treated as equivalent regardless of whether it's structured as a flight-total or a per-period minimum; the structure determines how much make-good risk the seller is carrying.

### Rate card
The standard, published list price per format before any negotiated discount is applied.
**In use:** "That's 20% off card — make sure the IO documents why."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a starting offer everyone is expected to negotiate down from by a similar amount, regardless of the format's actual sellout pressure.

### Upfront / scatter market
In broadcast/cable ad sales, "upfront" refers to inventory sold months in advance of a season at negotiated rates; "scatter" refers to inventory sold closer to air date, typically at a premium if demand is high or a discount if it isn't.
**In use:** "Scatter pricing is running hot this quarter — hold rate on anything sold this close to air."
**Common misuse:** Assumed scatter is always more expensive than upfront; it depends on current demand relative to what was locked in months earlier.

### Audience guarantee vs. impression guarantee
An audience guarantee commits to reaching a specific demographic composition; an impression guarantee commits only to a raw impression count regardless of who saw it.
**In use:** "Their brief wants 25-54 reach specifically — that needs an audience guarantee, not just an impression count."
**Common misuse:** Used interchangeably; a campaign can hit its impression guarantee while badly missing the audience composition the client actually needed.

### Programmatic (as a sell-side channel)
Automated, often real-time-bid inventory sales, typically used to move remnant inventory efficiently rather than through direct-sold negotiation.
**In use:** "Anything still unsold 48 hours before the flight starts gets pushed to programmatic."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to always yield lower prices than direct sales; well-targeted programmatic demand can sometimes clear at a comparable rate to a weak direct-sold discount.
