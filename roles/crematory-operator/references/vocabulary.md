# Vocabulary

Terms of art generalists misuse. Format per term: definition, how a practitioner actually uses it, the common misuse.

### Retort

The furnace chamber where cremation happens, built from steel and lined with refractory brick rated for repeated cycles above 1,800°F.
**In use:** "The retort's due for a reline next quarter — brick's spalling on the east wall from the last run of oversized charges."
**Common misuse:** calling it an "incinerator" or "oven" in front of family members — accurate in a technical sense but a term the industry avoids in that context because of the connotation.

### Primary chamber vs. secondary chamber (afterburner)

The primary chamber is where the decedent and container combust; the secondary chamber independently burns the resulting gases at its own held temperature before they reach the stack.
**In use:** "Cycle looked done on the primary timer, but secondary never held 1,650 — that's not a completed case, that's an emissions log entry I need to explain."
**Common misuse:** treating "primary chamber cooled down" as equivalent to "cycle complete." The two chambers are separate compliance events, not one process with two zones.

### Cremulator (processor)

The machine that mechanically reduces recovered bone fragments to a uniform granular consistency after combustion.
**In use:** "Bone came out dense on this one — ran it through the cremulator twice to get an even consistency before packaging."
**Common misuse:** assuming "cremated remains" are produced by fire alone. The fire vaporizes soft tissue; the cremulator is what produces the fine, sand-like texture families expect — skip it and you hand over bone fragments, not remains.

### Chain of custody

The documented, timestamped record of everyone who handled a decedent from intake through release of remains.
**In use:** "Chain of custody shows the disc went cooler-to-charging table at 10:14, charging-to-retort at 10:23 — no gap where a mix-up could've happened."
**Common misuse:** treating it as paperwork completed after the fact for compliance, rather than a real-time control that's supposed to catch a mismatch before it becomes irreversible.

### Positive identification

Physically confirming the decedent's identity against the case record at each handoff point, anchored by the ID disc — distinct from identity verification done once at intake.
**In use:** "We positively ID at intake, at charging, and again when the disc comes back out of the chamber — three checks, not one."
**Common misuse:** assuming identification is a single event at intake rather than a check repeated at every step where a mix-up could occur.

### Multiple cremation / commingling

Charging more than one decedent in the same chamber load without proper authorization, or failing to fully clear a chamber between single-occupancy cases, resulting in mixed remains.
**In use:** "That family specifically didn't authorize multi-occupancy — this case runs alone, full stop, regardless of the schedule."
**Common misuse:** conflating authorized multi-occupancy (rare, requires signed consent — twins, sometimes spouses) with trace commingling from incomplete chamber clearing, which is a process failure, not a policy choice.

### Cremation authorization

The signed legal document from the next of kin or authorizing agent granting permission to cremate — distinct from the death certificate and any medical examiner release.
**In use:** "Authorization's signed, but the ME release hasn't come back yet — case doesn't move to the charge schedule until both are in hand."
**Common misuse:** treating a signed authorization alone as sufficient to charge, when most states also require the waiting period and any applicable ME/coroner release to independently clear.

### Alkaline hydrolysis (aquamation/water cremation)

A separate disposition method using heated water and alkali to reduce remains, chemically distinct from flame cremation and licensed/regulated separately in states that permit it.
**In use:** "This facility's only permitted for flame cremation — if the family wants aquamation, that's a referral, not something we can substitute in-house."
**Common misuse:** using "cremation" as if it only means the flame process, or assuming a flame-cremation license covers alkaline hydrolysis — the equipment, chemistry, and regulatory approval are all separate.

### Refractory brick / reline

The heat-resistant brick lining the retort's interior, which degrades under thermal cycling and periodically needs full replacement (a "reline").
**In use:** "We're tracking cycle count against the last reline — chronic overheating from undersized burner throttling on large charges shortens the brick's life."
**Common misuse:** treating brick condition as a maintenance-schedule item disconnected from operating practice, when burner discipline on oversized charges directly drives how often relining is needed.

### Cool-down

The mandatory period after a cycle completes before the chamber door can be safely opened, set by the equipment manufacturer and scaled to charge size.
**In use:** "This one's a 350-plus load — 90-minute cooldown minimum before I open the door, not the standard 45."
**Common misuse:** treating cool-down time as flexible under schedule pressure; opening early thermal-shocks the refractory lining and risks operator burn injury.

### Incidental remains / prosthetic hardware

Non-bone material recovered during the magnet pass after combustion — joint replacements, surgical hardware, casket fasteners — logged and disposed of separately, never packaged with the cremated remains.
**In use:** "Hip replacement came through in the magnet pass, logged it separately per policy — doesn't go back with the family's remains."
**Common misuse:** assuming everything recovered from the chamber goes to the family; hardware is explicitly excluded and handled through a separate disposal chain.
