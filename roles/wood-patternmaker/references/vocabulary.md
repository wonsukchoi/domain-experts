# Vocabulary

### Shrink rule
A specialized measuring rule pre-scaled to a specific metal's shrinkage rate, allowing a patternmaker to lay out pattern dimensions that automatically include the correct oversized allowance without recalculating for every measurement.
**In use:** "Using the cast iron shrink rule for this pattern — reading twenty-four inches on it lays out the actual oversized dimension we need."
**Common misuse:** Using a single shrink rule (or none) regardless of the actual metal being cast — different metals shrink at meaningfully different rates, and using the wrong rule (or building to the target dimension directly) produces a systematically mis-sized casting.

### Draft angle
A deliberate taper applied to pattern surfaces in the direction the pattern will be withdrawn from the sand mold, preventing the pattern from binding against and damaging the mold on removal.
**In use:** "Two degrees of draft on every vertical face here — that's what lets us pull the pattern without tearing up the mold."
**Common misuse:** Treating draft as an optional refinement for a "simple" pattern shape — draft is a functional requirement for any surface in the withdrawal direction, and skipping it on the assumption a simple shape doesn't need it risks mold damage regardless of the pattern's overall complexity.

### Parting line
The plane along which a pattern (and the resulting mold) splits into two or more sections to allow removal from a two-part sand mold, chosen based on draft direction, core access, and finishing considerations.
**In use:** "Parting line goes at the flange, not the geometric midpoint — that's where draft and core print access actually line up."
**Common misuse:** Choosing a parting line purely for geometric convenience (the simplest split) without checking whether it aligns with draft direction and core print requirements — a poorly chosen parting line can make an otherwise correct pattern difficult to mold cleanly.

### Core print
An extension on a pattern that creates a corresponding seat in the mold, used to locate and support a sand core that forms an internal cavity in the finished casting.
**In use:** "Core print's sized for this core's actual weight — undersized and it risks shifting when the metal starts flowing around it."
**Common misuse:** Sizing a core print by appearance ("looks big enough") rather than calculating from the actual core's weight and the casting's pour dynamics — an undersized or mispositioned print risks the core shifting during pour, producing uneven wall thickness in the finished casting.

### Sand casting (mold)
A casting process where molten metal is poured into a mold formed by packing sand around a pattern, then removing the pattern before pouring.
**In use:** "This pattern's built for sand casting — the draft and parting line decisions are specific to how sand molds get formed and separated."
**Common misuse:** Applying pattern design principles developed for a different casting process (like investment casting, which doesn't require the same draft/withdrawal considerations) to sand casting pattern work — sand casting's specific requirement to physically withdraw the pattern from packed sand drives design decisions that other casting methods don't share.

### Cope and drag
The upper (cope) and lower (drag) halves of a two-part sand mold, formed by the pattern's two halves at the parting line.
**In use:** "Pattern splits into cope and drag halves right at the parting line we designed for draft compatibility."
**Common misuse:** Assuming cope and drag halves are simply a mechanical necessity of mold-making rather than a design decision tied directly to the pattern's parting line choice — the split location is deliberately chosen, not an arbitrary consequence of the process.

### First-casting verification
The practice of producing and dimensionally checking an initial casting from a new pattern against the target part specification before releasing the pattern for full production use.
**In use:** "First casting off this pattern measured within spec — releasing it to production now that we've confirmed the shrink rule and draft were applied correctly."
**Common misuse:** Releasing a new pattern to production based on the pattern's appearance or the patternmaker's confidence alone, without an actual first-casting dimensional check — since a pattern error repeats across every subsequent casting, verification before full release is disproportionately valuable compared to catching an error later.

### Gray iron / cast iron shrinkage
The specific, well-characterized shrinkage rate for gray cast iron (commonly around 1/8 inch per foot), one of several metal-specific shrink rates a patternmaker must apply correctly.
**In use:** "Gray iron shrinks an eighth per foot — that's the rate baked into this specific shrink rule, different from the rate we'd use for aluminum or steel."
**Common misuse:** Applying a single "standard" shrinkage rate across different metals — aluminum, cast iron, and steel each have distinct characteristic shrinkage rates, and treating them as interchangeable produces a systematically wrong pattern dimension for whichever metal wasn't actually being cast.

### Flash (casting finishing)
Excess material that forms along a casting's parting line where mold halves meet, requiring removal as a finishing step after casting.
**In use:** "Parting line placement here also determines how much flash finishing this casting needs afterward — that's part of why we didn't just pick the geometrically simplest split."
**Common misuse:** Treating flash/finishing burden as an afterthought unrelated to pattern design decisions — parting line placement directly determines where and how much flash forms, making it a consideration during pattern design, not just a downstream finishing problem to deal with separately.
