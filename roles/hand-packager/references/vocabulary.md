# Vocabulary

Terms generalists conflate or oversimplify — practitioners keep them precise because compliance, damage-prevention, and rate decisions depend on it.

## Maximum Allowable Variation (MAV)

The legal floor an individual sold-by-weight package may fall below its labeled weight, expressed as a percentage of that labeled weight that shrinks as the declared weight grows. It applies per package, separate from the lot's average.

**In use:** "That bag's at 39.2 grams — the MAV floor on this class is 39.95, so it's a failure on its own, independent of what the lot average looks like."

**Common misuse:** treating MAV as the only check that matters, when the average requirement is a separate, simultaneous obligation.

## Average requirement (weights and measures)

The rule that a sample lot's mean net weight must equal or exceed the labeled weight — a lot can have every individual package clear its MAV floor and still fail if the average sits below label.

**In use:** "Every bag in that sample cleared MAV, but the average came in under label — that's still a failed lot."

**Common misuse:** assuming that if no single unit is flagged, the lot as a whole is compliant.

## Cushion curve

A material-specific chart plotting peak deceleration (G) against static loading and cushion thickness for a given drop height, used to select the thickness that keeps a product's fragility rating under the shock it will actually experience in transit.

**In use:** "Pull up the foam's cushion curve at this drop height before adding another inch — past the flat part of that curve, more material isn't buying more protection."

**Common misuse:** treating "more cushioning" as always safer, when the curve shows a point past which added thickness stops reducing peak G and only adds cost and dimensional weight.

## Fragility rating (G-rating)

A product's tolerance for peak deceleration, expressed in G's, from a fragility (shock-sensitivity) test — the input a cushion curve is read against to select material and thickness.

**In use:** "This unit's rated at 40G — check the cushion curve at that rating and this drop height before deciding thickness."

**Common misuse:** guessing a fragility rating from how delicate a product looks or feels, instead of using a tested rating.

## Void fill

Material added inside a shipping container to eliminate free movement of the product during transit, sized to the product's fragility class and the container's dimensions — not a fixed handful applied regardless of what's being shipped.

**In use:** "The item's shifting corner-to-corner in the box — that's an under-fill problem, add material to the fragility-class spec, not just 'more.'"

**Common misuse:** equating "more void fill" with "safer," which drives dimensional-weight cost up without necessarily reducing damage past the cushion curve's effective range.

## Dimensional weight (dim weight)

A shipping-cost calculation based on a package's volume rather than its actual weight, used by carriers when a box is large relative to its contents. Over-cushioning inflates the billable weight even though the product's actual weight hasn't changed.

**In use:** "We're paying dim weight on this SKU now — check whether the void-fill amount crept up without a fragility-class change."

**Common misuse:** treating cushioning decisions as cost-free, when excess void fill can directly raise the shipping bill through dim weight.

## Allergen cross-contact

Unintended transfer of an allergenic ingredient from one product to another that doesn't declare it, typically via shared equipment, surfaces, or airborne residue between runs — distinct from cross-contamination with pathogens, and controlled through changeover procedure rather than sanitation alone.

**In use:** "We're not just cleaning for microbes at this changeover — it's an allergen cross-contact control, so it needs the swab reading, not just the wash-down."

**Common misuse:** assuming a standard sanitation wash-down also clears allergen risk, when allergen protein residue requires its own validated test.

## Changeover validation

The documented confirmation — cleaning steps completed, ATP swab and/or allergen strip reading recorded, approver signature — that a line is safe to run the next SKU after an allergen transition. The record, not the cleaning act itself, is what an audit or recall investigation asks for.

**In use:** "Don't restart on 'we wiped it down' — pull the changeover validation record and confirm the swab reading is logged."

**Common misuse:** treating the physical cleaning as sufficient on its own, without the paired documented reading that proves it worked.

## ATP swab test

A rapid test measuring adenosine triphosphate (a proxy for organic residue, including allergen protein) on a surface via a light-producing reaction, reported in relative light units (RLU); used as the pass/fail gate before restarting a changed-over line.

**In use:** "Swab came back at 22 RLU against our 10 RLU threshold — that's a fail, re-clean and re-test before restart."

**Common misuse:** treating a passing ATP reading as proof of zero allergen presence, when ATP measures general organic residue, not the specific allergen protein — a strip test is the more specific confirmation for a known allergen.

## Skip-lot sampling

An inspection scheme that checks every Nth unit (e.g., every 30th carton) rather than every unit, trading full coverage for a workable rate — valid only if the interval isn't widened informally under rate pressure.

**In use:** "We sample every 30th carton on this line — if that interval's been stretched to every 50th during the rush, we're not running the validated plan anymore."

**Common misuse:** treating the sampling interval as a soft guideline that can flex with volume, when it's the basis for the compliance claim about the whole run.

## Hand Activity Level (HAL)

A 0–10 rated measure of how repetitive and continuous a hand-intensive task is (short cycle time, limited recovery, sustained exertion), plotted against peak or normalized force to assess repetitive-strain risk — analogous to a Lifting Index for hand tasks rather than whole-body lifts.

**In use:** "This station's HAL is high even though the force is low — short cycle time alone can push it into the caution zone."

**Common misuse:** assessing repetitive-motion risk on force alone, ignoring that frequency and cycle time drive the HAL side of the risk independently.

## Line rate / takt time

**Line rate** is the throughput target a station must sustain (units or cartons per hour); **takt time** is the inverse — the maximum time allowed per unit to hit that rate. Both are set by demand and staffing, not negotiated unit by unit at the station.

**In use:** "Takt time on this line is 4 seconds per carton — if a check step doesn't fit inside that, the fix is rebalancing the line, not skipping the check."

**Common misuse:** treating the line rate as a target to protect at any cost, rather than a number that should be renegotiated when it can't be held alongside the required checks.
