# Red flags — what a purchasing manager notices instantly

Smell tests with thresholds. Any single one can be innocent; two or three together are a pattern.

### A supplier selection decision based on quoted unit price alone with no total-cost-of-ownership comparison
- **Usually means:** quality-related costs, delivery reliability, and switching costs may make the "cheaper" option more expensive overall once accounted for.
- **First question:** "What does the total cost of ownership comparison look like, not just the unit price?"
- **Data to pull:** defect/rework rates, delivery reliability history, and switching-cost estimates for each supplier option under comparison.

### A critical, hard-to-substitute input single-sourced with no documented risk analysis
- **Usually means:** the concentration risk may have been defaulted into for operational simplicity rather than deliberately evaluated against the actual consequence of a disruption.
- **First question:** "What's the estimated cost and probability of a supply disruption for this specific input, and has that been weighed against the single-sourcing pricing benefit?"
- **Data to pull:** input criticality/substitutability classification, disruption cost and probability estimates, current single-vs-multi-source pricing comparison.

### A supplier risk assessment that hasn't been refreshed since initial onboarding, for a supplier relationship of 2+ years
- **Usually means:** the supplier's financial health, capacity, or geographic exposure may have changed materially since the original vetting, with no process catching it.
- **First question:** "When was this supplier's risk profile last reassessed, and has anything material changed since onboarding?"
- **Data to pull:** date of last supplier risk reassessment, any recent changes in supplier ownership, financial health, or geographic exposure.

### Contract negotiation effort concentrated on unit price with weak or unexamined failure-scenario terms (delivery penalties, quality guarantees, termination rights)
- **Usually means:** negotiation effort may be misallocated relative to where the real risk and value actually live — failure-scenario terms are what matter when something goes wrong.
- **First question:** "What do the delivery penalty, quality guarantee, and termination terms actually say, and were they negotiated with comparable rigor to the price?"
- **Data to pull:** the contract's failure-scenario clauses, negotiation history/effort allocation across price versus these terms.

### A dual/multi-sourcing proposal evaluated only on its price premium, with no comparison against the expected cost of the disruption risk it addresses
- **Usually means:** the diversification decision may be rejected or approved without checking whether it's actually a good value trade against quantified risk — a "just in case" instinct without the math, in either direction.
- **First question:** "What's the guaranteed cost of diversifying, and how does that compare to the expected value of the disruption risk being insured against?"
- **Data to pull:** the pricing premium for a proposed multi-source split, disruption probability and cost estimates, any hybrid split options between full single- and full dual-sourcing.

### A negotiation approached as purely adversarial for a supplier relationship with real ongoing value
- **Usually means:** value may be left on the table, and the relationship's durability may be damaged for a one-time concession, when understanding the supplier's actual constraints could have found a better mutual outcome.
- **First question:** "Do we understand this supplier's actual cost structure and constraints, or are we only negotiating against their opening position?"
- **Data to pull:** supplier's stated versus likely actual constraints, the relationship's expected future value versus the one-time negotiation gain being sought.

### The same sourcing strategy (single-source, standard contract terms, minimal relationship investment) applied uniformly across categories with very different criticality
- **Usually means:** effort may be over-invested in low-stakes commodity purchases or under-invested in genuinely critical ones, since a uniform approach doesn't match the actual risk profile of each category.
- **First question:** "Does this category's sourcing strategy reflect its actual criticality and substitutability, or is it just the default approach applied everywhere?"
- **Data to pull:** category segmentation (e.g., a Kraljic-style criticality/complexity matrix), current sourcing strategy applied per category.

### A supplier's smooth historical track record being cited as evidence that concentration risk doesn't need active management
- **Usually means:** a good track record so far is evidence of reliability to date, not evidence the underlying concentration risk has disappeared — the risk exists independent of whether it's materialized yet.
- **First question:** "If this supplier fails or loses capacity tomorrow, what's the actual cost, independent of how smooth the relationship has been so far?"
- **Data to pull:** the specific disruption-cost estimate for this input, independent of the supplier's historical performance record.
