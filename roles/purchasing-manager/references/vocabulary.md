# Purchasing/sourcing working vocabulary

Terms a purchasing manager uses precisely. Format: definition → how a practitioner says it → common misuse.

**Total cost of ownership (TCO)** — The full cost of a sourcing decision including unit price, quality-related costs (defects, rework), delivery reliability, and switching costs — as opposed to unit price alone, the correct basis for comparing suppliers.
In use: "Supplier B's unit price is 8% higher, but their defect rate is a third of Supplier A's — TCO favors B once rework cost is included."
Misuse: selecting a supplier based on the lowest quoted unit price without accounting for quality, delivery reliability, or switching-cost differences that can make the "cheaper" option more expensive overall.

**Single-sourcing vs. multi-sourcing** — Concentrating purchases with one supplier (better pricing/simplicity, but a single point of failure) versus splitting volume across multiple suppliers (resilience against disruption, at a pricing/complexity cost) — a deliberate risk-tradeoff decision, not a default.
In use: "We're evaluating a 90/10 hybrid split rather than full single- or dual-sourcing, based on the actual disruption-cost math for this component."
Misuse: defaulting to single-sourcing purely for operational simplicity, or defaulting to multi-sourcing purely on a "just in case" instinct, without quantifying the actual tradeoff between pricing benefit and disruption risk.

**Supplier risk assessment** — An ongoing (not one-time) evaluation of a supplier's financial health, geographic/geopolitical exposure, and capacity constraints, since these can change materially after initial onboarding.
In use: "The supplier's parent company was just acquired — that triggers a risk reassessment even though we're two years past onboarding."
Misuse: treating supplier vetting as a one-time onboarding gate rather than a periodically refreshed assessment, missing a material change in the supplier's risk profile that developed after the initial review.

**Failure-scenario contract terms** — Contract provisions (delivery penalties, quality guarantees, termination/exit rights) that determine what protection exists when something goes wrong, as distinct from price and volume terms that govern the smooth-operation scenario.
In use: "We held firm on the delivery penalty clause even though it meant less movement on price — that's where the real protection lives if this supplier underperforms."
Misuse: negotiating hard on unit price while accepting weak failure-scenario terms, misallocating negotiation effort relative to where the actual risk and value live.

**Kraljic matrix (category management)** — A framework segmenting purchasing categories by criticality/supply risk and profit impact/complexity, used to assign a differentiated sourcing strategy (leverage, strategic, bottleneck, non-critical) rather than a uniform approach across all spend.
In use: "This component is a 'strategic' category in our Kraljic matrix — critical and hard to substitute — which is why it gets a different sourcing playbook than a commodity item."
Misuse: applying the same purchasing approach across categories with very different criticality and substitutability, over-investing effort on low-stakes purchases or under-protecting genuinely critical ones.

**Expected value (of a disruption risk)** — The probability of a supply disruption multiplied by its estimated cost if it occurs, used as the basis for comparing a guaranteed cost (e.g., a multi-sourcing premium) against the risk it's meant to address.
In use: "A 5% annual probability of a $9.2M disruption gives an expected cost of $460,000/year — that's the number the diversification premium should be compared against."
Misuse: evaluating a risk-mitigation cost (like dual-sourcing) purely against the worst-case disruption cost rather than its expected value, or conversely, ignoring expected value entirely and treating any premium as automatically justified by "just in case" reasoning.

**Volume pricing threshold** — A supplier's minimum purchase volume required to access a given price tier, a real constraint that a proposed multi-sourcing split needs to check against, since dropping below a threshold can raise the primary supplier's price materially.
In use: "Splitting volume 70/30 drops us below the primary supplier's 450,000-unit discount threshold — that's a real cost of the split, not just the secondary supplier's price."
Misuse: evaluating a multi-sourcing split only on the secondary supplier's price without checking whether the reduced volume with the primary supplier drops it out of its current pricing tier.

**Switching cost** — The cost (time, requalification, disruption) of moving from one supplier to another, a real factor in total cost of ownership that can make an apparently cheaper alternative less attractive once accounted for.
In use: "The alternative supplier's unit price is lower, but the requalification and validation cost of switching adds real expense that needs to be in the comparison."
Misuse: comparing supplier options only on ongoing unit price without accounting for the one-time switching cost of moving between them.

**Win-win negotiation (vs. distributive bargaining)** — A negotiation approach seeking terms that work for both parties' actual constraints and cost structures, as distinct from purely adversarial, zero-sum extraction of maximum concession from the other side.
In use: "Understanding the supplier's actual raw-material cost pressure let us find a term that worked for both of us, rather than just pushing hard on their opening price."
Misuse: treating every supplier negotiation as purely adversarial, potentially winning a one-time concession at the cost of the relationship's durability and future value.

**Hybrid sourcing split** — An intermediate volume allocation between full single-sourcing and an even multi-source split (e.g., 90/10 rather than 50/50 or 70/30), often capturing most of the pricing benefit of concentration while still maintaining a qualified backup supplier.
In use: "A 90/10 split keeps us at the primary supplier's full volume discount while still having a qualified, scalable backup — cheaper than a 70/30 split and safer than pure single-sourcing."
Misuse: treating multi-sourcing as a binary choice (single-source or an even split) without considering intermediate allocations that might capture most of the pricing benefit while still providing meaningful risk mitigation.
