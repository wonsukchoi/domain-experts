# Animal Caretaker — Vocabulary

### Capacity for Care (C4C)
A population-management framework (UC Davis Koret Shelter Medicine Program) that ties daily animal welfare to caretaker-minutes and length of stay rather than to the number of kennels a facility owns.
**In use:** "We're over Capacity for Care today — 42 dogs against a 36-dog full-protocol ceiling, so six stable long-stay dogs get abbreviated care, not everyone getting a rushed version."
**Common misuse:** Cited as justification for quietly lowering the daily-care standard across the whole population. The framework's actual prescription is a documented triage of fewer animals at full quality plus a push to shorten length of stay, not the same headcount served worse.

### FAS (Fear, Anxiety, Stress) scale
A 0–5 behavioral scoring scale (Fear Free) used to log an animal's stress state at each observation, compared against that animal's own intake baseline over time.
**In use:** "Remy's been flat at FAS 3 for two days post-intake — that's not decompressing, that's a flag even without a score increase."
**Common misuse:** Compared only day-to-day instead of against intake baseline, or treated as a one-time intake assessment rather than a running log — a score that never improves is as much a signal as a score that rises.

### Body Condition Score (BCS)
A 1–9 scale (WSAVA Global Nutrition Committee) for assessing an animal's weight/fat coverage by palpation, with 4–5 as the ideal range for most dogs and cats.
**In use:** "He's a BCS 3/9 on intake — ribs and spine easily visible, ration needs to go up, not stay at the standard maintenance amount."
**Common misuse:** Read off a scale without palpation (coat can visually hide or exaggerate true body condition), or used as a single-visit snapshot instead of a trend tracked across weigh-ins.

### Length of stay (LOS)
The number of days an animal remains in a facility from intake to outcome (adoption, transfer, return to owner, or euthanasia); the primary driver of both disease exposure and per-animal cost in a shelter population.
**In use:** "Census has been over capacity for three days straight — that's an LOS problem upstream, not something today's triage plan can fix."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a fixed constraint instead of the actual lever to pull when population exceeds capacity — reducing LOS (faster adoption processing, more transfer partners) is the structural fix; tighter daily triage is only the short-term patch.

### Double-compartment (double-sided) kennel
A housing unit with two connected sections, letting an animal move to the clean side while the soiled side is cleaned instead of being handled or crated during cleaning.
**In use:** "Shift him to the clean side before you hose the other one down — no need to crate him for a five-minute clean."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to be a luxury feature rather than a disease-control and stress-reduction tool — reduces both handling stress and the animal's direct contact with its own waste during cleaning.

### CIRDC (Canine Infectious Respiratory Disease Complex)
The umbrella term for the group of pathogens ("kennel cough" is the common name for one presentation) that spread readily in group-housed dog populations via shared airspace and surfaces.
**In use:** "Three dogs in that room have a new cough within four days of each other — that reads as a CIRDC cohort, not three unrelated colds."
**Common misuse:** Called "just kennel cough" as if it's always mild and self-limiting — presentation ranges from mild to pneumonia, and treating every case as automatically low-risk delays isolating a cohort that's actually spreading.

### Decompression period
The initial 48–72 hours (commonly cited window, varies by animal) after intake during which stress-driven appetite refusal, hiding, or reduced activity is expected and not automatically an escalation trigger.
**In use:** "She's only eaten half her ration for two days, but she's 24 hours post-intake — that's inside the normal decompression window, keep monitoring."
**Common misuse:** Used to excuse ignoring a genuine 24-hour no-food/no-output threshold — decompression explains reduced appetite, not zero intake past the escalation window.

### Enrichment
Structured environmental or activity input (toys, novel scents, out-of-kennel time, training sessions) intended to reduce stress and prevent the behavioral decline that comes from prolonged understimulation.
**In use:** "He's pacing more each day — bump his enrichment schedule before assuming it's a medical issue."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a nice-to-have extra rather than a documented part of the daily protocol — under census pressure, enrichment is often the first thing cut, which is frequently the wrong cut since it's what prevents the stress-driven behaviors that create more work later.

### Spot-clean vs. full clean
Spot-clean removes waste and refreshes bedding/water without a full protocol pass (no health/FAS observation); full clean includes the complete cleaning-plus-observation sequence.
**In use:** "These six are spot-clean only today — full protocol resumes for them tomorrow."
**Common misuse:** Used interchangeably as if "the animal got cleaned" is the whole story — spot-clean deliberately skips the observation component, which is the part that actually catches emerging problems.

### Pelted (matting)
Coat matting so dense and close to the skin that it moves as a solid mass rather than individual hair, often with skin tenting visibly under tension.
**In use:** "This is pelted, not just matted — we're not brushing this out, it's a shave-down."
**Common misuse:** Conflated with ordinary heavy matting that can still be dematted with time and the right tools — the distinction matters because forcing a brush-out on truly pelted coat risks skin tears.

### Zoonotic disease
An illness transmissible between animals and humans (ringworm and certain GI parasites are common shelter/kennel examples), relevant to PPE and handling protocol decisions.
**In use:** "New kitten intake has a suspicious lesion — treat as ringworm-possible and glove up until it's ruled out."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to only matter for exotic or rare pathogens — the routine shelter zoonotic risks (ringworm, some GI parasites) are common enough that PPE protocol should be a default, not a response to a known case.

### Live release rate
The percentage of animals leaving a shelter through a positive outcome (adoption, transfer, return to owner) rather than euthanasia; tracked alongside intake rate to judge whether population is trending toward or away from capacity.
**In use:** "Intake's been running ahead of live release for two weeks — that's the number driving the capacity shortfall, not a staffing gap."
**Common misuse:** Read as a single headline percentage without checking it against the current intake rate — a high live release rate can still coexist with a growing population if intake is rising faster.

### Husbandry
The full set of daily care tasks (feeding, watering, cleaning, exercise, basic health monitoring) required to keep an animal in a housed setting healthy, distinct from medical treatment or behavior training.
**In use:** "Husbandry's covered for the isolation ward — vet still needs to see kennel 6 for the actual treatment plan."
**Common misuse:** Used as a catch-all that blurs into medical or behavioral work — husbandry is the baseline maintenance layer; diagnosing or prescribing treatment is outside it and gets referred, not absorbed into the daily routine.
