# Red flags

Smell tests to catch on a crew sheet, manifest, or trip history before departure or before an irregularity becomes an incident.

### 1. Crew count on the sheet matches a prior day's assignment after a same-flight-number equipment/gauge swap

**Usually means:** the crew sheet carried over from before the swap without anyone recomputing the 121.391 requirement against the new seat count.

**First question:** "What's the actual seat count on today's aircraft, and does the crew sheet reflect a recompute against it, or the original assignment?"

**Data to pull:** today's aircraft tail number and seat configuration, dispatch release crew count, 121.391 tier calculation for that seat count.

### 2. A reassigned or reserve crew member accepted onto the manifest with no independently pulled duty/rest record

**Usually means:** the crew member's own account of their rest, or crew scheduling's dispatch release, was treated as sufficient without the purser checking the underlying duty log.

**First question:** "What time did this crew member's prior duty period actually end, and what does that make their rest as of report time?"

**Data to pull:** duty/rest log for the specific crew member, prior duty period length and end time, report time for today's trip.

### 3. Elapsed rest within roughly 30 minutes of the 121.467 floor with no buffer requested

**Usually means:** the assignment is technically legal today but has zero margin for the next irregular report time or delay — the same crew member's next trip is one small schedule shift away from an actual violation.

**First question:** "If this crew member's report time shifted 15 minutes earlier, would they still be legal?"

**Data to pull:** exact rest-period arithmetic, crew scheduling's standard buffer policy if one exists.

### 4. Pre-flight briefing shortened or skipped citing a short turn time

**Usually means:** the crew is treating the briefing as optional overhead rather than the only coordination mechanism for that day's specific manifest, especially risky when the crew hasn't flown together before.

**First question:** "What in today's manifest — special assistance, security flag, weather — did the crew actually hear before boarding, versus what was assumed to carry over from habit?"

**Data to pull:** briefing log/time if tracked, manifest special-service requests, whether any crew member is new to this aircraft type or this specific team.

### 5. ASAP/irregularity report volume dropping while passenger complaints or incident reports hold flat or rise

**Usually means:** deviations are being absorbed as informal coaching rather than filed through the non-punitive channel, so the leading-indicator data is going quiet right when the lagging numbers say it shouldn't.

**First question:** "What changed in how irregularity reports are received or acted on recently?"

**Data to pull:** ASAP report volume by month, passenger complaint/incident volume over the same period, any recent change in purser turnover or reporting culture.

### 6. Cabin-to-cockpit contact occurring below 10,000 feet for non-essential reasons

**Usually means:** the sterile-cockpit boundary isn't being enforced consistently, either because the purser is over-relaying routine cabin issues or because the crew hasn't internalized which situations qualify for the urgent-contact protocol.

**First question:** "Was this contact one of the designated urgent categories, or a routine matter that should have waited?"

**Data to pull:** cockpit voice/flight-deck log of cabin contacts by phase of flight (where available), the specific reason given for each below-10,000-foot call.

### 7. Same crew member repeatedly working in-charge/purser duties with no override or differential pay logged

**Usually means:** a labor-relations/compliance gap — the contractual override pay tied to purser duties isn't being tracked or triggered, which becomes a grievance liability separate from any operational issue.

**First question:** "Is this crew member logged as working in-charge for pay purposes on the same legs they're functioning as purser?"

**Data to pull:** crew scheduling assignment history, payroll/differential logs, applicable union contract language on purser override pay.

### 8. Door-arming cross-check not verbally confirmed between paired crew members before boarding close

**Usually means:** the cross-check sequence is being treated as implicit rather than a spoken confirmation step, which is exactly the gap that lets a door get armed or disarmed out of sequence.

**First question:** "Did each door pair verbally confirm arm/disarm status with each other, or did each crew member just assume the other completed it?"

**Data to pull:** boarding-close checklist sign-off, any prior irregularity reports involving door-arming sequence.

### 9. Inoperative cabin equipment carried across multiple legs with no MEL/logbook entry

**Usually means:** a discrepancy (broken galley equipment, a jammed bin, a malfunctioning call button) was noted verbally to the next crew but never logged, so it's invisible to maintenance control and to the next purser doing a pre-flight check.

**First question:** "Is this discrepancy in the aircraft's maintenance log, or only known because someone happened to mention it?"

**Data to pull:** cabin discrepancy/maintenance log for the tail number, MEL status for the item in question, how many legs it's been carried unlogged.

### 10. A crew member new to an aircraft type or flying their first trip with this specific team paired without an experienced partner on the adjacent exit

**Usually means:** the crew pairing was built on availability rather than on distributing experience across exits, leaving one exit pair with no one who's run this aircraft's evacuation procedure live before.

**First question:** "Which exits have a crew member who's never worked this aircraft type or this team before, and who's paired with them?"

**Data to pull:** crew assignment sheet with aircraft-type currency and first-trip-together flags, exit/door pairing.
