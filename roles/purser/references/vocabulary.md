# Vocabulary

Terms generalists misuse or flatten when talking about cabin-crew supervision.

### Crew complement

The regulatory minimum number of flight attendants required for a given aircraft's passenger seating capacity under 14 CFR 121.391.
**In use:** "Complement's four on this gauge — the swap to the 189-seat jet bumped it up from three."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a service-level staffing decision ("we want more FAs for a full flight") rather than a hard regulatory floor computed off seat count that doesn't move with workload or passenger load factor.

### Sterile cockpit

The prohibition on non-essential communication with the flight deck below 10,000 feet and during other critical phases of flight, under 14 CFR 121.542.
**In use:** "We're sterile below ten — hold the seatbelt question until we're through it."
**Common misuse:** Treated as an absolute no-contact rule with no exception, rather than a default that has a designated urgent-contact protocol for genuine safety or security concerns.

### Duty period

The full span of time a crew member is required to be available for flight-related duties, from report time to release — distinct from flight time or block time.
**In use:** "Her duty period ran 8 hours yesterday, so she needs 10 hours of rest minimum before this report time."
**Common misuse:** Confused with flight hours or block time; a duty period includes ground time, deadheading, and briefing time, not just time the aircraft was airborne.

### Rest period

The consecutive, uninterrupted time between duty periods a crew member must be given under 14 CFR 121.467 — 10 hours minimum for a prior duty period of 14 hours or less, 12 hours minimum for one over 14 up to 20 hours.
**In use:** "That's nine and a half hours against a ten-hour floor — she's not legal for this report time."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a target that can be shaved by a crew member volunteering to work, when it's a hard regulatory minimum independent of willingness.

### Reserve / standby

A crew member scheduled to be available on short notice for an assignment not known in advance, as opposed to a crew member with a pre-bid trip.
**In use:** "Crew scheduling pulled a reserve to cover the fourth seat after the gauge swap."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to mean "less experienced" — reserve status is about schedule assignment, not seniority or qualification level.

### Seniority bid / PBS (preferential bidding system)

The mechanism by which crew members bid for trips, bases, and positions (including purser/in-charge eligibility) based on seniority, typically governed by the union contract.
**In use:** "He's held enough seniority to hold purser lines for the last three years."
**Common misuse:** Treated as purely a scheduling convenience rather than a contractual eligibility structure that also determines who is authorized to work as purser and at what pay rate.

### ASAP (Aviation Safety Action Program)

A non-punitive, voluntary reporting program under which a crew member's report of a deviation or safety concern is reviewed jointly by the airline, the union, and the FAA, generally without disciplinary consequence.
**In use:** "File that door-arming sequence issue through ASAP, not as a verbal note to the crew."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to be interchangeable with an internal incident report or a supervisor's write-up; ASAP's non-punitive status is specifically what makes crew members willing to report things they'd otherwise omit.

### ASRS (Aviation Safety Reporting System)

The NASA-administered, FAA-funded voluntary safety-reporting system that collects incident and near-miss data across the industry, separate from any individual airline's internal program.
**In use:** "That near-miss pattern is worth an ASRS report too, not just the company's internal ASAP."
**Common misuse:** Conflated with ASAP; ASRS is a cross-industry, third-party system, while ASAP is airline-specific and jointly administered with the union and FAA.

### Deadhead

A crew member traveling as a passenger (or occupying a jump seat) to reposition for a future duty assignment, counted as duty time but not as flight time worked.
**In use:** "She's deadheading back to base after this leg, then goes on rest."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to be unpaid or off-duty travel; deadheading counts toward duty-period limits even though the crew member isn't working the flight.

### Silent review

The standardized individual self-check each crew member performs pre-flight, reviewing their specific exit's evacuation procedure, equipment location, and safety card without discussion.
**In use:** "Everyone do your silent review, then we'll cover today's manifest in the briefing."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a substitute for the live crew briefing; silent review covers standardized, exit-specific knowledge, not that day's manifest-specific details (special-assistance passengers, security flags, weather).

### Door-arming cross-check

The verbal, paired confirmation between two crew members that a door's evacuation slide has been armed (pre-departure) or disarmed (post-arrival), before boarding or deplaning begins.
**In use:** "Cross-check complete, 2L and 2R both armed and confirmed."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to be satisfied by each crew member independently arming their own door; the cross-check requires a spoken confirmation between the pair, not parallel individual action.

### Override / differential pay

Additional compensation, defined in the union contract, paid to a crew member working in the purser/in-charge role above their base flight-attendant rate.
**In use:** "He's working in-charge on this pairing, make sure the override differential is logged."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to be automatic whenever someone is senior on the crew; it's tied to actually being assigned and logged as in-charge for that specific trip, not to seniority alone.

### Line check / IOSA audit

A periodic, structured observation of a live crew's performance against operating procedures — briefing quality, equipment checks, cross-check discipline — conducted by a check airman or as part of an IATA Operational Safety Audit.
**In use:** "We're due for a line check this quarter — make sure the briefing agenda covers everything on the IOSA checklist."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a one-time certification event rather than a recurring external verification that documented procedures are actually being followed on live trips, not just on paper.

### Positive space

A confirmed, reserved seat or jump-seat assignment for a crew member traveling on duty, as opposed to a standby or space-available basis.
**In use:** "Book her positive space on the earlier flight so she makes report time with margin."
**Common misuse:** Confused with deadheading generally; positive space specifically means the seat is guaranteed, which matters when a tight connection is the difference between legal report time and a delay.
