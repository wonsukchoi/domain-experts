### Localization
The process of using exam findings to determine anatomic site of a lesion before generating a differential diagnosis.
**In use:** "Before we talk about what this is, where is it — cord, root, or peripheral nerve?"
**Common misuse:** treated as a formality to fill in after imaging comes back, rather than the step that determines which imaging to order in the first place.

### UMN signs (upper motor neuron)
Spasticity, hyperreflexia, and a Babinski sign, indicating a lesion at or above the anterior horn cell.
**In use:** "The Babinski and clonus put this above the cord, not in the nerve root."
**Common misuse:** used interchangeably with "weakness" generally, when the specific finding pattern is what does the localizing work.

### LMN signs (lower motor neuron)
Flaccid weakness, hyporeflexia, and fasciculations, indicating a lesion at or below the anterior horn cell.
**In use:** "Fasciculations plus atrophy in that distribution is an LMN pattern — think root, plexus, or nerve, not brain."
**Common misuse:** fasciculations alone (without weakness or atrophy) are assumed pathological, when benign fasciculation is common and not itself diagnostic.

### NIHSS (NIH Stroke Scale)
A 15-item, 0–42 point standardized exam scoring stroke severity, used for eligibility decisions and to track change over time.
**In use:** "Repeat the NIHSS at the bedside right before the bolus — don't treat off the triage number."
**Common misuse:** treated as a fixed snapshot from triage rather than something that needs re-scoring immediately before a time-sensitive treatment decision.

### ASPECTS
A 10-point non-contrast CT scoring system for early ischemic change in the MCA territory; higher scores mean less early damage.
**In use:** "ASPECTS 9 with the change confined to the insular ribbon — still a good thrombolysis candidate."
**Common misuse:** confused with infarct volume on later imaging; ASPECTS is specifically about early CT changes, not a general severity index.

### Todd's paralysis
Transient focal weakness following a seizure, typically resolving within 24–48 hours, that can mimic an acute stroke.
**In use:** "Post-ictal Todd's paralysis is on the differential here — check for a witnessed convulsion before assuming stroke."
**Common misuse:** invoked as a default explanation for any post-ictal weakness without confirming a witnessed seizure actually occurred first.

### Dissemination in space and time
The McDonald-criteria requirement that MS lesions be demonstrated at more than one anatomic site and at more than one point in time before the diagnosis is made.
**In use:** "One brainstem episode with matching MRI lesions isn't dissemination in time yet — we need either a second clinical event or new lesions on follow-up imaging."
**Common misuse:** a single MRI showing lesions in multiple locations is assumed to satisfy the "time" requirement too, when a single scan cannot establish temporal dissemination by itself.

### Status epilepticus
Continuous seizure activity for 5 minutes or more, or recurrent seizures without return to baseline between them — a time-based definition, not a fixed-duration observation period.
**In use:** "It's been seizing five minutes — that's status, start the benzodiazepine now."
**Common misuse:** treated as requiring 20–30 minutes of seizure activity (the older definition) before treatment starts, delaying first-line therapy.

### Stroke mimic
A non-vascular condition (seizure with Todd's paralysis, hypoglycemia, migraine with aura, functional neurological disorder) that presents with acute focal neurologic symptoms resembling stroke.
**In use:** "Mimics run 20–30% of stroke-code activations here — when in doubt within the window and no contraindication, we still treat."
**Common misuse:** used as a reason to delay treatment pending certainty, when the asymmetric cost usually favors treating within the window.

### Illness script
A learned pattern of typical presentation, risk factors, and course for a given disease, used for rapid pattern-matching in diagnosis.
**In use:** "This fits the classic GBS script, but check the FVC before you commit to it."
**Common misuse:** treated as sufficient on its own, without checking the case against disconfirming findings or formal diagnostic criteria.

### Xanthochromia
Yellow discoloration of CSF from hemoglobin breakdown products, indicating blood has been present for hours, used to detect SAH after a negative or late CT.
**In use:** "CT was negative at hour 10, so we still need the LP for xanthochromia."
**Common misuse:** assumed to be present immediately after a bleed; it takes several hours to develop, so an LP done too early can be falsely reassuring.

### Papilledema
Optic disc swelling specifically due to elevated intracranial pressure, bilateral by definition.
**In use:** "True papilledema is bilateral — unilateral disc swelling is a different problem, not early papilledema."
**Common misuse:** used loosely for any swollen-appearing disc, including unilateral optic disc edema from local optic neuropathy, which is not papilledema.

### Fasciculation
A visible, involuntary twitch of a motor unit under the skin, reflecting spontaneous discharge of a single anterior horn cell or its axon.
**In use:** "Fasciculations plus weakness plus hyperreflexia in the same limb is the combination that raises concern for ALS, not the fasciculations alone."
**Common misuse:** assumed inherently pathological; benign fasciculation without weakness, atrophy, or UMN signs is common and usually not a disease marker.

### ABCD2 score
A 7-point clinical score (age, blood pressure, clinical features, duration, diabetes) estimating short-term stroke recurrence risk after a TIA.
**In use:** "ABCD2 is 3, but that doesn't change that this gets a same-day MRI and vascular workup regardless."
**Common misuse:** used as a gate for whether a TIA needs urgent workup at all, rather than as a risk-stratification number layered on top of a workup that happens regardless of score.

### El Escorial criteria
Diagnostic criteria for ALS based on evidence of UMN and LMN degeneration across defined body regions, used to assign a certainty level (possible, probable, definite), not a binary diagnosis.
**In use:** "This meets probable El Escorial — UMN and LMN findings in two regions, but we need EMG confirmation in a third before calling it definite."
**Common misuse:** treated as a single yes/no diagnostic test rather than a certainty-tiered framework meant to standardize criteria across research and practice.
