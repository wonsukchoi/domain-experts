# Vocabulary

### dpm/100cm2
Disintegrations per minute per 100 square centimeters — the standard unit for reporting surface contamination, derived from a raw count rate after background subtraction and an efficiency/geometry correction.
**In use:** "That smear corrected out to 1,706 dpm/100cm2 — well over the 1,000 action level, it's not being released as-is."
**Common misuse:** Reporting or comparing raw instrument cpm directly against a dpm/100cm2 action level, skipping the efficiency conversion that makes the two units comparable.

### Removable (smear) contamination
The fraction of surface contamination that transfers to a wipe under standard rubbing pressure, as distinct from the total contamination present on or in the surface.
**In use:** "Smear's clean, but that only clears the removable fraction — fixed contamination still needs its own assessment before release."
**Common misuse:** Treating a passing smear result as clearance for the item's total contamination, including non-removable fixed activity.

### MDA (minimum detectable activity)
The smallest activity level a specific instrument, count time, and background combination can reliably distinguish from zero — a property of the measurement setup, not a fixed instrument specification.
**In use:** "MDA on a 1-minute count is 94 dpm/100cm2 here — plenty of margin under the 1,000 action level, no need to extend the count."
**Common misuse:** Reporting "not detected" as proof of compliance without checking whether the setup's MDA is actually below the action level being checked against.

### Counting efficiency (2π vs 4π geometry)
The fraction of actual disintegrations a detector registers as counts, which depends on the detector, the isotope's emission, and whether the counting geometry is 2π (source on one side of the detector) or 4π (source surrounded).
**In use:** "That's a 2π geometry count, 34% efficiency for this beta-gamma mix — don't reuse that factor for a 4π whole-item count."
**Common misuse:** Applying one instrument's or geometry's efficiency factor to a reading taken with a different instrument or counting geometry.

### ALARA (As Low As Reasonably Achievable)
The principle, and associated administrative program, of keeping occupational radiation dose below the regulatory limit through genuine cost/benefit tradeoffs against real work needs, not an absolute-minimum mandate.
**In use:** "We're not skipping the job — ALARA means optimizing dose for the work that has to happen, not refusing to do it."
**Common misuse:** Treating ALARA as a slogan demanding zero dose regardless of operational need, producing unrealistic stay-time budgets nobody follows in practice.

### TEDE (Total Effective Dose Equivalent)
The summed occupational radiation dose from external exposure and internal intake, the quantity 10 CFR 20.1201 limits to 5 rem (50 mSv) per year for adults.
**In use:** "Her TEDE for the quarter is tracking toward the site's ALARA control level, not the regulatory 5 rem/year limit — that's the number that matters day to day."
**Common misuse:** Confusing the annual regulatory TEDE limit with the much lower internal ALARA administrative control level that actually governs routine work decisions.

### Stay time
The maximum time a worker can remain in a given dose-rate field without exceeding a planned dose budget, calculated from the actual dose rate at the specific work location.
**In use:** "Stay time at the flange is 10 minutes based on the survey map's 150 mrem/hr hot spot, not the RWP's 40 mrem/hr area average."
**Common misuse:** Calculating stay time from a single RWP-listed average dose rate instead of the peak dose rate shown on the current survey map for the specific work location.

### Source check
A quick daily verification that an instrument's response to a known check source falls within an established expected count-rate range, distinct from a full calibration.
**In use:** "Source check passed within the ±20% band this morning — the meter's good to survey with today."
**Common misuse:** Treating a passed source check as equivalent to a full calibration, or skipping the daily check because the instrument was calibrated recently.

### Calibration interval
The fixed period (commonly six months for portable survey instruments per site technical specifications and ANSI N323A practice) after which an instrument must be recalibrated before further survey use.
**In use:** "This meter's calibration lapses next week — get it queued for recal before it goes back out on RCA rounds."
**Common misuse:** Continuing to use and trust survey results from an instrument whose calibration due date has already passed.

### CAM (continuous air monitor)
A fixed instrument that continuously samples and counts airborne particulate radioactivity, alarming when activity exceeds a preset threshold.
**In use:** "CAM alarmed at the west airlock — pull the filter and get an independent count before we call it a false alarm."
**Common misuse:** Dismissing a CAM alarm as noise based on the alarm event alone, without independently counting the filter or grab sample to confirm or rule it out.

### Whole-body count
A direct measurement of internally deposited radioactive material in a person using a whole-body counter, used to assess internal dose from inhalation or ingestion.
**In use:** "Baseline whole-body count is on file before RCA entry — that's what we compare the post-job count against."
**Common misuse:** Assuming external dosimetry (TLD/OSL badge readings) alone captures total dose, when internal intake requires a separate whole-body or bioassay assessment.

### RWP (Radiation Work Permit)
The controlling document for entry into a radiologically controlled area, specifying dose estimates, PPE, stay-time guidance, and survey requirements for a specific job.
**In use:** "RWP's dose estimate assumed the general-area average — the hot spot at the flange needs its own stay-time note added."
**Common misuse:** Treating the RWP's single estimated dose rate as sufficient for stay-time planning without cross-checking the actual survey map for localized hot spots.

### MARSSIM survey
A statistically designed survey, following the Multi-Agency Radiation Survey and Site Investigation Manual, used to demonstrate an area or item meets release criteria for unrestricted use.
**In use:** "This is a MARSSIM final status survey, not a routine RCA smear — the grid spacing and sample count are set by the design, not by judgment on the day."
**Common misuse:** Substituting a routine, informally-spaced survey for a MARSSIM-designed release survey when the release decision actually requires the statistical design.

### Gamma spectroscopy
Analysis of a sample's gamma-ray energy spectrum, typically with an HPGe detector, to identify and quantify specific radionuclides present rather than just gross activity.
**In use:** "Gross count says something's there — spec it to find out whether it's the expected Co-60 or something unexpected."
**Common misuse:** Relying on a gross-count survey instrument to identify which isotope is present, when only spectroscopy actually distinguishes nuclides.
