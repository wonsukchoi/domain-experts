# Facilities management working vocabulary

Terms a facilities manager uses precisely. Format: definition → how a practitioner says it → common misuse.

**Life-cycle cost** — The total cost of an asset over its full expected life — purchase/installation, maintenance, energy use, and eventual replacement — as opposed to upfront purchase price alone, the correct basis for major equipment decisions.
In use: "The life-cycle cost comparison shows replacing now costs $75,200 over 6 years versus $179,500 continuing to repair — the cheaper-looking repair path is actually far more expensive."
Misuse: comparing options on upfront purchase or repair cost alone, missing that a cheaper option today can be far more expensive once maintenance, energy use, and eventual emergency replacement are included.

**Deferred maintenance** — Maintenance work postponed past its recommended timing, framed as debt because the eventual cost (repair, replacement, or consequential damage) compounds the longer it's deferred, rather than staying flat.
In use: "This isn't a discretionary cut — deferred maintenance on this system is compounding at roughly 40%/year in repair cost as it approaches failure."
Misuse: treating deferred maintenance as a one-time budget saving rather than a compounding future cost, especially under short-term budget pressure.

**Life-safety issue** — A facilities issue with genuine risk to human life (fire suppression, egress, structural integrity, code compliance), categorically prioritized above budget-tradeoff logic rather than competing with discretionary spend.
In use: "This blocked fire exit is a life-safety issue — it gets fixed today, it doesn't go into next quarter's budget discussion."
Misuse: treating a life-safety issue as one priority among several competing for the same budget approval process, rather than recognizing it as a different category of decision entirely.

**Reliability-centered maintenance (RCM)** — A maintenance strategy that schedules preventive maintenance intensity based on asset criticality and failure consequence, rather than applying a uniform schedule to every system regardless of importance.
In use: "Under RCM, primary HVAC and life-safety systems get tighter inspection cycles than the low-consequence systems — not everything gets the same maintenance calendar."
Misuse: applying uniform maintenance scheduling across all assets regardless of criticality, either over-maintaining low-consequence systems or under-maintaining high-consequence ones.

**Failure curve (bathtub curve)** — The pattern of failure probability over an asset's life: higher early (infant mortality/installation issues), lower during a stable middle period, then rising again near end of service life — informing when to shift from reactive repair to planned replacement.
In use: "This unit is 3 years past its typical service life — we're on the rising end of the failure curve, which is exactly when repair costs start escalating."
Misuse: assuming a stable, low failure rate continues indefinitely regardless of asset age, missing the point on the curve where repair frequency and cost start rising sharply.

**Space utilization** — The measured actual use of allocated square footage (occupancy data, usage patterns) compared against the space's original allocation rationale, used to identify space that's become a real, ongoing cost without an obvious line-item flag.
In use: "Utilization data shows this floor running at 45% capacity — that's real cost sitting unexamined since the original headcount plan changed."
Misuse: leaving space allocation unreviewed for years on the assumption that the original allocation decision still reflects current need, missing a diffuse but real ongoing cost.

**Redundancy (facilities systems)** — Backup capacity or a secondary system that keeps a critical function operating if the primary system fails, deliberately sized to the actual consequence of that system's failure rather than applied uniformly.
In use: "Emergency power redundancy is justified here given what a full outage would cost — that same investment wouldn't make sense for the break room refrigerator."
Misuse: either building redundancy everywhere regardless of consequence (wasteful) or nowhere regardless of consequence (risky) — the right level of redundancy investment tracks the actual cost of failure for that specific system.

**Contractor qualification (safety-critical trades)** — The elevated vetting process (licensing verification, insurance certificates, safety track record, references) applied specifically to work touching structural, electrical, or fire-suppression systems, distinct from standard vendor price comparison.
In use: "This is fire-suppression work — it goes through the safety-critical contractor qualification process, not the standard three-bid comparison we'd use for landscaping."
Misuse: selecting a safety-critical trades contractor primarily on price, applying the same diligence used for a low-risk vendor decision to work that carries real liability and safety exposure.

**Building automation system (BAS)** — A centralized system monitoring and controlling building systems (HVAC, electrical, lighting) that surfaces anomalies before they become failures, distinct from a maintenance log that only records what already happened.
In use: "The BAS flagged an efficiency drop on this unit two weeks before it would have failed outright — that's the early warning a maintenance log alone wouldn't catch."
Misuse: relying solely on reactive maintenance logs or occupant complaints to detect building system issues, missing the proactive detection a BAS provides before a failure occurs.

**Rush/emergency service premium** — The additional cost charged for urgent, unplanned repair or replacement work (often 1.5-2x standard rates), a real and quantifiable cost of reactive rather than planned maintenance and replacement decisions.
In use: "Two of this year's three repair calls were at the emergency rate — that premium is part of why deferred maintenance costs more than it looks like on paper."
Misuse: comparing planned versus reactive replacement costs without accounting for the rush-rate premium that reactive, emergency work typically carries, understating the true cost of deferral.
