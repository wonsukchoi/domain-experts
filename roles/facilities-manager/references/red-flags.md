# Red flags — what a facilities manager notices instantly

Smell tests with thresholds. Any single one can be innocent; two or three together are a pattern.

### A building system's repair costs rising for 2-3 consecutive years, especially with any emergency/rush-rate service calls
- **Usually means:** the asset is entering the steep end of its failure curve — the "keep repairing" path is likely to cost significantly more than planned replacement once the trend is projected forward.
- **First question:** "What's the repair cost trend over the last 2-3 years, and does it project to more than the cost of planned replacement over a comparable horizon?"
- **Data to pull:** repair cost and frequency history for the asset, asset age against its typical service life, replacement cost quote.

### A life-safety or code-compliance issue sitting in the same budget-approval queue as discretionary/cosmetic requests
- **Usually means:** a genuine safety issue is being treated as budget-negotiable rather than funded first, regardless of the current budget cycle.
- **First question:** "Does this specific issue have a life-safety or code-compliance dimension, and if so, why is it competing with discretionary spend for approval?"
- **Data to pull:** the issue's safety/code classification, current budget approval queue and priority ranking.

### A major building system operating past its typical service life with no replacement plan or budget line
- **Usually means:** the organization is on a path toward reactive, emergency replacement — the most expensive way to replace a system — by default rather than by decision.
- **First question:** "What's this system's age versus typical service life, and is there a planned replacement budget, or are we waiting for it to fail?"
- **Data to pull:** asset age and typical service life, any existing capital replacement plan or budget allocation.

### A safety-critical contractor (electrical, structural, fire suppression) selected primarily on price with no documented licensing/insurance/track-record check
- **Usually means:** the vendor decision may be treating safety-critical work like a standard vendor comparison, missing the elevated diligence category this work requires.
- **First question:** "What's this contractor's licensing, insurance, and safety track record, independent of their bid price?"
- **Data to pull:** contractor licensing verification, insurance certificates, reference checks, and safety incident history if available.

### Space utilization data showing significant underuse (commonly under 50-60% of capacity) that hasn't been reviewed in 2+ years
- **Usually means:** space allocated for a past need is quietly costing the organization money that isn't showing up as an obvious line-item.
- **First question:** "When was this space's utilization last reviewed against its original allocation rationale, and does the rationale still hold?"
- **Data to pull:** occupancy/utilization data for the space, date of last space planning review, current organizational need versus original allocation.

### Preventive maintenance scheduled uniformly across assets regardless of criticality or failure consequence
- **Usually means:** high-consequence systems (life-safety, primary building systems) may be under-maintained relative to their actual risk, while low-consequence systems get the same attention they don't need.
- **First question:** "Does the maintenance schedule differentiate by asset criticality, or is every system on the same cycle?"
- **Data to pull:** current preventive maintenance schedule, asset criticality classification if one exists.

### A single point of failure on a system whose outage would be genuinely catastrophic (data center cooling, emergency power) with no redundancy investment
- **Usually means:** the organization may be under-invested in redundancy exactly where the consequence of failure is most severe.
- **First question:** "If this specific system fails with no backup, what's the actual consequence, and does that match our redundancy investment here?"
- **Data to pull:** system criticality and consequence-of-failure assessment, current redundancy/backup configuration.

### A capital replacement request framed only as "nice to have" or a vague future need, with no life-cycle cost comparison attached
- **Usually means:** the request may be easy for leadership to deprioritize without a explicit cost-of-delay argument, even when the underlying math favors acting now.
- **First question:** "What's the actual life-cycle cost comparison between replacing now and continuing to defer?"
- **Data to pull:** repair cost trend, replacement cost quote, projected cost of continued deferral.
