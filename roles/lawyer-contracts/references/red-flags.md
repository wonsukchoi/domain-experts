# Red flags — what a contracts lawyer notices instantly

Smell tests for reviewing a commercial agreement. Any one can be innocent drafting; two or three together means the other side's paper is doing deliberate work against you. Format per flag: signal, what it usually means, first question, what to pull.

**Unlimited liability is mutual on paper, but every carve-out tracks one side's exposure.**
Usually means: the drafter kept the LoL clause looking balanced while carving out exactly the obligations only *you* are likely to breach (confidentiality, IP infringement, data protection) — mutuality in form, one-way risk in substance.
First question: "For each carve-out, which party realistically triggers it?"
Pull: the LoL clause and the indemnity list side by side; map each carve-out to the party it practically applies to.

**Order of precedence favors documents you haven't read.**
Usually means: the MSA says the SOW/order form/online terms control on conflict — and the online terms are "as updated from time to time," letting the vendor amend the deal unilaterally after signature.
First question: "What's the full document stack, and which one wins on conflict?"
Pull: every document incorporated by reference, including the URL terms as of today — archive a dated copy.

**Auto-renewal with a notice window shorter than 30 days or buried in an exhibit.**
Usually means: the vendor's revenue-retention mechanic. A 60-day-before-anniversary cancellation window with a 12-month auto-renew is a one-day-late trap worth a full year of fees.
First question: "When is the actual last day we can give notice, and who owns that calendar entry?"
Pull: term and termination clauses across MSA and order form (they often conflict); diary the notice deadline before signature.

**"Sole discretion" appearing more than twice.**
Usually means: obligations that read as commitments but are legally optional — acceptance, refunds, SLA credits, renewal pricing all subject to one party's unreviewable judgment.
First question: "Which of our expected benefits survive if they exercise every 'sole discretion' against us?"
Pull: every instance of "sole discretion," "may," and "reserves the right"; rewrite the deal assuming each is exercised adversely.

**Indemnity obligation without a corresponding control-of-defense clause.**
Usually means: you could owe defense costs for litigation you don't control — the indemnified party can run up fees and settle generously on your money.
First question: "If a claim lands tomorrow, who picks counsel and who must consent to settlement?"
Pull: the indemnification procedures section; if there isn't one, that's the finding.

**Definitions that quietly expand mid-document.**
Usually means: "Confidential Information," "Services," or "Deliverables" defined narrowly up front, then broadened by usage ("including, without limitation...") exactly where the obligation bites.
First question: "Does the defined term mean the same thing in the obligation clause as in the definition?"
Pull: every capitalized term used in the liability, indemnity, and IP clauses; check each against its definition.

**Assignment clause blocks you but not them — or is silent on change of control.**
Usually means: in an acquisition, your side needs consent to assign while theirs transfers freely; silence on change of control can trigger a consent requirement (or termination right) at the worst possible moment in a deal.
First question: "What happens to this contract if either party is acquired?"
Pull: assignment clause plus termination triggers; check whether "assignment" is defined to include change of control.

**Acceptance criteria absent, subjective, or tied to payment already made.**
Usually means: for the buyer — you pay whether or not it works; for the vendor — the buyer can withhold payment indefinitely against undefined "satisfaction."
First question: "What objectively testable event releases payment, and who runs the test?"
Pull: SOW acceptance section, payment milestones, and any deemed-acceptance language (or its absence).

**Termination for convenience is one-way, or the wind-down terms don't exist.**
Usually means: the other side can exit cheaply while you're locked in; and with no data-return/transition-assistance clause, a SaaS termination can hold your data hostage during the dispute that caused the exit.
First question: "On the worst-case exit date, what do we get back, in what format, in how many days?"
Pull: termination clause, effects-of-termination section, and data-return/transition obligations; check survival clause covers them.

**Governing law, venue, and dispute mechanism each point somewhere different.**
Usually means: sloppy cut-and-paste — or a deliberate mix (e.g., their home venue + arbitration under rules that favor repeat players) that makes enforcement expensive enough that you won't try.
First question: "If we had to enforce this, what would it actually cost to show up where the contract sends us?"
Pull: governing law, venue/forum, arbitration clause, and jury-waiver provisions as a set; check the notice clause supports service where disputes must be filed.
