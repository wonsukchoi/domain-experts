# Vocabulary

Terms bell-service staff use precisely that generalists flatten into a single word or use loosely. Each: definition, a sentence a practitioner would actually say, and the common misuse.

## Bell stand

The organized station (physical location plus the dispatch/logging function) where bell staff post up, receive assignments, and log cart runs.

**In use:** "Nobody's covering the bell stand right now — that's why the group's bags aren't moving."

**Common misuse:** calling any spot where a bellhop happens to be standing "the bell stand" — the term implies the logging and dispatch function, not just physical presence in the lobby.

## Porterage

A fee specifically for baggage handling, sometimes itemized as its own line item on a folio or invoice, distinct from a general service charge or a discretionary gratuity.

**In use:** "Porterage is already on the group's master bill — you don't need to tip separately unless you want to."

**Common misuse:** treating porterage and tipping as the same thing; on many properties, especially internationally, porterage is a mandatory charge independent of whatever the guest chooses to tip.

## Bell captain

The supervisory role over bell staff scheduling, cart dispatch sequencing, and tip-pool administration — not simply the most senior or longest-tenured bellhop.

**In use:** "Route that through the bell captain — dispatch order during a surge is their call, not something to freelance."

**Common misuse:** assuming "bell captain" is an honorary seniority title with no operational duties, when the role actually owns triage sequencing and pool math that staff income depends on.

## Claim ticket (two-part stub)

A numbered receipt split into two matching halves — one attached to the bag in storage, one given to the guest — the only artifact that proves custody transfer and resolves a later dispute.

**In use:** "No ticket, no release — if the guest can't produce the stub, we match it against the storage log before the bag leaves the room."

**Common misuse:** verbally promising to "keep an eye on" a bag instead of issuing a ticket; a verbal promise doesn't survive a shift change and gives a dispute nothing to check against.

## Chain of custody

The unbroken, documented sequence of who held a bag from the moment it was received to the moment it was delivered or returned.

**In use:** "We can't confirm the item was missing before it reached us — there's a chain-of-custody gap between intake and delivery."

**Common misuse:** assuming a "supervised" storage room automatically counts as chain of custody; a storage room with no sign-in/out log has a documentation gap even if bags are physically watched.

## Group rooming list / bag manifest

The tour or group coordinator's stated guest count and expected bag count, provided ahead of arrival for staffing purposes.

**In use:** "The manifest says 100 bags, but recount at the bus — manifests run short more often than they run over."

**Common misuse:** treating the printed manifest number as ground truth for staffing math instead of a planning estimate that gets confirmed by an actual on-site count.

## Room reveal (orientation)

The brief in-room walkthrough — thermostat, in-room safe, mini-bar, wifi credential, checkout time — delivered while placing the guest's bags.

**In use:** "Don't skip the reveal just because they seem in a hurry — that's usually when the AC issue gets caught before it becomes a complaint."

**Common misuse:** treating it as optional small talk rather than the primary channel for surfacing a day-one problem while it's still cheap to fix.

## Two-person / team lift

A defined technique for moving a bag that exceeds a safe single-person lift weight or has poor grip geometry, using two staff working in coordination.

**In use:** "That one's soft-sided with no real handle and it's pushing 55 pounds — grab someone, team lift."

**Common misuse:** assuming a team lift only applies to obviously oversized items like trunks or golf bags; a mid-sized bag with an off-center load and no handle can require one even though it doesn't look like it.

## Porte-cochère vs. curbside

Porte-cochère is the covered vehicle drop-off structure at a hotel's main entrance, private property under the hotel's control. Curbside is unloading at a public street edge, common at properties without a porte-cochère.

**In use:** "We can hold a cart at the porte-cochère during the rain, but the overflow group is unloading curbside — get someone out there."

**Common misuse:** using the terms interchangeably in operations notes; the distinction matters for weather exposure and who actually controls traffic flow at that unloading point.

## Skycap

The airport-terminal counterpart in the same broad baggage-porter occupational category, working curbside or counter check-in tagging rather than hotel room delivery.

**In use:** "That's a skycap function, not ours — once it's airline-tagged it's under their baggage rules, not the hotel's."

**Common misuse:** assuming hotel bell-service protocols transfer directly to airport baggage handling; skycaps work under airline tagging procedures and federal liability limits that hotel bell service doesn't operate under.

## Tip pooling vs. tip splitting

Pooling combines all tips collected into one fund, redistributed by a stated formula (hours, points). Splitting divides a specific transaction's tip directly among whoever touched that transaction.

**In use:** "We pool by points across the week, not split per-bag — otherwise whoever worked the slow shift free-rides on whoever worked the group arrival."

**Common misuse:** calling an informal end-of-shift cash divide with no consistent formula "pooling" — that's ad hoc splitting, and it's the version most likely to produce disputes over fairness.

## On-call call-in threshold

The property-defined volume trigger — commonly a bag count within a time window — at which an off-duty or on-call staffer is summoned to cover a surge.

**In use:** "We hit the call-in threshold the second that manifest came through — get Marcus on the phone before the buses even arrive."

**Common misuse:** waiting until the floor is visibly overwhelmed to call on-call staff instead of triggering at the pre-defined threshold; by the time it's visibly overwhelmed, the delivery-time target is already blown for guests already waiting.
