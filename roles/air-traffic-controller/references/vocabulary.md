# Vocabulary

Terms generalists conflate that controllers keep sharply separate. Each: definition, how a practitioner actually uses it in a sentence, and the common misuse.

## Radar separation vs. non-radar (procedural) separation

**Radar separation** relies on continuous surveillance data (position, altitude, track) to maintain a distance-based minimum (e.g., 3 or 5 NM). **Non-radar (procedural) separation** relies on pilot position reports, time, and navigation aids when no radar surveillance exists, using larger time- or distance-based minima (e.g., 10 minutes) because there's no independent check on either aircraft's actual position.

**In use:** "We lost the radar feed on that sector — we're procedural now, minimum just went from 5 miles to 10 minutes."

**Common misuse:** treating a brief radar outage as a minor technicality and continuing to think in radar-scope distances instead of switching mentally to the larger procedural minima the situation actually requires.

## Wake-turbulence category vs. radar separation standard

**Wake-turbulence category** (Super/Heavy/Large/Small, or RECAT groups) governs a distance requirement based on the *leading* aircraft's weight and the vortex hazard it generates. The **radar separation standard** (3 or 5 NM) is a generic distance requirement independent of aircraft weight. Whichever number is larger for the specific pairing governs.

**In use:** "Standard's 3, but he's Small behind a Heavy — wake category makes it 6, that's the one that binds."

**Common misuse:** applying the generic radar minimum in a radar environment without checking whether a wake-turbulence pairing requires more, on the assumption that "radar environment" always means "the small number."

## Read-back vs. hear-back

**Read-back** is the pilot repeating an ATC instruction back verbatim. **Hear-back** is the controller actively listening to and verifying that read-back against what was actually issued — read-back without hear-back is a pilot talking to no one who's checking.

**In use:** "He read it back wrong and I caught it on hear-back — reissued before he turned the wrong way."

**Common misuse:** treating "the pilot read something back" as equivalent to "the instruction was verified," when the verification step is the controller's listening, not the pilot's speaking.

## Loss of separation (LOS) vs. proximity event / NMAC

**Loss of separation** is any instance where two aircraft close to less than the prescribed minimum for their environment and category — a defined, reportable threshold breach regardless of how close they actually got beyond that. A **near midair collision (NMAC)** or general proximity event is a broader, outcome-based category (how close aircraft actually came, sometimes including pilot-reported near-misses) that may or may not coincide with a formal separation-standard breach.

**In use:** "That's a loss of separation — 4.2 miles against a 6-mile wake minimum — even though nobody would call it a near miss."

**Common misuse:** assuming "loss of separation" means a dramatic near-collision; a LOS can be a small, non-dramatic shortfall against the specific required minimum and is still a mandatory-report event.

## Reduced Vertical Separation Minimum (RVSM)

The program and airspace designation (effective domestically January 2005) that allows 1,000 ft vertical separation between FL290 and FL410 for properly equipped and certified aircraft, versus the legacy 2,000 ft minimum in that band.

**In use:** "He's not RVSM-approved — keep him out of the 290-to-410 block or give him 2,000 feet instead of 1,000."

**Common misuse:** assuming 1,000 ft vertical separation applies uniformly above FL290 regardless of aircraft equipage; non-RVSM aircraft still require the older 2,000 ft minimum in that altitude band.

## MSAW vs. Conflict Alert (CA)

**MSAW (Minimum Safe Altitude Warning)** is an automated alert that an aircraft's altitude is projected to go below a safe terrain/obstruction clearance. **Conflict Alert (CA)** is a separate automated alert that two aircraft are projected to lose required separation from each other. Both are backstops to controller detection, not primary detection tools.

**In use:** "That was an MSAW, not a CA — he's not converging with anyone, he's descending into the terrain."

**Common misuse:** treating either alert as the detection method itself rather than a backstop, or conflating the two when diagnosing what actually triggered.

## Miles-in-trail (MIT) vs. wake-turbulence separation

**Miles-in-trail** is a traffic-management-imposed spacing restriction between aircraft on a route or into an airport, set for flow/capacity reasons and adjustable by the facility's traffic management unit. **Wake-turbulence separation** is a safety minimum set by aircraft weight-category pairing and is not adjustable for flow reasons.

**In use:** "MIT's 20 today for volume, but he's still getting the wake minimum on top of that if he's Small behind a Heavy."

**Common misuse:** treating a flow-management MIT restriction as if it substitutes for the safety-driven wake-turbulence minimum, or vice versa — they stack, they don't replace each other.

## Handoff vs. point-out

A **handoff** transfers control of an aircraft from one controller to another, who then assumes full responsibility for separation. A **point-out** shares awareness of an aircraft (and sometimes coordinates a specific maneuver) with another controller without transferring control — the originating controller remains responsible.

**In use:** "Point him out to the next sector before the turn — don't hand him off yet, I still need him on this frequency."

**Common misuse:** assuming a point-out transfers separation responsibility the way a handoff does; the receiving controller in a point-out is coordinating, not assuming control.

## TRACON vs. ARTCC vs. Tower

**Tower** controls the runway environment and immediate airport vicinity. **TRACON** (terminal radar approach control) handles arrivals/departures within roughly 30-50 NM and up to about FL230-FL240 of an airport or metro area. **ARTCC** (air route traffic control center, "Center") handles en route traffic at higher altitudes and longer distances between terminal areas.

**In use:** "He's still with Center — won't be our traffic until TRACON gets the handoff about 40 miles out."

**Common misuse:** using "tower" loosely to mean any ATC facility, when the term specifically denotes the airport-surface/immediate-vicinity position, distinct from the radar-approach and en route facilities that hand traffic to it.

## Compression (final approach speed compression)

The narrowing of in-trail spacing that occurs when a faster trailing aircraft and a slower leading aircraft both slow toward their respective final approach speeds at different rates, closing the gap between them as both progress down the approach.

**In use:** "Watch the compression on that pair — he's twenty knots faster right now, that gap's not staying six miles."

**Common misuse:** evaluating in-trail spacing only against the current gap distance instead of projecting the closure that compression will produce by the time both aircraft reach their final approach speed.

## Monitor Alert Parameter (MAP)

The traffic-count threshold, published per sector, above which the sector is considered to be operating at or beyond its planned staffing/complexity capacity — a trigger for a traffic-management response (restriction, sector split, handoff of noncritical traffic), not just a dashboard number.

**In use:** "We're four over MAP on this sector — either split it or start restricting the feed."

**Common misuse:** treating MAP as an informational statistic rather than an actionable threshold that should trigger a specific traffic-management decision once crossed.
