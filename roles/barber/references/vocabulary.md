# Vocabulary

Terms clients and generalists conflate that a barber keeps sharply separate.

## Fade vs. taper
**Fade** blends hair down to skin (zero length) at some point in the cut. **Taper** blends gradually but never reaches skin — there's always visible length remaining, even at the shortest point.
**In use:** "He asked for a taper but pointed at a photo of a skin fade — those aren't the same haircut, so I had him pick."
**Common misuse:** using "fade" to mean any short, blended haircut, including ones that never touch skin.

## Line-up / edge-up vs. fade
**Line-up (edge-up)** is defining the hairline, sideburns, and neckline borders with a razor or trimmer for crisp edges. It's an outline operation, separate from the fade, which is the blend across the sides and back.
**In use:** "The fade's holding fine, he just needs a line-up to clean the edges back up."
**Common misuse:** treating a line-up as part of "getting a fade" — a client can need one without the other.

## Grain
The direction hair naturally grows in a given zone of the scalp — not uniform across the head; whorls and cowlicks change it locally.
**In use:** "The crown grain runs the opposite way from the rest of the head, so I cut that section separately or it tracks."
**Common misuse:** assuming one clipper angle works across the whole head because it worked on most of it.

## Guard number vs. actual length
Clipper guard numbers (0–8) map to a length in inches/millimeters per manufacturer, but the mapping isn't standardized across brands or guard lines.
**In use:** "He's used to a Wahl 2 — that's a hair longer than the Andis 2 we've got, so I went half a step down."
**Common misuse:** assuming "a 2" is the exact same length regardless of brand or whether it's a standard vs. skin-safe guard.

## Blend zone / transition
The width of hair over which one length gradually becomes another — the actual craft variable behind whether a fade looks clean and how long it holds, distinct from blade sharpness.
**In use:** "The line's not the problem, the blend zone's too narrow for how fine his hair is — widen it and it'll hold past a week."
**Common misuse:** attributing a rough-looking fade to a dull blade or bad razor work when the real issue is zone width mismatched to density.

## Shavette vs. straight razor
A **shavette** uses disposable, single-use blades in a razor-shaped handle. A **straight razor** has a fixed blade that requires honing and stropping to maintain an edge.
**In use:** "We're shavette-only in this shop — nobody's stropping a blade between clients."
**Common misuse:** calling a shavette a "straight razor." Most shops moved to shavettes specifically to avoid the bloodborne-pathogen risk of reusing a single edge across clients — they aren't interchangeable terms for the same tool.

## Pseudofolliculitis barbae (PFB) / razor bumps
Ingrown-hair inflammation common after close shaving on curly/coily hair, mechanically distinct from acne or an infected follicle.
**In use:** "He's got a PFB history, so we're not doing a third against-the-grain pass — clipper-only edge instead."
**Common misuse:** treating all post-shave bumps the same and reaching for a product fix when the actual fix is technique — less tension, shaving with the grain, or skipping the razor near the jawline entirely.

## Booth rent vs. commission
**Booth rent** is a flat recurring fee for chair access; the barber keeps 100% of service revenue and tips. **Commission** is a revenue split with the shop, which typically also supplies products and tools.
**In use:** "I'm not at 18 clients a week yet, so commission's still the safer structure — booth rent's a fixed cost whether I'm busy or not."
**Common misuse:** assuming one model is categorically better; the right choice is a function of client volume against the breakeven math, not a fixed preference.

## Chair time / ticket
The billable minutes and revenue tied to one appointment slot — the core capacity constraint of the job, since an empty slot can't be recovered later.
**In use:** "Don't judge me on cuts-per-day against someone doing all fades — I've got two shave appointments eating 40 minutes each today."
**Common misuse:** comparing barbers on raw client count per day without adjusting for service mix, since a shave or detailed fade takes meaningfully longer than a basic trim.

## Consultation vs. execution
The diagnostic conversation before cutting — reading grain, density, and confirming the actual request — as distinct from the technical cutting itself.
**In use:** "That took an extra five minutes because the consult caught a double crown before I touched clippers to it."
**Common misuse:** treating consultation as small talk that delays the "real" work, rather than the step that determines every guard number and blend width that follows.

## Texturizing vs. thinning
**Texturizing** (shears or clippers-over-comb) removes bulk while preserving length and adding movement. **Thinning** removes overall density and, overdone, creates a patchy or see-through look, especially on fine hair.
**In use:** "He's already got low density on top — texturize for movement, don't thin it, or it'll look sparse."
**Common misuse:** using the terms interchangeably, which on fine or thinning hair leads to a visibly thinner-looking result than the client wanted.

## Wet cutting vs. dry cutting
Hair looks and measures differently wet than dry — wavy and curly types especially shrink as they dry. **Wet cutting** to a guard length without accounting for this can leave the dry result shorter than intended; **dry cutting** shows the true final shape immediately.
**In use:** "He's got tight waves — I cut wet a half-guard longer than usual and it landed right once it dried."
**Common misuse:** cutting curly or wavy hair wet to the exact requested guard length and being surprised the dry result reads shorter.
