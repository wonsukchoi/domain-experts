# Working vocabulary — terms a postsecondary art, drama, or music professor uses precisely

Format: definition → how a practitioner says it → common misuse.

**Barrier jury (qualifying jury)** — A required, high-stakes live performance evaluation (typically at the sophomore level) that a music student must pass to continue as a declared performance major, distinct from routine end-of-term juries that simply grade the semester's applied lessons.
In use: "That's her barrier jury, not just the end-of-term jury — a failure here triggers the probation pathway, not a grade."
Misuse: treating every semester's jury as equally consequential; only the designated barrier/qualifying jury carries the continue-or-change-major weight.

**Jury (music)** — A live performance before a panel of faculty, usually at the end of each term of applied study, scored against a rubric to assess semester progress.
In use: "His jury covered the two pieces from this term's lessons, scored on technique, interpretation, and stage presence separately."
Misuse: reading the single jury number as the whole verdict on the student's semester, ignoring the formative lesson-grade record that carries a larger sample.

**Crit (critique)** — A structured session, typically in a studio art or design course, where a student's in-progress or finished work is discussed by faculty and peers against stated project criteria.
In use: "We're running crit as a fixed nine-minute rotation this term, not open floor, so every student gets equal time."
Misuse: assuming any group discussion of student work counts as a crit; the term implies a structured, criteria-anchored session, not informal comments.

**Juried exhibition** — A show whose entries were selected by a panel through blind or semi-blind review against open submissions, as opposed to being curated by direct invitation.
In use: "The Triennial was juried — 340 submissions, 22 accepted — that's the entry doing the tenure-equivalent work of a peer-reviewed publication."
Misuse: calling any exhibition "juried" when it was actually invitational; the distinction is the whole basis of CAA's tenure-weighting standard, and conflating them understates or overstates a dossier.

**Invitational exhibition** — A show where the artist was selected by direct invitation from a curator or gallery, without an open, reviewed submission process.
In use: "That one's invitational — good for visibility, but it doesn't carry the same weight as the juried Triennial entry in the dossier."
Misuse: listing invitational and juried shows together on a CV without marking which is which, so a committee outside the discipline can't weigh the record correctly.

**Terminal degree** — The highest degree conventionally required to teach or hold a tenure-track line in a given discipline; for studio art, theatre, and much of applied/performance music, this is the MFA, not the PhD.
In use: "Her terminal degree is an MFA, so her tenure case runs on exhibition record, not a publication count."
Misuse: assuming every discipline's tenure case should look like a research-publication CV; comparing an MFA-holder's dossier against PhD norms misreads the currency entirely.

**Applied lesson (applied study)** — One-on-one instruction in a specific instrument, voice, or technique, graded each term and often gatekept by a jury, as distinct from a lecture or ensemble course.
In use: "She's in applied voice with the new adjunct this term, two lessons a week plus studio class."
Misuse: treating "applied" as a synonym for "practical" broadly; it specifically denotes the individual-lesson format tied to jury assessment, not group coursework.

**Studio course** — A course built around hands-on production (painting, sculpture, ceramics) rather than lecture, typically carrying a different contact-hour-to-credit ratio (roughly 2–3 studio hours per credit versus 1:1 for lecture) under NASAD conventions.
In use: "Scheduling-wise, that's a studio course — budget three contact hours for the one credit, not one."
Misuse: scheduling or staffing a studio course on the same hour-per-credit assumption as a lecture course, which understaffs the actual contact time.

**Resident faculty** — Full-time faculty physically based in a given applied area or discipline, as distinct from adjuncts who may teach the bulk of the lesson load in that area.
In use: "We have zero resident faculty in classical guitar — that's the line item a NASM site visit will flag."
Misuse: assuming an area is adequately staffed because enrollment is covered, without checking whether any full-time faculty member actually anchors it.

**Sophomore barrier** — The specific structural checkpoint (often but not always coinciding with the barrier jury) at which a music or theatre program formally decides whether a student continues in the performance track.
In use: "He's approaching the sophomore barrier — if the jury doesn't clear 70, we're into the probation pathway."
Misuse: using "barrier" loosely to mean any hard course; it refers to the specific, handbook-defined checkpoint with its own procedural pathway.

**Practice-room log** — A record (sign-in sheet or software) of a student's individual practice or rehearsal time, used as an early-warning signal ahead of a terminal assessment.
In use: "Her log shows five sessions a week all semester — that's not a preparation problem."
Misuse: treating the log as a formality nobody checks; it's often the single fastest way to distinguish a preparation gap from a performance-condition gap after a bad jury.

**Thesis exhibition / thesis production** — The culminating, often public-facing body of work (a gallery show for BFA/MFA art students, a directed or performed production for theatre students) required for degree completion, evaluated by a faculty committee against program-level criteria.
In use: "Her thesis exhibition is scheduled for week 14 — the committee reviews it against the BFA outcomes rubric, not just aesthetic taste."
Misuse: treating the thesis show as a personal celebration event rather than the formal, criteria-anchored assessment it actually is.

**Recital (degree recital)** — A required public performance, typically a full or half program, that fulfills part of a music degree's assessment requirements, distinct from a jury (which is faculty-only and shorter).
In use: "His senior recital is 45 minutes, public, and counts toward the degree separately from this term's jury."
Misuse: conflating a jury and a recital; a jury is an internal faculty assessment, a recital is a public degree requirement, and they're evaluated differently.

**Calibration (panel norming)** — A pre-session process where multiple jurors or crit panelists review the rubric and score a shared reference example together to align their standards before independently grading students.
In use: "We ran calibration on a recorded reference performance before jury week — the spread across the three of us came down to six points."
Misuse: assuming a shared rubric alone produces consistent scoring; without an actual norming session on a reference example, three faculty routinely diverge by 15-plus points on the same performance.
