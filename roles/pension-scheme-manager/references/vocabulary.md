# Vocabulary

### Funded status
The ratio of a pension plan's assets to the present value of its liabilities — the single core health metric of a defined benefit plan, distinct from asset performance alone.
**In use:** "Funded status dropped to 91% this quarter, but most of that was the discount rate, not the assets."
**Common misuse:** Treating funded status as purely a function of investment returns, ignoring that discount rate changes can move it just as much or more without any asset trade occurring.

### Liability-driven investing (LDI)
An investment approach that allocates a portion of plan assets — typically long-duration bonds — specifically to match the interest rate sensitivity of plan liabilities, reducing funded-status volatility from rate moves.
**In use:** "We're extending the LDI sleeve's duration to close the gap with our 14-year liability duration."
**Common misuse:** Treating LDI as simply "more bonds," when the actual design goal is duration matching to the liability, not a generic shift toward fixed income regardless of its rate sensitivity.

### Glide path
A pre-committed, rules-based schedule for shifting allocation from growth assets toward liability-matching assets as a plan's funded status improves.
**In use:** "The glide path calls for a shift to 65% LDI once we cross 90% funded — that trigger just fired."
**Common misuse:** Overriding the glide path reactively based on a single quarter's market move, which defeats its purpose of removing emotional and market-timing decisions from de-risking.

### Duration (liability or bond)
A measure of interest rate sensitivity expressed in years — approximately the percentage change in value for a 100bp change in interest rates; liabilities and bond portfolios both have a duration, and the gap between them is the plan's unhedged rate risk.
**In use:** "Our liability duration is 14, but the bond sleeve is only 8 — that 6-year gap is unhedged rate risk nobody chose deliberately."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a "bond-heavy" allocation is automatically well-hedged against rate risk without checking whether the bonds' duration actually matches the liabilities' duration.

### Surplus volatility
The variability of the funded-status surplus (assets minus liabilities) over time — the liability-relative risk metric, as distinct from the volatility of asset returns measured in isolation.
**In use:** "This manager's absolute returns look great, but they're adding surplus volatility we don't need at our current funded status."
**Common misuse:** Evaluating investment risk only through asset-return volatility, missing that a manager can reduce standalone volatility while still adding funded-status risk if their exposures don't track the liability.

### PBGC / variable-rate premium
The Pension Benefit Guaranty Corporation (in the U.S.) insures defined benefit plans and charges sponsors a variable-rate premium that scales with the plan's underfunding — a real, compounding cost of staying underfunded.
**In use:** "The variable-rate premium on our current underfunding is growing faster than a modest contribution would cost us — that's the case for accelerating."
**Common misuse:** Treating underfunding as a deferred, cost-free choice, when the variable-rate premium is an ongoing, quantifiable penalty for staying below full funding.

### Discount rate (actuarial)
The interest rate used to calculate the present value of future pension liabilities — typically tied to high-quality corporate bond yields — where a lower rate increases the present value of liabilities and a higher rate decreases it.
**In use:** "Rates fell 50bp this quarter, which alone added about $60M to our liability value."
**Common misuse:** Conflating the discount rate assumption with the plan's expected asset return assumption — they serve different purposes and moving one doesn't mechanically move the other.

### De-risking
The deliberate process of reducing a pension plan's investment risk — typically by shifting from growth assets to liability-matching bonds, or transferring liabilities entirely via annuity buyout — usually as funded status improves.
**In use:** "We're de-risking the retiree tranche via annuity buyout now that we're above 105% funded on that segment."
**Common misuse:** Using "de-risking" to describe any allocation change, when it specifically means reducing funded-status volatility relative to liabilities, not just moving toward a generically "safer" asset class.

### QDIA (qualified default investment alternative)
In a defined contribution plan, the default investment fund a participant's contributions go into if they don't make an active election — a regulatory category with specific requirements in the U.S. context.
**In use:** "Over 70% of participants are still in the QDIA — that default is doing far more work than our education materials."
**Common misuse:** Treating the default fund as a minor administrative choice rather than the primary lever determining most participants' actual investment outcomes.

### Sponsor risk
The risk that a plan's sponsoring employer becomes financially unable to make required contributions or fails entirely, distinct from the investment risk within the plan's own assets.
**In use:** "Funded status looks fine on paper, but sponsor risk just increased with that credit downgrade — that changes our risk tolerance."
**Common misuse:** Assessing plan security purely from the funded-status ratio without factoring in whether the sponsor could actually make good on a shortfall if one emerged.

### Actuarial valuation
The periodic (commonly annual) formal assessment of a pension plan's liabilities, funded status, and required contributions, prepared by a qualified actuary using stated assumptions.
**In use:** "The actuarial valuation just came in — funded status at 94.4%, driven mostly by the discount rate change, not asset performance."
**Common misuse:** Treating the actuarial valuation's output as a fixed fact rather than a calculation sensitive to the specific assumptions used, which should be understood and periodically challenged, not just accepted.

### Longevity risk
The risk that plan participants live longer than the mortality assumptions used to calculate liabilities, resulting in benefit payments continuing longer — and costing more — than projected.
**In use:** "Our mortality tables are five years old — given the longevity trend, we're probably understating the liability."
**Common misuse:** Treating longevity risk as a one-time assumption-setting exercise rather than an ongoing risk that needs periodic recalibration against actual plan experience.

### Investment policy statement (IPS)
The governing document setting a pension plan's allocation ranges, risk tolerance, manager selection criteria, and glide path rules — the reference point every allocation decision should trace back to.
**In use:** "This proposed allocation is outside the IPS's stated range — that needs a documented policy exception, not just a vote."
**Common misuse:** Treating the IPS as a static document reviewed only on a fixed calendar cycle, rather than something that should be revisited when sponsor risk or funded status materially changes.

### Contribution holiday
A period where a plan sponsor is not required to make (and chooses not to make) contributions, typically because the plan is well-funded under current assumptions.
**In use:** "We took a contribution holiday last year at 108% funded — worth stress-testing whether that cushion survives a downside scenario."
**Common misuse:** Treating a contribution holiday as risk-free simply because current funded status supports it, without stress-testing how quickly that cushion could erode under a combined market and rate scenario.
