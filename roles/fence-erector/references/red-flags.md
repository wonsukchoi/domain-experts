# Red flags

Smell tests a fence erector catches walking a job site or reviewing a bid, before any hole is dug. Format for each: what it usually means, the first question to ask, and the data to pull before a decision gets made.

## 1. Post embedment specified at a flat 2 ft regardless of location

**Usually means:** the bid or crew habit is using a national-average depth instead of the local frost-depth table — fine in a shallow-frost climate, a guaranteed heave-and-lean callback in a deep-frost one.

**First question:** "What's the local frost depth for this address, and does 2 ft clear it plus a buffer?"

**Data to pull:** county/city building-department footing table or state frost-depth map; compare against the greater of 1/3-of-exposed-height or frost depth + 6 in.

## 2. Existing "boundary" fence with no survey on file

**Usually means:** the current fence marks where a prior owner or installer *believed* the line was, not where the recorded plat puts it — and depending on how long it's stood, it may already have shifted the legal line in either direction under local boundary-by-acquiescence rules.

**First question:** "Do you have a recorded survey showing this fence on the actual property line, or are we assuming it?"

**Data to pull:** deed and recorded plat; if unavailable or ambiguous, a quote from a licensed surveyor before any post goes in on that line.

## 3. Pool-adjacent fence section under 48 in or with any gap over 4 in

**Usually means:** the section was built (or requested) to match the rest of the yard's aesthetic height rather than to the pool-barrier code, or grade has settled and opened a gap that wasn't there at install.

**First question:** "Has this section been measured against the pool code's exact numbers — height, gap, and latch height — independently, not just eyeballed against the rest of the fence?"

**Data to pull:** the adopted local pool-barrier code (ISPSC or IRC Appendix G) checklist; a tape measurement at the lowest point of the section, not the average.

## 4. Gate latch or release mechanism mounted at standard gate-latch height on a pool barrier

**Usually means:** hardware was installed using a general-purpose gate latch instead of a pool-rated one, missing the code-required 54 in release-mechanism height on the pool side.

**First question:** "Is this latch height measured from grade on the pool side, and does it clear 54 in?"

**Data to pull:** measured release-mechanism height; hardware spec sheet confirming it's rated/positioned for pool-barrier use, not a standard yard-gate latch.

## 5. Gate over 4 ft wide hung on two hinges with no diagonal brace

**Usually means:** the frame has nothing resisting rack under its own weight — sag is a matter of when, not if, regardless of hinge quality or price point.

**First question:** "What's resisting the frame racking on this gate — is there a truss rod, diagonal brace, or welded steel frame?"

**Data to pull:** gate width and estimated weight against the hinge/truss-rod sizing table; hinge count and rating currently specified.

## 6. Footing diameter roughly equal to the post's own width

**Usually means:** the hole was augured "just big enough to wrap the post" rather than sized to the ~3x-width rule, leaving little concrete mass to resist overturning load.

**First question:** "What auger bit size was used relative to the post's actual width?"

**Data to pull:** post width, footing diameter as installed or bid, and the site's wind-exposure category if the fence is a solid privacy style.

## 7. Solid privacy-panel fence in an open/coastal exposure with no wind-load check

**Usually means:** the panel is being sized like a picket or lattice fence when it functions as a solid sail — nobody has checked the site's exposure category or design wind speed against post spacing.

**First question:** "What's the design wind speed and exposure category here, and has post spacing or post size been adjusted for it?"

**Data to pull:** local design wind speed, exposure category (B/C/D), panel height and spacing, current post size at corners/ends versus line posts.

## 8. Layout done without string line or batter boards

**Usually means:** posts are being eyeballed into place, which shows up later as a wandering line, inconsistent spacing, or a run that doesn't actually sit parallel to the property line even if the boundary itself is correct.

**First question:** "Was this run laid out with a string line off fixed batter boards, or set by eye post to post?"

**Data to pull:** as-built post spacing measurements versus the plan; deviation from a straight string line at each post.

## 9. Wood posts with no ground-contact treatment rating

**Usually means:** above-ground-rated lumber was used for an in-ground application, which rots at the soil line well before the fence's expected life — a cost-saving substitution that shows up as a failure 3-5 years early.

**First question:** "What AWPA use-category rating is stamped on this lumber, and does it cover ground contact?"

**Data to pull:** lumber tag/stamp confirming UC4B (ground contact) rating; supplier invoice if the tag is missing.

## 10. Fence height or top-rail line varies across a visibly sloped run with no step or rack detail

**Usually means:** panels were forced level against sloped grade by cutting one end short, rather than stepping (open styles) or racking the top rail (solid panels) to follow the slope — the result reads as a construction error even when the underlying posts are sound.

**First question:** "Is this run stepped or racked to the slope, or was panel height just cut down at the low end?"

**Data to pull:** grade survey or simple string-line-and-line-level reading across the run; panel height measured at both ends of each section.
