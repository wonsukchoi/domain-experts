# Vocabulary

Terms generalists conflate that practitioners keep sharply separate. Each: definition, how a practitioner actually uses it in a sentence, and the common misuse.

## Frost line vs. frost heave

**Frost line** (or frost depth) is the maximum depth at which soil moisture is expected to freeze in a given climate, published by local building departments from climate data. **Frost heave** is the physical lifting of soil (and anything shallowly footed in it) as that freezing moisture expands.

**In use:** "The frost line here is 36 in — anything footed shallower than that is going to heave by the second winter, not maybe."

**Common misuse:** treating "frost line" as a nationwide constant (defaulting to "2 feet" everywhere) instead of pulling the actual local number, which can range from near 0 in in the Gulf Coast to 60+ in in the northern Midwest.

## Embedment depth vs. footing depth

**Embedment depth** is how far the post itself extends below grade. **Footing depth** (or footing size) refers to the concrete mass surrounding that embedded portion — usually the same depth as the embedment, but a distinct concept, since footing diameter is sized independently from embedment depth.

**In use:** "Embedment depth is 3.5 ft on this job; footing diameter is 12 in — don't conflate the two when you're pricing concrete."

**Common misuse:** using "footing" to mean only the diameter, or "embedment" to mean only the depth, when a full spec needs both numbers stated separately.

## Racking vs. stepping

**Racking** angles the top rail (and sometimes the whole panel) to follow a slope while keeping panel height constant along the slope — used on solid privacy panels. **Stepping** keeps each panel level but drops it in horizontal steps down a slope, leaving small triangular gaps at the bottom of each panel — used on open styles like picket or chain link.

**In use:** "We're racking the privacy sections since they need to stay solid, but stepping the picket run near the gate — a stepped privacy fence would leave gaps under every panel."

**Common misuse:** stepping a solid privacy fence (creates gaps a raccoon-sized animal or worse can pass through) or racking an open-style fence where stepping was the code-compliant/aesthetic default.

## Boundary line vs. fence line

**Boundary line** is the legally recorded property line per the deed and survey. **Fence line** is wherever a fence physically stands, which may or may not match the boundary line — sometimes by a few inches, sometimes by feet.

**In use:** "The fence line has drifted about 18 in onto the Whitfields' side over three fence generations — we're not rebuilding on it without a fresh survey confirming the actual boundary line."

**Common misuse:** using "fence line" and "boundary line" interchangeably, especially when quoting or scoping a job off an existing fence without confirming it against a survey.

## Boundary by acquiescence

A legal doctrine (state-dependent, statutory periods commonly 7-21 years) under which a long-standing, mutually treated-as-the-line fence can become the enforceable boundary regardless of what the deed says, if neighbors have acted as though it were the line for that period.

**In use:** "That fence has stood unchallenged for 24 years — depending on this state's rule, it may have already become the legal line even though the deed says otherwise. That cuts both ways for our client."

**Common misuse:** assuming a survey always "wins" over an old fence — in states with an acquiescence doctrine, a sufficiently old, mutually respected fence line can override the paper boundary, which is exactly why disputes need a lawyer's read, not just a surveyor's.

## Self-closing vs. self-latching

**Self-closing** means a mechanism (typically a spring hinge) returns the gate to a closed position without human action. **Self-latching** means a separate mechanism engages the latch automatically once the gate reaches the closed position. Pool-barrier code requires both, as two independent, verifiable functions.

**In use:** "The hinge is self-closing, but if the latch doesn't fully engage on its own when the gate reaches the frame, it's not self-latching — that's a code fail even with the spring hinge installed correctly."

**Common misuse:** treating a spring-hinge gate as automatically code-compliant because it swings shut, without verifying the latch itself engages without a manual push or lift.

## Sphere test (4-inch rule)

The pool/child-safety-barrier standard that no opening in the barrier — mesh gap, picket spacing, gap under the bottom rail — may allow a 4 in diameter sphere to pass through, since that dimension approximates the geometry a small child could otherwise squeeze through or use as a handhold.

**In use:** "Picket spacing on the pool section has to pass the 4-inch sphere test — the decorative wide-spaced pickets on the rest of the yard fence don't qualify for that run."

**Common misuse:** applying the 4 in threshold uniformly to a whole property's fencing when it's only mandatory for the pool-barrier-designated section, or eyeballing "close enough to 4 inches" instead of measuring.

## Line post vs. corner/end/gate post

**Line posts** run along a straight section of fence bearing mostly vertical load. **Corner, end, and gate posts** bear additional lateral load — the pull of a tensioned run terminating at that post, or the swing weight and leverage of a gate — and are conventionally sized and footed larger than line posts on the same run.

**In use:** "Line posts are 4x4 on 8 ft spacing, but the gate post and the two end posts are 6x6 with oversized footings — they're carrying load the line posts never see."

**Common misuse:** speccing every post in a run identically for material simplicity, which under-sizes exactly the posts carrying the highest load.

## Truss rod (anti-sag rod/cable)

A diagonal rod or cable run corner-to-corner inside a gate frame, tensioned with a turnbuckle, that resists the frame racking (and therefore sagging) under its own weight — distinct from the hinges, which only carry vertical load and resist swing, not frame rack.

**In use:** "The gate's still sagging after we replaced the hinges twice — it needs a truss rod, not another round of hinge hardware."

**Common misuse:** diagnosing gate sag as a hinge problem and upgrading hinge hardware repeatedly, when the frame itself has no bracing against racking.

## Exposure category (wind)

A classification (B, C, or D under ASCE 7) describing how exposed a site is to wind based on surrounding terrain — B is suburban/wooded with obstructions, C is open terrain, D is unobstructed coastal/large-water-body exposure — used to set the design wind pressure that drives footing and post-spacing decisions for solid panels.

**In use:** "This lot backs onto open farmland with nothing breaking the wind — that's Exposure C, not the B we'd assume for a typical subdivision, so we're tightening post spacing on the privacy run."

**Common misuse:** assuming a flat, suburban-sounding exposure category without checking whether the specific lot backs onto open water, farmland, or a similarly unobstructed area that bumps it to a higher category.

## Ground-contact rated (AWPA UC4B)

A wood-treatment classification under American Wood Protection Association standards indicating lumber is treated for direct, sustained soil contact — distinct from lighter "above-ground" treatment ratings meant for wood that stays dry or only sees incidental moisture.

**In use:** "Check the tag — this needs to be UC4B ground-contact rated since it's going straight into the footing, not the above-ground rating the deck boards use."

**Common misuse:** substituting above-ground-rated lumber for in-ground posts to save cost, which rots at the soil line years before the rest of the fence.
