# Postal Service Clerk — Vocabulary

### Dimensional weight (DIM weight)
A billing weight calculated from a package's cubic size (length × width × height ÷ a fixed divisor) rather than its actual scale weight, applied when the package exceeds a cubic-size threshold.
**In use:** "This box is under the actual weight, but it's over the cubic-size threshold, so we bill DIM weight instead."
**Common misuse:** Assuming DIM weight only matters for "big" packages in a general sense — it's a specific cubic-inch threshold, and a package can look modest in size while still crossing it.

### Zone
A pricing tier based on the distance between the origin and destination processing centers, not straight-line or driving distance.
**In use:** "That zip code resolves to Zone 6, even though it's the neighboring state, because of how the processing centers are routed."
**Common misuse:** Assuming zone correlates directly with geographic proximity — a nearby destination can be a higher zone than a farther one depending on network routing.

### Mailability
Whether an item is permitted to enter the postal network at all, and under what transportation mode (air vs. ground) and packaging conditions.
**In use:** "It's mailable by ground with proper hazmat labeling, but it's not air-mailable."
**Common misuse:** Conflating mailability with legality of ownership — an item can be perfectly legal to own and sell while still being barred from a specific transportation mode.

### Extra services
Add-on services (Certified Mail, Registered Mail, Signature Confirmation, insurance) layered onto a base mail class to add proof, security, or coverage.
**In use:** "The base rate covers delivery — if you want proof someone signed for it, that's a Signature Confirmation extra service on top."
**Common misuse:** Treating "extra services" as interchangeable upsells rather than distinct answers to distinct customer needs (proof of sending vs. proof of receipt vs. value protection).

### Certified Mail
An extra service providing proof of mailing and (optionally) proof of delivery, typically used for legal or official notices where the sender needs documented evidence of sending.
**In use:** "For a lease-termination notice, Certified Mail with Return Receipt is the standard choice."
**Common misuse:** Recommending Certified Mail for high-value item protection — it documents that something was sent, not what it was worth or that it was handled with extra security.

### Registered Mail
An extra service providing a manually tracked chain of custody at every handling point, used for high-value or irreplaceable items, with a substantially higher insurable coverage ceiling than standard insurance.
**In use:** "Given the declared value, this should go Registered, not just insured Priority Mail — it's above the standard insurance ceiling."
**Common misuse:** Defaulting to Registered Mail whenever a customer says "this is important," without checking whether the actual need is value-protection (which Registered addresses) or proof-of-sending (which Certified addresses more cheaply and quickly).

### Included insurance / automatic coverage
A baseline insured-value amount bundled into certain mail classes (e.g., Priority Mail) without an extra fee, up to a fixed dollar ceiling.
**In use:** "Priority Mail includes $100 of coverage automatically — anything above that needs additional insurance purchased separately."
**Common misuse:** Assuming the included coverage matches the customer's stated item value — it's a fixed ceiling regardless of what the customer says the item is worth.

### Nonstandard / oversized pricing
A pricing tier applied to packages exceeding a combined length-plus-girth threshold, typically priced at a premium above the standard weight/zone rate.
**In use:** "At 92 inches combined length and girth, this crosses into nonstandard pricing for that class."
**Common misuse:** Assuming any large package automatically qualifies as oversized — it's a specific combined-dimension threshold, distinct from the DIM-weight cubic-size threshold, and the two calculations don't overlap.

### Hazmat labeling
Required markings/documentation on a package containing a regulated hazardous material (batteries, flammables, aerosols), independent of whether the item is otherwise mailable.
**In use:** "It's ground-mailable, but only with proper hazmat labeling on the outer package — an unlabeled shipment gets refused even if the contents themselves are allowed."
**Common misuse:** Treating "mailable" and "properly labeled" as the same check — an item can be eligible for the network and still get refused at the window for missing required labeling.

### Restricted vs. prohibited
Restricted items are mailable under specific conditions (quantity limits, packaging, transportation mode); prohibited items cannot be mailed under any circumstances.
**In use:** "Dry ice is restricted — small quantities with proper venting are fine — but this particular chemical is outright prohibited, no exceptions."
**Common misuse:** Using "restricted" and "prohibited" interchangeably — a restricted item just needs the right conditions met, while a prohibited item has no path to acceptance.

### Signature Confirmation
An extra service confirming a specific delivery event occurred and capturing a signature, without the legal-notice documentation Certified Mail provides.
**In use:** "If you just want to know someone signed for it, Signature Confirmation is cheaper than Certified Mail and gets you that."
**Common misuse:** Assuming Signature Confirmation provides the same legal proof-of-mailing documentation as Certified Mail — it confirms delivery, not the sender's act of mailing.
