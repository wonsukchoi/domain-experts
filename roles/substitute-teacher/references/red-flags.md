# Red flags — what an experienced substitute notices instantly

Smell tests with thresholds. Any one can be innocent; two or three together is a pattern worth acting on before it compounds through the day.

### Usable plan content covers less than roughly a third of the period

Usually means: the plan is genuinely incomplete or was written for a different day, not that the students will simply finish early — proceeding without a filler plan invites unstructured time, the day's highest-risk window.
First question: "How many minutes does the actual usable content cover, versus the period length?"
Data to pull: the period's bell-schedule length against a real count of the plan's content.

### Plan's stated day or date doesn't match today's calendar

Usually means: the plan was prepared for a different day this week (or last week) and wasn't updated — giving an assessment or activity meant for different content already covered or not-yet-covered.
First question: "Does the office have a record of which plan is actually meant for today?"
Data to pull: the posted weekly schedule, the front office's copy of the absence request.

### No allergy/medical alert sheet found within the first five minutes in the room

Usually means: the folder is incomplete, or the alert sheet is posted somewhere in the room instead of in the folder — either way, this has to be resolved before instruction starts, not discovered during an incident.
First question: "Where does this building keep medical alerts when they're not in the folder?"
Data to pull: office roster with flagged medical conditions for this class period.

### Three or more students independently claim the same "the teacher always lets us..." exception

Usually means: the room is testing a boundary as a group, not reporting an actual standing rule — granting it costs compliance from the whole room, not just the students who asked.
First question: "Can the office or a paraprofessional confirm this is an actual standing exception?"
Data to pull: posted classroom rules/procedures, a quick office confirmation.

### A consequence issued at rung 4–5 with no rung 1–3 attempted and logged first

Usually means: the response escalated straight to a public or administrative consequence without the lower, less disruptive steps — reads as disproportionate to the office, the student, and (if it comes up later) the returning teacher.
First question: "What happened at proximity and the private-redirect stage before this, and is it logged?"
Data to pull: the incident log for that period.

### Handoff note under roughly two sentences, or entirely absent

Usually means: the day's only evidentiary record for the returning teacher doesn't exist — any deviation from plan, any incident, or any unfinished work becomes invisible to the one person who needs to act on it next.
First question: "Could the returning teacher act on this note without asking me a follow-up question?"
Data to pull: the note itself, reread against that test.

### A dispatch-platform job was accepted without viewing grade/subject or an attached plan, and there's a mismatch on arrival

Usually means: the assignment was accepted on schedule availability alone — increases the odds of arriving unprepared for the actual subject or grade band.
First question: "Did I check grade/subject and any attached plan before accepting, or only the date and pay?"
Data to pull: the platform listing as it appeared before acceptance.

### A same-classroom assignment approaches the district's long-term threshold (commonly ~10 consecutive school days) without a scope/pay conversation

Usually means: long-term duties (grading, parent contact, progress reporting) are being absorbed silently at short-term pay and short-term authority.
First question: "Has this crossed into long-term territory per this district's actual policy, and has that been raised with the assigning office?"
Data to pull: the district's short-term/long-term substitute policy and day count so far.

### No seating chart or roster available and the office can't produce one within about ten minutes of the period starting

Usually means: any consequence or incident assigned later in the day will be hard to tie to a specific, verifiable student without an independent record.
First question: "Do I need to build my own numbered seating record before this period starts?"
Data to pull: whatever partial roster or class list exists; a self-made seating note if nothing else is available.

### A fire drill, lockdown, or medical event occurs and the exit route or protocol isn't already known

Usually means: the safety-information read was skipped or incomplete during arrival verification — this is the highest-cost version of skipping that step, discovered at the worst possible moment.
First question: "What is the posted evacuation route and rally point for this specific room?"
Data to pull: the room's posted evacuation map, the folder's emergency-procedure page.

### A filler activity runs past roughly fifteen minutes as open/free time rather than an assigned task

Usually means: a plan gap was filled with the one option that most reliably produces a behavior incident in an unfamiliar room.
First question: "Is there a pre-vetted sponge activity that should be running right now instead?"
Data to pull: the department or grade-level standing filler-activity bank, if the building keeps one.
