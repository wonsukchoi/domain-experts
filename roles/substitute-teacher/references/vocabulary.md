# Working vocabulary — terms a substitute uses precisely

Format: definition → how a practitioner says it → common misuse.

**Short-term vs. long-term substitute** — Short-term covers a single classroom for roughly one to a few days with no continuity of authority; long-term takes over an absent teacher's full duties (grading, parent contact, progress reports) for an extended stretch, usually past a district-defined day count, at a different pay scale.
In use: "This is day 8 in the same room — I should ask the office whether we're close to their long-term cutoff before I keep absorbing grading duties."
Misuse: treating the short-term/long-term line as a vague feeling about how long the job "seems," instead of checking the specific district policy and day count.

**Sub folder / emergency plans** — The teacher-prepared packet of lesson plans, procedures, and safety information a substitute uses in place of any prior relationship with the room.
In use: "The folder's here, but I'm still cross-checking the date on the plan before I teach from it."
Misuse: assuming a folder's presence means its contents are current or correctly labeled for today.

**Sponge activity** — A pre-vetted, content-neutral, independent task (silent reading, a standing review worksheet) used to fill leftover time in a period, distinct from busywork because it's planned for in advance rather than improvised.
In use: "Plan only covers 15 of the 47 minutes — sponge activity for the rest, not free time."
Misuse: calling any filler "a sponge activity," including unstructured free time or a movie, which is exactly what the term exists to rule out.

**Escalation ladder / rungs** — The ordered sequence of behavior responses from least to most restrictive (proximity, non-verbal cue, private redirect, adult backup, office referral).
In use: "Rung 3 didn't land, so I looped in the paraprofessional at rung 4."
Misuse: skipping straight to a high rung for a routine first offense because there's no ongoing relationship to fall back on — the compressed timeline changes when to move up a rung, not whether to start at rung 1.

**Duty of care** — The substitute's legal responsibility for student safety and supervision from the moment they take over the room, independent of familiarity with the class or building.
In use: "I don't know this building's lockdown protocol yet, so that's the first thing I check, before any lesson content."
Misuse: treating duty of care as something that ramps up once the substitute "settles in" — it's in effect from the first minute, which is exactly why safety information is read before lesson content.

**Handoff note** — The specific, time-stamped written record left for the returning teacher describing what happened, what deviated from plan, and any unresolved incident.
In use: "The note names the exact worksheet substituted and the exact time of the incident, not just 'a good day.'"
Misuse: writing a vague summary ("it went fine") that gives the returning teacher nothing to act on.

**Dispatch/absence-management platform** — The software (e.g., Frontline Absence & Substitute Management/Aesop, SmartFindExpress) districts use to post open assignments and let substitutes accept or decline them.
In use: "I check the grade and subject on the listing before I accept, since a late cancellation after accepting counts against me."
Misuse: assuming all platforms treat a decline and a post-acceptance cancellation the same way — most weight the latter far more heavily against future priority.

**No-show / late-cancellation penalty** — A platform or district policy that lowers a substitute's future job priority or eligibility after accepting an assignment and failing to show or cancelling too close to the start time.
In use: "I decline right away if I can't make it work — accepting and cancelling later costs more than never accepting."
Misuse: treating a same-day decline and a post-acceptance no-show as equivalent risks to reputation/priority.

**Accommodation flag** — A day-specific note (from an IEP, 504 plan, or medical alert) telling the substitute what to deliver for a specific student that period, without any authority to modify it.
In use: "The flag says extended time on any timed work today — I deliver that, I don't decide whether it's still needed."
Misuse: treating an accommodation flag as optional or adjustable in the moment based on how the period is going.

**Continuity of instruction** — Keeping the class moving through the material and routines the regular teacher already set up, as opposed to designing or redirecting the curriculum.
In use: "My job today is continuity, not deciding whether this is the right unit to be teaching."
Misuse: equating continuity of instruction with continuity of curriculum design — the substitute executes the existing plan, doesn't revise it.

**Building sub coordinator** — The office staff member (not a department chair or fellow teacher) who owns substitute logistics in a given school: room assignments, folder issues, and who to flag a plan mismatch to.
In use: "I flagged the date mismatch to the sub coordinator, not another math teacher, since she's the one who can confirm what's actually scheduled today."
Misuse: assuming a nearby teacher in the same subject is the right first contact for a logistics or plan problem — that's the coordinator's job, not a peer's.

**Arrival verification** — The substitute's own first-minutes routine of confirming room, roster, safety information, and plan validity before starting any instruction.
In use: "Arrival verification caught the date mismatch before a single student walked in."
Misuse: skipping or compressing this sequence because the assignment is "only one day," which is precisely when an error made in it costs the most.
