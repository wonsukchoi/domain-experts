# Red flags

Smells caught at the bowl or downstream in the chair, with what to ask and what to check before deciding anything.

### Client flinches or pulls away mid-rinse
**Usually means:** water temperature wasn't confirmed on contact and is running too hot for that scalp — especially likely on a freshly processed or first-time client.
**First question:** "What temperature did we start at, and did we confirm it with the client before full saturation?"
**Data to pull:** none from a system — recheck temperature on the inside wrist immediately and drop 5–10°F.

### Lather won't build despite a normal product amount
**Usually means:** hard-water mineral buildup on the hair or the fixtures, not a bad batch of product.
**First question:** "When was a chelating treatment last run on this client, and is there visible scale on the bowl fixtures?"
**Data to pull:** water hardness test strip reading, or the fixture's visible scale if no test kit exists.

### Color grabs unevenly or comes out patchy after a standard pre-color shampoo
**Usually means:** buildup (dry shampoo, chlorine, hard-water minerals) wasn't cleared before color, blocking even uptake — not a formulation or application error by the colorist.
**First question:** "What shampoo and how many cycles were used in prep, and did it pass a squeak test?"
**Data to pull:** the prep handoff note or ticket log for that appointment.

### Perm processes unevenly — strong wave in some sections, weak in others
**Usually means:** product buildup or residual conditioner blocked waving lotion penetration in the weak sections during prep.
**First question:** "Was the hair clarified and squeak-tested before the perm, or did prep skip a cycle to save time?"
**Data to pull:** the shampoo technician's product log or verbal handoff for that client.

### Client reports hair feels dry or frizzy after every visit to this salon specifically
**Usually means:** water is running too hot by default, or double-cycling is happening as a blanket default rather than a condition-based decision.
**First question:** "What's the standard water temperature setting, and is a second cycle automatic or based on a buildup check?"
**Data to pull:** the mixing valve's default setting and a sample of recent prep notes across several clients.

### A color visibly fades faster than the brand's expected timeline
**Usually means:** the first post-color wash happened too soon, at too high a temperature, or with a sulfate-based shampoo instead of a color-safe one.
**First question:** "How many hours after the color service was the first wash, and what shampoo was used?"
**Data to pull:** the color-service timestamp against the first-wash log or handoff note.

### The same technician's prep consistently takes twice the shop's average time
**Usually means:** they're not yet reading the service ticket to select shampoo and cycle count, and are running a single default routine on every client regardless of what's ahead.
**First question:** "Walk me through how you chose the shampoo for the last three clients — by ticket, or the same product every time?"
**Data to pull:** a shadowed prep cycle against the day's ticket list.

### A scalp finding (flaking, redness, tenderness, an unfamiliar bump) surfaces mid-chemical-service instead of before it started
**Usually means:** a handoff communication gap at the bowl, not a rare coincidence — the technician saw it and didn't flag it in time, or didn't check closely enough during draping.
**First question:** "What's the handoff protocol between the shampoo station and the chair, and was anything noted for this client before the service started?"
**Data to pull:** any documented handoff note; if none exists, that absence is the actual finding.

### Visible mineral scale ring around the shampoo bowl fixtures
**Usually means:** the water system is untreated for hardness and is degrading the outcome of every service that runs through that bowl, not just an overdue cleaning.
**First question:** "When was the water softener or filter system last serviced or tested?"
**Data to pull:** maintenance log or a fresh water-hardness test strip reading.

### A technician reports wrist or forearm soreness by early afternoon on a normal shift
**Usually means:** the massage motion is being driven from the wrist instead of the shoulder and hip — a fixable technique issue, not an inherent cost of the job.
**First question:** "Can you show me the motion you're using on the next wash?"
**Data to pull:** none from a system — observe hand and wrist angle during one live wash cycle.
