# Vocabulary

Terms clients and generalists conflate that a shampoo technician keeps sharply separate.

## Clarifying shampoo vs. sulfate-free shampoo
**Clarifying** is a higher-surfactant formula built to strip oil, product, and mineral buildup — meant for occasional use before a chemical service, not daily. **Sulfate-free** describes the surfactant type and is generally gentler and more color-safe, but "sulfate-free" doesn't mean "clarifying"; plenty of sulfate-free formulas are conditioning-heavy and wrong for a pre-color prep.
**In use:** "She's color-treated, so we're sulfate-free day-to-day, but I clarified today because she's got a lightening service on the ticket."
**Common misuse:** treating "sulfate-free" as a synonym for "gentle enough to always use" — a sulfate-free formula can still leave buildup or conditioning agents that block a chemical service.

## Chelating shampoo
A treatment shampoo formulated specifically to bind and remove metal ions (chlorine, copper, hard-water minerals) from the hair shaft — mechanically different from a standard clarifying shampoo, which mainly targets oil and product film.
**In use:** "Lather won't build on her at all — that's not the product, that's mineral buildup, she needs a chelating pass, not more shampoo."
**Common misuse:** reaching for a chelating shampoo as a generic "deep clean" for any buildup, including ones caused by product film rather than minerals, where a standard clarifying formula would do the job with less stripping.

## Squeak test
A tactile check — pulling a damp strand between two fingers after rinsing — used to confirm buildup has actually been cleared before a chemical service, as distinct from judging by how the lather looked or felt.
**In use:** "Lather looked fine on the first pass, but it didn't squeak, so I ran it again before sending her up."
**Common misuse:** relying on visual cleanliness or lather volume alone; hair can look and lather clean while still carrying enough product residue to block even color or perm processing.

## Buildup
Any residue sitting on the hair shaft — styling product, dry shampoo, chlorine, or mineral deposits — that isn't visible to the eye but is detectable by touch (see squeak test) and can interfere with chemical service outcomes.
**In use:** "It's not damage, it's buildup — two chlorinated swims this week, that's why the color's not taking evenly."
**Common misuse:** assuming buildup is only a visual/greasy-looking condition; fine or clean-looking hair can carry buildup invisible to inspection.

## Cuticle open vs. closed
The hair's outer layer lies flat (closed) in a slightly acidic environment and lifts (open) in an alkaline one — the mechanism behind why a cool final rinse helps hold color and shine, and why a hot rinse works against it.
**In use:** "Finish on a cooler rinse for her — she's color-treated, we want that cuticle closed before she walks out."
**Common misuse:** treating rinse temperature as a comfort preference only, rather than a mechanism that affects color retention and shine.

## Porosity
How readily a hair shaft absorbs and releases moisture or chemical product, driven by the cuticle's condition — high-porosity (often damaged/over-processed) hair absorbs and fades faster; low-porosity hair resists penetration and needs more prep time.
**In use:** "She's high-porosity from the last two colors — don't run the water as hot, she'll fade faster than a virgin-hair client would."
**Common misuse:** assuming porosity tracks with hair thickness or curl pattern; it's a condition of the cuticle from chemical/heat history, not a fixed trait of the hair type.

## Virgin hair vs. processed hair
**Virgin hair** has never had a chemical service (color, perm, relaxer). **Processed hair** has had one or more, which changes its porosity, its reaction to heat, and how it should be prepped.
**In use:** "Confirm with the client before you assume — 'virgin' on the ticket sometimes just means no color in a while, not never processed."
**Common misuse:** taking a client's self-report of "virgin hair" at face value without checking porosity or asking about box-dye history, which the client may not think counts as "processed."

## Neutralizing shampoo
A shampoo specifically formulated to stop a perm or relaxer's chemical action and restore the hair's pH after processing — distinct from a standard post-service shampoo and only used in that sequence, never as pre-wash.
**In use:** "Neutralizing shampoo goes on after the rods come out, not before — using it as a regular wash does nothing useful."
**Common misuse:** treating "neutralizing" as a marketing synonym for "gentle" and reaching for it as an everyday shampoo.

## Draping
The towel-and-cape sequence that keeps a client's collar, clothing, and skin dry and protected during the wash — a technique with a correct sequence, not just wrapping a towel around the neck.
**In use:** "Redrape her before the second cycle — the first towel's already soaked through at the collar."
**Common misuse:** treating draping as a one-time step at the start of the service rather than something that needs checking and resetting during a longer double-cycle wash.

## Backwash vs. forward-wash bowl
A **backwash** bowl reclines the client with the neck supported at the bowl's edge, head tilted back. A **forward-wash** setup leans the client forward over a bowl, head down — used in some traditional or barbershop-adjacent setups. The two require different neck support and water-control technique, and aren't interchangeable defaults.
**In use:** "We're forward-wash only in this shop, so watch the collar line — it's a different spray angle than a backwash bowl."
**Common misuse:** assuming water and drape technique transfers directly between the two setups; ear and eye protection, water pressure angle, and neck strain all differ.

## Double cycle
Running the full lather-massage-rinse sequence twice in one wash — a condition-based decision (buildup, chemical service ahead, chlorine exposure), not a default applied to every client.
**In use:** "She swam twice this week and she's got color today, so she's getting a double cycle — not everyone needs it."
**Common misuse:** either skipping a needed second cycle to save time before a chemical service, or running double cycles on every client by habit regardless of whether buildup is actually present.
