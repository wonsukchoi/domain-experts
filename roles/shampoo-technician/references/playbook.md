# Playbook

Filled reference material a shampoo technician actually uses bowl-side — start here rather than guessing per client.

## 1. Shampoo selection by follow-on service (filled matrix)

| Follow-on service | Shampoo | Conditioner? | Cycles |
|---|---|---|---|
| Cut/style only, no chemical service | Standard sulfate-free daily shampoo matched to hair type | Yes | 1 |
| Single-process permanent color | Clarifying (or chelating if hard water/buildup present) | No | 1, or 2 if buildup suspected |
| Lightening/bleach service | Clarifying, product-free | No | 1–2, squeak-test to confirm |
| Perm (cold wave) | Clarifying | No | 1–2, squeak-test to confirm |
| Relaxer | Neutralizing shampoo (post-service only — never pre-relaxer on unprocessed sections) | Light, post-neutralize only | 2–3 post-service until water runs clear |
| Post-color (within 48–72 hrs of a prior color visit) | Sulfate-free, color-safe | Yes, color-safe | 1 |
| Scalp treatment add-on | Gentle/scalp-specific per treatment product instructions | Per product | 1 |

Rule of thumb: **any chemical service ahead removes conditioner from the pre-wash** — conditioning agents coat the shaft and slow or block even chemical penetration, regardless of how "light" the formula claims to be.

## 2. Water temperature guide (filled)

| Situation | Target |
|---|---|
| Standard wash, no chemical service | 98–100°F (comfortably warm, not hot) |
| First-time or elderly client | Start 5°F below standard, confirm verbally, adjust up only if requested |
| Freshly processed/chemically treated scalp | 95–98°F — heat accelerates cuticle opening and color fade |
| Final rinse (any service) | Drop 5–10°F below wash temperature to help close the cuticle before towel-off |

**Check:** confirm on the inside of the client's wrist or the back of the technician's own hand held under running water for 3+ seconds before first scalp contact — a technician's palm, thickened from repeated exposure, under-reads heat.

## 3. Double-cycle decision table (filled)

| Signal present | Second cycle? |
|---|---|
| Chemical service scheduled next | Yes |
| Heavy dry-shampoo use (3+/week reported) | Yes |
| Recent pool/chlorine or salt-water exposure (within ~1 week) | Yes |
| Visible product film or matting | Yes |
| Daily-wash client, fine hair, no chemical service | No — over-strips |
| Squeak test fails after first cycle | Yes, repeat until it passes |

Each standard cycle (lather, massage, rinse) runs approximately 3 minutes; a chelating cycle runs closer to 6 minutes once its mineral-binding dwell time is included. Budget prep time accordingly rather than rushing a needed second cycle to hit a fixed slot — flag the stylist instead (see the handoff template below).

## 4. Chelating-treatment frequency (filled)

- **Every visit** for clients who swim in chlorinated or salt water 2+ times a week.
- **Every 4–6 weeks** for clients on visibly hard water (scale on fixtures, dull lather at normal product dose) regardless of swim habits.
- **Immediately before any lightening or single-process color service** if either of the above applies, even if the last chelating treatment was recent — buildup accumulates between visits, not just between chelating sessions.
- **Never** as a default for every client — chelating shampoos are more stripping than a standard clarifying formula and will over-dry hair with no buildup problem to solve.

## 5. Squeak test (filled technique)

1. After the final rinse of a cycle, take a small strand (crown or nape — buildup collects differently by zone) between thumb and forefinger while still damp.
2. Pull gently along the strand length.
3. **Squeaks** — buildup cleared, hair is ready for the next step.
4. **Feels slick or silent** — buildup remains; repeat the cycle before handing off, especially ahead of a chemical service.

## 6. Ergonomic wash technique (filled)

1. Set the bowl/chair height so the technician's forearms stay roughly parallel to the floor during massage — not reaching down or up.
2. Drive the massage motion from the shoulder and hip shifting weight, not from flexing the wrist repeatedly — the wrist stays in a neutral, unbent position as the anchor point.
3. Alternate lead hand every few minutes on any wash running past the standard 3-minute cycle (double cycles, scalp treatments).
4. Take a full stance reset (shift feet, straighten back) between clients rather than staying in the same bent-forward posture for a full shift.
5. Soreness that shows up by early afternoon on a normal day is a technique signal, not a fitness problem — check hand/wrist angle against steps 1–2 before assuming it's just the job.

## 7. Handoff note template (filled)

```
[Stylist name] — heads up before [client]'s [service]:
[relevant history: buildup source / recent chemical service / water exposure]
Ran [shampoo type], [cycle count] cycle(s), [conditioner: yes/no] —
[squeak-test result / scalp finding, if any].
Prep ran [X] min instead of the usual [Y] min[, wanted you to know before
you plan the rest of your service].
```

The two things that make this template work: naming the exact product/cycle count (not "shampooed her"), and surfacing any time or scalp finding *before* the client is in the chair, not after the stylist notices something's off mid-service.
