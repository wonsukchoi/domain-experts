# Occupational Therapist — Vocabulary

### ADL (Activities of Daily Living)
Basic self-care tasks: feeding, grooming, bathing, dressing, toileting, functional mobility/transfers.
**In use:** "She's independent in ADLs but still needs moderate assist for the shower transfer specifically."
**Common misuse:** Treated as one global independence score instead of a task-by-task profile — a patient can be independent in most ADLs and still unsafe for discharge because of one specific gap.

### IADL (Instrumental Activities of Daily Living)
Higher-level tasks needed for independent community living: cooking, medication management, managing finances, using a phone, home management.
**In use:** "The physical ADL exam is normal, but the IADL cooking-task screen shows a sequencing deficit."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to track with ADL independence — a patient can be fully independent in basic ADLs and still unsafe for independent IADLs due to a cognitive deficit that a physical exam won't catch.

### FIM (Functional Independence Measure)
A standardized 18-item, 7-level scale scoring independence across motor and cognitive tasks, widely used in inpatient rehab to track and justify progress.
**In use:** "FIM motor subscore improved from 45 to 68 over the admission — that's the number that justifies the length of stay to the payer."
**Common misuse:** Scored from a verbal report or a partial demonstration rather than a directly observed trial of the actual task, which inflates the score.

### Barthel Index
A simpler 10-item ADL scale (0–100 scale), commonly used as an alternative to the FIM, particularly in settings without FIM certification/licensing.
**In use:** "Barthel Index of 60 at admission, 90 at discharge — consistent with moving from 'moderately dependent' to 'mildly dependent.'"
**Common misuse:** Compared directly to FIM scores as if interchangeable — the two scales have different item sets and scoring ranges and aren't numerically equivalent.

### Apraxia
An inability to perform learned, purposeful motor tasks despite intact strength, sensation, and comprehension of the task — a planning/execution deficit, not a physical or comprehension deficit.
**In use:** "He can name every tool in front of him and describe how to use a hammer, but can't actually pick it up and use it — that's apraxia, not confusion."
**Common misuse:** Mistaken for lack of understanding or poor effort, since the patient can often describe the task correctly even while failing to execute it.

### Neglect (unilateral/hemispatial)
A failure to attend to one side of space (usually left, following right-hemisphere stroke), not a visual field cut — the patient's eyes work, but attention doesn't register that side.
**In use:** "He's eating only the food on the right half of his plate and denies anything's missing — screen for neglect, not just visual acuity."
**Common misuse:** Confused with a visual field deficit (hemianopia) — the two can co-occur but have different mechanisms and different compensatory strategies.

### Compensatory strategy vs. restorative approach
Compensatory: working around a deficit with an adapted method or external cue, without necessarily improving the underlying impairment. Restorative: attempting to rebuild the underlying function itself.
**In use:** "We used a compensatory cueing strategy for the sequencing deficit rather than a restorative approach, given the timeline to discharge."
**Common misuse:** Treated as though one is always superior — the choice depends on prognosis, timeline, and the specific deficit; a restorative approach attempted with an unrealistic timeline wastes therapy time better spent on compensation.

### Functional Capacity Evaluation (FCE)
A standardized, structured assessment of a patient's physical capacity (lifting, carrying, standing tolerance, etc.) compared against a specific job's physical-demand requirements.
**In use:** "The FCE showed a 25 lb occasional-lift capacity against a job requirement of 35 lbs — that's the restriction to put in the return-to-work letter."
**Common misuse:** Confused with a general fitness or strength assessment — an FCE is only meaningful when compared line-by-line against the actual job's documented physical demands, not used as a standalone fitness score.

### Home modification
A physical change to a patient's living environment (grab bars, ramps, stairlifts, widened doorways) intended to enable safer or more independent function.
**In use:** "The home modification recommendation — grab bar at 38 inches — was based on a measured photo, not the catalog-standard 34-inch height."
**Common misuse:** Specified from a verbal description or a generic standard rather than the patient's actual measured environment, producing equipment that doesn't fit.

### Adaptive equipment
Assistive devices (reachers, long-handled shoehorns, transfer benches, built-up utensil handles) that compensate for a specific functional limitation.
**In use:** "Independent in lower-body dressing once the reacher and long-handled shoehorn were introduced."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a universal fix applied without matching the specific deficit — the wrong device for the specific limitation can create a new safety risk (e.g., a device requiring balance a patient doesn't have).

### Discharge readiness
The point at which a patient's functional status, with whatever supports (equipment, caregiver assistance, modifications) are in place, meets the safety threshold for the planned discharge environment.
**In use:** "Discharge readiness isn't 'as good as possible' — it's 'safe enough for this specific environment, with these specific supports.'"
**Common misuse:** Treated as equivalent to maximum medical improvement or zero remaining deficits — a patient can be discharge-ready with supports in place well before every deficit resolves.

### Minimum clinically important difference (MCID)
The smallest change on an outcome measure that represents a real, patient-noticeable improvement, as opposed to measurement noise or normal score variability.
**In use:** "The 8-point FIM gain exceeds the published MCID, so that's real functional progress, not measurement noise."
**Common misuse:** Ignored in favor of raw score changes — a 2-point change on an instrument with an MCID of 8 is not meaningful progress even though the number moved.

### Prior level of function (PLOF)
The patient's functional baseline before the current injury or illness — the reference point for setting realistic goals and measuring recovery.
**In use:** "Goals are set relative to PLOF — she was independent in all IADLs pre-stroke, so full IADL independence is the realistic target, not just basic ADLs."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to be full independence for every patient by default — an elderly patient who already required some assistance pre-injury has a different, lower realistic target, and setting goals against an assumed-normal baseline sets up a false failure.
