# Red flags

Smell tests a senior tile and stone setter catches in the first ten minutes of a walkthrough or quote review. Format for each: what it usually means, the first question to ask, and the data to pull before anyone signs off.

## 1. Wall or floor tile drums hollow when tapped over more than a few isolated spots

**Usually means:** mortar coverage fell short of the required 95% (wet area or LFT) or 80% (dry standard floor) — usually the wrong trowel size, no back-buttering on a large tile, or mortar that skinned before the tile was set.

**First question:** "What trowel size and technique were used, and was coverage verified by pulling a tile during the install?"

**Data to pull:** install method sheet, notch size and mortar class (ANSI A118 grade) used, any coverage pull-test photos, tile size relative to the 15-in LFT threshold.

## 2. Grout haze or dull etching returns on natural stone after cleaning

**Usually means:** an acidic cleaner or grout-haze remover was used on calcite-based stone (marble, travertine, limestone), or the stone wasn't sealed before grouting and grout pigment or moisture wicked into the pores.

**First question:** "Was the stone sealed before grout day, and what's the pH of the grout-haze remover and everyday cleaner being used?"

**Data to pull:** sealer product and application date, grout type (sanded/unsanded/epoxy), cleaner SDS or label pH, stone mineral classification.

## 3. Visible lippage at tile edges

**Usually means:** substrate out of flatness tolerance for the tile size, or an LFT installed without a leveling clip/wedge system.

**First question:** "What was the measured substrate flatness before setting, and was a leveling system used on this tile size?"

**Data to pull:** straightedge/wedge flatness readings against the applicable tolerance (1/4 in/10 ft standard, 1/8 in/10 ft LFT), leveling-clip spec if used, tile warpage rating (ANSI A137.1).

## 4. Shower installed with no documented flood test

**Usually means:** the membrane was covered with a mortar bed or tile before its watertightness was confirmed — a breach discovered later means demoing finished tile and often drywall to find it.

**First question:** "Where's the flood-test record — date, duration, water depth held?"

**Data to pull:** test log or photo, drain type, waterproofing method used (bonded membrane vs. pan liner), membrane manufacturer's minimum cure time before testing.

## 5. Large-format tile installed with the same trowel and mortar the crew uses on standard tile

**Usually means:** the crew defaulted to habit instead of the LFT-specific coverage requirement — under-coverage that shows up later as a crack under a point load or a hollow-sounding tile.

**First question:** "What coverage percentage was confirmed on this LFT run, and was it back-buttered?"

**Data to pull:** trowel notch size and shape, mortar class (standard vs. improved modified, ANSI A118.4 vs. A118.15), coverage pull-test photos.

## 6. Tile runs continuously over a known substrate control joint, expansion joint, or material transition

**Usually means:** no matching movement joint was carried through the tile field — the tile will eventually crack along the exact line the substrate moves, on its own schedule, not the installer's.

**First question:** "Where does the substrate's control or expansion joint fall relative to this tile field, and is there a matching movement joint in the tile layer?"

**Data to pull:** substrate/structural plans, TCNA EJ171 joint-spacing reference, photos of the joint location relative to the tile layout.

## 7. Drain type and waterproofing method don't match on a shower job

**Usually means:** a clamping/no-hub drain paired with a bonded-membrane assembly (or the reverse) — a common field mismatch that leaves the assembly without a coherent water path to the drain.

**First question:** "What drain type is specified, and which TCNA method — B421-family liner or B415-family bonded membrane — is this shower built to?"

**Data to pull:** drain manufacturer spec sheet, TCNA method number referenced on the job, membrane manufacturer's compatible-drain list.

## 8. Mortar batch has been sitting and gets "loosened back up" with more water

**Usually means:** the batch has passed its open time and started to skin or begin initial set; re-tempering with water restores workability in appearance but not bond strength or coverage transfer.

**First question:** "How long ago was this batch mixed, and has water been added back into it?"

**Data to pull:** mix-time log, mortar bag's stated open time/pot life, ambient temperature and humidity at time of mixing.

## 9. Polished or honed natural stone specified for a wet floor or high-traffic entry with no slip-resistance discussion

**Usually means:** the finish was chosen for appearance without checking dynamic coefficient of friction (DCOF) suitability for a wet-traffic location — a liability and safety issue separate from waterproofing or staining.

**First question:** "What's the DCOF rating for this finish in this location, and is it appropriate for a wet-traffic area?"

**Data to pull:** DCOF test data (ANSI A137.1 reference of ≥0.42 wet DCOF for level interior floor spaces), finish type (honed vs. polished vs. textured).

## 10. Material takeoff for a bonded liquid membrane shower has no line item for reinforcing fabric

**Usually means:** corners, floor-wall transitions, and the drain flange — the first places a liquid membrane fails — were never budgeted or scheduled for reinforcement.

**First question:** "Where's the fabric reinforcement called out at inside corners, the floor-wall transition, and the drain flange?"

**Data to pull:** material takeoff, membrane manufacturer's detail drawings, linear footage of corners/transitions on the actual shower geometry.
