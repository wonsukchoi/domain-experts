# Vocabulary

Terms generalists conflate that practitioners keep sharply separate. Each: definition, how a practitioner actually uses it in a sentence, and the common misuse to avoid.

## Large-format tile (LFT) vs. mosaic

**LFT** is tile with any edge 15 in or longer (TCNA's definition), which triggers tighter substrate flatness, mandatory back-buttering, and a 95% coverage requirement. **Mosaic** is small-format tile, typically under 6 in on a side, usually sheet-mounted, which flexes across many tile/grout breaks and tolerates more substrate variation.

*Practitioner usage:* "It's a 24x48 — that's LFT, so we're back-buttering and holding flatness to an eighth over ten feet, not the standard quarter."

*Common misuse:* treating "large format" as a marketing term rather than the specific 15-in threshold that changes the coverage and flatness requirements — a 12x24 tile still counts as standard, not LFT, but a 16x16 does.

## Lippage

The height difference between the edges of two adjacent, installed tiles — not the flatness of the substrate underneath and not the width of the grout joint between them. ANSI A108.02 caps it at 1/32 in plus tile warpage for standard tile, 1/16 in plus warpage for LFT.

*Practitioner usage:* "We're at 3/64 lippage on this joint against a 1/32-plus-warpage limit — that tile's coming back up before grout."

*Common misuse:* confusing lippage with tile warpage itself (warpage is a property of the tile measured before install, per ANSI A137.1; lippage is the installed result of warpage plus substrate/setting variation) or assuming a wider grout joint fixes it — joint width and edge-height difference are unrelated.

## Mortar coverage / back-buttering

**Coverage** is the percentage of a tile's back surface actually bonded to the substrate through the mortar, verified by pulling a set tile and inspecting the transfer pattern — not the thickness of mortar applied or how full the bucket looks. **Back-buttering** is applying a skim coat of mortar directly to the tile back in addition to the substrate, used to hit coverage targets on LFT or wet-area tile.

*Practitioner usage:* "Pulled a tile at fifty square feet — coverage's only around seventy percent, we need to back-butter the rest of this run to hit ninety-five."

*Common misuse:* assuming a bigger notch trowel alone guarantees higher coverage without checking transfer — ridge height, comb direction, and whether the tile was beaten in to collapse the ridges all matter as much as notch size.

## Bonded waterproofing membrane vs. shower pan liner

A **bonded waterproofing membrane** (sheet or liquid, ANSI A118.10-listed) adheres directly to the substrate and is itself the water barrier for both floor and walls in a single system. A **shower pan liner** is a separate sheet (PVC/CPE) installed under a mortar bed specifically at the shower floor, used with a clamping-ring drain, with the waterproofing decoupled from the finish mortar bed above it.

*Practitioner usage:* "This drain's a clamping ring, so we're doing a liner-and-mud-bed pan, not a bonded membrane — the two methods aren't interchangeable on this drain."

*Common misuse:* treating "waterproofing" as one generic step and picking whichever membrane is on the truck, rather than matching the method to the drain type the plumber actually installed.

## Crack isolation membrane vs. waterproofing membrane

A **crack isolation membrane** prevents a substrate crack from telegraphing up through the tile and grout — it manages movement, not moisture. A **waterproofing membrane** stops water from passing through — it manages moisture, not necessarily movement, unless the product is specifically rated for both. Many products are listed for only one function.

*Practitioner usage:* "That membrane's only rated crack-isolation, not ANSI A118.10 waterproof — we still need a separate waterproofing layer in the shower."

*Common misuse:* assuming any membrane on a shower job is waterproof because it looks like the same rubbery sheet material — check the specific ANSI listing on the data sheet, not the product category.

## Notch trowel size and shape (V-notch vs. square-notch)

**V-notch** trowels (e.g., 1/4x1/4 or 1/4x3/8 in) suit small tile and mosaic where lower mortar volume and easier ridge collapse are sufficient. **Square-notch** trowels (1/2x1/2 in and up) deliver taller, thicker ridges needed to hit the 95% coverage LFT and wet-area installs require.

*Practitioner usage:* "Switch to the half-inch square notch for the wall — the quarter V-notch we used on the floor mosaic won't get us to ninety-five on this LFT."

*Common misuse:* using the same trowel across an entire job regardless of tile size or location, because re-loading a different trowel feels like a slowdown.

## Thin-set mortar classes (A118.1 vs. A118.4 vs. A118.15)

**ANSI A118.1** is standard dry-set cement mortar — the baseline. **A118.4** is modified thin-set with added polymers for better bond and flexibility. **A118.15** is improved modified thin-set, with higher bond strength and deformability, typically specified for LFT and dense, low-absorption porcelain.

*Practitioner usage:* "This porcelain's under half a percent water absorption — we're using the A118.15 improved modified, not the standard A118.4, or bond strength's a gamble."

*Common misuse:* calling every bag "thin-set" as if the classes were interchangeable — a dense porcelain LFT installed with basic A118.1 can fail to achieve adequate bond regardless of coverage percentage.

## Flood test

A test where a shower or wet-area membrane, before being covered by mortar bed or tile, has its drain plugged and is filled with water to a specified depth and held for a minimum duration (commonly 24 hours per plumbing code) to confirm no leakage.

*Practitioner usage:* "Membrane's cured, drain's plugged, we're filling it tonight and checking the level again tomorrow morning before any mud goes down."

*Common misuse:* substituting a quick hose-spray test or a two-hour check for the full flood duration — most slow leaks don't show until the membrane has been under sustained hydrostatic pressure for many hours.

## Movement joint vs. grout joint

A **movement joint** (expansion/control joint, per TCNA EJ171) is a flexible, unfilled-with-rigid-material gap carried through the tile field at structural transitions, sized to absorb substrate movement. A **grout joint** is the narrow gap between tiles filled with grout for appearance and minor tolerance, not designed to absorb structural movement.

*Practitioner usage:* "Grout joints handle tile-to-tile tolerance; the actual control joint from the slab has to come straight up through the tile as a movement joint, not just a wider grout line."

*Common misuse:* assuming a wider grout joint at a slab control joint provides the same relief a true movement joint does — grout is rigid once cured and will crack along with the tile if it's asked to absorb real substrate movement.

## Picture framing (stone)

A staining pattern in natural stone where moisture, adhesive, or grout pigment migrates into the stone at the joint edges, leaving a visible darker or discolored border around each piece — most common on light, porous, or honed calcite-based stone.

*Practitioner usage:* "That travertine's picture-framing along every joint — moisture's wicking through from the gray thin-set underneath, has to come up and get re-set with white non-staining mortar."

*Common misuse:* mistaking picture framing for a grouting-technique problem (uneven grout color) rather than a mortar-chemistry and sealing problem — regrouting alone won't fix moisture already bled into the stone.

## DCOF (dynamic coefficient of friction)

A measured slip-resistance value for a tile surface under wet conditions, tested per ANSI A137.1, with a commonly referenced minimum of 0.42 wet DCOF for level interior floor applications expected to get wet.

*Practitioner usage:* "That polished porcelain's rated at 0.35 wet DCOF — fine for a dry powder room, not for this walk-in shower floor."

*Common misuse:* citing a static coefficient of friction (COF) number from an old spec sheet, or a dry-rated value, as if it applied to the actual wet, in-use condition the tile will face.

## Substrate flatness vs. deflection

**Flatness** is the planar deviation of a substrate's surface measured with a straightedge (1/4 in in 10 ft standard, 1/8 in in 10 ft for LFT) — a setting-quality issue. **Deflection** is how much a structural floor system bends under load (commonly limited to L/360 for standard tile, tighter at L/720 for stone or large-format tile), an engineering/framing issue set long before tile work starts.

*Practitioner usage:* "Flatness we can fix with self-leveler in an afternoon; if this joist span is only rated to L/360 under a stone floor, that's a framing problem, not something a setter corrects with more mortar."

*Common misuse:* treating a bouncy or springy floor as a tile-installation problem to be solved with a thicker mortar bed — excess deflection cracks tile and grout regardless of how well the tile itself was set.
