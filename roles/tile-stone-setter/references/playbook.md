# Playbook

Filled sizing and selection templates a tile and stone setter runs before and during a job. Every figure below is a worked example following common ANSI A108/A118 sections and typical manufacturer specs — always verify the actual product's data sheet and the local jurisdiction's code amendments before ordering material or signing off on a job.

## 1. Mortar coverage vs. trowel size and back-buttering

Method: pick the coverage target from exposure and size first (95% for any wet area, exterior work, or any tile with an edge ≥15 in; 80% for ordinary dry interior floor tile under 15 in), then select trowel notch size and whether back-buttering is mandatory to hit that target.

| Application | Coverage required (ANSI A108.5 / A108.02) | Typical trowel | Back-buttering |
|---|---|---|---|
| Dry interior floor, tile < 15 in edge | 80% min | 1/4x1/4 or 1/4x3/8 in V-notch | Optional, improves coverage |
| Wet area (shower wall/floor, tub surround), any tile size | 95% min | 1/2x1/2 in square notch (up to 3/4x3/4 for very large tile) | Mandatory |
| Exterior, any tile size | 95% min | 1/2x1/2 in square notch | Mandatory |
| Any tile with an edge ≥15 in (LFT), any location | 95% min | 1/2x1/2 or 3/4x3/4 in square notch | Mandatory |

**Filled example — 24x48 in porcelain wall tile (8 sq ft/tile), wet area:**

| Trowel/technique | Typical coverage | Void area/tile | Void size risk |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1/4x1/4 in V-notch, no back-butter | 65–75% | ≈2.0–2.8 sq ft | Individual voids can exceed 2-in-across / within-2-in-of-corner limits |
| 1/2x1/2 in square notch + back-butter | ≈95% | ≈0.4 sq ft | Distributed small enough to clear both limits |

**Coverage verification (every job, not spot-checked at the end):** pull a freshly set tile every 50–100 sq ft, inspect the mortar transfer pattern on both the tile back and substrate, and photograph it. A pattern showing ridges that never collapsed into full contact means the mortar skinned before setting or the tile wasn't beaten in — correct technique on the next tile, not at grout day.

## 2. Shower waterproofing method selection and membrane volume

Method: match the method family to the drain type before anything else, then size membrane volume and reinforcing fabric to the actual surface area.

| Drain type | Method family | Waterproofing layer | Slope location |
|---|---|---|---|
| Clamping ring / no-hub drain | Traditional pan liner (TCNA B421-family) | Sheet membrane (PVC/CPE) under a mortar bed, pre-sloped | Pre-slope mortar bed under the liner, finish mortar bed above it |
| Bonded flange or linear drain | Bonded membrane (TCNA B415-family) | Sheet (fabric-reinforced polyethylene) or liquid-applied ANSI A118.10 membrane, bonded directly to substrate | Sloped substrate or mortar bed under the membrane itself |

Fitting a clamping-drain liner into a bonded-membrane assembly (or the reverse) is a common field mismatch — verify drain spec against method before backer board goes final.

**Filled example — 42 in x 60 in curbless shower, 84 in wall height, linear drain (bonded membrane method):**

- Wall area = perimeter (42 + 60 + 60 = 162 in = 13.5 ft) x 7 ft = 94.5 sq ft.
- Floor area = 42 in x 60 in = 17.5 sq ft.
- Total surface = 112 sq ft.
- Liquid membrane, two coats required for ANSI A118.10 dry-film thickness: coverage ≈100 sq ft/gal per coat → ≈50 sq ft/gal for the full two-coat system.
- Volume required = 112 ÷ 50 ≈ 2.24 gal → order a 3-gallon pail (next standard size up from 1 gal).
- Reinforcing fabric: 2 inside corners (2 x 7 ft = 14 lf) + floor-wall transition on 3 enclosed sides (13.5 lf) + drain flange (call it 2.5 lf) ≈ 30 lf at ≈$0.60/lf ≈ $18.

**Flood test (mandatory, every wet-area membrane):** plug the drain, fill to within 2 in of the threshold/curb height (or per local plumbing code), hold a minimum of 24 hours, inspect for any drop in water level or visible leakage below. Do not cover with mortar bed or tile until the test passes — a membrane that looks dry two hours after cure is not evidence it holds water.

## 3. Lippage tolerance and leveling-clip spacing

Method: lippage limit is 1/32 in plus the tile's measured warpage (ANSI A137.1) for tile under 15 in on any edge, and 1/16 in plus warpage for LFT (any edge ≥15 in). Measure with a straightedge no shorter than 24 in and a calibrated wedge across the joint.

| Tile size | Lippage limit | Leveling system default |
|---|---|---|
| Under 15 in (any edge) | 1/32 in + warpage | Optional unless substrate flatness is marginal |
| 15 in or larger (LFT) | 1/16 in + warpage | Leveling clip/wedge system, standard practice |

**Filled example — 24x48 in wall tile, 94.5 sq ft of wall:**

- At this tile size, joint spacing runs roughly one clip per linear foot of joint on the long edges plus one per short edge — for a 94.5 sq ft wall field of 2x4 ft tiles, that works out to approximately 120 clips.
- Clip cost ≈$0.25 each → ≈$30 material, plus minor per-tile labor to seat and later snap the tabs.
- Substrate deviation check: a 1/8 in deviation over the 48 in tile length is inside the general 1/4-in-in-10-ft tolerance but can still produce lippage over the 1/16 in LFT limit at a butt joint if no clip system is used — clips are cheap insurance at this tile length, not an upsell.

## 4. Substrate flatness tolerance

| Tile category | Flatness tolerance (ANSI A108.02) |
|---|---|
| Standard tile (all edges < 15 in) | 1/4 in in 10 ft, no more than 1/16 in in 12 in from the required plane |
| LFT (any edge ≥15 in) | 1/8 in in 10 ft (tighter — the tile has no flex to absorb the difference across its rigid span) |

**Verification method:** lay a 10-ft straightedge across the substrate in multiple directions (including diagonally in problem areas), measure the gap at the low points with a wedge or feeler gauge. Where the gap exceeds tolerance, correct with self-leveling underlayment or an additional mortar-bed layer before spreading any thin-set — a heavier mortar-bed skim coat under the tile does not substitute for a corrected substrate plane, since coverage and bond both suffer when mortar thickness varies unevenly across the field to compensate.

## 5. Natural stone setting and grout chemistry

Method: classify the stone by mineral base and finish before selecting mortar color and grout type — this determines staining and etching risk independent of the tile-setting method otherwise used.

| Stone type | Staining/etching risk | Setting mortar | Grout |
|---|---|---|---|
| Dark/dense granite, slate | Low — verify against supplier data sheet | Standard gray thin-set generally acceptable | Sanded or unsanded per joint width |
| Marble, travertine, limestone (calcite-based) | High — moisture bleed-through and acid etching | White non-staining thin-set | Unsanded or epoxy (non-acidic); never acidic haze removers |
| Polished/honed light stone, agglomerates | High — bleed-through visible as picture-framing at joints | White non-staining thin-set or membrane-decoupled setting bed | Unsanded or epoxy; seal before grouting |

**Filled example — 2x2 in honed marble mosaic, 17.5 sq ft shower floor:**

- Seal the mosaic sheet (penetrating sealer, per manufacturer dwell time) before grout is applied — sealing after grouting doesn't prevent grout haze from etching into already-exposed pores.
- Set with white non-staining thin-set rated for the stone type, not the job's default gray thin-set used on the porcelain walls.
- Grout with an unsanded or epoxy grout; avoid acidic grout-haze removers on the cleanup pass — a neutral-pH cleaner formulated for natural stone instead.
- Re-seal after grout cure per the sealer manufacturer's schedule (commonly annually for a shower floor under regular water exposure).
