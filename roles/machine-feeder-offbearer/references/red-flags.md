# Red flags

Smell tests a feeder catches on a walk-down, a changeover, or a pace conversation. Format for each: what it usually means, the first question to ask, and the data to pull before deciding whether to hold the station or escalate.

## 1. Measured distance from a two-hand control to the point of operation falls below Ds = 63 × T

**Usually means:** a station rearrangement, tote move, or control relocation has shrunk the safeguard's engineered margin below the machine's actual stopping-time requirement — the buttons still work, the timing protection doesn't.

**First question:** "What's the current stop-time monitor reading, and what distance does that require — not what distance is actually installed?"

**Data to pull:** latest stop-time monitor reading, computed Ds, physically measured installed distance.

## 2. Manual feed-and-clear time consistently exceeds the machine's cycle time (e.g., 4.1 sec measured against a 3.0 sec cycle)

**Usually means:** a tooling, stock-gauge, or layout change increased the feed task's actual duration, and the station is now capacity-limited by the feed task, not by pace.

**First question:** "What's the 10-cycle average feed-and-clear time right now, under current stock and tooling — not the rate we used to hit?"

**Data to pull:** feed-time study (10-cycle average), nameplate stroke rate, date and nature of the most recent stock/tooling change.

## 3. A stop-time monitor reading trends upward across successive checks, even while still under the regulatory ceiling (e.g., 0.24 → 0.30 sec)

**Usually means:** brake wear is progressing and the Ds requirement is quietly increasing — a distance that was adequate at the prior reading may no longer be.

**First question:** "Has Ds been recomputed against this reading, or are we still using the number from the last check?"

**Data to pull:** last three stop-time monitor readings, date of last Ds recalculation, current installed distance.

## 4. A feeder reports a light curtain or two-hand control "gets in the way," or it's been muted, taped, or bypassed

**Usually means:** either a genuine nuisance-trip/ergonomic problem that needs a placement or process fix, or an active safeguard defeat in progress — both require immediate attention, neither is a productivity workaround.

**First question:** "Show me exactly what happens when it trips — is it tripping on debris/reach, or is someone disabling it to keep pace?"

**Data to pull:** trip-log frequency and cause, direct observation of the station in operation, any device settings changed from spec.

## 5. Parts are reached for or grabbed before full ejection or visible clearance from the point of operation

**Usually means:** pace pressure or habit has normalized treating a part as "basically clear" before the cycle has actually completed — the single most common precursor to a caught-hand event at a discharge point.

**First question:** "Is the part fully clear of the die/point of operation before any hand moves toward it, every cycle, not just usually?"

**Data to pull:** direct cycle observation, near-miss/first-aid log for that station, current cycle time vs. feed pace.

## 6. A tooling, stock-gauge, or layout changeover occurred without re-verifying Ds or re-timing the feed cycle

**Usually means:** the prior clearance and pace were assumed to carry over, when either or both may no longer hold under the new configuration.

**First question:** "Has anyone re-measured distance and re-timed the feed cycle since this changeover, or are we running on the old numbers?"

**Data to pull:** changeover date/log, most recent Ds recomputation date, most recent feed-time study date.

## 7. Feed rate runs faster than the machine's cycle time, causing material pile-up or jams at the infeed

**Usually means:** the feeder or an automated feed mechanism is outrunning the machine's actual cycle, creating a jam that then requires manual clearing — a second point-of-operation exposure caused by the first mismatch.

**First question:** "Is material arriving faster than the machine can take it, and is someone clearing jams by reaching into the point of operation to do it?"

**Data to pull:** measured feed rate vs. cycle time, jam-clearing frequency and method, whether jam clearing follows a lockout or reach-in procedure.

## 8. A fixed barrier guard's opening dimension exceeds the OSHA table maximum for its standoff distance

**Usually means:** the guard is undersized for its distance from the hazard — a finger or hand can reach through an opening the standard intended to make impossible at that distance.

**First question:** "What's the opening size at this standoff distance, measured against the table, not against how it looks?"

**Data to pull:** measured opening dimension, measured standoff distance, applicable table row.

## 9. Pace or production pressure is communicated as "just keep up with the machine" without addressing a known feed-time-vs-cycle-time gap

**Usually means:** a capacity limit already identified in a feed-time study is being pushed onto the feeder to close by hand speed, which is exactly the pressure that produces reach-before-clear behavior and safeguard workarounds.

**First question:** "Has this gap been reported as a layout/tooling issue, or is the expectation that the feeder just goes faster?"

**Data to pull:** feed-time study results, any escalation record for the gap, current production directive.

## 10. A hand-feeding tool (push stick, tongs, vacuum or magnetic lifter) specified for a station is missing, damaged, or not being used

**Usually means:** the operation's point-of-operation exposure was engineered around the assumption that tool use keeps hands out of the hazard zone — without the tool, that assumption no longer holds even though nothing else changed.

**First question:** "Is the specified hand-feeding tool present, functional, and actually being used on this station right now?"

**Data to pull:** tool inspection/condition, station's safeguarding spec sheet, direct observation of current practice.

## 11. A safeguard device was swapped for a different model or had its sensitivity/resolution setting changed without recomputing Ds or Dpf

**Usually means:** the new device's certified response time or object-sensitivity resolution differs from the one the original clearance was calculated against, silently changing the required minimum distance.

**First question:** "Was Ds recalculated for this specific device's rated response time and resolution, or was the old clearance just assumed to still apply?"

**Data to pull:** device documentation (response time, resolution/Dpf rating), date of device swap or setting change, current Ds calculation on file.
