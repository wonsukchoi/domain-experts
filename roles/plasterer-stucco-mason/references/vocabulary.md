# Vocabulary

Terms generalists conflate that a plasterer keeps sharply separate, because the difference changes what happens behind the wall.

## Scratch coat vs. brown coat vs. finish coat

**Scratch coat** is the first Portland-cement coat applied over lath, scored ("scratched") horizontally to key the brown coat into it. **Brown coat** is the second, leveling coat that establishes the final plane of the wall. **Finish coat** is the thin, decorative, colored top layer — texture and color live here, structure does not.

*In use:* "The cracking is in the brown coat, not the finish — recoloring the finish won't fix it, we need to know why the brown coat moved."

**Common misuse:** treating all three as "the stucco" interchangeably, so a structural problem in the brown coat gets diagnosed and priced as a cosmetic finish-coat fix.

## Three-coat system vs. two-coat (one-coat) system

**Three-coat** (scratch/brown/finish) is required over wood-based sheathing and lath per ASTM C926/C1063. **Two-coat** systems (sometimes marketed as "one-coat" proprietary systems, which are actually a base coat plus finish) are permitted over masonry or concrete backup where the substrate itself provides the rigidity the lath assembly otherwise supplies.

*In use:* "This is CMU backup, so a two-coat system is code-compliant here — don't quote it like the wood-frame elevation next door."

**Common misuse:** assuming "one-coat stucco" products eliminate the base-coat step entirely; they combine scratch/brown into one application but still require a separate finish coat and still carry a cure-time requirement before that finish goes on.

## Control joint vs. expansion joint vs. cold joint

**Control joint** is a planned weakened plane (tooled reveal with a joint accessory) sized from panel area/dimension/aspect-ratio limits, giving shrinkage cracking a place to happen on purpose. **Expansion joint** (in stucco, more precisely a "casing bead" or "control-joint accessory at dissimilar materials") separates the plaster from a different substrate or building element that moves at a different rate. **Cold joint** is an unintended seam where fresh plaster met plaster that had already begun to set, with no planned reinforcement across it.

*In use:* "That line is a cold joint from where the crew broke for lunch mid-panel — it's not doing the job a real control joint would."

**Common misuse:** calling every visible line in a stucco field a "control joint" regardless of whether it was placed by the panel math or is an accident of application sequencing.

## Weep screed vs. casing bead

**Weep screed** is the accessory at the base of a stucco wall that terminates the WRB/lath assembly and provides a drainage path for any water that gets behind the plaster to exit at the bottom. **Casing bead** is a trim accessory used at the edges of a stucco field (around openings, at dissimilar materials) to give a clean, reinforced termination — it doesn't drain anything.

*In use:* "Don't confuse the two — the casing bead around that window just gives us a clean edge; the weep screed at the base is the one doing drainage work."

**Common misuse:** treating any metal trim accessory at the base of a wall as functionally the same, when only a properly elevated weep screed actually manages water exiting the assembly.

## Water-resistive barrier (WRB) vs. building paper vs. house wrap

**Water-resistive barrier** is the code-defined performance category (vapor-permeable, water-resistive) that a product must meet. **Building paper** (Grade D kraft paper) and **house wrap** (synthetic sheet products) are both product types that can satisfy the WRB requirement, but they're not interchangeable one-for-one with each other's installation details (lap requirements, fastening, compatibility with stucco specifically differ by product).

*In use:* "Check the manufacturer's install instructions for this specific house wrap under stucco — the lap and fastening spec isn't identical to the two-layer building-paper detail we're used to."

**Common misuse:** assuming any WRB product satisfies the two-layer requirement the same way, without checking whether the specific product is approved and detailed for direct stucco application versus general wall-sheathing use.

## Drainage plane vs. face-sealed system

**Drainage plane** is an intentional gap or capillary break behind the cladding that lets any water that gets past the outer surface drain down and out, independent of the cladding itself staying perfectly sealed. **Face-sealed system** relies entirely on the outer surface (sealant joints, the cladding itself) staying watertight, with no fallback drainage path if that seal fails anywhere over the building's life.

*In use:* "This is a face-sealed EIFS wall — there's no drainage plane, so every sealant joint on this building has to hold perfectly, forever, which is exactly the assumption that failed industry-wide in the '90s."

**Common misuse:** assuming a system that has performed for years without visible problems has a drainage plane, when the absence of visible failure so far just means the face seal hasn't failed *yet*.

## EIFS vs. traditional (hard-coat) stucco

**EIFS** (Exterior Insulation and Finish System) is a foam (EPS) insulation board adhered to the substrate, covered with a fiberglass-mesh-reinforced acrylic base and finish coat — a different material system from Portland cement plaster even though it can look similar finished. **Traditional (hard-coat) stucco** is Portland-cement-based plaster over metal lath and a WRB, with some capacity to let trapped moisture dry outward through the assembly.

*In use:* "Don't spec a repair patch in Portland cement on an EIFS wall — it's an acrylic-over-foam system, and the two materials don't bond or move the same way."

**Common misuse:** treating EIFS as "synthetic stucco" in the sense of being a drop-in substitute with the same moisture tolerance, when the foam substrate's inability to dry out is what made missing drainage catastrophic in EIFS specifically.

## Self-furring lath vs. flat lath

**Self-furring lath** has dimples or crimps that hold the mesh away from the substrate, creating the key gap the scratch coat needs to fully embed around the wire. **Flat lath** sits directly against the substrate with no standoff, which can starve the scratch coat of the embedment it needs to grip properly.

*In use:* "That cracking along the lath lines is consistent with flat lath having been used where the spec called for self-furring — check the lath delivery tickets against the spec."

**Common misuse:** treating "lath is lath" and substituting flat for self-furring (or vice versa) based on what's on the truck that day, without checking whether the substrate and coat thickness assumed a standoff gap.

## Moist curing vs. air curing

**Moist curing** is actively keeping a fresh coat damp (misting, damp burlap) for an initial period so cement hydration continues without the surface drying out prematurely. **Air curing** is the subsequent period where the coat is allowed to dry and gain strength at ambient conditions before the next coat or a paint/sealer goes on.

*In use:* "We're moist-curing the brown coat for the first 24 hours given today's wind, then it air-cures the rest of the 7-day window before finish goes on."

**Common misuse:** assuming a coat is "curing" simply because time has passed, without distinguishing whether it needed active moisture management during that time given the day's temperature, humidity, and wind.

## Efflorescence vs. staining

**Efflorescence** is a white, powdery salt deposit carried to the surface by moisture movement through the plaster — a sign moisture is moving through the assembly, not necessarily a structural problem on its own. **Staining** (rust-colored, brown, or dark streaking) more often points to a specific source — corroding lath, a failed flashing, or organic growth — and usually needs tracing to a cause rather than just cleaning.

*In use:* "The white haze near the base is efflorescence — moisture's moving through there. The rust-colored streak above the window is different; that's telling us something specific is failing at that flashing."

**Common misuse:** treating any discoloration on a stucco or EIFS wall as cosmetic efflorescence that can be cleaned or ignored, without checking whether the color and location actually point to an active moisture-intrusion source.
