# Red flags

Smell tests a lead plasterer catches during the job or in the first years after, before a moisture or cracking defect becomes a callback.

## 1. Single layer of building paper found behind stucco on wood-based sheathing

**Usually means:** the WRB was value-engineered or misunderstood as a one-layer product, skipping the drainage gap the two-layer system is designed to create — the crew treated the stucco itself as the waterproofing and the paper as an afterthought.

**First question:** "Is this one layer of building paper, or two layers with the required lap and separation between them?"

**Data to pull:** photos from the pre-lath inspection (if any exist); the WRB product spec sheet and whether it was approved as a single-layer-equivalent system; whether the sheathing shows any staining consistent with prior moisture.

## 2. Control-joint spacing exceeds ~150 sq ft per panel or 18 ft in any dimension

**Usually means:** joints were placed by eye or for a design line rather than from the panel-area/dimension math, leaving a field of plaster large enough to shrink-crack on its own schedule.

**First question:** "What panel area and aspect ratio does this layout actually produce per section, and does any panel exceed 150 sq ft or 18 ft on a side?"

**Data to pull:** the elevation drawing with joint locations marked; measured panel dimensions as built (not as drawn, if the layout shifted during application); any existing cracking pattern relative to panel centers.

## 3. Finish coat applied less than 7 days after brown coat

**Usually means:** schedule pressure compressed the cure clock — the brown coat was still shrinking when the finish coat went over it, setting up delamination or map cracking that won't show for weeks.

**First question:** "What date did the brown coat go on, and what date did the finish coat go on, logged — not remembered?"

**Data to pull:** the coat application log with dates and weather conditions; whether supplemental moist-curing was used given that day's temperature/humidity/wind; any existing cracking visible now versus at handoff.

## 4. Weep screed clearance below 4 in over earth or 2 in over a paved surface

**Usually means:** grade or paving was added or raised after the screed was set, without anyone re-checking clearance — a common sequence-of-trades gap between the plasterer, the landscaper, and the concrete crew.

**First question:** "What's the current clearance from the screed to grade or paving, measured today, not at the time of install?"

**Data to pull:** current grade/paving elevation versus screed elevation; any staining or efflorescence at the base of the wall; landscaping or hardscape change orders issued after the stucco was completed.

## 5. Map-pattern (fine, random) cracking across a large stucco field with no control joint nearby

**Usually means:** the panel this cracking sits in exceeds the control-joint sizing limits, or the coat was cured too fast for the day's weather — shrinkage found its own path because no joint was placed to take it.

**First question:** "What's the distance from this cracking to the nearest control joint, and what was the cure schedule and weather on the days this section was coated?"

**Data to pull:** joint layout drawing vs. as-built panel dimensions; coat application dates and weather log; whether other panels of similar size on the same building show the same pattern.

## 6. EIFS wall with no visible weep holes or drainage track at the base

**Usually means:** the system was installed face-sealed (barrier EIFS) rather than as a drainage EIFS — any water that gets behind the insulation board has no path out, and EPS foam doesn't dry the way a Portland-cement stucco field can.

**First question:** "Is there a drainage track and weep path at the base of this wall, or was this installed as a face-sealed system?"

**Data to pull:** original EIFS spec/manufacturer system used; moisture-meter readings at the base of the wall and at penetrations; any documented history of moisture complaints on this building or others from the same era/builder.

## 7. Rust staining bleeding through the finish coat, especially along horizontal lines

**Usually means:** the metal lath beneath is corroding, most often from moisture reaching it through an inadequate WRB, a flashing gap, or galvanizing that was compromised at a fastener or cut edge.

**First question:** "Does the staining line up with a lath lap, a fastener line, or a flashing location where water could be reaching the lath?"

**Data to pull:** moisture-meter readings at the stained area and surrounding wall; lath type and galvanizing spec used; any known penetrations or flashing details near the stain line.

## 8. Vertical crack aligned exactly with a window or door jamb

**Usually means:** stress concentration at the opening corner wasn't relieved by a nearby control joint, or lath wasn't reinforced with the extra diagonal strips typically added at opening corners.

**First question:** "Is there a control joint within the panel this opening sits in, and was diagonal reinforcing lath used at this corner?"

**Data to pull:** joint layout drawing at this elevation; lath installation photos if available; whether other openings of similar size on the building show the same crack pattern.

## 9. Soft or spongy area detected by hand pressure on an exterior stucco or EIFS wall

**Usually means:** sheathing or framing behind the cladding has taken on moisture and begun to deteriorate — by the time it's soft to the touch from outside, the damage has usually been progressing for a while.

**First question:** "What does a pin-type moisture meter read at this spot and at a few surrounding control points, not just at the soft area itself?"

**Data to pull:** moisture-meter readings across a grid around the suspect area; any nearby penetration, flashing, or grade condition that could be the water's entry point; age and system type (traditional stucco vs. EIFS) of the wall.

## 10. Homeowner or GC states "stucco is waterproof" as the basis for skipping a flashing or WRB detail

**Usually means:** a misunderstanding of the assembly being used to justify cutting a corner on the part of the system that's actually doing the water-shedding work.

**First question:** "What's the WRB and flashing detail behind this section, independent of what the stucco itself is doing?"

**Data to pull:** the WRB/flashing spec called for in the contract documents; whether this section has been inspected before being covered; any history of moisture problems on similar assemblies from the same design or era.
