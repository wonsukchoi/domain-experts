# Optometrist — Vocabulary

### Vertex distance
The distance between the back surface of a spectacle lens and the front of the cornea, conventionally measured at 12mm. It matters for power conversion because a lens's effective focusing power at the eye changes with how far it sits from the eye.
**In use:** "That's a −7.00, we need to vertex it before we order the contacts."
**Common misuse:** Ignored below about ±4.00D, where the correction is clinically negligible — over-applying it to low powers wastes time without changing the outcome.

### Cup-to-disc ratio (CDR)
The proportion of the optic nerve head's visible surface occupied by the central "cup" depression, relative to the whole disc diameter. A larger cup, or significant asymmetry between eyes, is a glaucoma-risk indicator.
**In use:** "CDR is 0.6 in the left eye and 0.3 in the right — that asymmetry needs a field test."
**Common misuse:** Treated as diagnostic on its own; it's one leg of a three-part assessment (IOP, CDR, visual field), not a standalone glaucoma diagnosis.

### Cycloplegic refraction
A refraction performed after instilling drops that temporarily paralyze the eye's accommodation (focusing) muscle, revealing the eye's true refractive error without the patient's own focusing effort masking part of it.
**In use:** "The manifest refraction underestimated his hyperopia — cycloplegic exam showed the full amount."
**Common misuse:** Assumed necessary for every patient; it's reserved for cases where latent accommodation is suspected (young children, esotropia, accommodative spasm), not a routine step for every adult.

### Duochrome (red-green) test
A subjective refraction-confirmation technique using the eye's chromatic aberration — letters on a red background versus a green background should appear equally clear when the correction is accurate.
**In use:** "Red side clearer — we're still slightly under-plussed."
**Common misuse:** Used as the sole confirmation method; it's a cross-check alongside cross-cylinder testing and binocular balance, not a replacement for the full subjective refraction.

### Jackson cross-cylinder (JCC)
A lens with equal and opposite cylindrical powers on its two axes, flipped in front of the patient to refine the axis and power of astigmatism correction.
**In use:** "JCC confirmed the axis at 175 — close to what retinoscopy suggested."
**Common misuse:** Confused with the cylinder power itself; the JCC is the testing tool used to find the correct cylinder, not the prescription component.

### Applanation tonometry
The gold-standard method of measuring intraocular pressure by measuring the force needed to flatten a small area of the cornea, typically with a Goldmann tonometer at the slit lamp.
**In use:** "Applanation reads 22 — higher than the non-contact tonometer screening suggested, let's trust this one."
**Common misuse:** Assumed interchangeable with non-contact ("air puff") tonometry; applanation is more accurate and is the reference standard when readings are borderline or discordant.

### Ocular hypertension
Intraocular pressure above the statistical normal range with no accompanying optic-nerve damage or visual-field defect — a risk factor for glaucoma, not the disease itself.
**In use:** "He's ocular hypertensive — pressure's elevated but the nerve and field both look clean, so we're monitoring, not treating yet."
**Common misuse:** Conflated with glaucoma; the distinction (risk factor vs. diagnosed disease) determines whether treatment is indicated.

### Normal-tension glaucoma
Glaucomatous optic-nerve damage and visual-field loss occurring despite intraocular pressure remaining within the statistically normal range.
**In use:** "IOP's been normal on every visit, but the field defect and CDR asymmetry say this is normal-tension glaucoma."
**Common misuse:** Assumed impossible because "the pressure is fine" — IOP being normal doesn't rule out glaucoma if the nerve and field say otherwise.

### Spherical equivalent
A single number summarizing a lens's overall power (sphere power plus half the cylinder power), used for quick comparisons or when cylinder correction isn't practical (like some contact lens fits).
**In use:** "Spherical equivalent works out to −5.25 if we're not correcting the astigmatism separately."
**Common misuse:** Used as if it fully replaces the astigmatism correction; it's an approximation that sacrifices some sharpness for patients with meaningful cylinder power.

### Anisometropia
A significant difference in refractive power between the two eyes.
**In use:** "Two diopters of anisometropia between the eyes — that's enough to risk amblyopia if it's not corrected soon."
**Common misuse:** Treated as automatically requiring contact lenses over glasses; while large anisometropia can cause image-size differences (aniseikonia) that contacts reduce, the threshold for switching correction types depends on patient tolerance, not the number alone.

### Amblyopia
Reduced best-corrected visual acuity in an eye that isn't explained by a structural or refractive problem alone — the brain has failed to develop full visual processing for that eye, usually from a childhood risk factor (uncorrected anisometropia, strabismus, or deprivation) left untreated.
**In use:** "Even with the correct glasses, that eye's still 20/60 — that's amblyopia, not just uncorrected refractive error."
**Common misuse:** Confused with any reduced vision; amblyopia specifically means the reduction persists after the best possible optical correction, and it has a limited treatment window, mostly in childhood.

### Presbyopia
The age-related, progressive loss of the eye's ability to focus at near distances as the lens loses flexibility — a near-universal process, not a disease.
**In use:** "Presbyopia's setting in — he needs reading add power now even though his distance Rx hasn't changed."
**Common misuse:** Confused with hyperopia (farsightedness); presbyopia is a separate, additive near-focusing loss that happens regardless of a person's underlying distance prescription.

### Base curve
The curvature of the back surface of a contact lens, selected to approximate (with an offset) the curvature of the patient's cornea for proper fit.
**In use:** "Lens is riding too tight — let's flatten the base curve by one step."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to be the same as the corneal keratometry reading; the finished lens's base curve typically differs from raw K readings by a material-specific offset in the manufacturer's fitting guide.
