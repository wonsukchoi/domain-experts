# Corporate sustainability working vocabulary

Terms a CSO uses precisely. Format: definition → how a practitioner says it → common misuse.

**Scope 1, 2, and 3 emissions** — Scope 1: direct emissions from owned/controlled sources (facilities, vehicles). Scope 2: indirect emissions from purchased energy. Scope 3: all other indirect emissions in the value chain (suppliers, product use, logistics) — usually the largest and hardest to measure.
In use: "Our Scope 1+2 is 35,200 tons, but Scope 3 is estimated at 184,800 — 84% of our real footprint sits in the supply chain."
Misuse: reporting only Scope 1/2 as if it represented the company's full climate impact, or treating all three scopes as equally measurable and controllable when Scope 3 typically requires supplier-dependent estimation.

**Additionality** — The requirement that a claimed emissions reduction or offset represents a real, incremental outcome that would not have happened without the specific investment or purchase — as opposed to something that would have occurred anyway.
In use: "This offset project's independent review shows only 60% additionality — we can only credibly claim 21,120 of the 35,200 tons purchased."
Misuse: assuming a purchased offset's full credited volume is automatically additional; many offset project types have documented additionality shortfalls that a credible claim has to account for.

**Mitigation hierarchy** — The sequence of avoid, then reduce, then offset (only what's left after genuine avoidance and reduction efforts), used to prioritize environmental action and assess the credibility of a claim that relies heavily on offsets.
In use: "Before buying more offsets, show me what's been done on the avoid and reduce steps — offsets are supposed to be the last resort, not the first move."
Misuse: reaching for offsets to hit a headline number before doing the harder avoid/reduce work, which undermines the credibility of the resulting claim even if the offsets themselves are legitimate.

**Materiality assessment** — A structured process identifying which environmental/social issues most affect both financial performance and stakeholder decisions for a specific business, used to prioritize where sustainability effort and reporting actually go.
In use: "Our materiality assessment flagged supply chain labor practices and water use as the top two issues for us — that's where the next reporting cycle focuses."
Misuse: adopting a generic industry ESG checklist and reporting shallowly across all of it instead of running (or refreshing) a materiality assessment specific to the business's actual footprint and stakeholder concerns.

**Greenwashing** — Making an environmental claim that overstates, misrepresents, or lacks substantiation for the actual sustainability performance behind it — increasingly a legal and regulatory exposure, not just a reputational one.
In use: "Legal flagged the 'carbon neutral' language as a greenwashing risk given the Scope 3 gap and the offset additionality question."
Misuse: treating greenwashing risk as only a PR concern; in an increasing number of jurisdictions, unsubstantiated environmental claims carry direct regulatory and litigation exposure.

**GHG Protocol** — The Greenhouse Gas Protocol, the most widely used accounting standard for measuring and reporting Scope 1/2/3 emissions, providing the common methodology that makes emissions figures comparable across companies and time.
In use: "Our emissions figures are calculated per GHG Protocol so they're directly comparable to peer companies' disclosures."
Misuse: reporting an emissions number without stating the methodology it was calculated under, making the figure impossible for an outside party to verify or compare.

**Carbon offset vs. carbon reduction** — A reduction is an actual decrease in emissions produced by the company's own operations or value chain; an offset is a purchased credit representing an emissions reduction or removal achieved elsewhere, used to compensate for (not replace) unreduced emissions.
In use: "We're at 12% real reduction this year, plus offsets for what's left — those are two different numbers and we report them separately."
Misuse: presenting offset purchases and direct reductions as interchangeable or combining them into a single undifferentiated "progress" number, which obscures how much of the claimed progress is actually operational change.

**CSRD (Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive)** — An EU regulation requiring detailed, audited sustainability disclosure from a broad and expanding set of companies, an example of a disclosure requirement with a defined phase-in schedule that tightens over several years.
In use: "CSRD's phase-in means our reporting scope expands next year — we're building the data infrastructure now instead of scrambling then."
Misuse: building disclosure infrastructure to meet only the current year's requirement without accounting for the regulation's known future phase-in expansion.

**Life-cycle assessment (LCA)** — A methodology quantifying a product's environmental impact across its entire life cycle — raw material extraction, manufacturing, use, and disposal — not just the manufacturing step a company directly controls.
In use: "The LCA showed most of this product's footprint is in the use phase, not manufacturing — that changes where we should focus reduction effort."
Misuse: assessing a product's environmental impact based only on the manufacturing stage, missing a use-phase or end-of-life impact that may dominate the true footprint.

**Third-party assurance** — Independent verification of a company's disclosed sustainability metrics by an external auditor, distinct from self-reported figures with no outside check.
In use: "This year's Scope 1/2 figures carry limited third-party assurance — we're working toward reasonable assurance, the higher standard, next cycle."
Misuse: presenting self-reported, unverified figures with the same confidence as third-party-assured ones; investors and regulators increasingly weight assured figures differently from unassured self-reports.
