# Conservation Scientist — Vocabulary

### Animal Unit Month (AUM)
The amount of forage (standardized at roughly 780 lbs dry matter, based on a 1,000 lb animal eating ~2.6% of body weight per day for 30 days) required to sustain one animal unit for one month.
**In use:** "This pasture pencils out to 443 AUMs for the season at current production."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a fixed number of animals rather than a forage-quantity unit — a cow-calf pair, a bull, and a yearling all consume different AUM fractions, so "AUMs" and "head count" aren't interchangeable without the animal-unit conversion factor.

### Proper-use factor
The fraction of a site's standing forage production that can be harvested by grazing without degrading plant vigor, root reserves, or soil cover — typically 25-50%, adjusted by range condition class.
**In use:** "At mid-seral condition we're using a 45% proper-use factor, not the 50% good-condition default."
**Common misuse:** Applied as a universal constant (usually defaulting to 50%) regardless of the site's actual condition class, which locks degraded range into continued decline at a harvest rate meant for healthy range.

### Ecological Site Description (ESD)
A standardized NRCS document describing the vegetation, soil, and production potential expected for a specific combination of climate, landform, and soil type, used as the reference condition against which current site status is measured.
**In use:** "The ESD for this soil map unit lists 1,400 lbs/acre reference production — we clipped 1,200 this year, so we're below normal but not alarmingly so."
**Common misuse:** Applied across a pasture with mixed soil types as if one ESD covers the whole unit — a pasture with a shallow, rocky inclusion has a different reference condition and carrying capacity than its deep-soil majority.

### Seral stage
A site's successional position relative to its ecological potential (climax) plant community — described as early, mid, or late seral (or condition-class terms like poor/fair/good/excellent).
**In use:** "Transect data puts this pasture at mid-seral — that's the condition class driving our proper-use factor down from the good-condition default."
**Common misuse:** Confused with a permanent site classification rather than a management-responsive state — seral stage moves in response to grazing/fire/rest, which is the entire point of tracking it as a trend.

### Carrying capacity
The maximum sustained level of use (grazing, timber harvest, or visitor days) a site can support without degrading its long-term productive capacity — distinct from short-term or peak capacity.
**In use:** "Current lease demand is 176% of calculated carrying capacity — that's not a rounding gap, that's a structural overstock."
**Common misuse:** Calculated once from a long-term average and treated as fixed, when it should be recalculated from current-year production data, which varies with precipitation.

### Rest-rotation grazing
A grazing system that divides a range into multiple pastures and rotates livestock through them on a schedule that gives each pasture at least one full growing season of rest within a multi-year cycle.
**In use:** "We're moving this allotment from season-long continuous to a 4-pasture rest-rotation to give each unit a rest year."
**Common misuse:** Conflated with simple rotational grazing (moving livestock between pastures more frequently within the same season) — rest-rotation's defining feature is a full-season rest, not just shorter grazing periods per pasture.

### Fire behavior triangle
The three environmental factors — fuel, weather, and topography — that jointly determine how a fire will behave; in prescribed-fire planning, the weather leg (fuel moisture, humidity, wind, mixing height) is the controllable trigger for whether conditions fall inside prescription.
**In use:** "All five weather parameters landed inside prescription this morning — we're a go for the unit."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a single "good burn day" judgment call instead of a checklist where every parameter must independently fall in range; one out-of-range parameter is a no-go regardless of how favorable the rest look.

### Allowable cut
The volume of timber that can be harvested from a stand or forest annually or periodically without exceeding its sustained-yield capacity, typically bounded by the measured growth increment.
**In use:** "The cruise data puts mean annual increment at 420 board feet/acre — the proposed 600 board feet/acre cut draws down standing volume, it's not a sustained-yield harvest."
**Common misuse:** Set from a one-time timber cruise's standing volume rather than the growth increment, which produces a harvest plan that liquidates inventory rather than harvesting the annual growth.

### Multiple-use management
A land-management approach (codified in U.S. Forest Service and BLM statutory mandates) that allocates a single land base across several concurrent uses — grazing, timber, recreation, wildlife habitat, watershed — rather than dedicating it to one use exclusively.
**In use:** "This allotment is multiple-use — the grazing lease has to coexist with the elk winter-range objective, not override it."
**Common misuse:** Interpreted as "maximize every use simultaneously" rather than "manage an explicit tradeoff among uses" — multiple-use planning produces a tradeoff schedule, not a plan that satisfies every use at its individual maximum.

### Line-point intercept (LPI)
A rangeland-monitoring method that records the vegetation, litter, or bare ground touched by a pin at fixed intervals along a transect, producing a quantitative ground-cover composition for trend comparison over time.
**In use:** "This year's LPI transect shows bare ground up 8 percentage points from the 2021 baseline reading on the same line."
**Common misuse:** A single LPI reading is treated as a condition verdict rather than one data point in a trend series — LPI's value is in the repeated-transect comparison, not the standalone number.

### Riparian buffer
A vegetated zone maintained along a stream or water body, sized to filter sediment/nutrients and provide shade/bank stability, with the required width typically scaled to stream order and adjacent slope.
**In use:** "This is a second-order stream on a 15% slope — the watershed plan calls for a 100-foot buffer here, not the 50-foot default for flat first-order streams."
**Common misuse:** Applied as a single fixed-width standard across all stream types on a property, ignoring that sediment-filtering function scales with slope and stream order.
