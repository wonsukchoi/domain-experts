# Vocabulary

### Goodyear welted construction
A footwear construction method where the sole is attached via a strip of leather (the welt) stitched to both the upper and the sole, allowing the sole to be replaced repeatedly by removing and re-stitching at the welt without damaging the upper.
**In use:** "This is welted construction — the sole coming loose likely means the welt stitching failed, not that the cement gave out, since welted shoes don't primarily rely on adhesive."
**Common misuse:** Treating a welted shoe's sole separation as a simple adhesive bonding failure and fixing it with cement alone — welted construction's structural integrity comes from the stitching, and a cement-only fix masks a stitching failure that will likely recur.

### Cemented construction
A footwear construction method where the sole is attached to the upper primarily through adhesive bonding rather than stitching, common in many mass-produced shoes.
**In use:** "Cemented construction — sole separation here really is likely an adhesive issue, since there's no welt stitching involved in the first place."
**Common misuse:** Applying a welted-shoe repair approach (re-stitching) to a cemented shoe, or vice versa — the appropriate repair method depends entirely on which construction the specific item actually uses, and the two require fundamentally different techniques.

### Blake stitched construction
A footwear construction method where the sole is directly stitched to the upper and insole through a single line of stitching visible on the sole's underside, distinct from welted construction's separate welt strip.
**In use:** "Blake stitched — different repair approach than welted, since the stitching goes straight through rather than via a separate welt piece."
**Common misuse:** Confusing Blake stitched construction with Goodyear welted construction based on both having visible stitching — the actual stitching path and repair approach differ meaningfully between the two, and misidentifying one for the other can lead to an inappropriate repair method.

### Root cause vs. symptom (repair diagnosis)
The distinction between what a customer describes noticing (a loose sole, a squeak) and the actual underlying mechanical failure causing that symptom, which may require a different repair than what the symptom alone suggests.
**In use:** "The complaint was 'sole coming loose,' but the actual root cause is failed welt stitching — fixing the stitching addresses both."
**Common misuse:** Treating a customer's stated complaint as a complete diagnosis and applying a fix aimed directly at the described symptom — the symptom is what's visible from outside, not necessarily the mechanical cause, and a fix aimed only at the symptom can leave the actual cause unaddressed.

### Leather stretch limit
The maximum safe extent to which a specific leather type and thickness can be stretched to adjust fit before the material begins to stress, thin, or tear.
**In use:** "We can take this in about a quarter inch safely — pushing further risks visibly thinning the leather at the stress points."
**Common misuse:** Treating leather stretching as an open-ended solution to any fit complaint, continuing to stretch until the complaint is fully resolved regardless of the material's actual physical limit — leather has a finite, type-specific capacity, and exceeding it damages the item while attempting to fully satisfy the original request.

### Structural vs. cosmetic damage (footwear/leather repair)
The distinction between damage affecting an item's functional integrity (a failed sole bond, damaged welt, compromised stitching foundation) and damage affecting only appearance (surface scuffs, color fading), which determines actual repairability.
**In use:** "The scuffing's purely cosmetic and easy to address — but the sole delamination underneath is structural, and that's what actually determines whether this is worth repairing."
**Common misuse:** Assessing repairability based on surface/cosmetic condition alone — an item can look easily repairable at the surface while having structural damage that makes repair uneconomical, unsafe, or likely to fail quickly even with a good-looking surface fix.

### Repair material compatibility
The functional matching of adhesive, sole compound, or thread type to an item's original construction and materials, based on bonding/wear characteristics rather than visual similarity alone.
**In use:** "Right color, wrong compound — this sole material won't bond properly to that specific upper leather and will wear at a different rate."
**Common misuse:** Selecting repair materials based on visual match to the original (color, texture) without confirming functional compatibility (bonding, wear rate) with the specific construction and materials involved — a visually matched but functionally incompatible material can fail to bond correctly or create an uneven wear pattern.

### Welt stitching failure
Deterioration or rotting of the stitching connecting a welted shoe's welt to the sole and upper, commonly from moisture exposure over time, which produces sole separation as a downstream symptom.
**In use:** "That's welt stitching failure from years of moisture exposure — not a defect in the original construction, just wear over time."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a welted shoe's sole issue is always an original manufacturing defect or an adhesive problem — welt stitching failure from age and moisture exposure is a common, expected wear pattern in welted footwear that requires its own specific repair approach (re-stitching), not an adhesive fix.

### Economical/safe repair threshold
The point at which an item's structural damage makes repair either not cost-effective relative to the item's value, or unsafe to use even if technically repairable, distinct from whether a surface repair could be made to look acceptable.
**In use:** "Technically we could patch this, but the structural damage extends far enough that it wouldn't hold up safely — that's past the repair threshold, not just an aesthetic judgment call."
**Common misuse:** Basing a repairability decision solely on whether a surface fix would look acceptable — some structural damage genuinely makes an item unsafe or not worth repairing regardless of how good a cosmetic repair could look.
