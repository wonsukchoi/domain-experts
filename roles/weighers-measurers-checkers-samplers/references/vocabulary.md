# Weighers, Measurers, Checkers, and Samplers — Vocabulary

### Gross weight
The total weight of a loaded vehicle or container, including its own weight and its contents.
**In use:** "Gross came in at 76,340 — that's stable across two readings, so the scale itself isn't the problem here."
**Common misuse:** Treated as the figure to compare against a BOL's net-weight claim, when the comparison must be against the calculated net (gross minus tare).

### Tare weight
The weight of an empty vehicle or container, subtracted from gross to get net.
**In use:** "That tare ticket is two weeks old and this truck picked up a toolbox since then — we re-weigh it empty before trusting the net."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to be a fixed property of the vehicle rather than something that changes with fuel, residue, and equipment, and therefore treated as safe to reuse indefinitely.

### Net weight
Gross weight minus tare weight — the actual weight of the load itself.
**In use:** "Net comes out to 47,490, which is 510 pounds short of the BOL claim."
**Common misuse:** Calculated correctly but then compared against the wrong reference figure (e.g. an ordered quantity instead of the BOL-claimed weight).

### Legal for trade
A designation meaning a scale has been certified by a state or national weights-and-measures authority as accurate enough to use in commercial transactions.
**In use:** "This platform scale is legal for trade through November — anything read on it holds up in a claim dispute."
**Common misuse:** Confused with "accurate" in a general sense — a scale can read consistently but still not be legal for trade if it lacks current certification.

### Calibration seal
A physical or electronic seal placed on a scale after calibration, indicating it hasn't been adjusted since the last certified check.
**In use:** "Seal's intact and dated last month — no need to flag this reading as provisional."
**Common misuse:** Checked for presence but not for expiration date, missing that an expired-but-intact seal still means the reading isn't currently certified.

### AQL (Acceptable Quality Level)
The maximum percent-defective a sampling plan will still treat as an acceptable lot, expressed as a probability-based sampling scheme, not a promise of zero defects.
**In use:** "We're running AQL 1.0% on this lot — that's a bounded risk we've accepted, not a guarantee nothing slipped through."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a claim that a passed lot has zero defects, rather than a defined and accepted probability of passing a lot that still contains some.

### Inspection level
A setting in a sampling-plan standard (I, II, or III in Z1.4) that determines how large a sample is drawn relative to lot size — II is standard/default, I is reduced discrimination, III is increased.
**In use:** "We stay at Level II unless quality flags a reason to tighten scrutiny on this part number."
**Common misuse:** Confused with AQL itself — inspection level sets sample size, AQL sets the accept/reject threshold at that sample size; they're two independent dials.

### Ac/Re numbers
The accept and reject defect counts from a sampling plan's table for a given sample size and AQL — accept if defects found are at or below Ac, reject if at or above Re.
**In use:** "Sample of 80, Ac/Re is 5/6 — we found 4 defects, so this lot accepts."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a continuous scale where "close to the reject number" implies partial failure, when the rule is a hard threshold with no in-between disposition.

### Switching rules
The Z1.4-style rules governing when inspection intensity moves between Normal, Tightened, and Reduced based on a vendor or line's recent pass/fail history.
**In use:** "Two rejected lots in a row from this vendor — we're switching to Tightened per the rule, not waiting for a third."
**Common misuse:** Applied inconsistently or ignored, with each lot judged independently rather than as part of the running switching-rule state.

### Discrepancy report
A formal document recording a weight or quantity variance beyond tolerance, with the evidence (tickets, calibration status, re-weigh results) needed to support a claim.
**In use:** "This shortage is past tolerance even after a re-weigh — that gets a discrepancy report, not a quiet note in the log."
**Common misuse:** Skipped in favor of informally adjusting the recorded weight to match the claim, which erases the evidence a later claim would need.

### Weighmaster
A licensed or certified role (in some states, a specific legal designation) authorized to issue certified weight certificates used in commercial transactions.
**In use:** "That certificate needs a weighmaster's signature to be usable in a freight claim."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to be an informal job title rather than, in some jurisdictions, a specific state license with its own legal requirements.

### Re-tare interval
The maximum time (or condition, like a visible physical change) after which a vehicle's tare weight must be re-verified rather than reused.
**In use:** "Site policy is a 7-day re-tare interval — this ticket is 14 days old, so it's out."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a suggestion rather than a hard trigger, especially under time pressure to release a load quickly.

### Sampling lot
The defined batch of units a sampling plan is applied to as a single unit — its size determines the sample size and code letter used.
**In use:** "This is one lot of 620 units even though it arrived on two trucks — same PO, same production run."
**Common misuse:** Split or combined inconsistently (e.g. treating two partial shipments as separate lots when they're really one), which changes which sample-size code applies.
