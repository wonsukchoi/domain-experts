# Vocabulary

### Residual land value
The maximum defensible price for land, computed by subtracting hard costs, soft costs, financing costs, and required developer profit from projected sellout or lease-up value — never derived from comps for raw land.
**In use:** "The seller's ask is $2.3M above residual — that's the negotiating gap, not a reason to walk yet."
**Common misuse:** Using nearby raw-land sale comps to justify a price instead of computing the residual from the specific project's actual numbers — comps tell you what someone else paid, not what the deal supports.

### LTC (loan-to-cost) / LTV (loan-to-value)
LTC is the loan amount as a percentage of total project cost (used for construction loans); LTV is the loan amount as a percentage of the asset's appraised value (used for permanent/take-out financing) — they're different denominators and produce different numbers on the same deal.
**In use:** "Our construction lender caps at 65% LTC, not LTV — don't size the equity gap off the wrong ratio."
**Common misuse:** Treating LTC and LTV as interchangeable when sizing the equity requirement, which produces a real funding gap at closing.

### GMP (guaranteed maximum price)
A construction contract structure where the GC guarantees a cost ceiling, with savings below it typically shared per a negotiated formula and overruns above it borne by the GC, not the developer.
**In use:** "We don't break ground until the GMP is signed — cost-plus on a leveraged deal is not a risk we're taking."
**Common misuse:** Treating a GMP negotiation as a formality to get through quickly, when the specific inclusions/exclusions and the shared-savings formula are where the real risk transfer happens.

### Pro forma
The projected financial model for a development — revenue, costs, financing, and returns over the project timeline — used for underwriting and lender/investor presentation.
**In use:** "The pro forma pencils at a 19% IRR, but that's the base case — show me the downside scenario."
**Common misuse:** Presenting the pro forma's base case as a reliable forecast rather than a hypothesis whose most fragile assumptions (absorption, exit cap rate) need explicit stress-testing.

### Absorption rate
The pace at which units in a development sell or lease, typically measured as units per month, benchmarked against the submarket's actual historical pace.
**In use:** "Our absorption assumption is 8 units/month — the submarket's trailing 12-month average is 5.5, so we're underwriting to an optimistic case."
**Common misuse:** Setting the absorption assumption to whatever number makes the deal's IRR target work, rather than to the market's actual demonstrated pace.

### Cap rate (capitalization rate)
The ratio of a stabilized property's net operating income to its value, used both to estimate current value and, critically, to project exit value at a future stabilized cap rate assumption.
**In use:** "A 50bps cap rate expansion between underwriting and exit wipes out most of our projected profit on this leverage level."
**Common misuse:** Assuming the exit cap rate will match or improve on the entry cap rate, when cap rates are a market-cycle variable outside the developer's control over a multi-year hold.

### Entitlement
The legal approvals (zoning, variance, permits) required before a site can be developed to a given use and density — the process, not a single document.
**In use:** "This site isn't entitled for 120 units yet — we're buying the *right to pursue* entitlement, not the entitlement itself."
**Common misuse:** Treating entitlement as a formality or a paperwork step rather than a genuine approval-probability risk with its own timeline and failure rate.

### Waterfall / promote
The contractual sequence in which cash flow and profit are distributed among capital stack participants — typically returning capital and a preferred return to investors first, then splitting remaining profit with the GP receiving a disproportionate "promote" share above a return hurdle.
**In use:** "Above the 15% IRR hurdle, the GP promote jumps to 20% of profit — that's what's actually driving the sponsor's incentive to push for a fast, high-priced exit."
**Common misuse:** Describing a waterfall as simply "aligning incentives" without examining that it also concentrates upside asymmetrically, which can push the GP toward higher-risk decisions the LP wouldn't independently choose.

### Carry cost
The financing cost of holding a leveraged asset or loan balance over time — calculated as loan balance × interest rate ÷ 12 per month — that accrues regardless of whether the project is progressing.
**In use:** "Three months of entitlement delay just added $210,000 in carry cost nobody budgeted for."
**Common misuse:** Tracking schedule delay as a timeline problem only, without converting it into its actual dollar cost against the loan balance.

### DSCR (debt service coverage ratio)
The ratio of a property's net operating income to its annual debt service (principal + interest), used by lenders as a covenant to ensure the asset generates enough income to service its debt with margin.
**In use:** "We're at 1.25x DSCR against a 1.25x covenant floor — there's zero buffer if lease-up runs even slightly behind."
**Common misuse:** Underwriting to just clear the covenant at the base-case NOI projection instead of stress-testing DSCR against a realistic downside NOI scenario.

### Hard costs / soft costs
Hard costs are direct construction costs (materials, labor, site work); soft costs are everything else required to deliver the project (architecture, engineering, permits, legal, marketing, developer fee) — typically 15–20% of hard costs on a well-scoped project.
**In use:** "Soft costs are running at 22% of hard costs on this one — that's above our typical 16–18% range, worth an audit line by line."
**Common misuse:** Budgeting soft costs as a flat percentage without auditing which specific line items are actually driving it, which hides where the real overage is coming from.

### Value engineering
The process of reducing project cost by changing materials, systems, or scope, ideally targeting elements that don't affect the buyer/tenant's perceived value.
**In use:** "We value-engineered the parking structure finish and mechanical redundancy — nothing a buyer walks the unit and notices."
**Common misuse:** Applying cost cuts proportionally across the whole scope under budget pressure, which often guts exactly the finishes or amenities that justified the project's price point.

### Mezzanine debt
Subordinated debt layered between senior debt and equity in the capital stack, higher-cost than senior debt but cheaper than pure equity, used to close a funding gap without diluting sponsor equity as much.
**In use:** "Adding a mezz layer closes the gap, but recalculate the blended cost of capital before assuming the deal still clears its hurdle."
**Common misuse:** Treating mezzanine debt as "free" extra leverage without recomputing how its higher rate raises the deal's overall required return.

### Pre-leasing / pre-sales
Signed leases or purchase contracts secured before or during construction, used both to reduce lease-up/sellout risk and often required by lenders as a condition for loan draws or refinancing.
**In use:** "We need 40% pre-leased before the construction lender releases the next draw — that's the covenant, not a nice-to-have."
**Common misuse:** Counting soft reservations or non-binding letters of intent as pre-leasing when lender covenants typically require signed, binding leases.
