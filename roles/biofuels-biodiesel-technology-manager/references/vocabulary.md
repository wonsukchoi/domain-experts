# Working vocabulary

Terms of art practitioners use precisely, that generalists misuse. Load this when writing or reviewing content that needs to sound like an actual biofuels technology/product development manager, not a generalist describing one.

### Technology Readiness Level (TRL)
A 1-9 scale (originally NASA, widely adapted in process industries) describing how validated a technology is, from basic principles observed (TRL 1) through actual system proven in operational environment (TRL 9).
**In use:** "We're at TRL 4-5 with this bench data — that's component validation, not process validation at relevant scale. Commercial capital needs TRL 7+."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a vague maturity label rather than a specific gate with defined evidence requirements — "it's pretty mature" is not a TRL claim; a TRL claim should be defensible against a specific data set.

### Carbon intensity (CI) score
A quantified measure (typically gCO2e/MJ) of the lifecycle greenhouse gas emissions of a fuel pathway, used by credit programs like California's LCFS to determine credit generation.
**In use:** "This pathway scores a CI of 25 versus diesel's ~100 baseline — that spread is what generates the credit value, not just the fuel sale."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to track yield or process efficiency directly — a lower-yield process can have a materially better (lower) CI score depending on feedstock lifecycle emissions, which are often the dominant factor over process efficiency.

### RIN (Renewable Identification Number)
A tradeable credit generated under the US EPA Renewable Fuel Standard (RFS) for each qualifying gallon (gallon-equivalent) of renewable fuel produced, tied to an approved production pathway.
**In use:** "This fuel won't generate RINs until the pathway petition clears — that's a separate revenue stream from the fuel sale itself, and it's currently unpriced in our model."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to be automatic once fuel is produced — RIN generation requires an approved EPA pathway determination specific to the feedstock/process/fuel combination; producing fuel from an unapproved pathway doesn't generate RINs regardless of the fuel's actual environmental profile.

### Mass balance closure
The degree to which measured mass inputs to a process equal measured mass outputs (product + byproducts + measured losses), expressed as a percentage; used to validate that a process is fully characterized.
**In use:** "We closed the balance within 3% on that run — good enough to size the next tier's equipment with confidence."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a formality to report rather than a gating check — closure meaningfully outside 95-105% means something material is unaccounted for, and building the next scale tier on that gap compounds an unidentified risk rather than resolving it.

### Feedstock flexibility
A process design's tolerance for variation in feedstock composition (e.g., free fatty acid content, moisture, contaminant levels) without requiring reconfiguration or losing significant yield.
**In use:** "We priced in feedstock flexibility up front — costs about 8% more capital but lets us switch between UCO and tallow if one gets tight."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a nice-to-have rather than a priced tradeoff — the decision to skip flexibility is a deliberate bet on a specific feedstock's continued availability and price, and should be evaluated as such, not treated as a default with no downside.

### Trade secret vs. patent (in process IP)
Two distinct protection strategies: a patent grants time-limited exclusive rights in exchange for public disclosure; a trade secret protects indefinitely but only as long as the information stays undisclosed and isn't independently discoverable.
**In use:** "The catalyst loading is unobservable once the reactor's sealed — that's a trade secret candidate. The product's fatty acid profile shift is visible from an outside lab test — that needs a patent or we lose it the day a competitor buys our product."
**Common misuse:** Chosen based on which "sounds more valuable" rather than on the innovation's post-deployment observability — an observable process improvement kept as a trade secret is unprotected the moment a competitor reverse-engineers it.

### Techno-economic analysis (TEA)
A structured financial model combining process engineering data (yields, capital costs, operating costs) with market and regulatory assumptions (fuel price, credit value) to estimate a technology's commercial viability.
**In use:** "The TEA shows Process B beating Process A on credit-adjusted revenue per ton, even though A has the better yield."
**Common misuse:** Built once at project kickoff on initial assumptions and treated as static — CI scores, credit values, and fuel prices change; a TEA not refreshed against current values can justify a decision based on stale economics.

### Pathway (regulatory)
A specific, approved combination of feedstock, process, and fuel product recognized by a regulatory program (EPA RFS, CARB LCFS) as qualifying for credits, each requiring its own approval.
**In use:** "Our new feedstock blend needs its own pathway approval — we can't just assume it inherits the approval from the single-feedstock process."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to transfer automatically when a minor process or feedstock change is made — a modified pathway (even a blend of two already-approved feedstocks) frequently requires its own separate approval, with its own multi-year timeline.
