# Red flags

Smell tests a stonemason or inspector catches during a submittal review or walkthrough, before the veneer closes and the defect becomes invisible. Format for each: what it usually means, the first question to ask, and the data to pull before anyone signs off.

## 1. Stone submittal approved on color/finish alone with no stated ASTM absorption class for the region's freeze-thaw exposure

**Usually means:** the review checked appearance and size but never pulled the density/absorption class (ASTM C568/C503/C615/C616) against the project's climate.

**First question:** "What's the absorption class on this stone's data sheet, and does it clear the minimum for this climate's freeze-thaw exposure?"

**Data to pull:** quarry data sheet with ASTM classification, project location's weathering exposure, any documented spalling history for that quarry lot in a comparable climate.

## 2. Anchor spacing borrowed from a brick-veneer tie table or a different stone's spacing pattern

**Usually means:** the layout was copied from a prior job or a generic table without recomputing tributary weight for this stone's actual density and thickness.

**First question:** "What's this stone's actual weight per sq ft, and does the anchor spacing check out against the anchor's rated allowable load for that weight?"

**Data to pull:** stone density/thickness data, anchor manufacturer's allowable load rating, the tributary-area calculation as run for this specific stone.

## 3. New stone lot from an unfamiliar quarry laid without ASTM C97 absorption/bulk specific gravity data on hand

**Usually means:** the crew assumed a commercial stone name ("limestone," "bluestone") implies consistent performance across quarries, or no test data was requested from the supplier.

**First question:** "Has this specific lot's absorption and density been tested or documented, or are we going on the name alone?"

**Data to pull:** quarry lot/ticket number, ASTM C97 test data or supplier certification, any prior project history with this specific quarry source.

## 4. Portland-cement-rich mortar proposed for repointing or resetting visibly soft or historic stone

**Usually means:** the crew or GC defaulted to a modern mix "for strength" without checking the original mortar's lime content or the stone's relative softness.

**First question:** "What's the original mortar's approximate lime-to-cement ratio, and does the proposed repair mix stay softer and more permeable than the stone?"

**Data to pull:** core sample or historical spec of the original mortar, stone hardness/porosity data, proposed repair-mix data sheet.

## 5. Dry-stack wall built with a battered face but no check against the middle-third/thrust-line rule for the design height

**Usually means:** the batter angle was set by eye or habit rather than calculated for this wall's actual height and base width.

**First question:** "What height and base-width numbers was this batter calculated against, and where does the thrust line fall at the tallest course?"

**Data to pull:** wall height and base/top width measurements, batter angle as built, any surcharge condition (retained soil, adjacent grade change) not accounted for.

## 6. Efflorescence, spalling, or scaling on a stone veneer within the first two winters

**Usually means:** an absorption-class mismatch for the climate, a bedding-orientation error, or trapped moisture from an incompatible repair mortar — rarely normal weathering this early.

**First question:** "Is this concentrated on specific courses or units, and does it correlate with a particular stone lot, orientation, or mortar batch?"

**Data to pull:** as-built stone lot map, bedding-orientation spot checks on affected units, mortar mix records if this is restoration work.

## 7. Replacement stone on a historic facade matched to the original by color and finish only

**Usually means:** the match was made visually in the yard or showroom without a mineral-composition or absorption comparison to the original stone.

**First question:** "Has the replacement stone's mineral composition and absorption been compared to the original, or is this a visual match only?"

**Data to pull:** petrographic or absorption data for both the original and replacement stone, quarry source of each.

## 8. Stone units laid with visible grain/bedding lines running vertical on an exposed exterior face

**Usually means:** the units were face-bedded, either by a layout error in the yard or a decision to prioritize a particular coursing look over bedding orientation.

**First question:** "Which way do the bedding planes run on these units, and is any of this sedimentary stone face-bedded on a load-bearing or weather-exposed face?"

**Data to pull:** close-up photos of grain/bedding lines before the wall closes, stone-cutting yard instructions, spot check against the original stone order.

## 9. Anchors on existing stone veneer over roughly 20–30 years old showing surface rust or bleed staining at the joint

**Usually means:** carbon-steel anchors approaching or past typical service life are corroding, expanding, and beginning to crack the surrounding stone or mortar from the inside.

**First question:** "What anchor material was originally specified, and has any anchor been physically inspected rather than just visually assessed from the face?"

**Data to pull:** original anchor material spec if available, rust-bleed photo documentation and location map, any prior anchor-replacement records for the facade.

## 10. Cost comparison between stone options presented purely as price-per-sq-ft with no absorption/density class noted

**Usually means:** the estimate was built from a supplier price list without carrying the durability classification into the comparison.

**First question:** "Does this price-per-sq-ft comparison account for absorption class, or is the lower-cost option lower cost because it's a lower durability class?"

**Data to pull:** each option's ASTM classification alongside its price, expected service life or warranty terms if the supplier states any.
