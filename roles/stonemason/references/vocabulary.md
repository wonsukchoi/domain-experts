# Vocabulary

Terms generalists conflate or use loosely that a stonemason keeps sharply separate. Each: definition, how a practitioner actually uses it in a sentence, and the common misuse to avoid.

## Ashlar vs. rubble

**Ashlar** is stone cut and dressed to consistent, often rectangular shapes and laid in regular or near-regular courses. **Rubble** masonry uses uncut or roughly shaped stone laid without a uniform coursing pattern, relying on the mason's fitting judgment for each piece.

**In use:** "This elevation is coursed ashlar, but the foundation below grade is rubble — don't quote the ashlar labor rate for the rubble work, they're different skill sets."

**Common misuse:** treating "ashlar" as a synonym for "cut stone" in general, when it specifically implies a coursing discipline, not just a dressed surface.

## Dimension stone

Natural stone that has been cut and finished to specified sizes and shapes for a particular application (veneer, coping, sills), as distinct from rubble, fieldstone, or riprap used in its natural or roughly broken form.

**In use:** "The anchor tables in the Dimension Stone Design Manual apply to this veneer — they don't apply to the dry-stack fieldstone wall on the same site."

**Common misuse:** assuming any "cut stone" qualifies as dimension stone for design-manual purposes, when the term specifically implies fabrication to a spec dimension and finish.

## Natural bed vs. face-bedded

**Natural bed** orientation sets a sedimentary stone unit with its bedding planes horizontal, matching how it formed in the quarry — the load-bearing and weathering-capable orientation. **Face-bedded** stone has its bedding planes running vertical, parallel to the exposed face — a delamination risk on any load-bearing or weather-exposed application.

**In use:** "Check the grain lines before you set that limestone lintel — if it's face-bedded, it'll shear along the bed in a decade regardless of how good the joint looks today."

**Common misuse:** assuming bedding orientation doesn't matter once the stone is cut to size, or judging it by the finished face rather than sighting the actual grain lines.

## Rift and grain

**Rift** and **grain** describe a stone's directional splitting tendencies from its crystalline or bedding structure (most discussed in granite quarrying) — the rift is the easiest splitting direction, the grain the second-easiest, and the "hard way" the most resistant. Cutting with or against these directions changes both effort and finished surface behavior.

**In use:** "Split it along the rift, not across it — cutting against the rift on this granite doubles the tool wear and still won't give you a clean face."

**Common misuse:** treating all stone as directionally uniform and cutting for convenience without checking rift/grain, producing uneven surfaces or unnecessary breakage.

## Quarry sap

Residual moisture retained in freshly quarried stone before it has air-dried ("seasoned"); many stones are measurably softer and easier to work while sap is still present and harden somewhat as they dry.

**In use:** "Cut the detail work while it's still got quarry sap in it — this stone gets noticeably harder to dress once it's seasoned out."

**Common misuse:** assuming a stone's as-quarried workability represents its long-term in-service hardness, or storing green stone in freeze conditions before it has dried.

## Dry-stack (dry-laid) vs. mortared construction

**Dry-stack** construction relies on gravity, friction, and stone-to-stone fit for stability, with no mortar. **Mortared** construction bonds units with mortar, which also has to accommodate differential movement between units.

**In use:** "This retaining wall reads as dry-stack, but it's actually mortared rubble with a dry-stack face — check the spec before pricing labor by the dry-stack rate."

**Common misuse:** assuming any stone wall without visible mortar joints is genuinely dry-stacked, when a hidden mortar core changes both the structural analysis and the batter/thrust-line math that would otherwise apply.

## Batter

The intentional inward slope of a wall face as it rises, expressed as a ratio (e.g., 1:6) of horizontal setback to vertical rise, used on dry-stack and gravity walls to keep the thrust line centered without mortar.

**In use:** "We're battering both faces at 1:6 on this one — flatten that and you're gambling the thrust line stays inside the middle third."

**Common misuse:** applying a uniform batter number across different wall heights or soil conditions without rechecking the middle-third calculation for the specific wall.

## Thrust line / middle-third rule

The **thrust line** is the path the resultant compressive force takes through a gravity masonry structure; the **middle-third rule** states that a wall stays in pure compression (no tension) only if that line stays within the central third of the cross-section at every point.

**In use:** "At that height with this base width, the thrust line is already close to the outer third — either widen the base or reduce the rise before we go further."

**Common misuse:** treating wall stability as a visual/intuitive judgment ("it looks stable") rather than checking the actual thrust-line position against the middle-third geometry.

## Kerf anchor vs. pin (dowel) anchor vs. strap anchor

A **kerf anchor** engages a groove cut into the stone's edge; a **pin (dowel) anchor** engages a drilled hole; a **strap anchor** wraps or bolts to the stone's back or edge face. Each has a different allowable load rating and a different failure mode under the same stone weight.

**In use:** "The strap anchor's rated load doesn't transfer directly to a kerf detail on this stone — pull the kerf-specific allowable before we finalize spacing."

**Common misuse:** applying one anchor type's allowable load rating to a different anchor type "because they're both rated for stone," when each system's tested capacity is specific to its own attachment geometry.

## Saturation coefficient / absorption (ASTM C97)

The ratio (or percentage, depending on the test) describing how much water a stone unit absorbs relative to its dry or saturated capacity, tested per ASTM C97 — the primary data point behind a stone's freeze-thaw classification.

**In use:** "Pull the C97 absorption number off the data sheet before we approve this lot — the quarry's marketing sheet only shows color and finish options."

**Common misuse:** substituting a stone's visual density (how "solid" it looks) for actual tested absorption data, which can differ significantly between visually similar stones.

## Lime mortar (NHL) vs. Portland-cement mortar

**Lime mortar** (lime putty or natural hydraulic lime, NHL) is a low-strength, high-permeability mix historically used with stone, intended to be the sacrificial element in the assembly. **Portland-cement mortar** is a higher-strength, lower-permeability modern mix, appropriate for new load-bearing construction but frequently mismatched to historic stone repair.

**In use:** "This is a pre-1900 sandstone facade — we're repointing in NHL, not a Portland mix, or we're just moving the failure point into the stone."

**Common misuse:** selecting mortar strength as a general durability upgrade without checking whether the stone it's bonded to needs the mortar to stay softer, not stronger.

## Spalling vs. exfoliation vs. sugaring

**Spalling** is a chip or fragment breaking away from a stone's face, often from moisture/freeze-thaw pressure at or near the surface. **Exfoliation** (or delamination) is thin layers peeling away roughly parallel to the surface, frequently a face-bedding symptom. **Sugaring** is a marble-specific deterioration where the surface disaggregates into loose, granular crystals, usually from acid rain or pollution attack on the calcite matrix.

**In use:** "That's not spalling, it's sugaring — this marble's losing its crystal bond at the surface, and repointing the joints won't touch it."

**Common misuse:** using "spalling" as a catch-all for any visible stone surface damage, when the specific failure mode (moisture pressure, bedding orientation, or chemical attack) determines the actual repair.
