# Vocabulary

Terms generalists conflate or use loosely that a mason keeps sharply separate. Each: definition, how a practitioner actually uses it in a sentence, and the common misuse to avoid.

## Wythe vs. course

A **wythe** is a continuous vertical layer of masonry one unit thick (a cavity wall typically has two wythes with an air space between). A **course** is one horizontal row of units running the length of a wall.

**In use:** "We're two wythes with a 2-inch cavity — don't confuse a tie that spans the cavity with reinforcement within a single wythe."

**Common misuse:** using "wythe" and "course" interchangeably; they describe orthogonal directions of the wall (vertical layering vs. horizontal rows).

## Veneer wall vs. load-bearing (structural) wall

A **veneer wall** is a non-structural masonry facing tied back to a structural backup (steel stud, wood frame, or CMU) that actually carries building loads; the veneer carries only its own weight. A **load-bearing wall** carries building loads (floors, roof) directly through the masonry itself.

**In use:** "This is veneer over steel stud — the tie schedule and mortar type follow the veneer table, not the structural wall table."

**Common misuse:** applying load-bearing mortar-type or reinforcement rules to a veneer assembly (or vice versa) because both are "brick walls" — the code tables and the failure modes are different for each.

## Control joint vs. expansion joint

A **control joint** is a built-in weak point that accommodates shrinkage, used in concrete masonry (CMU) as it cures and dries. An **expansion joint** accommodates growth, used in clay brick, which expands irreversibly over its service life from moisture and thermal exposure.

**In use:** "This is a CMU backup — we need control joints for shrinkage, and a separate expansion joint in the brick veneer for growth. They're not interchangeable, and they don't even move in the same direction."

**Common misuse:** using one term (or one detail) for both material types, or assuming a single joint serves both the CMU's shrinkage and the brick's expansion.

## Initial rate of absorption (IRA) / suction

The rate at which a dry masonry unit absorbs water from mortar in the first minute of contact, measured per ASTM C67 in grams per minute per 30 sq in.

**In use:** "This lot's suction came in at 45 — pre-wet before we lay tomorrow, or we're building in a weak bond nobody will see until the first hard freeze."

**Common misuse:** assuming suction from how a brick looks or feels, or skipping the test because a similar-looking brick from a different lot or supplier tested fine previously.

## Efflorescence vs. vanadium (or manganese) staining

**Efflorescence** is soluble salt migrating to the masonry surface as water evaporates — white, powdery, generally washable and often temporary. **Vanadium staining** is a greenish-yellow discoloration from vanadium salts in certain clay sources, chemically bound and not removable by washing.

**In use:** "That green cast isn't efflorescence — it's vanadium staining from this clay source, and a pressure wash won't touch it."

**Common misuse:** treating any surface discoloration as efflorescence and pressure-washing it, when a chemical staining issue needs a different (and sometimes irreversible) response.

## Flashing vs. weep hole

**Flashing** is the water-impermeable membrane or metal that collects water inside the cavity and directs it back out. A **weep hole** is the exit opening in the outer wythe that lets the collected water actually leave the wall.

**In use:** "The weeps are useless without continuous flashing underneath them — check the flashing before you check the weep spacing."

**Common misuse:** installing weep holes without verifying the flashing beneath is continuous and properly lapped/terminated — a weep with no flashing behind it just lets air in, not water out.

## Tuckpointing vs. repointing

**Tuckpointing** is a cosmetic technique using a thin contrasting mortar line to create the illusion of fine, precise joints (historically to imitate rubbed brick). **Repointing** is the structural replacement of deteriorated mortar joints with new mortar to restore weather-tightness and bond.

**In use:** "This isn't a tuckpointing job — the joints are eroded an inch deep, that's structural repointing, and it needs a mortar matched to the original, not whatever's on the truck."

**Common misuse:** using "tuckpointing" as a generic term for any mortar-joint repair; a client asking for "tuckpointing" when a wall actually needs repointing can end up with a cosmetic fix over a structural problem.

## Header, rowlock, and soldier course

A **header** course lays units with the short end (end) exposed; a **rowlock** lays units on their side with the end exposed; a **soldier** course stands units vertically on end with the long face exposed. Each creates a different bond pattern and, for headers, can tie two wythes together structurally.

**In use:** "That's a rowlock sill, not a header course — it won't tie the wythes together the way a true structural header would."

**Common misuse:** calling any brick turned on an unusual face a "header" generically, when the orientation determines whether it's doing structural tying work or purely decorative work.

## Corbeling vs. cantilevering

**Corbeling** is a stepped masonry projection where each course oversails the one below, relying on the compressive weight of masonry above to hold it in place, with a per-course and cumulative projection limit. **Cantilevering** is the general engineering term for any structural member projecting unsupported beyond its support, which in masonry usually requires added reinforcement or a different structural system entirely, not just stacked units.

**In use:** "We're at the corbel's cumulative limit already — if it needs to project further than that, it's a cantilever detail and needs an engineer, not another course of corbeling."

**Common misuse:** extending a corbel past its typical cumulative projection limit (commonly cited around 1/3 unit width per course, with a firm cumulative cap) and calling it "just a corbel" without an engineering check for the added overhang.

## Mortar Type N vs. Type S vs. Type M (ASTM C270)

Application-specific mortar classes distinguished by compressive strength and flexibility: **Type N** (750 psi min) for above-grade veneer and general above-grade masonry, **Type S** (1,800 psi) for higher-load or higher-exposure load-bearing walls, **Type M** (2,500 psi) for below-grade and high lateral-load conditions.

**In use:** "This is above-grade veneer, so it's Type N — going to M just makes the assembly stiffer than it needs to be for what it has to tolerate."

**Common misuse:** picking a mortar type by "stronger is safer" instead of by the application table; a stiffer-than-needed mortar reduces the assembly's ability to flex with differential movement.

## Freeze-thaw grade (SW/MW/NW) vs. saturation coefficient

**SW/MW/NW** are ASTM C216 freeze-thaw durability grades (Severe, Moderate, Negligible Weathering) assigned to facing brick. The **saturation coefficient** — the ratio of a unit's 24-hour cold-water absorption to its boiling-water absorption — is the underlying test value the grade is derived from; a lower coefficient means more freeze-thaw-resistant pore structure.

**In use:** "Check the saturation coefficient on the data sheet, not just the grade label — we need SW for this climate, and I want to see the number, not take the grade on faith."

**Common misuse:** assuming any fired brick is freeze-thaw safe regardless of grade, or specifying/approving brick for an exterior wall in a Severe or Moderate Weathering region without confirming the grade on the actual submittal data sheet.

## Shelf angle vs. lintel

A **lintel** spans a wall opening (door, window) to support the masonry above it. A **shelf angle** is a steel angle at a floor line that transfers the weight of the veneer above back to the structural frame, interrupting the veneer's continuous vertical load path — which is exactly why a horizontal expansion joint belongs directly beneath it.

**In use:** "There's a shelf angle at every floor line on this high-rise veneer — that's a horizontal expansion joint location too, not just a steel detail."

**Common misuse:** treating a shelf angle as a purely structural steel detail and omitting the expansion joint beneath it, since the angle deflects under load and the veneer above it needs somewhere to move relative to the veneer below.

## Mortar joint tooling: concave vs. weathered vs. struck

**Concave** and **weathered** joint profiles slope to shed water off the wall face. A **struck** joint is tooled with the reverse slope, leaving a small horizontal ledge at the top of the joint that can collect rather than shed water.

**In use:** "Tool these concave — it's the exposed weather face, and a struck joint here just builds in a place for water to sit."

**Common misuse:** using a struck joint profile on an exterior exposed face for speed or a preferred look, without accounting for the water-shedding difference in a wind-driven-rain condition.
