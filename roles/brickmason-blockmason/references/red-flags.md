# Red flags

Smell tests a mason or inspector catches during a walkthrough, before the wall closes and the defect becomes invisible. Format for each: what it usually means, the first question to ask, and the data to pull before anyone signs off.

## 1. Wall run exceeds ~25 ft with no control/expansion joint shown or built

**Usually means:** the joint schedule was never coordinated between structural and architectural drawings, or the mason assumed the CMU backup would absorb all movement without a dedicated joint in the veneer.

**First question:** "What's the joint spacing on the elevation, and when was it last checked against BIA TN 18A or NCMA TEK 10-2/10-3?"

**Data to pull:** elevation drawings with joint locations marked, actual wall length and height, wythe material (brick vs. CMU) on each side of the cavity.

## 2. No visible weep holes above grade or above a shelf angle/lintel

**Usually means:** weeps were skipped on the assumption that brick "sheds water fine," or the flashing beneath was never properly lapped/terminated so there was nowhere for a weep to drain to anyway.

**First question:** "Where's the flashing at this line, and are weeps spaced at or under 24 in o.c. immediately above it?"

**Data to pull:** cavity-wall detail drawing, flashing termination photos taken before the wall closed, measured weep spacing.

## 3. Mortar type specified doesn't match the wall's structural role (e.g., Type M called out for above-grade veneer)

**Usually means:** the mortar spec was copied from a structural or below-grade detail without adjusting for a veneer condition; less often, the engineer over-specified strength as a perceived safety margin without weighing flexibility.

**First question:** "Is this wall load-bearing or veneer, and does the mortar type match ASTM C270's recommended use for that condition?"

**Data to pull:** wall section detail showing load path, load-bearing vs. veneer designation, ASTM C270 application table.

## 4. New brick lot laid without an IRA/suction test

**Usually means:** the crew assumed suction is similar to the last job's brick, or no suction test kit was on site for this delivery.

**First question:** "Has this specific lot been suction-tested, and were units pre-wetted if IRA exceeded 30 g/min/30 in²?"

**Data to pull:** brick delivery lot/ticket number, suction test log (if any), weather and unit-moisture conditions at time of laying.

## 5. Mortar work continuing below 40°F with no cold-weather plan in effect

**Usually means:** schedule pressure is overriding the cold-weather protocol, or nobody on site is tracking the day's forecast against the threshold table.

**First question:** "What's today's temperature against our cold-weather trigger points, and which protection tier are we supposed to be in right now?"

**Data to pull:** daily ambient temperature log, cold-weather protection checklist (MCAA/TMS 602 Article 1.8), mortar and mixing-water temperature records.

## 6. Veneer-tie spacing wider than the code cap, or corroded/undersized ties found during a pre-closure inspection

**Usually means:** the installer skipped ties to save time, or the original tie schedule was under-specified for the wall's actual seismic design category or wind exposure.

**First question:** "What's the actual tie spacing and tributary area per tie, measured, against TMS 402's 2.667 sq ft max?"

**Data to pull:** tie spacing survey taken before the wall closed, tie type/gauge spec, project's seismic design category.

## 7. Efflorescence appearing within the first year on new masonry

**Usually means:** an active water-intrusion path exists — missing or failed flashing, undersized/blocked weep drainage, or high-IRA brick laid with excess mixing water — rarely "just normal new-brick salts" if it recurs after rain rather than fading.

**First question:** "Is this efflorescence washable, and does it come back after the next rain, or was it a one-time construction-moisture event?"

**Data to pull:** recurrence pattern over multiple rain events, moisture-meter readings at the affected area, flashing/weep inspection photos from construction if available.

## 8. Corbel projecting more than about 1/3 of the unit width per course, with no engineering sign-off

**Usually means:** the mason applied a rule-of-thumb per-course limit without checking the cumulative total projection or the backup wall's weight/anchorage capacity for that specific overhang.

**First question:** "What's the total cumulative projection from the wall face, and has an engineer confirmed the backup's weight and anchorage for it?"

**Data to pull:** corbel detail drawing with cumulative projection dimension, engineering calculation if the projection exceeds the typical per-course limit.

## 9. Brick submittal approved without a stated ASTM C216 freeze-thaw grade in a Severe or Moderate Weathering region

**Usually means:** the submittal review checked color, size, and texture but never pulled the grade (SW/MW/NW) against the project location's BIA weathering index.

**First question:** "What's the saturation coefficient/grade on this brick's data sheet, and does it match the local weathering index?"

**Data to pull:** brick manufacturer's ASTM C216 test data sheet, project location's BIA weathering-index classification, any documented spalling history for that brick line in a comparable climate.

## 10. Exterior mortar joints tooled "struck" (recessed ledge at the top of the joint) on an exposed weather face

**Usually means:** the crew defaulted to the fastest jointing tool/technique rather than the water-shedding profile the exposure calls for; struck joints leave a horizontal ledge that collects rather than sheds water.

**First question:** "Is this joint profile concave or weathered for water-shedding, or does it leave a ledge that holds standing water after rain?"

**Data to pull:** joint-profile close-up photos, mortar-joint spec section, the wall face's weathering exposure (prevailing wind-driven rain direction).
