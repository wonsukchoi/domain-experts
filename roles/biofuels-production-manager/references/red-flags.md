# Red flags — what a plant manager notices instantly

Smell tests with thresholds. Any single one can be innocent; two or three together are a pattern.

### Crush/crack spread margin per bushel (or gallon) under ~$0.30, trending down for 2+ consecutive weeks
- **Usually means:** feedstock and product prices are moving against the plant independent of operational performance — a market condition, not (yet) an operational one.
- **First question:** "Is this a spread problem or a yield problem — has per-unit conversion efficiency actually changed, or only the input/output prices?"
- **Data to pull:** daily spread tracking (feedstock cost vs. product + co-product revenue), yield/efficiency trend over the same window.

### Pressure to increase throughput specifically to "make up margin" during a spread compression
- **Usually means:** conflating a market problem with a volume problem — running harder during negative or thin unit margin usually makes the financial position worse, not better.
- **First question:** "What's the marginal cost per additional unit of throughput right now, including energy/enzyme/catalyst, and does it clear the current unit margin?"
- **Data to pull:** marginal cost curve at current vs. proposed throughput, unit margin at both levels.

### Feedstock quality testing skipped or averaged across a blended supply rather than tested per lot
- **Usually means:** the plant is running a fixed recipe against variable input, risking yield loss or quality failures that trace back to unaddressed feedstock variability.
- **First question:** "When was the last per-lot feedstock quality test, and does the current process recipe reflect it?"
- **Data to pull:** feedstock quality test logs (moisture, oil content, contaminants) by lot, process parameter adjustment log.

### Co-product (DDGS, glycerin, meal) sold at spot with no quality specification or marketing plan
- **Usually means:** co-product revenue — often a material share of plant economics — is being left on the table by treating it as a waste stream rather than a managed product line.
- **First question:** "What's the quality spec on this co-product batch, and is it being marketed to the buyer segment that pays the most for that spec?"
- **Data to pull:** co-product quality test results, realized co-product price vs. best available market price for that spec.

### Hedge ratio (% of feedstock or product volume price-locked) at 0% heading into a period of known price volatility
- **Usually means:** the plant is carrying full, unmanaged commodity price exposure on an input or output it doesn't set the price for.
- **First question:** "What's the plan if the spread moves another 20% against us from here — is that survivable unhedged?"
- **Data to pull:** current hedge ratio and instruments, spread volatility history, plant's margin sensitivity to a further adverse move.

### A yield-improvement initiative evaluated only on output volume gained, with no input-cost accounting attached
- **Usually means:** a process change is being judged as a win on a metric (yield) that doesn't capture whether it's actually profitable once energy/catalyst/enzyme cost is included.
- **First question:** "What did the additional input cost of this change come to, and does the incremental product value actually exceed it?"
- **Data to pull:** before/after input consumption (energy, enzyme, catalyst) per unit output, incremental product value at current prices.

### Major capital decision modeled assuming current blending mandate or fuel-credit levels hold for the full payback period
- **Usually means:** a policy-dependent revenue stream is being treated as a stable given rather than a risk factor, exposing the investment to a change the plant has no control over.
- **First question:** "What does the payback period look like under a plausible reduced-incentive scenario, not just the current-policy case?"
- **Data to pull:** capital project pro forma under current policy and under a stated downside policy scenario, incentive program's legislative/regulatory renewal history.

### Uptime or throughput reported without a parallel yield-per-unit-feedstock figure
- **Usually means:** the plant may be running more volume through the process at a worse conversion rate, which throughput or uptime numbers alone won't reveal.
- **First question:** "What's the yield per bushel/ton this period versus the trailing average, at this throughput level?"
- **Data to pull:** yield-per-unit-feedstock trend alongside throughput/uptime, by shift or period.

### Feedstock supplier concentration above ~50-60% from a single source with no contingency supplier qualified
- **Usually means:** a supply disruption (weather, logistics, supplier financial trouble) can stop or slow the plant with no fallback in place.
- **First question:** "If this supplier can't deliver next week, what's the actual fallback and how fast can it be activated?"
- **Data to pull:** supplier concentration by volume, qualification status of alternate suppliers, historical disruption events.
