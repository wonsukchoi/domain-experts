# Biofuels production working vocabulary

Terms a plant manager uses precisely. Format: definition → how a practitioner says it → common misuse.

**Crush spread** — In grain processing, the margin between the cost of the feedstock (e.g., corn, soybeans) and the combined value of the products and co-products it's converted into; the core profitability metric for a processing plant, independent of how efficiently the plant itself runs.
In use: "The crush spread is under $0.30/bu this week — even a perfectly-run plant is thin right now."
Misuse: treating a bad spread period as evidence the plant is being poorly operated, or a good spread period as evidence of operational excellence — the spread is largely a market condition layered on top of operational performance, not a measure of it.

**Crack spread** — The refining-industry equivalent of the crush spread: the margin between crude/feedstock cost and refined product value; the biofuels-adjacent term used when discussing renewable diesel or similar fuel economics.
In use: "Renewable diesel's crack spread compressed after the co-product credit dropped — that's a market move, not a yield issue."
Misuse: using crack spread and crush spread interchangeably across feedstock types without checking which margin convention actually applies to the specific product.

**Feedstock variability** — The natural, expected variation in moisture, oil/starch content, contaminant levels, and composition of a biological input across harvests, suppliers, and storage conditions.
In use: "This lot's moisture is running 2 points higher than spec — the dryer residence time needs adjusting before it goes into the process."
Misuse: treating feedstock as if it were a specified industrial raw material with a fixed composition; a process tuned to one assumed spec will underperform against real incoming variability.

**Co-product** — A secondary output of the primary conversion process (distillers grains, glycerin, meal) that carries independent market value, distinct from waste or byproduct with no economic value.
In use: "DDGS revenue is about 15% of total plant revenue this quarter — it's not a rounding error in the margin."
Misuse: referring to co-products as "byproducts" or "waste streams," language that undersells their role as a first-class economic lever in overall plant profitability.

**Yield (conversion efficiency)** — The quantity of finished product obtained per unit of feedstock processed (e.g., gallons of ethanol per bushel of corn), the core efficiency metric for the conversion process.
In use: "Yield came in at 2.78 gal/bu this week, just under the 2.80 target — checking enzyme dosing before assuming it's a feedstock issue."
Misuse: chasing maximum yield without checking the input cost (energy, enzyme, catalyst) required to achieve it — the highest-yield operating point isn't necessarily the most profitable one.

**Blending mandate** — A regulatory requirement that transportation fuel contain a minimum percentage of biofuel, a demand-side policy driver for the industry's output.
In use: "The blending mandate sets a floor on ethanol demand, but it's a policy lever, not a guaranteed permanent feature of the market."
Misuse: modeling long-term plant economics as if the current mandate level is fixed indefinitely, without scenario-planning for a policy change.

**Fuel credit (e.g., RIN, LCFS credit)** — A tradable credit generated by producing qualifying biofuel, whose market value is a real and often significant part of the product's total realized revenue, separate from the physical fuel's sale price.
In use: "RIN values dropped 30% this quarter — that hit realized revenue per gallon even though the physical ethanol price didn't move."
Misuse: quoting only the physical product price when discussing plant economics and omitting the credit value, which can be a large share of total realized revenue and moves somewhat independently of the physical commodity price.

**Denaturant** — A substance (commonly gasoline) added to fuel ethanol to render it undrinkable for tax and regulatory purposes, a required additive rather than an optional blending component.
In use: "Denaturant is added at the required 2% before the ethanol leaves the plant — it's a regulatory step, not a formulation choice."
Misuse: treating denaturant addition as a discretionary product-quality decision rather than a fixed regulatory requirement.

**Marginal input cost (in a yield-optimization context)** — The additional cost (energy, enzyme, catalyst) required to achieve one additional unit of yield improvement, the correct basis for evaluating whether a process change is actually profitable.
In use: "The marginal enzyme cost of that last yield bump exceeded the marginal product value — we backed the dosing off a notch."
Misuse: evaluating a process change purely on the resulting yield number without pairing it against the marginal input cost that produced it.

**Hedge ratio** — The percentage of expected feedstock purchases or product sales that are price-locked via forward contracts, futures, or options, as distinct from the percentage left exposed to spot market movement.
In use: "We're at a 40% hedge ratio on corn heading into this quarter — the rest is exposed to spot."
Misuse: describing a plant as "hedged" or "unhedged" as a binary rather than stating the actual ratio and instrument mix — most real positions are partial.

**Nameplate capacity vs. actual throughput** — Nameplate capacity is the plant's designed maximum production rate; actual throughput is what's currently being run, which may be below (turndown) or, briefly, above (debottlenecked) nameplate depending on market and operational conditions.
In use: "We're running at about 92% of nameplate capacity this month — pulled back slightly given the current spread."
Misuse: treating running below nameplate capacity as automatically a sign of poor performance; deliberately reducing throughput can be the correct economic response to a compressed spread.
