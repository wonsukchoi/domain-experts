# Vocabulary

### Baker's percentage
Expressing every ingredient as a percentage of total flour weight — flour is always 100% — independent of batch size.
**In use:** "This formula's 68% hydration — that's 680g water to 1,000g flour, whatever the batch size actually is."
**Common misuse:** Assuming the percentages refer to a percent of total dough weight, rather than specifically a percent of flour weight — a common source of scaling errors.

### Hydration
The ratio of water weight to flour weight in a dough, expressed as a percentage.
**In use:** "This is a 68% dough — expect a slacker handling feel than the 60% dough we ran last week."
**Common misuse:** Conflating a stated hydration percentage with "how wet the dough feels," when actual flour absorption can make the same stated hydration behave very differently across flour types and lots.

### Windowpane test
Stretching a small piece of dough thin enough to see light through it without tearing, used to judge gluten development.
**In use:** "It's tearing, not stretching — needs another few minutes of mixing before it'll windowpane clean."
**Common misuse:** Judging dough readiness purely by elapsed mixing time instead of this behavioral test, which is what actually reflects gluten development.

### Poke test
Pressing a floured finger into proofing dough and observing how quickly and how fully the indentation springs back, used to judge proof readiness.
**In use:** "Poke test's springing back slowly and only partway — that's ready, not underproofed."
**Common misuse:** Proofing strictly to a fixed clock time regardless of what the poke test's actual recovery behavior shows on that specific batch.

### Oven spring
The rapid rise a loaf undergoes in the first several minutes of baking as trapped gas expands before the crust sets.
**In use:** "No oven spring on this one — poke test before baking showed it was already overproofed."
**Common misuse:** Assuming poor oven spring is always an oven-temperature problem, when it's frequently a proofing-state problem (over- or under-proofed dough) that no oven adjustment can fix.

### Bulk fermentation
The first rise period after mixing, before dividing and shaping, during which flavor and structure develop.
**In use:** "Bulk's running long today — kitchen's a few degrees cooler than the formula assumes."
**Common misuse:** Treating bulk fermentation time as fixed regardless of the dough's actual temperature, which is the primary variable controlling how fast it actually proceeds.

### Autolyse
A rest period of flour and water only, before salt and yeast are added, allowing gluten to begin developing through hydration alone before mechanical mixing.
**In use:** "Autolyse for 30 minutes before adding the salt and yeast — cuts the mixing time needed after."
**Common misuse:** Confusing autolyse with the full mixing process; it specifically excludes salt and leavening to isolate hydration-driven gluten development from fermentation.

### Retarding
Slowing fermentation by refrigerating dough, used to develop flavor over a longer timeframe or to schedule production around a shift.
**In use:** "Retard it overnight in the cooler — bulk ferment continues slowly, just don't expect it to be paused."
**Common misuse:** Assuming retarded dough is simply "paused" and follows the same timeline once brought back to room temperature, rather than continuing to ferment slowly the whole time and needing its own separate schedule adjustment.

### Scoring
Cutting the surface of a proofed loaf before baking to direct where it expands, rather than tearing randomly.
**In use:** "Score it deeper on a wetter dough — it needs a clear release point for that much oven spring."
**Common misuse:** Treating scoring as purely decorative, when its functional purpose is controlling exactly where the loaf's oven spring releases.

### Leaven / starter
A live culture of wild yeast and bacteria maintained through regular feeding, used instead of or alongside commercial yeast.
**In use:** "Starter's at 100% hydration, fed 1:1:1, doubling in about 6 hours at this kitchen's temperature."
**Common misuse:** Treating starter activity as constant or self-sustaining, ignoring that its rise speed and flavor profile shift with feeding ratio, feeding interval, and ambient temperature.
