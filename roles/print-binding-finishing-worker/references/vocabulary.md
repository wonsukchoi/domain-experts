# Vocabulary

### Creep (shingling)
The progressive outward push of inner signatures relative to outer signatures in a saddle-stitched, nested booklet, caused by the accumulated thickness of the nested sheets — requires compensation at imposition as page count grows.
**In use:** "This is a 48-page booklet — creep's going to be visible if we don't shingle the imposition to compensate."
**Common misuse:** Treating creep as a defect to fix after the fact rather than a mechanical certainty to compensate for at imposition — by the time a trimmed sample shows uneven margins, the fix requires reimposing and rerunning, not adjusting the finished piece.

### Grain direction
The predominant alignment of paper fibers in a sheet, established during manufacturing, which determines whether a fold runs with or against the fibers.
**In use:** "Check the grain before you set up the fold — this coated stock will crack if we fold against it."
**Common misuse:** Assuming grain direction is irrelevant for "just paper" or assuming it's the same on every stock a shop carries — grain direction varies by manufacturer and sheet size, and has to be verified per stock lot, not assumed from memory.

### Perfect binding
A binding method where pages are glued directly to a spine (as opposed to stitched or sewn), commonly used for paperback books and thicker catalogs.
**In use:** "This one's perfect-bound — we need to confirm the glue's rated for this coated cover stock before we run the full quantity."
**Common misuse:** Treating "perfect bound" as a guarantee of durability regardless of glue-stock match — the name describes the binding method, not a quality outcome, and a mismatched glue formulation on coated stock produces pages that pull loose.

### Saddle-stitching
A binding method where folded signatures are nested inside one another and stapled through the fold at the spine — common for booklets and magazines up to a practical page-count limit.
**In use:** "We're saddle-stitching this one — at 64 pages we're near the upper end of what holds together well with this stock."
**Common misuse:** Treating saddle-stitch as suitable for any page count without regard to spine bulk or creep — beyond a certain thickness, the stitched spine and accumulated creep both become visually and structurally problematic, and perfect binding becomes the better method.

### Collating (gathering)
The process of assembling individual signatures or sheets in the correct sequence to form a complete book or booklet before binding.
**In use:** "Collating's running on the six-station gatherer — we spot-check every fiftieth copy for a missing or out-of-sequence signature."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a collating machine's built-in sensor catches every possible error — sensors typically detect a missing signature at their station, but a duplicate or out-of-sequence error from an upstream station can still pass through undetected without a manual spot-check.

### Scoring
Creasing a sheet along the intended fold line before folding, to control where and how the paper fiber breaks, reducing cracking on coated or heavy stock.
**In use:** "This cover stock's heavy enough that we're scoring before folding, not just running it straight through the folder."
**Common misuse:** Treating scoring as unnecessary extra work for "just a fold" — on coated or heavier stock, skipping scoring is a common direct cause of visible cracking at the fold line.

### Die-cutting
Using a shaped steel-rule or rotary die to cut a printed sheet into a custom shape (beyond a simple rectangular trim), commonly used for packaging, labels, and specialty pieces.
**In use:** "Die's registered to the press sheet's marks — if registration drifted since the proof, we'll cut through live content instead of the trim line."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a die-cutting setup that worked on the proof sheet will automatically stay registered through a full production run — press-sheet registration can drift during a run and needs periodic verification against the die.

### Trim tolerance
The real, mechanical variance in a cutting device's actual cut position relative to its programmed setting, distinct from a design's intended trim line.
**In use:** "Our guillotine's tolerance is about a sixteenth of an inch — anything closer to the trim line than that is a real risk, not a hypothetical one."
**Common misuse:** Treating a trimmer's tolerance as a worst-case edge condition that "probably won't happen" rather than a routine variance that shows up across a normal production run.

### Signature
A single printed sheet folded down into a section of multiple pages, which gets nested or gathered with other signatures to form the complete bound piece.
**In use:** "Each signature on this job is eight pages — thirty-two total pages means four signatures nested for saddle-stitching."
**Common misuse:** Confusing "signature" with "page" — a signature is the folded sheet unit containing multiple pages, and page-count-to-signature-count math depends on how many pages each folded sheet actually contains.

### Spine bulk
The physical thickness of a bound piece's spine, determined by page count and stock caliper, which affects binding method choice and cover fit.
**In use:** "Spine bulk on this 300-page catalog is over three-eighths of an inch — cover needs to be scored to match, not just folded flat."
**Common misuse:** Designing a cover without accounting for the actual calculated spine bulk of the specific stock and page count being used — a cover sized for an assumed spine thickness that doesn't match the actual bound piece creates a visible fit mismatch.
