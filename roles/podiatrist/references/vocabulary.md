# Podiatrist — Vocabulary

### Loss of protective sensation (LOPS)
Absence of the ability to feel a 10g monofilament at one or more standard testing sites, indicating the patient cannot reliably feel pain, pressure, or temperature well enough to protect the foot from injury.
**In use:** "He's got LOPS at 3 of 4 sites, so his lack of pain doesn't rule anything out."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a formality on an exam checklist rather than a finding that changes how much weight to put on the patient's symptom report going forward.

### Ankle-brachial index (ABI)
The ratio of systolic blood pressure at the ankle to systolic blood pressure at the brachial artery, used to screen for peripheral arterial disease.
**In use:** "ABI came back at 0.4 — hold debridement and get vascular surgery involved before we touch this wound."
**Common misuse:** Assumed reliable in all patients; in long-standing diabetics, calcified non-compressible vessels can produce a falsely elevated ABI that looks reassuring when perfusion is actually poor.

### Wagner classification
A 0–5 wound-severity grading system for diabetic foot ulcers based on depth and tissue involvement (0 = intact at-risk skin, 5 = whole-foot gangrene).
**In use:** "It's a Wagner 2 — extends to the capsule but no bone involvement yet."
**Common misuse:** Used as the sole severity indicator, ignoring that it doesn't score infection or ischemia — a low Wagner grade with active infection can be more urgent than a higher grade without it.

### Probe-to-bone test
A bedside test where a sterile blunt probe is inserted into a wound to check for direct contact with bone, used as a rapid osteomyelitis screen.
**In use:** "Probe-to-bone was positive, so we're treating this as osteomyelitis pending imaging confirmation."
**Common misuse:** A negative result is treated as ruling out osteomyelitis; it doesn't, especially in early or partially-treated infections, and a negative probe with high clinical suspicion still warrants imaging.

### Charcot neuroarthropathy
Progressive joint and bone destruction in a neuropathic foot, often triggered by unrecognized minor trauma the patient couldn't feel, leading to deformity and collapse of the foot's arch structure.
**In use:** "The foot's warm, swollen, and deformed with near-normal X-rays — this is acute Charcot until proven otherwise, not cellulitis."
**Common misuse:** Misdiagnosed as cellulitis or gout because early presentations can look inflammatory before bony changes are visible on imaging.

### Offloading
Redistributing weight-bearing pressure away from a wound or at-risk area, typically via a cast, boot, or custom device.
**In use:** "The dressing's fine, but without offloading this wound will keep reopening every time she walks."
**Common misuse:** Treated as optional or secondary to wound-care technique, when for pressure-driven ulcers it's frequently the primary determinant of whether the wound closes at all.

### University of Texas (UT) classification
A wound classification system that scores both depth (grade 0–III) and the presence of infection and/or ischemia (stage A–D), used alongside or instead of Wagner grading.
**In use:** "UT stage D grade II — infected and ischemic, this needs a multidisciplinary team today."
**Common misuse:** Confused with Wagner grading as interchangeable; UT captures a dimension (infection/ischemia) that Wagner grade alone does not.

### Total contact cast (TCC)
A custom-molded, non-removable cast that distributes plantar pressure evenly across the foot, considered a gold-standard offloading method for plantar ulcers.
**In use:** "She's not a candidate for a removable boot given her adherence history — total contact cast it is."
**Common misuse:** Assumed interchangeable with a removable cast walker; a TCC's non-removability is the point, since it removes patient adherence as a failure mode, which a removable device cannot.

### Peripheral arterial disease (PAD)
Narrowing of arteries supplying the limbs, reducing blood flow and impairing wound healing.
**In use:** "His ABI and pulse exam both point to significant PAD — that's why this ulcer isn't closing despite good wound care."
**Common misuse:** Conflated with peripheral neuropathy; the two commonly coexist in diabetics but are distinct conditions requiring separate screening (vascular exam vs. monofilament testing) and separate management.

### Percent area reduction (wound healing trajectory)
The percentage decrease in wound surface area between two assessment points, used as a predictive marker for whether a wound is on track to heal.
**In use:** "We're at 57% area reduction at four weeks — above the 50% benchmark, so we're staying the course."
**Common misuse:** Ignored in favor of subjective "looks better" assessments, when the quantitative trajectory is a better predictor of eventual closure than visual impression at a single visit.

### Debridement
Removal of nonviable (necrotic or non-healing) tissue from a wound to promote healing.
**In use:** "Debrided the callused wound margin — that rim of hyperkeratotic tissue was mechanically blocking closure."
**Common misuse:** Treated as always beneficial regardless of vascular status; debriding a wound with inadequate perfusion can extend tissue loss rather than promote healing.

### Semmes-Weinstein monofilament
A standardized nylon filament calibrated to bend at 10 grams of force, used to test for loss of protective sensation at standard plantar sites.
**In use:** "Monofilament testing at the four standard sites — patient felt it at the hallux but not at any of the three metatarsal heads."
**Common misuse:** Applied inconsistently (wrong sites, insufficient pressure to cause the filament to bend, testing over callused skin) in a way that produces an unreliable result while appearing to have completed the screen.
