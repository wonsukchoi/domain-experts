# Podiatrist — Red Flags

### Ankle-brachial index below 0.5, or absent pedal pulses with a non-healing wound
- **Usually means:** Severe peripheral arterial disease; second most likely is a non-compressible vessel giving a falsely elevated reading masking equally severe disease.
- **First question:** Is there an open wound present, and does it show any sign of infection that can't wait for a vascular workup?
- **Data to pull:** ABI (both limbs), toe-brachial index if non-compressible, prior vascular imaging if available.

### Spreading erythema beyond 2cm of a wound margin with fever or elevated white count
- **Usually means:** Cellulitis progressing to a limb- or life-threatening soft-tissue infection; second most likely is early necrotizing fasciitis if pain is disproportionate to exam findings.
- **First question:** Are systemic signs present (fever, tachycardia, hypotension), and is pain out of proportion to the visible findings?
- **Data to pull:** Vital signs, CBC with differential, blood cultures if systemically ill, immediate surgical consult if necrotizing infection is suspected.

### Positive probe-to-bone test on a chronic ulcer
- **Usually means:** Osteomyelitis; second most likely is a false positive from probing through granulation tissue into an adjacent non-infected bony prominence.
- **First question:** How long has the wound been present, and does the clinical suspicion (chronicity, prior treatment failure, wound over a bony prominence) support imaging even if the probe result is ambiguous?
- **Data to pull:** Plain film, ESR/CRP trend, MRI if suspicion remains after a negative probe.

### Absent monofilament sensation at 2 or more of 4 standard sites in a patient with no known diabetic neuropathy diagnosis
- **Usually means:** Previously undiagnosed peripheral neuropathy; second most likely is a non-diabetic neuropathy (alcohol, B12 deficiency, chemotherapy-induced) requiring separate workup.
- **First question:** Is there a known diagnosis explaining the sensory loss, or does this warrant referral for a broader neuropathy workup?
- **Data to pull:** HbA1c if not recent, B12 level, alcohol/medication history.

### New foot deformity with pain out of proportion to imaging findings, in a diabetic patient
- **Usually means:** Acute Charcot neuroarthropathy, frequently misdiagnosed as cellulitis or gout because early X-rays can look near-normal; second most likely is a true fracture in a patient with reduced sensation who can't reliably report the injury.
- **First question:** Is the foot warmer than the contralateral foot by more than a few degrees (temperature asymmetry is a key early Charcot sign)?
- **Data to pull:** Bilateral skin temperature comparison, weight-bearing X-rays, MRI if X-rays are unremarkable but suspicion remains high.

### Wound with less than 50% area reduction at the 4-week reassessment checkpoint
- **Usually means:** Undiagnosed infection (including occult osteomyelitis), inadequate offloading compliance, or unrecognized ischemia; second most likely is an incorrect initial classification that missed one of these factors.
- **First question:** Has the offloading device actually been worn, or is non-adherence explaining the stall?
- **Data to pull:** Repeat ABI, imaging for osteomyelitis, direct conversation (not assumption) about device adherence.

### Gangrenous or black tissue change at a wound margin or digit
- **Usually means:** Progressing tissue necrosis from critical limb ischemia; second most likely is a dry gangrene demarcation that may auto-amputate without urgent intervention if perfusion elsewhere is adequate.
- **First question:** Is this wet (infected, spreading) or dry (demarcated, stable) gangrene — the two require different urgency?
- **Data to pull:** Vascular surgery consult, ABI/imaging, infection markers.

### Rapidly worsening pain in a patient with known loss of protective sensation
- **Usually means:** A patient with LOPS reporting pain at all is itself a red flag — it suggests either a severe process (deep infection, ischemia) exceeding what reduced sensation would normally mask, or a process outside the typical neuropathic-ulcer pattern (fracture, foreign body).
- **First question:** Is the pain new, and does it correlate with a visible or imaging-confirmed finding, or is it unexplained?
- **Data to pull:** Full re-exam including imaging, do not attribute to "just the ulcer" without confirming.

### First presentation of a diabetic foot ulcer in a patient with no prior podiatric care
- **Usually means:** Undiagnosed or poorly controlled diabetes with no prior risk-stratification; second most likely is a patient who has been risk-stratified elsewhere but not communicated it.
- **First question:** What is the current HbA1c and diabetes duration, and has this patient ever had a comprehensive foot exam?
- **Data to pull:** HbA1c, prior podiatric records if any exist, full baseline vascular/sensory/structural exam.
