# Personal Care Aide Playbook

Filled sequences for the recurring situations a PCA runs, not descriptions of them. Adapt the numbers to the client's actual care plan — these are the structure and the thresholds, not a substitute for it.

## 1. Start-of-visit care-plan check (2–3 minutes, every visit)

1. Pull up the current written care plan (paper binder in the home or agency app) — confirm nothing changed since the last visit.
2. Confirm today's authorized tasks against a standard checklist:
   - Bathing method (shower/tub/bed bath) and assist level (standby, one-person, two-person)
   - Transfer/mobility method (independent, gait belt, mechanical lift) and any equipment specified
   - Medication reminders: in scope or not, and exactly what "reminder" means in this state/agency (verbal cue only vs. handing a pre-filled organizer)
   - Meal prep restrictions (diabetic diet, pureed/thickened liquids, allergy list)
3. If anything about today's request from the client or family conflicts with the plan, do the plan's version and note the conflict — don't improvise a middle ground.

## 2. ADL/transfer decision sequence

| Step | Question | If yes | If no |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Can the client bear full weight and follow a one-step verbal cue? | Independent transfer with standby assist | Go to step 2 |
| 2 | Does the care plan authorize a one-person gait-belt assist for this client? | Gait belt, standard transfer technique | Go to step 3 |
| 3 | Is a mechanical/Hoyer-style lift specified in the plan? | Use the lift exactly as trained — never approximate with a manual lift because "it's just this once" | Do not attempt the transfer; call the supervisor before proceeding |

Never lift more than roughly 35 lb of a client's own weight manually (NIOSH-derived safe-patient-handling reference) — if the client can't reliably assist, that's a mechanical-lift or two-person-assist situation regardless of how the last visit went.

## 3. Weight/intake trend log (fill every visit, compare against the last 2)

| Date | Weight | Meal % eaten | Fluid intake (oz) | Notable change from last visit |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| e.g. Mon 6/2 | 132 lb | 80% lunch | 16 of 16 offered | baseline |
| e.g. Wed 6/4 | 129 lb | 50% lunch | 10 of 16 offered | 3 lb down, appetite down |
| e.g. Fri 6/6 | 126 lb | 30% lunch | 8 of 16 offered | 6 lb down in 5 days — report threshold crossed |

**Report threshold:** roughly 5% of body weight lost in a month, or a pace that would clearly exceed that if it continued (as in the example: 4.5% in 5 days) → same-day call, not end-of-week mention.

## 4. Skin-check description template (used during bathing/dressing assist, never a stage)

```
Location: [specific body part, e.g. "left heel"]
Size: [measurement in cm if visible, or "approx." if not measurable safely]
Color: [e.g. "non-blanchable red," "purple," "pale"]
Temperature/texture: [e.g. "warm to touch," "boggy," "dry and intact around edges"]
New since: [last visit date, or "first noticed today"]
Action taken: [e.g. "repositioned," "reported to on-call RN 1:15pm"]
```
Never write a stage number or a diagnostic label (e.g. "stage 2," "looks infected") — that's the nurse's assessment, built from this description.

## 5. Escalation call script (adapt names/numbers to the actual visit)

```
"This is [aide name] on [client]'s [day] visit.
I want to flag [a pattern / an event], not just today's number:
[state the trend or event factually, with the specific numbers/dates].
[State what the client said, quoted, if relevant — e.g. 'she says she's just tired'].
I haven't changed anything about how I'm caring for her.
I'd like someone to [see her before the next visit / call the family / advise next steps]."
```
Match the phone report word-for-word in the written visit log/EVV entry, with the specific numbers and no added interpretation.

## 6. Home fall-risk / safety pass (once per week or on any noted mobility change)

- Clear paths: loose rugs, cords, clutter between bed/chair and bathroom
- Lighting: nightlight or accessible light switch between bed and bathroom
- Grab bars/rails present and secure where the plan specifies them
- Footwear: non-skid, properly fitting, not loose slippers, during transfers
- If the client has started needing assistance for a transfer that was independent last visit, treat that alone as a fall-risk report — don't wait for an actual fall to flag it.

## 7. Refusal-of-care fallback sequence (in preference order)

1. Ask once, plainly, why (pain, fear, fatigue, dignity) — often changes the approach, not just the outcome.
2. Offer the same task at a different point in the visit or with a modification the plan allows (e.g., shower instead of bath, later in the visit).
3. If still refused and it's a safety-relevant task (e.g., a needed transfer, wound-adjacent care), don't force it — document the refusal factually and the reason given, and report it the same day.
4. Never substitute a family member's on-the-spot instruction for the client's own refusal or the written plan — report the refusal and let the case manager/RN decide next steps.
