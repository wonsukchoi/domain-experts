# Vocabulary

### Care plan
The written document, set by an agency RN or Medicaid case manager, that defines exactly which tasks are authorized, at what assist level, and how often.
**In use:** "The plan says one-person gait-belt transfer — it doesn't say two-person, so if she can't do it that way today, that's a call to the supervisor, not a judgment call for me."
**Common misuse:** Treating the care plan as a rough guide the aide can adjust in the moment based on how the client seems that day — it's an authorization boundary, not a suggestion.

### ADL (activities of daily living)
The basic self-care tasks — bathing, dressing, toileting, transferring, eating — a PCA is authorized to assist with.
**In use:** "She's independent with feeding but needs assist for the other four ADLs."
**Common misuse:** Used as a catch-all for anything physical an aide does, including tasks (wound care, medication administration) that are explicitly outside ADL scope and outside the aide's authorization entirely.

### IADL (instrumental activities of daily living)
Higher-level home-management tasks — light meal prep, laundry, light housekeeping, medication reminders — distinct from ADLs and often the larger share of a PCA's authorized task list.
**In use:** "Most of this visit is IADL — laundry and meal prep — she only needs ADL help with bathing."
**Common misuse:** Assuming IADL support (like meal prep) implies authorization for medically adjacent tasks like managing a special diet's clinical rationale — the aide follows the diet restriction listed, not the reasoning behind it.

### Medication reminder (vs. administration)
A verbal cue or handing over an already-filled organizer, where state law allows it — distinct from administration, which a PCA never performs.
**In use:** "I remind her at noon and hand her the organizer — I don't open it or decide the dose."
**Common misuse:** Treating "reminder" and "administration" as a difference of degree rather than a hard legal line; opening a pre-filled box to help a client who's fumbling with it can cross into administration depending on the state.

### Scope of practice
The set of tasks a PCA is legally and professionally authorized to perform, set by the state and the agency — narrower than a CNA's, since a PCA has no clinical training component.
**In use:** "Taking her blood pressure isn't in my scope — that's something the visiting nurse does."
**Common misuse:** Assuming scope is a matter of comfort or experience ("I've done this before, I know how") rather than a legal boundary that doesn't move with the aide's confidence.

### Baseline
This specific client's normal range for weight, appetite, mood, cognition, and function — built from the aide's own visit history with them, not a generic population norm.
**In use:** "That's actually her baseline confusion level in the evening — it's the daytime confusion today that's new."
**Common misuse:** Comparing a client only against textbook-normal ranges instead of against their own trend, which misses the earliest and most useful signal a long-term aide has.

### Reportable vs. diagnosable
The same observe-and-report discipline every unlicensed direct-care role runs on: describe what was seen, not what it means clinically.
**In use:** "I reported the redness and the temperature — I didn't say it was a pressure sore, that's not mine to call."
**Common misuse:** Waiting to have an explanation before reporting, which delays the one thing the supervisor actually needs — the raw, timely observation.

### EVV (Electronic Visit Verification)
The mandated system (federally required for Medicaid personal-care and home-health services since the 21st Century Cures Act) that logs visit start/end time, location, and tasks performed.
**In use:** "Clock in through the EVV app when you arrive, not before — a mismatch between the app time and the actual visit gets flagged."
**Common misuse:** Treating EVV as a punch-clock formality rather than the primary record an agency and state auditor will use to verify the visit happened as billed.

### Care conference / case review
A periodic meeting (agency-scheduled, often monthly or at a plan change) where the RN/case manager, sometimes family, and occasionally the aide review whether the current plan still fits the client's needs.
**In use:** "Put the appetite trend in your notes so it's ready for the care conference, even if it doesn't need a same-day call."
**Common misuse:** Saving genuinely urgent findings for the next care conference instead of same-day report — the conference is for patterns and plan adjustments, not for time-sensitive findings.

### Standby assist vs. hands-on assist
The care-plan's specified assist level for a transfer or ADL — standby means the aide stays close but doesn't physically support the client; hands-on means physical support is given.
**In use:** "She's standby assist for the shower transfer but hands-on for getting dressed afterward — don't upgrade to hands-on without a reason and a note."
**Common misuse:** Silently upgrading or downgrading the assist level based on how the aide feels the client is doing that day, without reporting the change — the level is a plan element, and a persistent change in what's actually needed is itself a reportable finding.

### Mandatory reporting (elder abuse/neglect)
The legal obligation, present in every state in some form, to report suspected abuse, neglect, or financial exploitation of a vulnerable adult to the state's adult protective services, typically within a short fixed window (commonly 24 hours).
**In use:** "That bruise pattern doesn't match her story — I'm making the mandatory report today, not waiting to see if it happens again."
**Common misuse:** Treating mandatory reporting as optional or something to defer until the aide is "sure" — the legal threshold is reasonable suspicion, not certainty.

### Person-centered care
An approach that organizes the visit around the client's own preferences, pace, and history rather than a fixed task-completion order.
**In use:** "She always wants her hair done before breakfast, not after — that's not a deviation from the plan, that's the plan working as intended."
**Common misuse:** Used as a justification for skipping a plan-required task the client would rather avoid — person-centered means adapting *how*, not whether, an authorized and needed task gets done.
