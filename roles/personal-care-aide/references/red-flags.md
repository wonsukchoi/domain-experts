# Red flags

### Unintentional weight loss ≥5% of body weight in a month (or a faster pace, e.g. >2–3 lb/week for a smaller adult)
- **Usually means:** Reduced appetite from illness, depression, medication side effect, or dental/swallowing problem; second most likely is a shift in who's preparing/offering food (caregiver change, reduced supervision at meals).
- **First question:** Has intake (not just weight) actually dropped, or is this a hydration/scale-consistency issue — same scale, same time of day, same clothing?
- **Data to pull:** The aide's own weight/meal-intake log for the last 3 visits; care plan's dietary section; whether a new medication started in the same window.

### New non-blanchable redness or open area over a bony prominence (heel, sacrum, hip, elbow)
- **Usually means:** Early pressure injury from reduced mobility or time in one position; second most likely is friction/shear from an assist technique that needs adjusting.
- **First question:** Was this area intact at the last visit, and has time-in-bed or mobility changed since then?
- **Data to pull:** Prior visit's skin-check note (if any); repositioning schedule in the care plan; any recent change in mobility/transfer ability.

### A transfer or task the client managed independently last visit now needs assistance
- **Usually means:** New pain, a UTI or other infection (common in older adults presenting as functional decline before fever), or a medication effect; second most likely is fear after a near-fall the client didn't report.
- **First question:** Did this happen suddenly (today) or gradually (over the last 2–3 visits)?
- **Data to pull:** Visit-to-visit function notes; medication list for anything started/changed recently; whether the client mentioned any fall or near-fall.

### A fall, even one the client says was "no big deal"
- **Usually means:** A genuine mechanical trip/slip, or an early sign of a mobility/medical change (orthostatic drop, new weakness); second most likely is an environmental hazard that will cause a repeat fall if untouched.
- **First question:** Did the client lose consciousness, hit their head, or have any pain on movement afterward — even if they say they're fine?
- **Data to pull:** Time and circumstances of the fall; medication list for anything affecting blood pressure/alertness; the home fall-risk checklist from the last pass.

### Client or family requests a task outside the written care plan (medication administration, wound care, extra hours)
- **Usually means:** A genuine unmet need the care plan hasn't caught up to yet; second most likely is a family member testing the aide's boundaries under time or financial pressure.
- **First question:** Is this a one-time ask or has it come up before — and does the agency know the current plan may be insufficient?
- **Data to pull:** Care plan's authorized-task list; date of the plan's last review; prior notes about similar requests.

### Confusion, disorientation, or behavior that's new for this specific client
- **Usually means:** Infection (UTI is common and often presents as confusion before other symptoms in older adults), medication effect, or dehydration; second most likely is a normal fluctuation for a client with an existing cognitive diagnosis — compare against their actual baseline, not a generic one.
- **First question:** Is this different from how this client normally presents, or within their known day-to-day range?
- **Data to pull:** Prior visit notes describing baseline cognition/behavior; hydration and intake log; medication list.

### Medication not taken as reminded, or found untaken from a prior visit
- **Usually means:** The client forgot, is intentionally skipping (side effects, cost, denial of need), or a family member administered it outside the aide's knowledge; second most likely is a genuine plan gap (reminder timing doesn't match the client's actual routine).
- **First question:** Is this the first time, or a pattern across visits?
- **Data to pull:** Medication-reminder log; pill organizer/count if visible; whether family reports administering doses themselves.

### Bruising in an unusual location, or an injury with an explanation that doesn't match the injury
- **Usually means:** A genuine accidental fall/bump the client can't fully recall; second most likely (and the one that must not be dismissed) is a possible abuse or neglect indicator requiring mandatory reporting under state elder-abuse law.
- **First question:** Does the explanation given match the location and pattern of the injury?
- **Data to pull:** Photo/description per agency policy; state mandatory-reporting timeline (commonly 24 hours); prior incident history for this client.

### Home environment newly unsafe (spoiled food, unpaid utility shutoff notice, hoarding progression, no working smoke detector)
- **Usually means:** Cognitive or physical decline outpacing the current care-plan hours; second most likely is a financial-exploitation or neglect-by-others situation.
- **First question:** Is this new since the last visit, or a longstanding condition the plan already accounts for?
- **Data to pull:** Home fall-risk/safety checklist history; care-plan hours vs. what the home actually needs; whether other family/support is involved.

### Client expresses a wish to stop treatment, refuses food/fluids entirely, or makes a comment suggesting self-harm
- **Usually means:** Depends heavily on context (grief, depression, genuine end-of-life wish, frustration) — this is explicitly outside a PCA's scope to assess, not to log and move past.
- **First question:** Is this a first-time statement or something the client has said before?
- **Data to pull:** None the aide should try to gather beyond the client's own words, quoted exactly — this goes to the supervisor/RN the same day regardless.
