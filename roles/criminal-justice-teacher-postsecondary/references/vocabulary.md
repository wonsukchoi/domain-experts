### Crime rate (per 100,000)
Offense count divided by population, scaled to a common base (usually 100,000), so counts from places or years of different population sizes can be compared.
**In use:** "Don't hand me the count difference — give me the rate per 100,000 for both years before we talk trend."
**Common misuse:** Comparing raw counts across years or jurisdictions with different populations and calling the difference a trend.

### NIBRS (National Incident-Based Reporting System)
The FBI's incident-based crime-reporting system, mandatory nationwide from January 1, 2021, which records every offense in an incident rather than only the single most serious one.
**In use:** "That 'spike' in 2021 is partly the NIBRS cutover — check whether the pre-2021 series was converted before you compare it."
**Common misuse:** Treating pre-2021 (Summary Reporting System) and post-2021 (NIBRS) totals as one continuous, directly comparable series.

### Hierarchy Rule
The old Summary Reporting System convention of counting only the most serious offense in a multi-offense incident, discontinued under full NIBRS reporting.
**In use:** "Under the hierarchy rule this would've counted as one robbery; under NIBRS it's a robbery and an assault, both counted."
**Common misuse:** Assuming an apparent post-transition increase in offense totals reflects more crime rather than a counting-rule change.

### Dark figure of crime
Crime that occurs but never appears in official police records — the gap victimization surveys exist to estimate.
**In use:** "UCR/NIBRS tells you what got reported; the dark figure is why we also run the NCVS."
**Common misuse:** Treating official police-record totals (UCR/NIBRS) as the complete crime rate rather than one instrument with a known reporting gap.

### NCVS (National Crime Victimization Survey)
The Bureau of Justice Statistics' household survey asking a sample of the public directly about victimization, independent of whether it was reported to police.
**In use:** "The NCVS estimate moved differently from the NIBRS count this year — that divergence is itself the finding, not a data error."
**Common misuse:** Expecting NCVS and UCR/NIBRS trends to always move in the same direction, since they measure different things (survey-reported victimization vs. police-recorded offenses).

### ACJS Certification
A voluntary program-quality credential from the Academy of Criminal Justice Sciences for CJ academic programs, based on curriculum and outcome standards.
**In use:** "We're ACJS-certified, which tells an applicant our curriculum meets a peer-reviewed standard — it isn't a licensing requirement to work in the field."
**Common misuse:** Describing it as equivalent to a licensure-linked accreditation (like a bar-exam-eligible law program), when it carries no such gate.

### IRB Subpart C (45 CFR 46)
Additional federal human-subjects protections that apply when research subjects are institutionalized, involuntarily confined, or detained in a penal setting.
**In use:** "Flag Subpart C at submission even though these subjects are on parole, not confined — let the IRB make that call rather than assuming it doesn't apply."
**Common misuse:** Assuming the protections only trigger for currently incarcerated subjects and never for parolees, probationers, or pretrial-supervised populations under correctional authority.

### POST (Peace Officer Standards and Training)
The state body that sets minimum curriculum hours and standards for law-enforcement basic-training academies.
**In use:** "That's a POST-mandated 60 hours on use of force — we build the semester around it, not the other way around."
**Common misuse:** Treating a POST-affiliated academy course's syllabus as fully discretionary, the way an elective course would be.

### Ride-along
A student's supervised observational placement with a sworn officer during a patrol shift, arranged as part of a practicum or internship.
**In use:** "The ride-along waiver has to be signed and the agency's rules of engagement briefed before the student sets foot in the car."
**Common misuse:** Treating it as informal, unsupervised observation that doesn't require a signed waiver or agency-specific confidentiality terms.

### Exoneration / wrongful conviction
A conviction later vacated based on new evidence (commonly DNA, recanted testimony, or newly discovered records) establishing the convicted person's likely innocence.
**In use:** "Before we teach this case, check its entry in the National Registry of Exonerations against the documentary's account."
**Common misuse:** Building a teaching module entirely from a single dramatized secondary account without checking the case's primary record.

### Use-of-force continuum
A training model depicting escalating levels of officer response calibrated to a subject's level of resistance.
**In use:** "The continuum is a training aid for calibrating response — the legal question in an actual incident is Graham's objective-reasonableness standard, not where a step falls on this chart."
**Common misuse:** Presenting it as a rigid, legally binding decision tree officers must follow step-by-step, rather than a training heuristic subordinate to the constitutional reasonableness standard.

### Graham v. Connor "objective reasonableness" standard
The Supreme Court's Fourth Amendment standard (Graham v. Connor, 1989) judging an officer's use of force by what a reasonable officer would do given the facts known at the time, not with hindsight.
**In use:** "Under Graham, we ask what a reasonable officer knew in that moment — not what we know now the case has been reviewed."
**Common misuse:** Citing "reasonableness" generically without tying it to the specific "facts known at the time, not hindsight" framing the standard actually requires.

### Recidivism rate
The proportion of a studied population that reoffends within a defined follow-up period, measured as rearrest, reconviction, or reincarceration — three different, non-interchangeable definitions.
**In use:** "Before you compare these two programs' recidivism rates, check whether both used the same measure and the same follow-up window."
**Common misuse:** Comparing recidivism rates across studies or programs without checking whether they use the same outcome definition (rearrest vs. reconviction vs. reincarceration) and follow-up period.

### PREA (Prison Rape Elimination Act)
Federal law and standards requiring sexual-abuse prevention training and compliance measures for anyone with contact access to a correctional facility, including visiting researchers and practicum students.
**In use:** "Before that practicum student sets foot in the facility, they need the PREA compliance training the facility requires of every visitor."
**Common misuse:** Assuming PREA compliance requirements apply only to correctional staff and not to visiting students or researchers with facility access.
