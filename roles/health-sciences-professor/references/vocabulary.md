# Vocabulary

Working vocabulary that generalists reliably misuse. Format per term: definition, how a practitioner actually uses it, the common misuse.

### First-time pass rate vs. ultimate (cumulative) pass rate

- **Definition:** first-time pass rate counts only a cohort's initial licensure/board exam attempt; ultimate pass rate counts everyone who eventually passes, including repeat test-takers, usually within a stated window (e.g., one year).
- **In use:** "Our first-time rate is 73%, but ultimate is 91% once repeaters are counted — the state board's approval threshold is based on first-time, so the ultimate number doesn't protect us."
- **Common misuse:** citing the higher ultimate pass rate in a context (accreditation status, marketing) where the regulator specifically evaluates first-time rate — the two numbers answer different questions and aren't interchangeable.

### Rolling aggregate (pass rate)

- **Definition:** a pass rate computed across multiple consecutive cohorts (commonly two years) rather than a single cohort, used by many state boards to smooth single-cohort volatility before triggering review status.
- **In use:** "This year's cohort alone is below floor, but the rolling two-year aggregate is still above it — that buys us one cycle, not permanent safety."
- **Common misuse:** treating a rolling aggregate still above the floor as evidence no action is needed — the aggregate can cross the floor next cycle even without further deterioration, once an older, stronger cohort ages out of the window.

### EPA (Entrustable Professional Activity)

- **Definition:** a defined unit of professional practice (e.g., "obtain informed consent," "perform a routine history and physical") that a learner can be trusted to perform with a specified level of supervision, used in competency-based frameworks (notably AAMC's Core EPAs for medical students).
- **In use:** "She's rated for indirect supervision on this EPA, not unsupervised — that's a documented entrustment decision, not a subjective impression."
- **Common misuse:** treating an EPA rating as equivalent to a grade or a single assessment score — it's a supervision-level entrustment decision, typically built from multiple observations over time, not one exam result.

### Clinical hours (direct-care) vs. simulation hours

- **Definition:** direct-care clinical hours are supervised time with real patients in a clinical setting; simulation hours use standardized patients, mannequins, or virtual scenarios and may substitute for a portion of direct-care hours under a specific evidence-backed ceiling.
- **In use:** "We're at 45% simulation, still under the 50% substitution ceiling the NCSBN study supports — pushing further isn't backed by that evidence base."
- **Common misuse:** treating any hours spent in a skills lab as "simulation" for substitution-ceiling purposes — the studied substitution applies to simulation meeting a defined fidelity and structure standard, not unstructured practice time.

### Preceptor vs. clinical instructor

- **Definition:** a preceptor is typically a practicing clinician at a clinical site who supervises a student as part of their own patient-care duties, often unpaid or minimally compensated by the program; a clinical instructor is a program-employed faculty member whose primary assignment is supervising students in a clinical setting.
- **In use:** "The preceptor covers day-to-day supervision on the unit; the clinical instructor is the one accountable to the program for that student's evaluation."
- **Common misuse:** assuming a preceptor's informal feedback substitutes for the program's formal evaluation record — preceptor input feeds the evaluation, but the program-employed instructor (or the program itself) owns the documented, gradable assessment.

### Adverse accreditation action

- **Definition:** a formal accreditor status change short of or including loss of accreditation (warning, probation, show-cause, withdrawal) triggered by a program falling out of compliance with specific standards.
- **In use:** "A warning status isn't loss of accreditation, but it starts a clock — if the cited standards aren't resolved by the next review, the next step is probation."
- **Common misuse:** treating any accreditor citation as equivalent to an adverse action — most citations are findings requiring a response plan, well short of a formal status change, and conflating the two overstates the urgency in a way that can misdirect resources.

### Competency-based education (CBE) vs. credit-hour/seat-time framework

- **Definition:** CBE certifies a learner's progress based on demonstrated mastery of defined competencies regardless of time spent, as opposed to a traditional framework where course credit is earned by completing a set number of instructional hours.
- **In use:** "Under the CBE model, a student who demonstrates the medication-administration competency early doesn't need to sit through the remaining scheduled hours on that unit."
- **Common misuse:** assuming a program has adopted CBE simply because it lists "competencies" in a syllabus — true CBE changes the progression and assessment structure itself (mastery-based advancement), not just the vocabulary describing existing seat-time courses.

### Clinician-educator track vs. tenure track

- **Definition:** a clinician-educator (or "clinical") faculty track evaluates promotion primarily on clinical practice and teaching contributions, typically with lower or no research productivity requirement, distinct from a tenure track evaluated substantially on research output and grant funding.
- **In use:** "He's on the clinician-educator track — his promotion case leans on teaching evaluations and clinical volume, not a grant portfolio."
- **Common misuse:** applying tenure-track promotion criteria (publication count, grant funding) to a clinician-educator dossier, or vice versa — the two tracks are evaluated against different, explicitly stated criteria, and using the wrong rubric under- or over-credits the actual work.

### RVU (relative value unit)

- **Definition:** a standardized measure of clinical productivity used to set physician compensation and workload targets, based on the work, time, and complexity of billed clinical services — independent of teaching time.
- **In use:** "My RVU target assumes 60% clinical FTE; if precepting is actually eating 32% of my time instead of the stated 20%, that same target now has to be hit in fewer clinical hours than it was set against."
- **Common misuse:** assuming RVU targets automatically adjust when a clinician-educator's teaching load increases — they don't unless the effort allocation is formally renegotiated; an informal increase in teaching time silently makes the same RVU target harder to hit.

### MOU (clinical affiliation agreement)

- **Definition:** a formal, typically multi-year agreement between an academic program and a clinical site or health system specifying student placement capacity, supervision requirements, and liability/insurance terms.
- **In use:** "The MOU is up for renewal next year and specifies 24 slots per term — that's the actual ceiling, not whatever the site verbally offered last semester."
- **Common misuse:** treating a clinical partner's verbal willingness to take students as equivalent to contracted capacity — only the signed MOU's specified slot count is a reliable planning number for cohort sizing.

### Remediation plan (with predictive cut score)

- **Definition:** a structured, documented intervention triggered when a student scores below a stated threshold on a predictive licensure exam (ATI, HESI, Kaplan), specifying required activities and a re-test or clearance gate before graduation.
- **In use:** "Below the cut score, remediation is mandatory before clinical clearance — it's not optional coaching, it's a gate."
- **Common misuse:** treating remediation as informal, voluntary extra study rather than a documented, gated requirement — without the gate and the documentation, it doesn't function as the risk-reduction tool it's designed to be, and a later pass-rate review can't credit it as evidence of program response.

### Interprofessional education (IPE)

- **Definition:** structured learning activities where students from two or more health professions (e.g., nursing, medicine, pharmacy) learn together to build collaborative practice skills, often a specific accreditation requirement in its own right.
- **In use:** "The IPE simulation counts toward the accreditor's collaborative-practice standard, but only because it's jointly designed and debriefed across both programs' faculty, not just co-located."
- **Common misuse:** counting students from different programs simply being in the same room (a shared lecture hall, a shared clinical site) as IPE — most accreditors require the activity itself to be jointly designed with shared objectives and debrief, not mere proximity.

### Board certification vs. state licensure

- **Definition:** state licensure is the legal requirement to practice (e.g., RN licensure via NCLEX-RN); board certification is a separate, often voluntary credential from a specialty board attesting to advanced competency in a specific area, not required to practice generally.
- **In use:** "Passing the NCLEX gets her licensed to practice as an RN; the CCRN board certification afterward is a specialty credential, not a second license."
- **Common misuse:** conflating a program's board-certification pass rate (a specialty add-on metric) with its licensure pass rate (the core accreditation and legal-practice metric) — accreditors and state boards act on the licensure number, not the certification number.
