# Vocabulary

### Moves management
The staged process (Identification → Qualification → Cultivation → Solicitation → Stewardship) for advancing a major-gift prospect from stranger to donor.
**In use:** "She's still in Cultivation — two touches logged, no ask amount set yet."
**Common misuse:** Generalists treat it as a checklist to complete quickly; practitioners treat the stage-exit criteria (documented affinity and capacity signal) as a gate, not a formality, since asking too early reliably produces a decline that's hard to recover from.

### Gift range chart (gift table)
A pyramid model showing how many gifts of each size a campaign needs to hit its goal, typically doubling in count and halving in size per tier below the lead gift.
**In use:** "Our gift table says we need one $600k lead gift before we can seat the rest of the pyramid."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a fundraising afterthought built after the goal is set, when it should size the goal in the first place — if the top tiers have no real prospects, the goal is wrong.

### Quiet phase (leadership phase)
The pre-public portion of a capital campaign where lead and major gifts are solicited privately before any public announcement.
**In use:** "We can't go public until quiet phase clears 50-60% of goal."
**Common misuse:** Confused with secrecy for its own sake; the real purpose is sequencing — setting the gift ceiling high before broad solicitation, not concealment.

### Wealth screening
A data service (e.g., DonorSearch, WealthEngine) that estimates a prospect's giving capacity from public records (real estate, SEC filings, other nonprofit board/gift disclosures).
**In use:** "She screens at $1M+ capacity, but her affinity score is low — she's never attended an event."
**Common misuse:** Treated as sufficient on its own; capacity without affinity/engagement data produces a wealthy stranger, not a viable prospect.

### Case for support
The core narrative document justifying why a campaign or program deserves funding, written before any solicitation material.
**In use:** "Every ask letter has to trace back to a paragraph in the case for support — if it doesn't, we're improvising."
**Common misuse:** Written as a brochure describing the organization in general, rather than making a specific, falsifiable case for why this dollar amount, now, for this outcome.

### LYBUNT
"Last Year But Not This year" — a donor who gave in the prior fiscal year but has not yet given in the current one.
**In use:** "Pull the LYBUNT list before we spend on a new acquisition buy — it's cheaper to reactivate."
**Common misuse:** Lumped in with cold prospects in acquisition mailings, rather than segmented for a warmer, higher-response reactivation appeal.

### SYBUNT
"Some Year But Not This year" — a donor who has given in some prior year (not necessarily last year) but not the current one.
**In use:** "Our SYBUNT segment 3+ years lapsed gets digital-only outreach — phone time isn't worth it there."
**Common misuse:** Treated identically to LYBUNT, when response rates and appropriate channel spend differ sharply by lapse length.

### Retention rate
The percentage of donors from one fiscal year who give again in the next.
**In use:** "Retention dropped to 38% this year — that's below the FEP industry benchmark and it's a stewardship problem, not an acquisition problem."
**Common misuse:** Conflated with total-dollars-raised growth; a shop can post rising totals while retention quietly erodes, masked by new acquisition.

### Ask amount
A specific dollar figure proposed to a donor, derived from screened capacity and giving history, as opposed to an open-ended range.
**In use:** "The ask amount is $150,000 — three times her largest prior gift, within her screened capacity."
**Common misuse:** Softened into a range ("$50,000 to $150,000") on the theory that it feels less pressuring; practitioners know a range reliably anchors toward its low end.

### Stewardship
The post-gift relationship-management work — acknowledgment, impact reporting, recognition — that precedes any future ask to the same donor.
**In use:** "No further ask to this donor until the impact report goes out — stewardship isn't optional between gifts."
**Common misuse:** Reduced to a form-letter thank-you; real stewardship reports the specific outcome the specific gift funded.

### Planned gift
A gift arranged during a donor's lifetime but realized at death or after a triggering event (bequest, charitable trust, beneficiary designation).
**In use:** "She's a 12-year consecutive $500 donor — that's a planned-giving conversation we haven't had yet."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to require a major-donor-sized current gift; the strongest planned-giving prospects are often long-tenure small-dollar donors, not current major donors.

### Naming opportunity
A recognition right (a room, program, or position name) attached to a gift at a defined threshold, typically negotiated as part of a major or capital gift.
**In use:** "The $150,000 gift endows and names the tutoring coordinator position."
**Common misuse:** Priced arbitrarily rather than against a documented naming-gift schedule tied to the capital project's actual cost centers.

### Matching gift
A gift multiplied by a second source — a donor's employer, a challenge grant, or another donor — contingent on meeting a condition (often a deadline or a dollar-for-dollar match).
**In use:** "The board chair's $50,000 match expires June 30 — that deadline is the case for going public early on this piece."
**Common misuse:** Treated as free money; matches usually carry a real deadline and conditions that, if missed, forfeit the match entirely.

### Cost to raise a dollar
The ratio of fundraising expense to dollars raised for a given channel or the organization overall.
**In use:** "This gala costs $0.35 to raise a dollar — it's a friend-raising and acquisition event, not a net-revenue line."
**Common misuse:** Compared directly across channels with very different purposes (mass acquisition vs. major-gift cultivation) as if a single benchmark applied to both.

### Pledge
A donor's written commitment to give a specified total, typically paid over 3-5 years for capital gifts, counted toward campaign totals at signing rather than at cash receipt.
**In use:** "We're at $2.4M in pledges but only $900k in cash received against this year's payment schedule."
**Common misuse:** Treated as equivalent to cash on hand in operating budget planning, creating a liquidity gap that surfaces only when program spending outpaces the pledge payment schedule.
