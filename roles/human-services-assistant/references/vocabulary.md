# Human Services Assistant — Vocabulary

### Categorical eligibility
Automatic qualification for a benefit program based on enrollment in another program (e.g., receiving TANF or SSI), bypassing the standard income test entirely.
**In use:** "She's categorically eligible through SSI — we don't need to run the SNAP income test at all."
**Common misuse:** Confused with simply having low income; categorical eligibility is a specific program-linkage rule, not a synonym for "clearly needs help."

### Broad-based categorical eligibility (BBCE)
A state-level SNAP policy that extends categorical eligibility (and a higher income limit, commonly 200% FPL) to households receiving a TANF-funded noncash service, such as an information-and-referral contact.
**In use:** "The intake itself is a TANF-funded service in this state, so BBCE applies and the 130% gross test isn't the binding one."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to apply everywhere; BBCE is state-optional and its income threshold varies by state, so it must be confirmed against current state policy, not assumed present.

### Gross income test / Net income test
The two income thresholds SNAP applies to non-categorically-eligible households: gross income compared to a percentage of the federal poverty line before deductions, and net income compared to a lower percentage after standard and earned-income deductions are applied.
**In use:** "He fails the gross test on paper, but check BBCE before you tell him he's denied — and even under the standard rule, run the net test with the earned-income deduction before assuming."
**Common misuse:** Treated as one combined test; a household can pass one and fail the other, and which test applies depends on whether categorical eligibility is in play.

### ABAWD (Able-Bodied Adult Without Dependents)
A SNAP recipient category subject to a work requirement (commonly 80 hours/month of work or a qualifying activity) and a 3-month benefit time limit within a 36-month period if the requirement isn't met, absent an area waiver.
**In use:** "He's an ABAWD in a non-waived county — we need his hours documented before month three or benefits stop."
**Common misuse:** Applied to any single adult on SNAP; the category has specific age, disability, and household exclusions that must be checked, not assumed from household composition alone.

### VI-SPDAT (Vulnerability Index-Service Prioritization Decision Assistance Tool)
A standardized scored questionnaire used in coordinated entry to prioritize homeless-services clients for shelter, rapid rehousing, or permanent supportive housing based on vulnerability and acuity.
**In use:** "His VI-SPDAT score puts him in the rapid-rehousing tier, but the score's two points from the PSH threshold — that's a case-conference flag, not an automatic assignment."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a fully automatic sorting mechanism rather than a decision aid; boundary scores and time-bound risks the tool doesn't capture (like a 30-day housing deadline) still need human review.

### Coordinated entry
The single-point-of-access system a community uses to intake, assess, and prioritize people experiencing homelessness for the region's available housing resources, rather than each agency running its own separate waitlist.
**In use:** "That referral has to go through coordinated entry first — we can't place her directly onto our program's list."
**Common misuse:** Confused with a single agency's intake process; coordinated entry is a community-wide system multiple agencies feed into and draw from.

### Case aide
A paraprofessional role that supports a licensed caseworker's caseload with direct-contact tasks (screening, documentation, follow-up) but does not carry independent case-decision authority.
**In use:** "As the case aide, I can run the intake screen and document it, but the eligibility determination itself gets signed off by my supervisor."
**Common misuse:** Treated as interchangeable with "case manager"; the distinction is legal authority to make the final case decision, not the amount of direct client contact.

### Scope of practice
The defined boundary of tasks a human services assistant is trained and legally authorized to perform, beyond which the task must go to a licensed social worker or clinician.
**In use:** "I can screen for a behavioral-health concern and document what the client reported, but the assessment itself is outside my scope."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a flexible guideline the assistant can stretch when they feel confident; in most states it's a statutory boundary tied to the licensed practice of social work.

### Diversion
A conversation-based intervention at the point of a homelessness services request that explores safe alternatives to shelter entry (staying with family, a small one-time payment to resolve a specific barrier) before defaulting to a shelter bed.
**In use:** "Before we put her on the shelter waitlist, let's do a diversion conversation — is there a family member she could stay with for two weeks if we covered a utility bill?"
**Common misuse:** Confused with denying service; diversion is an active problem-solving conversation offered alongside shelter access, not a gatekeeping step to reduce shelter demand.

### Sanction
A benefit reduction or termination imposed for documented noncompliance with a program requirement (e.g., missed work-requirement hours, missed mandatory appointment), distinct from a routine eligibility redetermination.
**In use:** "That's a sanction, not a redetermination denial — it means the case file has to show a documented noncompliance finding, not just an income change."
**Common misuse:** Used loosely for any negative benefit action; a sanction specifically requires a noncompliance finding and typically a cure/appeal window the client is entitled to.

### HMIS (Homeless Management Information System)
The federally mandated database homeless-services agencies use to record client-level data on service use, required for coordinated entry and federal funding compliance.
**In use:** "That intake has to go into HMIS today — the funder's annual performance report pulls straight from those entries."
**Common misuse:** Treated as optional internal record-keeping; HMIS entry is typically a funding-compliance requirement with its own timeliness standards, not a convenience tool.

### Psychoeducation group
A structured group session that teaches specific skills or information (budgeting, tenant rights, parenting strategies) using a set curriculum, distinct from an open-ended therapeutic support group.
**In use:** "This is a psychoeducation group on tenant rights, not a processing group — we stick to the curriculum and redirect personal crises to a one-on-one follow-up."
**Common misuse:** Run as an unstructured support group because it feels more responsive to whoever shows up, which drifts the group outside the assistant's facilitation scope and away from its funded purpose.

### Warm handoff
A referral made through a direct, personal introduction to the receiving program (a joint call, an accompanied visit) rather than handing over a phone number or flyer.
**In use:** "A cold referral to that clinic gets maybe a third of clients in the door — do a warm handoff instead."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to mean simply being polite about the referral; the defining feature is the direct introduction between client and receiving resource, not the tone of the conversation.
