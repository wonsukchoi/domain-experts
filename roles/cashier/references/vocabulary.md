# Vocabulary

Terms generalists conflate or use loosely that a cashier or loss-prevention practitioner keeps sharply separate.

### Over/short vs. shrinkage

**Over/short** is the per-till, per-shift cash variance between expected and counted drawer totals — a specific, immediately measurable number. **Shrinkage** (or "shrink") is the store-wide, period-level loss between recorded inventory and actual inventory, from all causes (theft, damage, admin error, vendor fraud) combined, usually measured monthly or quarterly.

**In use:** "My till was $3 over last night, that's normal — shrink is a whole different conversation the store manager has with loss prevention, not something one register variance tells you."

**Common misuse:** treating every till shortage as evidence of "the shrink problem," or conversely assuming shrink is only about shoplifting when register-level cash handling is a distinct, often larger, contributor per NRF's National Retail Security Survey.

### Sweethearting

Giving a friend, family member, or acquaintance an unrung item, a scan that doesn't register, or an unauthorized discount at the register — a form of internal theft that looks like generosity or customer service rather than theft, which is exactly why loss-prevention research identifies it as the largest internal-theft category at POS.

**In use:** "That wasn't a discount override, that was sweethearting — she scanned half his cart and just didn't ring the rest."

**Common misuse:** dismissing it as "not a big deal, it's just one item" — the research finding is that it rarely stays a single incident once normalized, and stores treat it as a zero-discretion policy violation for that reason.

### Skimming vs. grazing

**Skimming** is removing cash from a completed or apparent transaction without recording the full sale (e.g., the no-sale-open pattern in this role's worked example). **Grazing** is consuming store product without paying — eating or drinking store merchandise during a shift, common in grocery and food service, and a separate loss category from cash skimming.

**In use:** "The variance points at skimming, not grazing — this is a cash shortage, not a missing-inventory count."

**Common misuse:** using "skimming" loosely to mean any form of employee theft; practitioners keep it specific to cash-handling manipulation because the investigation method differs (register journal vs. inventory count).

### No-sale vs. void vs. return

**No-sale** opens the drawer without any transaction, for legitimate reasons like making change. **Void** cancels an item or transaction before it completes, removing it from the sale entirely. **Return** processes a completed prior sale being reversed, ideally against a lookup record. Each has a different fraud profile and a different audit trail.

**In use:** "Don't void it if the customer already paid — that's a return, and it needs the original transaction pulled up, not a void."

**Common misuse:** using "void" to describe reversing an already-tendered sale, which either breaks the reconciliation (the payment is gone but the sale is voided) or requires the register to force it into a refund workflow anyway.

### Till float vs. bank

**Float** is the starting cash placed in a drawer at the beginning of a shift, expected back at close plus net cash sales. **Bank** (or "safe bank") is the store's reserve cash held in the safe used to replenish floats and make large change — a separate pool from any individual till.

**In use:** "We're out of $20s in the drawer — pull from the bank, don't shortchange the customer with a fistful of fives."

**Common misuse:** conflating "the bank" with total store cash on hand, when it specifically refers to the safe reserve, not drop-safe deposits already earmarked for pickup.

### X-report vs. Z-report

**X-report** is a mid-shift snapshot of register totals that does not reset the counters — used to check progress without closing anything out. **Z-report** closes and resets the register, producing the final totals used for shift reconciliation.

**In use:** "Pull an X-report if you just want to see where we're at — don't Z it out mid-shift or you'll reset the totals before close."

**Common misuse:** running a Z-report to "check" totals mid-shift, which resets counters and makes the actual end-of-shift reconciliation wrong or impossible to reconstruct.

### Chargeback vs. refund

A **refund** is the store voluntarily returning payment to a customer, initiated at the register or by customer service. A **chargeback** is the customer's card issuer forcibly reversing a payment after a dispute, initiated by the bank, often weeks after the transaction and without the store's prior agreement.

**In use:** "That's not a refund we did — it's a chargeback that came through from her bank three weeks after the sale."

**Common misuse:** treating a chargeback notice as something the cashier or store chose, or as equivalent to a same-day refund for reconciliation purposes — chargebacks hit accounting separately and usually much later.

### Wardrobing / ticket-switching

**Wardrobing** is buying an item, using it, and returning it as unused for a refund. **Ticket-switching** is swapping a price tag from a cheaper item onto a more expensive one before purchase or return. Both are return-fraud patterns distinct from a simple no-receipt return, and both get escalated to loss prevention rather than processed as ordinary returns.

**In use:** "The tags don't match the SKU on the receipt — that's ticket-switching, not just a no-receipt return, flag it."

**Common misuse:** processing an item with obvious wear or a mismatched tag as a routine return because the customer has a receipt — a receipt confirms a purchase happened, not that the item being returned is the item that was bought.

### Dual control

A cash-handling principle requiring two people to jointly verify an action — a drop, a large refund, a safe count — so no single person can both create and conceal a discrepancy. Distinct from a manager "approving" something after the fact.

**In use:** "Drops always need a witness signature — that's dual control, not just a formality for the drop slip."

**Common misuse:** treating a manager's after-the-fact sign-off on a report as dual control, when the point is two people present at the moment of the action, not two signatures on paperwork afterward.

### PIN pad skimmer

A physical device illegally attached to or overlaid on a card reader/PIN pad to capture card data and PINs. Distinct from a legitimate reader malfunction, which is far more common but gets confused with it.

**In use:** "Check the reader for anything loose or a mismatched color before opening — that's the skimmer check, not just wiping it down."

**Common misuse:** assuming skimmer checks are only an issue for gas-station pumps; PCI DSS guidance and retailer loss-prevention programs apply the same physical-tampering checks to any unattended or low-supervision card reader, including self-checkout.

### Loss prevention vs. asset protection

Largely interchangeable in modern retail, but **loss prevention (LP)** historically emphasizes theft and shrink specifically, while **asset protection (AP)** is the broader, more current umbrella covering safety, workplace violence, and operational risk alongside theft — the terminology shift reflects an expanded scope, not a rebrand of the same job.

**In use:** "Call it in to asset protection — they handle the safety side too now, not just shoplifting."

**Common misuse:** assuming the title change is cosmetic; in stores that have made the shift, AP's remit and reporting lines genuinely differ from a legacy LP team's.
