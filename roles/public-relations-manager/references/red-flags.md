# Red flags — what a PR manager notices instantly

Smell tests with thresholds. Any single one can be innocent; two or three together are a pattern.

### A draft statement issued before the underlying facts are fully confirmed, under deadline pressure
- **Usually means:** a fast but inaccurate statement risks a correction later, and the correction is often more damaging than a short delay for accuracy would have been.
- **First question:** "What's actually substantiated right now, and what's still under investigation — does the statement distinguish the two clearly?"
- **Data to pull:** the current state of internal fact-finding, what specifically is confirmed versus still being verified.

### A statement drafted and reviewed only by people already sympathetic to the organization's position
- **Usually means:** it hasn't been tested against the most adversarial plausible reading, and a critical journalist or skeptic will likely find the gap the friendly review missed.
- **First question:** "Has anyone whose job is finding the hole in this statement actually reviewed it, or only people invested in it looking good?"
- **Data to pull:** the review process and reviewers for the statement, whether an adversarial/red-team pass was actually conducted.

### A developing story met with "no comment" or silence while the underlying facts are going to become known regardless
- **Usually means:** silence isn't neutral — it cedes the narrative to less-informed or adversarial voices who will fill the gap while the organization isn't part of shaping it.
- **First question:** "Is silence actually protecting us here, or just delaying an inevitable disclosure and ceding how the story gets framed?"
- **Data to pull:** what's already known or knowable to the journalist/public, comparable past cases of delayed disclosure versus proactive engagement.

### Messaging to different stakeholder audiences (customers, investors, employees) that differs on the underlying facts, not just emphasis
- **Usually means:** inconsistent messaging across audiences gets discovered and is treated as evidence of dishonesty, compounding the original issue with a credibility problem.
- **First question:** "Do all these audience-specific messages state the same underlying facts, or do they actually differ on what happened?"
- **Data to pull:** the message house/key facts document, the actual audience-specific communications being sent.

### A crisis response being improvised with no pre-existing escalation tree or disclosure plan
- **Usually means:** a crisis communication plan built during the crisis is already too late — decisions about who approves what and who speaks for the company are being made under time pressure instead of following a pre-established process.
- **First question:** "Does a standing crisis communication plan exist for this type of situation, and is it being followed?"
- **Data to pull:** the organization's crisis communication plan (or its absence), the actual decision process being used right now.

### A recovery/apology statement committing to a specific fix or timeline with no internal confirmation it's achievable
- **Usually means:** overpromising in a crisis statement sets up a second credibility failure when the commitment slips, compounding rather than resolving the original issue.
- **First question:** "Has the relevant internal team (engineering, operations, legal) confirmed this specific commitment and timeline are actually achievable?"
- **Data to pull:** the specific commitments in the draft statement, confirmation status from the teams responsible for delivering on them.

### Communication effort being invested in managing perception of an issue with no corresponding internal push to fix the underlying problem
- **Usually means:** PR is being used as a substitute for fixing a real problem, which can't durably work — the underlying issue eventually resurfaces, often worse.
- **First question:** "Is there an internal effort actually addressing the root problem, or is communication the only active response?"
- **Data to pull:** internal remediation plan and status for the underlying issue, versus the communication plan alone.

### A statement or disclosure decision being made based on what's most flattering to the organization rather than stakeholder materiality
- **Usually means:** the disclosure test has shifted from "what would a reasonable, informed stakeholder want to know" to "what's easiest to say," which tends to under-disclose exactly the information that matters most.
- **First question:** "Would a reasonable, informed stakeholder consider this material, regardless of how it reflects on us?"
- **Data to pull:** the specific information being considered for inclusion or omission, its materiality to the relevant stakeholder group.
