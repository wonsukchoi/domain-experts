# Public relations working vocabulary

Terms a PR manager uses precisely. Format: definition → how a practitioner says it → common misuse.

**Front-page test** — Asking how a statement would read as a quote on the front page of a major publication, framed unfavorably, before sending it — a discipline applied before publication, not as a reaction to criticism after the fact.
In use: "This line doesn't survive the front-page test — reframe it before it goes out, not after a reporter quotes it back to us."
Misuse: applying the front-page test only after a statement has already drawn criticism, rather than as a pre-publication check that catches the problem before it's public.

**Proactive disclosure vs. reactive confirmation** — Getting ahead of a story with a controlled, honest disclosure, as opposed to only confirming facts once a journalist or third party has already surfaced them — the former generally reads as accountability, the latter as being caught.
In use: "We're disclosing this now, before the journalist's deadline, specifically to be the proactive-disclosure case rather than the reactive-confirmation one."
Misuse: treating "no comment" or silence as a safe, neutral default when the underlying facts are going to become known regardless — silence in that situation is reactive by default, not actually neutral.

**Bridging (media technique)** — Answering the question actually asked, honestly and directly, before pivoting to a key message — as distinct from obviously dodging the question, which reads as evasive regardless of how legitimate the pivot itself is.
In use: "Bridge, don't dodge — answer the direct question about the recall count first, then pivot to what we're doing about it."
Misuse: pivoting to a prepared message without first directly addressing the actual question asked, which comes across as evasive even when the underlying answer isn't actually damaging.

**Message house** — A structured document defining the core facts and key messages for a situation, allowing audience-specific tailoring in emphasis and detail while keeping every audience's communication consistent on the underlying facts.
In use: "The message house has the core facts locked — customer, investor, and employee versions can differ in emphasis but not in what actually happened."
Misuse: developing separate messaging for each audience independently without a shared source-of-truth document, risking factual inconsistency that gets discovered and read as dishonesty.

**Materiality (disclosure context)** — The threshold of significance that determines whether information should be disclosed to a given stakeholder group, based on what a reasonable, informed member of that group would want to know — not based on what's most flattering to disclose.
In use: "This detail is material to investors even though it's uncomfortable — the test is what they'd want to know, not what's easiest for us to say."
Misuse: deciding what to disclose based on what reflects best on the organization rather than on an honest materiality assessment for the relevant stakeholder group.

**Crisis communication plan (standing)** — A pre-established set of decisions (who approves statements, who's authorized to speak publicly, general disclosure principles) built before a specific crisis occurs, as distinct from an improvised response built during one.
In use: "Per the standing crisis plan, legal and the CEO's office both sign off before any statement on a safety issue goes out — that's already decided, not being debated right now."
Misuse: building the escalation and approval process for the first time during an active crisis, which adds delay and risk of error exactly when speed and accuracy both matter most.

**Post-incident review (comms)** — A structured review of how a crisis communication response performed, similar in spirit to an engineering postmortem, used to update the standing crisis plan rather than treating each crisis as a one-off lesson.
In use: "The post-incident review showed our approval chain took too long — that's now built into the updated standing plan."
Misuse: treating each crisis response as an isolated event with no structured review afterward, missing the opportunity to fix a standing plan and repeatedly relearning the same lessons.

**Adversarial read (red-teaming a statement)** — Having someone specifically tasked with finding the damaging or exploitable interpretation of a draft statement before it's published, as distinct from review by people already invested in the statement looking good.
In use: "Before this goes out, get someone whose job today is to find the worst possible headline this statement could generate."
Misuse: relying only on friendly internal review for a sensitive statement, missing the adversarial interpretation a critical journalist or skeptic would find immediately.

**Reputational reserve** — The accumulated trust and credibility an organization has built through consistent, honest communication over time, which absorbs and buffers the impact of a single difficult disclosure but can be substantially depleted by a discovered lie or cover-up.
In use: "We have enough reputational reserve from past transparent handling of issues that this honest disclosure should be well-received, even though the news itself is bad."
Misuse: treating reputational reserve as infinite or unaffected by short-term communication choices, when a single instance of getting caught minimizing or hiding an issue can deplete years of accumulated trust quickly.

**Silence (as a communication choice)** — Declining to comment or engage during a developing story, which is itself an active choice with consequences (ceding narrative control to other voices), not a neutral or risk-free default.
In use: "Going dark here isn't neutral — it just means the story gets shaped by voices that don't have our facts."
Misuse: treating "no comment" as automatically the safest option, missing that it has its own real cost when the underlying facts are going to surface regardless of the organization's silence.
