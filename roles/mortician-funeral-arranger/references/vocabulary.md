# Mortician / Funeral Arranger — Vocabulary

### General Price List (GPL)
The FTC Funeral Rule-mandated itemized price list every funeral home must provide before discussing arrangements or merchandise, and read over the phone if asked.
**In use:** "Before we talk about caskets, let me walk you through the General Price List so every cost is clear."
**Common misuse:** Treated as optional if the family seems ready to move past pricing — it's a legal requirement regardless of the family's apparent readiness or deference.

### Statement of Funeral Goods and Services Selected
The itemized, signed record of exactly what a family selected during the arrangement conference, used to reconcile the final invoice.
**In use:** "The Statement shows six line items totaling $4,035 — let's confirm this matches what we discussed before you sign."
**Common misuse:** Confused with the GPL; the GPL shows all available options and prices, the Statement shows what was actually chosen.

### Next-of-kin hierarchy
The legally defined order of family relationships (spouse, then adult children, then parents, etc., varying by state) determining who has authority to make funeral/disposition decisions absent a named agent.
**In use:** "With no surviving spouse and no named executor, next-of-kin hierarchy in this state points to the eldest adult child as the decision-maker."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to default to whoever is present or most vocal — it's a specific legal order, not a matter of who shows up to the conference.

### Cash-advance item
A good or service the funeral home pays for on the family's behalf and passes through at cost (obituary placement, clergy honorarium, flowers) — must be disclosed if a markup is applied.
**In use:** "The obituary placement is a cash-advance item — we're passing through the paper's exact rate, no markup."
**Common misuse:** Bundled into the service fee without itemized disclosure, which is a Funeral Rule violation if a markup exists and isn't stated.

### Disposition authorization
The signed legal authorization required before cremation or burial can proceed, which must come from the confirmed legal decision-maker, not any present family member.
**In use:** "Cremation can't proceed until we have disposition authorization signed by the named executor specifically."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a formality signed by whoever is available in the moment — an authorization from someone without legal standing can be challenged after the fact, when the disposition can no longer be undone.

### Informant (on a death certificate)
The person providing personal-history information (birth date, SSN, marital status, etc.) about the decedent for the death certificate, distinct from who pronounces cause of death.
**In use:** "Maria is listed as the informant for the personal-history section; the medical examiner's office provides the cause-of-death section separately."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to be a single all-purpose "informant" for every field — cause and manner of death always comes from the certifying physician or medical examiner, never from family testimony.

### Preneed vs. at-need arrangement
Preneed: funeral arrangements and often payment made in advance of death; at-need: arrangements made after a death has already occurred.
**In use:** "This is an at-need conference — there's no preneed contract on file for her mother."
**Common misuse:** Assumed at-need families have no prior plan at all; always worth checking for an existing preneed contract before starting from scratch, since it may already specify selections and pricing.

### Forwarding/receiving remains
The service of transporting a decedent to or from another funeral home, typically for out-of-area deaths or burials.
**In use:** "Since she passed while traveling, we'll need a receiving-remains arrangement with the funeral home in her home state."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to be a simple shipping fee; it involves coordinating permits and documentation between two licensed facilities, not just transportation logistics.

### Restorative art
Reconstructive and cosmetic techniques used to restore a natural appearance after trauma, illness, or decomposition, typically performed by the embalmer, not the arranger.
**In use:** "Given the circumstances, I'll loop in our embalmer to discuss what restorative art can achieve before you decide on a viewing."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to be something the arranger promises or performs directly — it's the embalmer's technical domain, and the arranger's role is setting expectations and coordinating, not executing it.
