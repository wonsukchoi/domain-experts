# Vocabulary

### Ecological fallacy
Inferring an individual-level relationship from a group- or area-level correlation.
**In use:** "The county-level correlation between median income and vote share doesn't tell us how low-income individuals voted — that's an ecological inference, and it's often wrong in the direction people assume."
**Common misuse:** treated as a pedantic technicality rather than a specific, frequently-wrong inference; people cite "correlation isn't causation" but skip the separate point that the unit of the data doesn't match the unit of the claim.

### Selecting on the dependent variable
Choosing cases for study based on the value of the outcome being explained, which removes the variation needed to identify what causes it.
**In use:** "A study of only successful coups can tell you what successful coups had in common with each other, not what made them succeed versus the coup attempts that failed."
**Common misuse:** conflated with small sample size in general — the problem isn't N, it's that every case shares the outcome, so nothing in the sample varies on the thing being explained.

### Instrumental variable
A variable that shifts the causal factor of interest but has no direct effect on the outcome except through that factor — used to break confounding you can't otherwise measure.
**In use:** "Settler mortality shifted which colonial institutions got built, but it doesn't plausibly affect a country's income today except through those institutions — that's the exclusion restriction AJR are leaning on, and it's the part reviewers push back on hardest."
**Common misuse:** treated as a mechanical statistical fix rather than a substantive causal claim that itself needs defending — the exclusion restriction is an assumption, not a test result.

### Most-similar-systems design
A comparative method that pairs cases alike on as many background variables as possible, differing mainly on the variable of theoretical interest.
**In use:** "Chile before and after its 2012 voluntary-voting reform is a stronger most-similar comparison than Australia versus Canada, because Chile holds the party system and federal structure fixed within the same country."
**Common misuse:** used as a synonym for "similar countries" without checking that the cases actually differ on the variable being tested — a comparison of two similar countries that are also similar on the causal variable of interest proves nothing.

### House effect
A pollster's systematic, consistent lean relative to the eventual outcome or to the polling average, distinct from random sampling noise.
**In use:** "Firm Z has run 3 points more favorable to Republican candidates than the polling average across the last two cycles — that's a house effect, and it should shift how much weight the aggregate gives their next release, not be treated as this cycle's real movement."
**Common misuse:** confused with a single poll being "wrong" — a house effect is a track-record pattern across many polls, not a verdict on one release.

### Non-attitude
A survey response constructed at the moment of being asked rather than reflecting a pre-existing, stable opinion.
**In use:** "Support for the trade provision moved 14 points depending on which framing we used — that's the signature of a non-attitude on a low-salience issue, not real preference change."
**Common misuse:** applied only to "don't know" answers, when the more consequential version is a confidently stated opinion that is just as unstable under different question wording.

### Process tracing
A within-case method that evaluates a causal claim by classifying each piece of evidence by what it can rule in or rule out, rather than by aggregating cases statistically.
**In use:** "The leaked pre-negotiation memo is a hoop test the domestic-driven story had to pass — it did, but that alone doesn't confirm the story, it just keeps it alive."
**Common misuse:** used loosely to mean "close reading of a single case," without the actual evidentiary typing (hoop, smoking-gun, straw-in-the-wind, doubly decisive) that gives the method its inferential leverage.

### Mandate (electoral)
A claim that an election result authorizes a specific policy direction, beyond simply determining who holds office.
**In use:** "A 1.5-point win with turnout driven by an unrelated local ballot measure isn't the evidentiary basis for a mandate claim on the top-ticket issue."
**Common misuse:** invoked automatically for any win regardless of margin, turnout composition, or whether exit/panel data actually shows the claimed issue as decisive.

### Democratic backsliding
Gradual erosion of democratic institutions and norms by incumbents using nominally legal means, rather than by coup or election cancellation.
**In use:** "The relevant V-Dem sub-indices — judicial constraints on the executive, media freedom — moved before the headline regime-type classification changed, which is the pattern backsliding research expects."
**Common misuse:** applied to any policy the speaker dislikes, detached from the institutional-measurement indicators (media freedom, judicial independence, electoral integrity sub-scores) the term is meant to track.

### Retrospective voting
Voters rewarding or punishing incumbents based on past performance rather than evaluating prospective policy platforms.
**In use:** "The incumbent's vote share tracked county-level income growth almost one-for-one over the cycle — textbook retrospective voting."
**Common misuse:** assumed to mean rational, policy-attributable retrospection, when the empirical pattern (Achen & Bartels) includes voters punishing incumbents for events plainly outside their control, like local weather disasters.

### Salience
How much a given issue is at the front of a respondent's mind and weighted in their vote choice or survey answer, independent of their underlying position on it.
**In use:** "Support for the policy is stable, but salience is what moved — it went from a bottom-tier issue to a top-three 'most important problem' answer, which is what actually predicted vote switching."
**Common misuse:** conflated with the direction or intensity of opinion itself, when salience is a separate dimension (how much it matters to the vote) from position (which side someone is on).

### Coding scheme (content/institutional)
The explicit, documented rule set used to classify text, events, or institutional features into comparable categories.
**In use:** "V-Dem and Polity5 disagree on this country's 2019 classification because their coding schemes weight de facto executive constraint differently — that's a documented, defensible difference, not a data error."
**Common misuse:** treated as an objective measurement rather than an operationalization choice — swapping indices without acknowledging their coding schemes differ is a common way an argument's result quietly depends on which index was picked.
