# Red Flags

## A single poll shift inside its own margin of error is reported as "momentum"

- **Usually means:** sampling noise is being narrated as a trend because a 2–3 point move makes a better headline than "no change."
- **First question:** "Does at least one other pollster show the same direction over the same window?"
- **Data to pull:** the last 4–6 polls from the same and other houses, plotted with each poll's own MOE band, not just the point estimates.

## A demographic group's vote share is inferred from county- or precinct-level returns

- **Usually means:** an ecological-fallacy jump from an aggregate correlation to an individual-level claim — the actual individual-level data (exit poll, validated voter file, panel survey) either wasn't consulted or doesn't support as clean a story.
- **First question:** "Is this claim about how a group voted, or about how areas with more of that group voted?"
- **Data to pull:** individual-level survey (validated voter file, CCES, exit poll microdata) for the same election; check whether the individual-level estimate matches the aggregate-implied one.

## A causal claim about institutions rests on a cross-sectional correlation with no instrument or discontinuity

- **Usually means:** the true causal arrow, or a common historical cause, runs in a direction the analysis can't rule out — richer/more-stable countries can afford the institution as easily as the institution can cause the wealth.
- **First question:** "What varies the institution for reasons unconnected to the outcome?"
- **Data to pull:** the paper's identification section — is there an instrument, discontinuity, or natural experiment, and does the exclusion restriction or continuity assumption hold up to a stated critique (e.g., Albouy's critique of the AJR settler-mortality instrument)?

## A small-N case study was built from cases where the outcome already happened

- **Usually means:** selection on the dependent variable — the "successful revolutions" or "wars that broke out" sample excludes the near-misses needed to identify what actually distinguished the cases.
- **First question:** "What are the closest cases where this didn't happen, and are they in the comparison set?"
- **Data to pull:** a matched set of null cases (same region/era/structural conditions, different outcome) added to the comparison.

## A poll aggregate treats a campaign-sponsored or party-internal poll identically to independent polling

- **Usually means:** the aggregate is picking up selective release bias — sponsored polls that don't show the sponsor ahead are far less likely to ever become public.
- **First question:** "Who commissioned this poll, and does the house publish independent work with a track record?"
- **Data to pull:** poll disclosure/sponsor field (per AAPOR transparency standards); the house's historical error on non-sponsored races if one exists.

## A close election result is described as a policy mandate

- **Usually means:** the margin is being read as ideological endorsement without checking whether turnout composition, a single local issue, or an unpopular opponent explains the margin as well as the stated headline issue does.
- **First question:** "What was the margin, what was turnout relative to the prior comparable election, and does exit/panel data show the stated issue as the top reason for vote choice?"
- **Data to pull:** turnout by subgroup vs. the prior comparable election; exit-poll or panel "most important issue" breakdown; down-ballot results in the same jurisdiction.

## An institutional-quality score (democracy index, corruption index) is used as if coders agree

- **Usually means:** the specific score reflects one coding scheme's judgment calls on borderline cases (e.g., contested elections, hybrid regimes), and a different reputable index would rank the case differently.
- **First question:** "Do V-Dem, Polity5, and Freedom House agree on this case's classification and trend direction?"
- **Data to pull:** the same country-year across at least two independent indices; if they diverge by more than a small band, treat the divergence itself as the finding to report, not a single index's number.

## A survey finding on a low-salience issue is presented as a stable public preference

- **Usually means:** a non-attitude — the respondent constructed an answer on the spot, and question wording or ordering would move the topline substantially.
- **First question:** "Was this issue salient to respondents before being asked, and has the question been tested for wording/order sensitivity?"
- **Data to pull:** a split-sample or alternate-wording version of the same question from the same or a comparable survey; item nonresponse and "don't know" rates, which run high on genuine non-attitudes.

## A retrospective-voting or economic-voting claim ignores events outside incumbent control

- **Usually means:** the analysis is crediting or blaming the incumbent for outcomes (a natural disaster, a global price shock) that voters respond to but the incumbent didn't cause — the Achen & Bartels "blind retrospection" pattern.
- **First question:** "Is there a plausible non-policy shock in the window that could explain the swing on its own?"
- **Data to pull:** timeline of local/global shocks (weather events, commodity prices, court rulings) overlaid on the polling or vote-share series in the affected jurisdiction vs. a comparable unaffected one.
