# Vocabulary

Working vocabulary that generalists reliably misuse. Format per term: definition, how a practitioner actually uses it, the common misuse.

### Modular budget

- **Definition:** an NIH budgeting format for most R01s where direct costs are requested in $25,000 increments without item-by-item justification in the application itself (an internal budget still has to exist for planning).
- **In use:** "We're at 9 modules this cycle, down from 10 — that's a $25,000 direct-cost cut, not a 10% cut, because indirect costs sit on top and aren't part of the module count."
- **Common misuse:** treating "modules" as the total award — F&A/indirect costs are calculated on top of direct costs and aren't part of the modular number, so a budget conversation that ignores indirects understates the real award size and overstates what a module cut actually removes from the PI's discretionary total.

### F&A / indirect costs

- **Definition:** Facilities and Administrative costs — the negotiated overhead rate an institution charges on top of direct costs to cover space, utilities, and administrative support; typically negotiated institution-wide, not per grant.
- **In use:** "Our negotiated F&A rate is 58% of modified total direct costs — the sponsor's total outlay is a lot higher than the $250,000 direct number the lab budget shows."
- **Common misuse:** assuming the PI has any discretion over the indirect-cost line, or that a lab's "budget" includes it — indirects go to the institution, not the lab, and are non-negotiable by individual PIs.

### IACUC

- **Definition:** Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee — the federally mandated body that reviews and approves every vertebrate-animal research protocol before work can begin, per the Animal Welfare Act and PHS Policy.
- **In use:** "The protocol covers up to 200 mice under this procedure — if we need more, that's an amendment, not a note in the next progress report."
- **Common misuse:** treating IACUC approval as a one-time gate rather than a living document — any change to species, procedure, personnel, pain category, or animal numbers requires a formal amendment before the change happens, not after.

### IBC (Institutional Biosafety Committee)

- **Definition:** the committee that reviews research involving recombinant/synthetic nucleic acids and biohazardous materials, assigning a biosafety level (BSL-1 through BSL-4) and approving containment practices.
- **In use:** "That construct pushes us from BSL-1 to BSL-2 containment — new SOPs and a protocol amendment before anyone touches it."
- **Common misuse:** conflating IBC review with IACUC review — a project can need one, both, or neither depending on whether it involves animals, biohazardous agents, or both; assuming one committee's approval covers the other's scope.

### CRediT (Contributor Roles Taxonomy)

- **Definition:** a standardized, NISO-approved taxonomy of 14 contributor roles (conceptualization, data curation, formal analysis, writing, etc.) increasingly required by journals in place of a free-text authorship-contribution statement.
- **In use:** "Check the CRediT statement before you assume author order reflects contribution — the journal requires it now, and it's often more informative than the byline order."
- **Common misuse:** assuming author order alone communicates contribution type — CRediT exists precisely because order conventions (first = most work, last = PI/senior) vary by subfield and don't capture who did what.

### R01 vs. R21 vs. K99/R00

- **Definition:** NIH mechanism types — R01 is the standard multi-year (usually 3–5 year) independent research grant; R21 is a smaller, shorter (usually 2-year) exploratory/high-risk award without preliminary-data requirements; K99/R00 is a career-transition award funding a postdoc's final mentored years (K99) then their first independent-lab years (R00).
- **In use:** "This idea is too preliminary for an R01 — no pilot data yet. Write it as an R21 first, then use R21 results as the R01's preliminary data."
- **Common misuse:** pursuing an R01 without pilot data because "the idea is strong" — study sections score preliminary feasibility data heavily on R01s specifically; that's what R21s exist to generate.

### DFW rate

- **Definition:** the percentage of enrolled students who receive a D, an F, or withdraw from a course — a standard institutional metric for course difficulty/outcome, distinct from the average grade.
- **In use:** "DFW went from 18% to 24% after the redesign — before concluding the redesign failed, check whether that's concentrated pre-midterm or holds through finals."
- **Common misuse:** reading a DFW change in isolation from exam-content changes — a redesign that also raised the cognitive level tested (per Bloom's taxonomy) will show a DFW increase that reflects harder, not worse, assessment, and comparing it to the old course's DFW on different exams isn't a clean comparison.

### Preprint

- **Definition:** a complete manuscript posted to a public server (bioRxiv, medRxiv) before or during peer review, citable and dated but not yet formally reviewed.
- **In use:** "We preprinted it the day we submitted to the journal — that establishes priority on the finding regardless of how long review takes."
- **Common misuse:** treating a preprint as equivalent to a peer-reviewed publication for tenure/grant purposes — it establishes priority and enables early feedback, but most review panels still weight it below a peer-reviewed accept, and a preprint can be corrected or withdrawn without the peer-review paper trail.

### Cost-share (effort)

- **Definition:** faculty or institutional effort committed to a grant without being charged to the grant's budget — commonly PI salary effort on federal grants where the institution absorbs the cost.
- **In use:** "My 15% effort on this R01 is cost-shared by the department — it's on my annual effort report but doesn't draw from the modules."
- **Common misuse:** assuming committed effort not charged to the grant is "free" — it still counts against the PI's total effort commitments across all grants (NIH caps total committed effort at 100%), and over-committing on paper is a compliance finding during an audit.

### Tenure clock / stop-the-clock

- **Definition:** the fixed pre-tenure review period (commonly 6–7 years) during which a faculty member must build the case for tenure; "stop-the-clock" is an institutional policy pausing that period for defined events (parental leave, documented medical leave), extending the deadline without penalty.
- **In use:** "I'm stopping the clock for parental leave — that moves my tenure review from year 6 to year 7, and it's not optional to disclose, it's automatic at most institutions now."
- **Common misuse:** assuming a stop-the-clock extension signals weaker productivity to a tenure committee — most current policies explicitly instruct committees to evaluate the same volume of work regardless of the extension, though norms vary by institution and it's worth confirming locally.

### Co-PI vs. multi-PI

- **Definition:** multi-PI is a formal NIH designation (since 2006) where two or more PIs share formal authority and accountability on a single award, each named on the notice of award; "co-investigator" is a different, subordinate role with no independent budget authority.
- **In use:** "We're structuring this as multi-PI, not PI-plus-co-I, because both labs need independent authority over their sub-budgets."
- **Common misuse:** using "co-PI" informally to describe a co-investigator — the terms carry different formal authority and reporting obligations to the sponsor, and using them interchangeably in an application can trigger an administrative query.
