# Playbook

Filled sequences and templates for the recurring processes in running a biology research program. Defaults, not laws — deviate consciously and document why.

## 1. NIH modular budget table (annual, R01)

Modules are $25,000 increments of direct costs; NIH doesn't want line-item detail for modular budgets in the application, but the PI needs one internally to plan personnel and to justify a rebudget later.

| Category | Year 1 | Year 2 | Year 3 (planned) | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| PI salary (effort, no direct charge on NIH cap institutions typically cost-share) | — | — | — | Often institutional cost-share, not drawn from modules |
| Postdoc salary + fringe (30% fringe assumed) | $68,000 | $70,700 | $75,400 | NIH postdoc salary scale, ~4% annual step |
| Grad student stipend + tuition | $42,000 | $43,300 | $44,600 | Tuition remission often billed separately, check institutional policy |
| Technician (0.5 FTE) | $19,200 | $19,600 | $20,000 | IACUC/IBC-listed personnel changes require protocol amendment |
| Reagents/supplies | $95,000 | $105,000 | $118,600 | Scales with animal cohort size and assay throughput |
| Equipment (>$5,000 threshold) | $20,000 | $0 | $30,000 | Front-load or align to instrument replacement cycle |
| Publication/travel | $5,000 | $5,500 | $6,000 | First line cut under a reduced award |
| **Direct total** | **$249,200** | **$244,100** | **$250,000** (rounds to 10 modules) | Modules round to nearest $25k in the application |

**Rebudget rule of thumb:** NIH allows up to 25% rebudget between categories without prior approval on most R01s; above that, or any change affecting the scope of the specific aims, requires prior written approval. Track cumulative rebudget percentage, not just the current change.

## 2. Authorship-contribution worksheet

Fill before proposing an order — every contributor rates every category, PI reconciles disagreements against lab notebook/data evidence, not memory.

| Contributor | Conception/design | Data generation | Analysis/interpretation | Manuscript drafting/editing | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| PI (Chen) | Primary | — | Major | Major (drafted discussion, edited all) | Corresponding author by default at PI-run labs |
| Postdoc (Alvarez) | Contributed (proposed Aim 2 pivot) | Primary (80% of figures) | Major | Major (drafted results, methods) | First-author candidate |
| Grad student (Kim) | — | Substantial (Figs 3–4, 20%) | Moderate | Minor (methods subsection) | Second-author candidate |
| Rotation student (Patel) | — | Minor (pilot data, not in final figures) | — | — | Acknowledgment, not authorship — below CRediT substantial-contribution bar |

**Decision rule:** first author = largest combined data-generation + drafting contribution; corresponding author = the person accountable for data integrity post-publication (usually the PI in a PI-run lab, sometimes a senior postdoc moving to their own lab, negotiated explicitly before submission — not assumed). Anyone below "substantial" in every column goes to acknowledgments, not the byline.

## 3. IACUC/IBC protocol amendment sequence

1. **Draft the amendment against the specific change** — new procedure, new personnel, new species/strain, or increased animal numbers. Vague amendments ("expanding scope of study") get sent back for specifics; this alone can cost 1–2 weeks.
2. **List all personnel by name with current training certification dates** — an amendment adding a technician who hasn't completed the institution's animal-handling course will stall at the compliance-office desk check, before it even reaches the committee.
3. **Submit and track the compliance-office desk-review clock separately from the full-committee-review clock** — desk review for minor amendments: 1–2 weeks; full committee (required for pain-category changes, new procedures, or species changes): 4–8 weeks, committee meets on a fixed monthly or bimonthly cycle, missing the submission deadline by a day adds a full cycle.
4. **Do not schedule bench time contingent on "likely approval."** Treat the protocol as not covering the work until the approval letter is in hand; a lab that starts early on an amendment that gets sent back for revision has generated data under an invalid protocol, which is a finding in an audit, not just a delay.
5. **File the approval letter and amended protocol number with the grant's compliance documentation** — a funder audit checks that the active protocol number matches what's cited in progress reports.

## 4. Wet-lab course section design gate

1. **Compute TA-to-student ratio for the section.** Above ~1:24, no TA can grade open-ended lab reports or troubleshoot individual bench failures in real time — the format has to be results-guaranteed ("cookbook") or the section becomes unmanageable.
2. **Below that ratio, decide if open-inquiry format is worth the reagent risk.** Open-inquiry (real chance of a failed experiment, students troubleshoot) teaches experimental reasoning better per Scientific Teaching principles, but costs more in reagents (failed runs get repeated) and TA training time.
3. **Write the rubric and answer key for the lab report before the first section runs**, same backward-design principle as lecture assessment — see [postsecondary-educator](../../postsecondary-educator/references/playbook.md) for the generic rubric-building mechanics, which apply unchanged here.
4. **Pilot new protocols with the TA team, not with students first.** A protocol that works in the PI's hands but hasn't been run by someone at the TA's experience level routinely reveals a missing reagent-prep step or an underspecified timing window.
