# Vocabulary

### Macula-on / macula-off
Describes whether a retinal detachment has spread to involve the central macula. "Macula-on" is a same-day surgical emergency because central vision is still salvageable; "macula-off" is urgent but the emergency framing no longer changes the final visual outcome once fluid has already undermined the macula.
**In use:** "It's macula-on, get him into the OR today — this isn't a 'sometime this week' case."
**Common misuse:** Treating any retinal detachment as equally time-critical, or conversely treating a macula-off detachment as non-urgent since "the damage is already done" — the surgery still needs to happen within about a week to prevent further vision loss and re-detachment risk.

### Relative afferent pupillary defect (RAPD / Marcus Gunn pupil)
A finding on the swinging-flashlight test indicating asymmetric optic nerve or severe retinal disease between the two eyes — the affected pupil paradoxically dilates when light swings onto it because the afferent signal from that eye is weaker.
**In use:** "There's a 2+ APD OS — this isn't just cataract, there's an optic nerve or severe retinal problem on that side."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a dense cataract alone can cause an APD — cataracts, however dense, do not cause a true RAPD; finding one always means real posterior-segment or optic-nerve pathology.

### Treat-and-extend
An anti-VEGF injection protocol where the interval between injections is lengthened or shortened at each visit based on that visit's OCT fluid status, rather than a fixed monthly schedule or a symptom-triggered PRN schedule.
**In use:** "She's dry at 8 weeks now, extend to 10 — but if she recurs, we drop straight back to 6, not down to 8."
**Common misuse:** Extending the interval on a fixed schedule ("it's been six visits, let's try 10 weeks") instead of on that specific visit's OCT findings — the interval is earned per visit, not per elapsed time.

### Central subfield thickness (CST)
The OCT-measured retinal thickness in the central 1mm of the macula — the single most-tracked anatomic number in AMD/DME management because it correlates with fluid presence and treatment need better than overall macular volume.
**In use:** "CST is 285 microns and dry — that's her baseline, extend the interval."
**Common misuse:** Reading overall macular volume or a qualitative "some fluid" note instead of the actual CST number and its trend — the treatment decision should reference the number, not an impression.

### Ectasia risk (corneal)
The risk that a cornea, after refractive surgery (LASIK/PRK), progressively steepens and thins because it was borderline abnormal (thin, or topographically irregular) before surgery and lost structural support it couldn't spare.
**In use:** "Topography shows inferior steepening and the residual stromal bed calculates under 250 microns — this patient's an ectasia risk, not a LASIK candidate, regardless of how stable the refraction looks today."
**Common misuse:** Screening only the manifest refraction and basic keratometry instead of full tomography (Pentacam) — a stable refraction does not rule out a topographically abnormal, ectasia-prone cornea.

### Posterior capsule rupture (PCR)
An intraoperative cataract surgery complication where the thin membrane behind the natural lens tears, risking vitreous prolapse, dropped lens fragments, and a higher risk of retinal detachment and endophthalmitis.
**In use:** "Case-mix adjusted, his PCR rate is right at 1.5% — that's benchmark, not a red flag."
**Common misuse:** Quoting a single population-average PCR rate (~1-2%) to every patient regardless of individual risk factors (pseudoexfoliation, dense nucleus, poor dilation) that meaningfully raise the actual risk for that specific case.

### Treat the number vs. treat the disease (IOP-in-isolation)
Shorthand for the error of managing glaucoma purely by chasing an intraocular pressure reading instead of corroborating it against structural (OCT-RNFL) and functional (visual field) findings.
**In use:** "He's got a normal IOP but a real RNFL defect and a matching field loss — this is normal-tension glaucoma, don't let the pressure number talk you out of treating it."
**Common misuse:** Assuming "IOP is normal" rules out glaucoma, or conversely starting drops purely because IOP is numerically elevated without any structural or functional corroboration (ocular hypertension, not glaucoma).

### Scleral depression
An exam technique using gentle external pressure on the eye during indirect ophthalmoscopy to bring the peripheral retina — anterior to the equator — into view; the only reliable way to examine the far periphery where most retinal tears occur.
**In use:** "Depress out to the ora before you call this a clean PVD exam — that's where tears actually hide."
**Common misuse:** Calling a peripheral exam complete after direct ophthalmoscopy or non-depressed indirect exam alone, both of which miss the anterior retina where tears are most common.

### Barrett Universal II / Kane formula
Modern-generation IOL power calculation formulas that outperform older formulas (SRK/T, Holladay 1) across a wider range of axial lengths, particularly at the short and long extremes.
**In use:** "Axial length's 24.1, unremarkable — Barrett's fine here, no need to reach for Hoffer Q."
**Common misuse:** Defaulting to whichever formula the biometer displays first, or using SRK/T for a short or long eye where its documented error is largest — formula choice should be deliberate, not default.

### OHTS risk calculator
A validated tool combining age, IOP, central corneal thickness, vertical cup-to-disc ratio, and pattern standard deviation to estimate 5-year risk of converting from ocular hypertension to primary open-angle glaucoma.
**In use:** "His calculated 5-year risk is 8% — that's below where I'd start drops on IOP alone, we'll re-image annually instead."
**Common misuse:** Treating "IOP over 21" as sufficient justification for medication without running the actual risk calculation — a numerically elevated IOP with a low calculated risk does not automatically warrant treatment.

### PVD (posterior vitreous detachment)
The age-related separation of the vitreous gel from the retinal surface, presenting as new flashes and floaters; benign on its own but the event that most commonly generates the traction that causes a retinal tear.
**In use:** "This is a classic PVD — Weiss ring present, no tear on depression today, but she needs the 4-6 week recheck, not just reassurance."
**Common misuse:** Treating "PVD" and "retinal tear" as the same finding, or discharging a symptomatic PVD patient with no scheduled recheck since the exam looked clean on the first visit.
