# Securities Sales Agent — Vocabulary

### Suitability (Reg BI "Care Obligation")
The requirement that a broker-dealer representative have a reasonable basis to believe a recommendation is in the client's best interest at the time made, considering cost among other factors — a narrower bar than fiduciary duty.
**In use:** "This file supports suitability at the time of the recommendation — the client's objective and horizon were documented before the trade."
**Common misuse:** treated as interchangeable with "fiduciary duty," which requires finding the objectively best available option, not merely a reasonable one.

### Trail fee (12b-1 fee)
An ongoing annual fee, typically 0.25%–1.00% of assets, paid out of fund assets to compensate the selling representative and cover marketing/distribution costs, embedded in the fund's expense ratio for the life of the holding.
**In use:** "The C-share's 1.00% trail is the whole reason the breakeven math favors A-shares past year seven."
**Common misuse:** ignored when comparing "no-load" products, as if a lack of front-end load means no ongoing cost to the client.

### Front-end load (Class A shares)
A one-time sales charge, commonly 3–5.75%, deducted from the initial investment at purchase; typically paired with the lowest ongoing trail fee among share classes.
**In use:** "The 5.75% load reduces the initial investment to $188,500 before any return is applied."
**Common misuse:** treated as making the share class categorically "expensive" without running the breakeven comparison against the client's actual horizon.

### Contingent deferred sales charge (CDSC)
A declining back-end sales charge (e.g., 5% in year one, stepping down to 0% by year five or six) applied if a B-share or similar product is sold before the surrender schedule expires.
**In use:** "The CDSC disqualifies B-shares here — the client may need this money in year three."
**Common misuse:** overlooked when a client's liquidity timeline isn't explicitly checked against the schedule at the point of sale.

### Breakpoint
A purchase-amount threshold (e.g., $50,000, $100,000, $250,000) at which a fund's front-end load percentage drops, sometimes retroactively across a household's related accounts (rights of accumulation).
**In use:** "Aggregating the couple's two accounts crosses the $100,000 breakpoint and drops the load from 5.75% to 4.50%."
**Common misuse:** not checked across related household accounts, leaving a client paying a higher load than they qualify for.

### Guaranteed lifetime withdrawal benefit (GLWB)
An annuity rider that guarantees a fixed withdrawal percentage on a "benefit base" for the client's life, independent of the actual account value, at an additional annual cost.
**In use:** "The GLWB rider costs 0.95%/yr to guarantee a 5% withdrawal rate on the benefit base for life."
**Common misuse:** described as "the annuity's return" rather than the cost of insuring a specific longevity risk, conflating the guarantee with investment performance.

### Reasonable basis, customer-specific, and quantitative suitability
The three components historically evaluated under FINRA Rule 2111 and folded into Reg BI's Care Obligation: whether a product is suitable for any investor, for this specific investor, and whether a series of recommended transactions together (not just individually) is suitable.
**In use:** "Each individual switch passed customer-specific suitability, but the file needs to address quantitative suitability across all four switches together."
**Common misuse:** evaluating only whether each individual transaction was suitable, missing that a pattern of individually-defensible transactions can still fail quantitative suitability (churning).

### Churning
Excessive trading or product-switching in a client account primarily to generate commissions, disproportionate to the client's objective or trading needs.
**In use:** "Three annuity replacements in eighteen months with no documented change in objective is a churning pattern, not three individually defensible trades."
**Common misuse:** assumed to require proof of intent; regulators evaluate the pattern and documentation, not just the rep's stated motive.

### Rights of accumulation
A provision allowing a client's related accounts (spouse, dependent, or household accounts within the same fund family) to be aggregated when calculating breakpoint discounts.
**In use:** "Rights of accumulation let us combine both spouses' IRAs to hit the next breakpoint."
**Common misuse:** not offered proactively, leaving eligible clients paying a higher load than the household qualifies for in aggregate.

### Series 7 / Series 63 / Series 65 / Series 66
FINRA and state licensing exams: Series 7 (general securities registered representative), Series 63 (state securities law), Series 65 (investment adviser representative), Series 66 (combines 63 and 65).
**In use:** "She holds a 7 and 66, so she can both sell securities and act in an advisory capacity depending on the account type."
**Common misuse:** assumed a single license covers both the commission-based sales role and the fiduciary advisory role — the license held determines which legal standard applies to a given account, and a rep can operate under different standards for different accounts.

### Reg BI Disclosure Obligation vs. Care Obligation
Two of Reg BI's four component obligations: Disclosure requires informing the client of material facts about the recommendation and relationship; Care requires the recommendation actually be in the client's best interest considering cost, risk, and alternatives — satisfying one does not satisfy the other.
**In use:** "Disclosing the commission difference satisfies the Disclosure Obligation, but the file still needs to show the Care Obligation's cost analysis independently."
**Common misuse:** treated as a single combined requirement, where disclosing a conflict is assumed to excuse a recommendation that fails the cost/best-interest test.
