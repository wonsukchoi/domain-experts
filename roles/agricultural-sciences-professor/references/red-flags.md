# Red Flags — Smell Tests a Land-Grant Faculty Member Catches Instantly

Each entry: what the smell usually means, the first question to ask, and what to actually pull. None are automatic verdicts — they're triggers for a specific check that routinely changes the response.

---

## 1. Grad student produces no new data for 6+ weeks with no reported cause

**Usually means:** an infrastructure or data-access failure the student didn't escalate (irrigation, equipment, IRB/IACUC lapse), or a mentoring-cadence gap where check-ins quietly slipped from biweekly to ad hoc — rarely a pure motivation problem, though it gets diagnosed as one by default.

**First question:** "What broke, and when did you first notice it?"

**Data to pull:** the student's field/lab notebook or data log for the gap period, the last three committee-meeting minutes (are they even happening on schedule?), and any facilities/equipment tickets filed in that window.

---

## 2. SET scores drop for the same course, 3 consecutive semesters

**Usually means:** a logistics failure (slow grade turnaround, syllabus/assignment mismatch, unclear rubric) compounding each semester, not that the material got harder — content difficulty doesn't usually trend downward in student ratings three semesters running unless something structural changed.

**First question:** "What changed in this course three semesters ago — instructor, grading turnaround, assignment sequence, TA support?"

**Data to pull:** grade-posting timestamps against assignment due dates, the syllabus diff across the three semesters, and the specific SET comment themes (logistics complaints vs. content complaints are different problems).

---

## 3. Grant annual progress report shows <50% of proposed milestones met at the midpoint

**Usually means:** either the original proposal's timeline was optimistic relative to real field-season constraints (common in ag research — one bad planting season can cost a full year), or a key hire (postdoc, technician) was delayed and cascaded every downstream milestone.

**First question:** "Which specific milestone slipped first, and did everything after it slip by the same amount or compound?"

**Data to pull:** the original proposal timeline against the hiring/onboarding dates actually achieved, and whether the delay traces to one weather-dependent field season (recoverable, explainable to the program officer) or a structural understaffing problem (not).

---

## 4. Field trial shows >30% coefficient of variation on the variable you plan to publish on

**Usually means:** insufficient true biological/field replicates (not technical replicates), a plot-design confound (edge effects, uneven irrigation, soil heterogeneity across the block), or genuine high biological variability that needs a different statistical model, not more reps.

**First question:** "Is this variance from too few reps, or would more reps at the same plot design still show this much noise?"

**Data to pull:** a quick power calculation at current n vs. the CV, and a soil/topography map of the trial block checked against the randomization scheme — heterogeneity that lines up with block position is a design problem, not a sample-size problem.

---

## 5. Extension field-day attendance drops more than 40% year over year

**Usually means:** the topic no longer matches what growers are dealing with this season (a drought-tolerance field day the year after a historically wet season, for instance), a scheduling conflict with a competing county event, or the county agent relationship that drives attendance has lapsed.

**First question:** "Did the county agent co-promote this the way they did the last three years?"

**Data to pull:** this year's RSVP list against the agent's promotion timeline, and whether the topic still maps to what's showing up in current extension call-in questions from growers.

---

## 6. A multistate Hatch project hasn't produced a joint publication in 3+ years

**Usually means:** the project has become an administrative renewal (shared formula funds, no shared work) rather than an active collaboration — common once the original PIs who drove the joint work rotate off or retire.

**First question:** "When collaborating stations last met, was there an actual joint dataset discussed, or just individual station updates?"

**Data to pull:** the last three annual meeting minutes for evidence of a shared dataset or manuscript in progress, versus a round-robin of individual reports.

---

## 7. IACUC, IRB, or biosafety protocol lapses mid-experiment

**Usually means:** the renewal deadline was missed because it wasn't calendared separately from the grant's own deadlines — protocol cycles and funding cycles rarely align, and this is a common gap for new PIs used to tracking only the grant calendar.

**First question:** "What is the exact date the protocol lapsed, and was any data collected after that date?"

**Data to pull:** the protocol's approval and expiration dates against the lab notebook's data-collection log — any data collected post-lapse is compromised for publication and must be flagged to the compliance office, not quietly kept.

---

## 8. Annual Faculty Activity Report shows research time below the appointment percentage for 2+ consecutive years

**Usually means:** teaching or service commitments (course overloads, committee assignments) have quietly absorbed research time because they carry weekly deadlines while research doesn't — the single most common structural drift in a land-grant appointment, and the one that shows up too late if not checked annually.

**First question:** "What specifically filled the hours that should have gone to research — is it recurring or a one-time overload?"

**Data to pull:** two years of FAR time allocations against the appointment letter's stated percentages, and whether any of the overage is a standing assignment (a committee seat that was never rotated off) rather than a temporary spike.

---

## 9. AFRI or major-program proposal returns reviewer scores of "very good" two cycles running, never funded

**Usually means:** the proposal is close but consistently loses on the same weakness (commonly preliminary-data power, or a co-PI team that looks thin relative to competing applications) that isn't being addressed between resubmissions — reviewers are telling the PI the same thing twice.

**First question:** "Did the resubmission actually change the specific weakness the panel summary named, or mainly add new content elsewhere?"

**Data to pull:** the panel summary's stated weaknesses from both cycles, compared line-by-line against what changed in the resubmission.
