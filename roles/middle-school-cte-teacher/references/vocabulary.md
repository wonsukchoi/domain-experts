# Working vocabulary — terms a middle school CTE teacher uses precisely

Format: definition → how a practitioner says it → common misuse.

**Exploratory wheel (rotation model)** — A scheduling structure where students cycle through short (typically 6-9 week) blocks in several different career/technical areas across a year, rather than taking one year-long course.
In use: "Section 4 rotates out of Manufacturing & Materials Friday — new cohort starts Monday with a clean safety slate."
Misuse: treating "exploratory" as a synonym for "less rigorous," rather than as a different purpose (career awareness) with its own rigor (safety, honest interest signal).

**CTE concentrator (Perkins V)** — A student who has completed two CTE courses taken for high school credit within a single career pathway, as defined under the Perkins V accountability framework.
In use: "He's not a concentrator yet — this rotation doesn't carry high school credit, so it doesn't start that count."
Misuse: assuming any completed CTE-labeled course, including a middle school exploratory rotation, contributes to concentrator status — only high-school-credit-bearing courses in a single pathway count.

**Program of study** — A coordinated, multi-year sequence of courses (often spanning middle school exposure through high school pathway courses to a postsecondary credential) aligned to a specific career area.
In use: "The wheel feeds our program of study, but it isn't itself a credit-bearing step in it."
Misuse: treating "part of a program of study" as equivalent to "earns credit toward it" — exposure and credit are different claims even when a district markets them together.

**CTSO (Career and Technical Student Organization)** — A student organization (e.g., TSA, FCCLA) tied to CTE coursework, running chapter meetings and competitive events separate from, but building on, classroom instruction.
In use: "TSA runs separate middle school and high school competition tracks — she competes in the middle school division this year."
Misuse: assuming CTSO participation itself functions like a class grade or credit — it's co-curricular, not a graded course component, even when it reinforces the same skills.

**Career cluster** — One of the broad, standardized groupings (e.g., Manufacturing, Health Science, Business Management) used to organize career-awareness content and pathway options.
In use: "This rotation gives every student direct exposure to the Manufacturing cluster before they pick a high school pathway."
Misuse: using "cluster" and "pathway" interchangeably — a cluster is the broad category; a pathway is the specific multi-course sequence within it.

**Safety pass-off** — The formal, equipment-specific certification process (written test plus, where needed, individual item review) a student must complete before operating a specific machine.
In use: "He's cleared for the band saw and belt sander, but not the drill press yet — pass-off is per machine, not blanket."
Misuse: treating a single, general classroom safety talk as satisfying the pass-off for every piece of equipment in the room, rather than requiring the equipment-specific test for each machine category.

**Station rotation** — A within-period scheduling structure where small groups of students cycle through different equipment stations rather than the whole class using the same tool at once.
In use: "Three students per band saw, rotating every 8 minutes — that's how 24 students share 8 machines in one period."
Misuse: confusing it with the exploratory wheel (the year-long rotation between electives) — station rotation is a within-period, within-block structure, a different scale entirely.

**Articulation agreement** — A formal, signed agreement between a school district and a high school (or postsecondary institution) specifying that a given course counts for credit at the receiving institution.
In use: "Unless there's a named articulation agreement for this exact course code, assume the credit doesn't transfer."
Misuse: assuming general subject-matter overlap ("it's basically the same content as the high school course") substitutes for a named, signed agreement — it doesn't.

**Employability skills** — The transferable workplace behaviors (following multi-step directions, working a shared schedule, basic team communication) assessed and taught alongside, but separately from, technical content.
In use: "His employability-skills note this cycle: consistently on-task in station rotations, still needs prompting to clean a station without being asked."
Misuse: folding employability-skills feedback into the technical skill grade — they're reported as a separate line so neither signal is diluted by the other.

**Career interest inventory** — A short, typically ungraded instrument (district-provided, e.g., a Kuder- or Xello-style tool) collecting a student's self-reported interest level in the cluster just experienced, used to inform counselor pathway conversations.
In use: "The inventory result goes to the counselor for scheduling conversations — it's not a gradebook entry."
Misuse: grading the inventory's content, which gives students a reason to answer strategically rather than honestly, corrupting the exact signal it exists to produce.

**Exposure vs. mastery** — The distinction between a student having encountered and practiced a skill briefly (exposure, the exploratory wheel's actual goal) versus having reached an assessable proficiency level in it (mastery, the year-long high school course's goal).
In use: "This block is exposure, not mastery — one crosscut and one rip cut, not a full joinery unit."
Misuse: designing a 6-week block's assessment as if it needs to certify mastery, which produces an over-packed unit that achieves neither goal well.

**PLTW unit (Project Lead The Way Gateway)** — A named, nine-week, 45-minute-period curriculum unit (e.g., Design & Modeling, Automation & Robotics) used by many middle school exploratory programs as a structured alternative to a locally built rotation.
In use: "We're running the PLTW Automation & Robotics unit this cycle — nine weeks, VEX kits, culminating build."
Misuse: assuming any robotics or design unit is "PLTW" regardless of whether it follows the named curriculum's specific unit structure and materials — PLTW is a specific, licensed curriculum, not a generic label for hands-on STEM content.
