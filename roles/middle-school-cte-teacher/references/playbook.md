# Playbook: Rotation Cycle, Safety Certification, CTSO Matching, and Pathway-Credit Conversations

Filled process for the four recurring executions of the role. Reference context throughout: a "Manufacturing & Materials" 6-week exploratory rotation, 24 students per cohort, six cohorts per year, 8 total machines (3 band saws, 2 drill presses, 3 belt sanders).

## 1. Rotation Cycle Master Plan (filled example)

Block: Manufacturing & Materials, 6 weeks, 30 scheduled 40-minute periods, Section 4 (24 students).

**Stage 1 — Desired result (exploratory, not concentrator-track)**

- Career-awareness goal: a student can say, with evidence from having actually done it, whether hands-on fabrication work is something they'd want to pursue further — not whether they've mastered the trade.
- Sub-objectives, capped at 2 for a 6-week block: (a) measuring and layout to 1/16 inch, (b) a single-operation machine cut (one crosscut, one rip) on the band saw and belt sander.

**Stage 2 — Evidence** (decided before Stage 3 activities)

- Safety gate: 40-item equipment-specific test, 100% required, individual item review for any miss, administered Day 1 before any machine contact.
- Deliverable: one pine pencil box per student requiring exactly the two sub-objective skills — no additional joinery, finishing chemistry, or blueprint-reading content, which belong to the year-long high school course, not this block.
- Interest signal: a short, ungraded reflection ("would you take a full course in this?") collected on the last day, routed to the counselor record, never to the gradebook.

**Stage 3 — Day-by-day plan (30 periods)**

| Day(s) | Focus | Activity | Ties to |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Safety certification | 40-item test, 100% + individual signoff; no machine contact until cleared | Gate, not a sub-objective |
| 2-3 | Measuring & layout | Layout practice on scrap stock to 1/16 inch, checked before any cut is made | sub-obj (a) |
| 4-9 | Band saw / belt sander operation | 3-student-per-machine station rotation (24 ÷ 8 machines); one supervised crosscut + one rip per student across the rotation | sub-obj (b) |
| 10-27 | Deliverable build | Pencil box assembly, sanding, finish; station rotation continues for any remaining cuts | (a), (b), deliverable |
| 28 | Deliverable completion / cleanup | Final assembly, tool return, workstation reset | deliverable |
| 29 | Interest reflection + rotation-close summary | Ungraded reflection collected; skill grade finalized separately | interest signal, routed separately |
| 30 | Rotation changeover | New cohort's roster pulled; chart rebuild begins for the next cycle | next-cycle prep |

29 of 30 periods are usable instructional time once Day 1 is spent entirely on certification — the block is planned around that number, not around 30.

## 2. Day-One Equipment Safety Certification Process

Run this in full before any student touches a band saw, drill press, or belt sander — every cohort, every cycle, regardless of what a prior class or a building-wide orientation covered.

**Step 1 — Confirm zero prior certification for this specific roster.** The building's fall fire/hallway orientation and any prior cohort's signed test do not transfer to this roster on this equipment.

**Step 2 — Administer the 40-item written test.** Example result set (n=24): 14 students score 40/40 on the first attempt.

**Step 3 — Individually review every missed item with the remaining 10 students.** Example distribution: 6 students miss exactly 1 item, 3 miss exactly 2 items, 1 misses 4 items. Total missed-item reviews: (6×1) + (3×2) + (1×4) = 6 + 6 + 4 = 16, across 10 students (average 1.6 items/student). Each review is the student explaining the correct answer back and the instructor signing off on that specific item — not simply re-marking the test.

**Step 4 — Clear the full roster before any hands-on work.** All 24 students clear by end of Day 1 in this example; if any student cannot clear by end of period, that student's hands-on start is delayed to Day 2 individually rather than delaying the whole cohort.

**Step 5 — Log the certification per cohort per cycle**, not per student per year: roster ID, date, item-miss counts, and instructor signoff, filed against this specific 6-week block.

**Step 6 — Rebuild the tool-checkout, PPE-sizing, and workstation-pairing chart from this roster**, distributing any students with prior documented equipment exposure (from a prior elective or an older sibling's tools at home, if disclosed) across stations rather than clustering them, and never carrying forward the prior cohort's chart.

## 3. CTSO (TSA/FCCLA-Style) Event-Matching Worksheet

For chapter roster decisions, match each student to an event tied to a skill they actually practiced this cycle — not to enthusiasm or open roster slots alone.

| Student | Rotation skill demonstrated | Candidate event | Match quality |
|---|---|---|---|
| A | Measuring/layout + single-operation cut, strong finish quality | Manufacturing/fabrication-category event | Good match — event tests exactly this skill set |
| B | Enthusiastic, but rotation ended before machine stations (transferred in late) | CAD/design-only event, or a general team/leadership event | Redirect from a machine-based event — no demonstrated machine skill this cycle |
| C | Passed safety cleanly but struggled with layout tolerance (consistently off by 1/8"+) | Team/leadership event, or hold for next rotation cycle before a precision-based event | Redirect — entering now risks a public poor showing on a skill not yet solid |

General/leadership category events (not tied to one technical skill) are the right default for students whose enthusiasm outruns their demonstrated technical skill this cycle — not a technical event picked to fill a roster slot.

## 4. Pathway/Concentrator-Credit Conversation Script

Trigger: a parent or counselor asks whether completing the wheel (or this specific rotation) "counts toward" a high school CTE pathway or Perkins V concentrator status.

**Step 1 — Ask which specific high school course code they mean**, if any — don't answer the general version of the question first.

**Step 2 — Check the district's Perkins V articulation/course-equivalency list for that exact code.** Default assumption: no middle school exploratory course is on it, because Perkins V concentrator status requires two CTE courses taken for high school credit within a single career pathway; middle school courses are categorically excluded regardless of content overlap.

**Step 3 — State the distinction plainly, in both directions.** The rotation is real career-awareness value (the student now has direct evidence of whether this pathway interests them) and it is not credit-bearing toward concentrator status or a high school transcript.

**Deliverable (quoted, sent in response to a parent inquiry):** "Completing the Manufacturing & Materials rotation is genuinely useful for [student]'s course planning — they now have hands-on evidence of whether this is a pathway worth choosing in high school. To be precise about credit, though: under Perkins V, concentrator status requires two CTE courses taken for high school credit within one career pathway. This middle school rotation isn't a high-school-credit course, so it doesn't count toward that status by itself — that count would begin with [student]'s first high-school-credit-bearing CTE course, likely in 9th grade. I'm flagging this now so scheduling expectations match what actually counts."

## 5. Incident Log & Station-Pause Protocol

- Log every incident or near-miss the same day, tagged to the specific station (not just "the shop") and the specific cohort/cycle.
- On any incident, pause that specific station for the remainder of the current cycle pending a root-cause check — not the whole room, unless the cause is room-wide (e.g., a power/electrical issue).
- Before clearing the station for the next cohort's cycle, check the log for any prior incident at that same station across earlier cohorts this year — a repeat at the same station across different cohorts points to an equipment or layout problem, not student carelessness.
- Do not resolve an incident by unilaterally changing a written safety procedure in the moment — flag the specific gap to whoever owns the shop safety policy and update the test/procedure formally before the next cohort's Day 1.
