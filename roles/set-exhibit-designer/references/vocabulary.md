# Set and Exhibit Designer — Vocabulary

### Load-in / Strike
Load-in is the process of assembling and installing the set or exhibit inside the venue; strike is the process of disassembling and removing it.
**In use:** "We've got a 10-hour load-in window and a 4-hour strike the following morning — the schedule has to fit both."
**Common misuse:** Treating "load-in" as synonymous with "install time for the whole show" — it specifically refers to the physical assembly window, not rehearsal, tech, or opening prep that follows it.

### Sightline
The unobstructed line of vision from a specific seat or viewing position to a specific point on the set or exhibit.
**In use:** "Check the sightline from house-left row 3 — the proscenium leg might clip the left flank of the centerpiece."
**Common misuse:** Checking sightlines only from a "typical" or best seat rather than the physically worst position in the house or gallery, which is where sightline failures actually surface.

### Rigging point-load rating
The maximum weight a specific structural attachment point (grid, batten, truss point) is certified to bear, set by the venue's structural engineering and periodically re-inspected.
**In use:** "Grid point 4 is rated for 500 lbs — we're hanging 170, well inside the 60% touring margin."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a rating confirmed at one venue applies at another, or treating the rated maximum as a safe working load rather than leaving margin for touring wear and inspection variance.

### C-value (sightline calculation)
A stadium/theatre seating-design metric describing the vertical clearance a seated viewer has over the head of the person in front, used to verify unobstructed sightlines across a raked seating section.
**In use:** "The rake doesn't give us enough C-value in the back third — we'll lose the lower half of that backdrop from row 18 back."
**Common misuse:** Applying it only to fixed-seating theatre design and overlooking that the same obstruction logic applies to standing-room exhibit viewing with columns or other patrons in the sightline.

### Strike-and-rebuild cycle
One complete cycle of disassembling a touring set or exhibit at one venue and reassembling it at the next.
**In use:** "This material choice needs to survive twelve strike-and-rebuild cycles, not just the one install."
**Common misuse:** Pricing and specifying materials as if for a single, permanent install when the actual use case is a touring show with repeated disassembly stress.

### Crew-hour
One hour of labor by one crew member; used to convert a fixed install window and crew headcount into a total labor budget comparable against a task-time estimate.
**In use:** "We've got 80 crew-hours available — 8 crew times a 10-hour window — and the zone estimates total 72."
**Common misuse:** Estimating install time in calendar hours ("it'll take about a day") without multiplying by crew headcount, which hides whether the actual labor budget is sufficient.

### Hero element
The single scenic or exhibit element designed to carry the most visual weight and audience attention — typically the first thing the eye lands on.
**In use:** "The animatronic is our hero element — its budget line should reflect that, not an even split with the graphic panels."
**Common misuse:** Splitting budget evenly across all elements by count, which under-funds the hero element and over-funds secondary pieces the audience barely notices.

### Fabrication cut-list
An itemized list of every physical component to be built, with material, quantity, dimensions, and unit cost — the document a fabrication shop bids and builds from.
**In use:** "Get the cut-list to the shop by Friday so they can price the Dibond order before the deadline."
**Common misuse:** Treating a rendering or a verbal description as sufficient to send to a fabrication shop — a cut-list has to specify exact material, dimension, and quantity to be biddable.

### Point-of-obstruction check
Verifying whether a physical structure (column, proscenium leg, another exhibit case) blocks the sightline from a specific viewing position, distinct from a general sightline review.
**In use:** "Run the point-of-obstruction check for every column in the gallery, not just the ones we remember from the walkthrough."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a single walkthrough catches every obstruction, when different viewing angles reveal different obstructions the designer's own eye-level pass missed.

### Dock-to-floor route
The full physical path an element travels from the delivery truck through the dock, freight elevator or ramp, and any doorways, to its final install position.
**In use:** "The specimen case clears the dock door but not the freight elevator door — we need to check the whole dock-to-floor route, not just the first bottleneck."
**Common misuse:** Checking only the largest single opening (usually the dock door) and assuming the rest of the route is clear, when a narrower downstream doorway or elevator is the actual constraint.

### Punch list
The list of unfinished or defective items identified during a final walkthrough, to be corrected before opening.
**In use:** "We've got a six-item punch list from this morning's walkthrough — two are cosmetic, four need to close before doors open."
**Common misuse:** Treating every punch-list item as equal priority rather than triaging which items block opening versus which are cosmetic fixes that can wait.

### Traveling exhibition
An exhibit designed and fabricated to move between multiple venues on a defined tour schedule, as distinct from a permanent or single-venue installation.
**In use:** "This is built as a traveling exhibition — twelve stops over eighteen months — so every material choice gets priced against that run length."
**Common misuse:** Designing and budgeting a traveling exhibition the same way as a permanent installation, missing the repair/replacement costs that accumulate across multiple strike-and-rebuild cycles.
