# Working vocabulary

Terms of art practitioners use precisely, that generalists misuse. Load this when writing or reviewing content that needs to sound like an actual medical/health services manager, not a generalist describing one.

### Acuity
A measure of how complex or resource-intensive a patient's care needs are, distinct from simple patient count (census) — used to adjust staffing beyond a headcount basis.
**In use:** "Census is flat but acuity's up 18% — the unit isn't actually staffed the way the numbers make it look."
**Common misuse:** Conflated with census — a unit can be "fully staffed" by patient-count ratio and meaningfully understaffed once acuity is factored in.

### Denial rate
The percentage of submitted insurance claims rejected by the payer, tracked as a revenue-cycle health indicator and, more usefully, broken down by root-cause category.
**In use:** "Denial rate's up to 7% from 5.2% — before we blame the payer, let's see which category grew."
**Common misuse:** Reported and discussed only as an aggregate percentage — the aggregate number doesn't tell you what to fix; the category breakdown does.

### Conditions of Participation (CoP)
The set of federal health and safety standards a healthcare facility must meet to participate in and receive payment from Medicare/Medicaid, enforced through survey and certification.
**In use:** "That staffing gap could put us out of compliance with the CoP for this unit type — this isn't a discretionary policy, it's a participation requirement."
**Common misuse:** Treated as equivalent to general "good practice" guidance — CoPs are a hard regulatory floor tied to the facility's ability to bill Medicare/Medicaid at all, not a best-practice recommendation.

### Root-cause analysis (RCA) / just culture
A structured, systems-focused review of an adverse event or near-miss aimed at identifying contributing factors, conducted within a "just culture" framework that distinguishes human error from reckless behavior in how it responds.
**In use:** "This near-miss goes through RCA, not a write-up — we need to know what let it happen, not just who was there."
**Common misuse:** Treated as synonymous with disciplinary investigation — just culture RCA is specifically designed to separate systems-level learning from individual blame, reserving discipline for reckless or intentional conduct, not honest error.

### Readmission rate (and penalty)
The percentage of discharged patients who return for inpatient care within a defined window (commonly 30 days), tracked both as a quality indicator and, for certain conditions under CMS programs, tied to reimbursement penalties.
**In use:** "That readmission was preventable per the case review — those are exactly what the CMS penalty program is measuring us against."
**Common misuse:** Treated purely as a quality metric disconnected from finance — readmission rates for specific conditions directly affect Medicare reimbursement under programs like the Hospital Readmissions Reduction Program, making this simultaneously a clinical and financial metric.

### Prior authorization
Payer approval required before a service is rendered (or reimbursed) for it to be covered — a process step whose failure is a common, specific, fixable cause of claim denials.
**In use:** "That denial was a missing prior auth, not a coding issue — different fix, different owner."
**Common misuse:** Lumped together with general "billing errors" rather than tracked as its own distinct denial category with its own specific process fix (typically a front-end scheduling/registration workflow issue, not a coding or clinical documentation issue).

### Cost avoidance (quality investment)
The framing of a quality/safety investment's value in terms of adverse-event costs it prevents, rather than as a pure expense evaluated against direct revenue generated.
**In use:** "This isn't a $85K cost against margin — it's avoiding an estimated $148K in fall-related costs, for a net positive."
**Common misuse:** Quality initiatives evaluated only as cost centers competing for budget against revenue-generating initiatives, without quantifying the adverse-event cost being avoided, which often makes the "cost" a net financial positive once calculated properly.

### Sentinel event
A patient safety event resulting in death, permanent harm, or severe temporary harm requiring immediate response and reporting, distinct from a routine incident or near-miss.
**In use:** "That's a sentinel event, not a routine incident report — it triggers a mandatory root-cause analysis and Joint Commission notification timeline."
**Common misuse:** Used loosely for any serious-sounding incident — the sentinel event classification carries specific mandatory reporting and RCA-timeline obligations that a lesser incident doesn't, so misclassifying it either under- or over-triggers the wrong response process.
