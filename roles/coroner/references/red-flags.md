### Postmortem interval estimates from temperature, rigor, and lividity disagree by more than ~3-4 hours
- **Usually means:** An environmental factor (unusual ambient temperature, body wrapped/clothed heavily, obesity or thin body habitus affecting cooling rate) is distorting one of the three methods, most often the temperature-based estimate.
- **First question:** What was the ambient temperature and body covering at the scene, and does it explain the outlier method?
- **Data to pull:** Scene temperature log, clothing/covering description, body mass/build notes from the exam.

### Scene "looks like" suicide, accident, or homicide before toxicology or autopsy results are in
- **Usually means:** A manner-of-death conclusion is being drawn from scene aesthetics rather than evidence, and risks being contradicted once lab results return.
- **First question:** What specific physical or toxicological evidence, beyond scene appearance, supports this manner classification right now?
- **Data to pull:** Scene photographs, witness statements, pending toxicology/autopsy order status.

### Death occurred in law-enforcement custody
- **Usually means:** Nothing specific by default, but this category carries statutorily and procedurally higher scrutiny requirements (often mandatory autopsy, sometimes an independent/external pathologist) regardless of how routine the apparent cause appears.
- **First question:** Does this jurisdiction's custody-death protocol require an external or independent examiner, and has that requirement been triggered?
- **Data to pull:** Jurisdiction's in-custody death investigation protocol, chain-of-command documentation for the case assignment.

### No attending physician has seen the decedent within the statutory window, but the death is being certified without coroner referral
- **Usually means:** The reportability check was skipped or misapplied — this is one of the most common statutory triggers for coroner jurisdiction and is easy to miss when the apparent cause looks unremarkable.
- **First question:** When did the decedent last see a physician, and does that gap exceed this jurisdiction's statutory threshold?
- **Data to pull:** Decedent's medical records, jurisdiction's specific reportable-death statute.

### Chain-of-custody documentation for scene evidence starts after the body or scene was already disturbed
- **Usually means:** Evidence was handled (moved, photographed after alteration, specimens collected) before a formal log existed, creating a gap a defense attorney can challenge.
- **First question:** What is the earliest timestamped chain-of-custody entry, and what happened at the scene before that entry?
- **Data to pull:** Chain-of-custody log, first responder or family member accounts of what was touched or moved before investigators arrived.

### A final death certificate is issued while toxicology or autopsy results are still pending
- **Usually means:** Pressure (from family, insurance, or investigative timeline) to close the case pushed certification ahead of complete evidence, risking a later amended certificate.
- **First question:** Are all ordered lab results back, and if not, why was final certification issued rather than an interim/pending entry?
- **Data to pull:** Lab order status log, certificate issuance date versus lab result return dates.

### Manner of death is certified as "accident" or "natural" for a death involving a known history of self-harm or a prior suicide attempt
- **Usually means:** The manner determination didn't account for available psychosocial history, which is a required part of a thorough manner-of-death investigation, not an optional add-on.
- **First question:** Was the decedent's mental health and self-harm history reviewed as part of this determination?
- **Data to pull:** Available medical/psychiatric history, family/witness interviews regarding recent behavior.

### Rigor mortis stage and lividity fixation point to meaningfully different postmortem intervals than witness-reported "last seen alive" time
- **Usually means:** Either the witness timeline is inaccurate (common — people misremember or round times), or the scene findings are being misread.
- **First question:** How was the "last seen alive" time established, and how confident is that source in the specific time?
- **Data to pull:** Witness statement with basis for the time estimate (e.g., a phone call log, a security camera timestamp) versus the scene-finding-based estimate.
