### Cause of death
The specific injury, disease, or physiological mechanism that directly resulted in death (e.g., "gunshot wound to chest," "acute myocardial infarction").
**In use:** "Cause of death is exsanguination due to a stab wound to the abdomen."
**Common misuse:** Using cause of death and manner of death interchangeably — they are separate certificate fields answering different questions.

### Manner of death
The legal classification of how the cause of death came about: natural, accident, suicide, homicide, or undetermined.
**In use:** "Same cause — gunshot wound — but the manner here is homicide, not suicide, based on the trajectory and scene evidence."
**Common misuse:** Assuming manner follows automatically from cause (e.g., any gunshot wound is automatically ruled a homicide) without evaluating the actual circumstances.

### Reportable death
A death that meets a jurisdiction's statutory criteria requiring coroner or medical examiner notification and jurisdiction, as opposed to routine certification by an attending physician.
**In use:** "No physician saw her in the required window, so this is a reportable death — it goes to our office, not her family doctor."
**Common misuse:** Treating "reportable" as a subjective judgment about how unusual a death seems, rather than checking it against the jurisdiction's specific statutory list.

### Algor mortis
The postmortem cooling of the body toward ambient temperature, used as one input for estimating time since death.
**In use:** "Algor mortis puts this in the 5-6 hour range, but we need rigor and lividity to narrow it down."
**Common misuse:** Treating the temperature-based estimate alone as a precise, standalone time of death rather than one of three cross-checked indicators.

### Rigor mortis
The postmortem stiffening of muscles, which typically begins within a few hours after death, peaks around 12 hours, and resolves by roughly 24-36 hours — used as a coarse time-since-death indicator.
**In use:** "Rigor's present in the jaw and neck but not the limbs yet — that's an early-stage finding, consistent with a shorter interval."
**Common misuse:** Using rigor mortis timing as a precise clock rather than a stage-based, temperature- and body-composition-sensitive range.

### Livor mortis (lividity)
The postmortem pooling and settling of blood under gravity, producing discoloration in dependent body areas, which fixes (stops blanching under pressure) after a number of hours.
**In use:** "Lividity's still blanching, so fixation hasn't occurred yet — consistent with under roughly 8-10 hours."
**Common misuse:** Failing to check whether lividity's location matches the position the body was found in — a mismatch suggests the body was moved after death.

### Autopsy
A postmortem medical examination, typically performed by a forensic pathologist, to determine or confirm cause of death, often including internal examination and tissue/fluid sampling.
**In use:** "Cause of death isn't clear from the scene alone — this one needs a full autopsy."
**Common misuse:** Assuming autopsy is optional whenever cause seems obvious, when many jurisdictions statutorily mandate it for specific death categories regardless of apparent cause.

### Toxicology screen
Laboratory testing of blood, urine, or tissue samples for drugs, alcohol, or other substances that may have contributed to death.
**In use:** "Certificate's on hold until the toxicology screen comes back — could change the cause finding."
**Common misuse:** Issuing a final cause-of-death finding before toxicology results return, when a substance finding could materially change or contradict the preliminary cause.

### Inquest
A formal legal proceeding, used in some jurisdictions, in which a coroner (sometimes with a jury) hears evidence to determine the facts and circumstances of a death.
**In use:** "This death is going to inquest given the public-interest questions around the circumstances."
**Common misuse:** Assuming inquests are used universally — many U.S. jurisdictions don't use them at all, relying instead on the coroner's or medical examiner's own findings.

### Chain of custody
The documented, unbroken record of who collected, handled, and transferred physical evidence, from the scene through final analysis.
**In use:** "We need a chain-of-custody entry logged the moment that specimen leaves the scene, not after it reaches the lab."
**Common misuse:** Starting the chain-of-custody log only once evidence reaches a formal facility, leaving a documentation gap for anything handled at the scene beforehand.

### Death certificate
The official legal document recording cause and manner of death, among other identifying and demographic information, used for legal, insurance, and vital-records purposes.
**In use:** "The interim death certificate lists cause and manner as pending until autopsy results are in."
**Common misuse:** Treating the certificate as a single combined "cause of death" entry rather than distinct cause and manner fields that each need independent support.

### Undetermined (manner of death)
A legitimate manner-of-death classification used when the evidence does not sufficiently support natural, accident, suicide, or homicide.
**In use:** "Evidence doesn't clearly point to accident or suicide here — this is going to be certified as undetermined."
**Common misuse:** Treating "undetermined" as a failure or an embarrassment to avoid, rather than the correct finding when the evidence genuinely doesn't support a more specific manner.
