# Occupational Therapy Assistant — Vocabulary

### Plan of care
The OT's formal document specifying goals, precautions, contraindications, and authorized treatment approach for a patient — the boundary within which a COTA operates.
**In use:** "The plan of care caps grip resistance until week 8, so today's session stays passive-active only."
**Common misuse:** Treating it as a loose guideline the COTA can deviate from based on how the patient seems that day — precautions and goals in the plan are not the COTA's to modify.

### Supervision (direct vs. general)
The state-specified level of OT oversight required for a COTA — direct supervision means the OT is on-site and immediately available; general supervision means the OT is available by communication but not necessarily on-site.
**In use:** "This state requires direct supervision for COTAs in the first year of practice, general supervision after."
**Common misuse:** Assuming supervision requirements are uniform nationally — they're state-specific and sometimes setting-specific (SNF vs. outpatient vs. home health).

### Co-signature
The supervising OT's required sign-off on a COTA's evaluation-adjacent documentation (progress notes, in some states every note) confirming the OT has reviewed it.
**In use:** "This note needs the OT's co-signature before it's submitted for billing."
**Common misuse:** Treating co-signature as a formality — it's the OT's documented confirmation of oversight, and a pattern of rubber-stamped co-signatures without real review is a compliance risk for both parties.

### Grading (an activity)
Adjusting the difficulty, resistance, repetitions, or complexity of a therapeutic activity within the plan's authorized range, based on the patient's demonstrated tolerance that session.
**In use:** "Graded the pinch-grip task down to 1 lb today — patient reported more fatigue than usual."
**Common misuse:** Confusing grading (adjusting difficulty within the plan) with modifying the plan (changing the goal, adding a new activity type, lifting a precaution) — only the former is within COTA scope without OT authorization.

### Standardized measure
A validated, repeatable assessment tool (goniometry, dynamometry, a validated ADL index) used the same way each session so results are comparable over time.
**In use:** "Same measure, same positioning, same time of day where possible — otherwise the trend line isn't real."
**Common misuse:** Switching measurement method or conditions session to session (different device, different time of day, different observer technique) and then treating the resulting numbers as a comparable trend.

### Plateau
Two or more consecutive sessions with no meaningful change on the plan's standardized measure.
**In use:** "Two sessions flat on grip strength — that's a plateau, flagging for OT review."
**Common misuse:** Calling a single session's unchanged number a plateau — one data point isn't a trend; the threshold is consecutive sessions with no movement.

### Skilled intervention
Treatment that requires the clinical judgment and technique of a licensed OT or supervised COTA, as distinct from activity a patient or caregiver could perform independently (a home program).
**In use:** "Once the patient can do this independently and safely, it's a home-program item, not a skilled-intervention goal."
**Common misuse:** Continuing to bill/deliver an activity as skilled intervention after the patient has demonstrably mastered it and could do it unsupervised — payers audit for exactly this.

### Contraindication vs. precaution
A contraindication is an absolute prohibition on an activity or approach; a precaution is a conditional limit (allowed, but only under specific conditions or up to a specific threshold).
**In use:** "No grip resistance is a precaution until week 8, not a contraindication — it lifts on a schedule, it's not permanent."
**Common misuse:** Using the terms interchangeably — a precaution has a lifting condition built in, a contraindication generally doesn't, and treating a precaution as absolute (or a contraindication as negotiable) is a real error, not a semantic one.

### Functional carryover
Whether a skill or gain demonstrated in a therapy session actually transfers to the patient's real-world performance of the task outside the clinical setting.
**In use:** "Grip strength is up, but we haven't confirmed carryover to actually opening jars at home yet."
**Common misuse:** Treating an in-session gain as equivalent to a functional outcome — the whole point of OT is the real-world task, and a clinic-only gain hasn't closed the loop yet.

### Discharge criteria
The specific, plan-stated conditions under which a patient is ready to end skilled OT/OTA services (goal attainment, plateau with maximal benefit reached, transition to a home program).
**In use:** "She's hit the plan's discharge criteria for the hand — recommending transition to a home program at the next OT check-in."
**Common misuse:** A COTA independently deciding a patient is "basically done" and reducing session frequency or intensity without OT sign-off — discharge is an OT determination, not a COTA one, even when the COTA's read is probably correct.
