# Rail Car Repairer — Vocabulary

### Bad order (BO)
A car formally tagged defective under FRA/interchange rules, removing it from interchange movement immediately until it is re-inspected and released — a binding network-wide status, not a maintenance note.
**In use:** "She's bad-ordered on the flange call — she moves light to the rip track and nowhere else until we clear the tag."
**Common misuse:** Treating a bad-order tag as advisory or reversible on the spot by a train crew or dispatcher, when only a qualified inspector can release it after the defect is actually corrected.

### Condemning limit
The specific, published measurement (flange thickness, flat-spot length, shoe thickness) past which a component must be replaced, defined by FRA regulation or AAR standard precisely so different inspectors reach the same verdict.
**In use:** "That flange is at seven-eighths — that's the condemning limit, not a judgment call."
**Common misuse:** Using "condemning limit" loosely to mean "looks pretty worn," when it specifically refers to the exact published number for that component and defect type.

### Single Car Test (SCTD)
The Single Car Test Device procedure, per AAR Standard S-486, that charges, leak-tests, and verifies brake cylinder development on an individual car's air brake system before it's released back to interchange.
**In use:** "Brake work's done — she doesn't get re-stenciled until she passes the single-car test."
**Common misuse:** Treating a subjective "brakes feel fine" check as equivalent to running the actual SCTD test and recording the pressures and leakage rate.

### Flat spot / wheel slide
A worn, flattened section of wheel tread caused by the wheel sliding (not rotating) against the rail during a brake application, distinct from gradual tread wear from normal rolling contact.
**In use:** "That's a slide flat, not wear-out — check the shoe on that side for a matching pattern."
**Common misuse:** Attributing any rough-riding wheel to "just old wear" without checking whether the wear pattern is a slide event, which changes both the urgency and the billing classification.

### Knuckle
The hinged, heat-treated steel casting at the coupler's open end that engages the mating car's knuckle to form the coupling; a safety-critical part that is scrapped, never welded, if cracked.
**In use:** "MPI shows an indication on that knuckle — scrap it, we don't weld those."
**Common misuse:** Treating a knuckle crack as a weldable repair like other steel castings, when welding destroys the heat-treatment temper that gives the part its fatigue strength.

### COT&S (Clean, Oil, Test & Stencil)
The periodic tank car air brake and safety equipment reconditioning and testing requirement, with the resulting date stenciled on the car; an expired COT&S date is itself a bad-order condition.
**In use:** "Her COT&S expired last month — she's bad-order regardless of how the brakes test today."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a car is interchange-eligible because the brakes function correctly, without checking whether the periodic COT&S date itself has lapsed.

### Umler (Universal Machine Language Equipment Register)
The AAR's database of car specifications, ownership, and service/repair history, used to look up a car's mileage-since-service and prior defect record before an inspection call.
**In use:** "Pull Umler before you write the cause — this wheelset's only fourteen months old, that's not wear-out mileage."
**Common misuse:** Classifying a defect as ordinary wear-and-tear without first checking Umler for how recently the component was serviced, which is exactly the check that separates wear from a handling event.

### Interchange
The regulated process and set of rules governing a car moving from one railroad's control to another's, including the condition standards a car must meet and the billing rules for repairs made along the way.
**In use:** "She's not interchange-legal until that brake test passes — doesn't matter whose car it is."
**Common misuse:** Assuming interchange condition standards are each railroad's own internal preference, when they're a shared set of rules every road in the interchange has agreed to and is bound by.

### Running repair
A repair made to correct a specific defect found at inspection (a wheel, shoe, or coupler replacement), billed per-item under AAR interchange rules, distinct from a scheduled heavy or programmed shop overhaul.
**In use:** "This is a running repair — one wheelset and a shoe change, not a shop visit."
**Common misuse:** Treating "running repair" as a synonym for "minor repair" rather than what it actually is: a defect-driven repair with its own specific billing-responsibility rule, regardless of dollar size.

### Rip track
The repair track at a yard where bad-ordered cars are set out for inspection and running repairs before being released back to interchange or forwarded to a heavier shop.
**In use:** "Set her out on the rip track, I want eyes on that wheel before she moves another mile."
**Common misuse:** Confusing the rip track (running-repair inspection and light repair) with a full car shop, which handles heavier structural or programmed work the rip track isn't equipped for.

### Car hire / per diem
The daily charge an owning road collects (or a handling road pays) for use of a freight car while it's off the owner's rails, separate from and unaffected by which road is billed for a specific repair.
**In use:** "Car hire keeps running while she's bad-ordered — that dwell cost is on top of whatever the repair bill says."
**Common misuse:** Assuming the repair-billing classification (owner vs. handling road) also determines who pays per diem during the dwell — the two are separate charges governed by separate rules.

### Hot box / hot journal
A bearing showing signs of overheating from lubrication loss or seal failure, historically detected by touch or smell on plain journal bearings and now primarily by wayside hot-bearing detectors on roller bearings.
**In use:** "Detector flagged a hot box three miles back — set her out now, don't wait for the next siding."
**Common misuse:** Treating a hot-bearing detector alert as a maybe-check-it-later item rather than an immediate set-out condition, given how fast a failing bearing can progress to a burn-off and derailment.

### Retainer valve
A brake system valve that holds a portion of brake cylinder pressure after a release, used to control train speed on descending grades without fully releasing every car's brakes at once.
**In use:** "Set the retainers before you start down that grade — full release on every car isn't going to hold this tonnage."
**Common misuse:** Confusing the retainer valve's function with the single-car test's release check — the retainer is a train-handling tool for grade descents, not a defect being tested for.
