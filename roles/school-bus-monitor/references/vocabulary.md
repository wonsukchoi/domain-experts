# Vocabulary

Terms generalists flatten together that school bus monitors and pupil-transportation supervisors keep sharply separate.

## Manifest

The transportation department's authoritative, dated list of which students ride which route and stop — distinct from a general school roster, since it reflects that specific day's transportation assignment including mid-year additions, removals, and temporary changes.

*In use:* "Don't count from memory — the manifest says fourteen today, not the thirteen we've been running all month."

*Common misuse:* treating the manifest as static once printed at the start of the year. It changes whenever a student's transportation assignment changes, and a monitor working from an outdated copy will miscount by design, not by error.

## Four-point tiedown

The wheelchair securement method specified under SAE J2249 and its successor standards WC19/WC18: four straps attached to designated points on the wheelchair frame, tensioned evenly, combined with a separately vehicle-anchored pelvic and shoulder belt.

*In use:* "Two points isn't a shortcut version of the tiedown — it's an unsecured wheelchair with two straps on it."

*Common misuse:* treating partial securement (front points only, or belt without straps) as proportionally safer than full securement. The chair is not meaningfully restrained until all four points and both belt anchors are in place.

## WC19 / WC18

WC19 (published 2000) is the ANSI/RESNA standard defining wheelchairs designed with four accessible tiedown attachment points and crash-tested for use as a motor-vehicle seat; WC18 (implemented December 2015) extended and updated the securement-system testing that standard depends on. They describe different halves of the same system — the chair's design and the tiedown hardware's performance.

*In use:* "Ask if the chair is WC19-compliant before you assume the four points are even there to attach to."

*Common misuse:* using "WC19" as a general safety label rather than the specific attachment-point design standard it is — a non-WC19 wheelchair may still need to be transported, but requires a different securement approach entirely.

## Individualized health care plan (IHCP)

A student-specific, medically informed protocol on file with the transportation department — thresholds and steps for a known condition (seizure disorder, severe allergy, breathing device dependency) written by the student's care team, not a generic first-aid procedure.

*In use:* "His plan says five minutes, not 'until it looks bad' — that's the number we act on."

*Common misuse:* treating an IHCP as a suggestion the monitor can override based on how an event "looks" in the moment. The plan exists specifically because subjective severity judgment is unreliable under stress; the thresholds are fixed for that reason.

## Related service

Under IDEA (34 CFR §300.34), a required support — including transportation — that a student needs to benefit from special education, established by the IEP team rather than by district discretion.

*In use:* "Monitoring his breathing device on the bus isn't a favor we're doing — it's a related service written into his IEP."

*Common misuse:* treating transportation-related supports (a monitor's presence, a specific accommodation) as goodwill or convenience rather than a legally required service tied to a specific student's IEP.

## Three-count checkpoint

The headcount discipline run at boarding, mid-route, and unloading/end-of-route — three distinct reconciliation points against the manifest, not one count assumed to hold for the whole trip.

*In use:* "We don't just count at the depot — boarding, mid-route, and end of route, every time, that's the three-count."

*Common misuse:* treating a single headcount at departure as sufficient for the whole route. Students board and exit at multiple stops; only a checkpoint at each transition catches a discrepancy before it compounds.

## Evacuation drill-card role

A written, per-route assignment naming which adult handles which evacuation task and which specific non-ambulatory student, rehearsed at the state-mandated drill cadence rather than improvised during an actual emergency.

*In use:* "The card says I've got Student A for evacuation — that's not up for improvising in the moment."

*Common misuse:* assuming evacuation roles can be worked out in real time based on who's closest to a given student. Non-ambulatory students specifically need a pre-assigned, rehearsed plan because real-time triage under emergency stress reliably deprioritizes them.

## Non-ambulatory

A student who cannot independently self-evacuate a vehicle — not limited to wheelchair users; includes students with mobility, cognitive, or medical conditions that prevent independent, rapid exit.

*In use:* "He's ambulatory day-to-day but non-ambulatory for evacuation purposes — his mobility plan says he needs physical assistance to move quickly."

*Common misuse:* equating "non-ambulatory" strictly with wheelchair use. The evacuation-planning category is broader — any condition that prevents rapid independent exit puts a student in this category regardless of how they get around under normal conditions.

## Hazard callout

A specific, location-tagged verbal alert from monitor to driver ("your two o'clock, curb side, object in the road") as distinct from a general warning.

*In use:* "Give me a callout, not a warning — where, not just 'watch out.'"

*Common misuse:* treating any verbal alert as equally useful. A driver acting on a hazard has seconds; an unspecific warning costs time the specific version doesn't.

## Positioning vest / harness

Restraint or postural-support equipment beyond a standard seatbelt, specified per student by an occupational therapist or the IHCP/IEP team — distinct from a generic child safety harness, since fit and attachment points are individualized to the student's physical needs.

*In use:* "His vest isn't optional comfort gear — it's the postural support his OT specified for the seat he's assigned."

*Common misuse:* treating positioning equipment as interchangeable between students or optional when running behind schedule. Fit and attachment points are individualized; using the wrong vest or skipping it changes the restraint's actual effectiveness.
