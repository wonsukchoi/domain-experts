# Red flags

Smell tests a transportation supervisor or safety reviewer catches early. Format for each: what it usually means, the first question to ask, and the data to pull.

## 1. Headcount discrepancy discovered after the bus is already moving between stops

**Usually means:** the headcount at the prior stop was a glance down the aisle rather than a seat-by-seat walk-through — the failure mode that lets a student who never boarded, or who left with an unauthorized adult, go unnoticed until the next checkpoint.

**First question:** "Walk me through exactly how you counted at the last stop — did you walk the aisle or count from your seat?"

**Data to pull:** the day's manifest with checkpoint timestamps; any GPS/telematics data showing dwell time at each stop versus the monitor's typical count duration.

## 2. Wheelchair tiedown completed in under 60–90 seconds

**Usually means:** the four-point sequence is being compressed or a point skipped — a full front-and-rear tiedown plus separately anchored pelvic/shoulder belt reliably takes longer than that when run completely.

**First question:** "Show me the last three wheelchair securements on video — which points get attached and in what order?"

**Data to pull:** dashcam/interior camera footage timestamped against door-open and departure events; comparison against the monitor's own average securement time on prior routes.

## 3. IHCP or behavior-plan binder missing an entry for a flagged student on the manifest

**Usually means:** a student was added to the route (new enrollment, IEP update, mid-year transfer) faster than the paperwork caught up, leaving the monitor without the specific protocol for that student's condition.

**First question:** "When was this student added to the route, and has the transportation office confirmed the plan is filed?"

**Data to pull:** IEP/504 transportation-services record; date the student was added to the manifest versus the date the IHCP was filed with the transportation department.

## 4. Radio hazard calls to the driver are consistently non-specific ("watch out," "careful")

**Usually means:** the monitor is registering hazards but not converting them into an actionable, location-tagged instruction — which gives the driver nothing to act on in the few seconds available.

**First question:** "Pull the last five radio hazard calls from this monitor — do any name a specific location or object?"

**Data to pull:** radio call logs or dashcam audio; any near-miss or incident report correlated with a vague-call event.

## 5. A student released at a stop requiring a designated adult, with no confirmation logged

**Usually means:** schedule pressure overrode the release-verification step — releasing a young or vulnerable student to whoever is present at the curb rather than confirming against the authorized-recipient list.

**First question:** "Was the required adult visually confirmed before release, or assumed present because someone was at the curb?"

**Data to pull:** release-protocol log for that stop; parent/guardian authorized-pickup list on file; any parent complaint or incident tied to that stop.

## 6. A behavior-incident pattern between the same two students with no seat-chart change on file

**Usually means:** the monitor is relying on verbal redirection alone rather than the one enforceable, documented tool — a pattern that predicts an unresolved conflict escalating past the point verbal management can contain.

**First question:** "Pull this monitor's last five behavior incident reports for these two students — how many resulted in a documented seat reassignment?"

**Data to pull:** behavior incident reports; seating-chart revision history; whether the transportation supervisor was radioed at the appropriate escalation level.

## 7. Evacuation drill-card role assignments not updated after a roster change

**Usually means:** a non-ambulatory student was added to or removed from the route and the drill card — which assigns a specific evacuation role per student — wasn't reissued to match.

**First question:** "When did the roster last change, and when was the drill card last reissued?"

**Data to pull:** roster change log; drill-card revision dates; most recent evacuation drill record for the route.

## 8. Wheelchair tiedown strap logged as "adjusted" mid-route rather than fully redone

**Usually means:** a strap loosened in transit and was re-tensioned at a single point instead of triggering the full four-point recheck — a partial fix can leave the other three points untouched and untested.

**First question:** "When the strap was adjusted, was the full four-point sequence rerun, or just that one strap?"

**Data to pull:** incident/adjustment log; dashcam footage of the mid-route stop; pre-trip inspection record for that vehicle's tiedown hardware.

## 9. Medical-event response time inconsistent with the student's IHCP threshold

**Usually means:** the monitor is responding to how the event "looks" rather than timing it against the plan's specific threshold — either escalating too early (before the plan calls for it) or too late (missing the defined action point).

**First question:** "Walk me through the timeline of the last medical event for this student — onset, pre-alert, and action-threshold times, compared to the IHCP."

**Data to pull:** incident report with timestamps; the student's IHCP; radio log for the event.
