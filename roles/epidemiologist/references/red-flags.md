# Epidemiologist — Red Flags

### Case count exceeds 2x the seasonal baseline with no known reporting-artifact explanation
- **Usually means:** A true excess worth investigating, or a change in surveillance sensitivity (new test rolled out, new reporting requirement) that only looks like a case increase.
- **First question:** Did anything change in how cases are detected or reported in this period (new lab test, new mandate, media attention driving self-report)?
- **Data to pull:** Historical case counts for the same period across the prior 5 years; any known changes to testing or reporting protocols.

### Epi curve shows multiple peaks spaced roughly one incubation period apart
- **Usually means:** Propagated (person-to-person) transmission rather than a single point-source exposure — control measures need to target transmission chains, not one contaminated source.
- **First question:** Is there evidence of secondary transmission (household contacts, healthcare exposure) between the peaks?
- **Data to pull:** Line list with onset dates and known contact relationships between early and later cases.

### Relative risk or odds ratio close to 1.0 (0.8–1.4) for the leading hypothesized exposure
- **Usually means:** The hypothesized exposure is not the cause; descriptive epidemiology likely missed the real signal or generated the wrong hypothesis.
- **First question:** What did the epi curve and person/place/time breakdown suggest that hasn't been tested yet?
- **Data to pull:** Full exposure questionnaire results across all candidate exposures, not just the leading hypothesis.

### Case-control study with fewer than 4-5 controls per case and a wide confidence interval crossing 1.0
- **Usually means:** The study is underpowered to detect a real association, not that no association exists.
- **First question:** Can additional controls be recruited, or does a case-control design need to shift to matched pairs to increase power?
- **Data to pull:** Confidence interval width and control-to-case ratio from the current analysis.

### More than 15% of case-control questionnaire non-response, differential between cases and controls
- **Usually means:** Selection bias — the responders and non-responders likely differ systematically, skewing the odds ratio in an unknown direction.
- **First question:** Is non-response higher among cases or controls, and does it correlate with the exposure being studied?
- **Data to pull:** Response rate broken out by case/control status and by exposure status where known.

### Crude odds ratio changes by more than roughly 10% after stratifying on a candidate confounder
- **Usually means:** The crude association was confounded; the stratum-specific (adjusted) estimate is the more trustworthy number.
- **First question:** Is the candidate variable biologically or behaviorally plausible as a confounder, or was it stratified on fishing expedition?
- **Data to pull:** Stratum-specific odds ratios and the Mantel-Haenszel pooled estimate.

### Surveillance system reporting lag exceeds one incubation period for the disease in question
- **Usually means:** The system is structurally too slow to support real-time outbreak control, regardless of its eventual case-capture completeness.
- **First question:** Where in the reporting chain is the delay concentrated (diagnosis to lab confirmation, lab to health department, health department to public report)?
- **Data to pull:** Time-stamped reporting-chain data for a sample of recent cases.

### A single food/water/environmental sample tests negative and the source is declared ruled out
- **Usually means:** Premature closure — a negative sample from one batch doesn't rule out a source that has since been fully consumed, discarded, or was contaminated intermittently.
- **First question:** Was the specific implicated lot/batch/time-window actually sampled, or a later, different one?
- **Data to pull:** Sample collection date/lot relative to the exposure window identified by the epi curve.

### Attack rate among the "unexposed" group is higher than expected background illness
- **Usually means:** Exposure misclassification — some people coded unexposed were actually exposed (e.g. ate a shared dish they didn't report, or cross-contamination occurred).
- **First question:** How was exposure status collected, and is there a plausible secondary exposure route not captured by the questionnaire?
- **Data to pull:** Raw questionnaire responses for the "unexposed but ill" subgroup, cross-checked against any available food-prep or contact records.
