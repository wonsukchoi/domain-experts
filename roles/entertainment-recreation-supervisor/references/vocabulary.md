# Vocabulary

### Ratio (staff-to-participant ratio)
The minimum number of certified staff required per group size or program area, set by an outside standard (ACA, a facility's aquatic safety program, a ride manufacturer, ACCT) — a floor, not a target.
**In use:** "We're at ratio in the 7-8s but not the 9-14s — pull a float before you worry about the property-wide average."
**Common misuse:** treating it as a property-wide average that can be balanced across groups, instead of a per-area minimum checked independently.

### Float staff
Cross-trained staff held in reserve or on-call specifically to restore ratio when a scheduled worker doesn't show, not additional headcount for a busy day.
**In use:** "Float A is cert'd for both age bands — send them to whichever group is short first."
**Common misuse:** using floats as routine extra labor for peak periods rather than reserving them for the contingency chain, which leaves no buffer when an actual call-out hits.

### Shadow shift
A supervised shift a new hire works before being signed off to run a post independently, with a defined minimum count per post type.
**In use:** "She's got two shadow shifts logged on waterfront — one more before she's solo."
**Common misuse:** treating shadow shifts as a formality to burn through quickly rather than a genuine competence check, especially under staffing pressure.

### Pre-shift briefing
A short, structured briefing at the start of a shift covering what changed since the last shift — not a generic recap of standing procedures.
**In use:** "Briefing's five minutes, but it has to name today's wind forecast and the two call-outs, not just repeat the standard script."
**Common misuse:** running it as a roll call or a repetition of yesterday's content instead of surfacing the day's actual risk.

### Vigilance rotation
Scheduled interval swapping on high-attention posts (lifeguard chairs, ride load stations) to counter attention decay over time on task.
**In use:** "Rotate the chairs every 20 minutes regardless of crowd size — vigilance drops whether or not the pool looks busy."
**Common misuse:** skipping or stretching the interval on a "quiet" shift, on the assumption that low apparent risk means attention hasn't decayed.

### Consolidation
Temporarily merging or closing a lower-priority program area to free certified staff for a ratio-critical one — step 2 of the contingency chain, meant to be same-day and reversible.
**In use:** "Consolidate the craft elective into the main rotation today — we need that counselor in the 9-14 group."
**Common misuse:** letting a "temporary" consolidation run for days without reporting it up as a recurring staffing gap that needs a schedule fix.

### Near-miss report
Documentation of an event that could have caused injury or harm but didn't, filed the same shift it occurs — distinct from an injury/incident report but tracked with the same rigor.
**In use:** "That's a near miss, not just a complaint — file the report before end of shift."
**Common misuse:** treating a near miss as not worth documenting because no one was actually hurt, which erases the pattern data that would catch a real incident before it happens.

### Progressive discipline
A staged response to a performance or conduct issue — coaching conversation, then written warning, then further action — applied consistently and documented at each stage.
**In use:** "This is his second coaching note for the same issue — next step is a written warning, not another verbal."
**Common misuse:** skipping straight to a severe consequence for a first offense, or conversely, letting repeat issues slide as "just another conversation" without escalating the stage.

### Service recovery
The immediate, on-the-spot response to a guest complaint intended to resolve it before it needs formal escalation.
**In use:** "That's a Tier 1 — comp the reride and move on, no report needed."
**Common misuse:** applying service recovery alone to a complaint that actually describes a safety-relevant near miss, closing it out without the incident report it needed.

### Certified operating limit
A hard threshold (wind speed, water condition, rider restriction) set by a ride manufacturer or inspection body that a supervisor enforces but has no authority to waive.
**In use:** "We're at 32 mph gusting — that's inside the 35 mph limit, but we start the 15-minute check cycle now."
**Common misuse:** treating the limit as a guideline that can flex under guest pressure or high attendance, rather than a fixed floor.

### On-call chain
The named, pre-arranged sequence of who gets called and in what order when a shift is short, checked and current before the shift opens.
**In use:** "Check the on-call chain before you open, not after the first call-out."
**Common misuse:** having an on-call list that exists on paper but isn't verified as current, so the first name called is unreachable or no longer qualified.

### Post
A specific staffed position (a lifeguard chair, a ride station, an activity group) with its own ratio, rotation, and certification requirements, distinct from a general "staff member."
**In use:** "She's certified for that post specifically — being certified on the carousel doesn't cover the coaster."
**Common misuse:** treating certification or headcount as portable across posts without checking post-specific requirements.
