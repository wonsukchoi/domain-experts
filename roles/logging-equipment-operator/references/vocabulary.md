# Vocabulary

Terms generalists flatten together that mechanized-logging practitioners keep sharply separate. Each: definition, a sentence a practitioner would actually say, and the common misuse.

## Feller-buncher vs. harvester (single-grip)

A **feller-buncher** cuts a tree and grapples/piles it whole (unprocessed) for a whole-tree system — no bucking, no delimbing. A **harvester** (single-grip head) cuts, delimbs, and bucks the stem to length at the stump, using an onboard computer to optimize log lengths against mill instructions, for a cut-to-length system.

*Practitioner usage:* "Don't call it a harvester if it isn't processing the stem — that's a feller-buncher, and the delimbing happens at the landing, not the stump."

*Common misuse:* using "harvester" as a generic word for any felling machine. The distinction determines whether the wood arrives at the landing whole (needs a delimber/slasher) or already bucked to length.

## Skidder vs. forwarder

A **skidder** drags logs (or whole trees) along the ground to the landing, using a grapple or cable. A **forwarder** carries logs fully loaded on its own bunk, with no ground contact for the wood during transport.

*Practitioner usage:* "Put the forwarder on that stream-buffer zone — we can't afford the ground disturbance a skidder's drag marks would leave there."

*Common misuse:* treating the two as interchangeable "extraction machines." The soil-disturbance difference is the reason one gets specified over the other on sensitive ground, not operator preference.

## Whole-tree vs. cut-to-length (CTL) system

**Whole-tree** systems fell and drag/pile entire trees to a landing for processing there (feller-buncher + skidder + landing delimber/slasher). **Cut-to-length** systems process each stem into logs at the stump (harvester + forwarder), never bringing unprocessed wood to the landing.

*Practitioner usage:* "Small pulpwood stems, whole-tree wins — the harvester head's cycle time is all boom repositioning on wood that small."

*Common misuse:* picking the system by crew habit rather than stem size, soil sensitivity, and sawlog:pulp ratio — the same crew can lose money on the wrong system for a given tract.

## ROPS vs. FOPS

**ROPS** (rollover protective structure) is rated to keep a survivable space intact if the machine tips. **FOPS** (falling object protective structure) is rated to protect the cab from a falling object — a separate hazard, separate structure, separate test standard (SAE J1040 / ISO 3471 covers ROPS specifically).

*Practitioner usage:* "That cab's ROPS-rated, but check the FOPS rating separately before working under standing hang-ups — they're not the same certification."

*Common misuse:* assuming a ROPS-rated cab is automatically FOPS-rated, or treating either rating as a guarantee of safety at any load orientation rather than a specific static test result.

## Hang-up (hung tree)

A felled tree that lodges against a standing tree instead of reaching the ground. In mechanized operations, it's cleared with the felling machine's grapple or a winch — never by a person on foot.

*Practitioner usage:* "We've got a hang-up on that lean — pull it with the grapple before moving to the next stem, don't leave it standing."

*Common misuse:* treating a hang-up as routine enough to clear manually with a cant hook, which reintroduces the exact struck-by exposure mechanization exists to remove.

## Two-tree-length rule

The OSHA minimum-distance requirement (29 CFR 1910.266(h)(2)(vi)) keeping people on foot at least two tree-lengths from a tree being felled during felling operations.

*Practitioner usage:* "That rule is for people on the ground — inside a ROPS cab you're not exempt from rollover risk, but you're not the two-tree-length hazard it's written for either."

*Common misuse:* assuming the rule doesn't apply to mechanized work at all, or conversely assuming cab isolation from felling hazards also means isolation from rollover hazards — it addresses one specific hazard, not the job's full risk profile.

## Winch-assist (tethered) harvesting

A ground-based machine anchored by steel cable to a stationary anchor machine or tree, extending safe operation to slopes (up to roughly 100% grade / ~45°) that would otherwise require cable/skyline yarding.

*Practitioner usage:* "Winch-assist gets us onto that pocket without a yarder, but budget the 15–25% cycle-time penalty before promising the same daily tonnage."

*Common misuse:* treating winch-assist as removing the slope limit entirely rather than extending it at a real cost — anchor rating, tether angle, and cycle time all still bound what's safe and what's profitable.

## Cable/skyline yarding vs. ground-based skidding

**Cable yarding** (highlead or skyline systems) moves logs by suspended or dragged cable from a stationary yarder, used where ground-based equipment can't operate (typically beyond winch-assist's practical slope range). **Ground-based skidding** uses a wheeled or tracked machine driving to the wood.

*Practitioner usage:* "Past 100% grade this isn't a winch-assist question anymore — that's a yarder job."

*Common misuse:* comparing cable yarding's cost-per-ton directly against ground-based systems without accounting for the terrain that made ground-based infeasible in the first place — they're not competing options on the same ground, they're sequential fallbacks.

## Side-slope vs. longitudinal slope

**Side-slope** is the grade perpendicular to the machine's direction of travel — the axis that governs lateral tip-over risk for wheeled equipment. **Longitudinal slope** is the grade along the direction of travel — governs traction, braking, and stall risk.

*Practitioner usage:* "The map says 22% average, but that's longitudinal — the side-slope in that draw is closer to 34%, and that's what'll roll a skidder."

*Common misuse:* citing a single "slope %" figure without specifying direction, which hides the actual tip-over-relevant number inside an average that includes the safer axis.

## Machine utilization vs. production rate

**Production rate** is tons (or stems) per productive machine-hour when the machine is actually cutting/hauling. **Utilization** is productive hours ÷ scheduled hours — how much of the paid shift was actually productive.

*Practitioner usage:* "Rated production rate is fine — it's utilization that's killing this job, we're at 55% because of the mid-shift breakdowns."

*Common misuse:* quoting a machine's rated production rate as if it were the job's actual throughput, ignoring downtime, weather, and bottleneck waiting that determine what's actually delivered.

## Bunching vs. decking

**Bunching** is grouping felled trees or stems streamside/in-block for extraction pickup. **Decking** is stacking processed logs at the landing for loadout.

*Practitioner usage:* "Check the bunching pattern before blaming the skidder for short turns — if the piles are scattered, no operator hits rated payload."

*Common misuse:* using the two interchangeably to mean "piled wood" in general, which obscures whether a throughput problem is happening in the block (bunching/layout) or at the landing (decking/loadout).
