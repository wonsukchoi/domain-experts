# Vocabulary

Terms generalists conflate that a patient representative keeps sharply separate. Each entry: definition, how a practitioner actually uses it, and the common misuse.

### Complaint vs. grievance

A **complaint** is an issue resolved by staff present at the time, using a process already in place — no written-response clock starts. A **grievance** is anything requiring investigation beyond the encounter, or touching quality of care, abuse, or neglect — CMS requires a dated written response.

**In use:** "The charge nurse handled it on the floor in ten minutes — that's a complaint. The billing dispute needs finance to investigate, so that one's a grievance and the clock started today."

**Common misuse:** treating the distinction as a politeness scale (grievance = "the patient was really mad") rather than the regulatory test it actually is — how the issue was resolved, not how upset the patient sounded.

### Good Faith Estimate vs. balance bill

A **Good Faith Estimate (GFE)** is the pre-service cost estimate required for self-pay/uninsured patients under the No Surprises Act. A **balance bill** is the separate, distinct problem of an insured patient being billed the difference between an out-of-network provider's charge and what their insurer paid — governed by different protections entirely.

**In use:** "This patient's uninsured, so we're looking at a GFE variance and a possible PPDR filing — not a balance-billing case, that's only for insured patients hitting an out-of-network provider."

**Common misuse:** routing every large, unexpected bill through the same process regardless of insurance status — the GFE/PPDR mechanism and balance-billing protections have different eligibility rules and different remedies.

### Service recovery vs. customer service

**Service recovery** is a structured response to a specific failure that already happened — acknowledge, apologize for the organization's part, act, follow up. **Customer service** is the ongoing baseline of how staff treat people; it's what's supposed to prevent the need for recovery in the first place.

**In use:** "Good customer service would have caught this before it became a complaint — now we're in service recovery, and the sequence matters more than the smile."

**Common misuse:** applying customer-service politeness (smiling, saying "no problem") to a service-recovery situation without the acknowledge-apologize-act sequence — patients read pleasantness without ownership as not being taken seriously.

### HIPAA authorization vs. facility directory

**Authorization** is the patient's specific, documented consent for a named person or entity to receive specific information. The **facility directory** is a lighter-weight, opt-out-by-default listing (name, general condition, location) that a caller can access simply by asking for the patient by name, unless the patient opted out.

**In use:** "She's not in the facility directory — she opted out at admission — so I can't even confirm she's a patient here, let alone give a condition update."

**Common misuse:** assuming being listed in the facility directory means a caller can get clinical details — the directory covers only the coarse "confirm presence and general condition" tier, not treatment specifics.

### Qualified interpreter vs. ad hoc interpreter

A **qualified interpreter** is a trained, competency-assessed professional (in person, phone, or video) bound by confidentiality and accuracy standards. An **ad hoc interpreter** is anyone else pressed into service — a family member, a bystander, a bilingual staff member from an unrelated department.

**In use:** "This is an informed-consent conversation, so we're using the qualified interpreter line — not her daughter, no matter how willing she is to help."

**Common misuse:** using a bilingual family member for convenience on anything with legal or consent weight, on the assumption that fluency alone substitutes for the accuracy and neutrality a qualified interpreter is trained and accountable for.

### MOON / observation status vs. inpatient admission

**Observation status** is an outpatient billing classification for patients who need monitoring but don't yet meet inpatient criteria — it affects Medicare cost-sharing and post-acute benefits differently than inpatient status. The **MOON** (Medicare Outpatient Observation Notice) is the required notice, delivered within 36 hours, telling the patient they're in observation status and what that means financially.

**In use:** "She's been in observation for thirty hours — the MOON should already be signed, or we're out of compliance."

**Common misuse:** assuming "observation" is just informal language for "not admitted yet" rather than a distinct billing status with its own notice requirement and financial consequences the patient needs explained, not just delivered as a form to sign.

### Advance directive vs. POLST

An **advance directive** is a patient's own legal document (living will, healthcare proxy) stating future wishes, executed while competent, usable in any future setting. A **POLST** (Physician Orders for Life-Sustaining Treatment) is a signed medical order, based on a conversation with a clinician, meant to be actionable immediately by EMS and across care settings.

**In use:** "She has a living will naming her son as proxy, but no POLST — if EMS is called today, they need the POLST, not the advance directive, to know what to actually do."

**Common misuse:** treating the two as interchangeable or assuming having one means the other exists — an advance directive without a POLST leaves emergency responders without an actionable order.

### Root-cause rollup vs. case closure

**Case closure** is resolving and documenting one patient's specific complaint. A **root-cause rollup** is recognizing that multiple closed cases share an underlying process failure and escalating that pattern, separately from any individual case, to the department that owns the process.

**In use:** "The case is closed for this patient, but this is the third discharge-instruction complaint from that unit this month — that's going to service-line leadership as its own item."

**Common misuse:** treating every closed case as the end of the job — closure resolves the patient's individual issue, not the process that created it.

### Delegated authority (service-recovery fund)

The **dollar ceiling** a patient representative is authorized to approve on the spot (commonly $100–$250 per case) without additional sign-off, distinct from what finance or a manager can approve above that line.

**In use:** "A parking voucher's inside my authority — I can approve that right now. A bill adjustment isn't, so that goes to finance."

**Common misuse:** offering or implying a specific dollar amount or outcome that exceeds the rep's actual authority, which turns a good-faith gesture into a second broken promise when it isn't honored.
