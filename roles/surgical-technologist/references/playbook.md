# Surgical Technology Playbook

Operational defaults with concrete steps and thresholds. Facility policy and the supervising surgeon's instructions govern in any conflict — these are the field-level defaults a CST executes absent a specific override.

## 1. Back table and mayo stand setup

Zones, not piles. A back table set up by zone means the surgeon or first assistant's hand finds an instrument without the scrub redirecting them verbally.

- **Zone 1 (nearest the field):** the instruments used most often for the current step — e.g., in an open abdominal case, retractors and clamps currently in play.
- **Zone 2:** instruments queued for the next step, in the sequence they'll be needed (per the preference card's procedure sequence, not alphabetically or by type).
- **Zone 3 (back of table):** rarely used or specialty instruments, sterile but out of the immediate rotation.
- **Mayo stand:** only what's needed in the surgeon's hand in the next 60–90 seconds — knife, current suture, current clamp. Nothing "just in case" — the mayo stand is the fastest-changing surface in the room and the easiest place to lose count of an item.

**Sequence discipline:** an instrument returns to the same tray position every time it's set down. If it doesn't, the next reach for it becomes a search, and a search under time pressure is where items get missed at count time.

## 2. Preference card execution

The preference card is the default open list — not a suggestion.

1. Pull the card for this surgeon and this procedure (not a generic procedure card — the same operation differs by surgeon in instrument choice, suture, and sequence).
2. Cross-check the card against the actual patient/procedure (laterality, approach — open vs. laparoscopic — and any noted implant).
3. Open only what's on the card plus what the circulator and surgeon confirm for this specific case (e.g., an unplanned drain).
4. If a case needs something not on the card, open it, use it, and flag it to the circulator for the card's post-case update — the fix belongs on the card for next time, not as a standing "open extra" habit.

**Rule of thumb:** every item opened beyond the card is either wasted supply cost or added count-and-search burden if unused — there's no free option to "have it ready."

## 3. Count checkpoints and sequence

Counts happen at fixed points, not just "at the end":

1. **Baseline** — before the procedure starts, scrub and circulator count together and out loud: sponges (by type: raytec, lap/abdominal, kittner, etc.), sharps (needles, blades, electrosurgical tips), instruments (against the tray's count sheet, piece by piece — a "ratchet + jaw + screw" instrument counts as one unit only if the tray sheet defines it that way).
2. **Additions mid-case** — any sponge, sharp, or instrument added to the field after baseline is counted into the running total immediately, not batched for later.
3. **Before closing a body cavity** (e.g., peritoneum, pleura) — full recount.
4. **Before fascial closure** — full recount.
5. **Before skin closure** — full recount.
6. **At permanent relief of the scrub or circulator, if it happens mid-case** — full recount at the handoff, both outgoing and incoming staff present.

**Discrepancy resolution, in order — do not skip a step to save time:**

1. Recount independently (scrub counts, circulator counts, separately — not a shared recitation) to rule out a tally error.
2. Search the field, methodically by quadrant.
3. Search the floor within a 6-foot radius of the field, under table drapes and equipment.
4. Search linen, trash, and kick buckets.
5. If unresolved and the item is radiopaque (standard for lap/abdominal sponges): intraoperative X-ray before final closure of the relevant layer. This step is not optional or "if time allows" — it's the guideline-mandated resolution when physical search fails.
6. Document every step taken, times, and the resolution — see the SKILL.md worked example for the exact record format.

## 4. Sharps handling — neutral zone

- Establish a neutral zone (a designated mat, basin, or tray) at the start of the case; state it out loud so the surgeon and assistant know where it is.
- Every sharp (scalpel, suture needle, electrosurgical tip when hot) is placed in the neutral zone by the person finished with it and retrieved from the neutral zone by the next person — never passed hand-to-hand.
- Announce "sharp in the zone" and "sharp out" so the exchange is heard, not just seen, especially with the surgeon's eyes on the field.

## 5. Immediate-use steam sterilization (IUSS) decision gate

IUSS runs at 270–275°F, as briefly as 3–4 minutes unwrapped, specifically for **immediate single-instrument reuse** — not as a standing solution to inventory shortfalls.

Use IUSS when:
- A single instrument is dropped or contaminated mid-case and is needed again within minutes, and a replacement sterile set isn't otherwise available in time.
- The instrument's manufacturer instructions for use (IFU) validate IUSS for that specific instrument (not all do — check before defaulting to it).

Do not use IUSS when:
- The actual problem is "we don't have enough of this instrument" for a scheduled case — that's an inventory/scheduling gap, not an emergency, and IUSS trades sterility assurance margin (no full cool-down, frequently no packaging, biological indicator read after the fact rather than before use) to paper over it.
- An implant is involved — implants generally require a biological indicator with a rapid-readout confirmation before implantation, not the IUSS shortcut.

## 6. Specimen handling at the field

1. The moment a specimen is off the patient, it is named out loud by the surgeon and confirmed by the scrub and circulator — site, laterality, and specimen type, using the patient's actual identifiers, not "the gallbladder" as if there's only one patient in the building.
2. The specimen is placed in an appropriate container immediately — not set down on the back table "for a second."
3. The circulator labels the container at that moment, reading the label back to the field for confirmation — two-identifier check (patient name/MRN + specimen description) before anything else happens in the case.
4. Chain of custody is logged: who collected it, who labeled it, time off the field, time to pathology.

Mislabeling errors cluster at this exact handoff — treating it as "we'll label it when we have a second" is the single highest-yield fix available to reduce specimen errors.
