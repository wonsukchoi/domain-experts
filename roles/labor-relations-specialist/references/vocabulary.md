### Collective bargaining agreement (CBA)
The negotiated contract between an employer and a union governing wages, hours, discipline, grievance procedure, and other terms of employment for the bargaining unit.
**In use:** "Article 9.4 of the CBA sets the attendance point termination threshold at 10."
**Common misuse:** Relying on a general understanding of "what the contract probably says" instead of citing the specific article and language.

### Grievance
A formal complaint filed under the CBA's grievance procedure alleging a violation of the contract, typically processed through defined steps with contractual response deadlines.
**In use:** "The grievance was filed at Step 2 after the supervisor's response didn't resolve it."
**Common misuse:** Treating grievance-step deadlines as flexible administrative timing rather than binding contractual deadlines with real consequences for missing them.

### Just cause
The standard requiring an employer to have adequate justification for disciplining an employee, typically evaluated against a set of factors: notice, reasonable rule, fair investigation, proof, equal treatment, and proportionality.
**In use:** "The termination fails just cause here because the equal-treatment factor wasn't met."
**Common misuse:** Treating just cause as a single overall judgment ("was this deserved?") rather than a multi-factor test where failing any one factor can overturn the discipline.

### Past practice
An unwritten, consistently applied workplace practice that, when clear, long-standing, and mutually accepted, can function as a binding term alongside the written CBA.
**In use:** "Even though it's not in the contract, this scheduling practice has been followed for eight years — that's binding past practice."
**Common misuse:** Assuming that anything not explicitly written in the CBA can be changed unilaterally by management.

### Weingarten rights
An employee's right, under the National Labor Relations Act, to request union representation during an investigatory interview the employee reasonably believes could lead to discipline.
**In use:** "She asked for a union rep before answering further questions — that's a Weingarten request, and we have to pause the interview."
**Common misuse:** Assuming management decides whether representation is warranted, rather than the right being triggered by the employee's own request.

### Progressive discipline
A disciplinary approach using escalating steps (verbal warning, written warning, suspension, termination) rather than jumping directly to the most severe penalty for a first offense.
**In use:** "Absent a serious safety violation, this CBA expects progressive discipline before termination."
**Common misuse:** Skipping progressive steps for an offense that doesn't meet the CBA's or past practice's threshold for immediate termination.

### Disparate treatment
Treating similarly situated employees differently for comparable conduct, without a legitimate distinguishing factor — a common and often successful basis for overturning discipline.
**In use:** "Two employees committed the same policy violation, but only one was terminated — that's a disparate treatment problem."
**Common misuse:** Assuming discipline decisions are evaluated only against the individual case's facts, without checking how comparable cases were previously handled.

### Arbitration
The final, binding step of most CBA grievance procedures, in which a neutral third party (arbitrator) hears the case and issues a decision interpreting the contract.
**In use:** "This grievance is heading to arbitration since it wasn't resolved at Step 3."
**Common misuse:** Preparing an arbitration case around general fairness arguments rather than the specific contract language and precedent an arbitrator will actually weigh.

### Back pay
The wages an employee would have earned during a period of wrongful termination or suspension, typically offset by any interim earnings during that period, awarded as a grievance remedy.
**In use:** "Back pay is calculated from the termination date, less what she earned in temporary work during the layoff."
**Common misuse:** Calculating back pay as the full wage amount without subtracting documented interim earnings, which most remedies require offsetting.

### Duty of fair representation
The union's legal obligation to represent all bargaining-unit members fairly, without discrimination or bad faith, in processing grievances and contract administration.
**In use:** "If the union refuses to process her grievance without any legitimate reason, that could raise a duty of fair representation issue on their side."
**Common misuse:** Assuming this duty applies to the employer rather than the union — it governs how the union treats the employees it represents.

### Unfair labor practice (ULP)
A violation of the National Labor Relations Act by either an employer or a union, such as retaliating against protected concerted activity or refusing to bargain in good faith.
**In use:** "Disciplining her right after she raised a group complaint about pay could be seen as retaliation for protected activity — a potential ULP."
**Common misuse:** Assuming ULP charges only apply to union conduct; employers are equally subject to ULP claims for actions like retaliation or unilateral changes to mandatory bargaining subjects.
