# Vocabulary

Terms generalists conflate that a crew lead keeps sharply separate — because the distinction changes what's legal, what's safe, or what the client is actually agreeing to.

## Reduction cut vs. heading cut vs. topping

**Reduction cut** removes a limb back to a lateral at least one-third the diameter of the wood being removed, so the remaining branch can take over the terminal role — the ANSI A300-compliant way to shorten a limb. **Heading cut** removes a limb or leader at an arbitrary point with no lateral to take over, leaving a stub that responds with weakly attached epicormic sprouts. **Topping** is heading cuts applied across the whole crown — the practice ANSI A300 prohibits outright, regardless of the percentage of crown removed.

*Practitioner usage:* "We're not topping this — we're reducing to that lateral there, it's a third the diameter of what's coming off."

*Common misuse:* clients (and some contractors) use "trim back" and "cut back" to mean any of the three interchangeably, when only one of them is a defensible pruning technique.

## Live crown ratio (LCR)

The percentage of a tree's total height occupied by live crown (crown length ÷ total height). It's a proxy for how much of the tree's photosynthetic capacity remains after a cut, not a cosmetic measurement.

*Practitioner usage:* "We're already at 40% live crown ratio — taking that limb drops us under a third, so it waits for next season unless it's rated as a hazard."

*Common misuse:* estimating LCR by eye from the ground rather than measuring crown base height and total height; a crown that "looks full" can still be under the one-third floor on a tree with a tall, bare lower trunk.

## Codominant stems vs. included bark

**Codominant stems** are two or more leaders of similar diameter arising from close to the same point — a structural pattern, not automatically a defect. **Included bark** is bark trapped inside a narrow union because the two stems grew into each other rather than forming continuous interlocking wood — this is the actual defect, and it typically co-occurs with codominant stems but isn't synonymous with them.

*Practitioner usage:* "It's codominant, sure, but the angle's wide and there's no included bark — that union's fine. It's the narrow one at 25° with the bark seam that's the problem."

*Common misuse:* treating "codominant" itself as the hazard and removing every codominant fork on sight, which takes out plenty of structurally sound unions along with the genuinely weak ones.

## Minimum approach distance (MAD) vs. non-qualified clearance

**MAD** is the distance a line-clearance-qualified worker may approach an energized conductor, set by voltage class per ANSI Z133/OSHA tables. **Non-qualified clearance** is the flat 10-ft floor that applies to anyone on a crew without that specific qualification, regardless of voltage class or the worker's general experience.

*Practitioner usage:* "MAD for this voltage class is 2 foot 2 for a qualified climber — but nobody on today's crew carries that ticket, so we're at the 10-foot floor and this job gets referred out."

*Common misuse:* assuming a senior climber's general skill covers the electrical qualification — MAD only applies to workers who hold the specific line-clearance credential; everyone else defaults to the 10-ft floor no matter their tenure.

## Qualified line-clearance arborist vs. ISA Certified Arborist

**ISA Certified Arborist** is a general professional credential covering tree biology, pruning, and risk assessment. **Qualified line-clearance arborist** (or line-clearance tree trimmer) is a distinct OSHA/ANSI Z133-recognized qualification specifically for work within reduced clearance of energized conductors — a worker can hold one without the other.

*Practitioner usage:* "He's ISA Certified, but he's not line-clearance qualified — he can plan the pruning, but he can't be the one making the cut inside ten feet of that wire."

*Common misuse:* treating "certified arborist" as a blanket qualification that covers electrical-proximity work; clients and even some contractors assume any credential means "cleared for anything."

## Working load limit (WLL) vs. breaking strength vs. design factor

**Breaking strength** is the load at which rigging hardware or rope actually fails in testing. **Working load limit (WLL)** is the manufacturer-rated safe operating load, set well below breaking strength. **Design factor** is the ratio between them (breaking strength ÷ WLL) — arborist rigging commonly carries factors well above general industrial rigging because of the shock-loading inherent to tree work.

*Practitioner usage:* "Breaking strength on that sling is 5,000 lb, WLL is rated around 1,000 — that's roughly a 5:1 design factor, and our estimated dynamic load on this piece is still under 500, so we're fine."

*Common misuse:* comparing a piece's static weight against breaking strength instead of WLL, which erases the safety margin the design factor exists to provide.

## Shock load / dynamic loading

The load a rigging system experiences when a moving mass is arrested, which can be several times the object's static (resting) weight depending on how far it fell before the line took tension.

*Practitioner usage:* "Static weight's only 175 pounds, but with a two-foot drop before the line catches it, we're planning for something closer to 400."

*Common misuse:* rigging a piece based on its static weight alone and treating any drop distance as negligible — "it's a short drop" is exactly the assumption that produces failures on paper-safe static numbers.

## Drop zone vs. exclusion zone

**Drop zone** is the area sized to contain a piece if it lands as planned, sized off piece length. **Exclusion zone** is the broader area kept clear of people and property for the duration of the cut, which has to account for the piece landing off-plan, not just on-plan.

*Practitioner usage:* "Drop zone's the 40-foot radius under the rigging point; exclusion zone is the whole driveway and both neighboring yards until this piece is down and checked."

*Common misuse:* treating the two as the same radius, which under-clears the area for anything that doesn't land exactly where intended — bark tear-out, hinge failure, or wind gust.

## TRAQ risk rating vs. hazard

A **hazard** is any structural defect capable of failing. A **TRAQ risk rating** combines that failure likelihood with the likelihood of hitting a target and the consequence severity into a single actionable category (e.g., Low through Extreme) — the same hazard can carry a different risk rating depending entirely on what's underneath it.

*Practitioner usage:* "It's a genuine hazard — but it's rated Moderate, not High, because nothing sits under that side of the canopy. The identical defect over the porch would be High."

*Common misuse:* using "hazardous" and "high risk" interchangeably, which collapses the target/consequence half of the assessment and either over-escalates low-consequence defects or under-escalates high-consequence ones.
