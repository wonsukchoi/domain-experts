# Red flags

Smell tests a crew lead catches on the pre-job walk-around, before a saw starts. Format for each: what it usually means, the first question to ask, and the data to pull before anyone makes a cut.

### Co-dominant union with attachment angle under ~30° and stem-diameter ratio above ~0.5

**Usually means:** included bark — the union never formed interlocking wood grain and is mechanically weaker than it looks from the ground, especially under wind or ice load.

**First question:** "Has anyone put a hand on this union and checked for a seam or bark ridge separation, not just looked at it from thirty feet away?"

**Data to pull:** measured attachment angle, diameter of each stem at the union, any visible bark ridge separation or included bark line, prior wind/ice event history for the site.

### Live crown ratio below one-third of total tree height

**Usually means:** either the tree is already in physiological decline (self-thinning, storm damage, root loss) or a prior pruning removed more than the tree could recover from — either way, further crown removal this cycle risks pushing it into stress-driven epicormic sprouting.

**First question:** "What did the crown look like two seasons ago — was this ratio always this low, or did a prior cut get it here?"

**Data to pull:** current crown base height and total tree height; prior work orders/pruning records for this tree if available; visible epicormic sprout clusters (a tell that a prior cut already crossed the line).

### Client or dispatcher requests "topping" or "cutting it back" without a target lateral specified

**Usually means:** the request is describing an outcome (shorter tree) without understanding that a heading cut into random wood is the mechanism most likely to cause the decay and weak-sprout regrowth the client will be unhappy about in three years.

**First question:** "Is there a specific lateral we're reducing to, or are we just told to cut it shorter?"

**Data to pull:** target lateral diameter versus the diameter of wood being removed (needs to be at least one-third); whether the tree has been topped before (look for clusters of same-age epicormic shoots at old cut sites).

### Work envelope or drop path comes within 10 feet of a visible conductor

**Usually means:** this job is at or past the non-qualified worker's clearance floor regardless of what the crew's saw skills look like — someone needs to check the certification roster before the pole climbers go up, not after.

**First question:** "Is anyone on this crew line-clearance qualified for this voltage class, and has anyone actually measured the clearance rather than eyeballed it?"

**Data to pull:** measured horizontal/vertical clearance from canopy to conductor; visible voltage class markers on the pole or utility records; crew certification roster.

### Conks (fruiting bodies), cavities, or seams at the base or trunk

**Usually means:** internal decay whose extent isn't visible from the surface — the presence of a conk means active fungal colonization, not "some rot somewhere."

**First question:** "How much of the cross-section at that point do we think is sound wood, and has anyone bored or resistograph-tested it?"

**Data to pull:** location and species of any fruiting bodies (different fungi indicate different decay patterns/severity); resistograph or increment-borer reading if available; trunk circumference at the defect for percent-of-cross-section estimate.

### New soil heave, mounding, or hairline cracking at the base after wind or heavy rain

**Usually means:** a root plate beginning to fail — this is a whole-tree failure precursor, not a canopy-level defect, and changes the entire risk conversation from "which limb" to "should this tree come down at all."

**First question:** "When did anyone last look at the base of this tree, and is the heave new or has it always been there?"

**Data to pull:** photos of current soil condition at the base; lean angle measurement if any exists; recent weather events (wind speed, rainfall) preceding the observation.

### Epicormic/water-sprout clusters following prior pruning

**Usually means:** the prior pruning cycle removed more than roughly a quarter to a third of live crown in one pass, and the tree's stress response is exactly the weakly-attached regrowth that becomes next cycle's hazard.

**First question:** "Do we have a record of what was removed last time, or are we estimating from what's growing back now?"

**Data to pull:** density and attachment quality of sprout clusters; any available prior work order noting percentage of crown removed; species-specific sprouting tendency (some species sprout heavily even from moderate cuts).

### Rigging piece estimated within ~20% of hardware's rated working load limit

**Usually means:** the static-weight estimate alone is not accounting for shock loading from the planned drop distance — a piece that "should be fine" at rest can exceed rated capacity the moment the line takes tension.

**First question:** "What's our estimated drop distance before the line catches this piece, and did we apply a shock multiplier or just the static weight?"

**Data to pull:** estimated piece weight and dimensions; planned drop distance before load transfer; hardware's rated working load limit and design factor.

### Utility's vegetation-management cycle on this circuit is overdue

**Usually means:** growth has had extra seasons to close the clearance gap since the last cycle, so a "routine" trim request may already be at or inside the non-qualified clearance floor by the time the crew arrives.

**First question:** "When was this circuit last cycled, and does that match what we're seeing in the canopy versus the wire?"

**Data to pull:** utility's last-trim date for this circuit/span if obtainable; current measured clearance versus the clearance recorded at last cycle.

### Client or scheduler is pushing to skip the risk assessment ahead of an incoming storm

**Usually means:** time pressure is real, but the assessment is exactly what determines whether the crew can safely execute a rushed removal at all — skipping it under storm pressure is how a crew ends up improvising rigging decisions mid-cut instead of before it.

**First question:** "What's the fastest version of the assessment we can do without skipping the union check and the clearance measurement?"

**Data to pull:** time until forecast weather event; whether any part of the job touches a conductor (this alone determines whether "fast" is even an option); crew's current certification coverage for the job as scoped.
