# Red flags

Smell tests worth catching before the next session runs, not at the end-of-course debrief.

## 1. Enrollment clears the studio's stated minimum but not your own break-even
**Usually means:** the studio's minimum-to-run policy protects its own fixed costs (space, kiln, accompanist), not the instructor's target wage — the two numbers are unrelated unless someone has actually run both.
**First question:** "At this headcount, what's my net per hour once materials and the studio's cut come out — not what's the gross?"
**Data to pull:** the enrollment sensitivity table (see `references/playbook.md` §1) recomputed at the actual signup count, not the count assumed when the price was set.

## 2. Rebooking rate for the next session is materially lower than end-of-course survey satisfaction
**Usually means:** verbal enthusiasm at the last session is a politeness signal, not a retention signal — people who genuinely intend to return say so with a deposit, not a compliment.
**First question:** "Of the people who said they loved it, how many have actually paid for the next level as of today?"
**Data to pull:** registration-open-day signup count for the next section against the prior section's roster; cross-reference which specific students didn't rebook and whether they share a session-1 placement tier.

## 3. No-show rate above ~20% on a drop-in-priced class
**Usually means:** drop-in pricing removes the cost of not showing up, so the roster is a soft commitment — this is a pricing-structure problem, not a scheduling or reminder-email problem.
**First question:** "What's the no-show rate on our punch-card/prepaid students versus our drop-in students in the same section?"
**Data to pull:** attendance by payment type over the trailing month; if prepaid no-show rate is meaningfully lower, the fix is the pricing switch in `references/playbook.md` §5, not more reminder texts.

## 4. A student explicitly asks to skip the fundamentals ("I've done this before")
**Usually means:** either a genuinely experienced student correctly self-assessing, or a student overestimating a related-but-different skill (e.g., hand-building experience mistaken for wheel-throwing readiness) — indistinguishable from the request alone.
**First question:** "Can you show me [the specific skill] right now, before we decide where you start?"
**Data to pull:** nothing beyond the two-minute placement task — resolve this in the room, not by negotiation.

## 5. A safety incident happens in a medium with no licensing requirement
**Usually means:** the instructor treated "no external body requires a safety protocol here" as "no safety protocol needed," which is the single most common failure mode in unlicensed self-enrichment domains.
**First question:** "What was the safety walkthrough for this specific piece of equipment or hold, and when did it happen relative to first contact with it?"
**Data to pull:** the medium-specific safety checklist (kiln loading, knife handling, partnered dance holds, joint-loading movements) and whether it was run before or after the incident, plus waiver-capture timestamp relative to when the activity began.

## 6. A section has been run at a loss (relative to target wage) for more than one consecutive term
**Usually means:** optimism bias ("it'll fill up next time") substituting for recomputing break-even, or a genuine strategic loss-leader that was never labeled as one and is quietly becoming the default.
**First question:** "Was this section priced as a deliberate loss-leader, and if so, what's it supposed to be buying us — or did we just not redo the math?"
**Data to pull:** the break-even worksheet from the term this section was originally priced, compared against the last two terms' actual enrollment and effective hourly wage.

## 7. Credential is being used to answer "can this person teach," not "can this person safely run the room"
**Usually means:** a credential like RYT-200, ACE-CPT, or ServSafe is a liability/insurance floor and a marketing signal, not a validated measure of instructional skill — hiring or marketing decisions built on the credential alone will occasionally produce a safe but poor teacher.
**First question:** "Has anyone actually observed this person teach a session, or are we relying entirely on the certificate?"
**Data to pull:** any available observed-session feedback, trial-class rebooking rate if one was run, and what the credential specifically attests to versus what's being assumed from it.

## 8. Session one is longer on theory/rules than on a hands-on result
**Usually means:** the instructor is over-indexing on "proving the course is rigorous" at the direct cost of the early win that drives rebooking — common among instructors newer to teaching adults versus their original craft.
**First question:** "What did every student in the room actually make, cook, play, or complete by the end of session one?"
**Data to pull:** session-1 lesson plan's time allocation between demonstration/lecture and hands-on student output; compare against the placement-and-win template's 10-minute placement + guaranteed-result structure.

## 9. Waiver/liability paperwork has expanded every term since the last incident, with no corresponding retention check
**Usually means:** overcorrection — each incident adds another disclaimer or restriction without anyone checking whether the class still feels like the hobby activity people signed up for, versus a compliance exercise.
**First question:** "What did we add after the last incident, and has rebooking moved since then, in either direction?"
**Data to pull:** waiver/paperwork length and content across the last 3–4 terms, layered against rebooking rate for the same window.
