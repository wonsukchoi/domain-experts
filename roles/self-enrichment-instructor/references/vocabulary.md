# Vocabulary

Terms generalists flatten together that practitioners keep separate — the value is in the misuse column.

## Break-even enrollment vs. studio minimum-to-run
**Break-even enrollment** is the headcount at which the instructor's own net pay hits their target hourly wage, after the studio's revenue-share cut and materials cost. **Studio minimum-to-run** is the headcount at which the studio's fixed costs (space, kiln, utilities) are covered — a separate, usually lower, number.
**In use:** "We hit the studio's minimum of 4, but my break-even is 7 — those aren't the same threshold and nobody should treat them as one."
**Common misuse:** accepting "we hit the minimum" from a studio manager as confirmation the section is worth running, without recomputing the instructor's own number.

## Drop-in vs. punch-card vs. block pricing
**Drop-in** is pay-per-session with no commitment. **Punch-card** is a prepaid bundle of sessions redeemable flexibly (e.g., 10 classes over 4 months). **Block/series pricing** is a fixed multi-session course paid up front with a set schedule, no redemption flexibility.
**In use:** "No-shows are killing us on drop-in — move this slot to punch-card so the revenue is captured whether or not they show."
**Common misuse:** treating punch-card and block pricing as interchangeable "prepaid" options — punch-card still tolerates a high no-show rate per visit since the revenue is banked regardless; block pricing additionally fixes the schedule, which is the lever that actually reduces no-shows by creating a standing commitment.

## Placement task vs. registration self-report
A **placement task** is a brief demonstrated skill check run at session one. A **registration self-report** is the level a student picked when signing up ("beginner," "intermediate"), unverified.
**In use:** "Don't group the room off the registration form — half the 'intermediates' haven't touched a wheel in three years."
**Common misuse:** building session-1 groupings entirely from registration categories, then being surprised when the "beginner" group contains someone who's taken the course twice before.

## Rebooking rate vs. course satisfaction
**Rebooking rate** is the percentage of a roster that pays for the next session/level when registration opens. **Course satisfaction** is self-reported sentiment, usually collected verbally or via survey at the final session.
**In use:** "Satisfaction scores were high, but rebooking was 30% — something in the experience didn't survive the gap between sessions."
**Common misuse:** treating a warm final-session mood as evidence the course is working, when it's the registration-day number that reflects actual behavior.

## Contact hours vs. total hours committed
**Contact hours** are time spent in front of students. **Total hours committed** adds prep, materials setup, grading-equivalent tasks (glaze loading, sheet music prep), and cleanup — the number the instructor's real hourly wage should be computed against.
**In use:** "It's billed as 12 contact hours, but with kiln loading and glazing it's 15 hours of my time — price and pacing both need to account for that."
**Common misuse:** computing effective hourly wage off contact hours alone, which overstates real pay by ignoring the unpaid or flat-fee prep time around it.

## Revenue-share vs. flat per-session rate
**Revenue-share** pays the instructor a percentage of gross tuition (aligns pay with enrollment, transfers enrollment risk to the instructor). **Flat per-session rate** pays a fixed fee regardless of headcount (transfers enrollment risk to the studio).
**In use:** "At 70/30 revenue-share I need 7 students to hit my target wage; at a flat $180/session I hit it at any enrollment above 2 — which structure do we actually want here?"
**Common misuse:** comparing the two structures by "which pays more" at a single hypothetical enrollment, instead of by who bears the risk when enrollment is uncertain.

## Scope of practice (self-enrichment context)
The boundary between instruction in the craft/skill and advice that requires a different credential — nutrition guidance beyond "here's what's in the recipe," therapeutic exercise modification for an injury, financial advice beyond "here's how a budget spreadsheet works."
**In use:** "I can show you a modified stretch, but 'is this safe for your specific knee injury' is a physical therapist question, not mine."
**Common misuse:** assuming that because the class itself is uncredentialed and low-stakes, there's no scope boundary at all — the class can be entirely non-licensed and still have a real line past which a referral, not an opinion, is the right answer.

## Small-batch / small-cohort surcharge
A published price adjustment applied when enrollment falls below a stated threshold, framed as paying for more individual attention rather than as a shortfall penalty.
**In use:** "Under 6 enrolled, the small-batch surcharge kicks in automatically — it's in the registration terms, not a decision we make section by section."
**Common misuse:** improvising a price increase on already-registered students without it having been disclosed at signup, which reads as a bait-and-switch even when the underlying economics are sound.

## Loss-leader section
A section deliberately run below the instructor's target wage (or at a studio-subsidized rate) for a stated strategic reason — building a pipeline to a paid intermediate level, retaining a valued studio relationship, seasonal marketing.
**In use:** "This intro section is a labeled loss-leader feeding our intermediate class — as long as intermediate conversion stays above 40%, the math still works."
**Common misuse:** running an under-enrolled section at a loss repeatedly without ever labeling it a loss-leader or checking the conversion number that would justify it — at that point it's just an unexamined loss.
