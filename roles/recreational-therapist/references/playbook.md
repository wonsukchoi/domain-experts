# Recreational Therapy Playbook

Filled operational templates. Defaults, not laws — deviate consciously and say why in the note.

## 1. Assessment-selection matrix

| Referral reason | Instrument | What it actually tells you | Re-assess interval |
|---|---|---|---|
| Post-stroke/ortho, functional carryover into leisure | Leisure Diagnostic Battery (LDB), short form | Perceived freedom in leisure (PFL) — whether the client believes they can still choose and enjoy activity, not just whether they physically can | Every 2 weeks or at discharge, whichever first |
| Dementia, behavioral-health unit — agitation/disengagement | Cohen-Mansfield Agitation Inventory (CMAI) | Frequency of 29 agitated behaviors over the prior 2 weeks, rated 1 (never) to 7 (several times/hour) | Every 2 weeks; also same-day pre/post a single new activity trial |
| General functional severity (read, not administered by RT) | FIM (Functional Independence Measure), from PT/OT | 18 items, 1 (total assist) to 7 (complete independence) — RT uses this to set goal difficulty, not to duplicate the score | At PT/OT's admission/discharge cadence |
| No published norms for this population/setting | Facility in-house checklist | Directional only — label every result "locally normed, not diagnostic" in the chart | Facility-defined |

**Rule:** pick the validated instrument that matches the referral reason first. Reach for an in-house checklist only when no validated tool has norms for the population — then the note must say so explicitly, because an unlabeled in-house score reads as validated to anyone auditing the chart later.

## 2. Goal-writing template (APIE, stage 2)

Bad (activity description, not a goal): "Client will attend and enjoy weekly bingo group."

Good (functional, measurable, shared vocabulary):

```
Goal: Client will initiate and complete a 3-step sequenced leisure task
(select game piece → take turn → pass to next player) with no more than
1 verbal cue, in a group of ≤8, 4 of 5 trials, by [date].

Baseline: 0 of 5 trials at intake (required 2–3 cues per step).
Linked discipline goal: OT goal #2 (sequencing for AM self-care routine).
Instrument: task-trial count, direct observation, logged each session.
```

The test: could another discipline read this goal and know exactly what "met" looks like without asking the RT what "engaged well" meant? If not, rewrite it.

## 3. Task-grading ladder (Montessori-based dementia/behavioral programming)

When a client disengages, step down **one rung**, run one more trial, then decide — don't jump straight to the bottom rung or the client never uses skill they still have.

1. **Full activity, multi-step, choice of materials** (e.g., "arrange this flower centerpiece your way").
2. **Reduced choice, same steps** (2 material options instead of 6).
3. **Single-step, real recognizable object, one instruction at a time** ("hold this flower" → wait → "put it in the vase").
4. **Physical guidance/hand-under-hand with a single real object**, verbal instruction dropped to naming only ("flower... vase").
5. **Sensory-only participation** (holding, smelling, watching) with no completion expectation — used only when rungs 1–4 all failed same-session, and documented as a ceiling check, not a stopping point for future sessions.

Log which rung the client succeeded at, each session — a client stuck at rung 3 for three consecutive sessions is a signal to revise the goal (see red-flags.md), not to keep repeating rung 3 with more encouragement.

## 4. Intensity/billing coordination checklist (IRF 3-hour rule example)

Before adding RT minutes to close a therapy-hours gap:

1. **Compute the actual shortfall.** Required = 3 hrs/day × 5 days = 15.0 hrs/week. Sum PT + OT + SLP minutes already scheduled; convert to hours; subtract from 15.0.
   - Example: PT 300 min + OT 300 min + SLP 180 min = 780 min = 13.0 hrs. Shortfall = 2.0 hrs.
2. **Pull each discipline's active goal list** — do not schedule RT time until at least one goal per RT session is identified to coordinate with.
3. **Design the RT session so its content targets that goal's underlying skill**, not merely happens in the same time slot — grasp-release pattern for a dressing goal, gait-relevant terrain for an ambulation goal, sequencing for a self-care routine.
4. **Write the RT minutes into the same weekly total**, explicitly stating the coordinated goal by name/number in the note (auditors look for the phrase "in addition to and coordinated with," not just co-located timing).
5. **Confirm the arithmetic closes the gap exactly** or explain the remainder — a summary showing 14.25 of 15.0 hours with no explanation is itself a red flag on review (see red-flags.md).
6. **Never use RT minutes to replace a PT/OT/SLP session that was reduced or missed** — that reclassifies the week and can trigger a full billing review, not just a line-item correction.

## 5. Discharge / leisure-education handoff checklist

Complete before the final session, not as an afterthought during it:

- [ ] Named continuation: which specific person (family member) or program (community day program, senior center adaptive class, home-health follow-up) takes over which specific activity.
- [ ] Adapted equipment list with source (durable medical equipment vendor, community loan closet) if any equipment was used in-facility and is needed at home.
- [ ] One sentence of "why this matters" written for the family in plain language, not clinical vocabulary — this is the one place jargon should be dropped entirely.
- [ ] Final re-assessment score recorded against the same instrument used at intake, with the delta stated numerically (e.g., "LDB composite 62 → 79").
- [ ] A single follow-up contact point or date if any adjustment period is expected (e.g., a 2-week check on whether the community program accepted the referral).
