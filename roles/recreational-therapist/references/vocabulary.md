# Vocabulary

### APIE
The core therapeutic-recreation process: Assessment, Planning, Implementation, Evaluation, with documentation running through all four stages rather than sitting outside them.
**In use:** "We're stuck at the planning stage because the assessment never produced a baseline number to plan against."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a one-time intake checklist instead of a cycle — teams "do APIE" at admission and never re-enter it at evaluation, so no delta ever gets measured.

### Perceived Freedom in Leisure (PFL)
The core construct measured by the Leisure Diagnostic Battery — whether a client believes they can choose to participate in and derive benefit from recreation, independent of physical capacity.
**In use:** "His FIM score is unchanged, but his PFL composite moved because he finally believes he can still garden one-handed."
**Common misuse:** Conflated with physical ability — a client can be fully capable and still score low on PFL because of learned helplessness or loss of confidence, which is exactly the population this construct is built to catch.

### Leisure Ability Model
A three-stage program-design framework (functional intervention → leisure education → recreation participation) that maps where a client is in the treatment arc.
**In use:** "This group is still functional intervention — she's not ready for leisure education until the sequencing goal is met."
**Common misuse:** Used as a label for the whole department's approach rather than as a per-client staging tool, so every group gets called "leisure education" regardless of what stage the individual clients are actually in.

### Health Protection/Health Promotion Model
A competing program-design framework oriented around wellbeing and prevention rather than a specific functional deficit.
**In use:** "This wellness group is legitimately health-promotion — there's no individual functional goal, and that's fine because it's not billed as treatment."
**Common misuse:** Invoked to justify diversion-style programming as if it were treatment-equivalent to the Leisure Ability Model, which collapses under an accreditation or payer audit that expects a functional goal.

### CTRS (Certified Therapeutic Recreation Specialist)
The NCTRC credential that defines scope of practice; distinct from a general "activity director" or "recreation leader" title, which carries no clinical assessment or goal-writing scope.
**In use:** "This needs a CTRS eval, not an activity-department referral — there's a specific functional concern here."
**Common misuse:** Job titles and credentials get used interchangeably in casual conversation, but only the CTRS scope covers assessment, goal-setting, and clinical documentation that survives an audit.

### Task grading
Adjusting a leisure activity's complexity in real time (choice count, step count, verbal versus physical cueing) to keep a client at a challenge level they can succeed at most of the time.
**In use:** "Grade it down to single-step before you chart 'unable to participate' — that's a task-difficulty problem, not a capacity ceiling."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a one-time setup decision at the start of a group rather than a continuous, session-by-session adjustment.

### Just-right challenge
The specific difficulty level — hard enough to build skill, easy enough to succeed most trials — that grading is aiming for; not a synonym for "keep it simple."
**In use:** "He's succeeding 100% of trials — that's not just-right anymore, bump the difficulty up a rung."
**Common misuse:** Read as "always simplify," which under-challenges clients who have already progressed and stalls their goal.

### Community reintegration
The structured practice of real-world environments and tasks (transportation, stores, public seating, uneven terrain) before discharge, distinct from generic "community outing."
**In use:** "The Thursday session is community reintegration tied to the ambulation goal, not a field trip."
**Common misuse:** Logged as a recreational outing with no goal linkage, which is exactly the pattern an auditor flags as non-billable.

### Diversional activity
Recreation programming intended for enjoyment or time structure with no assessed functional goal — a legitimate category in its own right, but a different one from treatment.
**In use:** "Bingo tonight is diversional, not treatment — it's not going in anyone's therapy note."
**Common misuse:** The label is skipped and diversional programming gets documented as if it were treatment, which is the single most common source of denied RT minutes.

### 3-hour rule
The inpatient rehabilitation facility (IRF) requirement, under 42 CFR 412.622, to average 3 hours/day of intensive therapy across a 5-day, 7-day-window period to retain IRF-level payment classification.
**In use:** "We're 2 hours short this week — RT can close it, but only if the sessions coordinate with an existing PT or OT goal."
**Common misuse:** Treated as satisfiable by any therapy-adjacent activity, when in fact RT minutes only count when explicitly coordinated with another discipline's active goal.

### Interdisciplinary plan of care
The single shared goal document across PT/OT/SLP/RT/nursing that every discipline's notes reference, rather than each discipline keeping a separate, uncoordinated goal list.
**In use:** "That goal needs to go on the shared plan of care, not just in your RT note, or OT will never know to reinforce it."
**Common misuse:** Each discipline writes goals in isolation and calls the result "interdisciplinary" because everyone happens to be treating the same client, without actually cross-referencing each other's goals.

### Behavior as communication
The framing, standard in dementia and behavioral-health care, that agitation or refusal is information about an unmet need or excessive task demand, not defiance.
**In use:** "He hit the table because the task had six steps and he was three steps past his capacity — that's the antecedent, not just 'agitated.'"
**Common misuse:** Charted as "patient refused" or "combative" with no antecedent recorded, which erases the information the framing exists to capture.

### FIM (Functional Independence Measure)
An 18-item, 7-point functional assessment scale administered by PT/OT (and sometimes nursing) that RT reads to calibrate goal difficulty, without administering it directly.
**In use:** "His FIM transfer score is a 3 — my sequencing goal needs to sit at a difficulty PT's already working toward, not ahead of it."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to be an RT-administered or RT-owned score, leading to duplicated goals that add no new clinical information.
