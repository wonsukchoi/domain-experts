# Vocabulary

### Giveaway
The amount of product given to customers for free by consistently portioning above the label or target weight, incurred as a cost of maintaining regulatory compliance margin against underweight violations.
**In use:** "8g average giveaway per 200g portion — that's a real 4% cost we could reduce if we tighten the process variability."
**Common misuse:** Treating giveaway as an unavoidable fixed cost rather than a calculable value that depends directly on the process's measured weight variability and can be reduced by tightening that variability.

### Kerf loss
Material lost as waste with each cut, from the blade's thickness or the material removed/destroyed in the cutting action, reducing total usable yield from a given input.
**In use:** "Kerf loss went up noticeably after the blade dulled — that's material being crushed and lost, not cleanly cut away."
**Common misuse:** Treating all cutting waste as equivalent, without distinguishing kerf loss, inherent to the cutting method/blade, from trim loss, which comes from pattern or layout inefficiency.

### Portion weight variability (standard deviation)
A statistical measure of how much individual portion weights vary around the process mean, which directly determines the overweight margin needed to achieve a target individual-compliance rate.
**In use:** "SD's at 6g right now — that's what sets how much overweight margin we actually need."
**Common misuse:** Setting a portion target without referencing the process's actual current measured standard deviation, using an assumed figure that may not reflect current blade/process condition.

### Individual-minimum tolerance
A regulatory requirement setting the maximum allowable amount any single portion can fall below its labeled weight.
**In use:** "Average's fine, but check the individual-minimum tolerance too — that's what the 190g floor is protecting."
**Common misuse:** Assuming compliance is achieved as long as the average portion weight looks correct, without verifying the individual-minimum requirement is also satisfied given actual process variability.

### Blade sharpness (cut quality)
The condition of a cutting blade's edge, which affects both cut quality and kerf loss independent of the machine's thickness setting accuracy.
**In use:** "Thickness setting's dead-on, but edges are ragged — that's blade condition, not a setting problem."
**Common misuse:** Adjusting thickness or speed to compensate for declining cut quality from a dulling blade, rather than addressing blade condition directly.

### Yield (cutting/slicing)
The percentage or amount of usable product obtained from a given input after cutting, accounting for both kerf loss and pattern/trim inefficiency.
**In use:** "Portion weights are all in spec, but overall yield's down — check the pattern efficiency on the irregular input, not just individual portions."
**Common misuse:** Measuring yield only in terms of individual portion accuracy without also tracking total material recovery from the input, missing pattern-level inefficiency that portion-level accuracy alone wouldn't reveal.

### Cut pattern (irregular input)
The specific sequence and positioning of cuts applied to a piece of input material, which can be fixed or adaptive to each piece's actual dimensions.
**In use:** "Switching to an adaptive cut pattern on this irregular product recovered yield the fixed pattern was leaving on the table."
**Common misuse:** Applying one fixed cut pattern to input material with natural size/shape variation, leaving avoidable yield loss that an adaptive pattern approach would capture.

### Density/diameter variation (product lot)
Natural differences in a raw product's density or physical dimensions between different production lots, which change the relationship between a given thickness setting and the resulting portion weight.
**In use:** "New lot's denser than the last one — same thickness setting won't hit the same portion weight."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a thickness setting validated on one product lot transfers directly to a new lot without re-verification, when natural lot-to-lot variation changes the actual thickness-to-weight relationship.

### Checkweigher (slicing context)
An in-line or offline scale used to verify actual portion weights against target, serving the same statistical verification function in slicing as in packaging fill operations.
**In use:** "Checkweigher's the actual proof of portion weight — the thickness dial reading isn't a substitute for it."
**Common misuse:** Relying on the machine's thickness setting alone as proof of correct portion weight, without independent scale verification of actual output.

### Trim loss
Material removed from an irregular-shaped input piece to conform it to a uniform cutting pattern, distinct from kerf loss caused directly by the blade.
**In use:** "That waste is trim loss from the fixed pattern, not kerf loss — an adaptive pattern would recover most of it."
**Common misuse:** Conflating trim loss with kerf loss, when trim loss specifically stems from pattern/layout choices relative to the input piece's actual shape, and is addressable by adaptive cutting rather than blade maintenance.
