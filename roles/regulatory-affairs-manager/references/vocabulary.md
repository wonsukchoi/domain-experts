# Regulatory affairs working vocabulary

Terms a regulatory affairs manager uses precisely and a generalist blurs together. Format: definition → how a practitioner says it → common misuse.

**510(k) vs. De Novo vs. PMA** — Three distinct FDA pathways. A 510(k) claims substantial equivalence to an existing cleared predicate device. De Novo is for novel low-to-moderate-risk devices with no valid predicate. PMA (Premarket Approval) is required for high-risk (Class III) devices and requires independent demonstration of safety and effectiveness, not equivalence to anything.
In use: "There's no predicate with our combined intended use, so we're not forcing a 510(k) — this is a De Novo."
Misuse: treating pathway choice as purely a speed/cost decision without checking whether a valid predicate with matching intended use actually exists — a strained 510(k) against a mismatched predicate has worse expected cost and time than doing the right pathway from the start.

**Substantial equivalence** — The 510(k) legal standard: the new device has the same intended use as the predicate and either the same technological characteristics, or different characteristics that don't raise new questions of safety/effectiveness and are supported by data showing equivalent performance.
In use: "Same intended use, different sensor — we need performance data showing the new sensor doesn't raise a new safety question, that's the substantial-equivalence argument."
Misuse: assuming "similar enough" product category equals substantial equivalence — the standard is a specific two-part legal test, not a general resemblance.

**Predicate device** — An already-legally-marketed device the 510(k) submission claims substantial equivalence to; its clearance history, recall status, and stated intended use become part of the new submission's risk profile by association.
In use: "This predicate cleared in 2019 with a clean adverse-event record — that's the reference we want, not the newer one with an open recall."
Misuse: picking the most technologically similar predicate without checking its regulatory history, on the assumption that only technical similarity matters to the reviewer.

**Intended use vs. indications for use** — Intended use is the general purpose of the device (e.g., "aid in detection of pulmonary nodules"). Indications for use is the specific clinical circumstance, patient population, or condition the device is intended for within that general purpose.
In use: "Intended use is triage assistance; indications for use specifies adult chest X-ray, ages 18+, standard PA view only — narrowing indications doesn't change intended use."
Misuse: treating the two terms interchangeably in labeling, which creates ambiguity about exactly what population and use case the clearance actually covers.

**Letter to file vs. new submission (change control)** — A letter to file documents a post-approval design change internally, without agency notification, when the change doesn't affect intended use, safety-critical function, or claimed performance. Any change that does affect those triggers a new submission or supplement instead.
In use: "This is a minor UI change with no functional impact — letter to file, documented in the DHF. The sensor recalibration touching accuracy claims is not — that needs a Special 510(k)."
Misuse: defaulting to "letter to file" because a new submission is slower and more expensive, without running the change through the actual decision tree first.

**Q-sub / pre-submission meeting** — A formal FDA meeting request seeking agency feedback on a proposed regulatory strategy, study design, or classification argument before a full submission is filed.
In use: "We're requesting a Q-sub on the evidence plan before locking the clinical protocol — cheaper to redirect now than after a full submission gets an AI letter."
Misuse: skipping the pre-sub meeting to save calendar time when there's real ambiguity in classification or intended use — the time saved up front is often lost several times over in an Additional-Information cycle.

**MDUFA review clock** — The negotiated FDA performance goals (under the Medical Device User Fee Amendments) for review timelines by submission type, used as the baseline for timeline planning, not a legal guarantee.
In use: "MDUFA IV's target for this device type is 90 days, but the historical average including one AI cycle runs closer to 177 — plan the launch timeline off the historical number, not the target."
Misuse: building a launch timeline off the stated MDUFA target without accounting for the historical average including Additional-Information cycles, which is almost always longer.

**MDR (FDA) vs. MDR (EU)** — In the U.S., MDR is Medical Device Reporting — the adverse-event reporting requirement to FDA (30-day standard, 5-day for specific triggers). In the EU, MDR is the Medical Device Regulation (2017/745) — the overall regulatory framework, distinct from its own serious-incident vigilance reporting requirement (15-day standard, 2-day for serious public health threats).
In use: "Don't confuse the two MDRs in this memo — file the FDA MDR within 30 days of awareness, separately from the EU MDR vigilance report on the same event."
Misuse: using "MDR" unqualified in a global regulatory memo, which reads ambiguously to a reader working across both US and EU frameworks.

**Off-label claim (label expansion)** — Marketing or promoting a device for a use, population, or performance claim not included in its cleared/approved labeling — a claim that outruns the label regardless of clinical plausibility.
In use: "The physicians using it that way informally doesn't make it labeled for that use — promoting it for that indication is off-label until a supplement clears it."
Misuse: treating physician off-label use (which is legal for the physician) as equivalent to the manufacturer promoting that use (which is not) — the restriction is on promotion, not on clinical practice.

**Design History File (DHF)** — The compiled record demonstrating a device was designed and developed according to the approved design plan, required to trace every design input to its verification/validation evidence.
In use: "The DHF needs the recalibration's verification testing linked to the original accuracy spec — a change without that trace reads as untested to an auditor."
Misuse: treating the DHF as a filing-cabinet formality completed after the fact, rather than the live trace an auditor uses to confirm every claim and change was actually tested.

**Notified Body (EU MDR)** — An independent organization designated to assess conformity of medical devices under EU MDR before they can carry a CE mark; functionally analogous to FDA review but privately operated and selected by the manufacturer.
In use: "Our Notified Body flagged the same evidence gap FDA would likely raise — worth addressing before either submission."
Misuse: assuming all Notified Bodies apply identical rigor or timelines — capacity and interpretation vary meaningfully between them, which is itself a strategic factor in which one to engage.
