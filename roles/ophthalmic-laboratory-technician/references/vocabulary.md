# Vocabulary

### Lensometer
An instrument that measures a lens's actual sphere, cylinder, axis, and prism power, used to verify a ground or finished lens against its prescription.
**In use:** "Lensometer's showing 085° — ticket calls 090°, that's a 5° axis error, not within tolerance for this cylinder power."
**Common misuse:** Confirming a lens is clear and undamaged without actually taking a lensometer reading against the prescribed values.

### Axis (cylinder)
The orientation, in degrees (0–180), of a cylindrical lens correction's power meridian.
**In use:** "At -1.50D cylinder, we're working with a tighter axis tolerance than we'd have at -0.50D."
**Common misuse:** Treating a small axis error as negligible regardless of the cylinder power, when tolerance for axis error tightens significantly as cylinder power increases.

### Induced prism
Unintended prismatic effect introduced when a lens's optical center is positioned away from where the wearer's eye actually looks through it, often from mis-decentration.
**In use:** "No prism was prescribed here, but decentering it 3mm off PD introduced measurable induced prism anyway."
**Common misuse:** Assuming prism only exists if intentionally prescribed, missing that any optical-center misplacement introduces prism the prescription never called for.

### Optical center
The point on a lens where light passes through without being prismatically deviated, which should align with the wearer's line of sight.
**In use:** "Optical center needs to sit at the wearer's measured PD, not wherever the frame's midpoint happens to fall."
**Common misuse:** Aligning the optical center to the frame's geometric center by default rather than the wearer's actual measured pupillary distance.

### Pupillary distance (PD)
The distance between a wearer's pupils, or between one pupil and the nose bridge for monocular PD, used to position lens optical centers correctly.
**In use:** "Monocular PD measured at 32mm OD — that's what the optical center gets decentered to, not an assumed average."
**Common misuse:** Using a frame's geometric center or an assumed average PD instead of the wearer's actual measured PD.

### Base curve
The curvature of the front surface of a lens, selected based on the prescription's power range to balance optical performance and cosmetic/fit factors.
**In use:** "This is a high-minus prescription — needs a flatter base curve than we'd use for a low-power lens."
**Common misuse:** Using one default base curve for all jobs regardless of prescription power, introducing avoidable aberration at higher powers.

### Decentration
The process of positioning a lens blank relative to its final optical center location before edging, to achieve the wearer's specific PD and segment height.
**In use:** "Decenter this blank 2mm inward from the tracing's geometric center to hit the wearer's actual PD."
**Common misuse:** Treating decentration as a purely mechanical frame-fitting step rather than an optical-accuracy step that determines whether the wearer gets the prescribed correction where they actually look.

### Edging
The process of cutting a lens blank down to the shape needed to fit a specific frame.
**In use:** "Don't edge this yet — lensometer check hasn't passed, and edging can't be undone if there's a grinding error."
**Common misuse:** Proceeding to edge a lens before its power, axis, and prism have been fully verified, since edging removes material irreversibly and any grinding error found afterward requires starting over with a new blank.

### Add power
Additional plus power incorporated into the lower portion of a multifocal lens for near vision correction, distinct from the distance prescription.
**In use:** "Distance power checks out, but confirm the add power separately — that's a different zone on the lensometer."
**Common misuse:** Confusing add power verification with distance power verification, treating a lensometer check of the distance zone alone as sufficient for a multifocal lens.

### Segment height
The vertical placement of a multifocal lens's near-vision segment relative to the wearer's pupil, critical for correct near-vision use.
**In use:** "Segment height's measured at 18mm for this wearer — use that, not a standard default position."
**Common misuse:** Using a standard or default segment height rather than the wearer's actual measured segment height, producing a multifocal lens that doesn't align with where the wearer's eye falls when looking down for near tasks.
