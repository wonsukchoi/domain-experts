# Vocabulary

### Marker efficiency
The percentage of fabric actually used by pattern pieces relative to the total fabric area laid out for cutting, a calculable yield metric directly tied to fabric cost.
**In use:** "Marker's at 88% efficiency — that 12% waste adds up fast across a 500-marker run."
**Common misuse:** Judging a marker layout as efficient by visual impression alone without calculating the actual utilization percentage.

### Ply height (cutting)
The number of fabric layers stacked together for a single cutting pass, which has a practical accuracy limit specific to fabric thickness and cutting equipment.
**In use:** "This fabric's accuracy limit is 60 plies — stacking to the machine's 100-ply max would distort the bottom layers."
**Common misuse:** Maximizing ply height to the equipment's physical capacity rather than the fabric-specific limit that preserves cutting accuracy at all layers.

### Fabric relaxation
The process of allowing fabric to reach dimensional equilibrium after being unrolled, since fabric under roll tension can be temporarily stretched.
**In use:** "Let it relax the full 24 hours before spreading — cutting it straight off the roll risks shrinkage after the fact."
**Common misuse:** Cutting fabric immediately off the roll without allowing documented relaxation time, risking cut pieces that shrink or distort after cutting.

### Marker (cutting layout)
The layout plan showing how pattern pieces are arranged on fabric to be cut, optimized for both fabric utilization and, for directional fabrics, consistent piece orientation.
**In use:** "This marker's optimized for yield, but check it maintains nap direction — that's not automatic in the software's efficiency calculation."
**Common misuse:** Treating marker layout purely as a fabric-utilization optimization problem without also enforcing directional consistency requirements for napped or printed fabrics.

### Nap / directional fabric
Fabric with a one-way pile, printed design, or weave direction that requires consistent orientation across all pattern pieces in a garment.
**In use:** "This velvet's directional — every piece on the marker needs to point the same way, or panels will show different shading once sewn."
**Common misuse:** Laying a marker for maximum fabric efficiency without regard to nap or direction consistency, producing mismatched panel shading or an inverted pattern once assembled.

### Spreading (fabric)
The process of laying multiple layers of fabric on a cutting table in preparation for cutting, building up the ply stack the marker will be cut through.
**In use:** "Check spreading tension is consistent across the whole lay — uneven spreading introduces its own dimensional variation before the blade even touches it."
**Common misuse:** Assuming spreading tension and alignment are uniform across a tall stack without verification, when inconsistent spreading can itself introduce dimensional variation independent of the cutting process.

### Ply (single layer)
One individual layer of fabric within a stacked cutting lay.
**In use:** "Check plies from the bottom of the stack, not just the top — that's where deflection shows up first."
**Common misuse:** Assuming every ply in a stack behaves identically during cutting, when accuracy can degrade specifically at lower plies in a tall stack due to blade deflection or fabric shift.

### Fabric utilization
The proportion of total fabric consumed that becomes usable cut pieces versus waste, a broader concept than marker efficiency alone since it can also be affected by fabric flaws requiring pattern piece relocation.
**In use:** "Marker efficiency was 90%, but actual fabric utilization came in lower once we accounted for the flaw-avoidance relays."
**Common misuse:** Treating marker efficiency, the layout's theoretical utilization, as identical to actual achieved utilization, without accounting for real-world losses like fabric flaws or spreading defects.

### Straight-knife / band-knife / die cutting
Different cutting equipment types with different accuracy characteristics and ply-height capabilities.
**In use:** "Band-knife holds accuracy at a much lower ply count than the die-cutting setup does — don't apply the same limit to both."
**Common misuse:** Applying the same ply-height assumptions across different cutting equipment types, when each has its own specific accuracy-preserving ply height limit.

### Dimensional verification (cut pieces)
The practice of directly measuring actual cut pieces against pattern specifications, rather than assuming accuracy from following the marker outline.
**In use:** "Pulled pieces from the bottom of the stack and measured them directly — that's the actual verification, not eyeballing the top layer."
**Common misuse:** Verifying cutting accuracy only by visual inspection of the cut edge against the marker line on the top layer, rather than direct measurement of pieces from multiple layers in the stack.
