# Vocabulary

Terms generalists conflate that a route captain or vendor keeps sharply separate. Each: definition, how a practitioner actually uses it, and the common misuse.

## Sit

A full presentation given to an engaged prospect — indoors, at a table, or standing at length on a doorstep — as distinct from a brief doorstep contact that never develops past a sentence or two.

**In use:** "We got 70 contacts this week but only 16 sits — most of them were polite five-second no's, not real conversations."

**Common misuse:** counting every contact as a sit. A sit is specifically the subset of contacts where a real presentation happened; conflating the two inflates the apparent conversion rate and hides where the funnel is actually leaking.

## Pitch stack

The structured sequence of a doorstep or table presentation — intro, permission/transition, presentation, close, referral ask — used as a skeleton that adapts to what's observed, not a script recited word for word.

**In use:** "He's skipping the permission line and going straight into the presentation — that's why doors are closing on him at word ten."

**Common misuse:** treating "the stack" as a fixed script to memorize verbatim rather than a sequence of jobs each part has to do, adapted to the specific doorstep.

## Green River ordinance

A municipal ordinance requiring door-to-door solicitors to register with police or obtain a permit before soliciting, named for the original 1931 Green River, Wyoming ordinance and upheld by the U.S. Supreme Court in *Breard v. City of Alexandria* (1951); thousands of U.S. municipalities use a version of it today.

**In use:** "This town runs a Green River ordinance — permit and ID on you at all times, or it's a citation even if every knock is polite."

**Common misuse:** assuming a permit obtained in one municipality carries into a neighboring one. Each town's ordinance is independent; "we're licensed" without specifying where isn't a real answer.

## FTC Cooling-Off Rule

The federal rule (16 CFR Part 429) giving a buyer 3 business days to cancel a $25-or-more sale made away from the seller's permanent place of business — a doorstep, a street table, a temporary location — with the seller required to give written notice of that right in the language the sale was conducted in.

**In use:** "That's a $340 contract signed on the porch — cancellation notice goes in the packet or the sale isn't compliant, full stop."

**Common misuse:** assuming it only applies to "big" sales or that a verbal mention of cancellation rights satisfies the rule. The notice has to be written and given at the time of sale, regardless of how confident the rep is the customer won't cancel.

## General vendor license vs. mobile food vendor permit

A **general vendor license** covers selling merchandise, subscriptions, or non-food goods from a fixed spot or cart on public property. A **mobile food vendor permit** is a separate track with its own health and inspection requirements, and the two aren't interchangeable even when the physical setup (a cart on a sidewalk) looks similar.

**In use:** "He's got a general vendor license, not a food permit — the second he starts selling hot food off that cart he's exposed."

**Common misuse:** assuming any vendor permit covers any kind of vending. The permit type is tied to what's being sold, not to the act of standing on public property with a cart.

## Float

The starting cash bank counted and signed for at the beginning of a shift, used to make change and reconciled against the sales log at shift close.

**In use:** "Count the float with me before you head out — I want two signatures on the fifty, not one."

**Common misuse:** treating the float as part of the day's earnings rather than a loan against which the day's cash sales get reconciled; conflating the two makes any real shortage invisible until it's much larger.

## DNC / no-soliciting flag

An address-level flag in the crew's own route log marking a household as off-limits for future knocks — distinct from the National Do Not Call Registry, which covers telephone solicitation only and has no bearing on in-person doorstep visits.

**In use:** "That address is flagged from last month — skip it, doesn't matter that the DNC registry doesn't cover door knocks, our own flag list does."

**Common misuse:** assuming the national Do Not Call Registry protects a household from door-to-door contact. It doesn't; the only enforceable in-person protections are local ordinance, a posted no-soliciting sign, and the crew's own flag discipline.

## Chargeback vs. cancellation

A **cancellation** is the customer exercising a disclosed right (like the FTC cooling-off window) to void the sale before it's fulfilled. A **chargeback** is the customer's card issuer reversing a payment after the fact, often after the cancellation window has passed, and it costs the seller a fee on top of the lost sale.

**In use:** "That's not a cancellation, the window closed two weeks ago — that's a chargeback, and it's going to cost us the processing fee too."

**Common misuse:** treating the two as the same event with the same cost. A clean cancellation inside the disclosed window is a normal, low-cost part of doing business; a chargeback outside it is a compliance and cost problem worth investigating.

## Prior invitation exception

The common carve-out in most solicitation ordinances exempting a visit the resident explicitly requested (a callback, a scheduled return) from permit or registration requirements that otherwise apply to unsolicited knocks.

**In use:** "She asked me to come back Thursday after she talks to her husband — that's a prior invitation, no separate registration issue for that visit."

**Common misuse:** stretching the exception to cover any address where the rep has knocked before. It only covers a visit the resident actually requested, not a return knock the rep decided to make on their own.

## Spot vs. route

A **spot** is a claimed, usually fixed selling location (a vendor's cart position, a table). A **route** is an assigned geographic block a canvasser works door to door, expected to change week to week as it gets worked and rested.

**In use:** "He's treating his spot like a route — moving it every few days instead of building repeat customers who know where to find him."

**Common misuse:** using the terms interchangeably. A vendor optimizes a spot for consistency and recognition; a canvasser optimizes a route for freshness and avoiding over-knocking the same addresses.

## Trunk slammer

Trade slang, usually derogatory, for a fly-by-night rep or crew operating without a valid permit who collects deposits or payment up front and disappears before delivering the product or service.

**In use:** "That crew that hit three streets last month and vanished — classic trunk slammers, no permit on file with the city at all."

**Common misuse:** using it loosely as an insult for any pushy rep. It specifically describes the no-permit, take-the-money-and-go pattern, not aggressive but legitimate selling.

## Territory saturation ("burning" a block)

The point at which a neighborhood's response rate has degraded from repeated recent knocking — residents recognize the pattern and stop engaging — distinct from a block that was simply never receptive to begin with.

**In use:** "Contact rate on Maple cratered because we burned it — three passes in ten days. Give it two weeks before going back."

**Common misuse:** concluding a block "doesn't work" after one saturated cycle, when the real fix is spacing, not abandoning the territory.
