# Playbook

Filled operational tools a route captain or senior rep actually runs, with real structure and example numbers — starting points to adapt, not scripts to recite verbatim.

## 1. The pitch stack (filled example: home pest-control D2D)

Five parts, each with a job. Skipping the permission ask is the single most common reason a technically good pitch gets a door closed early.

1. **Intro (5–8 seconds).** Name, company, and *why you're on this specific block* — a reason ties the visit to the neighborhood, not to the resident personally, which lowers defensiveness.
   *"Hey, I'm Jordan with Greenline Pest — we're out doing the Maple Street side today, your neighbor at 214 just signed up."*
2. **Permission/transition (one line).** Ask before pitching; it signals respect for the threshold and filters out brush-offs fast.
   *"Got sixty seconds? I'll tell you what we're doing and you can tell me if it's not a fit."*
3. **Presentation (60–90 seconds, adapted to what's observed).** Lead with the specific problem this block has (visible ant trails, a wasp nest two doors down), not a generic feature list.
   *"We're targeting the ant activity coming up from the creek line this block — most houses here haven't had a barrier treatment since spring."*
4. **Close (direct, binary, no hedging).** Ask for the decision, don't describe the decision.
   *"I can get you on the schedule for Thursday or Friday — which works better?"*
5. **Referral ask (every interaction, closed or not).** Costs nothing, and referral-sourced sits close at a materially higher rate than cold knocks (see Rep A's week 3 in `SKILL.md`).
   *"Even if it's not for you — who else on this block should I knock on?"*

**Objection-handling table (filled):**

| Objection, verbatim | Timing | Read | Response pattern |
|---|---|---|---|
| "Not interested" (door barely open) | First 5 sec | Brush-off | Thank them, move on — don't spend the next door's time here |
| "I already have someone for this" | After presentation | Genuine comparison | Ask what they're paying and what's covered; answer the specific gap, not a generic pitch |
| "I need to talk to my spouse" | At close | Stall or real | Offer a specific callback window ("I'm back on this street Thursday, want me to swing by after you've talked?") instead of leaving it open-ended |
| "How do I know you're legit?" | Anytime | Verification request | Show the permit/ID immediately, unprompted next time — don't treat the question as an objection to overcome |

## 2. Route/territory planning table (filled example, one crew, one week)

| Block | Households | Last knocked | Flagged (no-soliciting/DNC) | Curfew | Plan |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Maple St 100–300 | 84 | Never | 3 | 8:00pm (city ord.) | Full knock, 4:30–7:45pm |
| Maple St 300–500 | 76 | 6 days ago | 5 | 8:00pm | Skip this cycle — re-knock in 7+ days minimum |
| Elm Ct | 22 | Never | 1 | 8:00pm | Full knock |
| Birchwood (HOA, gate) | 140 | Never | n/a | n/a | Hold — confirm HOA solicitation policy before entering |

**Rule:** never re-knock a block inside 5–7 days of the last pass; response rate on a re-knocked block inside that window runs well below a fresh one, and it's also the fastest way to stack up complaints from the same addresses.

## 3. Cash/float reconciliation template (filled, one shift)

```
SHIFT: 2025-06-14, Rep: J. Alvarez, Route: Elm/Birchwood

OPENING FLOAT:            $50.00  (counted, both signatures)
CASH SALES (from log):    $180.00  (3 transactions @ $60)
CARD SALES (from log):    $420.00  (7 transactions, terminal batch)
COMPS/SAMPLES GIVEN:      1 unit, logged, no cash value

EXPECTED CLOSING CASH:    $50.00 + $180.00 = $230.00
ACTUAL COUNTED CASH:      $224.00
DISCREPANCY:              -$6.00

WALK-THROUGH: 3rd cash sale, customer paid with a $100, change
given was $34 not $40 — miscounted change, not a missing sale.
RESOLUTION: logged as a $6 shortage against Rep J. Alvarez,
same-day, correction noted for tomorrow's float.
```

**Rule:** reconcile before leaving the block, not back at the office — the closer the count is to the transaction, the more explainable a discrepancy is. Anything over $10 unexplained after the walk-through gets escalated same day, not carried to the next shift.

## 4. Permit/ordinance compliance checklist (by jurisdiction type)

Run this before the first knock in any town not already verified this cycle:

- [ ] Does this municipality require a solicitor's permit or police registration (a "Green River"-style ordinance)? If yes, is the crew's permit current and carried on-person?
- [ ] Is there a posted curfew (commonly sunset or 8–9pm) beyond which soliciting is a separate violation from the base permit requirement?
- [ ] Does the block/neighborhood have an HOA or gated-community rule layered on top of the municipal ordinance? HOA rules can be stricter than city code and aren't covered by a city permit.
- [ ] Is any sale here likely to total $25 or more and happen away from a fixed place of business? If yes, the FTC 3-business-day cancellation notice is required at point of sale, in the language the sale was conducted in.
- [ ] Are there addresses on this block already flagged no-soliciting or DNC from a prior cycle? Confirm the flag list is current before starting, not after a complaint.

## 5. Vendor spot-selection playbook (street/news vending)

**Chokepoint scoring (filled example, three candidate spots for a subscription/news table):**

| Spot | Raw daily foot traffic (est.) | Forced-pause factor | Existing vendor nearby | Permit type required | Verdict |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Mid-block storefront row | ~3,200/day | Low — moving crowd | No | General vendor | Skip — high volume, low stop rate |
| Subway exit / crosswalk | ~1,800/day | High — forced wait at signal | No | General vendor, check MTA/transit-authority overlay | Take — fewer people, more stops |
| Existing farmers-market row | ~900/day | Medium | Yes, 2 vendors | Market-specific permit, may be exclusive | Verify exclusivity terms before setting up — could void both permits |

**Rule:** a permit that grants exclusivity for a spot is worth confirming in writing before relying on custom or first-come-first-served precedent — contested spots are one of the more common sources of vendor-to-vendor and vendor-to-police friction (see `references/red-flags.md`, #7).
