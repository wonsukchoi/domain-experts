# Red flags

Smell tests a route captain or senior rep catches early. Format for each: what it usually means, the first question to ask, and the data to pull before deciding anything.

## 1. Contact rate drops below ~25% in a previously productive block

**Usually means:** the block has been over-knocked this cycle (residents recognize repeat visits and stop answering), or work patterns shifted (more remote work moved the peak-answer window later or earlier than the crew's schedule assumes).

**First question:** "When did we last work this block, and at what hours?"

**Data to pull:** route log of last-knocked date per block; hour-of-day contact-rate breakdown for the last two weeks on this block versus the crew's current working hours.

## 2. Sit rate is normal but the close rate is near zero for the week

**Usually means:** either a genuine pitch/product mismatch this week, or the sit-quality mix quietly shifted — cold-knock sits close at a materially lower rate than referral sits, and a week heavy on cold sits can look like a script problem when it's a lead-source problem.

**First question:** "How many of this week's sits were referrals versus cold knocks?"

**Data to pull:** sit source breakdown for the week; close rate split by lead source (referral vs. cold) over the last month for comparison.

## 3. Doors-knocked-per-hour trending down with no schedule change on paper

**Usually means:** burnout or avoidance behavior — lingering at easier doors, longer breaks, a later start or earlier stop that never got logged as a schedule change — rather than a real ceiling on pace.

**First question:** "What's the actual clock time between doors right now versus week one?"

**Data to pull:** GPS or app-tracked pace and gaps between knocks; start/stop timestamps for the last two weeks against the assigned working window.

## 4. Cash float short at end of shift by more than a token amount (>$10, unexplained)

**Usually means:** miscounted change on a cash sale, or a sale that happened but wasn't logged — rarely theft on the first occurrence, but an unexplained pattern across multiple shifts changes that read.

**First question:** "Walk me through the last three cash transactions in order, dollar by dollar."

**Data to pull:** sales log timestamps against the float count; card-terminal batch total for the same shift as a cross-check.

## 5. Rep or vendor working past a posted curfew or into a flagged no-soliciting block

**Usually means:** the route sheet wasn't checked or updated before the shift, or a rep is knocking flagged addresses anyway for volume — either way it's a permit-level risk, not a one-address problem.

**First question:** "Did you check the block flags and curfew before starting today?"

**Data to pull:** route flag list versus GPS/knock-log timestamps for the shift; any prior complaint history tied to this block.

## 6. Same-week spike in cancellations or chargebacks on newly closed contracts

**Usually means:** high-pressure closing outran proper disclosure — the customer signed under pressure and reconsidered once they re-read the terms, or the required cancellation-rights notice wasn't given at point of sale.

**First question:** "Were signed cancellation-rights notices given at the point of sale for these contracts?"

**Data to pull:** copies of signed disclosures; timing of each cancellation relative to the sale date (inside vs. outside the 3-business-day window).

## 7. A vendor spot's productivity drops right after a competing cart or table appears nearby

**Usually means:** a genuine foot-traffic split, but sometimes signals the spot was never actually exclusive — just first-come-first-served custom — and is now legitimately contested.

**First question:** "Is this spot covered by a permit that grants exclusivity, or is it custom-only?"

**Data to pull:** permit terms and any exclusivity language; the competing vendor's operating hours and overlap with this spot's hours.

## 8. A complaint is filed with local police or a licensing office about a rep or crew

**Usually means:** either one rep ignored flags or a curfew on a single occasion, or the whole crew's permit has lapsed or was never valid for that specific municipality — the second case is far more expensive to fix and needs checking first.

**First question:** "Is our permit current for this specific town, and did the address in question have a no-soliciting flag on file?"

**Data to pull:** permit expiration and jurisdiction list; address flag history for the complaint address; the complaint text itself for specifics.

## 9. Close rate jumps sharply right after a new, more aggressive script rolls out

**Usually means:** a short-term close-rate gain that's masking a coming cancellation wave — the script is pushing decisions faster than the cooling-off disclosure gives customers room to reconsider, and the real cost shows up 30 days later, not this week.

**First question:** "What's the 30-day cancellation rate on contracts closed with the new script versus the old one?"

**Data to pull:** cancellation rate by script version and cohort week, tracked at least 30 days past close date before crediting the new script with anything.

## 10. Field inventory count doesn't match units sold plus units remaining

**Usually means:** unlogged breakage or damage, an unlogged comp/sample given away, or — least likely on a first occurrence — theft; the walk-through almost always finds it before escalating to the worst-case read.

**First question:** "Walk the day's stock from opening count to closing count, sale by sale."

**Data to pull:** opening stock count, sales log, closing stock count, and the sample/comp log for the same shift.
