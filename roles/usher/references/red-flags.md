# Red flags

Smell tests a house manager or lead usher should catch inside the first few minutes of a door check. Format: what it usually means, the first question to ask, and the data to pull before deciding anything.

## 1. One door handling a single working lane for 10+ minutes during peak ingress

**Usually means:** a scanner jam, an untrained substitute usher, or understaffing that's silently rerouting all traffic through half the rated capacity — a queue that "looks normal" for the time of night can be running at half throughput and about to blow past curtain.

**First question:** "How many lanes are actually working at every door right now, not how many are staffed?"

**Data to pull:** door-by-door scans-per-minute for the last 10 minutes, lane status log, queue length estimate at each door.

## 2. Standing-room or general-admission capacity estimated from ticket sales, not measured net area

**Usually means:** someone calculated "how many we can sell" once, using an assumed square-footage-per-person factor that was never checked against the space's certified occupancy placard — this is the exact gap (5 sq ft/person assumed vs. 7 sq ft/person required) documented in the Astroworld 2021 crowd crush.

**First question:** "What's this area's certified cap at our jurisdiction's net-area factor, and who's counting heads against it in real time tonight?"

**Data to pull:** the venue's fire-marshal-certified occupancy placard, the jurisdiction's net-sq-ft-per-person factor, live clicker count at the standing-room barrier.

## 3. A fire exit propped open, blocked, or an aisle narrowed below its rated width by a table or line

**Usually means:** a vendor placement, merch table, or overflow crowd decision was made without anyone checking the egress width requirement for that path — it reads as harmless space use until it's the evacuation route that's short.

**First question:** "What's the clear width left in that path right now, and is it a code-rated exit or aisle?"

**Data to pull:** the as-built egress/aisle width for that path, a current measurement or photo of the obstruction.

## 4. Late-seating overrides granted mid-scene more than once in a run

**Usually means:** house policy isn't being enforced consistently — either a training gap among newer ushers, or pressure being applied by a specific patron category (VIP, donor, group leader) that staff feel they can't say no to.

**First question:** "Who authorized each override, and does it match the house manager's published hold points for this show?"

**Data to pull:** late-seating log cross-checked against the stage manager's break sheet, name of authorizing manager per incident.

## 5. Accessible seating clustered in one section rather than dispersed across price tiers

**Usually means:** a renovation, temporary staging change, or a season of ad hoc swaps has quietly consolidated ADA seats into one block, putting the venue under the 2010 ADA Standards' dispersal minimum even if the total count is technically still met.

**First question:** "Does the current seating map disperse accessible spaces across the required price tiers and sightlines, or did something reallocate them into one area?"

**Data to pull:** current accessible-seating map against the original ADA compliance filing, count of ADA swaps logged this season, any patron complaints on file.

## 6. Visible crowd density at a doorway, stair, or line reaching "can't turn without touching someone" while the headcount is still comfortably under the posted cap

**Usually means:** the posted cap is a whole-room average that's masking a local pinch point — Still's crowd-flow research puts the onset of congestion around 2–4 persons/m² and crush risk from roughly 5 persons/m² upward, both well below what a whole-room average would flag.

**First question:** "Where exactly is it tight right now, and can we open a second path or meter entry there immediately?"

**Data to pull:** the specific pinch-point location, current door/lane configuration serving it, time since flow was last adjusted there.

## 7. Ticket-scan reject or duplicate rate spikes above roughly 2–3% in a short window

**Usually means:** a resale-fraud batch of duplicated barcodes hitting one gate, or a sync failure between box office and gate scanners — either way, a cluster is a different problem than scattered individual mistakes.

**First question:** "Are the rejects clustering around one ticket source or reseller batch, or spread evenly across the crowd?"

**Data to pull:** reject log with ticket source/batch ID, box-office duplicate-scan report for the same window.

## 8. A same-night incident report has no timestamp or headcount attached to the location

**Usually means:** the escalation happened entirely by radio and word of mouth, and the paper trail needed for liability review and pattern detection (same door, same failure mode, repeatedly) never got built.

**First question:** "Walk me through the radio call — what time, what exact location, who responded, and when."

**Data to pull:** radio log timestamps, names of responding staff, time of handoff to EMS or security.

## 9. Two large-group ticket batches scheduled to arrive at the same door in the same 10-minute window

**Usually means:** group sales sold arrival windows without checking them against the house's door-by-door staffing and flow plan — a batch mismatch that FOH only discovers when the queue is already backed up.

**First question:** "Did group sales coordinate these arrival times with house management before selling them?"

**Data to pull:** group booking arrival windows, current door assignment plan, that door's historical throughput on a comparable night.

## 10. Front-of-house staff describe the late-seating cue or evacuation assembly point as "we just figure it out"

**Usually means:** written, drilled standing orders either don't exist or exist but were never trained on — the gap that shows up not on a normal night, but on the one night the plan actually gets used.

**First question:** "When did this team last walk the evacuation plan and late-seating points together with stage management, not just read them?"

**Data to pull:** training/drill log, date the cue sheet was last updated, current written standing orders for late seating and evacuation.
