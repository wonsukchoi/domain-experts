# Vocabulary

Terms front-of-house staff keep sharply separate that generalists conflate. Each: definition, a sentence a practitioner would actually say, and the common misuse.

## House manager vs. stage manager

The **house manager** owns everything on the audience side of the curtain line — doors, seating, ushers, occupancy, patron issues. The **stage manager** (or event director) owns everything on the performance side — cues, scene breaks, timing. Late seating and evacuation are joint calls made at the threshold between the two roles, not owned by either alone.

**In use:** "That's a house call, not a stage call — get the house manager, not the SM, on the radio."

**Common misuse:** assuming an usher reports to or takes cues directly from the stage manager; the usher's chain of command is the house manager, who then coordinates with the SM.

## Will call vs. box office vs. comp

**Will call** is a name-verified pickup window for tickets already purchased and held under a patron's name — it requires ID matching, not payment. **Box office** is the point of sale for new tickets. A **comp** is a complimentary ticket authorized by marketing, the production, or a specific manager, separate from both.

**In use:** "That's a will-call pickup, not a walk-up sale — check the name against ID before you hand anything over."

**Common misuse:** treating will call as a same-day sale window, or letting a patron claim a "comp" without a manager's authorization on file.

## Grace period vs. late seating

A **grace period** is how long a reserved seat or held ticket stays available before it's released to someone else (relevant to box office and reservations). **Late seating** is the separate question of when a patron who's already inside can physically be walked to their seat once the show has started. A ticket surviving its grace period doesn't mean the patron can be seated immediately.

**In use:** "Their ticket's still valid, grace period's not the issue — it's a late-seating hold until the next scene break."

**Common misuse:** telling a late patron "your seat's still yours" as if that settles when they can sit in it.

## Egress width vs. aisle width

**Egress width** is the code-rated clear, unobstructed width of an exit path, measured to the innermost obstruction (handrail, sign, furniture) — it's what the fire marshal calculated evacuation time against. **Aisle width** as commonly used can mean the nominal or decorative width, including things that narrow the actual clear path.

**In use:** "The nameplate stand cuts three inches off the egress width there — measure to the obstruction, not to the wall."

**Common misuse:** approving a table or sign placement because the aisle "looks fine," without checking the clear width against the code-rated minimum.

## Occupancy load vs. ticketed capacity

**Occupancy load** is the fire-code maximum for a space, including staff, standees, and anyone present regardless of ticket status. **Ticketed capacity** is the number of seats sold — a subset of occupancy load, not the same number.

**In use:** "We're sold out on seats, but occupancy load still has standing-room headroom — check the clicker count, not the seat manifest."

**Common misuse:** treating "sold out" as synonymous with "at capacity," which undercounts standees, staff, and vendors also inside the occupancy limit.

## Crowd density vs. crowd count

**Density** is a spatial rate — persons per square meter at a specific location — that predicts crush risk. **Count** is a cumulative total across the whole space. A safe total count can coexist with a dangerous local density at one pinch point.

**In use:** "Total count's fine — it's the density at the Door 3 vestibule that's the problem right now."

**Common misuse:** reassured by a headcount well under the posted cap while a specific doorway or stair is already at crush-risk density.

## Dispersal vs. accessible seating count

**Dispersal** (2010 ADA Standards §221) requires wheelchair spaces and companion seats spread across price tiers, sightlines, and levels — not just present in the required total number. Meeting the count while clustering the seats in one section still fails the requirement.

**In use:** "We have the right total, but they're all in the last row — that's a dispersal failure, not a count failure."

**Common misuse:** treating accessible seating as satisfied once the numeric minimum is met, without checking where those seats actually sit.

## Crush vs. crowd surge vs. stampede

Three distinct crowd-failure mechanisms. A **crush** is progressive compressive force building in a dense, largely stationary crowd — often the most dangerous because it's not visible as fast motion. A **crowd surge** is a sudden mass shift toward or away from a point (a stage rush, a barrier failure). A **stampede** is panicked, high-speed flight, usually away from a perceived threat. Each demands a different response.

**In use:** "This was a crush, not a stampede — nobody was running, the density just built until people couldn't move."

**Common misuse:** using the terms interchangeably in an incident report, which obscures the actual causal mechanism responders and investigators need to identify.

## Standing-room vs. general admission (GA)

**Standing-room** is a fixed, capped area within an otherwise reserved-seat house, tracked against its own separate occupancy limit. **General admission** is an entire ticket class for the show with no assigned seat at all, typically the whole floor or venue. The two have different capacity rules and can't be managed with the same playbook.

**In use:** "Standing-room cap is 100 and it's separate from the seated count — GA floor downstairs is a different calculation entirely."

**Common misuse:** applying a standing-room headcount cap to a GA floor, or vice versa, when the underlying occupancy math and the space itself are different.

## First responder vs. first receiver

Front-of-house staff are **first responders** — the people who notice, report precisely, and provide only basic first aid within their training. **First receivers** are the trained specialists (EMS, security, medical staff) who take over the actual intervention. The role's value is in the gap between those two, not in becoming the second one.

**In use:** "Our job stopped at the radio call and clearing space — EMS is the first receiver from here."

**Common misuse:** an usher attempting restraint or advanced first aid because "someone had to do something," which extends liability past the role's actual scope.

## Comp vs. house seats

A **comp** is a complimentary ticket authorized by box office or marketing for general promotional purposes. **House seats** are a specific reserved block held by the production itself — for the creative team, investors, or emergency use — and are not interchangeable with a comp pool.

**In use:** "That's a house seat, not a comp — it needs sign-off from the production, not box office."

**Common misuse:** an usher or box office staffer pulling from the house-seat block to solve a walk-up problem, which eats into a reservation the production is depending on.
