# Vocabulary

### Relief angle
The angle ground behind a cutting edge that prevents the tool's body from rubbing against the workpiece during cutting, a specific geometric value that must match the tool's original design specification.
**In use:** "Relief angle's spec'd at ten degrees — regrinding to anything meaningfully different changes how this tool actually cuts, even if the edge looks equally sharp."
**Common misuse:** Treating "sharp" as a single, sufficient quality standard for a regrind — a tool can be technically sharp while ground to the wrong relief angle, producing different cutting force and chip formation than the tool was designed for.

### Rake angle
The angle of a cutting tool's face relative to the workpiece, determining how the tool shears material and forms chips, another geometric specification that must be restored accurately during a regrind.
**In use:** "Rake angle came back at four-point-nine degrees against a five-degree spec — within tolerance, confirmed with the optical comparator, not just eyeballed."
**Common misuse:** Assuming rake angle restoration is close enough "by feel" without instrument verification — small angle deviations can meaningfully change cutting behavior, and confirming them requires measurement, not visual estimation.

### Wear pattern (diagnostic)
The specific appearance and distribution of wear or damage on a cutting tool's edges, which carries information about the actual cause of that wear — normal use, impact, or a process condition — distinct from the wear itself as a defect to simply grind away.
**In use:** "Chipping on two of four flutes, not all four — that's not random, that's a specific signature pointing at excessive feed or an interrupted cut."
**Common misuse:** Treating a wear pattern as just a defect to remove rather than information to read first — regrinding away the visible damage without diagnosing its pattern misses the chance to catch and correct a process condition that will otherwise cause the same failure again.

### Regrind depth
The amount of material removed from a cutting tool's edge during resharpening, which must be calculated to clear beyond any subsurface damage from the observed wear/failure mode, not just eliminate the visible defect.
**In use:** "Chip depth measured at eight-thousandths — we're grinding to twelve to clear four-thousandths of expected subsurface micro-cracking beyond that."
**Common misuse:** Setting regrind depth by how the surface looks after grinding rather than by measured damage depth plus a clearance margin — a shallow regrind that looks clean can leave damage below the visible surface that causes an early repeat failure.

### Subsurface damage
Micro-cracking or a work-hardened layer beneath a cutting tool's visibly worn or chipped surface, not eliminated by removing only the visible defect and requiring a deeper regrind to fully clear.
**In use:** "The visible chip's only five-thousandths deep, but subsurface damage from that impact likely extends further — grinding past it, not just to it."
**Common misuse:** Assuming the visible extent of damage represents the full extent of the actual damage — impact or heat-related failures commonly extend below the surface further than what's immediately visible.

### Tool life (regrind economics)
The total remaining usable material/geometry a cutting tool has across its expected series of regrinds before retirement, a finite resource that each regrind consumes.
**In use:** "We're tracking cumulative regrinds against expected tool life — this one's on its fourth regrind of an expected six before retirement."
**Common misuse:** Grinding deeper than necessary "to be safe" without regard for tool life consumption — since every regrind is a trade-off between damage clearance and remaining life, unnecessary depth wastes a finite resource without a corresponding quality benefit.

### Heat-affected zone (tool sharpening)
The region of a cutting tool where grinding-generated heat has altered the material's hardness, directly reducing the performance of the exact edge being restored, distinct from heat effects on a separate workpiece.
**In use:** "Light passes with coolant on this regrind — the tool itself is what we're heating, and losing hardness here defeats the whole point of sharpening it."
**Common misuse:** Treating heat management during tool sharpening as a generic grinding-safety concern rather than a direct quality issue specific to this operation — since the tool being ground is also the tool that will perform the cutting, overheating it during sharpening directly undermines its restored performance.

### Interrupted cut
A cutting condition where the tool periodically enters and exits contact with the workpiece (rather than continuous engagement), creating repeated impact loading that can cause chipping distinct from steady-state abrasive wear.
**In use:** "This chipping pattern matches an interrupted cut signature — checking whether the application actually involves intermittent engagement."
**Common misuse:** Assuming all edge chipping traces to tool quality or sharpening error — interrupted cutting is a process condition that produces impact-driven chipping regardless of how well the tool was ground or how good the carbide grade is.

### Optical comparator (tool geometry verification)
An instrument that projects a magnified silhouette of a tool's edge for precise angle and profile measurement, used to verify restored cutting geometry against specification.
**In use:** "Putting this back on the comparator to confirm relief and rake before it goes back into the machine."
**Common misuse:** Relying on visual inspection under a loupe or magnifier alone to judge whether restored geometry matches specification — an optical comparator provides actual angle measurement, which a visual check, however careful, cannot reliably replicate.
