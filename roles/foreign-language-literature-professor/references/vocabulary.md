# Vocabulary

### Proficiency (ACTFL sense)
What a learner can do with the language, unrehearsed, in a real communicative task — assessed via an Oral Proficiency Interview (OPI) or Writing Proficiency Test (WPT) against the Novice-through-Distinguished scale.
**In use:** "Her course grades are strong, but we still need an OPI before certifying her for the exchange program — proficiency and achievement aren't the same measurement."
**Common misuse:** treating a course grade or GPA as a proxy for proficiency; the two frequently diverge by a full sublevel or more.

### OPI (Oral Proficiency Interview)
ACTFL's standardized, certified-tester interview protocol for rating a speaker's proficiency sublevel.
**In use:** "The state licensure office requires an official OPI rating of Advanced-Low, not our department's own conversation-course grade."
**Common misuse:** assuming any faculty member can informally "OPI" a student and the result carries the same weight as a certified rater's score — certification-grade determinations require an ACTFL-certified tester.

### ACTFL sublevel (e.g., Intermediate-Mid, Advanced-Low)
The scale's finer gradations (Low/Mid/High within Novice, Intermediate, Advanced) used to set specific institutional benchmarks like study-abroad or teacher-certification gates.
**In use:** "The partner university's exchange program requires Intermediate-Mid; she tested Intermediate-Low, so we have one sublevel to close before the application deadline."
**Common misuse:** rounding a sublevel up in conversation ("she's basically Advanced") when the specific sublevel, not the broad category, is what an institutional gate actually requires.

### Input hypothesis (comprehensible input, i+1)
Krashen's theory that language acquisition occurs primarily through exposure to input slightly beyond a learner's current level, understandable through context.
**In use:** "The new curriculum sequence is built around comprehensible input at each level rather than front-loaded grammar rules."
**Common misuse:** treating "more input" as sufficient on its own, without attention to output practice or interaction, which later SLA theory (Long's interaction hypothesis, Swain's output hypothesis) identifies as also necessary.

### Two-tiered curriculum
The traditional structure separating "language" courses (grammar, skills) from "literature" courses (content, taught once skills are judged sufficient), critiqued by the MLA's 2007 report as an artificial split.
**In use:** "We're moving off the two-tiered model — content and language instruction run together from the second year on, per the MLA's integrated-curriculum recommendation."
**Common misuse:** assuming the critique means literature courses should be eliminated or delayed further, when the actual recommendation is integration earlier, not less literary content overall.

### Translingual and transcultural competence
The MLA's proposed goal for language majors: the ability to operate between languages and cultural frameworks, not just achieve native-like performance in one.
**In use:** "The senior capstone assesses translingual competence — code-switching and cultural-frame navigation — not just grammatical accuracy."
**Common misuse:** using the term as a synonym for basic bilingualism, when it specifically names the ability to move between cultural frames of reference, not just between two language systems.

### Service course
An introductory or intermediate language course that exists to satisfy a college-wide degree requirement, taken predominantly by students who will never declare the major.
**In use:** "GER 101 and 102 are service courses — they'd run at roughly this enrollment even if we had zero declared majors."
**Common misuse:** treating service-course enrollment as evidence of major-track health, when the two are functionally separate populations with separate funding logic.

### Heritage learner
A student who acquired the language in a home or community setting before formal instruction, typically with an oral/written skill profile that doesn't match a classroom L2 learner's.
**In use:** "Placement testing misplaced two heritage learners into GER 201 this term — their oral skills are well past that level, but their formal writing tested lower."
**Common misuse:** assuming heritage learners are simply "ahead" across the board and can skip levels uniformly, when their profile is typically uneven across the four skills.

### Placement cut score
The threshold score on a placement instrument that assigns a student to a specific course level.
**In use:** "We revalidated the GER 201 cut score after DFW rates there hit 29% — the band, not the whole test, needed adjustment."
**Common misuse:** treating the cut score as fixed once set, rather than an instrument that needs periodic revalidation against actual course outcomes.

### Non-tenure-track (NTT) instructional line
A teaching position (lecturer, adjunct, visiting instructor) without a tenure track, common in language departments' introductory sequences.
**In use:** "At 70%+ NTT-taught intro sections, we have a continuity risk feeding the major, not just a cost question."
**Common misuse:** evaluating NTT staffing purely as a budget line item, without accounting for its effect on advising continuity and major-pipeline recruitment.

### SCH (student credit hour)
One student enrolled in one credit hour of coursework; the base unit most university budget (RCM) models use to attribute revenue and cost.
**In use:** "The program generates 487 SCH, but 96.9% of that comes from service courses, not the major."
**Common misuse:** citing a program's total SCH as if it were evenly distributed across all course levels, obscuring the service-vs-major split that actually determines viability.

### CEFR-ACTFL crosswalk
An approximate mapping between the Common European Framework of Reference (used internationally, especially by European partner institutions) and the ACTFL scale (standard in US higher ed).
**In use:** "The Berlin exchange partner requires CEFR B2 — that's roughly ACTFL Advanced-Low to Advanced-Mid, but the two scales aren't a precise one-to-one match."
**Common misuse:** treating the crosswalk as an exact equivalence rather than an approximation — the two frameworks assess somewhat different constructs and a partner institution's stated CEFR requirement should be confirmed directly, not just converted.
