# Vocabulary

Terms generalists conflate or misuse; a practitioner keeps each one narrow and precise because the diagnosis or the safety call depends on which one actually applies.

## 1/3 rule

The mowing principle that no more than one-third of the grass blade's height should be removed in a single cut, regardless of how long it's been since the last mow. It links height and frequency into one decision.

**In use:** "We're not on a weekly schedule at this property — we mow when it hits 5.25 inches, whichever day that is, because the target's 3.5."

**Common misuse:** treating mowing as a calendar task ("Tuesdays are mowing day") independent of how much the grass actually grew, which routinely violates the rule in fast-growth weeks and wastes a visit in slow ones.

## Scalping

Cutting turf below its species' minimum healthy height, exposing stem tissue (or bare soil) instead of leaf blade, causing a stress injury distinct from a merely short cut.

**In use:** "That's not just short, that's scalped — you can see stem, not blade, and it'll be three weeks before this recovers."

**Common misuse:** using "scalping" loosely for any low cut, including intentional low final-season cuts on warm-season turf before dormancy, which is a deliberate cultural practice, not an injury.

## Thatch vs. clippings/mat

**Thatch** is the layer of living and dead organic material (stems, roots, stolons) that accumulates between the soil surface and the green leaf canopy. **Mat** is a compacted thatch-plus-soil layer. **Clippings** are the cut leaf blades from mowing.

**In use:** "Thatch is over half an inch on this Bermuda — that's a dethatching job, not something more nitrogen fixes."

**Common misuse:** blaming grass clippings for thatch buildup. Mulched clippings from a properly mowed lawn (1/3 rule observed) are mostly water and decompose quickly; thatch builds from stem and root tissue, not surface clippings.

## Evapotranspiration (ET)

The combined water loss from soil evaporation and plant transpiration, used as the basis for irrigation scheduling instead of a fixed calendar. Typical turf ET need is roughly 1-1.5 inches per week including rainfall, varying with heat, wind, and species.

**In use:** "ET's been running high this week with the heat — bump the irrigation deficit calc, don't just leave the timer where it was in May."

**Common misuse:** setting an irrigation controller once at installation and never adjusting it against actual weather, which over-waters in cool/rainy stretches and under-waters in heat spikes.

## Leaf wetness duration

The number of continuous hours turf foliage stays wet from dew, rain, or irrigation. Most fungal turf diseases need a minimum wetness window (commonly cited around 10-12 hours) to establish and spread.

**In use:** "Moving the start time from 9 p.m. to 5 a.m. cuts leaf wetness duration almost in half — that's the actual fix, not a fungicide."

**Common misuse:** assuming watering timing doesn't matter as long as the weekly total is correct — the same volume of water applied at night versus early morning produces very different disease risk.

## Cultural control vs. chemical control

**Cultural control** adjusts mowing height, irrigation timing, fertility, or airflow to make conditions unfavorable for a pest or disease. **Chemical control** applies a pesticide, fungicide, or herbicide to kill or suppress it directly, and in most jurisdictions requires a licensed applicator above general-use products.

**In use:** "Coverage is under 10% and the cultural fix hasn't had time to work yet — hold the fungicide call until the recheck."

**Common misuse:** reaching for a chemical treatment as the first response to any visible problem, before ruling out a mowing- or irrigation-timing fix that costs nothing and doesn't require a licensed applicator.

## Precipitation rate

The rate, in inches per hour, at which a specific irrigation head actually delivers water — measured, not assumed from the head's model or the manufacturer's generic spec sheet.

**In use:** "This zone's spray heads measured 1.5 in/hr in the catch-cup test, not the 1.0 the spec sheet says — recalculate the run-time off the measured number."

**Common misuse:** setting irrigation run-time in minutes without ever measuring the actual precipitation rate, so two zones with the same minutes setting deliver very different amounts of water.

## Grub threshold

The population density of white grubs per square foot above which economic/aesthetic damage becomes likely enough to justify treatment — commonly cited around 8-10 grubs/sq ft, though it varies by turf species and tolerance for damage.

**In use:** "We counted 4 grubs in the sample — below threshold, note it and recheck in two weeks instead of treating today."

**Common misuse:** applying preventive grub treatment on every property every year regardless of actual count, which spends product and money on populations the lawn would tolerate or outgrow.

## Warm-season vs. cool-season turfgrass

**Cool-season** species (fescue, bluegrass, ryegrass) grow fastest in spring and fall and go semi-dormant in summer heat. **Warm-season** species (Bermuda, zoysia, St. Augustine, centipede) grow fastest in summer heat and go dormant after frost — the opposite calendar.

**In use:** "This is a transition-zone property — check which species is actually planted before you copy the fertilizer schedule from the last stop."

**Common misuse:** applying one species type's mowing height, fertilization timing, or irrigation schedule to the other, which stresses the grass during what is actually its dormant or slow-growth season.

## Chlorosis

Yellowing of turf or plant tissue from a nutrient deficiency (commonly iron or nitrogen) or a pH/uptake problem, distinct in cause and fix from disease-driven yellowing or drought stress.

**In use:** "That's iron chlorosis from the high soil pH here, not a fungus — a foliar iron application clears it faster than guessing at a fungicide."

**Common misuse:** assuming any yellow patch is disease and skipping a soil test or pH check that would point to a nutrient or pH cause instead.

## Girdling ("mower blight")

Repeated mechanical injury to a tree's bark and cambium at the base — from string trimmer line or mower deck contact — that accumulates over seasons into a ring of damage that can eventually cut off the tree's vascular flow.

**In use:** "That canopy dieback traces back to three seasons of trimmer contact at the trunk — put mulch rings in before you touch it with anything else."

**Common misuse:** treating a single season's bark scuffing as cosmetic and not tracking it as a cumulative injury that worsens with every visit the guard/mulch ring isn't in place.

## Broadcast vs. drop spreader

A **broadcast (rotary) spreader** throws granular product in a wide arc, faster but less precise at edges. A **drop spreader** releases product straight down in a narrow, precise band, slower but with no overthrow onto beds or hardscape.

**In use:** "Use the drop spreader along the bed line so we don't throw fertilizer into the mulch, then switch to broadcast for the open lawn."

**Common misuse:** using a broadcast spreader everywhere for speed, including property edges next to beds, storm drains, or water features where overthrow becomes runoff and waste.

## Root zone moisture vs. surface moisture

**Surface moisture** is what's visible or felt in the top inch of soil. **Root zone moisture** is the moisture at the depth (typically 4-6 inches for turf) where the active root system actually draws water.

**In use:** "Surface looks dry but the probe's coming up moist at 4 inches — this isn't a watering problem."

**Common misuse:** judging irrigation adequacy by how the surface looks or feels, which can be dry from heat/wind evaporation while the root zone underneath is still adequately moist, or the reverse.
