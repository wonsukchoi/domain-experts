# Playbook

Filled operational references a crew member or lead actually runs on-site, with real thresholds and worked numbers — starting points to adapt per property, not scripts.

## 1. Mowing height and frequency by species

Target height sets the 1/3-rule trigger height (target × 1.5) — mow when growth reaches the trigger, not on a fixed calendar day.

| Species | Season type | Target height (in) | 1/3-rule trigger (in) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Kentucky bluegrass | Cool-season | 2.5-3.5 | 3.75-5.25 |
| Tall fescue | Cool-season | 3-4 (raise to 3.5-4 in summer heat stress) | 4.5-6 |
| Perennial ryegrass | Cool-season | 1.5-2.5 | 2.25-3.75 |
| Bermudagrass (common) | Warm-season | 1-2 | 1.5-3 |
| Hybrid Bermuda | Warm-season | 0.5-1.5 | 0.75-2.25 |
| Zoysia | Warm-season | 1-2 | 1.5-3 |
| St. Augustine | Warm-season | 2.5-4 (shade-tolerant, prefers taller) | 3.75-6 |
| Centipede | Warm-season | 1-2 | 1.5-3 |

**Worked example:** A St. Augustine lawn kept at 3 inches has a trigger height of 4.5 inches. In peak summer growth (Gulf Coast, June), that lawn can hit 4.5 inches in 4-5 days; in October slowdown, the same lawn might take 12-14 days. A fixed "every 7 days" schedule over-cuts in June and under-cuts in October — both violate the rule in opposite directions.

**Seasonal note:** cool-season species grow fastest in spring/fall and go semi-dormant in summer heat; warm-season species do the opposite, growing fastest in summer heat and going dormant/brown after first frost. Applying one calendar to the wrong species type means mowing (and fertilizing) it hardest during its stress season.

## 2. Irrigation scheduling from ET

Target: 1-1.5 inches of water per week, including rainfall, for actively growing turf (less for dormant warm-season turf in winter).

**Step 1 — measure actual precipitation rate.** Set out 4-6 straight-sided catch cups in a zone, run the zone 15 minutes, measure depth collected, multiply by 4 to get in/hr. Typical spray heads: ~1.5 in/hr. Typical rotor heads: ~0.5-0.75 in/hr. Never assume — measured rates on the same model head commonly vary ±30% by pressure and nozzle wear.

**Step 2 — convert weekly target to run-time.**

```
Weekly target (in) − rainfall this week (in) = irrigation deficit (in)
Irrigation deficit (in) ÷ precipitation rate (in/hr) = total weekly run-time (hr)
Total weekly run-time ÷ number of sessions = minutes per session
```

**Worked example:** Target 1.25 in/week, 0.25 in rain fell this week, spray-head zone measured at 1.5 in/hr, split across 2 sessions:
- Deficit = 1.25 − 0.25 = 1.0 in
- Run-time = 1.0 ÷ 1.5 = 0.667 hr = 40 minutes total
- Per session = 40 ÷ 2 = 20 minutes/session, twice a week

**Step 3 — timing.** Start no later than early morning (roughly 4-6 a.m.) so the canopy is dry by mid-morning. Evening/night starts routinely push leaf-wetness duration past 10-12 hours, the range that favors fungal disease (Brown Patch, dollar spot) in humid climates.

**Step 4 — slope/soil adjustment.** On slopes or clay soil where runoff starts before the target depth infiltrates, split the session into 2-3 shorter cycles with a 30-60 minute soak gap ("cycle and soak") rather than one long run.

## 3. Fertilization calendar (N-P-K)

Target total nitrogen per year, split across applications of roughly 0.75-1 lb N/1,000 sq ft each so no single application exceeds ~1 lb N/1,000 sq ft (higher risk of burn and flush growth above that).

| Species type | Total N/year (lb per 1,000 sq ft) | Timing |
|---|---|---|
| Cool-season (fescue, bluegrass) | 2-4 | Majority in fall (Sept-Nov); light spring application; avoid heavy summer N during heat stress |
| Warm-season (Bermuda, zoysia, St. Augustine) | 3-5 | Late spring through summer, during active growth; stop 6-8 weeks before first expected frost |

**Spreader calibration (catch-pan test), worked example:** Label rate calls for 1 lb N/1,000 sq ft using a 32-0-4 fertilizer (32% N by weight). Bag needed per 1,000 sq ft = 1 ÷ 0.32 = 3.125 lb. Run the spreader at a test setting over a measured 1,000 sq ft, catch and weigh the output: if it delivers 2.5 lb instead of 3.125 lb, the setting is under-applying by 20% — open the gate and retest before running the property, rather than trusting the dial's printed number.

## 4. Turf decline diagnostic sequence (field order)

1. Map pattern: whole-lawn / patchy / zone-bounded / sun-shade-bounded.
2. Soil probe at 4-6 in — moist rules out drought as primary driver.
3. Tug test — loose lift + grub count ≥8-10/sq ft → grub damage; firm hold → continue.
4. Early-morning visual check for disease signatures (smoke-ring margins, mycelium, lesion pattern).
5. Check mowing height/frequency and blade condition against species target.
6. Cross-check against 2-week weather/irrigation log.
7. Route fix: cultural (same-visit) vs. licensed-applicator escalation (document + photo + coverage %).

## 5. Single-crew daily task sequence

Fixed order, because reversing it recontaminates finished work:

1. **Arrival hazard scan** (2-3 min) — debris, holes, irrigation heads up, pets/people in yard, sprinkler zones running.
2. **Mow** — full property, correct height per species/season.
3. **Trim** — string trimmer around obstacles the mower flagged (fences, trees, beds); mulch rings around tree trunks kept clear of trimmer contact.
4. **Edge** — walks, driveways, bed lines.
5. **Blow** — clear clippings and debris off hardscape and beds, last, so nothing gets tracked back onto cleaned surfaces.
6. **Treatment/inspection pass** (as scheduled) — fertilizer/pesticide application by licensed applicator, or the diagnostic sequence above if a problem was flagged.
7. **Departure check** — equipment off property, gates closed, note left for anything escalated.

**NALP production-rate reference point:** a 2-person crew with a 48-60 inch zero-turn commonly mows and blows a 1/4-1/3 acre residential property in 20-30 minutes; a 1/2 acre-plus property or one requiring significant trim/edge work runs 45-60+ minutes. Route planning that doesn't leave room for a diagnostic stop on a flagged property is the most common cause of a missed problem turning into a callback.
