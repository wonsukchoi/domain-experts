# Vocabulary

Terms generalists conflate or use loosely that a quarry rock splitter keeps sharply separate.

### Rift

The easiest natural splitting direction in a stone, set by its crystalline or bedding structure — most discussed in granite quarrying, where it's the direction a block is preferentially oriented to split along.

**In use:** "Run the line with the rift on this face — we're not fighting the stone on this one."

**Common misuse:** treating "rift" as a synonym for "grain" or "any natural crack," when it specifically names the single easiest of the three directional splitting tendencies (rift, grain, hard way).

### Grain

The second-easiest natural splitting direction in a stone, typically running roughly perpendicular to the rift.

**In use:** "That face splits fine on the grain, just tighten the hole spacing from what we'd run on rift."

**Common misuse:** using "grain" loosely to mean "any visible line in the stone," rather than the specific second-order splitting direction it names relative to rift and hard way.

### Hard way

The most resistant of a stone's three directional splitting tendencies, roughly perpendicular to both rift and grain.

**In use:** "That east face is hard way past the nine-foot mark — route it to the wire saw, don't hand-wedge it."

**Common misuse:** treating a hard-way cut as simply "a harder version of a normal split" solvable by driving wedges harder, rather than as a direction where hand splitting has a materially higher failure rate regardless of driving force.

### Plug and feather

A three-piece splitting tool set — one tapered plug (wedge) and two feathers (shims) — placed in a drilled hole so that driving the plug spreads the feathers and puts the surrounding rock in tension along a marked line.

**In use:** "Set the plugs and feathers the full length of the line before you start driving — don't drive as you go."

**Common misuse:** calling any wedge-and-hammer splitting technique "plug and feather" even when the tool set or hole pattern used doesn't match the specific three-piece method.

### Sounding

Tapping a stone face with a hammer and listening to the return tone to detect internal seams or fractures not visible on the surface.

**In use:** "That whole section rang dead when I sounded it — there's a seam under there somewhere."

**Common misuse:** skipping sounding on a face that "looks solid," treating visual inspection alone as sufficient to rule out an internal seam.

### Seam

A plane of weakness or discontinuity running through a stone mass, often invisible or barely visible at the surface, that will cause an uncontrolled or off-line fracture if a split line crosses it.

**In use:** "Mark the block a foot shorter — there's a seam running right through where the east face would land."

**Common misuse:** using "seam" interchangeably with any visible crack or color line, when the term specifically implies a structural weakness that affects splitting behavior, not just appearance.

### Sound rock vs. seamy rock

**Sound rock** is stone free of significant internal seams or fractures, splitting predictably along its rift/grain. **Seamy rock** has enough internal discontinuity that predictable splitting isn't guaranteed even along the "correct" direction.

**In use:** "This ledge has run seamy the last two benches — tighten hole spacing and sound every line before marking."

**Common misuse:** assuming a quarry's rock quality is uniform across the whole ledge, rather than checking each new bench or face independently.

### Channeling

Cutting a narrow, continuous slot into a rock mass (historically with a channeling machine, now more often a jackhammer or wire saw) to free a large section from the surrounding ledge, as distinct from plug-and-feather, which splits along a line of discrete points rather than a continuous cut.

**In use:** "Channel the north and west sides first, then we can plug-and-feather the block free from the mass."

**Common misuse:** using "channeling" to describe any drilling operation, when it specifically means cutting a continuous separating slot rather than a spaced hole line.

### Block

A quarried piece of dimension stone cut to specified rough dimensions for sale or further processing, as distinct from rubble, spall, or aggregate-grade waste stone produced when a split fails or the rock doesn't meet block quality.

**In use:** "That one didn't come off clean — it's rubble now, not a block, log it as a loss."

**Common misuse:** calling any freed piece of stone a "block" regardless of whether it met the dimensional and quality bar to be sold or processed as dimension stone.

### Quarry sap

Residual natural moisture in freshly quarried stone before it has air-dried ("seasoned"); many stones split and work measurably easier while sap is present.

**In use:** "Get this block split before it seasons out — it'll fight us more once the sap's gone."

**Common misuse:** assuming a stone's splitting difficulty is fixed and ignoring the time-since-quarrying factor when comparing how "hard" two blocks were to split.

### Presplit blasting

A blast technique used at some quarries to create a fracture plane along a bench boundary before or instead of production blasting, producing a rough-isolated mass that still requires hand or mechanical finishing to reach block dimensions.

**In use:** "Presplit gave us the bench boundary clean, but every block on it still needs its own rift/grain read before we mark a line."

**Common misuse:** treating a presplit-blasted face as already reduced to finished block geometry, skipping the grain-reading and sounding steps that still apply to the finish cuts.

### Powder factor

The ratio of explosive charge weight to rock volume broken in a blast, used in blast design at quarries that use drilling and blasting to remove rock mass.

**In use:** "That's a blast-crew number, not ours — powder factor decides how the mass gets isolated, not how we split a block off it."

**Common misuse:** conflating powder factor (a blast-design metric) with anything related to hand or mechanical splitting technique, which is a separate discipline downstream of any blasting.

### Hand-arm vibration action level / exposure limit value

The two thresholds (2.5 m/s² and 5.0 m/s² as an 8-hour A(8) figure under ACGIH/EU guidance) that define when hand-arm vibration exposure from tools like pneumatic drills requires additional controls (action level) or must not be exceeded (exposure limit value).

**In use:** "We're past the action level on that drill by lunch most days — we need to rotate operators, not just note it."

**Common misuse:** treating the action level as merely advisory or "the number that matters is the exposure limit value," when the action level is the trigger for adding controls before exposure reaches the harder limit.

### Guillotine splitter

A hydraulic machine that applies a straight-line splitting force across a stone's full width in one stroke, used for mechanized block or slab splitting at higher volume than hand plug-and-feather supports.

**In use:** "Once we're past twenty blocks a day on this spec, it's cheaper to run the guillotine than keep hand-wedging."

**Common misuse:** treating the guillotine splitter as a universal replacement for hand splitting, when its economics only favor it once setup time is amortized across a high enough repeat-cut volume.
