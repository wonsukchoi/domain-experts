# Red flags

Smell tests a quarry rock splitter or crew lead catches before a line is drilled or a wedge is driven — once the wedges go in, most of these become a lost block instead of a re-mark.

### Split line marked without a sounding pass or grain check on that specific face

**Usually means:** the layout was copied from the previous block on this face, or from the order sheet's dimensions alone, without re-confirming rift/grain/hard-way direction or checking for a new seam.

**First question:** "Has this exact line been sounded and grain-checked today, or are we assuming it's the same as the last block off this face?"

**Data to pull:** the face's rift/grain orientation log, the sounding pass results for the marked line, the previous block's actual split outcome on this face.

### A cut face on the marked block lands on the hard way with hand plug-and-feather selected as the method

**Usually means:** the block spec was drawn from customer dimensions without checking splitting direction, and the default hand method was chosen out of habit rather than routed to a saw.

**First question:** "Does any face on this block cross the hard way, and if so, why is this going to hand wedges instead of the wire saw or chop saw?"

**Data to pull:** the face's rift/grain/hard-way map, this ledge's logged hand-split clean rate for hard-way lines, wire/chop saw availability and setup time.

### Plug-and-feather hole spacing wider than 8 in on coarse-grained or seamy rock

**Usually means:** the spacing was set from a standard production table without adjusting for this specific rock's grain coarseness or seam history.

**First question:** "Is this rock's grain fine enough for standard 8 in spacing, or has this face shown seams/stalled cracks at that spacing before?"

**Data to pull:** grain-size assessment for the current lot, any logged stalled-crack incidents on this face at the current spacing.

### One wedge in a line driven to full seat before its neighbors are struck

**Usually means:** a crew member is working fast rather than working the line evenly, or hasn't been trained on sequential light-pass technique.

**First question:** "Are the wedges being walked down the line in even passes, or is one being driven home before the rest are started?"

**Data to pull:** direct observation of the driving sequence, any history of off-line jumps on this crew's cuts.

### A crack visibly jumps off the marked line mid-split

**Usually means:** either the line crossed a seam that wasn't caught in sounding, or an uneven driving sequence concentrated stress at one point on the line.

**First question:** "Did the jump happen near a hole that was driven out of sequence, or does it track toward a spot that read dull on the sounding pass?"

**Data to pull:** the sounding log for the line, the driving sequence as executed, a photo of the jump point relative to the marked line.

### Pneumatic drill trigger time not tracked against shift length

**Usually means:** hand-arm vibration exposure is being managed by "feel" or by total hours on the job rather than actual tool-on trigger time against the tool's rated vibration figure.

**First question:** "What's today's actual trigger time on this drill, and what's its declared vibration figure — does that combination stay under the 5.0 m/s² exposure limit value for an 8-hour day?"

**Data to pull:** logged trigger time, the specific drill's manufacturer vibration rating, maintenance record (a poorly maintained drill's actual vibration output can exceed its rated figure).

### Dry drilling on a face without water suppression during an extended run

**Usually means:** water suppression wasn't set up or was skipped for a short job that turned into a long one, or the equipment is unavailable/broken.

**First question:** "Is dust suppression running on this hole line, and if not, what's the plan to keep respirable silica exposure under the action level?"

**Data to pull:** silica exposure monitoring results if available, water-suppression equipment status, duration of the dry-drilling period.

### A block's as-split weight comes in noticeably different from its computed spec weight at loadout

**Usually means:** the block didn't split exactly on the marked plane (over- or under-cut), or the density assumption used to plan the cut doesn't match this specific lot's rock.

**First question:** "Is the discrepancy from an off-line split, or does this lot's rock run a different density than what the spec math assumed?"

**Data to pull:** scale weight at loadout, as-split dimension measurements, the density figure used in the original spec calculation.

### A block logged as a clean split but downgraded later in the yard for a hairline crack

**Usually means:** a hairline crack from a seam or an uneven wedge pass wasn't visible at the loadout inspection and only opened up or became visible after handling/transport.

**First question:** "Was this block's final-face inspection done in raking light and after the block had settled, or was it logged clean immediately off the split?"

**Data to pull:** the loadout inspection record, transport/handling history between quarry and yard, comparison against this face's seam map.

### Crew defaulting every hard-way-adjacent block to a downsized spec without checking mechanized-cutting availability

**Usually means:** an earlier hard-way hand-split failure taught the crew to avoid hard-way faces entirely, rather than to route them to a saw.

**First question:** "Is this block being downsized because the hard-way face genuinely isn't achievable, or because hand wedging is being ruled out without checking whether the wire saw is available?"

**Data to pull:** wire/chop saw schedule and availability, the actual expected-yield comparison (hand-downsized vs mechanized-as-spec'd) for this block.
