# Vocabulary

Terms generalists blur that a pit boss keeps sharply separate. Each: definition, how a practitioner actually uses it, and the common misuse.

## Fill vs. credit

A **fill** moves chips from the cage to the table (replenishing inventory, usually because players are winning). A **credit** moves chips from the table back to the cage (removing excess inventory, usually because the table is winning). Both require an independently signed slip; neither is optional paperwork.

*Practitioner usage:* "Table's on its third fill this session with only one credit back — that ratio's the flag, not the dollar total."

*Common misuse:* using "fill" loosely for any chip movement, or treating a credit as just "the opposite of a fill" rather than its own control point requiring its own signature.

## Drop vs. win vs. hold percentage

**Drop** is the total cash (and marker/front-money equivalents) that goes into the table's drop box — the raw buy-in volume. **Win** is what the house actually keeps after paying out winners, net of fills and credits. **Hold percentage** is win ÷ drop, the standard efficiency metric for a table over a period.

*Practitioner usage:* "Drop's up 20% this month but hold's flat at 14% — volume's growing, the game itself isn't performing any differently."

*Common misuse:* citing drop as if it were revenue (it's turnover, not the house's take), or reacting to a single shift's hold percentage as a real signal when it's a noisy short-window number.

## Theoretical win (theo) vs. actual win

**Theoretical win** is the statistically expected house result for a player's session — average bet × decisions per hour × time played × house edge. **Actual win** is what really happened at the table for that player, which can sit far from theo over any single session due to variance.

*Practitioner usage:* "His theo for that session was $180, he actually won $2,400 — rate the comp off the $180, the $2,400 is variance, not his real value."

*Common misuse:* comping or rating a player off actual results from one session, or off buy-in size, instead of the theo calculation.

## Marker vs. front money vs. credit line

A **marker** is a signed IOU drawn against an approved credit line, redeemable at the table for chips. **Front money** is cash the patron has already deposited with the cage, available to draw against with no credit risk to the house. A **credit line** is the pre-approved limit the credit department has extended to a patron, which markers draw down against.

*Practitioner usage:* "He's got $10,000 in front money, no marker needed — draw against that first before anyone talks about extending his line."

*Common misuse:* treating a marker request as routine table service rather than a credit decision that belongs to the credit department above a pit boss's authority.

## MTL vs. CTR (Title 31)

The **MTL (Multiple Transaction Log)** is an internal record, kept at a lower property-set threshold (commonly $2,500–$3,000), used to track and aggregate a patron's currency transactions across a gaming day. The **CTR (Currency Transaction Report)** is the federal filing required under Title 31 / the Bank Secrecy Act once aggregated cash-in or cash-out for one patron exceeds $10,000 in a gaming day.

*Practitioner usage:* "He's logged three MTL entries today totaling $8,700 — not over the CTR line yet, but log the next one and watch for structuring."

*Common misuse:* treating the MTL threshold as the reporting requirement (it isn't — it's the internal tracking floor that feeds the $10,000 CTR aggregation) or assuming a single transaction under $10,000 clears a patron regardless of same-day pattern.

## Past-posting vs. pinching

**Past-posting** is adding a bet after the outcome is already known (e.g., sliding chips onto a winning number after the ball drops). **Pinching** is removing chips from a losing bet before it's paid out or collected. Both are forms of bet manipulation, but they're opposite directions and caught differently — past-posting is a hand-speed/camera-angle catch, pinching is a collection-sequence catch.

*Practitioner usage:* "Dealer called a hold on 12 — looked like a past-post on the outside bet, not a pinch, get surveillance on the outcome-to-bet timing."

*Common misuse:* using either term as a generic label for "cheating" without specifying the direction, which sends surveillance looking at the wrong part of the footage.

## Cut card vs. shuffle tracking

The **cut card** is the marker card placed in the shoe to signal when the remaining cards are too few to deal another round, forcing a reshuffle. **Shuffle tracking** is an advantage-play technique where a skilled player tracks clumps of favorable cards through an imperfect shuffle to bias their bets — a real but far less common threat than basic procedural drift.

*Practitioner usage:* "Cut-card placement was deeper than house standard tonight — check whether that's a training gap before assuming anyone's tracking the shoe."

*Common misuse:* jumping to "shuffle tracking" as the explanation for a losing shoe when a shallow or inconsistent cut-card placement — a procedural issue — is the far more likely and far more common cause.

## Toke vs. comp

A **toke** is a tip — cash or a chip given directly to a dealer by a player, pooled and distributed per the property's toke policy. A **comp** is a complimentary good or service (room, meal, show) extended by the house based on a player's rated value, unrelated to tipping.

*Practitioner usage:* "That's a toke, goes in the box — comps route through the rating, not through what a player hands the dealer."

*Common misuse:* conflating the two when a player asks "can I just tip my way to a better room" — tokes and comp decisions are separate systems with separate approval chains.

## Floor authority / signing limit

The dollar (and pattern) threshold up to which a pit boss can approve a fill, credit, or other transaction without a shift-manager co-sign. Distinct from a *credit line*, which is patron-specific and set by the credit department.

*Practitioner usage:* "That's inside my signing limit dollar-wise, but it's the third max fill this session — pattern trips it up to the shift manager regardless."

*Common misuse:* treating the dollar threshold as the only trigger for escalation and missing the pattern-based triggers (repeated max requests, no surveillance note) that also require sign-off.

## Rating vs. average bet

A player's **rating** is the full output of the theoretical-win calculation used for comp decisions — average bet, duration, and game combined. **Average bet** is just one input: the typical wager size observed per hand, not the full picture of the player's value.

*Practitioner usage:* "His average bet's high, but he's only played 40 minutes — the rating comes out mid-tier once duration's factored in."

*Common misuse:* using "average bet" and "rating" interchangeably, which shortchanges a long-session grinder and over-values a short high-bet visit.

## Hole-carding

An advantage-play technique where a player catches a glimpse of the dealer's hole card due to a dealing-procedure flaw (angle, grip, or exposure during the peek for blackjack), and adjusts play accordingly — a procedural game-protection issue traced to dealing mechanics, not a hidden device or collusion.

*Practitioner usage:* "That losing streak lines up with one specific dealer's peek angle — get them retrained on the no-peek procedure before assuming it's a systemic table problem."

*Common misuse:* assuming any unusually accurate player decision-making is card counting (a legal, non-collusive statistical strategy) when it's actually hole-carding (a procedural exposure) — the fix for each is completely different: retraining a dealer versus a legal counter-measure decision by management.
