# Vocabulary

Terms generalists conflate or use loosely that room attendants and housekeeping supervisors keep sharply separate.

### Credit (housekeeping credit)

A weighted unit of workload used to build a fair quota, distinct from a raw room count — a checkout typically counts as 1.0 credit, a stay-over as 0.5, a suite or deep-clean as 1.5–2.0.

**In use:** "She's only got 11 rooms today but it's 14 credits — that's not a light floor."

**Common misuse:** Comparing attendants or floors by room count alone ("he did 16 rooms, she did 13, he worked harder") without checking the checkout/stay-over mix, which can make a lighter room-count day the heavier actual workload.

### Par (linen/amenity par level)

The target stock quantity a cart or supply closet should hold, expressed as a multiple of what's in active use — e.g., "3 par" on sheets means one set on the bed, one in reserve, one cycling through laundry.

**In use:** "We're down to 1 par on king sheets — restock before the floor opens or we'll run short by 11am."

**Common misuse:** Treating "par" as just "how many we have" rather than a target tied to turnover rate; a cart at par for a light stay-over day will run short on a heavy checkout day with the same room count.

### Turn / turning a room

Fully resetting a checkout room to sellable, guest-ready condition, as distinct from a stay-over refresh — "turning" implies the complete sequence, not a partial clean.

**In use:** "We turned 322 in 25 minutes for the early check-in."

**Common misuse:** Using "turn" for a stay-over service, which understates the work difference — a turn is roughly double a stay-over's time budget and a different sequence, not the same sequence done faster.

### Stay-over vs. checkout vs. early make-up

**Stay-over** is a room where the guest is continuing their stay; **checkout** is a room the departing guest has vacated and that must be fully reset; **early make-up** is a stay-over room serviced ahead of the normal order because the guest requested a specific time or is departing later that day.

**In use:** "Put 210 on early make-up — guest has a 9am call and doesn't want service mid-meeting."

**Common misuse:** Treating "early make-up" as a synonym for "priority" broadly — it specifically means a scheduling reorder for guest convenience, not an escalation like a biohazard or maintenance issue.

### DND (Do Not Disturb) vs. "do not service"

**DND** is a guest-controlled signal (sign or digital flag) that can be posted or removed at any time and defaults to "do not enter." **"Do not service"** is a standing preference logged at check-in or by request (e.g., environmentally-conscious guests skipping daily service) that doesn't carry the same urgency escalation as an aging DND.

**In use:** "That's a logged do-not-service, not a DND — no welfare check needed, just confirm it's still current at the 3-day mark."

**Common misuse:** Escalating every do-not-service note as if it were an unexplained DND, or conversely ignoring a genuinely aging DND because "the guest probably just doesn't want service."

### Room status codes (VD / OC / OD / OOO)

Property management system shorthand: **VD** (vacant dirty, needs cleaning before resale), **OC** (occupied clean, guest staying, already serviced), **OD** (occupied dirty, guest staying, needs service), **OOO** (out of order, not sellable regardless of cleanliness).

**In use:** "Board shows six VDs on my floor before I even start on stay-overs."

**Common misuse:** Reading "dirty" in these codes as a judgment about guest tidiness rather than a housekeeping-workflow status — an OD room can be immaculate and still coded "dirty" simply because service hasn't run yet.

### Cross-contamination (color-coded cloth system)

The transfer of bacteria or contaminants between surfaces via a shared cleaning cloth or tool — controlled by assigning cloth colors to fixed zones (bathroom, glass, general surface) that never cross.

**In use:** "Inspection failed on 415 — cloth colors were mixed, that's a cross-contamination flag, not just a missed spot."

**Common misuse:** Treating the color system as a labeling convenience rather than a contamination control; swapping cloths between zones to save a cart trip defeats the entire purpose even if the room looks visually clean afterward.

### Biohazard / bloodborne pathogen incident

Any find involving blood, bodily fluids, or a sharps object (needle, syringe, lancet) that triggers a fixed OSHA-aligned containment and reporting sequence, distinct from a routine bathroom or spill cleanup.

**In use:** "That's a bloodborne pathogen incident, not a stain — glove up, contain, and call it in before you touch anything else."

**Common misuse:** Downplaying a sharps find as "just a needle in the trash" and disposing of it in a regular liner instead of the sharps container, which is both a safety violation and an unreported-incident gap.

### Chain of custody (lost and found)

The documented, unbroken record of who found an item, when, where, and how it was stored and released — what establishes that an item wasn't taken rather than turned in.

**In use:** "Log it before you bag it — chain of custody starts at the find, not when it reaches the safe."

**Common misuse:** Treating logging as paperwork that can happen "later" or "when there's time," which is precisely the gap that turns a legitimate find into an unprovable theft dispute.

### Inspection score / QA audit

A supervisor's scored walkthrough of a completed room against a defect checklist with category-weighted deductions, distinct from a subjective "looks clean" judgment.

**In use:** "It's a 92 — passed, but the bathroom deduction is the one to watch, that's the expensive category."

**Common misuse:** Treating the aggregate score as the whole signal; two rooms scoring 88 can mean very different things depending on which defect category the points came off of.

### Deep-clean / project clean

A scheduled, rotation-based intensive clean (mattress rotation, vent dusting, wall spot-cleaning) distinct from a checkout turn, typically run quarterly per room rather than triggered by a single complaint.

**In use:** "312 is due for project clean this month regardless of how it looks — it's been eleven weeks."

**Common misuse:** Treating a deep-clean as something that only happens reactively after a guest complaint, rather than a scheduled maintenance rhythm that prevents the complaint in the first place.
