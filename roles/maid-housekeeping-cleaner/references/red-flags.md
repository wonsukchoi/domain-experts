# Red flags

Smell tests a floor supervisor or executive housekeeper catches early. Each: what it usually means, the first question to ask, and the data to pull.

### Do Not Disturb sign up past 24–48 hours with no guest contact

- **Usually means:** most often a guest who values privacy and left the sign up out of habit; less often a genuine welfare concern (medical event, or a guest who checked out through a side entrance without returning the key).
- **First question:** "When was the last confirmed guest sighting or key-card access on this room?"
- **Data to pull:** key-card access log for the room, front-desk check-in date and expected checkout, any "do not service" note left at check-in versus a DND sign put up mid-stay.

### Room attendant's completed credits sustained below quota for 3+ consecutive shifts

- **Usually means:** either a genuinely heavier floor assignment (more suites, more back-to-back checkouts) that was never rebalanced, or a specific step in the sequence taking longer than budgeted that training would fix.
- **First question:** "Is this attendant's floor assignment credit-weighted the same as everyone else's, or just room-count the same?"
- **Data to pull:** credit totals by attendant by day for two weeks, floor/room-type mix per attendant, time-stamped PMS status changes per room to see which step of the sequence is slow.

### Inspection score drop concentrated in one defect category (e.g., bathroom, 3+ audits running)

- **Usually means:** a specific skill or sequence step is being skipped under time pressure, not a general quality decline — attendants under quota pressure sacrifice categories in a predictable order (cosmetic first, then dust, rarely bathroom unless badly behind).
- **First question:** "Pull the deduction category breakdown, not just the score — which category is failing?"
- **Data to pull:** last 5 scorecards for the attendant broken down by category, that attendant's average time-per-room versus budget, whether the failing category correlates with days they were running behind.

### Cart linen or amenity count short at shift end, no incident logged

- **Usually means:** either legitimate extra usage (a spill, a suite needing double amenities) that wasn't logged, or shrinkage/misplacement that will surface as tomorrow's under-stocked cart.
- **First question:** "Was there a logged reason for the shortfall, and does it match what's in the room-status notes?"
- **Data to pull:** cart restock log, room notes for the shift, comparison against the par-stocking table.

### Stay-over guest room shows no service for 2+ consecutive scheduled days, no DND logged

- **Usually means:** a guest preference for no service that was never documented, or — less often — a welfare concern that's being silently skipped rather than escalated.
- **First question:** "Did the guest verbally decline service, and is that noted anywhere, or has the attendant just been skipping it?"
- **Data to pull:** service log for the room, any front-desk or concierge note of guest preference, attendant's own notes.

### Found high-value item (jewelry, cash, electronics) logged by only one person

- **Usually means:** either a genuine time-pressure shortcut, or — rarely — the first step of an actual theft, since single-witness logging is exactly the gap a dishonest find would exploit.
- **First question:** "Who witnessed this item being bagged besides the finder?"
- **Data to pull:** lost-and-found log entry, cart assignment and timing for that room, whether a second attendant or supervisor was reachable at that time on that floor.

### Bloodborne pathogen or sharps incident resolved with no incident report filed

- **Usually means:** the attendant contained the item correctly but skipped the reporting step, often to avoid the time cost or a perceived overreaction — but an unreported exposure risk means no medical-evaluation offer was made and no pattern-tracking (e.g., which rooms/guests generate repeat sharps finds) is possible.
- **First question:** "Was this reported at the time, or only mentioned after the fact?"
- **Data to pull:** incident report log, radio/call log timestamps, cross-reference against which rooms had diabetic-guest amenity requests (a soft predictor, not a rule).

### Bed bug suspicion where the room was returned to inventory same-day without pest control inspection

- **Usually means:** pressure to keep occupancy up overrode the hold protocol — this is the single costliest shortcut in the job, since one skipped hold can seed an infestation across multiple rooms via cart linens.
- **First question:** "Who cleared this room back to sellable status, and was pest control actually on-site?"
- **Data to pull:** maintenance-hold log, pest control visit log, which cart and which other rooms that attendant serviced the same day.

### A single attendant's found-item log is unusually long or unusually empty compared to peers

- **Usually means:** either genuinely different room mix (more late-checkout business travelers who leave chargers, say), or — at either extreme — a reporting behavior problem: over-logging trivial items to look scrupulous, or under-logging real finds.
- **First question:** "Does this attendant's found-item rate track with room type and guest segment, or is it flat regardless of floor assignment?"
- **Data to pull:** found-item log by attendant over a month, floor/room-type assignment history, guest segment mix by floor.
