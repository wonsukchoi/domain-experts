# Compensation, benefits, and job analysis specialist working vocabulary

Terms this role uses precisely and generalists conflate. Format: definition → how a practitioner says it → common misuse.

**Compa-ratio** — An individual's base salary divided by their range's midpoint, expressed as a percentage; the standard single-number read on where someone sits relative to the pay structure for their job.
In use: "His compa-ratio is 87% — he's in the developing zone, which is expected at 8 months, but I'd want to see it moving toward 95%+ by his second review cycle."
Misuse: treating a low compa-ratio as automatically a problem regardless of tenure — it's the expected position for someone new to the role, and only becomes a flag when tenure is long and the ratio hasn't moved.

**Range penetration** — Where a salary falls within the full min-to-max range, expressed as a percentage: (salary − min) ÷ (max − min). Distinct from compa-ratio, which anchors to the midpoint specifically.
In use: "Compa-ratio and range penetration usually move together, but after a structure refresh they can diverge — check both before concluding someone's over- or under-paid."
Misuse: using compa-ratio and range penetration interchangeably; they answer slightly different questions and a band with an asymmetric spread (min further from mid than max is) makes them diverge more than people expect.

**Market pricing (slotting) vs. job evaluation (point-factor)** — Market pricing directly matches a job to external survey data by job content and sets pay from the resulting percentile; job evaluation scores a job's internal compensable factors (Hay's know-how/problem-solving/accountability, or Mercer IPE's impact/communication/innovation/knowledge) into points, independent of any survey match.
In use: "This role has a clean Radford match, so we're slotting it — no need to run it through the full point-factor exercise."
Misuse: running full point-factor scoring on every job regardless of survey match quality, or slotting a genuinely novel role that has no clean external match and needs point-factor evaluation instead.

**Percentile philosophy (lead / lag / match)** — The organization's stated choice of which survey percentile (P25, P50, P60–65, P75) to target for a job family, distinct from the survey data itself; "the market" only becomes a pay target once this choice is applied.
In use: "The survey says P50 is $145K, but our philosophy for this family is P60 — the target midpoint is interpolated, not the raw P50 number."
Misuse: quoting a raw survey percentile as if it were the pay decision, without naming which percentile the org's philosophy actually calls for.

**Compensable factors** — The specific, defined attributes a point-factor job evaluation method scores to determine a job's grade — e.g., Hay's know-how, problem-solving, and accountability, or Mercer IPE's impact, communication, innovation, and knowledge — deliberately excluding the incumbent's identity or performance.
In use: "The compensable-factor score didn't change — the job's know-how requirement is identical to before, so the grade stays put even though the new hire is stronger than the last one."
Misuse: letting a strong incumbent's performance quietly raise the job's compensable-factor score, conflating job evaluation with individual performance calibration.

**Red-circle / green-circle** — Red-circled means an incumbent's pay sits above the range maximum (typically frozen from further base increases until the range catches up, but not cut); green-circled means pay sits below the range minimum (typically flagged as a priority for an increase, sometimes on an accelerated timeline).
In use: "Three people went red-circle when we moved the midpoint — no pay cuts, but no base increase for them until the new max clears their salary."
Misuse: assuming red-circle means a pay cut; the standard practice is a freeze on further increases, not a reduction, and treating it as a cut when communicating to the employee creates unnecessary alarm.

**Cohort (similarly-situated-employee-group) analysis vs. regression analysis** — Cohort analysis groups employees by job/level/location and compares mean or median pay across protected classes within each group, used when a cell has too few observations (rule of thumb: under ~30) for a reliable regression; regression analysis statistically controls for multiple compensable factors simultaneously across a larger population.
In use: "The Senior Director cohort only has 6 people — we're running a cohort comparison there, not a regression; the engineering IC population is big enough for the full regression model."
Misuse: forcing a regression onto a small cell and treating a noisy, low-powered coefficient as equivalent in reliability to a well-powered one from a larger cohort.

**Adjusted pay gap vs. unadjusted (raw) pay gap** — The raw pay gap is the unweighted average pay difference between groups with no controls applied; the adjusted pay gap is what remains after controlling for legitimate compensable factors (level, tenure, location, performance) — the number that actually matters for identifying discrimination risk.
In use: "The raw gap is 12%, but almost all of that is explained by level and tenure — the adjusted gap is 3.1% and that's the number we act on."
Misuse: leading with the raw gap in a leadership or public conversation without the adjusted figure, which either overstates or understates the actual risk depending on the population mix.

**Four-fifths rule** — An adverse-impact test from EEOC's Uniform Guidelines on Employee Selection Procedures: a selection rate for a protected group below 80% of the highest group's selection rate signals possible discrimination in hiring/promotion decisions. A selection-rate test, not a pay-equity test.
In use: "The four-fifths rule flags our promotion rates, not our pay — that's a separate analysis from the compensation regression."
Misuse: applying four-fifths-rule logic to a pay gap; it measures selection-rate disparity, not compensation disparity, and using it in a comp context signals the wrong methodology is being reached for.

**Loss ratio** — The percentage of premium dollars paid out in claims (claims PEPM ÷ premium PEPM) for an insured benefits plan; the core metric carriers use to justify a renewal increase, and the metric this role uses to decide whether to accept, re-shop, or model self-funding.
In use: "Loss ratio's been above 85% for two renewals running — time to get a self-funded feasibility model in front of the CFO before we just accept another 6% increase."
Misuse: evaluating a renewal increase purely on the percentage asked for, without checking whether the underlying loss ratio actually justifies it or whether the carrier is pricing in extra margin.

**PEPM (per employee per month)** — The standard unit for expressing benefits plan cost, normalized to a monthly per-covered-employee basis, used to compare premium cost, claims cost, and administrative fees on a common footing regardless of enrollment changes.
In use: "Premium PEPM went from $760 to $810 — that's the number to compare against claims PEPM, not the total annual premium dollar figure, which enrollment changes would distort."
Misuse: comparing total annual premium dollars year over year without normalizing to PEPM first, which conflates a headcount change with an actual cost-per-person change.

**Self-funded vs. fully insured, and stop-loss** — In a fully insured plan, the carrier bears claims risk in exchange for a fixed premium; in a self-funded plan, the employer bears claims risk directly (paying actual claims) and buys stop-loss insurance to cap exposure above a set attachment point per individual claim and in aggregate.
In use: "At 850 lives we're well past the size where self-funding usually starts favoring the employer — model the stop-loss cost at a $125K attachment point before deciding."
Misuse: recommending self-funding purely on population size without modeling the stop-loss premium cost against the carrier retention/margin it's meant to replace — a bad claims year under-covered by a too-high attachment point can erase the projected savings.

**FLSA primary duty (job-architecture level)** — The principal, most important duty an employee performs, used as the core test for executive/administrative/professional exemption (29 CFR §541) — assessed here as a property of a job level's written classification across an entire job family, not a single employee's actual day-to-day mix.
In use: "The Store Manager II level's written primary duty clears the executive exemption test, but our sampling audit shows 20% of incumbents are actually spending most of their time on the floor — that's a level-wide classification drift, not one person's case."
Misuse: treating a job-architecture-level duties-test conclusion as settled forever, or conflating it with an individual employee's actual-duties audit (that recheck belongs to hr-specialist, working one case at a time).
