# Loan interviewer/clerk working vocabulary

Terms of art a loan processor uses precisely. Format: definition → a sentence a practitioner would actually say → the common misuse.

**PTA / PTF (prior-to-approval / prior-to-funding)** — Conditions underwriting attaches to a loan, sorted by when they must clear: PTA conditions must clear before the file gets conditional approval; PTF conditions can clear any time before closing/funding.
In use: "The insurance binder is PTF, so it's not blocking approval — but the income letter is PTA, that one has to clear first."
Misuse: treating all open conditions as equally urgent regardless of PTA/PTF designation, which misallocates follow-up effort.

**LOE (letter of explanation)** — A signed, dated statement from the borrower explaining an anomaly in the file (large deposit, credit inquiry, employment gap, name discrepancy) — required in writing, not accepted as a verbal note.
In use: "We need an LOE for the collection account, not just what he told me on the phone."
Misuse: writing the explanation on the borrower's behalf and having them sign it — the LOE needs to be the borrower's own account, not the processor's summary.

**VOE (verification of employment)** — A third-party confirmation of a borrower's employment, income, and tenure, obtained directly from the employer (written or verbal), distinct from paystubs the borrower self-provides.
In use: "Paystubs get us qualified, but we still need a VOE within 10 days of closing to confirm nothing's changed."
Misuse: treating a paystub as sufficient in place of a VOE — most investor guidelines require both, at different points in the process.

**Sourcing (a deposit)** — Documenting where a large or unexplained deposit came from, sufficient to show it isn't an undisclosed loan or liability that would change the borrower's qualifying debt.
In use: "That $9,200 needs to be sourced — LOE plus the client invoice and the payer's cancelled check."
Misuse: accepting a plausible verbal explanation as "sourced" — sourcing means documented, not merely explained.

**Conditional approval** — Underwriting's determination that the loan will be approved once specific listed conditions are satisfied — not a guarantee, and can be withdrawn if a condition reveals new information (like an income discrepancy).
In use: "We're at conditional approval, but that name discrepancy needs to clear before I'd call this a sure thing."
Misuse: describing conditional approval to a borrower as "approved" — it isn't final until all conditions clear and underwriting signs off.

**Stip (stipulation)** — Shorthand for an underwriting condition — a specific item or clarification required before the file can move forward. Used interchangeably with "condition" in most shops.
In use: "We cleared four of the five stips, just waiting on the insurance binder."
Misuse: none common — mostly informal shorthand, but some shops distinguish "stips" (document requests) from "conditions" (broader approval requirements) — confirm local usage.

**Trending income (self-employed)** — Averaging a self-employed borrower's income across the required lookback period (typically 2 years), or using a declining-trend calculation if income dropped year-over-year, rather than using the most recent year alone.
In use: "Income's trending up, so we average both years — if it were trending down, we'd likely have to use the lower year or explain the decline."
Misuse: using only the most recent (higher) year's income when the trend is increasing — most programs require averaging regardless of direction unless a specific declining-trend exception applies.

**DTI (referenced, not calculated by this role)** — Debt-to-income ratio; this role gathers and verifies the inputs (verified income, documented debts) that the loan officer or underwriting uses to calculate DTI, but does not make the qualifying-ratio decision.
In use: "I've got the income verified at $5,375/month — that's what goes to the LO for the DTI calc."
Misuse: a processor independently deciding a borrower "doesn't qualify" based on their own DTI math — that's the loan officer's or underwriter's call, not the processor's.

**Compensating factor** — A strength in the file (large reserves, high credit score, low LTV) that can offset a weakness (elevated DTI) in an underwriter's overall approval decision — not something a processor identifies or applies, but something to be aware exists as context for why a marginal file might still clear.
In use: "DTI's a little high, but there's six months of reserves — that's the kind of thing that might be a compensating factor for underwriting."
Misuse: a processor telling a borrower their high reserves will "make up for" a DTI issue — that's underwriting's judgment call, not a guarantee.

**Investor guideline** — The specific document-and-qualification requirements set by whoever will ultimately own or guarantee the loan (Fannie Mae, Freddie Mac, FHA, VA, USDA, or a portfolio investor) — these vary and change, and the applicable one depends on which loan program the file is using.
In use: "Freddie's guideline wants 2 months of business bank statements for this borrower type — Fannie only requires 1."
Misuse: applying one investor's document checklist to a file underwritten to a different investor's guidelines.

**Preliminary title report** — An early title-company document showing the property's current recorded ownership and any liens, used to catch name-discrepancy or lien issues before closing, distinct from the final title policy issued at closing.
In use: "The title report shows the seller's name slightly differently than the application — flag that before we get further along."
Misuse: treating the preliminary report as final — it's a snapshot that can change if new liens or issues surface before closing.

**Rate-lock expiration (as it affects processing)** — The date a borrower's locked interest rate expires if the loan hasn't closed — processors don't set or extend locks, but their conditions-clearing pace directly determines whether the file closes inside the lock window.
In use: "Lock expires the 28th — if that insurance binder doesn't come in by the 20th, we're going to be tight."
Misuse: treating the rate lock as the loan officer's problem alone — the processor's follow-up cadence on open conditions is what determines whether the lock date is realistic.
