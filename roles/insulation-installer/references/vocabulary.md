# Insulation Installer Vocabulary

### R-value
A material's resistance to conductive heat flow per inch of thickness, at full designed loft and continuous coverage.
**In use:** "That's the nominal R-19 on the bag, but at 4 inches of compression it's testing closer to R-13."
**Common misuse:** Treating the printed R-value as the installed, real-world performance regardless of compression, gaps, or settling.

### ACH50
Air changes per hour measured with a blower door depressurizing the building to 50 Pascals — the standard number for how leaky a building envelope is.
**In use:** "We tested at 14 ACH50 before the work and need to get under 5 to hit code for this zone."
**Common misuse:** Describing a house as "tight" or "leaky" without an actual ACH50 number to back it up.

### Thermal bridging
Heat flow through a continuous conductive path — typically wood or steel framing — that bypasses the insulated cavity next to it.
**In use:** "The cavity's at R-20, but the studs are still bridging straight through, so the whole-wall R is lower than the cavity number."
**Common misuse:** Quoting a wall's cavity R-value as if it were the whole assembly's effective R-value, ignoring the framing fraction.

### Vapor retarder / vapor barrier
A material rated by its permeance class (Class I, II, or III) that slows or blocks moisture diffusion through an assembly; placement depends on which direction vapor drives in that climate.
**In use:** "This is a mixed-humid climate — we're not adding a Class I vapor barrier on the interior, it'll trap moisture coming in from the AC side."
**Common misuse:** Using "vapor barrier" generically for any vapor-slowing material and installing it on the interior by habit, regardless of climate or vapor-drive direction.

### Dense-pack
Blown cellulose or fiberglass installed into a fully enclosed cavity at high density (roughly 3.5 lb/ft³ for cellulose) to resist settling and add air-flow resistance.
**In use:** "This is a closed wall cavity, so we're dense-packing it, not loose-blowing it — density matters here."
**Common misuse:** Calling any blown-in insulation "dense-pack," including open-attic loose-fill blown at a much lower density.

### Loose-fill
Blown insulation installed in an open cavity (typically an attic floor) at a lower density than dense-pack, expected to settle over time to the bag's stated settled R-value.
**In use:** "We're loose-filling the attic to 10 inches to hit the settled R-value on the bag chart."
**Common misuse:** Confusing loose-fill attic density with the higher density required for a dense-packed wall cavity.

### Baffle
A rigid or semi-rigid chute installed in a rafter bay above a soffit vent to keep the airflow path from the eave to the ridge open when insulation is added.
**In use:** "Baffle every vented bay before you blow, or you'll bury the soffit intake."
**Common misuse:** Treating baffles as optional trim rather than a required part of a vented-attic insulation job.

### Thermal barrier vs. ignition barrier
A thermal barrier (commonly 1/2" gypsum, 15-minute rated) is required over foam plastic insulation exposed in habitable space; an ignition barrier is a lesser, tested alternative allowed for some foams in attics/crawlspaces under their specific listing.
**In use:** "That foam's not listed as an ignition barrier for exposed use, so it still needs gypsum over it in the finished room."
**Common misuse:** Assuming any spray foam can be left exposed in an attic without checking whether that specific product carries an ignition-barrier listing.

### Open-cell vs. closed-cell spray foam
Two different spray polyurethane foam formulations with different density, R-per-inch, and vapor permeance — not interchangeable products with the same name.
**In use:** "We're going closed-cell in the rim joist — we don't have the depth for open-cell to hit code there."
**Common misuse:** Referring to both as generic "spray foam" without distinguishing which one an assembly actually calls for.

### HERS Grade I / II / III
RESNET's installation-quality grading for insulation — Grade I means full cavity fill with no gaps or compression, Grade II and III allow progressively more voids and derate the assembly's modeled performance accordingly.
**In use:** "This job's contracted as Grade I, so the rater's going to fail any cavity with a gap around the wiring."
**Common misuse:** Assuming "insulated" automatically means Grade I without an inspection confirming fill quality.

### Blower door
A calibrated fan mounted in an exterior door frame used to pressurize or depressurize a building and measure its air-leakage rate.
**In use:** "Run the blower door before we start so we have a baseline to compare against after the air sealing."
**Common misuse:** Running the test once, after the job, with no pre-work baseline to show the actual improvement.

### Settled R-value
The R-value printed on a loose-fill insulation bag that already accounts for the material settling after installation — distinct from its R-value at the moment it's blown.
**In use:** "The bag's settled R-value is 3.7 per inch, so we're not over-ordering material assuming it'll shrink further than that."
**Common misuse:** Using the freshly-blown depth and thickness to calculate R-value instead of the settled figure the manufacturer already built in.

### Presumed asbestos-containing material (presumed ACM)
Legacy insulation material — most notably vermiculite — treated as asbestos-containing under EPA guidance until a lab test proves otherwise.
**In use:** "It's vermiculite and there's no test on file, so it's presumed ACM until we get a sample back."
**Common misuse:** Assuming old vermiculite is safe because it "looks fine" or because only some vermiculite deposits were contaminated.

### Air sealing
The work of closing air-leakage pathways through the envelope — separate from, and a prerequisite to, adding cavity insulation.
**In use:** "Air seal first, then insulate — insulating over open bypasses just wastes material."
**Common misuse:** Treating "insulation job" and "air sealing job" as the same scope, or skipping air sealing because insulation alone looks like it addresses drafts.

### Knee wall
A short vertical wall enclosing the triangular unconditioned space at a roof's slope, common in finished attics — a frequent air-sealing and insulation gap.
**In use:** "The knee wall backside isn't insulated at all — that's where the cold is coming from, not the ceiling."
**Common misuse:** Insulating the visible face of a knee wall while leaving the unconditioned attic side behind it completely open.

### Vented vs. unvented (conditioned) attic assembly
Two different roof-insulation strategies with opposite baffle and air-sealing logic: a vented attic keeps the roof deck cold and relies on soffit-to-ridge airflow, while an unvented assembly insulates at the roofline and brings the attic inside the conditioned envelope.
**In use:** "This is an unvented assembly with foam at the roofline — we're not installing baffles, there's no vent path by design."
**Common misuse:** Applying vented-attic baffle and ventilation rules to a spray-foam unvented roofline assembly where they don't apply.
