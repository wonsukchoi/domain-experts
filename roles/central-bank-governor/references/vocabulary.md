# Vocabulary

### Dual mandate
A statutory requirement (as with the U.S. Federal Reserve) to pursue both price stability and maximum sustainable employment, as distinct from a single-mandate central bank focused on inflation alone.
**In use:** "Under the dual mandate, we can't just ignore the labor market data because inflation's still above target — we have to weigh both."
**Common misuse:** Treating the two mandate goals as always in conflict, when in most conditions low, stable inflation and sustainable employment move together rather than trading off.

### Forward guidance
Public communication about the likely future path of policy, used as a commitment device to shape current long-term rates and behavior ahead of the policy itself taking effect.
**In use:** "Our forward guidance said we'd hold until inflation showed sustained progress — breaking that now, absent that condition, costs us more than the rate cut itself is worth."
**Common misuse:** Treating forward guidance as a soft prediction that can be walked back without cost, when markets price it as closer to a commitment and react accordingly when it breaks.

### Taylor rule
A formula (Taylor, 1993) suggesting a policy rate level as neutral rate plus weighted adjustments for the inflation gap and output gap — used as a benchmark against pure discretion.
**In use:** "The Taylor rule suggests we're still below where the formula says we should be, given how far inflation is from target."
**Common misuse:** Applying it as a mechanical mandate rather than a sanity check, ignoring that its key input — the neutral rate — is itself uncertain and shifts over time.

### Neutral rate / r-star
The theoretical policy interest rate that neither stimulates nor restricts economic activity when the economy is at full employment and stable inflation — a reference point that's estimated, not directly observed.
**In use:** "We think r-star has drifted up post-pandemic, which means today's rate is less restrictive than it looks against historical norms."
**Common misuse:** Treating r-star as a fixed, known number rather than a genuinely uncertain estimate that shifts with structural changes in the economy, which overstates the precision of any policy-rate-versus-neutral comparison.

### Quantitative easing (QE) / quantitative tightening (QT)
QE is central bank purchase of long-term securities to expand its balance sheet and lower long-term rates, typically used at the effective lower bound; QT is the reverse — letting the balance sheet shrink or actively selling.
**In use:** "QT is proceeding at a set monthly pace, but we're watching money-market stress signals closely for whether that pace still makes sense."
**Common misuse:** Treating QE/QT as working purely through the direct portfolio-balance effect of the purchases/sales, when a substantial part of their effect comes from what they signal about the future policy rate path.

### Lender of last resort
The central bank's function of providing emergency liquidity to solvent financial institutions during a panic, to prevent a liquidity crisis from becoming a solvency crisis through forced asset fire-sales.
**In use:** "This is a lender-of-last-resort situation, not a bailout — the bank's assets are worth more than its liabilities at par, it just can't sell them fast enough to meet withdrawals."
**Common misuse:** Applying lender-of-last-resort support to an institution that's actually insolvent, which converts a liquidity tool into a public subsidy for private losses and creates moral hazard.

### Time inconsistency
The economic problem where a policy that's optimal to announce in advance stops being the policy an authority actually wants to follow once the future arrives, unless credibly bound to it (Kydland & Prescott).
**In use:** "The time-inconsistency problem is why we need to actually follow through on tightening even when it's politically costly in the moment — otherwise nobody believes the next promise either."
**Common misuse:** Treating time inconsistency as a one-time credibility cost rather than a standing structural incentive that has to be actively resisted on every decision, not just the dramatic ones.

### Anchored inflation expectations
The state where the public and markets broadly expect inflation to return to and stay near the central bank's stated target over the medium term, regardless of short-term fluctuations.
**In use:** "Expectations are still anchored around 2% even with current inflation at 4.2% — that's the credibility buffer we're spending down if this drags on much longer."
**Common misuse:** Assuming anchored expectations are a permanent, self-sustaining state rather than a credibility asset that has to be actively maintained and can be spent down by policy inconsistency.

### Effective lower bound
The practical floor near zero below which a central bank's conventional policy rate can't usefully go, at which point unconventional tools (QE, forward guidance, negative rates in some jurisdictions) become the primary levers.
**In use:** "We're back near the effective lower bound — that's when balance sheet tools and guidance do more of the work than the rate itself."
**Common misuse:** Treating the effective lower bound as an absolute zero rather than a practical, somewhat jurisdiction-dependent floor — some central banks have gone modestly negative, with mixed results on the tool's effectiveness.

### Discount window
A standing central bank facility allowing eligible institutions to borrow directly against collateral, historically carrying a stigma that discouraged use even when institutions genuinely needed it.
**In use:** "Discount window borrowing spiked this week — that's a real-time liquidity stress signal worth watching closely."
**Common misuse:** Assuming low discount window usage means no liquidity stress exists, when stigma around being seen borrowing from it can suppress usage even during genuine strain.

### Yield curve inversion
The condition where short-term interest rates exceed long-term rates, historically correlated with — though not deterministically predictive of — an upcoming economic slowdown.
**In use:** "The curve's been inverted for six months now — that's a signal worth factoring into the forecast, even though it's not a guarantee."
**Common misuse:** Treating an inverted curve as a certain recession predictor rather than a historically correlated signal with real but imperfect predictive power and variable lead times.

### Moral hazard
The risk that protecting an institution or actor from the consequences of excessive risk-taking encourages more of that same risk-taking in the future, since the downside has been socialized.
**In use:** "If we bail out an insolvent institution here, we're teaching every other bank that reckless risk gets covered — that's the moral hazard cost showing up next cycle."
**Common misuse:** Using moral hazard as a blanket argument against any intervention, when a well-designed, appropriately penalty-priced liquidity facility for solvent institutions doesn't carry the same moral hazard as an outright bailout of an insolvent one.

### Data-dependent (vs. date-dependent) policy
A commitment framed around economic conditions that must be met before a policy changes, rather than a fixed calendar date, preserving flexibility while still anchoring expectations to a stated condition.
**In use:** "We're data-dependent, not date-dependent — if inflation surprises to the upside next month, that changes the calculus regardless of what calendar quarter we're in."
**Common misuse:** Announcing data-dependent policy but never actually specifying which data or what threshold, which leaves the "condition" so vague it functions as no commitment at all.
