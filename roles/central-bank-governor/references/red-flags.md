# Red flags

### Inflation expectations de-anchoring from target despite the policy rate at or above neutral
- **Usually means:** the public or markets no longer believe the central bank's stated reaction function, often because of a prior broken commitment or a perceived tolerance for above-target inflation.
- **First question:** "what specifically changed in our communication or actions that the market is now pricing differently?"
- **Data to pull:** market-implied inflation breakevens and survey-based long-run inflation expectations, plotted against the policy rate path.

### Policy rate decisions reverse direction within a single quarter without a clear underlying data change
- **Usually means:** the institution is reacting to noise in a single data print rather than a genuine shift in the economic trajectory, which reads to markets as an unstable reaction function.
- **First question:** "what data point actually changed between the two decisions, and was it large enough to justify a reversal?"
- **Data to pull:** the specific data releases between the two decisions, compared against their normal volatility range.

### Forward guidance is broken without a stated data-dependent trigger having been met
- **Usually means:** the guidance was treated internally as flexible while being communicated externally as a firm commitment, and reality forced a reversal the public wasn't prepared for.
- **First question:** "what was the explicit condition attached to this guidance, and was it met?"
- **Data to pull:** the original guidance language and the data against the stated condition at the time of the reversal.

### An emergency lending facility extends credit to an institution without verified solvency
- **Usually means:** the illiquidity-versus-insolvency distinction wasn't rigorously checked under time pressure, risking the central bank absorbing credit losses that should belong to the institution's owners and creditors, not the public.
- **First question:** "has the primary regulator confirmed this institution is solvent at fair value, not just that it's facing withdrawal pressure?"
- **Data to pull:** the institution's fair-value balance sheet assessment from its primary supervisor.

### Public statements from elected officials demanding a specific rate action precede a policy shift in that direction
- **Usually means:** independence is being compromised, whether or not the policy shift is independently justified by data — the sequencing itself damages credibility.
- **First question:** "would this decision have been made on the same timeline and magnitude absent the public pressure?"
- **Data to pull:** the data available at the time of the decision, compared against what would have independently justified the same action.

### Yield curve inversion persists alongside continued policy tightening with no acknowledgment of recession risk in communication
- **Usually means:** the institution may be under-communicating a known risk signal to avoid appearing to second-guess its own tightening stance.
- **First question:** "what does our own forecast say the inverted curve implies, and are we saying that publicly?"
- **Data to pull:** internal recession-probability models against the public communication record on the same topic.

### Bank stress test pass rates decline across a supervisory cycle with no corresponding tightening of requirements
- **Usually means:** supervisory standards haven't kept pace with a deteriorating risk environment, often because tightening standards is politically or industry unpopular during good times.
- **First question:** "what's driving the decline — a harder test, or genuinely weaker balance sheets?"
- **Data to pull:** stress test methodology changes year-over-year against the actual capital ratio trends of tested institutions.

### Balance sheet runoff pace is unchanged despite emerging money-market liquidity stress signals
- **Usually means:** quantitative tightening is proceeding on a preset schedule rather than being monitored against real-time liquidity conditions, risking a funding-market disruption.
- **First question:** "what do repo market rate spreads and reserve levels look like right now, and does the runoff pace still make sense?"
- **Data to pull:** repo market rate spreads (e.g., SOFR versus the policy rate) and aggregate bank reserve levels.

### Committee votes are unanimous for many consecutive meetings despite genuinely divided underlying economic data
- **Usually means:** dissent is being suppressed for the appearance of institutional unity, which removes a valuable signal to markets about the range of internal views and risk.
- **First question:** "given how split the data actually is, why is there no recorded disagreement on the committee?"
- **Data to pull:** the voting record alongside the range of individual member economic projections, if published separately.

### A single public communication line triggers outsized, repeated market volatility
- **Usually means:** communication process isn't adequately stress-testing language before release, or a governor is inserting off-script commentary that isn't calibrated to its market impact.
- **First question:** "was this line reviewed for market impact before the statement or press conference, and has this happened before?"
- **Data to pull:** market reaction (rate moves, volatility indices) in the minutes following the specific statement, compared against prior incidents.
