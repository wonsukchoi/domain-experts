# Vocabulary

### Process control strip
A standardized, pre-exposed test strip processed alongside production runs and read on a densitometer to verify chemistry condition against established control limits.
**In use:** "Ran a control strip at the start of the shift — densitometer's within limits, cleared to start production."
**Common misuse:** Relying on the visual appearance of finished production prints or negatives instead of a control strip's actual densitometer readings to judge chemistry condition.

### Densitometer
An instrument measuring the optical density of a processed film or print, used to numerically verify a control strip or finished product against target values.
**In use:** "Densitometer's reading 0.05 above the upper control limit on the strip — chemistry needs attention before we continue."
**Common misuse:** Judging density or color balance by eye alone when a densitometer reading would catch a drift not yet visually obvious.

### Replenishment (chemistry)
The practice of adding fresh chemistry to a processing tank at a rate proportional to the volume of film or paper processed, to maintain consistent chemical activity.
**In use:** "Replenishment's tracked per roll processed, not per hour — that's what keeps the chemistry consistent through a long run."
**Common misuse:** Replenishing based on elapsed time or an assumed average volume rather than actual measured throughput, letting chemistry drift from its intended condition.

### Time-temperature compensation
The calculated adjustment to processing time required when chemistry temperature differs from the process's documented standard temperature.
**In use:** "Tank's 3.6 degrees under standard — compensated time comes out to 3:50 instead of the standard 3:15."
**Common misuse:** Adjusting processing time by feel or a rough guess rather than using the process's documented compensation relationship.

### Fixer exhaustion
The progressive reduction in a fixing chemical's capacity to remove undeveloped silver halide as it processes more film or paper volume.
**In use:** "Fixer's past 80% of rated capacity — running a fixer-check test before trusting the standard fixing time."
**Common misuse:** Applying the same fixing time regardless of how much volume the fixer has already processed, risking incomplete fixing as the chemistry nears its rated capacity.

### Cross-contamination (chemistry)
The transfer of one process's chemistry into another — developer into fix, or between two different process types — via inadequate rinsing or shared equipment, degrading chemical function.
**In use:** "Full rinse/purge sequence before switching this tank from C-41 to E-6 — a quick rinse isn't enough."
**Common misuse:** Relying on visual cleanliness of equipment rather than a documented rinse/purge procedure to prevent chemistry transfer between process steps or process types.

### Control limits
The numeric upper and lower bounds within which a process control strip's densitometer readings must fall for the chemistry to be considered in specification.
**In use:** "Reading's close to the limit but still inside it — logged, no correction needed yet."
**Common misuse:** Treating a control strip reading that's "close" to the limit as automatically acceptable without checking it's actually within the documented bounds.

### Chromogenic process (e.g. C-41)
A color film or print development process using dye-coupling chemistry, distinct from black-and-white silver-only development.
**In use:** "This is a chromogenic C-41 run — the compensation table is different from what we'd use for black-and-white."
**Common misuse:** Assuming time-temperature relationships or chemistry handling from black-and-white processing transfer directly to chromogenic processes, when the chemistry and its sensitivity differ.

### Reciprocal time-temperature relationship
The general principle that a chemical development process's required time changes predictably, though not always linearly, as temperature changes, allowing compensation calculations.
**In use:** "There's a documented compensation curve for this process — use that, not a rough guess based on how far off the temperature seems."
**Common misuse:** Assuming any temperature deviation can be ignored as long as it's "close," rather than applying the documented compensation relationship for that specific process.

### Batch/run record
Documentation of the actual process parameters — temperature, time, control strip results, replenishment amounts — used for a specific processing run, for traceability.
**In use:** "Batch record shows the exact temperature and compensated time used — that's what let us trace the density issue back to this run."
**Common misuse:** Logging a run as "processed normally" rather than recording the actual measured parameters, which prevents tracing a later-discovered defect back to its specific cause.
