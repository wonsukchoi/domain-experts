# Vocabulary

Terms generalists and DIY owners flatten together that a practitioner keeps sharply separate — each with the misuse that leads to a missed leak, a wrong electrical diagnosis, or a structural repair caught too late.

## NFPA 1192 vs. RVIA seal

**NFPA 1192** is the construction and test standard covering LP-gas, electrical, and plumbing systems in recreational vehicles. The **RVIA seal** is a manufacturer's certification that a unit was built to that standard (and others) at time of manufacture.

**In use:** "The seal tells us it left the factory compliant — it doesn't tell us the manometer will still hold at 11 in. W.C. six years and forty thousand road miles later."

**Common misuse:** treating the RVIA seal as an ongoing guarantee of current condition rather than a point-in-time manufacturing certification.

## Manometer lock-up test vs. soap-bubble test

**Manometer lock-up test** pressurizes the LP-gas system and measures whether it holds pressure over a timed interval — a quantitative pass/fail. **Soap-bubble test** is a qualitative leak-location tool, applied to a specific connection once the manometer has confirmed a leak exists somewhere in the system.

**In use:** "Manometer failed the 3-minute hold — now we soap-test every fitting to find where."

**Common misuse:** running only a soap test at a few visible connections and calling the system cleared, without ever confirming a pass on the manometer's timed hold.

## EPDM vs. TPO roof membrane

**EPDM** is a synthetic rubber roofing membrane, typically black, with a 10–15 year service life and a lap sealant that needs periodic recoating as it UV-degrades. **TPO** is a thermoplastic membrane, typically white, with better UV tolerance and a somewhat longer service life, but requiring TPO-compatible sealant.

**In use:** "It's TPO, not EPDM — don't reach for the rubber lap sealant, it won't bond right."

**Common misuse:** using EPDM-rated lap sealant on a TPO roof (or vice versa) because "roof sealant" is treated as one interchangeable product category.

## Moisture content (wood-substrate scale) vs. visible leak

**Moisture content** is a percentage reading from a meter indicating how much water is present in a wall/floor substrate, independent of whether any water is currently visible. A **visible leak** is water actively present or staining at the moment of inspection — a much later-stage sign than an elevated moisture reading.

**In use:** "No stain on the ceiling, but the meter's reading 24% at that seam — treat it as a leak, not a clean bill of health."

**Common misuse:** inspecting only for visible staining or a soft spot and skipping the moisture meter, missing intrusion that hasn't progressed far enough to show yet.

## Converter vs. inverter vs. inverter-charger

**Converter** takes AC shore/generator power and converts it to DC to charge the house batteries and run 12V loads. **Inverter** does the reverse — converts DC battery power to AC for household outlets when no shore/generator power is present. **Inverter-charger** combines both functions in one unit with an automatic transfer switch between them.

**In use:** "The lights are dim on battery power — that's an inverter-charger transfer question, not a converter problem, because there's no AC input right now for a converter to act on."

**Common misuse:** using "converter" and "inverter" interchangeably when diagnosing a power complaint, which sends the troubleshooting to the wrong half of the system.

## Bulk vs. absorption vs. float (charge stages)

**Bulk** is the initial fast-charge stage at the highest voltage. **Absorption** tapers current at a slightly lower voltage to top off remaining capacity without overcharging. **Float** is the low, indefinite-hold maintenance voltage once the battery is full.

**In use:** "It's been sitting at bulk voltage for two days — it should have stepped down to absorption by now, the charger's stuck in one stage."

**Common misuse:** reading a single voltage snapshot and calling it "charging fine" without knowing which stage it should be in given time-on-charge and battery state.

## Racking vs. binding (slide-out)

**Racking** is the slide opening itself going out of square, usually from frame twist (tire pressure, leveling, or floor structure). **Binding** is resistance within the slide mechanism itself — a cable, roller, or gearmotor issue — with the opening still square.

**In use:** "Measure the gap top and bottom first — if it's racked, no amount of cable adjustment fixes it."

**Common misuse:** diagnosing every hard-to-close slide as a mechanism problem and adjusting or replacing hardware before measuring whether the opening itself is out of square.

## Delamination

Separation of the bonded layers of a laminated sidewall or roof panel (typically a fiberglass or aluminum skin bonded to a foam or wood-frame core), most commonly caused by long-term water intrusion breaking down the adhesive bond.

**In use:** "That soft spot isn't cosmetic — press on it again, that's delamination starting, not just a loose interior panel."

**Common misuse:** treating early delamination as a cosmetic wall-panel issue and patching the interior surface without addressing the water source or the compromised bond underneath.

## Gray water vs. black water vs. holding tank

**Gray water** is wastewater from sinks and showers. **Black water** is wastewater from the toilet. **Holding tank** is the general term for either tank storing wastewater onboard until it's dumped at a dump station.

**In use:** "Dump black first, then gray — the gray water rinses the hose on the way out."

**Common misuse:** using "holding tank" as if it refers to a single tank, or reversing the black/gray dump order, which leaves waste residue in the hose.

## Bypass kit (water heater)

A set of valves plumbed around the water heater tank that let antifreeze bypass the tank entirely during winterization, protecting the fresh-water lines without wasting antifreeze filling and then draining the tank.

**In use:** "Confirm the bypass valves are actually turned before you start pumping antifreeze — don't just assume the kit's installed correctly."

**Common misuse:** assuming a bypass kit is installed and correctly positioned without physically checking valve position, resulting in antifreeze wasted into a tank still plumbed for normal flow.

## Shore power vs. generator vs. inverter power

**Shore power** is AC power from an external pedestal or outlet. **Generator power** is AC power from the unit's onboard or portable generator. **Inverter power** is AC power generated from the house batteries via the inverter when neither of the other two sources is connected.

**In use:** "Test on all three sources separately — a fault that only shows on shore power points at the pedestal or the shore inlet, not the RV's internal wiring."

**Common misuse:** testing an AC fault on only one power source and generalizing the result to all three, when the fault may be specific to that source's connection or wiring path.

## Ground-neutral bond

The single point in an AC system where the neutral and ground conductors are electrically connected — required at exactly one point in the shore-power path (typically at the transfer switch or main panel), not duplicated at the pedestal and the RV simultaneously.

**In use:** "Check for a duplicate ground-neutral bond before assuming the GFCI itself is bad — a second bond downstream trips it every time."

**Common misuse:** assuming any GFCI trip is a faulty breaker or a wet outlet without checking for a bonding-point conflict, which is a wiring-configuration issue no amount of outlet replacement fixes.

## PDI (Pre-Delivery Inspection)

The dealership's systematic check of every system — LP-gas, 12V/120V electrical, plumbing, appliances, slide-outs, roof — performed before a new unit is delivered to its first owner, distinct from a later service visit or a pre-purchase inspection on a used unit.

**In use:** "This is a PDI, not a repair visit — every system gets the same tests we'd run on a complaint, whether or not anything's been reported yet."

**Common misuse:** treating a PDI as a quick walkthrough or feature demonstration for the customer rather than the same measured tests (manometer, moisture meter, loaded-voltage check) used to diagnose an actual complaint.
