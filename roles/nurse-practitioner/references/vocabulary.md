# Nurse Practitioner — Vocabulary

### Practice authority
The legal category (full, reduced, or restricted) defining whether an NP can diagnose, treat, and prescribe independently or requires a collaborating/supervising physician's involvement, set by state law.
**In use:** "This is a reduced-practice state, so I need a collaborating physician's sign-off before I can prescribe this controlled substance."
**Common misuse:** Treating "I've been doing this competently for years" as equivalent to having full practice authority — competence and legal authority are separate questions.

### Collaborative practice agreement
A formal, state-required agreement between an NP and a supervising/collaborating physician defining which actions require physician involvement, common in reduced- or restricted-practice states.
**In use:** "Check the collaborative agreement — does it cover ordering a CT, or does that need the collaborating physician's approval?"
**Common misuse:** Assuming the agreement's scope from a prior job or a colleague's agreement rather than reading the specific document.

### Differential diagnosis
The ranked list of plausible diagnoses generated from a presentation, narrowed by findings and tests, not a single hypothesis confirmed by the first supporting fact.
**In use:** "My differential includes strep, viral pharyngitis, and mono — the Centor score and a rapid strep test will narrow it."
**Common misuse:** Using the term to describe a single diagnosis already decided on, rather than the actual list of candidates being weighed.

### Clinical decision rule
A validated tool (Centor, Wells, Ottawa ankle rules, CURB-65, etc.) that converts specific findings into a probability estimate or risk category, informing whether testing or treatment is indicated.
**In use:** "McIsaac score is 4 — that's the band where a rapid strep test is indicated, not empiric treatment or no testing."
**Common misuse:** Applying a rule outside its validated population (e.g., a pediatric rule on an adult, or a rule validated in one country's population applied elsewhere without adjustment) and treating the output as equally reliable.

### Pretest probability
The estimated likelihood of a condition before a specific test is run, which determines whether the test result will meaningfully change management.
**In use:** "Pretest probability here is low enough that even a positive result wouldn't change what we do, so the test isn't indicated."
**Common misuse:** Ordering a test reflexively without first estimating whether the result — positive or negative — would actually change the plan.

### Medication reconciliation
The active process of cross-checking a patient's reported medication list against an objective source (pharmacy fill history, e-prescribing records, other providers' notes) to identify discrepancies before prescribing.
**In use:** "I reconciled her med list against the pharmacy record and found two prescriptions she hadn't mentioned."
**Common misuse:** Treating "I asked what medications you're taking" as equivalent to reconciliation — self-report alone routinely misses supplements, PRN meds, and other providers' prescriptions.

### Scope of practice
The set of clinical activities an NP is trained and licensed to perform, distinct from practice authority (the legal independence to perform them without physician sign-off).
**In use:** "Managing this is within my scope of practice, but in this state it still requires a collaborating physician's co-signature on the controlled-substance prescription."
**Common misuse:** Conflating scope of practice (a competence/training question) with practice authority (a legal-independence question) — a task can be within scope but outside independent authority in a given state.

### Return precautions
Specific, concrete triggers given to a patient for when to seek follow-up or emergency care, distinct from a vague instruction to "follow up if it doesn't get better."
**In use:** "Return precautions: if the swelling spreads past the knee, if you develop a fever over 101, or if it's not improving in 48 hours."
**Common misuse:** Giving vague instructions ("call if you're worried") that don't specify an objective trigger, which leaves the patient unable to judge when escalation is actually warranted.

### Empiric treatment
Starting treatment based on clinical presentation and probability before or without a confirmatory test result, as distinct from treatment guided by a positive test.
**In use:** "Given the low pretest probability, empiric antibiotics aren't indicated here — we'll test first."
**Common misuse:** Using "empiric" to justify skipping a decision-rule-indicated test out of convenience, rather than as a deliberate strategy justified by the specific probability band or clinical urgency.

### Watchful waiting
A deliberate management strategy of delaying treatment (e.g., a delayed antibiotic prescription) pending symptom progression or test results, distinct from simply not addressing the complaint.
**In use:** "This is a low-probability bacterial presentation — watchful waiting with a delayed prescription the patient can fill if symptoms worsen is appropriate here."
**Common misuse:** Confusing watchful waiting (an active, documented strategy with a specific re-evaluation trigger) with simply telling a patient to "wait and see" without a plan.

### Anchoring bias
The clinical-reasoning error of fixating on an initial diagnosis and interpreting subsequent findings to fit it, rather than remaining open to revising the differential.
**In use:** "I want to check myself for anchoring bias here — am I only looking for evidence that confirms strep, or am I actually considering the alternatives?"
**Common misuse:** Treating anchoring bias as something that only happens to inexperienced clinicians — experienced clinicians are also susceptible, particularly under time pressure.
