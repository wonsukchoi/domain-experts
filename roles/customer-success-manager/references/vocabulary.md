# Customer success working vocabulary

Terms a CSM uses precisely. Format: definition → how a practitioner says it → common misuse.

**Net revenue retention (NRR)** — The percentage of recurring revenue retained from an existing customer base over a period, including expansion and contraction, excluding new-customer revenue — the standard metric for whether an existing book is growing or shrinking.
In use: "Book NRR is 108% this quarter — expansion in healthy accounts is outpacing the contraction risk we're watching in a couple of at-risk ones."
Misuse: confusing NRR with gross retention (which excludes expansion) or with overall revenue growth (which includes new customers) — they answer different questions and aren't interchangeable.

**Time-to-first-value (TTFV)** — The elapsed time between contract signing and the customer reaching their first meaningful, defined outcome from the product — the single strongest early predictor of long-term retention.
In use: "TTFV for this segment is supposed to be under 30 days — this account is at day 52 with no milestone hit, which is the actual risk signal, not the renewal date."
Misuse: measuring "time to first login" or "time to onboarding call completed" as if it were the same thing as time to an actual meaningful outcome — activity isn't value.

**Health score** — A composite metric (usage depth, support sentiment, stakeholder engagement, NPS/CSAT) built and calibrated against actual historical churn outcomes, used to triage attention across a book proactively rather than reactively.
In use: "This account dropped from green to yellow on the health score three weeks before the champion's departure was confirmed — the model caught it early."
Misuse: building a health score once and never validating it against which accounts actually churned or renewed, letting an inaccurate model create false confidence.

**Multi-threading** — Maintaining active relationships with multiple stakeholders at an account (economic buyer, day-to-day users, executive sponsor) rather than relying on a single point of contact, so the relationship survives any one person's departure.
In use: "We multi-threaded this account after the champion's manager left last year — that's exactly why we didn't lose the relationship when the champion moved on too."
Misuse: treating a single strong champion relationship as sufficient risk management, especially at a high-ARR account, leaving no fallback if that one person leaves.

**Champion** — The internal advocate at a customer organization who drove or strongly supported the original purchase and can speak to its value internally — distinct from a general user, since a champion actively defends the renewal case to other stakeholders.
In use: "The champion left for a different team — we're not just losing a user, we're losing the person who would have defended this renewal in the budget conversation."
Misuse: treating any engaged user as a "champion" interchangeably; a champion specifically carries internal advocacy weight that a regular user doesn't.

**QBR (Quarterly Business Review)** — A structured, recurring review with the customer focused on restating achieved business outcomes in the customer's own terms (revenue impact, time saved, risk reduced), distinct from a feature-usage report or status update.
In use: "The QBR opened with the $45,000/year labor savings this team has achieved, not a list of what shipped this quarter."
Misuse: using QBR time to showcase new product features instead of connecting usage back to the specific outcome the customer bought — this leaves the value connection unmade, which the customer often won't do themselves.

**Health x value segmentation** — Triaging a book of accounts along two axes — current health (at-risk vs. healthy) and account value (ARR tier) — so scarce CSM attention goes to high-value at-risk accounts first, rather than being spread evenly or driven by whichever account is loudest.
In use: "This account is high-value but currently healthy — light-touch monitoring and an expansion conversation, not the same urgency as the high-value at-risk account."
Misuse: applying uniform attention across the book regardless of value or health tier, which under-serves the accounts where intervention would actually change the outcome.

**Single-threaded (account risk)** — An account where the CSM has only one meaningful stakeholder relationship, creating fragility if that person leaves, changes roles, or disengages.
In use: "This account has been single-threaded on the VP for two years — that's the real risk here, more than any usage number."
Misuse: assuming a single strong relationship is a sign of account health rather than a structural risk, regardless of how positive that one relationship currently is.

**Fit mismatch (non-fit churn)** — Churn that stems from the customer never being a genuine fit for the product's use case or segment, as distinct from at-risk-but-salvageable churn caused by a fixable adoption or relationship gap.
In use: "This is a fit mismatch — they bought for a use case the product doesn't actually solve well. No amount of onboarding effort was going to fix that."
Misuse: treating every at-risk account as equally salvageable and pouring effort into a genuine fit-mismatch account, which costs the capacity that could have saved a different, fixable account.

**Escalation (internal)** — Proactively routing a customer-facing risk (a broken integration, a missed expectation, a slipping timeline) to the appropriate internal team before the customer escalates externally (a public review, a call to leadership).
In use: "I escalated the integration bug to engineering as soon as I saw it affecting this account's core workflow — cheaper to fix now than after the customer escalates it themselves."
Misuse: waiting for a customer to raise an issue before routing it internally, missing the window where escalating proactively would have been cheaper and preserved more trust.
