# Vocabulary

### Performance Validity Test (PVT)
A measure of whether a patient put forth genuine effort on *cognitive* tasks, distinct from symptom self-report.
**In use:** "TOMM Trial 2 was 32/50 — we can't interpret the memory scores until we understand why effort failed."
**Common misuse:** treated as interchangeable with a symptom validity test; a passed PVT is sometimes wrongly assumed to also validate the patient's self-reported symptom checklist, which it does not test.

### Symptom Validity Test (SVT)
A measure of whether a patient's *self-reported* symptoms are consistent and non-exaggerated (e.g., MMPI-3 validity scales, PAI NIM).
**In use:** "FBS-r was elevated, so the somatic complaint list needs to be read with that in mind, separate from the cognitive data."
**Common misuse:** used as if it also certifies cognitive test validity — a patient can overreport symptoms while still performing with valid effort on cognitive tasks, and vice versa.

### Multivariate base rate
The statistically expected number of "abnormal" scores in a healthy person given the size of the battery administered.
**In use:** "Two low scores out of 28 is within the expected base rate for a battery this size — I wouldn't call that a finding on its own."
**Common misuse:** ignored entirely, so any single low score gets treated as a diagnostic finding regardless of battery size.

### Reliable Change Index (RCI)
A statistic that determines whether a change between two testing occasions exceeds what measurement error and practice effects alone would produce.
**In use:** "The raw score went up three points, but after the RCI correction for practice effect, that's not a reliable improvement."
**Common misuse:** skipped in favor of eyeballing raw score movement, which conflates practice effect with genuine clinical change.

### Premorbid functioning estimate
An inference about a patient's cognitive ability before the onset of the condition being evaluated, derived from reading ability, education, or occupation.
**In use:** "Her WTAR estimate puts premorbid FSIQ around 118 — a current score of 100 isn't 'average,' it's a real drop for her."
**Common misuse:** skipped in favor of comparing raw scores to the population mean of 100, which misses decline in high-premorbid patients and overcalls impairment in low-premorbid ones.

### Double dissociation
A pattern where two independent cognitive functions are affected in opposite directions across two different measures or patients, used to support a localization or differential-diagnosis argument.
**In use:** "Intact naming with impaired verbal fluency and preserved category fluency is a dissociation pattern that points away from a primary language deficit."
**Common misuse:** applied loosely to any two different-looking scores, without checking that the two functions are actually independent of each other.

### Ecological validity
The degree to which a test score predicts real-world functional performance, as opposed to performance in a quiet, structured testing room.
**In use:** "The executive function scores were normal, but that has limited ecological validity for a job with constant multitasking and interruptions — the informant report matters more here."
**Common misuse:** assumed automatically high for any standardized test, when in fact structured single-task testing often underestimates real-world executive demands.

### Anosognosia
A neurologically based deficit in self-awareness of one's own impairment, distinct from psychological denial.
**In use:** "The discrepancy between his self-rated function and his wife's report looks like anosognosia given the frontal-pattern findings, not minimization."
**Common misuse:** used interchangeably with "denial," which implies a psychological defense mechanism rather than a neurological deficit of self-monitoring — the distinction changes how you counsel the family.

### Fixed vs. flexible battery
Fixed: the same standardized set of tests given to every patient regardless of referral question (e.g., Halstead-Reitan). Flexible: instrument selection tailored to the specific referral question (Boston Process Approach).
**In use:** "This is a forensic case, so we're running the fixed battery for full standardization even though it adds two hours."
**Common misuse:** treating "flexible" as meaning unstandardized or improvised — flexible-battery testing still uses fully standardized, normed instruments; only the selection is tailored.

### Demographically corrected norms
Normative comparison data adjusted for factors like age, education, sex, or race/ethnicity that affect expected test performance in a healthy population.
**In use:** "Using the demographically corrected norms instead of the general norms drops her from 'impaired' to 'low average' on this subtest."
**Common misuse:** framed as "lowering the bar" for certain groups, when the actual purpose is comparing the patient to the appropriate reference population so scores reflect pathology rather than demographic mismatch with the norm sample.

### Pseudodementia
A historical term for cognitive impairment that appears dementia-like but is primarily driven by depression, often with a retrieval-effort profile that improves with cueing.
**In use:** "The recall-with-normal-recognition pattern and the PHQ-9 of 19 both point toward a pseudodementia presentation rather than a primary amnestic process."
**Common misuse:** used to imply depression and true dementia are mutually exclusive — they frequently co-occur, and depression can also be an early symptom or mild accelerant of a genuine neurodegenerative process.

### Halstead-Reitan Battery
A comprehensive, standardized fixed battery of neuropsychological tests, historically central to forensic and research neuropsychology, yielding a composite Halstead Impairment Index.
**In use:** "The referral is disability litigation, so we're using the Halstead-Reitan for its established validity record under cross-examination."
**Common misuse:** treated as obsolete or purely historical — still in active forensic use precisely because of its long validation history, even though many clinical settings now favor flexible batteries.

### Practice effect
The improvement in test scores on retest attributable to prior exposure to the test itself, independent of any real cognitive change.
**In use:** "A 5-point gain on a 6-month retest is within the published practice-effect range for this subtest — I wouldn't call that recovery."
**Common misuse:** assumed to be roughly equal across all tests and domains; in reality, practice effects vary widely by instrument and are typically larger for novel-problem-solving tasks than for delayed-recall measures.

### Boston Process Approach
An assessment philosophy (Kaplan) that layers qualitative analysis of *how* a patient fails a task onto standardized quantitative scoring, to sharpen localization and differential diagnosis.
**In use:** "The process approach to the WCST shows he's making perseverative errors from failure to shift set, not from forgetting the rule — that's a different executive deficit than the raw error count alone would suggest."
**Common misuse:** confused with unstandardized or purely qualitative testing — administration and scoring remain fully standardized; the process analysis is added on top, not a replacement for it.

### Board certification (ABPP/ABCN)
Formal specialty credentialing through the American Board of Professional Psychology's Clinical Neuropsychology specialty board, requiring supervised fellowship training and a credentialing exam beyond state licensure.
**In use:** "For the surgical-candidacy evaluation, we want an ABPP/ABCN board-certified neuropsychologist, not just a licensed generalist psychologist doing occasional testing."
**Common misuse:** assuming "neuropsychologist" is itself a protected, verified title — in most states any licensed psychologist can call themselves one without board certification, so the credential (not the self-description) is what to check.
