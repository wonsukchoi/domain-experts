# Vocabulary

### Crosslevel
The difference in elevation between the two rails at a given point, measured perpendicular to the rails.
**In use:** "Crosslevel came back at 1.7 over the 62, that's a hard exception at this elevation, not a note."
**Common misuse:** Used interchangeably with "superelevation" — crosslevel is the measured condition at a point; superelevation (or "elevation") is the designed banking of a curve. A curve can have correct superelevation and still fail a crosslevel check if the *rate of change* between two nearby points is too abrupt.

### Warp
The difference in crosslevel between two points within a short interval (commonly discussed alongside the 62-ft crosslevel rule) — a twist in the track between those two points.
**In use:** "That's not a single low joint, the warp reading says the whole panel is twisting."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a synonym for "out of surface" generally. Warp specifically describes a twisting condition between two nearby points, not an overall profile dip — the fix (spread a gradual correction) differs from a straightforward lift.

### Runoff
The gradual distribution of a geometry correction (a lift, a line shift) over a length of track rather than taking it out abruptly at one point.
**In use:** "Don't take that whole inch out at one tie, runoff it over the panel or you'll trade a profile defect for a crosslevel one."
**Common misuse:** Skipped under time pressure by correcting a low spot at the single low tie — technically "fixes" the flagged station but creates a new, sharper defect immediately adjacent to it.

### Box anchoring
An anchor pattern using four anchors per tie (two per rail, one on each side of the tie), as opposed to single anchoring in only one direction.
**In use:** "This grade's boxed every tie, not every other — check the pattern before you assume it's standard."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to be a fixed, universal spec. The correct pattern (every tie vs. every other tie vs. every third tie) is territory-specific, driven by grade and traffic, not a constant.

### Consolidation
The settling and compaction of ballast under a tie to reach working density, achieved by a dynamic stabilizer pass or by early train loadings after tamping.
**In use:** "It'll test clean today, but it hasn't consolidated — get the stabilizer through before we run it at track speed."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to happen automatically or instantly from the tamping tools' squeeze action alone. Tamping loosens and repositions ballast around the tie; consolidation is a separate, subsequent process.

### Spot tamping
Tamping targeted at a specific defect location, using a cyclic (stop-at-each-tie) machine, as opposed to a continuous-action production tamper working a long section.
**In use:** "It's one low joint at a turnout, send the spot machine, don't mobilize the production tamper for that."
**Common misuse:** Used as a cost-cutting default for sections that actually need production tamping — spot tamping doesn't reach the surrounding panel's condition, so a spot fix next to an otherwise degrading section is a short-term patch.

### Fouled ballast
Ballast whose voids have filled with fines, coal dust, mud, or other material, reducing drainage and the ballast's ability to hold a tamped tie in place.
**In use:** "That's fouled, not just settled — tamping it again just repacks the fines, it needs undercutting."
**Common misuse:** Treated as visually obvious (dark, dirty-looking ballast). Fouling can be present below a clean-looking surface layer; the tell is repeated settlement at the same location, not appearance alone.

### Foul time
An on-track safety method where the dispatcher/control operator confirms no train will move within a defined section of controlled track until the roadway worker reports clear.
**In use:** "We're on foul time between the two switches, nothing moves till we report clear."
**Common misuse:** Confused with "working limits" broadly or with watchman/lookout protection — foul time is one specific method of establishing on-track safety, not a general term for "the crew has permission to be on the track."

### Fouling a track
Placement of a person or equipment close enough to a track to be struck by a train or on-track equipment — defined as within 4 feet of the field side of the near running rail, or closer if the equipment/person could be struck.
**In use:** "Machine's fouling the adjacent track, we need protection on it too before we start."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to mean physically standing on the rails. The 4-foot standard is a proximity definition, not a contact definition.

### Cant deficiency
The difference between a curve's actual (built) elevation and the theoretical elevation that would put the curve in balance at a given train speed.
**In use:** "At that speed on that elevation we're inside the 3-inch cant deficiency limit, so we're fine without a speed reduction."
**Common misuse:** Confused with elevation itself. A curve can have high elevation and still run high cant deficiency if train speed is high enough relative to that elevation, and vice versa at low speed.

### Versine
The offset measured from a chord to the rail at the chord's midpoint, used as a hand method to check curvature or profile without a geometry car.
**In use:** "Versine's off at station 40, pull a geometry-car confirmation before we trust the tamper's reference."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a precise, calibrated substitute for a geometry-car run. It's a fast field spot-check, useful for verifying a design reference is still current, not a replacement for instrumented measurement.

### Slow order / speed restriction
A temporary reduction in authorized speed over a section of track, commonly applied after disturbance (surfacing, undercutting) until the track has consolidated or been re-inspected.
**In use:** "Leave a slow order on it till the stabilizer gets through, don't release it at track speed off today's tamp."
**Common misuse:** Treated as an admission that the work wasn't done correctly. A planned, documented restriction after disturbance is standard practice, not a sign of a bad job — the failure mode is releasing disturbed track at full speed with no restriction at all.

### Undercutting
Removing and replacing fouled ballast from beneath the ties, typically with a dedicated undercutter machine, as opposed to surface-level tamping or regulating.
**In use:** "Tamping won't hold here, it's a drainage problem — this needs undercutting, not another surfacing pass."
**Common misuse:** Deferred repeatedly in favor of tamping because undercutting requires heavier mobilization — treated as a last resort rather than the correct fix once fouling is confirmed.
