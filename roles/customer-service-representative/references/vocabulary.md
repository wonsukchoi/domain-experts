# Customer Service Representative — Vocabulary

### First Call Resolution (FCR)
The percentage of contacts fully resolved during the customer's first interaction, with no follow-up contact needed for the same issue.
**In use:** "Our FCR on billing questions is 58% — that's why billing tickets keep reopening."
**Common misuse:** Treating a ticket marked "resolved" by the agent as proof of FCR — FCR should be measured by whether the customer actually didn't need to contact again, not by the agent's self-reported status.

### Customer Effort Score (CES)
A post-interaction survey metric (typically 1-5 or 1-7 scale) measuring how much effort the customer felt they had to expend to get their issue resolved.
**In use:** "CES on that ticket was a 5 even though it's marked resolved — something in the process broke."
**Common misuse:** Confusing CES with CSAT — a customer can be satisfied with the eventual outcome (high CSAT) while still reporting the process took too much effort (high/bad CES); they measure different things and can diverge.

### Service Recovery Paradox
The finding that a customer whose problem was well-handled after a failure can end up more loyal than a customer who never had a problem at all — conditional on the recovery being fast, complete, and genuine.
**In use:** "This is a service-recovery moment — the carrier failure was ours, and handling it well could land closer to the paradox than a routine complaint would."
**Common misuse:** Treating the paradox as automatic — assuming any apology plus small gesture will trigger it. Slow, partial, or clearly scripted recovery does not produce the paradox; it just adds a second disappointment.

### Net Promoter Score (NPS)
A single-question loyalty metric ("how likely are you to recommend us") scored -100 to +100 based on promoter/detractor/passive segmentation.
**In use:** "NPS is a good quarterly trend line, but I wouldn't use it to judge whether this one ticket went well."
**Common misuse:** Using NPS as a real-time or per-ticket quality signal — response rates are typically low and skew toward extreme experiences, making it a noisy instrument for judging a single interaction.

### Average Handle Time (AHT)
The average duration of a contact, including talk time, hold time, and after-call wrap-up work.
**In use:** "AHT went up on this issue type because the fix genuinely takes longer, not because agents are being slow."
**Common misuse:** Optimizing AHT in isolation — pushing agents to shorten calls without accounting for FCR often just shifts the same work into a second, third contact, raising total cost while the metric looks better.

### Warm transfer / cold transfer
A warm transfer briefs the receiving agent on the issue before connecting the customer, so the customer doesn't repeat themselves; a cold transfer simply routes the call/chat with no context passed.
**In use:** "Warm-transfer this to billing — I've already confirmed the double-charge, they just need to apply the credit."
**Common misuse:** Calling any transfer "warm" because a name was mentioned — the test is whether the customer has to re-explain the core facts, not whether the receiving agent was told who's calling.

### Discretion authority / approval threshold
The dollar amount or policy scope an agent is authorized to approve without escalation.
**In use:** "That's $180 total across three components — over my $150 combined threshold, so it's going to my supervisor with a recommendation."
**Common misuse:** Evaluating each component of a multi-part request against the threshold separately rather than the total ask — approving the parts under threshold and declining the rest fragments the resolution and often reads worse to the customer than one clean escalation.

### Root-cause tag
A categorical label applied to a resolved ticket identifying the underlying cause (defect, process failure, expectation mismatch, etc.), independent of how the ticket was resolved.
**In use:** "Tag this `process-failure` — the carrier missed the window, the product itself worked fine."
**Common misuse:** Tagging by symptom instead of cause (e.g. tagging everything about a late package as "shipping issue" without distinguishing carrier failure from a warehouse packing error) — symptom-level tags don't roll up into anything a team can act on.

### Escalation matrix
A documented table mapping request types and dollar thresholds to the authority level required to approve them.
**In use:** "Check the escalation matrix — free-shipment waivers over 3 orders need supervisor sign-off."
**Common misuse:** Treating the matrix as static — thresholds should be revisited as it becomes clear certain categories are chronically over- or under-escalated relative to actual risk.

### Churn
The loss of a customer who does not renew, cancel-and-leaves, or stops purchasing.
**In use:** "This account's churn risk went up the moment they mentioned the competitor's pricing."
**Common misuse:** Treating churn as a single event rather than a lagging indicator — by the time an account formally churns, the decision was usually made weeks or months earlier at the moment of the unresolved friction.

### Lifetime Value (LTV)
An estimate of the total revenue a customer will generate over their relationship with the business, used here as a simple annual-value × expected-tenure calculation.
**In use:** "At $85/month and a 4-year average tenure, this account's rough LTV is about $4,080 — the $180 exception is well within that."
**Common misuse:** Comparing a one-time cost (refund, credit) against a single order's value instead of against LTV — a $180 exception looks expensive against an $85 order and cheap against a $4,080 relationship; using the wrong denominator produces the wrong decision.

### Abuse pattern
A pattern of requests (repeated refunds, exploiting a return policy's edges) that suggests deliberate policy exploitation rather than genuine one-off issues.
**In use:** "Check the account history before approving — three 'damaged item' refund requests in two months might be an abuse pattern, not bad luck."
**Common misuse:** Labeling a single unusual request as abuse without checking history — one legitimate edge case looks identical to the first instance of a pattern; the distinction only shows up in the account's history, not in any single ticket.
