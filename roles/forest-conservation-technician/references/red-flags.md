# Red flags

Smell tests a crew lead, forester, or database reviewer catches when looking at cruise, remeasurement, or health-survey data. Format for each: what it usually means, the first question to ask, and the data to pull before deciding.

## 1. Every borderline prism call on a plot resolved "in"

**Usually means:** the crew is defaulting ambiguous calls to inclusion to keep pace rather than running the tape-and-distance check, which biases the plot's basal-area figure upward.

**First question:** "Show me the tape-and-clinometer check for each borderline call on this plot."

**Data to pull:** raw distance and DBH readings for each borderline tree, calculated limiting distance for the BAF in use, plot-level in-tree count before and after a spot recheck.

## 2. A remeasurement plot's growth figure is negative or wildly above the species' known growth rate

**Usually means:** a DBH measurement-point disagreement between crews (different point on a leaning, forked, or butt-swollen stem) or a plot-relocation error that swapped in a different tree, not real growth or bark loss.

**First question:** "What measurement point did this crew use versus the prior-cycle card, and is this the same tree?"

**Data to pull:** prior-cycle plot card measurement-point notes, current-cycle photos or notes on stem condition, plot relocation method used this visit.

## 3. Plot relocated using a hand-drawn map or memory instead of witness-tree bearings or logged GPS

**Usually means:** the crew skipped the documented relocation method, risking a remeasurement plot that's actually measuring a different set of trees than the original.

**First question:** "What witness-tree bearing/distance or GPS accuracy reading was logged for this relocation?"

**Data to pull:** original plot card witness-tree data, current visit's GPS accuracy/PDOP log, distance between this visit's plot center and the prior recorded coordinates.

## 4. No slope correction recorded on a plot with visibly steep terrain

**Usually means:** distances were taken as raw slope-distance readings without converting to horizontal, systematically overstating plot area, tree count, or fuel-transect length on that plot.

**First question:** "What was the clinometer slope reading, and was the correction factor applied to every distance on this plot?"

**Data to pull:** logged slope percentage, raw vs. corrected distance readings, plot-radius or limiting-distance calculations for any borderline trees on that plot.

## 5. Crown-dieback or damage class jumps by more than one class between survey cycles with no documented event

**Usually means:** either a real disturbance (drought, pest outbreak, storm damage) went unrecorded, or the current crew is applying the classification scale differently than the prior crew.

**First question:** "Is there a documented disturbance event for this plot between cycles, or did the crew change?"

**Data to pull:** prior and current class assignment against the published class-definition thresholds, disturbance/event log for the area, crew roster for both cycles.

## 6. A fuel-load transect record has a count but no logged azimuth, slope, or total length

**Usually means:** the transect was walked and tallied without capturing the metadata needed to reproduce or slope-correct the fuel-load estimate later.

**First question:** "What azimuth and slope were logged for this transect, and does the recorded length match the protocol's standard?"

**Data to pull:** transect metadata fields, protocol's specified standard length, any slope-correction factor applied to the raw count.

## 7. An increment core's ring count doesn't reach the pith

**Usually means:** the bore missed center, and the resulting age or growth-rate figure is an undercount rather than a true reading — not automatically a re-borable error, but not usable as-is either.

**First question:** "How far off-center did the core miss, and was that distance estimated and logged?"

**Data to pull:** core photo or scan, estimated miss-distance from pith, whether a second core was taken from a different angle.

## 8. A crew's tree tally counts are suspiciously round across many plots (e.g., consistently ending in 0 or 5)

**Usually means:** counts are being estimated or rounded in the field rather than tallied individually, especially under time pressure on a large plot.

**First question:** "Walk me through the tally sheet for one of these plots, tree by tree."

**Data to pull:** raw tally marks or data-recorder log (not the summarized count), time-per-plot compared to the crew's typical pace, cross-check count from a second crew member if available.

## 9. A full dataset from a crew shows zero QA flags across an entire cruise or survey season

**Usually means:** either the crew is unusually careful, or QA checks (borderline-call taping, out-of-range scans, relocation logging) aren't actually being run — a suspiciously clean dataset is itself a signal worth a spot audit.

**First question:** "Pull three random plots from this crew's season and re-run the QA checklist independently."

**Data to pull:** independent recheck of a random plot sample, comparison against the crew's logged QA checklist completions.

## 10. A cruise-derived volume estimate diverges sharply from actual harvest scale receipts on a comparable recently-cut stand

**Usually means:** a systematic cruise error (uncorrected borderline calls, wrong volume table, missed slope correction) rather than natural stand-to-stand variation, especially if the divergence is one-directional across multiple stands.

**First question:** "Is the divergence one-directional across stands, or does it vary — and which cruise inputs (BAF, volume table, slope correction) were common across the affected stands?"

**Data to pull:** cruise basal-area and volume-table inputs for the affected stands, mill scale receipts, plot-level borderline-call resolution rate for the cruising crew.
