# Vocabulary

Terms generalists conflate that cruising and inventory crews keep sharply separate. Each: definition, how a practitioner actually uses it in a sentence, and the common misuse to avoid.

## Limiting distance vs. plot radius

**Limiting distance** is the tree-specific boundary in a variable-radius (BAF/prism) point sample — it changes with every tree's diameter. **Plot radius** is a single fixed boundary that applies to every tree, used in fixed-radius plot designs. They answer the same "is this tree in or out" question with two different, non-interchangeable methods.

*In use:* "Don't use the fixed 24-foot subplot radius to check that prism call — this is a BAF-10 point sample, the limiting distance for that tree is DBH times 2.75, not a flat 24 feet."

*Common misuse:* applying a fixed-radius plot's boundary rule to a variable-radius cruise, or vice versa, when the plot design in use determines which rule applies.

## Basal-area factor (BAF) vs. fixed-radius plot

**BAF (basal-area factor)** point sampling uses an angle gauge (prism or relascope) so each "in" tree represents a fixed number of ft²/acre of basal area regardless of its size — efficient, but every borderline call carries weight. **Fixed-radius plot** sampling counts every tree within a set physical radius regardless of size — simpler borderline calls, but requires walking and measuring the full boundary.

*In use:* "This young stand's too dense and even for a clean BAF cruise — every point's giving us a pile of marginal calls, switch to fixed-radius for this unit."

*Common misuse:* assuming one method is universally "more accurate" — each has a stand condition where it performs worse (BAF in narrow-diameter dense stands, fixed-radius in stands requiring many plots to hit precision targets on sparse large trees).

## Subplot vs. microplot (FIA-style design)

**Subplot** is the larger fixed-radius plot component (24.0 ft radius) that tallies trees ≥ 5.0 in DBH. **Microplot** is the smaller nested component (6.8 ft radius) that tallies saplings and seedlings 1.0–4.9 in DBH.

*In use:* "That six-inch stem goes on the subplot tally, not the microplot — it's past the breakpoint diameter."

*Common misuse:* tallying a borderline-diameter stem on whichever plot is more convenient to reach instead of by the diameter breakpoint rule.

## Crown class vs. crown-dieback class

**Crown class** describes a tree's competitive position in the canopy (dominant, codominant, intermediate, suppressed) — a structural/competitive measure. **Crown-dieback class** describes the percent of the crown showing dead branches or foliage loss — a health/condition measure. A dominant tree can have severe dieback; a suppressed tree can have none.

*In use:* "It's a codominant crown class with 30% dieback — don't read the dieback number as if a suppressed tree just happened to look healthy."

*Common misuse:* treating crown position as a proxy for health, or vice versa — they're independent measurements on the same tree.

## Slope distance vs. horizontal distance

**Slope distance** is the raw tape or laser reading taken along the ground's actual slope. **Horizontal distance** is that reading corrected by the cosine of the slope angle — the number plot-radius and limiting-distance rules are actually defined against.

*In use:* "Forty feet on the tape at 25% slope is only about 38.8 feet horizontal — check the limiting distance against the corrected number, not the tape reading."

*Common misuse:* using an uncorrected slope-distance reading directly against a limiting-distance or plot-radius threshold, which systematically overstates plot area on any sloped ground.

## Cruise vs. inventory vs. stand exam

**Cruise** is a one-time or periodic sample (often BAF point sampling) to estimate current volume or basal area for a specific decision, like a timber sale. **Inventory** is a systematic, often permanent-plot program (like FIA) designed for repeated remeasurement and trend detection over years or decades. **Stand exam** is a broader, often qualitative-plus-quantitative walkthrough assessing regeneration, health, and silvicultural condition, not just volume.

*In use:* "We're cruising Stand 7 for the sale prospectus, but the permanent inventory plots in that same stand are a separate program — don't mix the two datasets."

*Common misuse:* treating a one-time cruise's plot data as if it were part of the permanent inventory's remeasurement record, or expecting a stand exam to produce a cruise-grade volume figure.

## Increment core vs. cross-section (for age/growth)

**Increment core** is a small-diameter boring taken with an increment borer from a living tree, minimally damaging, used to count rings without felling the tree. **Cross-section** (or "cookie") is a full disk cut from a felled or downed tree, giving a complete ring record but requiring the tree to already be down.

*In use:* "We can't cut a cross-section on a live leave-tree — take an increment core and make sure it reaches pith."

*Common misuse:* treating an increment core that missed the pith as equivalent in accuracy to a full cross-section, when a missed-pith core is a documented undercount, not a complete record.

## Witness tree vs. plot monument

**Witness tree** is a tagged, mapped tree near a plot center used as a bearing-and-distance reference to relocate the plot on remeasurement. **Plot monument** is a physical marker (rebar, PVC stake) driven at plot center itself. Programs often use both; either alone is weaker than the pair.

*In use:* "The monument's gone — pulled out or grown over — but the witness tree bearings still get us back to center within a foot."

*Common misuse:* relying on GPS alone when both a monument and witness trees exist and could cross-check the coordinate, especially under canopy where GPS accuracy degrades.

## Basal area vs. volume

**Basal area** is the cross-sectional area of a stem at breast height, summed per acre (ft²/acre) — a density measure independent of tree height or form. **Volume** (board feet, cubic feet, cords) converts basal area plus height and form information into an actual wood-quantity estimate via a volume table or equation.

*In use:* "Basal area's up to 130 per acre, but don't quote a volume number off that alone — pull the local volume table and factor in average merchantable height."

*Common misuse:* reporting basal area as if it directly implies a harvestable volume figure without applying a volume table or equation.

## 1-hr / 10-hr / 100-hr / 1000-hr fuel classes

Time-lag fuel classes describe how quickly a dead fuel particle's moisture content responds to atmospheric changes, and correlate roughly with diameter (1-hr: under 0.25 in; 10-hr: 0.25–1.0 in; 100-hr: 1.0–3.0 in; 1000-hr: over 3.0 in) — used in Brown's planar-intersect fuel-load transects.

*In use:* "The 1000-hr count on this transect includes two rotten logs — log those separately from sound ones, decay class changes the fuel-loading calculation."

*Common misuse:* treating the size-class labels as literal drying times measured in the field rather than a standardized diameter-based classification used to model fuel behavior.
