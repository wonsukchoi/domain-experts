# Vocabulary

### Bobbin tension
The tension applied to the lower (bobbin) thread in a lockstitch machine, which must be balanced against top thread tension for a properly formed stitch.
**In use:** "Top looks fine, but check bobbin tension too — loops on the underside usually trace back there, not the top setting."
**Common misuse:** Adjusting only top tension in response to a visible stitch problem — the visible symptom's side doesn't necessarily indicate which tension (top or bobbin) is actually unbalanced, and fixing the wrong side can mask rather than resolve the issue.

### Ballpoint needle
A sewing machine needle with a rounded (rather than sharp) point, designed to push between knit fabric fibers rather than piercing/cutting them.
**In use:** "Switching to a ballpoint 80/12 for this knit job — the sharp point we used on the woven bundle will cut fibers on this fabric."
**Common misuse:** Using a sharp-point needle (designed for woven fabric) on knit fabric out of habit or convenience — the sharp point cuts knit fibers instead of pushing between them, weakening the fabric at each stitch without visible damage at the time of sewing.

### Stitch density
The number of stitches per inch (or per unit length) along a seam, which affects both seam strength and the degree to which the fabric is perforated by needle penetrations.
**In use:** "We're not just bumping stitch density for strength on this lightweight fabric — too many perforations per inch and we risk tearing along the seam line instead."
**Common misuse:** Assuming higher stitch density is unconditionally stronger — on lightweight or loosely woven fabric, excessive perforation from a too-high stitch density can weaken the fabric along the seam line even as the stitching itself becomes denser.

### Seam type (stretch vs. non-stretch)
The specific stitch/seam construction used to join fabric pieces, chosen to match the fabric's behavior — stretch-capable seam types (like overlock) for knit/stretch fabrics, rigid seam types (like straight lockstitch) for wovens.
**In use:** "Overlock, not straight lockstitch — this fabric stretches and the seam needs to move with it, not fight it."
**Common misuse:** Selecting seam type based on what looks acceptable when the fabric is tested flat and unstressed — a seam's actual adequacy depends on the garment's use condition (stretching, washing, wear), which a flat visual check doesn't simulate.

### Stretch test
A quality check pulling a seam to a target elongation percentage to verify it holds without popping, simulating the fabric's actual use condition rather than relying on a flat, unstressed visual check.
**In use:** "Pulled the seam to about thirty percent stretch, simulating normal wear movement — held fine, cleared for production."
**Common misuse:** Relying solely on a flat visual inspection for stretch fabric seams — a seam can look perfectly formed at rest and still fail under the stretch the garment will actually experience in normal use.

### Piece-rate production
A pay structure where an operator is compensated per unit produced rather than per hour, creating an incentive to maximize speed that can conflict with taking time for in-process quality checks.
**In use:** "I know it's piece-rate, but the start-of-bundle test seam isn't optional — catching a setup error at unit two beats catching it at unit two hundred."
**Common misuse:** Treating piece-rate incentives as a reason to skip verification steps — the time cost of a quick test seam is far smaller than the cost of reworking or scrapping units produced under an undetected setup error.

### Needle burr
A small, often invisible-to-the-naked-eye defect on a needle's point or shaft, typically from wear or a minor impact, that can cause fabric snags, skipped stitches, or thread damage.
**In use:** "Thread's fraying at the same spot every few stitches — that's a burred needle, not a tension issue."
**Common misuse:** Attributing thread breakage or fabric damage automatically to tension settings without checking needle condition — a burred needle produces symptoms that look similar to a tension problem but require a different fix (needle replacement, not a tension adjustment).

### Bundle
A grouped batch of cut fabric pieces (with an accompanying ticket specifying fabric, style, and quantity) moving together through production as a unit.
**In use:** "New bundle just came in — different fabric than the last one, so we're not just picking up where the machine left off."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a new bundle can be started on the machine's existing settings without checking whether the fabric type has changed from the prior bundle — bundles frequently differ in fabric, and settings appropriate for one can be wrong for the next.

### Lockstitch vs. overlock (serger)
Two distinct stitch/machine types: lockstitch interlocks a single top and bobbin thread in a straight, generally non-stretch seam; overlock (serger) uses multiple threads looping over the fabric edge, producing a seam that can stretch with knit fabric and often finishes the raw edge simultaneously.
**In use:** "This side seam needs to be overlocked, not lockstitched — the knit fabric needs a seam that stretches with it."
**Common misuse:** Treating "lockstitch" and "overlock" as interchangeable stitch options chosen by appearance or convenience — they have fundamentally different mechanical behavior under stretch, and the wrong choice on stretch fabric produces a seam that fails under normal wear even though it looks fine unstressed.

### Thread weight/type matching
Selecting a thread's weight and fiber type to match the fabric weight, needle size, and seam's functional requirement, rather than using a single default thread across different jobs.
**In use:** "Heavier thread for this canvas job — the lightweight thread we used on the knit bundle would be undersized for this seam's load."
**Common misuse:** Using the same thread across fabric types regardless of weight differences — a thread too light for a heavy fabric under-delivers seam strength, while one too heavy for lightweight fabric can cause puckering or excessive fabric distortion.
