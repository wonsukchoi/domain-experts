# Vocabulary

Terms generalists conflate or misuse, kept sharply separate by anyone managing a training/competition/career cycle for a living.

## ACWR (acute:chronic workload ratio)

The ratio of an athlete's training load over the last 7 days (acute) to their average weekly load over the trailing 28 days (chronic). A single number meant to catch "did load spike faster than the body adapted to it."

**In use:** "We're sitting at 1.6 this week — that's not a good week, that's the injury-risk zone, trim Thursday and Friday."

**Common misuse:** treating a high single-day load as the problem instead of the ratio — one very hard session inside a well-built chronic base is fine; the same session on top of two weeks off is not the same risk at all.

## LSI (limb symmetry index)

The injured limb's test result expressed as a percentage of the uninjured limb's result (strength, hop distance, etc.). The standard return-to-sport threshold is ≥90% on each individual test, not the average across tests.

**In use:** "Hop testing's fine at 90.5%, but quad strength is 78.6% — that's the number that matters, and it fails."

**Common misuse:** averaging multiple LSI scores together and clearing an athlete because the average clears 90%, when the actual rule is every test individually.

## Periodization (macrocycle / mesocycle / microcycle)

The structured division of a training year (macrocycle) into phases of several weeks (mesocycles: general prep, specific prep, competition, taper) built from individual training weeks (microcycles), each with a defined volume/intensity target.

**In use:** "We're in the specific-prep mesocycle now — volume comes down, intensity comes up, and we hold that until the taper starts week 12."

**Common misuse:** treating "periodization" as a synonym for "having a training plan" — the actual content is the deliberate, phase-specific manipulation of volume and intensity toward a target date, not just sequencing workouts.

## Taper

A planned, temporary reduction in training volume (typically 40–60%) while intensity is held near-competition level, in the 1–3 weeks before a target competition, to dissipate accumulated fatigue while preserving fitness.

**In use:** "Two-week taper starting Monday — volume's dropping to about half, but Tuesday and Friday sessions stay at competition pace."

**Common misuse:** cutting intensity along with volume (or resting completely), which loses sharpness and often costs more readiness than the extra rest returns.

## Session-RPE / training load

A load-quantification method: session duration in minutes multiplied by a 0–10 rating of perceived exertion (Borg-derived scale) for that session, summed across a day or week. Requires no wearable.

**In use:** "That session was 75 minutes at an 8 — that's 600 load units, one of the heaviest days this block."

**Common misuse:** comparing raw RPE scores across athletes as if they measured the same absolute intensity — RPE is a within-athlete, relative measure; two athletes reporting "7" may be at very different physiological intensities.

## Functional vs. non-functional overreaching vs. overtraining syndrome

A spectrum of accumulated fatigue. Functional overreaching: short-term performance dip that supercompensates into a gain within days given recovery (a normal, intended part of hard training blocks). Non-functional overreaching (NFOR): the same dip, but recovery takes weeks and performance doesn't rebound on schedule. Overtraining syndrome (OTS): a much longer (months), often hormonally and psychologically involved state requiring extended recovery.

**In use:** "This dip looks like planned overreaching from the block we just ran — give it 5 days and reassess before calling it anything worse."

**Common misuse:** labeling any bad training week "overtraining" — true OTS is rare and severe; most fatigue dips are functional overreaching resolving within the recovery week already on the calendar.

## Whereabouts (WADA / ADAMS)

The requirement for athletes in a national or international registered testing pool to file their daily location (including a specific 60-minute testing window) via the ADAMS system, enabling no-notice testing.

**In use:** "That's strike two on whereabouts this year — one more miss in the rolling 12 months and it's a violation, full stop, no test required."

**Common misuse:** assuming whereabouts failures only matter if a test was actually missed as a result — three filing failures alone constitute a violation under the strict-liability framework, independent of any test.

## Strict liability (anti-doping)

The legal principle that any prohibited substance found in an athlete's body, or any whereabouts failure, is charged to that athlete regardless of intent, knowledge, or fault — the finding itself triggers the process; intent affects the sanction length, not whether a violation occurred.

**In use:** "It doesn't matter that the supplement label didn't list it — strict liability means it's on him that it was in his system."

**Common misuse:** treating "I didn't mean to" or "I didn't know what was in it" as a defense against the violation itself, rather than (at most) a mitigating factor in sanction length.

## Guaranteed vs. non-guaranteed contract money

Guaranteed money is owed to the athlete regardless of subsequent injury, release, or performance decline; non-guaranteed money (common in some leagues' base salary structures) can be voided if the team releases the athlete before it's earned.

**In use:** "The headline number is $40M, but only $18M of that is actually guaranteed — that's the number to negotiate on, not the topline."

**Common misuse:** quoting total contract value as if it were a promised sum — the guaranteed fraction is the number that survives injury or release, and it can be a small fraction of the reported total.

## Contract advisor / agent fee cap

The maximum percentage of a negotiated contract's value that a certified agent or contract advisor may charge, set by the relevant players' union (varies by league — commonly a few percent of the contract).

**In use:** "Their standard rate is at the union cap — anything above that in the fine print needs an explanation."

**Common misuse:** assuming the cap applies to every dollar an agency touches — training, marketing, and personal-services fees are frequently separate agreements not covered by the negotiation-fee cap, and need their own scrutiny.

## HRV (heart rate variability) baseline

Variation in time between successive heartbeats, tracked as a rolling personal baseline (commonly 7-day) rather than a population norm or single-day value.

**In use:** "He's two days below his own rolling baseline — not a crisis yet, but if it's still down tomorrow we back off."

**Common misuse:** comparing one athlete's raw HRV to another's, or to a generic "healthy" range — the signal is deviation from that individual's own trend, not an absolute threshold.

## Return-to-sport vs. return-to-play vs. return-to-performance

Return-to-play: cleared to participate in team training/competition. Return-to-sport: cleared against sport-specific objective testing criteria (strength, function, psychological readiness). Return-to-performance: back to pre-injury competitive output level, which can trail return-to-sport clearance by additional months.

**In use:** "He's return-to-sport cleared, but don't expect return-to-performance numbers for another 6–8 weeks of live competition reps."

**Common misuse:** using the three terms interchangeably, especially reporting "cleared to play" (return-to-play) as if it means the athlete is back to their pre-injury performance level.

## Monotony and strain (Foster method)

Monotony: the ratio of mean daily training load to the standard deviation of daily loads across a week — a high number means the week lacked hard/easy variation. Strain: weekly total load multiplied by monotony — a composite fatigue-risk index.

**In use:** "Monotony's at 1.7, fine, but if we run three more weeks like this without a real easy day it'll climb past 2 and strain goes with it."

**Common misuse:** monitoring total weekly load alone and missing that two weeks of identical moderate totals (high monotony) can carry more illness/injury risk than one week with a much higher total but real hard/easy contrast.
