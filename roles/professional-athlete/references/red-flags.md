# Red flags

Smell tests a veteran athlete or performance team catches before they become an injury, a violation, or a bad contract. Format: what it usually means, the first question to ask, and the data to pull.

## 1. ACWR above ~1.5 for two or more consecutive weeks

**Usually means:** training volume or intensity ramped faster than the body's adapted-to (chronic) load, most often from a compressed pre-season, a return from a break, or a coach chasing a result with extra sessions.

**First question:** "What changed in the last 7–10 days — new sessions added, longer sessions, or a jump in intensity — versus the prior month?"

**Data to pull:** daily session-RPE or GPS load for the last 5 weeks; acute (7-day) vs. chronic (28-day) load ratio trend, not just this week's snapshot.

## 2. HRV or resting heart rate off baseline for 3+ consecutive mornings

**Usually means:** accumulating fatigue, incomplete recovery, illness onset, or a life-stress load stacking on top of training load — HRV reflects total systemic stress, not just training.

**First question:** "Is sleep, travel, or anything outside training different this week — and has this reading recovered at all across the 3 days, or is it flat?"

**Data to pull:** 7-day rolling HRV/RHR baseline vs. daily readings; sleep duration/quality log; subjective wellness score (soreness, mood, energy) for the same window.

## 3. Quad/hop-test LSI under 90% but the athlete reports feeling ready

**Usually means:** the athlete's subjective confidence has recovered faster than objective strength or control — common, and specifically the profile associated with elevated reinjury risk, because confidence outpacing capacity is exactly when an athlete pushes a joint past what it can currently protect.

**First question:** "Show me this week's actual LSI numbers on each test, not the trend direction — where exactly do we sit against 90%?"

**Data to pull:** most recent strength and hop-test battery results per limb; psychological-readiness score; retest date and trajectory over the last 3 tests.

## 4. Weekly monotony above ~2.0 with strain trending up

**Usually means:** training days have become too uniform — every day roughly the same load — removing the hard/easy contrast that recovery depends on; this combination is the profile most associated with illness and non-contact soft-tissue injury clusters.

**First question:** "What does the actual day-by-day load look like this week, not just the weekly total?"

**Data to pull:** daily session-RPE or GPS load values (not just the weekly sum) for monotony/strain calculation; illness/injury incidence log for the prior similar high-monotony period, if any.

## 5. Two whereabouts filing failures within a rolling 12-month window

**Usually means:** an administrative gap (missed the 60-minute testing window, wrong location on file, quarterly update not submitted) — but the anti-doping system treats it identically regardless of intent, and a third failure in the window is itself a violation with no positive test required.

**First question:** "What exactly caused the last miss, and is the filing system (calendar, assistant, app) actually being checked daily, or only when a test is expected?"

**Data to pull:** ADAMS filing history and the exact dates/reasons for prior misses; current quarter's filed whereabouts against the athlete's actual daily schedule.

## 6. Rapid weight cut exceeding ~5% of body mass in under a week (weigh-in sports)

**Usually means:** the cut is being made through dehydration rather than fat-mass loss, which degrades strength, cognitive function, and cardiovascular capacity right before competition — the opposite of the intended effect if it's severe enough.

**First question:** "What fraction of this cut is water versus actual mass loss over the training camp, and what's the rehydration/refeed plan between weigh-in and competition?"

**Data to pull:** body-mass trend over the full camp (not just fight-week numbers); urine specific gravity or similar hydration markers if available; performance metrics (power output, reaction time) from the last cut of similar size.

## 7. Agent/advisor fee above the union cap or bundled with unclear pass-throughs

**Usually means:** either an honest miscalculation or a fee structure designed to exceed the cap under a different label ("marketing fee," "training-facility fee") — either way it needs to be itemized before signing.

**First question:** "Can you itemize the negotiation fee separately from any marketing, training, or personal-services fees, and show me each against the union's published cap?"

**Data to pull:** the union's current fee-cap rule for this league/sport; the full fee schedule in the proposed representation agreement, line by line.

## 8. Public return-date promise made before testing criteria are met

**Usually means:** media, sponsor, or team pressure has set an external deadline that the recovery process now has to be bent to fit, rather than the reverse — this is how testing gets rushed or downplayed near the promised date.

**First question:** "What were the actual test results the last time we checked, and does the promised date assume we hit every subsequent milestone with zero slippage?"

**Data to pull:** most recent objective testing results and trend; the specific public statement and who made it (athlete, agent, team PR).

## 9. Sudden drop (>10%) in a standard performance metric (sprint time, jump height, bar speed) with unremarkable soreness reports

**Usually means:** early non-functional overreaching or the leading edge of overtraining syndrome — performance markers typically degrade before subjective soreness catches up, so a "feels fine" report alongside a real metric drop is not reassuring, it's a leading indicator.

**First question:** "How does this week's number compare to the athlete's own rolling baseline over the last 8–12 weeks, not to a single best-ever mark?"

**Data to pull:** the specific metric's trend (not single-session value) over 8–12 weeks; concurrent load, sleep, and HRV data for the same window.

## 10. No named post-career plan within 2 years of the sport's typical retirement age

**Usually means:** identity and income are still fully tied to active competition with no transition structure in motion, which is the setup for the well-documented post-career financial-distress pattern in multiple pro leagues.

**First question:** "What's the specific next step — coaching, media, ownership, education, business — and what's actually been done toward it this year, not just discussed?"

**Data to pull:** current savings/investment rate versus peak-year income; any concrete steps taken (certifications started, relationships built, equity secured) toward the named next step.
