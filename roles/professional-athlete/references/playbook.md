# Playbook

Operational sequences an athlete (with their performance/medical/agent team) actually runs, with structure, thresholds, and example numbers. Adapt the specific numbers to the sport — the sequences and thresholds transfer.

## 1. Periodized training block, built backward from a competition date

**Structure (16-week block example, field-sport pre-season into a season opener):**

| Phase | Weeks | Volume | Intensity | Focus |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| General preparation | 1–5 | High (100% baseline) | Low–moderate (60–75% max) | Aerobic base, tissue capacity, movement quality |
| Specific preparation | 6–11 | Moderate–high (85–100%) | Moderate–high (75–90%) | Sport-specific conditioning, strength transfer |
| Pre-competition | 12–14 | Moderate (70–85%) | High (85–95%) | Speed, power, competition-simulation |
| Taper | 15–16 | Low (40–60% of peak) | Held high (90–100%) | Fatigue dissipation, peak readiness |

**Weekly load guardrail:** compute ACWR = (last 7 days' total load) ÷ (trailing 28-day average load, expressed as a weekly rate). Load = session-RPE (duration in minutes × RPE 0–10) summed per day, or GPS-derived total distance/high-speed-running distance where available.

*Worked example, week 9 of the block:*
- Daily loads (session-RPE, last 7 days): 420, 380, 0 (rest), 510, 440, 360, 0 (rest) → acute total = 2,110, acute weekly rate = 2,110.
- Trailing 4-week daily average: 285/day → chronic weekly rate = 285 × 7 = 1,995.
- ACWR = 2,110 ÷ 1,995 = **1.058** — inside the 0.8–1.3 sweet spot. Hold planned progression.
- If instead the acute total had been 3,200 (ACWR = 3,200 ÷ 1,995 = **1.60**), that crosses the ~1.5 risk-spike line — the next planned hard day gets converted to a moderate or recovery day, and the week's total volume gets trimmed 15–20%, not just the single session.

**Monotony/strain check (Foster method), same week:**
- Mean daily load = 2,110 ÷ 7 = 301.4. Standard deviation of the 7 daily values (420, 380, 0, 510, 440, 360, 0) = **195.7**.
- Monotony = mean ÷ SD = 301.4 ÷ 195.7 = **1.54** — under the ~2.0 flag threshold, acceptable given the two rest days provide real hard/easy contrast.
- Strain = weekly total load × monotony = 2,110 × 1.54 = **3,250** — compare week-over-week; a strain spike >30% over the prior week's strain with monotony also elevated is the illness/injury-risk signature, independent of the ACWR read.

## 2. Taper protocol

- **Duration:** 1–3 weeks depending on the preceding block's length and accumulated fatigue — longer blocks (12+ weeks) taper toward 3 weeks; shorter in-season blocks taper 1 week.
- **Volume:** cut 40–60% from peak week (e.g., peak week acute load 2,400 → taper week 1 target 960–1,440 → taper week 2 target further down toward the bottom of that band, ~960–1,100, if running a 2-week taper).
- **Intensity:** held at 90–100% of competition intensity — a small number of high-quality, competition-speed efforts, not steady-state volume.
- **Frequency:** reduced modestly (drop one session/week), not collapsed — total silence in the days before competition often costs more sharpness than it returns in freshness.
- **Monitoring during taper:** HRV/resting-HR should trend back toward or above baseline by the final week; if it's still depressed 2–3 days out, the preceding block under-recovered and the taper needs an extra easy day inserted, not more volume cut from an already-light week.

## 3. Return-to-sport testing protocol (post-injury)

**All three legs required — passing two of three is not clearance:**

1. **Strength symmetry:** isokinetic (or 1RM-equivalent) peak torque/force, injured vs. uninjured limb. LSI = injured ÷ uninjured × 100. Threshold ≥90%.
2. **Functional symmetry:** hop-test battery (single-leg hop for distance, triple hop for distance, crossover hop, 6-meter timed hop), each scored as an LSI. Threshold ≥90% on each, not just the average.
3. **Psychological readiness:** validated instrument (e.g., ACL-Return to Sport after Injury scale, 0–100). Cohort benchmark ≥65 associated with lower reinjury risk; scores well below that with strong physical testing usually mean the athlete needs graded exposure drills (controlled contact, reactive agility) before full clearance, not more strength work.

**Retest cadence:** weekly once within ~15 percentage points of threshold on any test; every 2–3 weeks earlier in the process. Log every retest — the trend line matters as much as any single number for setting an honest return date.

**When criteria conflict with the calendar:** state the gap in percentage points and the retest date, not a vague "not ready" — a team can plan around "quad LSI is 78.6%, needs ~11 points, retest in 3 weeks" in a way it can't plan around "not comfortable yet."

## 4. Contract / financial checklist (career-window framing)

- **Guaranteed vs. non-guaranteed money:** what fraction of total contract value is guaranteed regardless of injury or release, and does injury-guarantee language cover the specific injury types common to this sport.
- **Fee structure:** confirm agent/advisor fee against the union cap (e.g., ~3% NFL, ~4% NBA max) and get every "marketing," "training," or "recovery" pass-through itemized separately from the negotiation fee — bundled fees are the most common way a fee exceeds the cap without saying so.
- **Disability/loss-of-value insurance:** in place before training camp or the next competitive cycle starts, not after an injury makes it uninsurable or prohibitively priced.
- **Post-career income plan:** a named next step (coaching pipeline, media, ownership stake, formal education, business) identified and in motion at least 2 years before the median retirement age for the sport — not started at the retirement press conference.
- **Savings/investment rate during peak-earning years:** default to a majority of peak-year income saved or invested (not lifestyle-inflated to match peak income) when the sport's typical career length is under 6–8 years, absent a specific reason post-career income is already secured.

## 5. Media / public-statement protocol during injury or contract situations

- **Never state a specific return date publicly** until the testing criteria for that phase have actually passed — a stated date becomes an external deadline that pressures the criteria-based process.
- **Standard holding language:** "Testing well, following the medical team's protocol, will be ready when the numbers say so" — true, non-committal, and doesn't hand an opponent or a negotiating counterpart a data point.
- **Contract-year injuries:** route all specifics through the agent; the athlete's public comments and the agent's negotiating position need to match, and a casual "feeling great" in an interview can undercut a disability claim or a negotiating stance built on documented risk.
