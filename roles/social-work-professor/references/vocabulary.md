# Working Vocabulary — Terms Generalists Misuse

Format per term: definition, a sentence the way a practitioner actually uses it, and the common misuse to avoid.

---

**Field instructor vs. field liaison vs. task supervisor**
Field instructor: the agency-based MSW professional (2+ years post-degree, EPAS M3.2) who holds primary responsibility for the student's field education and competency evaluation. Field liaison: the program-based faculty/staff member who visits, monitors the placement, and connects agency practice back to the curriculum. Task supervisor: an on-site, non-MSW staff member who supervises day-to-day tasks when the field instructor is off-site, but cannot sign off on competency evaluation.
*Usage:* "The task supervisor manages her daily caseload assignments, but the field instructor is who completes the Tevera competency evaluation at midterm."
*Misuse:* calling anyone who oversees a student's daily work a "field instructor" — the credential requirement and the evaluation authority attach specifically to that title, not to whoever happens to supervise tasks.

**EPAS (Educational Policy and Accreditation Standards)**
CSWE's governing document (currently the 2022 revision) specifying the nine practice competencies, field-education hour minimums, and faculty/field-instructor qualifications every accredited BSW/MSW program must meet.
*Usage:* "That curriculum change needs to map to a specific EPAS competency before it goes to the curriculum committee."
*Misuse:* treating EPAS as a vague "accreditation stuff" umbrella rather than citing the actual standard number — the site-visit team cites standards, not themes.

**Benchmark vs. threshold**
Benchmark: the target percentage of students expected to demonstrate a competency at or above the "competent" rating (e.g., 80%). Threshold: the specific point on the assessment rubric's scale that counts as "meeting" the competency for any individual student.
*Usage:* "Our benchmark is 80% of the cohort at or above threshold on Competency 7; this cohort came in at 62%."
*Misuse:* using the words interchangeably in a self-study, which makes it unclear whether the reported number describes the cohort or an individual student's rubric score — reviewers read this as sloppy data reporting.

**Signature pedagogy**
Lee Shulman's term (2005) for the characteristic teaching method that shapes how a profession's practitioners learn to think and act, applied to social work by Bogo, Wayne & Raskin (2010) to describe supervised field education.
*Usage:* "Field education isn't a graduation requirement bolted onto the classroom — it's the signature pedagogy, which is why the site visit audits it first."
*Misuse:* using the phrase as a synonym for "important" or "core" generically, detached from the specific claim that it's where professional judgment is actually formed, not merely applied.

**Advanced standing**
An accelerated MSW track for students who hold a CSWE-accredited BSW, allowing partial or full exemption from the foundation-year field hours (since the foundation competencies were already demonstrated in the BSW program).
*Usage:* "She's advanced standing, so she starts directly in the 900-hour concentration-year placement rather than repeating the foundation practicum."
*Misuse:* assuming advanced standing shortens the *concentration*-year requirement too — it exempts foundation-year content, not the full degree's field-hour minimum.

**Practice-informed research vs. research-informed practice**
Two directions of the same EPAS competency (4): practice-informed research means practitioner experience shapes what gets studied and how; research-informed practice means published findings shape what practitioners do in the field.
*Usage:* "The study came out of a practice-informed research design — the intervention was built from what our field instructors were already seeing work, then tested formally."
*Misuse:* treating the competency as a one-directional pipeline (research produces findings, practice consumes them) when the standard explicitly requires evidence of both directions.

**Reaffirmation vs. initial accreditation**
Initial accreditation: the first-time review a new program undergoes. Reaffirmation: the recurring review (an 8-year cycle) an already-accredited program undergoes to keep its status, involving a self-study and site visit.
*Usage:* "We're two years out from reaffirmation, so this is exactly the cycle to be building the competency-benchmark trend data we'll need."
*Misuse:* treating reaffirmation as a formality because the program is already accredited — the site visit team can and does cite programs for standards drift since the last review.

**Boyer's scholarship categories**
Ernest Boyer's four-part framework (1990): discovery (original research), integration (synthesis across fields), application (practice-engaged, problem-driven scholarship), and teaching (scholarship of pedagogy itself) — used in social work tenure review to credit work that doesn't fit a bench-science "discovery only" model.
*Usage:* "This program evaluation contract is Boyer's application category — label it that way in the packet instead of letting the committee default to calling it consulting."
*Misuse:* letting a tenure committee (or the candidate) collapse all four categories into "counts" or "doesn't count as research," when the framework's purpose is to name which category a given piece of work belongs to and argue its weight explicitly.

**ASWB (Association of Social Work Boards) exam**
The licensure examination (Bachelors, Masters, Clinical, and Advanced Generalist categories) most states require for social work licensure, distinct from the academic degree itself.
*Usage:* "Our pass rate on the Masters-level ASWB exam is a program outcome we track separately from EPAS competency benchmarks."
*Misuse:* conflating ASWB pass rates with EPAS competency benchmarks — they're two different accountability systems (state licensure board vs. accreditor) measuring different things, and a strong result on one doesn't imply the other.

**Concentration year vs. foundation year**
Foundation year: generalist practice curriculum and field hours common to all MSW students. Concentration year: the specialized second year (e.g., clinical, macro) with its own field placement and the bulk of the 900-hour EPAS minimum.
*Usage:* "The competency benchmark that's failing is measured in the concentration-year placement, not the foundation year — so the fix belongs in the specialized curriculum, not the intro courses."
*Misuse:* applying a foundation-year fix (a generalist skills lab) to a concentration-year benchmark problem, which addresses the wrong curriculum layer.

**Mandatory reporting**
The legal obligation (state-specific, but universal in substance) to report suspected child abuse, elder abuse, or certain other harms disclosed during field placement or research contact, regardless of confidentiality norms that would otherwise apply.
*Usage:* "That disclosure triggers mandatory reporting the same day — route it through the agency and campus counsel simultaneously, don't hold it for the field seminar."
*Misuse:* treating a mandatory-reporting disclosure as primarily a pedagogical moment (a case study to discuss in class) before the compliance obligation is satisfied — the reporting duty is not deferrable for teaching purposes.

**Common Rule / 45 CFR 46 vulnerable-population subparts**
The federal human-subjects research regulation; Subparts B, C, and D add specific protections for pregnant women/fetuses, prisoners, and children respectively, on top of the general Subpart A protections.
*Usage:* "Since a third of the sample is under 18, this protocol needs Subpart D review, not just the standard expedited category."
*Misuse:* assuming a study population's status as "clients receiving services" rather than "research subjects" exempts it from Subpart review — service-context data collection for a published study is still human-subjects research.
