### Debt management plan (DMP)
A structured repayment plan, typically administered by a nonprofit credit counseling agency, that consolidates a client's unsecured debts into a single monthly payment, often with negotiated creditor interest-rate concessions.
**In use:** "The DMP consolidates all three cards into one $519/month payment at a negotiated 9% blended rate."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a DMP automatically means a lower interest rate without verifying the agency's actual negotiated terms with each specific creditor.

### Debt-to-income ratio (DTI)
The ratio of a person's debt payment obligations to their income, used as one indicator of debt burden — but not a complete picture of whether debt is actually being paid down.
**In use:** "DTI looks manageable at 17.9%, but that doesn't tell us whether these payments are actually retiring principal."
**Common misuse:** Treating an acceptable DTI as evidence the debt situation is under control, without checking whether payments at current rates are actually reducing the balance over a reasonable time.

### Creditor concession
A reduction in interest rate, waiver of fees, or other modified term a creditor agrees to as part of a debt management plan, typically negotiated through the counseling agency's established relationship with that creditor.
**In use:** "This creditor's concession under our agency's DMP program brings the rate down to 9% from 24%."
**Common misuse:** Assuming concession terms are standardized across all creditors and agencies rather than specific to each agency's negotiated relationships.

### Debt settlement
An arrangement where a creditor agrees to accept less than the full amount owed to resolve a debt, typically negotiated by the debtor or a settlement company rather than through a structured DMP.
**In use:** "Debt settlement could reduce the balance owed, but it will likely tank her credit score and create a tax bill on the forgiven amount."
**Common misuse:** Presenting debt settlement as a straightforwardly better outcome than a DMP without accounting for its credit-score damage and tax consequences on forgiven debt.

### Cancellation of debt (COD) income
Income the IRS treats as taxable when a creditor forgives or settles a debt for less than the full amount owed, typically reported to the debtor via Form 1099-C.
**In use:** "The $8,000 forgiven in this settlement will likely generate a 1099-C and be taxed as income next year."
**Common misuse:** Assuming forgiven debt has no further financial consequence once the settlement is reached, without accounting for the tax liability it can create.

### Means test
The income-based test used in Chapter 7 bankruptcy to determine eligibility, comparing a debtor's income against the state median for their household size.
**In use:** "His income is above the state median, so we need to check whether he passes the means test for Chapter 7 or needs Chapter 13 instead."
**Common misuse:** Assuming Chapter 7 is automatically available regardless of income level, without applying the specific means test calculation.

### Approved credit counseling provider
An agency specifically approved by the U.S. Trustee Program to provide the pre-filing credit counseling and pre-discharge financial management course required under federal bankruptcy law.
**In use:** "Before she can file, she needs a certificate from an agency approved for her specific district."
**Common misuse:** Assuming any reputable nonprofit credit counseling agency satisfies the bankruptcy counseling requirement, without confirming its specific approval status for the applicable court district.

### Debt snowball method
A debt payoff strategy that prioritizes paying off the smallest balance first (while making minimums on others), intended to build early motivation and momentum.
**In use:** "Given his history of abandoning payoff plans, the snowball method's quick wins might keep him engaged longer than avalanche would."
**Common misuse:** Assuming the snowball method is inferior simply because it isn't mathematically optimal, without considering whether it better fits the client's likely follow-through.

### Debt avalanche method
A debt payoff strategy that prioritizes paying off the highest-interest-rate balance first, minimizing total interest paid over the life of the payoff plan.
**In use:** "Avalanche will save her more in total interest, and she's shown strong follow-through on past financial goals, so it's the right fit here."
**Common misuse:** Defaulting to avalanche for every client as the "correct" method without considering whether a client's behavioral pattern makes snowball more likely to actually succeed.

### Disposable income (for debt counseling purposes)
The amount of income remaining after essential living expenses (housing, utilities, food, transportation, insurance, minimum required obligations), available for debt repayment.
**In use:** "Disposable income is $1,400/month — that's the ceiling for what any DMP or repayment plan needs to fit within."
**Common misuse:** Confusing disposable income with gross or net income overall, without first subtracting essential expenses to find what's actually available for debt payments.
