# Vocabulary

Terms generalists conflate that choreographers keep sharply separate. Each: definition, how a practitioner actually uses it in a sentence, and the common misuse to avoid.

## Marking vs. full-out

**Marking** is running a phrase at reduced physical output — walking through positions and gesturing the shape — to conserve stamina between full runs. **Full-out** is dancing at intended performance energy and speed. Both are standard, deliberate rehearsal tools, not shortcuts.

**In use:** "We've marked this three times today — I need one full-out before we call it, or we won't know if the lift timing actually holds at speed."

**Common misuse:** treating marking as wasted rehearsal time to be minimized, or treating it as equivalent to a full-out run for judging whether a phrase is ready.

## Eight-count / count structure

An **eight-count** is the basic unit of choreographic phrasing — eight beats of music, typically two measures of 4/4 — used to teach, rehearse, and discuss a piece regardless of the underlying musical meter. The **count structure** is the full map of eight-counts across a piece's music.

**In use:** "The lift needs to land on count 5, not 6 — shift the prep back an eight-count and it'll clear."

**Common misuse:** assuming the count structure is fixed once the audio track is locked; the track can often still be edited if the count structure itself needs to change for legibility.

## Spacing rehearsal vs. blocking

**Blocking** is the initial assignment of where dancers stand and move for a formation. A **spacing rehearsal** is a dedicated session that re-checks that blocking against the *actual* performance space — its wings, traps, sightlines, and dimensions — which almost never match the rehearsal room exactly.

**In use:** "That formation was blocked in the studio at 40 feet wide; the stage is 32. We need a spacing rehearsal before tech, not just a walkthrough."

**Common misuse:** assuming a blocking rehearsal in the studio substitutes for a spacing rehearsal on the actual stage — the two catch different problems.

## Dance captain vs. assistant choreographer

The **dance captain** maintains choreographic integrity after a show opens — running understudy and replacement rehearsals, giving cleaning notes, and serving as the choreographer's proxy once the choreographer moves to another project. The **assistant choreographer** works alongside the choreographer during the creation process itself, often originating or refining material before it's taught to the full cast.

**In use:** "Route that note to the dance captain — I'm off this show as of tonight, and she's maintaining it from here."

**Common misuse:** treating the two roles as interchangeable job titles rather than distinct phases (creation vs. maintenance) of a piece's life.

## Stager / répétiteur vs. choreographer of record

The **choreographer of record** is the credited author of a piece. A **stager** or **répétiteur** is a separate, often trust- or estate-designated professional licensed to teach and restage that choreographer's existing work for a new production — with final authority over step content on that specific staging, per the license.

**In use:** "This is a Balanchine Trust production — the répétiteur's word on the steps is final, regardless of what the artistic director prefers."

**Common misuse:** assuming a hired choreographer or resident artistic staff can freely adapt a licensed repertory piece; staging authority on trust-licensed work belongs to the assigned stager, not the producing company.

## Ten-out-of-twelve

An Equity/AGMA convention for tech rehearsals: 10 hours of actual work inside a 12-hour call, with the remaining 2 hours allocated to meal and rest breaks per the governing agreement.

**In use:** "We're in a ten-out-of-twelve tomorrow — plan the schedule around two real breaks, not an optimistic straight run."

**Common misuse:** scheduling a tech day as if all 12 hours are workable rehearsal time; the break structure is contractual, not optional, and eats into the usable hours a choreographer can plan against.

## Freeze / lock date

The declared point in a rehearsal process after which choreography stops changing except for a structural note that overrides the freeze explicitly. Distinct from simply "running out of rehearsal time" — a freeze is a decision, not a default.

**In use:** "We froze the piece Tuesday — if something needs to change now, it goes through me and gets flagged to stage management, it doesn't just happen in the room."

**Common misuse:** treating tech week as an informal extension of the creative process, continuing to adjust blocking after the crew has already cued to it.

## Labanotation / Benesh notation

Symbolic systems (respectively developed from Rudolf Laban's and Rudolf Benesh's work) for recording choreography on paper in enough detail to reconstruct it without video or the original choreographer present — the archival backbone of repertory licensing.

**In use:** "The Trust requires a Labanotation score on file before this piece can be licensed to another company."

**Common misuse:** assuming reference video alone is an adequate substitute for a notated score when a piece needs to be reconstructed by someone who never saw the original production.

## Unison vs. canon

**Unison** is multiple dancers performing identical movement on the identical count. **Canon** is the same movement performed by different dancers offset by a set number of counts, creating a rippling or staggered visual effect.

**In use:** "Take that phrase out of unison and put it in a two-count canon — it'll read as a wave instead of a block."

**Common misuse:** using "canon" loosely to mean any staggered entrance, when it specifically implies the same movement material repeated at a fixed count offset, not different movement happening at different times.

## Upstage/downstage focus

The audience's attention defaults to whichever performer is positioned closest to them (downstage) or most brightly lit, regardless of which performer the choreography intends as the visual focus.

**In use:** "Move the soloist downstage of the ensemble for that phrase, or focus will land on the corps instead of her, no matter how the movement is designed."

**Common misuse:** assuming intended focus (via movement quality or music emphasis) alone will direct audience attention, without checking who is actually standing closest to the house.

## Work-for-hire

A copyright-law term determining whether an original creator (the choreographer) or the commissioning entity (a company, studio, or producer) owns a piece of choreography, depending on the terms of the commissioning agreement.

**In use:** "Check the commissioning contract before agreeing to license this out — if it wasn't drafted as work-for-hire, the company may not actually own the rights to restage it without you."

**Common misuse:** assuming a company automatically owns choreography it paid a choreographer to create; ownership depends on the specific contractual language, not simply on who paid for the commission.
