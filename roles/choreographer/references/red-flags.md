# Red flags

Smell tests a choreographer catches within the first week of a rehearsal process. Format for each: what it usually means, the first question to ask, and the data to pull before deciding.

## 1. The cast is still marking every run less than a week from tech

**Usually means:** stamina, partnering timing, and spacing collisions haven't been stress-tested yet — marking hides exactly those three problems, and dress rehearsal is too late to discover them.

**First question:** "When was the last full-out run, and what did it show?"

**Data to pull:** the rehearsal log's marked-vs-full-out count for the last two weeks; any injury or near-miss reports from partnering sections; remaining hours before tech.

## 2. More than one new formation change was taught in a single rehearsal with no spacing rehearsal scheduled

**Usually means:** spacing debt is accumulating silently; it will surface as a tech-week rewrite, which is the most expensive place to discover it.

**First question:** "Which of these formations has actually been run against the real stage dimensions, not the rehearsal room's?"

**Data to pull:** rehearsal room dimensions vs. actual stage dimensions (wing space, trap locations, sightline cutoffs); count of formation changes taught since the last spacing rehearsal.

## 3. No freeze/lock date has been declared before tech

**Usually means:** the choreographer is still treating tech week as rehearsal time, which resets blocking the crew has already cued — a classic driver of tech-week overtime and cue-sheet chaos.

**First question:** "What date did we agree the choreography locks, and has anything changed since?"

**Data to pull:** the production calendar's stated freeze date (if none exists, that's the root cause); a diff of blocking notes before vs. after that date.

## 4. Rehearsal-hour budget was built on a 1:1 minutes-to-hours assumption

**Usually means:** whoever scheduled rehearsal time didn't account for company skill level or phrase density; the piece will arrive at tech unfinished.

**First question:** "How many eight-counts are in this piece, and how many are actually taught and retained per rehearsal hour so far?"

**Data to pull:** total eight-counts in the piece; eight-counts-per-hour actually achieved in the first two rehearsals (the real rate, not the planned one); hours remaining vs. hours the real rate implies are needed.

## 5. A repertory piece is being staged without sign-off from the licensing trust or estate's stager

**Usually means:** the company is treating a licensed work as open to directorial or choreographic revision it isn't legally entitled to make, risking the license itself.

**First question:** "Who is the assigned répétiteur or stager of record for this piece, and have they approved this staging?"

**Data to pull:** the licensing agreement's staging-authority clause; correspondence with the trust/estate; any deviations from the stager's set choreography currently in rehearsal.

## 6. Injury or "tweaked something" reports cluster around one repeated phrase or lift

**Usually means:** the phrase itself has a mechanical problem (unsafe angle, insufficient prep count, mismatched partner strength) rather than individual dancers being careless — repeating it as-is will keep producing injuries.

**First question:** "Is this happening to more than one dancer or pairing in this same phrase?"

**Data to pull:** injury/incident log filtered by phrase or count range; which specific counts precede the reported strain; whether the same pairing or a rotating cast is affected.

## 7. Notes keep saying "commit more" or "bigger" on a phrase, repeatedly, with no improvement

**Usually means:** the actual problem is legibility, not effort — the shape of the phrase isn't clear enough to read at distance, and no amount of commitment fixes an unclear shape.

**First question:** "Have we watched this from the back of the house, not just from the rehearsal room mirror?"

**Data to pull:** video of the phrase shot from an audience-distance angle; whether the note has repeated across more than two rehearsals unchanged.

## 8. A new cast member or understudy is given only reference video, with no dance-captain rehearsal

**Usually means:** the company is treating video as a substitute for hands-on teaching; video preserves the shape but not the counts, spacing cues, or partnering timing a live rehearsal catches.

**First question:** "Has the dance captain actually run this person through the piece, or just handed them the video?"

**Data to pull:** the understudy/replacement rehearsal log; time booked for captain-led rehearsal vs. self-study time; any spacing errors caught in the first live run.

## 9. The music track was locked before the count structure was reconciled against it

**Usually means:** the choreography was built to a beat count that doesn't actually match the edited track's phrase lengths, which will only surface once dancers are running to the final audio.

**First question:** "Has anyone counted the locked track against the count sheet, out loud, start to finish?"

**Data to pull:** the count sheet; the final edited track's waveform or timecodes at each formation-change point; any discrepancy between planned and actual beat counts per section.
