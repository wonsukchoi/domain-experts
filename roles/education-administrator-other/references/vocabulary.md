# Non-traditional education administration working vocabulary

Terms an administrator uses precisely. Format: definition → how a practitioner says it → common misuse.

**Completion rate vs. enrollment rate** — Completion rate is the percentage of enrolled participants who actually finish the program; enrollment rate/numbers measure how many start. The gap between the two is where real program-design problems tend to hide.
In use: "Enrollment is strong at 200/year, but completion is only 58% — that gap is the actual problem, not the enrollment number."
Misuse: reporting or optimizing for enrollment numbers alone as a proxy for program success, when completion is the metric that reflects actual delivered value.

**Exit-cause data (attrition diagnosis)** — Structured data (surveys, exit interviews) categorizing why specific participants didn't complete a program, used to target interventions at the actual documented cause rather than an assumed one.
In use: "Exit data shows 42% of non-completers cited scheduling conflicts — that's the actual top cause, not academic difficulty."
Misuse: designing a retention intervention based on an assumed cause (academic preparedness) without checking it against actual exit-cause data, which often reveals a different primary driver.

**Wraparound support services** — Non-academic supports (advising, financial aid navigation, childcare, transportation) integrated into program design specifically because they materially affect completion for this population, not treated as peripheral to the "real" academic content.
In use: "The childcare partnership is a wraparound support service — it's core to program design, not an add-on."
Misuse: treating support services as a nice-to-have layered on top of academic content rather than a core lever for completion, especially for a population balancing the program against work and family obligations.

**Employer/industry alignment** — The degree to which a program's curriculum and credential map to current, actual employer need in the relevant field, validated through direct and ongoing employer engagement rather than assumed from the curriculum's internal coherence.
In use: "We revalidate curriculum alignment with our employer advisory board every year — the credential's value depends on employers still recognizing it."
Misuse: assuming a curriculum remains valuable because it was well-designed historically, without checking whether the field's actual needs have shifted since.

**Credential recognition** — Whether employers in the relevant field actually value and recognize a specific credential as meaningful for hiring or advancement decisions, distinct from whether the credential is academically rigorous or well-designed.
In use: "The credential has strong content, but recognition among the three largest employers in this market has weakened over the last two years — that's the actual issue to fix."
Misuse: assuming academic rigor or curriculum quality automatically translates into employer recognition; the two are related but distinct, and recognition needs to be checked directly with employers.

**Modular/part-time pacing** — A program structure allowing participants to complete coursework in smaller units over a flexible timeline, as opposed to a fixed full-time cohort pace, designed to accommodate participants balancing the program against work/family obligations.
In use: "We added modular pacing so a participant can complete one module per semester instead of the full sequence in one term — that's what let several working parents actually finish."
Misuse: assuming a flexible-sounding program is actually accessible without checking whether the pacing structure genuinely accommodates the target population's real constraints, versus flexibility in name only.

**Placement rate** — The percentage of program completers who obtain employment in the relevant field within a defined period after completion, a core outcome metric for programs whose value proposition is job-outcome-focused.
In use: "Placement rate is 71% within 6 months of completion — that's the number driving our outcome claim to prospective participants."
Misuse: citing a placement rate without specifying the timeframe, the definition of "relevant field," or how current the data is — vague placement claims can overstate a program's actual current value.

**Workforce development program evaluation** — A specific evaluation framework (common in federally-funded workforce programs) emphasizing completion, credential attainment, and employment/wage outcomes as the core success measures, distinct from traditional academic evaluation metrics (grades, satisfaction).
In use: "Under our workforce development program evaluation framework, the metrics that matter are completion, credential attainment, and wage outcome at 6 and 12 months — not course satisfaction scores."
Misuse: evaluating a workforce-oriented program primarily on satisfaction or academic metrics that don't capture whether it actually produced the job/credential outcome participants enrolled for.

**Cost-benefit calculation (participant decision framing)** — The direct, near-term weighing of program cost (time, money) against a specific expected outcome (credential, job, pay increase) that characterizes how participants in non-traditional settings typically decide whether to enroll and persist, as distinct from a broader credentialing or life-stage motivation.
In use: "For this population, the decision to keep attending is a live cost-benefit calculation every week — if the near-term value case weakens, they leave, which is why validated outcome data matters so much here."
Misuse: assuming participant motivation and persistence work the same way as in a traditional full-time degree program, missing that the ongoing, concrete cost-benefit calculation is what actually drives persistence decisions in this setting.

**Financial strain (mid-program attrition cause)** — A documented attrition cause where a participant leaves a program due to a financial hardship arising during enrollment (not at the point of initial enrollment decision), distinct from upfront cost being a barrier to starting.
In use: "Mid-program financial strain accounts for 26% of our non-completions — that's a different problem than the upfront tuition cost, and it's what the emergency grant fund specifically targets."
Misuse: addressing only upfront affordability (financial aid at enrollment) without a mechanism for mid-program financial shocks, missing a documented and distinct cause of attrition.
