# Vocabulary

### Functional unit
The quantified unit of function a product or system delivers, which anchors every comparison in an LCA (e.g., "packaging sufficient to deliver 1,000 kg of tomatoes intact," not "1 kg of tray material").
**In use:** "Before we run any numbers, what's the functional unit — because comparing these two trays per kilogram instead of per delivered-tomato is going to produce a meaningless answer."
**Common misuse:** Comparing options on a per-mass or per-item basis when their service life, loss rate, or delivered function differs, producing an apples-to-oranges result that looks rigorous.

### System boundary
The set of processes and flows included in an analysis — cradle-to-gate (raw material to factory gate), cradle-to-grave (through use and disposal), or gate-to-gate (one process step only).
**In use:** "This is cradle-to-gate only — if marketing wants to say it beats the competitor's product, we need cradle-to-grave and a critical review first."
**Common misuse:** Treating boundary choice as a neutral technical detail rather than the single biggest lever on the final number.

### Allocation
The method for splitting shared environmental burden across multiple co-products of one process — by mass, by economic value, or by system expansion (crediting the avoided alternative product).
**In use:** "We're using system expansion for the glycerin co-product, not economic allocation — the glycerin price is too volatile to base a footprint on."
**Common misuse:** Defaulting to economic allocation because it's the easiest to compute, without acknowledging it makes the result move with commodity prices rather than physical reality.

### Industrial symbiosis
A network — Chertow's threshold is at least three distinct entities exchanging at least two distinct resources — in which byproducts, energy, water, or infrastructure from one facility become inputs to another.
**In use:** "That's a two-party byproduct sale, not symbiosis yet — we can call it symbiosis once a third participant joins the exchange."
**Common misuse:** Applying "industrial symbiosis" to any single bilateral byproduct sale, which overstates the resilience and network effects the term implies.

### Material flow analysis (MFA)
A systematic accounting of the mass of materials entering, accumulating in, and leaving a defined system (a plant, region, or economy) over a defined period, used to find losses and inefficiencies before running impact assessment.
**In use:** "The MFA shows a 2.9% gap between inputs and outputs — inside tolerance, but let's confirm it's evaporative loss before we close the balance out."
**Common misuse:** Treating any unreconciled gap as a genuine physical loss ("leakage") without first checking metering and estimation quality.

### Substance flow analysis (SFA)
Like MFA but tracking a single substance (e.g., nitrogen, a specific metal) through a system rather than aggregate material mass — used when the substance of concern is a small fraction of total mass flow.
**In use:** "Aggregate MFA won't catch the cadmium — we need an SFA specifically tracking that substance through the recycling stream."
**Common misuse:** Running an aggregate MFA and assuming it captures trace-substance risks, which it structurally cannot.

### Life-cycle inventory (LCI) vs. life-cycle impact assessment (LCIA)
LCI is the raw compiled inventory of all inputs and outputs (energy, water, emissions) for the system; LCIA translates that inventory into impact category scores (GWP, eutrophication, etc.) using a characterization method.
**In use:** "The LCI is solid — it's the LCIA method choice, TRACI versus ReCiPe, that's going to change which category dominates the story."
**Common misuse:** Using "LCA" as if it names a single fixed number, when the LCIA step alone involves a method choice that changes results.

### Characterization factor
The numeric conversion (from a published LCIA method) that translates a raw inventory flow, such as kg of a specific gas, into an impact-category-equivalent unit, such as kg CO2e for global warming potential.
**In use:** "Check which GWP time horizon the characterization factors assume — 100-year versus 20-year methane factors aren't the same number."
**Common misuse:** Assuming characterization factors are fixed physical constants rather than method- and version-specific published values that change between database updates.

### Functional recycling vs. downcycling
Functional recycling returns a material to a use of equivalent quality that displaces virgin material demand for that same use; downcycling routes it into a lower-value application that doesn't displace virgin extraction of the original material.
**In use:** "That's downcycling, not functional recycling — the PET isn't becoming bottles again, it's becoming carpet backing, and virgin PET production for bottles is unaffected."
**Common misuse:** Reporting any landfill-diversion rate as "recycling" without checking whether the diverted material is functionally recycled or downcycled.

### Eco-industrial park
A planned industrial estate designed from the outset for member facilities to exchange energy, water, and materials, as distinct from an industrial symbiosis network that emerges organically among existing, unplanned facilities (like Kalundborg).
**In use:** "This greenfield site is being designed as an eco-industrial park from day one, so we can size the shared utilities for symbiosis rather than retrofitting exchanges later."
**Common misuse:** Using "eco-industrial park" and "industrial symbiosis" interchangeably, when the former is a deliberate design outcome and the latter can be either planned or emergent.

### Dematerialization
A reduction in the material or energy input required to deliver the same unit of function, distinct from simply using a lower-impact material for the same mass.
**In use:** "Lightweighting the can from 13.5 g to 12.2 g is dematerialization; switching to a different alloy at the same mass is a material-substitution move, not dematerialization."
**Common misuse:** Labeling any environmental improvement "dematerialization," including material substitutions that don't actually reduce material throughput.

### Rebound effect
The tendency for efficiency gains to be partly or fully offset by increased consumption enabled by the resulting cost or resource savings.
**In use:** "The 12% material-efficiency gain on the packaging could see some rebound if the cost savings gets passed through as a lower price and volume grows faster than it otherwise would."
**Common misuse:** Ignoring rebound entirely when reporting efficiency-gain projections, treating the engineering savings as if it maps one-to-one onto system-level impact reduction.

### Cutoff criteria
The threshold, stated in the goal-and-scope phase, below which minor flows may be excluded from an LCA without materially changing the result (e.g., flows under 1% of any tracked impact category).
**In use:** "Anything under our 1% cutoff gets listed as excluded, not silently dropped — that's what a reviewer checks first."
**Common misuse:** Applying cutoff criteria inconsistently or retroactively to exclude an inconvenient flow after seeing its effect on the result.

### Data pedigree
A structured quality score (e.g., ecoinvent's pedigree matrix) rating each input data point on dimensions like reliability, completeness, and temporal/geographic representativeness, used to weight confidence in the final result.
**In use:** "This headline number is carried by a pedigree-2 input — supplier-reported, not third-party verified — so the range we report needs to reflect that."
**Common misuse:** Presenting a result with false precision (a single decimal-point figure) when the underlying data pedigree is low and a range would be honest.
