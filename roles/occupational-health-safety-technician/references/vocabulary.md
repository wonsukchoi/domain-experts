# Occupational Health and Safety Technician — Vocabulary

### Breathing zone
The imaginary sphere around a worker's head, roughly 6-9 inches in radius, where air-sampling media must be positioned to represent what the worker actually inhales.
**In use:** "The cyclone was clipped to the collar, not the belt — belt placement isn't breathing zone and the sample would've been thrown out on review."
**Common misuse:** treating "on the worker's body somewhere" as equivalent to breathing zone; a sample collected at waist height for a task with a low-hanging contaminant source can read differently from a true breathing-zone sample.

### Calibration drift
The difference between an instrument's flow rate or response measured before versus after a sampling run, expressed as a percentage of the pre-cal value.
**In use:** "Drift came in at 7.1% — that's the average-and-flag band, not a clean accept and not a void."
**Common misuse:** treating any nonzero drift as automatically disqualifying, or conversely ignoring drift entirely once a post-cal number exists to compare against.

### Field blank
A piece of sampling media taken to the field, handled identically to the real samples, but never actually exposed to air — used to establish the contamination baseline for blank correction.
**In use:** "Both field blanks came back under 3.5 µg, consistent with clean handling — the sample correction is solid."
**Common misuse:** confusing a field blank with a lab/media blank (never taken to the field); only a true field blank captures contamination from field handling and transport, not just manufacturing.

### Minimum detectable concentration (MDC)
The lowest concentration a specific sample could have reliably shown a positive result for, calculated from the analytical limit of detection divided by the actual sample volume collected.
**In use:** "MDC on this one is 6.4 µg/m³ because the volume was smaller than planned — anything reported below that number is really 'less than 6.4,' not a precise low reading."
**Common misuse:** treating MDC as a fixed number for a method; it moves with sample volume, so a short or low-flow sample has a worse (higher) MDC than a full-shift one even using the identical analytical method.

### Chain of custody (COC)
The documented, unbroken record of who possessed a sample and when, from field collection through lab analysis, used to establish the data's legal defensibility.
**In use:** "The COC has a gap between courier pickup and lab intake — until that's explained, this result is vulnerable in a contest."
**Common misuse:** treating the COC form as a shipping label rather than as evidence; an unsigned handoff is a break in custody even if nothing was actually tampered with.

### Quantitative fit test (QNFT) / fit factor
A numeric measure (via PortaCount or similar) of how well a respirator seals to an individual's face, calculated from ambient particle concentration divided by in-mask concentration during a set of exercises.
**In use:** "Fit factor came back 115 on the half-face — technically a pass at the 100 minimum, but that's marginal, not clean."
**Common misuse:** reporting only the overall harmonic-mean fit factor and discarding the individual exercise scores, which is where a specific seal weakness (jaw movement, bridge of nose) actually shows up.

### Qualitative fit test (QLFT)
A pass/fail respirator fit check based on whether the wearer detects a test agent (saccharin, Bitrex, irritant smoke) during a set of exercises — no numeric fit factor produced.
**In use:** "QLFT only, since this facility doesn't have QNFT equipment — it's pass/fail, so there's no margin to flag the way there would be with a fit factor."
**Common misuse:** assuming QLFT and QNFT are interchangeable for any respirator class; QLFT isn't permitted for certain higher-protection-factor negative-pressure respirators under OSHA's fit-testing rule.

### Similar exposure group (SEG)
A group of workers with similar job tasks, materials, and work patterns, assumed to have similar exposure profiles, used by the specialist to decide who and how many people to sample.
**In use:** "All four saw operators on this line are in the same SEG, so sampling two of them stands in for the group, per the plan."
**Common misuse:** a technician redefining or expanding the SEG on-site because "it seemed similar enough" — SEG boundaries are the specialist's call, made before sampling, not a field judgment.

### Direct-reading instrument (DRI)
A field instrument (PID, combustible gas meter, real-time particulate monitor) that gives an immediate numeric reading without lab analysis, generally used for screening, not compliance determination.
**In use:** "PID spiked to 40 ppm during the tank entry — that's a screening trigger to stop and reassess, not a documented exposure result."
**Common misuse:** citing a DRI reading as the compliance number in a report when the standard requires an integrated method; DRIs are typically calibrated to a surrogate reference gas or particle size that doesn't match the actual contaminant one-for-one.

### Action level
An exposure threshold, defined within a specific OSHA standard (commonly around half the PEL), that triggers ongoing monitoring or program requirements even though the PEL itself hasn't been exceeded.
**In use:** "We're at the lead action level, so periodic monitoring starts now — not a citation-worthy result, but not a one-and-done sample either."
**Common misuse:** assuming every OSHA substance-specific standard defines an action level; several (including the respirable silica standard) don't use the concept at all, so citing "the action level" without checking the specific standard is a fabricated threshold.

### Time-weighted average (TWA)
An exposure result averaged over a defined period (commonly 8 hours), accounting for both the concentration and the duration of exposure, as opposed to a peak or grab reading.
**In use:** "78.8 µg/m³ is the 8-hour TWA — the peak during the actual cutting task was almost certainly higher, since the TWA blends in setup and cleanup time too."
**Common misuse:** reporting a partial-shift sample's raw concentration as if it were already the full-shift TWA without extrapolating or disclosing the coverage gap.

### AIHA-LAP accredited laboratory
A laboratory accredited under the AIHA Laboratory Accreditation Programs for the specific analytical method being used — accreditation is method-specific, not lab-wide.
**In use:** "Confirmed the lab holds AIHA-LAP accreditation for NIOSH 7500 specifically, not just for metals — the silica result is defensible."
**Common misuse:** assuming a lab's general accreditation covers every method it offers; a lab can be accredited for some analyses and not others.

### Sampling media
The physical collection device matched to the target analyte and method — cyclone plus filter for respirable dust, sorbent tube for organic vapors, impinger for certain gases.
**In use:** "Wrong media on hand for this method — a mixed-cellulose-ester filter won't hold up for this solvent, need the charcoal tube instead."
**Common misuse:** treating media as interchangeable across methods because they look similar; the wrong filter material or sorbent can pass air fine and still produce an invalid or biased result.
