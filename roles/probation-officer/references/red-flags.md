# Red flags — signals a probation officer notices instantly

Smell tests with thresholds. Any one alone can be a manageable case-management moment; two or more together warrants an immediate risk reassessment or supervision-plan review.

### A revocation petition is being considered for a first, isolated technical violation with no safety risk indicator
- **Usually means:** the response may be disproportionate to the actual risk, disrupting stabilizing factors (employment, housing) that themselves reduce recidivism, without evidence the disruption is warranted.
- **First question:** "Is this the first violation in an otherwise compliant supervision period, or part of a repeated pattern despite prior intervention?"
- **Data to pull:** violation classification decision tree (see `references/playbook.md` §4), supervision compliance history to date.

### Supervision intensity was set based on offense severity rather than the validated risk assessment score
- **Usually means:** a low-risk individual may be over-supervised (increasing disruption and recidivism risk) or a high-risk individual under-supervised, based on an intuitive read of the offense rather than the actuarial assessment.
- **First question:** "What does the validated risk-needs assessment actually show, and does the current supervision intensity match that score?"
- **Data to pull:** risk assessment results and sub-domain scores, current supervision level and check-in frequency.

### A case plan lists generic goals with no connection to a specific criminogenic need from the assessment
- **Usually means:** limited case management resources may be spent on non-criminogenic wellness goals instead of the specific dynamic risk factors that actually predict reoffending.
- **First question:** "Which specific criminogenic need does this case plan intervention address, per the assessment's findings?"
- **Data to pull:** criminogenic-needs case plan template (see `references/playbook.md` §2), risk assessment's identified top needs.

### An actuarial risk tool's recommendation is overridden with no documented reasoning
- **Usually means:** the override may reflect an unexamined bias or intuition rather than a specific, defensible case fact, and it's not auditable later without documentation.
- **First question:** "What specific case fact justifies deviating from the tool's recommendation, and is that reasoning written down?"
- **Data to pull:** risk assessment output, case notes for the override decision and its stated rationale.

### A presentence investigation report includes an unsourced factual claim or omits mitigating information
- **Usually means:** the judge may rely on an inaccurate or one-sided account at sentencing, an error that's difficult to correct once the sentence is imposed.
- **First question:** "What's the specific source for this claim, and does the report include the mitigating factors alongside the aggravating ones?"
- **Data to pull:** PSI structure checklist (see `references/playbook.md` §3), source documents for each factual claim in the draft.

### A positive drug test or missed check-in is treated as generic non-compliance rather than connected to the individual's flagged criminogenic needs
- **Usually means:** an opportunity to reinforce or adjust the actual risk-reduction intervention (e.g., substance-use treatment) may be missed if the violation is treated as a standalone compliance issue rather than tied back to the case plan.
- **First question:** "Does this violation connect to a specific domain already flagged in the risk-needs assessment, and does the response address that domain specifically?"
- **Data to pull:** original risk-needs assessment domains, violation details, current case plan interventions.

### A risk-needs reassessment hasn't been conducted since intake, despite significant time elapsed or a changed circumstance
- **Usually means:** the supervision plan may no longer match the individual's current risk level or needs, which can shift meaningfully over a long supervision term.
- **First question:** "When was the last risk-needs reassessment, and has anything material changed (employment, relationships, substance use pattern) since then?"
- **Data to pull:** assessment history and dates, case notes on any significant life changes during supervision.

### Multiple violations are being aggregated together as if equally severe, without classifying each individually
- **Usually means:** a lower-severity technical violation may be getting swept into the same response category as a higher-severity or safety-relevant one, producing an escalation the individual violations don't independently justify.
- **First question:** "Classify each violation separately — does any one of them independently warrant the response being proposed, or is the aggregate framing driving the decision?"
- **Data to pull:** each violation's individual classification (technical vs. new offense, safety indicator present or not) and pattern history.
