# Probation and community supervision working vocabulary

Terms a probation officer uses precisely and a generalist blurs together. Format: definition → how a practitioner says it → common misuse.

**Risk-Need-Responsivity (RNR) model** — An evidence-based framework for community supervision holding that supervision intensity should match assessed risk level (risk), interventions should target the specific dynamic factors that predict reoffending (need), and the intervention approach should match the individual's learning style and circumstances (responsivity).
In use: "The RNR model says this low-risk individual should get less supervision, not more — over-supervising here can actually increase recidivism."
Misuse: applying uniform supervision intensity or generic case planning regardless of assessed risk level, treating RNR as a general philosophy rather than a specific framework with operational implications for supervision intensity and case plan content.

**Criminogenic needs vs. non-criminogenic needs** — Criminogenic needs are the dynamic risk factors empirically linked to reoffending (antisocial attitudes, antisocial peer associations, substance abuse, employment/education instability, family/marital problems); non-criminogenic needs (self-esteem, general life satisfaction) may matter for wellbeing but don't carry the same evidence base for reducing recidivism.
In use: "The case plan needs to prioritize the antisocial-associates and substance-abuse domains — those are the criminogenic needs driving this person's risk score."
Misuse: treating any need the individual expresses (housing preference, general counseling desire) as equally prioritized in the case plan, rather than distinguishing which needs are actually criminogenic and prioritizing limited resources accordingly.

**Technical violation vs. new offense** — A technical violation is a breach of a supervision condition (missed check-in, curfew violation, positive drug test) that isn't itself a new crime; a new offense is a violation of the law independent of the supervision conditions.
In use: "This is a technical violation, not a new offense — the response should be a graduated sanction, not treated the same as a new arrest."
Misuse: treating any violation as equally serious regardless of whether it's a technical infraction or a new law violation, which leads to disproportionate responses in either direction.

**Graduated sanctions** — A structured, escalating set of responses to violations, calibrated to severity and pattern rather than defaulting to full revocation for any infraction — ranging from increased contact to brief structured sanctions to treatment intensification.
In use: "We're applying a graduated sanction here — increased check-ins and additional treatment — not filing for revocation on a first technical violation."
Misuse: treating "graduated sanctions" as simply a euphemism for leniency — it's a structured, documented, severity-matched response system, not an absence of accountability.

**Validated risk-needs assessment instrument** — A structured actuarial tool (e.g., LSI-R, ORAS) that scores an individual's recidivism risk and identifies specific criminogenic need domains, empirically shown to outperform unstructured professional judgment alone for predicting reoffending.
In use: "The LSI-R composite score is moderate, but the sub-domain breakdown is what actually tells us where to focus the case plan."
Misuse: treating the overall composite score as the only useful output — the sub-domain breakdown identifying specific criminogenic needs is often more actionable for case planning than the single composite number.

**Documented override** — A case-specific deviation from an actuarial risk tool's recommendation, made with explicit written reasoning tied to specific facts not captured by the tool, distinct from an unexplained or intuition-driven departure.
In use: "We're overriding the tool's recommendation for standard supervision given the documented new information about a specific safety concern — here's the reasoning in the file."
Misuse: silently deviating from the tool's output based on general impression, without documenting the specific case fact that justifies the departure — an undocumented override isn't auditable and undermines the evidence-based framework's purpose.

**Presentence investigation report (PSI)** — A report prepared for the sentencing court summarizing the offense, the defendant's history, risk-needs assessment findings, and relevant aggravating/mitigating factors, meant to be a balanced factual account rather than an advocacy document.
In use: "The PSI needs to source every claim and include the mitigating factors — the judge is going to rely heavily on this document at sentencing."
Misuse: drafting a PSI that emphasizes only aggravating factors or a particular recommended outcome, rather than a balanced, sourced account the judge can independently weigh.

**Absconding** — Willfully avoiding supervision by failing to report, relocating without authorization, or otherwise evading contact with the supervising officer, distinct from a missed check-in with an identifiable, disclosed reason.
In use: "This isn't absconding — the missed check-in was disclosed and explained, and the individual remained reachable and compliant otherwise."
Misuse: labeling any missed contact as "absconding" regardless of whether there's evidence of willful evasion, which can trigger a disproportionately severe response to what may be an isolated, explainable technical violation.

**Reentry stability factors** — Employment, housing, and family/social stability that both reflect and support successful community reintegration, treated as core risk-reduction levers rather than peripheral social services.
In use: "Disrupting his employment with an unnecessary revocation would remove one of the stabilizing factors actually reducing his risk — that's a cost to weigh, not a side issue."
Misuse: treating employment/housing support as a "nice to have" separate from the actual risk-reduction case plan, rather than recognizing that disruption to these factors is itself a criminogenic risk driver.

**Responsivity (RNR model)** — The RNR model's third component, addressing how an intervention's delivery style, format, and approach should match an individual's learning style, motivation level, and circumstances to actually be effective, distinct from simply assigning the "correct" intervention type.
In use: "The substance-use treatment referral is right for the criminogenic need, but the responsivity factor here means group format won't work — this person needs an individual format given documented social anxiety."
Misuse: assuming that identifying the correct criminogenic need and assigning a generically appropriate intervention is sufficient — the responsivity factor addresses whether the specific delivery format will actually work for this individual, which is a distinct and sometimes overlooked consideration.
