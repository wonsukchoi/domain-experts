# Clinical reasoning working vocabulary

> Educational content modeling diagnostic reasoning terms of art — not medical advice. Any real health concern needs a licensed clinician or emergency care.

Format: definition → how the term is used in reasoning → common misuse.

**Pretest probability** — The estimated likelihood a patient has a given condition before a specific test is performed, based on history, risk factors, and prevalence — the necessary context for interpreting what a subsequent test result actually means (Bayesian reasoning).
In use: "At a Wells' score of 6.0, pretest probability for PE is high enough that a negative D-dimer wouldn't be reassuring — CTPA is the appropriate next test."
Misuse: interpreting a test's sensitivity/specificity as if it applied the same way regardless of pretest probability, missing that the same result means something very different in a high- versus low-pretest-probability patient.

**Differential diagnosis** — An actively maintained, ranked list of possible explanations for a presentation, updated as new evidence arrives, as distinct from a single working conclusion reached early and defended.
In use: "The differential includes anxiety, PE, and musculoskeletal pain, ranked by both probability and consequence-if-missed."
Misuse: treating the differential as a single answer settled at first impression rather than a ranked, revisable list that should update as new findings arrive.

**Can't-miss diagnosis** — A possible explanation for a presentation that carries catastrophic consequence if missed, warranting active rule-out even when it's not the most probable explanation, because the cost asymmetry (missed catastrophic diagnosis vs. a negative test) justifies the workup.
In use: "PE is the can't-miss diagnosis here — even though anxiety is statistically more likely, the risk factors present mean PE has to be actively ruled out, not just deprioritized."
Misuse: ranking possible diagnoses purely by probability without separately weighting consequence, which can lead to deprioritizing (rather than actively ruling out) a rare but catastrophic possibility.

**Premature closure** — Stopping the diagnostic process once a plausible explanation is found, without checking it against all available evidence, particularly findings that don't fit the leading hypothesis — one of the most common documented diagnostic errors.
In use: "Premature closure is the risk here — the first plausible story explains most, but not all, of the findings."
Misuse: treating "a plausible explanation was found" as equivalent to "the correct explanation was found," without the additional step of checking it against every available piece of evidence.

**Anchoring** — Continuing to favor an initial diagnostic impression despite new, disconfirming evidence, especially after the diagnosis has already been stated to a patient or colleague.
In use: "The lack of expected treatment response is disconfirming evidence — anchoring on the original diagnosis here means treating it as an annoyance instead of a reason to reconsider."
Misuse: treating new disconfirming evidence as noise to be explained away in service of the original diagnosis, rather than as a genuine trigger to revisit the differential.

**Illness script** — A pattern-recognition template (typical presentation, risk factors, course) built from prior clinical experience, which speeds recognition of common presentations but requires active checking against atypical evidence rather than automatic trust.
In use: "The illness script for a typical MI doesn't quite fit here — the atypical features are worth checking against the script rather than force-fitting."
Misuse: trusting an illness script automatically without checking it against the specific case's atypical features, which is exactly the pattern-matching shortcut that produces premature closure.

**Wells' Criteria** — A validated clinical decision rule assigning points to specific risk factors and findings to estimate pretest probability of pulmonary embolism, used to standardize the next diagnostic step (D-dimer vs. imaging) rather than relying purely on gestalt.
In use: "Wells' score of 6.0 places this in the 'PE likely' category (>4), which changes the recommended next test from D-dimer to CTPA."
Misuse: estimating pretest probability purely by clinical impression without applying a validated scoring tool where one exists, introducing more variability and less reproducibility into the estimate.

**Diagnostic time-out** — A deliberate, structured pause to re-examine a case with fresh eyes, particularly for high-stakes or non-improving cases, designed to catch anchoring or premature closure that a continued, momentum-driven reasoning process might miss.
In use: "This patient isn't responding as expected — time for a diagnostic time-out to revisit the differential from scratch rather than escalating the same treatment."
Misuse: continuing to intensify the same treatment plan when a patient doesn't respond as expected, rather than treating the lack of response as data prompting a structured reconsideration of the diagnosis itself.

**Morbidity and mortality (M&M) conference** — A blameless, structured case review process (analogous to an engineering postmortem) analyzing cases with adverse outcomes for systemic and cognitive contributing factors, not individual blame.
In use: "The M&M review identified anchoring as a contributing factor in this missed diagnosis — the corrective action targets the diagnostic process, not the individual clinician."
Misuse: running a case review focused on identifying who made the error rather than what systemic or cognitive factor allowed it, which discourages honest reporting and misses the actual, fixable contributing cause.

**Occam's razor (with clinical exception)** — The principle of preferring a single diagnosis that explains all findings over multiple coincidental ones, held loosely in older or medically complex patients where multiple concurrent conditions become statistically more likely, not less.
In use: "Occam's razor would suggest one diagnosis, but in this 78-year-old patient with several comorbidities, checking for two concurrent conditions is the more statistically appropriate approach."
Misuse: applying Occam's razor rigidly regardless of patient complexity, force-fitting all findings into a single diagnosis in a population where multiple concurrent conditions are actually common.
