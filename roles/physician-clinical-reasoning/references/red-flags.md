# Red flags — cognitive failure-mode signals in clinical reasoning

> Educational content modeling diagnostic reasoning quality — not medical advice, and not to be used to evaluate a real patient. Any real health concern needs a licensed clinician or emergency care.

Smell tests for reviewing or modeling a diagnostic reasoning process. Any single one can be innocent; two or three together are a pattern worth a structured second look.

### A differential diagnosis with only one entry, reached quickly
- **Usually means:** premature closure — the reasoning process stopped at the first plausible explanation without checking it against all available evidence.
- **First question:** "What's the second-most-likely explanation, and what would distinguish it from the leading one?"
- **Data to pull:** the documented differential (or its absence), findings that don't fit the single leading diagnosis.

### A "can't-miss" diagnosis with no documented rule-out step, despite matching known risk factors for it
- **Usually means:** a low-probability but high-consequence explanation is being deprioritized based on probability alone, without the asymmetric-cost weighting that should apply to catastrophic-if-missed diagnoses.
- **First question:** "What's the most dangerous explanation this presentation could represent, and has it been actively ruled out, not just left off the top of the list?"
- **Data to pull:** documented risk factors present, any validated decision rule applied (or not applied) for the relevant can't-miss diagnosis.

### A test ordered with no stated hypothesis about what result would change management
- **Usually means:** test-ordering without a hypothesis, which generates false leads (incidental findings) at least as often as it generates useful answers.
- **First question:** "What result from this specific test would change what happens next, and for which differential?"
- **Data to pull:** the stated reason for ordering the test, the differential it's meant to discriminate between.

### A test result interpreted without reference to pretest probability
- **Usually means:** the same result can mean very different things depending on pretest probability (Bayesian reasoning) — interpreting it in isolation risks a wrong conclusion regardless of the result's face value.
- **First question:** "What was the pretest probability before this test, and does the result actually shift it meaningfully?"
- **Data to pull:** a documented or estimated pretest probability (ideally via a validated scoring tool), the test's sensitivity/specificity in this clinical context.

### An initial diagnostic impression maintained despite new, disconfirming evidence or lack of expected treatment response
- **Usually means:** anchoring — sticking with an initial story rather than treating disconfirming evidence as a genuine trigger to reconsider.
- **First question:** "What's the specific new evidence that doesn't fit, and has the differential actually been revisited in light of it?"
- **Data to pull:** timeline of new findings/treatment response versus any documented reconsideration of the diagnosis.

### A dramatic or recently-encountered diagnosis being weighted heavily with no base-rate justification
- **Usually means:** availability bias — a diagnosis being over-weighted because it's memorable rather than because it's actually likely given this presentation's base rates.
- **First question:** "What's the actual base rate for this diagnosis in this population, separate from how memorable or recent a similar case was?"
- **Data to pull:** epidemiological base rate for the diagnosis in the relevant population, the reasoning offered for weighting it highly.

### Multiple findings attributed to a single diagnosis in an older or medically complex patient with no check for concurrent conditions
- **Usually means:** applying Occam's razor too rigidly in a population where multiple concurrent conditions are statistically more, not less, likely.
- **First question:** "Does one diagnosis genuinely explain every finding, or are some findings being force-fit to a single story?"
- **Data to pull:** the full list of findings, whether each is actually explained by the single proposed diagnosis or only loosely consistent with it.

### Diagnostic certainty communicated to a patient or colleague that doesn't match the actual evidentiary basis
- **Usually means:** either overconfidence (projecting certainty not supported by the evidence) or excessive hedging (avoiding a clear statement the evidence does support) — both miscalibrate the listener's ability to make an informed decision.
- **First question:** "Does the confidence being communicated actually match how much is known at this point?"
- **Data to pull:** the actual evidentiary basis for the stated diagnosis, the specific language used to communicate certainty.
