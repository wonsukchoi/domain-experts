# Red flags — what a marketing manager notices instantly

Smell tests with thresholds. Any single one can be innocent; two or three together are a pattern.

### A "do more with the same budget" ask with no explicit reallocation or capacity tradeoff presented back
- **Usually means:** the team may quietly overcommit and deliver a diluted version of everything rather than the leadership team getting an honest picture of what's actually achievable.
- **First question:** "What does reallocating the current budget realistically deliver, and what does the remaining gap to the ask actually require?"
- **Data to pull:** current portfolio allocation and ROI by program, diminishing-returns/saturation data for the highest-ROI channels.

### 100% of budget allocated to already-proven programs with nothing in an experimental bucket
- **Usually means:** the portfolio may be optimized for short-term defensibility at the cost of finding the next lever — an all-proven portfolio stops generating new options.
- **First question:** "What percentage of budget is in unproven/experimental programs, and when was that split last deliberately set rather than inherited from last year?"
- **Data to pull:** budget allocation by proven vs. experimental category, date of last deliberate portfolio review.

### A quarterly plan committed to leadership with no check against actual team capacity, including recurring/maintenance work
- **Usually means:** the plan is likely to slip or produce burnout, since it was built against an idealized fully-available team rather than real bandwidth.
- **First question:** "Does this plan account for the team's recurring work, or only the new initiatives on the roadmap?"
- **Data to pull:** team capacity/utilization data, recurring work load versus new initiative commitments for the period.

### A recurring report with no open decision it's meant to inform
- **Usually means:** the report may be pure activity documentation rather than a management tool, consuming effort without changing any decision.
- **First question:** "What decision is currently open that this report is meant to inform, and if none, why does it still exist in this form?"
- **Data to pull:** the report's current content and cadence, any decision it has actually influenced recently.

### A major initiative planned without input from sales or product on what they actually need next
- **Usually means:** the initiative risks executing cleanly on its own terms while not connecting to what those functions actually need, producing output that doesn't convert to business value.
- **First question:** "What did sales/product specifically ask for or confirm before this plan was finalized?"
- **Data to pull:** the initiative's stated dependencies, documented input (or lack of it) from sales/product stakeholders.

### An underperforming program being cut without checking whether it was under-resourced or mistimed rather than fundamentally not working
- **Usually means:** a program that could have worked with proper investment may be discarded, losing the lesson a properly-resourced retry would have taught.
- **First question:** "Was this program's underperformance a resourcing/timing issue or a fundamental fit issue, and how do we know which?"
- **Data to pull:** the program's actual resourcing level versus what a comparable successful program received, timeline/market-timing context.

### A vendor/agency relationship unchanged for multiple years with no re-evaluation against current team scale or needs
- **Usually means:** the relationship may no longer fit the team's current scale or skill gaps, persisting out of habit rather than continued fit.
- **First question:** "When was this vendor relationship last evaluated against our current scale and needs, not just at the last contract renewal?"
- **Data to pull:** vendor relationship history and last review date, current team scale/needs versus what the vendor was originally engaged for.

### Budget cuts applied to the most visible or easiest-to-explain program rather than the lowest-ROI one
- **Usually means:** the cut may be politically easier rather than economically correct — the visible program survives because it's harder to explain cutting, not because it's performing better.
- **First question:** "Is this cut targeting the lowest-ROI program, or the one that's easiest to justify cutting to leadership?"
- **Data to pull:** ROI/performance data across all programs being considered for cuts, not just the one initially proposed.
