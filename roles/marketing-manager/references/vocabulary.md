# Marketing operations working vocabulary

Terms a marketing manager uses precisely. Format: definition → how a practitioner says it → common misuse.

**Portfolio allocation (proven vs. experimental)** — Deliberately splitting marketing budget between programs with established, measurable ROI and newer, unproven initiatives, revisited as evidence accumulates rather than set once and left static.
In use: "We're at 85% proven / 15% experimental this quarter — deliberately, not by inertia from last year's split."
Misuse: allocating 100% of budget to already-proven programs because it's the easiest case to defend to leadership, which slowly starves the experimentation that finds the next lever.

**Channel saturation (diminishing returns)** — The point beyond which additional spend in a channel produces a lower marginal return than it did at lower spend levels, a real constraint on how much a reallocation toward a "high-ROI" channel can actually deliver.
In use: "Paid search's 4.2x ROI holds to about $1.2M in spend — beyond that, the marginal return drops to roughly 3.0x."
Misuse: assuming a channel's current ROI multiplier applies linearly to any additional spend, overstating what reallocating toward the "best" channel would actually deliver.

**Capacity-first planning** — Checking a proposed plan against the team's actual available bandwidth — including recurring/maintenance work not visible on the roadmap — before committing a timeline, rather than planning against an idealized fully-available team.
In use: "The roadmap has to account for the 30% of team capacity that goes to recurring campaign maintenance, not just the new initiatives."
Misuse: building a quarterly plan based only on new initiative scope, missing the recurring work that consumes real capacity and produces predictable slippage.

**Dependency map** — An explicit accounting of what a marketing initiative needs from other functions (sales input, product data, leadership approval) before committing to a timeline, as opposed to assuming frictionless cooperation.
In use: "This campaign's dependency map shows we need product's Q3 roadmap confirmed before we can finalize messaging — that's a real constraint on our timeline, not a formality."
Misuse: building a plan's timeline without identifying its cross-functional dependencies, producing a plan that assumes an untested level of cooperation from other teams.

**Leading vs. lagging metrics** — Leading metrics (pipeline generated, engagement) are reported frequently for course-correction; lagging metrics (revenue attribution, retention impact) are reported on a longer cycle for real judgment on whether an initiative worked.
In use: "The weekly leading indicators look soft, but this is a long-cycle brand initiative — the lagging metric that actually judges it isn't due for another quarter."
Misuse: treating a single weekly (leading) number as the final verdict on a long-cycle initiative, producing premature and often wrong conclusions about whether it's working.

**Build-vs-buy (vendor/agency)** — The periodic evaluation of whether an external agency or vendor relationship still fits the team's current scale and skill gaps, as opposed to a relationship maintained purely by habit or inertia.
In use: "We re-evaluate our agency relationships annually against current team scale, not just when a contract renewal forces the question."
Misuse: defaulting to "keep the existing vendor" without periodically re-checking fit, letting a relationship that made sense at a different team size or budget level persist as quiet inefficiency.

**Attribution (marketing)** — The methodology used to connect marketing spend/activity to a downstream business outcome (pipeline, revenue), the basis for evaluating a program's real ROI rather than its activity volume.
In use: "Our attribution model credits this program with $3.36M in pipeline — that's the number that matters, not the 40 campaigns launched."
Misuse: reporting activity metrics (content produced, campaigns launched) as if they were equivalent to attributed business impact, which erodes credibility once leadership starts asking what the activity actually produced.

**Scope cut (explicit vs. quiet under-delivery)** — Deliberately communicating what's being deprioritized when resourcing tightens, as opposed to quietly under-delivering across all initiatives without flagging the tradeoff.
In use: "We're explicitly cutting the regional events program this quarter to protect the paid search budget — here's why, and here's what that means leadership should expect."
Misuse: absorbing a resourcing cut by quietly under-delivering a little across every program, so leadership discovers the shortfall after the fact instead of being able to make an informed call on the tradeoff.

**Marketing operating rhythm** — The recurring cadence of planning, execution, and review (quarterly planning, monthly business reviews) that keeps marketing aligned with the rest of the business, distinct from ad hoc reporting only when leadership asks.
In use: "The monthly business review is where we surface budget reallocation needs — it's a standing part of our operating rhythm, not a one-off request."
Misuse: only producing performance reporting or budget discussions reactively when leadership asks, rather than maintaining a predictable, standing cadence that surfaces issues proactively.

**Marginal ROI (for incremental budget decisions)** — The return generated by the next dollar of spend in a given channel, as distinct from that channel's average or historical ROI — the correct basis for deciding where incremental (not total) budget should go.
In use: "Content/SEO's marginal ROI on the next $200K is 3.8x — that's higher than paid search's marginal ROI once it's past its saturation point, so the incremental dollar goes there."
Misuse: allocating incremental budget based on a channel's historical average ROI rather than its current marginal ROI, missing that the two can diverge significantly once a channel is near saturation.
