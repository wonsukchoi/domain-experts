# Field playbook — stop sequences and report structure

Executed process, not description. Two parts: (1) stop/approach sequences with decision points, (2) report structure that survives cross-examination.

## Traffic stop sequence

1. **Position before lights.** Call location, plate, and description to dispatch before activating lights if there's any lag; if not, call it in immediately after. Choose a stop location with a shoulder wide enough to open the passenger door and a straight sightline down the vehicle — never stop on a blind curve if a safer spot is 200 feet ahead.
2. **Approach offset, not squared.** Walk the driver's-side line at the C-pillar, weapon side away from the vehicle, hand on the trunk/rear panel briefly to check for a hidden occupant and to leave a print if something happens.
3. **First 10 seconds: hands, then everything else.** "Hands on the wheel" before "license and registration." A driver who reaches into the glovebox before being told to is a specific fact — note the exact sequence (told to keep hands visible, reached anyway) not just "was reaching around."
4. **Ask, don't assume, for the legal basis to extend the stop.** The initial violation (equipment, speed) justifies the stop and a citation. Extending it — asking to search, running a K9, extended questioning beyond the traffic purpose — needs independent reasonable suspicion (nervousness alone is not enough; nervousness plus inconsistent travel-plan answers plus visible air-freshener overload plus a record for narcotics trafficking, in combination, can be).
5. **If reasonable suspicion of a weapon develops** (furtive movement toward a specific location, visible bulge, admission), a frisk of the driver's outer clothing for weapons is justified — not a search of the vehicle interior on that basis alone.
6. **If probable cause develops** (plain-view contraband, positive K9 alert on the exterior, admission of contraband), a vehicle search is justified under the automobile exception — document the specific fact that created PC before opening a single door or bag.
7. **Write the citation/report basis in the order it actually happened**, not in the order that reads most convincingly — a report reordered for effect is the first thing a cross-examining attorney finds inconsistent with body-cam timestamps.

## Domestic disturbance response sequence

1. **Stage before contact.** Cover officer parks short of the address; both officers approach from angles, never directly in front of a door or window.
2. **Listen before knocking.** 10–15 seconds of audible information (voices, sounds, sudden silence) is often the single highest-value fact in the whole call — note what you heard verbatim in the report.
3. **Split contact and cover on the doorway**, bladed stance, off the hinge side. Knock announced, request occupants step outside rather than officers stepping in, when safely possible — controls the fatal-funnel exposure.
4. **Separate all parties before taking statements** — same room, same story contaminates both. Interview out of earshot of each other.
5. **Apply the state's primary-aggressor factors explicitly**, not "who called 911": comparative and relative injuries, fear expressed by each party, history of the relationship (prior calls, existing protective order), disparity in size/strength, and any claim of self-defense. Document each factor with the specific observation, not a conclusion ("Party A had a 2-inch red mark on the left forearm consistent with a grab; Party B had no visible injury").
6. **If children are present, note their location and demeanor** at first contact, independent of whether they're interviewed — courts and child-welfare referrals weigh this even when it isn't the focus of the criminal charge.
7. **Arrest decision follows the state's mandatory/preferred-arrest statute** where one exists — many states remove officer discretion once probable cause for domestic battery exists, regardless of victim preference not to press charges.

## Report structure (incident / use-of-force narrative)

Four blocks, in this order, every time:

1. **Dispatch and approach** — what you were told, what you observed on approach, before any contact (this establishes your state of knowledge at each subsequent decision point).
2. **Chronological narrative of contact** — first person, timestamped where possible, one sentence per discrete fact. Pair every characterization with the specific act: not "the subject was uncooperative" but "the subject was told three times to place his hands on the vehicle and did not comply."
3. **Force/decision points, if any** — for each use of force: what the subject did immediately before, what force was used, and what happened immediately after (injury or lack of injury, resistance stopping). This block is what a Graham analysis is built from later — write it as if the reviewing attorney has nothing but this paragraph.
4. **Disposition** — citation, arrest, referral, no action, and the specific statute or department policy section that authorizes it.

### Fact vs. conclusion — before/after examples

| Conclusion (unusable alone) | Specific fact (usable) |
|---|---|
| "Subject was aggressive." | "Subject stepped to within 2 feet of me, raised both fists to chest height, and said 'come on then.'" |
| "Subject resisted arrest." | "When I attempted to place my hand on subject's left wrist, subject pulled the arm back and turned his torso away from me." |
| "The scene was chaotic." | "Three additional adults were present in the living room; two were shouting at each other and at officers simultaneously." |
| "Subject appeared intoxicated." | "Subject's speech was slurred, subject swayed while standing still, and I detected an odor of an alcoholic beverage on his breath from approximately 2 feet away." |
| "I felt threatened." | "Subject reached toward the right side of his waistband with his right hand while stepping backward, at which point I drew my firearm and ordered him to show his hands." |

## Radio/dispatch prioritization ladder (when calls stack)

1. Weapon displayed or described, in progress.
2. Injury or medical emergency, in progress.
3. Crime in progress with no weapon confirmed.
4. Officer needs backup (any call — this jumps the queue regardless of the underlying call type).
5. Cold crime report (property already gone, suspect already left).
6. Administrative/civil (parking, noise with no other factors).

Update your own priority read the moment new facts come over the air — dispatch's initial code is a starting triage, not a ceiling or floor on your response.
