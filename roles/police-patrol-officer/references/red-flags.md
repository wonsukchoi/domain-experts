# Red flags — what a patrol officer notices instantly

Smell tests with thresholds. Format per flag: signal → usually means → first question → data to pull.

### Dispatch call type says "verbal only" or "civil matter" but you hear silence mid-shout on approach

- **Usually means:** someone was physically interrupted (grabbed, struck, or restrained) — dispatch is relaying what the caller heard, not what's inside; a caller who only heard "yelling" may not have heard what happened in the same room seconds later.
- **First question:** "What were you doing right before the yelling stopped?" asked to the calmer party first, separately from the other.
- **Data to pull:** prior calls to the address (CAD history), any existing protective order, 911 call audio/transcript for the exact words used.

### Driver's hands leave view or move toward a specific location more than once after being told to keep them visible

- **Usually means:** deliberate movement toward a weapon, contraband, or a document being hidden — repetition after a direct instruction is the discriminating fact, not the movement itself.
- **First question:** none verbal — reposition to cover, then: "Show me your hands right now."
- **Data to pull:** none in the moment; for the report, the exact number of times and the specific location reached toward.

### Injury reported by dispatch as "minor" but the on-scene party won't let you see the injury or changes their account of how it happened

- **Usually means:** either the injury is worse than described (embarrassment, fear of consequences to the other party) or the "victim" is covering for the other party out of fear of retaliation, immigration status concerns, or financial dependency.
- **First question:** "Can I just take a quick look so I can document it's not serious?" — framed as ruling out harm, not building a case.
- **Data to pull:** photograph regardless of stated severity; prior CAD history at the address; any medical records the party volunteers.

### Two occupants of a residence or vehicle give accounts that are consistent on major facts but differ on small, checkable details

- **Usually means:** genuine independent recall (small discrepancies are normal and expected — two people never see identical details) rather than the perfectly matching story, which is the actual red flag for coaching or collusion.
- **First question:** ask each party the same small factual question separately ("what was on TV," "who was standing where") — divergence here is healthy, not suspicious.
- **Data to pull:** timestamps from any available video (doorbell cam, dashcam) to check against both accounts.

### Subject is unusually calm and cooperative immediately after a reported violent incident, with no elevated heart rate or breathing

- **Usually means:** either genuinely not involved as described, or someone experienced enough with the system to know that calm compliance reads as credible — this cuts both ways and is not itself evidence of guilt or innocence.
- **First question:** ask about the sequence of events in detail rather than accepting a short denial — inconsistencies surface in detail, not in demeanor.
- **Data to pull:** any body-worn camera from a prior contact with this subject, criminal history for pattern (prior domestic calls, prior weapons charges).

### A stop's articulated reasonable suspicion consists only of "nervous behavior" or "high-crime area"

- **Usually means:** the stop is not yet supported for anything beyond the original traffic violation — nervousness and location are permitted as *contributing* factors in the totality but courts have repeatedly rejected them as sufficient standing alone.
- **First question:** "What specific fact, beyond nervousness or the neighborhood, do I actually have?"
- **Data to pull:** dashcam/BWC timestamp of the specific statement or observation that would need to support extending the stop; department policy on stop-extension documentation.

### A subject's account of an injury doesn't match the injury's visible location or pattern

- **Usually means:** the stated mechanism is false — defensive wounds on forearms don't match a claimed "walked into a door," and a single linear mark is inconsistent with a claimed fall.
- **First question:** "Can you show me exactly how that happened?" and have them physically demonstrate it.
- **Data to pull:** photograph from multiple angles with a scale reference; note whether the demonstrated mechanism could physically produce the observed injury pattern.

### Your own report draft uses "aggressive," "combative," "uncooperative," or "irate" without a specific act attached in the same sentence

- **Usually means:** the narrative is reconstructing a conclusion from memory of the overall impression rather than the specific sequence of events — this is exactly what a defense cross-examination targets first.
- **First question:** "What did I actually see or hear, in the order it happened, that made me use that word?"
- **Data to pull:** body-worn camera footage, reviewed before finalizing the narrative, to reconstruct the specific sequence rather than relying on memory alone.

### Backup's ETA exceeds the time you estimate the situation can hold (subject escalating, weapon possibly present, multiple parties)

- **Usually means:** the tactically correct move is to reposition to a position of advantage and wait, not to make contact solo against a clock that isn't actually running against you as urgently as it feels.
- **First question:** "Is there an active, ongoing injury risk to someone right now, or is the urgency mine?"
- **Data to pull:** none — this is a live tactical call; radio the specific reason for any decision to act before backup arrives so it's on record in real time, not reconstructed after.
