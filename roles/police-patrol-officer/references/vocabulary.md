# Patrol working vocabulary

Terms a patrol officer uses precisely and generalists misuse.

### Reasonable suspicion
Specific, articulable facts — not a hunch — that criminal activity is afoot, sufficient to justify a brief investigative stop and, if the person is believed armed, a frisk (*Terry v. Ohio*, 1968).
**In use:** "I had reasonable suspicion for the stop — the vehicle matched the BOLO description and the plate came back to an address three blocks from the burglary — but I didn't have PC to search the trunk until the K9 alerted."
**Common misuse:** treating it as interchangeable with probable cause, or as satisfied by "high-crime area" or "seemed nervous" standing alone — courts have repeatedly held that neither, alone, clears the bar.

### Probable cause (PC)
Facts and circumstances sufficient for a reasonable person to believe a crime was committed and this person committed it (for arrest), or that evidence of a crime will be found in a particular place (for a search).
**In use:** "Once I saw the baggie in plain view on the passenger seat, that's PC for the vehicle, not just the seat area."
**Common misuse:** using PC and reasonable suspicion as synonyms, or believing PC requires certainty — it doesn't; it requires more than suspicion but well short of proof beyond a reasonable doubt.

### Objective reasonableness
The Graham v. Connor (1989) standard for judging use of force: what a reasonable officer would do given the facts known at the time, not the officer's actual intent and not facts discovered afterward.
**In use:** "Judge it objectively reasonable at the moment I decided, not against what we found out he had in his pocket after."
**Common misuse:** using 20/20 hindsight ("but it turned out to be a phone") to evaluate a force decision — the standard explicitly excludes information unavailable at the time.

### Totality of the circumstances
The requirement that reasonable suspicion, probable cause, and use-of-force reasonableness are each judged on the full combination of facts together, not any single fact in isolation and not a fixed checklist.
**In use:** "No single thing here is enough alone — nervous plus inconsistent travel story plus a fresh air-freshener smell plus a rental car dropped off two states from the pickup location — but together it's there."
**Common misuse:** citing one factor (nervousness, location) as if it alone clears the standard, or applying a rigid sequential checklist (a force continuum) in place of weighing everything together.

### Force continuum
A now largely deprecated training model that arrays force options as ascending steps (presence, verbal, soft hands, hard hands, less-lethal, lethal) implying force should escalate one step at a time.
**In use:** "We don't run a continuum anymore — write it against objective reasonableness, not against which rung we were supposedly on."
**Common misuse:** treating the chart as the legal test in a report or in training, which invites a false narrative that force must be tried in ascending order regardless of how fast the threat actually changed.

### Reactionary gap
The distance and time an officer needs to perceive a threat and respond before it closes, commonly referenced against the "21-foot rule" (Tueller, 1983) — an average subject can cover 21 feet in roughly 1.5 seconds, close to or faster than a holstered-to-fired response time.
**In use:** "I backed off two car lengths to keep the reactionary gap open before he was between me and the exit."
**Common misuse:** treating 21 feet as a hard legal line that justifies deadly force at that distance alone — it's a training rule of thumb about response time, not a use-of-force standard; the actual standard is still Graham's totality of circumstances.

### Fatal funnel
Any confined space (doorway, hallway, gap between vehicles) that limits an officer's ability to move laterally, making them a stationary and predictable target.
**In use:** "Blade off the hinge side, don't stand square in the doorway — that's the fatal funnel."
**Common misuse:** applying it only to entries/raids; it applies equally to a porch doorway on a routine knock-and-talk.

### Primary aggressor
The person determined, under state-specific statutory factors (comparative injury, size/strength disparity, history, self-defense claims), to have caused the domestic incident — distinct from who called 911 or who is present when police arrive.
**In use:** "She called it in, but the primary-aggressor factors point to him — she has defensive bruising on both forearms and he has none."
**Common misuse:** defaulting to "whoever called is the victim" or arresting whoever is most agitated at arrival, rather than applying the statutory factors.

### Excited utterance
A hearsay exception for a statement made under the stress of a startling event, admissible even though it's out-of-court, because the stress is believed to leave no time to fabricate.
**In use:** "Document the exact words and the time elapsed — 'he's got a knife!' yelled while still inside the house qualifies; the same statement given calmly to us twenty minutes later at the station probably doesn't."
**Common misuse:** assuming any statement made at a chaotic scene qualifies — the exception turns on temporal proximity to the startling event, and officers should record timing precisely because that's what's litigated.

### Terry frisk (pat-down)
A limited pat-down of outer clothing for weapons, justified by reasonable suspicion the person is armed and dangerous — not a general search for evidence.
**In use:** "I frisked for weapons only — felt nothing consistent with a weapon, so I didn't go into the pocket."
**Common misuse:** using a frisk as a pretext to search for contraband generally; if an officer feels something whose incriminating nature isn't immediately apparent by touch, retrieving it exceeds the scope (the "plain feel" doctrine has real limits).

### Custodial interrogation / Miranda trigger
Miranda warnings are required only when a person is both in custody (not free to leave) and being interrogated (asked questions likely to elicit an incriminating response) — routine investigatory questions during a stop, or before custody attaches, don't trigger it.
**In use:** "He wasn't in cuffs and wasn't detained beyond the traffic stop's scope when he made that statement, so Miranda doesn't apply yet."
**Common misuse:** assuming Miranda is required for any question asked by police, or that its absence makes a voluntary, non-custodial statement inadmissible — it doesn't.

### Articulable facts
The specific, describable observations (not impressions or categories) an officer can name to support a stop, a search, or a use of force — the raw material both reasonable suspicion and probable cause are built from.
**In use:** "Give me the articulable facts, not the summary — what did you actually see him do before you frisked him?"
**Common misuse:** substituting a category word ("suspicious," "furtive," "aggressive") for the fact itself; a category is a conclusion the fact is supposed to support, not a fact.

### Field interview (FI) card / contact card
A documentation form for a Terry stop that doesn't result in arrest, recording the specific facts supporting reasonable suspicion at the time, independent of whether anything is found.
**In use:** "No arrest, but I still filed the FI card with the specific facts — matched the BOLO vehicle description and plate partial."
**Common misuse:** skipping documentation because "nothing came of it" — the FI card is exactly what establishes the stop was lawful if it's ever challenged later, whether or not an arrest followed.
