# Vocabulary

Terms generalists conflate or use loosely that a refractory materials repairer keeps sharply separate.

## Refractory vs. firebrick vs. insulating brick

**Refractory** is the general category of heat-resistant materials — brick, castable, gunned, plastic, or fiber — used to line a vessel against high temperature. **Firebrick** specifically means fired, shaped brick (as opposed to poured monolithic material). **Insulating brick** (or insulating firebrick, IFB) is a lightweight, low-density refractory chosen for low thermal conductivity, not for hot-face wear resistance — it's usually a backup layer behind a denser working lining, not the surface exposed to the process.

*In use:* "The hot face is dense high-alumina brick; behind it we've got IFB backup — don't spec the IFB for wear resistance, it's doing insulation, not duty."

**Common misuse:** calling any brick "firebrick" regardless of density or purpose, or specifying insulating brick where the actual requirement is hot-face erosion resistance.

## Castable vs. gunned vs. plastic refractory

**Castable** refractory is a dry mix combined with water and poured or vibration-cast into forms — highest strength and most predictable cure, slowest to place. **Gunned** (shotcrete-style) refractory is sprayed through a nozzle with water added at the nozzle — faster placement, more operator-dependent density and finish. **Plastic refractory** is a pre-tempered, ready-to-ram material rammed into place by hand or pneumatic tool — no water mixing on site, used for patches and irregular shapes.

*In use:* "We're gunning the sidewalls to hit the schedule, but the burner block gets cast — too much precision needed there for gunning."

**Common misuse:** treating all three as interchangeable "monolithic refractory" with the same cure schedule — gunned material's water content and density vary by crew and nozzle technique, so its dry-out curve can't be assumed identical to a cast section of the same nominal thickness.

## Service temperature vs. PCE (pyrometric cone equivalent)

**Service temperature** is the maximum temperature a refractory is rated to withstand in continuous use under specified conditions. **PCE** is a laboratory measure of the temperature at which a small cone of the material actually deforms under standardized heating — a raw material property test, not a service rating, and it doesn't account for load, atmosphere, or chemical attack in actual use.

*In use:* "PCE says this mix could go to 3300°F, but the service rating under load in a reducing atmosphere is closer to 2900 — don't spec off the cone test alone."

**Common misuse:** quoting PCE as if it were the safe operating temperature for the installed lining under real load and atmosphere.

## Spalling — thermal vs. mechanical vs. steam

**Thermal spalling** is cracking/flaking from rapid temperature change stressing the material beyond its thermal-shock tolerance. **Mechanical spalling** is physical loss from impact, abrasion, or structural load. **Steam spalling** is explosive or progressive material loss from trapped moisture flashing to steam faster than it can escape during dry-out or first heat-up — a distinct failure mode from the other two, driven by cure-schedule error rather than in-service conditions.

*In use:* "That's steam spall, not thermal shock — it happened during first heat-up, before the kiln ever ran a full cycle."

**Common misuse:** calling any lining failure "thermal spalling" by default, which misdirects the fix toward material selection when the actual cause was a compressed dry-out schedule.

## Hot face vs. cold face vs. working lining vs. safety/backup lining

**Hot face** is the surface directly exposed to the furnace interior; **cold face** is the surface against the shell. **Working lining** is the layer engineered to wear and be replaced on a campaign cycle; **safety (or backup) lining** is a second layer behind it, sized to protect the shell if the working lining fails before the next planned reline — not meant to see process conditions in normal operation.

*In use:* "If we're reading shell temperature climbing, the working lining's gone and we're into the backup — that's an unplanned-outage signal, not a routine wear reading."

**Common misuse:** describing backup lining wear as if it were expected working-lining wear — backup-layer erosion means the working lining has already failed.

## Campaign life vs. reline

**Campaign life** is the total service duration a lining is expected (or achieves) to last before it must be replaced. A **reline** is the actual replacement event. Campaign life is a planning/benchmarking number; a reline is the deliverable.

*In use:* "Design campaign life here is 12 months; we relined at 8 — that's a 33% shortfall to explain before we spec the next one the same way."

**Common misuse:** using "reline" and "campaign" interchangeably in scheduling conversations, which blurs whether a schedule is tracking expected life or just counting past events.

## Slag penetration vs. chemical attack

**Chemical attack** is the general process of a refractory reacting with process chemistry (alkalis, iron oxide, sulfates) and degrading. **Slag penetration** is the specific mechanism where molten slag infiltrates the refractory's pore structure, changing its properties in depth (not just at the surface) before visible spalling occurs — found by coring and cross-section analysis, not by surface inspection.

*In use:* "Surface looks intact, but the core shows slag penetration two inches deep — this section's structurally compromised even though nothing's spalled yet."

**Common misuse:** judging remaining lining life from surface appearance alone; slag penetration is invisible from the hot face until the weakened zone finally spalls.

## Dry-out vs. cure vs. sintering

**Cure** is the chemical/physical setting of a castable's binder after mixing — happens at or near ambient temperature over hours. **Dry-out** is the controlled heat-up that drives free and chemically bound water out of the cured material before full service temperature. **Sintering** is the high-temperature bonding that occurs at or near service temperature, after dry-out, that gives the refractory its final in-service strength and microstructure.

*In use:* "It's cured and ready to dry out, but don't confuse dry-out with sintering — sintering doesn't happen until we're near service temp, well after the moisture's gone."

**Common misuse:** treating "cure time" as if it covers the full path to service-ready, which skips the dry-out schedule entirely and invites steam-spall.

## Expansion joint / expansion allowance

The gap engineered into a refractory lining, filled with a compressible burn-out spacer, sized to absorb the material's calculated linear thermal growth between installation temperature and service temperature.

*In use:* "Joint spacing on this run is every 24 inches at a quarter-inch each — that's sized for a 1% expansion coefficient over a 40-foot run, not a standard detail copied from the last job."

**Common misuse:** applying a "standard" joint spacing/width regardless of the specific material's expansion coefficient or the lining's actual thickness — the two jobs it was copied from may have had different growth entirely.

## Basic vs. acidic vs. neutral refractory (chemistry classification)

Refractories are classified by their chemical behavior toward slag, independent of temperature rating: **basic** (magnesia, magnesia-chrome, dolomite) resists basic slags and alkalis; **acidic** (silica, fireclay) resists acidic slags but is attacked by basic/alkaline chemistry; **neutral** (high-alumina, chromite) sits between and tolerates a wider but not unlimited chemistry range.

*In use:* "The kiln dust here runs alkaline — we need a basic lining in the burning zone regardless of what the temperature chart says is 'good enough.'"

**Common misuse:** selecting purely by temperature chart while ignoring this classification, which is exactly how a temperature-rated-fine material fails fast to chemical attack.
