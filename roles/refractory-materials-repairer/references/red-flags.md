# Red flags

Smell tests a senior repairer catches early in a job walk-down or a schedule review. Format for each: what it usually means, the first question to ask, and the data to pull before committing to a plan.

## 1. Dry-out schedule compressed to hit a restart date

**Usually means:** the moisture-hold time was cut on a >4 in castable section to save one or two days against the plant's target restart — the single most common precursor to steam-spall failure in the first weeks of a new lining.

**First question:** "What's the hold time in this schedule versus the supplier's rated hold for this thickness, and who approved the difference?"

**Data to pull:** the as-planned heat-up curve against the material data sheet's minimum hold/ramp for the actual poured thickness; any prior steam-spall incidents on this crew's compressed schedules.

## 2. Lining failed at less than 70% of its stated campaign life

**Usually means:** wrong material class for the zone's actual chemistry (most common), or a dry-out/install defect from the prior reline; less often, a process upset that pushed the zone hotter or more corrosive than design.

**First question:** "What did the post-mortem cores show — slag penetration depth, and where exactly did the failure start?"

**Data to pull:** core samples and slag-penetration analysis from the failed section; zone temperature and dust/atmosphere chemistry logs for the campaign; installation records (mix water, dry-out log) for the prior reline.

## 3. Wear rate outpacing the sister-furnace/kiln benchmark

**Usually means:** either this unit's actual operating chemistry or temperature has drifted from its sister units, or the last reline used an off-spec material or mix — either way it's an early signal, not noise.

**First question:** "How does this zone's measured thickness loss per month compare to the same zone on the sister unit running the same duty?"

**Data to pull:** thickness-gauge readings by zone and by month across sister units; process logs (temperature, dust loading, feed chemistry) for both units over the same window.

## 4. Refractory spec chosen by installed cost per square foot alone

**Usually means:** the spec was set from a quote comparison without a zone-specific atmosphere/chemistry check — the leading cause of a material that meets the temperature rating on paper and fails from chemical attack in practice.

**First question:** "What's this zone's alkali/sulfate loading and slag basicity, and does the proposed material's compatibility data cover it?"

**Data to pull:** dust/feed chemistry analysis for the zone; the material's chemical-compatibility data sheet; annualized cost comparison (material + downtime × relines/year), not per-event cost.

## 5. Cracking or spalling concentrated at or near expansion-joint locations

**Usually means:** joint spacing or width was copied from a prior campaign's different thickness or material instead of recalculated, so the lining's actual growth exceeds what the joints can absorb.

**First question:** "What expansion coefficient and run length was this joint spacing calculated against, and does it match the material and thickness actually installed?"

**Data to pull:** the joint-spacing calculation (or its absence) from the install record; the installed material's supplier expansion-coefficient data at service temperature; photos/measurements of joint condition versus mid-panel condition.

## 6. Steam visible or popping sounds during heat-up, inside the planned schedule window

**Usually means:** moisture is still migrating out of the lining faster than assumed, or local thickness is greater than the schedule was built against (e.g., a low spot in the pour) — continuing the ramp on schedule risks steam-spall even though the clock says it's fine.

**First question:** "What do the embedded thermocouples show right now versus the planned curve at this hour mark?"

**Data to pull:** live thermocouple readings (hot-face and mid-wall); as-built thickness survey of the pour versus the design thickness used to build the schedule.

## 7. Shell hot spots on infrared survey above the process's normal baseline

**Usually means:** localized lining thinning or a breach has reduced insulating thickness to the point heat is reaching the shell — an early warning ahead of a burn-through, not yet an emergency.

**First question:** "How does this hot-spot reading compare to the shell-temperature baseline for this zone at this operating rate, and when was it last surveyed?"

**Data to pull:** infrared survey history for the zone; internal thickness-gauge data if accessible; operating rate/temperature at time of survey (a hot spot at reduced throughput is worse than the same reading at full rate).

## 8. Anchor tips visible or discolored on a lining inspection

**Usually means:** hot-face erosion has progressed far enough to expose the anchor system, which is now itself exposed to the process atmosphere and will fail faster than the surrounding refractory, accelerating local loss.

**First question:** "How much hot-face material is left over the anchor at this point, against the original installed cover?"

**Data to pull:** thickness-gauge reading at and around the anchor location; original design cover-over-anchor spec; anchor material rating versus zone temperature.

## 9. Second reline required at the same location within one campaign

**Usually means:** the root cause of the first failure (material mismatch, process upset, or install defect) was never addressed — the patch treated the symptom.

**First question:** "What was diagnosed as the cause the first time, and what changed before this repair versus that diagnosis?"

**Data to pull:** the prior repair's root-cause note (or absence of one); process data for the interval between the two failures; whether the second repair used the same material and method as the first.

## 10. Full reline scheduled with no allowance for controlled dry-out in the outage plan

**Usually means:** the outage duration was set from install time alone, without adding the required heat-up curve — guarantees pressure to compress the dry-out once the crew is on site and the clock is already running.

**First question:** "Does the outage schedule's total duration include the full dry-out/heat-up curve for the specified thickness, or just demolition and install?"

**Data to pull:** the outage Gantt against the material's rated dry-out schedule for the as-specified thickness; downtime-cost-per-day figure used to justify any compression.
