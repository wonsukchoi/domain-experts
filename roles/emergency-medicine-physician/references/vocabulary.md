# Vocabulary

### Medical screening exam (MSE)
The EMTALA-mandated exam, performed by a qualified provider, sufficient to determine whether an emergency medical condition exists — required regardless of ability to pay, before any other disposition consideration.
**In use:** "We can't even discuss transfer until the MSE is documented as complete."
**Common misuse:** Treating a brief triage vitals check as satisfying the MSE, when it has to be thorough enough to actually rule an emergency condition in or out.

### Boarding
The time an admitted patient spends physically in the ED, after the admission decision and bed request, before moving to an inpatient bed.
**In use:** "Boarding's at six hours tonight, which is exactly why the reassessment interval matters more, not less."
**Common misuse:** Conflating boarding time with total ED length of stay for a patient who's ultimately discharged, or with wait-to-be-seen time.

### Left without being seen (LWBS)
A patient who registers or triages but leaves before completing a physician evaluation.
**In use:** "LWBS crept to 6% overnight — that's an input/throughput problem, not a triage-nurse problem."
**Common misuse:** Tracking LWBS purely as a patient-satisfaction metric rather than a safety metric — patients with real emergencies leave too when the wait is long enough.

### Disposition
The discharge, observation, admission, or transfer decision that closes an ED encounter — distinct from diagnosis.
**In use:** "The disposition is observation for serial troponin, not discharge, even though the diagnosis is still open."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a disposition decision requires a firm diagnosis first, when a well-reasoned disposition can close an encounter with genuine diagnostic uncertainty remaining.

### Can't-miss diagnosis
A low-probability, high-consequence diagnosis that must be actively excluded for a given presentation regardless of where it ranks by pretest probability.
**In use:** "PE is the can't-miss here, even though the history and vitals point toward costochondritis."
**Common misuse:** Treating "can't-miss" as a synonym for "most likely," which inflates workups for genuinely low-risk presentations instead of targeting the specific dangerous alternative.

### ESI (Emergency Severity Index)
A five-level triage algorithm that predicts expected resource utilization, not diagnosis or prognosis.
**In use:** "ESI 3 just means we expect two-plus resources — it doesn't mean this patient is stable."
**Common misuse:** Using the ESI level as a proxy for how sick a patient actually is, rather than for how many resources the visit is expected to consume.

### EMTALA
The Emergency Medical Treatment and Labor Act (42 U.S.C. §1395dd) — requires a medical screening exam and stabilization for anyone presenting to a Medicare-participating ED, before transfer or turn-away for inability to pay.
**In use:** "EMTALA doesn't care what her insurance says — we stabilize first."
**Common misuse:** Assuming the obligation ends once a bed is requested elsewhere, or that it applies only to uninsured patients rather than to every presenting patient.

### Safety-netting / return precautions
Explicit, named discharge instructions describing exactly what symptom change should trigger an immediate return, tied to the specific badness that was ruled out.
**In use:** "Return precautions: any chest pain at rest, new shortness of breath, or fainting — come back, don't call the office first."
**Common misuse:** Generic "return if it gets worse" language, which gives the patient no specific, actionable trigger to act on.

### Sepsis hour-1 bundle
The Surviving Sepsis Campaign's bundle — lactate measurement, blood cultures before antibiotics, broad-spectrum antibiotics, and a 30 mL/kg crystalloid bolus for hypotension or lactate ≥4 — all initiated within one hour of recognition.
**In use:** "Hour-1 bundle starts now — cultures and lactate before the first antibiotic dose goes in."
**Common misuse:** Treating the "one hour" as a soft target rather than a clock that starts at clinical recognition, not at triage or door time.

### Premature closure
Stopping the diagnostic process once a plausible explanation is found, without checking it against all available evidence.
**In use:** "Don't premature-closure this as anxiety just because she's young and it's a busy Friday."
**Common misuse:** Used loosely as a synonym for "the diagnosis turned out wrong," rather than specifically the failure to keep considering disconfirming evidence before closing.

### HEART Pathway
A validated chest-pain protocol combining the HEART score with serial troponins, 3 hours apart, to determine same-visit discharge eligibility.
**In use:** "She's HEART Pathway low-risk — score of 2, two negative troponins three hours apart — safe for discharge with outpatient follow-up."
**Common misuse:** Declaring a patient "pathway negative" from the HEART score alone, without the required second troponin.

### NEDOCS
A validated ED overcrowding score combining bed count, boarder count, and wait times into a single crowding-severity number.
**In use:** "NEDOCS is reading severely overcrowded — that changes staffing and reassessment cadence, not the clinical admission threshold."
**Common misuse:** Treating a high NEDOCS score as license to lower the workup or admission threshold rather than as an operational-staffing signal.

### Split-flow / vertical model
An ED operations design routing ambulatory, lower-acuity patients to a separate rapid-treatment area instead of a bed, to protect throughput for higher-acuity patients.
**In use:** "Vertical flow can take him — he's ambulatory and ESI 4."
**Common misuse:** Routing a patient to vertical flow based on chief complaint alone, without confirming the ESI level and vital-sign stability that actually make it appropriate.

### qSOFA
A bedside score (altered mentation, respiratory rate ≥22, systolic BP ≤100) used to flag possible sepsis-associated organ dysfunction outside the ICU.
**In use:** "qSOFA is 2 — that's enough to trigger the sepsis pathway even before the lactate's back."
**Common misuse:** Using qSOFA as a tool to rule sepsis out when it's negative — it has poor sensitivity for exclusion and is designed as a screening flag, not a diagnostic or exclusion criterion.
