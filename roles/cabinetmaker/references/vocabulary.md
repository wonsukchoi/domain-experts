# Vocabulary

### Wood movement
The dimensional change wood undergoes, primarily across the grain, as its moisture content shifts with ambient humidity.
**In use:** "Build in movement allowance now — this panel's still going to move seasonally even after it's finished."
**Common misuse:** Assuming dry, finished wood no longer moves; properly dried wood keeps expanding and contracting seasonally with humidity changes for its entire life.

### Equilibrium moisture content (EMC)
The moisture content wood settles to when in balance with a given ambient humidity and temperature.
**In use:** "This shop runs about 8% EMC — anything wetter than that needs to acclimate before we mill it."
**Common misuse:** Treating a single kiln-dried moisture content reading as permanent, rather than understanding it as the EMC for the drying environment specifically, which shifts again once the wood moves to a different environment.

### Cross-grain construction
Joining two pieces of wood with their grain directions running perpendicular or at a significant angle to each other.
**In use:** "That's cross-grain — needs a breadboard joint, not a straight glue-up, or it'll split within a year."
**Common misuse:** Not recognizing that a rigid glue joint between cross-grain pieces guarantees eventual cracking as differential movement stresses the joint, regardless of glue strength.

### Floating panel
A solid wood panel held in a groove within a frame without being glued along its full perimeter, allowing it to expand and contract with humidity.
**In use:** "Panel floats in the groove — glued only at the center point so it can move symmetrically both ways."
**Common misuse:** Assuming any panel set into a groove is automatically "floating," when gluing it even partially along a long edge can still restrain the movement it actually needs.

### Breadboard end
A cross-grain end cap joined to a tabletop's long-grain edge using a method — a tongue with elongated holes, a sliding dovetail — specifically designed to accommodate the differential movement between the two grain directions.
**In use:** "Breadboard's pegged through slotted holes, not fixed holes — that's what lets the top move under it."
**Common misuse:** Attaching a breadboard end with a rigid, fully-glued joint, which defeats the entire purpose the design exists for.

### Mortise and tenon
A joint where a projecting tenon on one piece fits into a matching mortise cavity in another, commonly used for load-bearing connections.
**In use:** "This leg-to-apron joint is mortise and tenon — it's carrying real racking load, not just holding the pieces together."
**Common misuse:** Treating it as interchangeable with faster joinery methods (pocket screws, dowels) regardless of the actual load the joint will carry in service.

### Racking resistance
A joint or structure's ability to resist distortion under a lateral or shear-type load.
**In use:** "Chair joints fail from racking under use, not from someone pulling the legs straight apart — design for that stress specifically."
**Common misuse:** Evaluating a joint only for how it resists a straight pull-apart force, ignoring that many furniture failures happen under racking or lateral stress instead.

### Veneer
A thin layer of wood applied to a substrate — often plywood or MDF — for appearance, distinct from solid wood construction.
**In use:** "Veneer's walnut, substrate's MDF — checked the movement match before committing to this combination."
**Common misuse:** Assuming veneer and its substrate move identically; a mismatch in their movement characteristics can cause delamination or surface checking over time.

### Species movement coefficient
A value, or practical shop-derived figure, describing how much a specific wood species expands or contracts per unit change in moisture content or humidity.
**In use:** "Walnut's practical shop figure is about 1/4" per foot across a full seasonal swing — that's what the groove depth is sized against."
**Common misuse:** Applying a generic "wood moves" assumption uniformly across species, when actual movement rates vary substantially between species and even by how the board was cut.

### Quarter-sawn vs. flat-sawn
Two different methods of milling lumber from a log that produce different grain orientation and, correspondingly, different dimensional stability.
**In use:** "Quarter-sawn stock for this panel — it moves less and more predictably across width than the flat-sawn boards we had."
**Common misuse:** Assuming all boards of the same species move identically, without accounting for how the cut itself changes the expected movement direction and magnitude.
