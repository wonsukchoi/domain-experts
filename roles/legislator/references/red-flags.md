# Red flags — signals worth noticing in bill text and process

Smell tests with thresholds. Any single one can be innocent; two or three together are a pattern. Neutral process observations, not positions on any real policy.

### A bill's popular framing describes a benefit with no mention of its funding mechanism
- **Usually means:** the real cost or tradeoff is likely concentrated on a different, less visible group — tracing the funding mechanism itself is where the actual second-order effect is usually found.
- **First question:** "What specifically funds this benefit, and who bears that cost?"
- **Data to pull:** the bill's actual funding/offset provisions, fiscal/impact office scoring if available.

### A vote being framed as a clean ideological yes/no with no acknowledgment of the amendments and compromises that shaped the actual bill text
- **Usually means:** the bill in front of the chamber is being judged against an unavailable ideal version rather than the realistic alternative (a different, less favorable bill, or no bill at all).
- **First question:** "What's the realistic alternative if this specific version fails — a better bill, or nothing?"
- **Data to pull:** the bill's amendment history, any competing versions that were considered and dropped.

### Constituent input on an issue coming overwhelmingly from one small, organized group with no comparable data on broader sentiment
- **Usually means:** the loudest voice may not be the representative one — a vocal, organized minority can create an impression of broad support or opposition that doesn't match actual constituency sentiment.
- **First question:** "What does broader constituent data (surveys, district-wide contact volume) show, separate from this organized group's outreach?"
- **Data to pull:** constituent contact volume and source breakdown, any available district-wide survey data.

### A bill's stated intent or title differing meaningfully from its operative text, funding mechanism, or enforcement provisions
- **Usually means:** the actual effect of the bill may diverge substantially from how it's being marketed, whether the divergence is intentional or not.
- **First question:** "Does the operative text and funding mechanism actually deliver what the title/framing promises?"
- **Data to pull:** the bill's full operative text and funding/enforcement sections, compared against its stated purpose.

### A coalition trade where the exchanged support serves a narrow private or donor interest with no identifiable broader public benefit
- **Usually means:** this may have shaded from ordinary coalition politics into a trade that serves narrow interest over public interest — the line worth checking explicitly.
- **First question:** "What's the public interest case for this specific trade, separate from the private/donor interest it also serves?"
- **Data to pull:** the specific trade being made, any disclosed donor or private-interest connections to either side of it.

### A vote-counting effort proceeding with no realistic path to the needed majority identified
- **Usually means:** effort may be spent on persuasion or advocacy for a position that has no plausible route to passage, when that effort could be redirected toward a version that does.
- **First question:** "What's the actual whip count, and what would have to change to get from here to a majority?"
- **Data to pull:** current vote count/whip tally, identified swing votes and what they've stated they'd need to move.

### A procedural decision (committee assignment, amendment rule, floor schedule) that effectively determines a bill's fate independent of its substantive merit
- **Usually means:** procedural leverage may be deciding the outcome more than the argument on the merits — worth naming explicitly rather than treating the outcome as purely substantive.
- **First question:** "Did this bill's fate turn on the substance of the debate, or on which committee it was assigned to / whether a favorable amendment was ruled germane?"
- **Data to pull:** the bill's procedural history (committee assignment, amendment rulings, scheduling), compared against the substantive arguments made.
