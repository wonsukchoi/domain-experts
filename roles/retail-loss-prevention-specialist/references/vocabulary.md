# Floor loss-prevention working vocabulary

Terms a floor specialist uses precisely and a generalist blurs together. Format: definition → how a practitioner says it → common misuse.

**Reasonable suspicion** — The behavior-based standard (not appearance-based) that justifies opening surveillance on a subject: an observed act like concealment, tag removal, or camera-avoidance, not a hunch or a demographic.
In use: "Camera-checking twice and moving to a blind spot is reasonable suspicion to start watching — it's not enough to stop yet."
Misuse: treating reasonable suspicion as equivalent to probable cause to stop — reasonable suspicion justifies *observation*, not an approach; the stop still needs the full evidentiary chain.

**Continuous observation** — Unbroken personal sightline (or a corroborated hand-off to another trained observer) on a subject and item from concealment through every point-of-sale opportunity, with no gap.
In use: "I lost him behind the rack for maybe fifteen seconds — that's not continuous anymore, we're documentation-only."
Misuse: assuming a brief gap doesn't matter if the specialist is confident it's still the same person — the standard is about the sightline, not the specialist's confidence level.

**Base-and-click (behavior sequence)** — The informal term for the repeatable phase pattern shoplifters exhibit: establishing a "base" of normal shopping behavior, then a "click" into the theft-specific behavior (camera-check, concealment) that a trained observer learns to distinguish from ordinary browsing.
In use: "He was in base behavior for the first two minutes — normal browsing — then clicked into concealment right at the endcap."
Misuse: treating any nervous-looking behavior as the "click" — the click is a specific observable act (concealment, tag removal), not general nervousness or unusual dress.

**Diversion technique** — A theft method where a second person distracts an associate or draws attention away while a first person conceals merchandise.
In use: "The return-policy question went on two minutes longer than any real question would — that's a diversion, watch the other one."
Misuse: assuming diversion always requires two people working the exact same item — the diverting party is often not carrying anything themselves.

**SCO shrink (self-checkout shrink)** — Loss specifically attributable to self-checkout lanes: skip-scanning, barcode swapping to a cheaper item, or weight-mismatch exploitation, tracked separately from traditional register shrink because the intervention point differs.
In use: "SCO shrink is up at lanes 3 and 5 specifically — that's a pre-payment intervention problem, not a general shrink problem."
Misuse: folding SCO shrink into the general external-theft category without noting it needs a different response (pre-payment lane monitoring) than traditional concealment theft does.

**Wardrobing** — Buying an item, using it once (often with tags carefully preserved or reattached), and returning it as unused.
In use: "The formalwear return came back with makeup on the collar — that's wardrobing, not a defective-product return."
Misuse: treating every clothing return as a potential wardrobing case rather than checking for the specific signature (wear signs, timing tied to an event, tag reattachment).

**Chain of custody** — The documented, unbroken record of who has physical possession of recovered merchandise or evidence from the moment of recovery through final disposition.
In use: "I logged the earbuds into the LP office safe at 2:22, signed over to the manager at shift change — that's the chain of custody if this goes anywhere."
Misuse: recovering an item and setting it aside without a timestamped handoff log — an unbroken chain matters as much for evidentiary integrity as the observation itself.

**No-pursuit policy** — The standing rule that a specialist never chases a fleeing subject beyond the immediate stop area or off store property, regardless of item value.
In use: "He bolted for the parking lot — that's outside the no-pursuit line, let him go and call it in."
Misuse: treating no-pursuit as optional based on how valuable the item is — the policy exists because injury and liability risk don't scale down just because the item is expensive.

**Non-confrontational approach (interview)** — A calm, fact-stating, non-accusatory script used when stopping a subject or gathering initial account — states the observed fact and the request, not an accusation of intent.
In use: "I said 'I saw you place these in your bag, please come back inside' — not 'why did you steal this.'"
Misuse: treating a calm tone as optional politeness rather than a deliberate technique that reduces both legal exposure and the chance of the subject escalating.

**Organized retail crime (ORC)** — Coordinated theft across multiple stores or regions by groups stealing for resale rather than personal use.
In use: "Same vehicle, same three individuals, four stores in one afternoon — that's an ORC pattern, not four unrelated shoplifting attempts."
Misuse: labeling any repeat shoplifter "ORC" without the actual coordination signals (multi-location, resale intent, group coordination) — the label should change the response (police/task-force referral), so it shouldn't be applied loosely.

**Integrity shop** — An undercover compliance check where a shopper (often the specialist or a colleague) makes a tracked purchase to verify a specific cashier scans and rings items correctly.
In use: "We ran an integrity shop on register 4 after the void pattern — every item scanned clean, so the flag was a false positive."
Misuse: treating an integrity shop as a one-time clearance rather than a periodic check — a cashier who passes once can still develop a pattern later.

**Deterrence value (presence)** — The loss avoided purely from a specialist's visible or felt presence in a zone, distinct from and often larger than the value recovered through apprehensions.
In use: "We can't put a case number on deterrence, but shrink in this zone dropped the two weeks I was walking it on a visible rotation."
Misuse: judging a shift's value only by apprehension count — a shift with zero stops in a heavily-walked high-shrink zone may still have been the most valuable shift of the week.
