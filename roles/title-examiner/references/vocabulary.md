# Title examination working vocabulary

Terms a title examiner uses precisely and a generalist blurs together. Format: definition → how a practitioner says it → common misuse.

**Chain of title** — The documented sequence of ownership transfers (deeds, wills, court orders) from a confirmed root of title through the current owner, where every link must connect to the prior one without a gap.
In use: "The chain traces clean for 45 years — every grantor matches the prior grantee, no gaps."
Misuse: assuming a chain is established just because the current owner's deed exists — the chain requires the full unbroken sequence back to the confirmed root, not just the most recent transfer.

**Marketable title** — Title that's reasonably free from defects, encumbrances, or the threat of litigation such that a reasonably prudent buyer would accept it, established (in many states) by statute through a defined search period covered without a break.
In use: "The 40-year marketable title act period is satisfied — our search goes back 45 years to a clean root."
Misuse: conflating marketable title with "perfect" title — marketable title tolerates certain standard exceptions (easements, standard tax provisions) and doesn't require the complete absence of every recorded interest.

**Root of title** — The instrument (typically a deed) that begins the required search period, treated as the starting point from which the chain of title is traced forward to the current owner.
In use: "The root of title here is the 1978 deed — that's where the 45-year search begins."
Misuse: treating the earliest available recorded instrument as automatically the root — the root is specifically the instrument that satisfies the jurisdiction's required search period, which may be later than the earliest instrument on record.

**Race statute (recording priority)** — A recording statute type where the first party to record their interest wins priority, regardless of whether they had notice of a prior unrecorded competing interest.
In use: "This is a race jurisdiction — even though the second buyer knew about the first sale, recording first still wins here."
Misuse: assuming notice of a prior interest defeats a later recorder in every jurisdiction — under a pure race statute, notice is irrelevant to the priority outcome.

**Notice statute (recording priority)** — A recording statute type where a subsequent bona fide purchaser without notice of a prior unrecorded interest wins priority, regardless of who recorded first.
In use: "Under this state's notice statute, the second buyer wins because they had no notice of the first sale — recording order doesn't matter here."
Misuse: assuming recording first always wins — in a pure notice jurisdiction, an unrecorded first interest can still lose to a later, unaware purchaser even if that purchaser never records.

**Race-notice statute (recording priority)** — A recording statute type requiring both that a subsequent purchaser have no notice of a prior unrecorded interest AND that they record first — either condition failing defeats their priority.
In use: "This is a race-notice state — the second buyer had no notice, which is necessary but not sufficient; they also have to record first to win."
Misuse: treating race-notice as identical to either pure race or pure notice — it requires both conditions together, which is a materially different and often more protective standard for the first party.

**Curative title work** — Action taken to fix a title defect so it no longer impairs marketability — a corrective deed, a mortgage satisfaction/release, or a quiet title action, distinct from simply insuring around the defect.
In use: "This is a curable defect — we need a corrective deed from the original grantor before closing, not an exception."
Misuse: assuming every defect requires curative work — many defects can instead be resolved through an insurable exception, which is faster and doesn't require locating or re-executing documents from parties who may no longer be reachable.

**Insuring over (exception) vs. requiring cure** — Insuring over means the title insurer issues coverage subject to a specific listed exception for the defect, accepting the risk (often for a lower-risk issue); requiring cure means the defect must be resolved before the policy issues at all.
In use: "Underwriting agreed to insure over the easement as a listed exception — no need to cure it since it doesn't affect marketability."
Misuse: assuming an "exception" means the defect is being ignored — it's a deliberate underwriting decision to accept and disclose a specific risk, not an oversight.

**Judgment lien duration/enforceability** — The statutory period during which a recorded judgment remains enforceable as a lien against real property, after which it expires unless renewed, varying by jurisdiction.
In use: "This judgment is 8 years old — well within the state's 20-year enforcement period, so it's still live if it attaches at all."
Misuse: treating every recorded judgment as permanently enforceable, or conversely assuming any older judgment has automatically expired without checking the specific jurisdiction's duration and any renewal filings.

**Name-identity affidavit** — A sworn statement, typically from the current property owner, affirming they are not the same individual as a similarly-named party appearing in a judgment, lien, or other index, used to resolve a common-name match without requiring payoff of an unrelated debt.
In use: "We're resolving this with a name-identity affidavit corroborated by the address and occupation mismatch, rather than requiring payoff of a judgment that isn't his."
Misuse: accepting a bare affidavit with no corroborating documentary evidence as sufficient — underwriters typically expect the affidavit paired with independent corroboration (address, occupation, date of birth mismatch), not a standalone assertion.
