# Roofer Helper — Vocabulary

### Square
The standard roofing unit of area: 100 sq ft of actual (sloped) roof surface, used for material ordering, labor estimating, and debris weight calculations.
**In use:** "It's 20.1 squares after the slope multiplier, order disposal off that, not the ground footprint."
**Common misuse:** Estimating squares off the building's ground-floor footprint instead of the actual sloped roof area — a 6:12 roof's true area runs about 12% larger than its footprint, and skipping the slope multiplier undercounts both material and disposal weight.

### Exposure
The portion of each shingle course left visible after the course above overlaps it — determines how many courses are needed to cover a given roof height.
**In use:** "Standard 5-in exposure on these 3-tabs, don't crowd the courses tighter than that."
**Common misuse:** Treating exposure as a fixed universal number rather than a spec tied to the specific shingle's dimensions — architectural shingles and 3-tabs commonly use different exposure figures.

### Tear-off
Complete removal of existing roofing material (shingles, felt, and sometimes decking) down to a clean substrate, as opposed to a "layover" (roofing over existing material).
**In use:** "This is a full tear-off, not a layover — two layers coming off before dry-in starts."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a "reroof" job is automatically a tear-off — many jurisdictions and some jobs allow a layover up to a code-limited number of layers, and the scope has to specify which.

### Dry-in
The point at which a roof section has underlayment installed and is water-resistant, even if the finish roofing material isn't installed yet.
**In use:** "West slope isn't dried in yet, don't leave it open if rain's forecast tonight."
**Common misuse:** Treating "tear-off complete" and "dried in" as the same milestone — a torn-off deck with no underlayment is fully exposed to weather, not just cosmetically incomplete.

### Presumed asbestos-containing material (presumed ACM)
Material in a pre-1980-era building (felt, mastic, roofing cement, and similar) that regulatory guidance treats as containing asbestos by default until a lab sample proves otherwise.
**In use:** "House was built in '74 — that felt's presumed ACM until the sample comes back, don't keep tearing off around it."
**Common misuse:** Treating "presumed" as "probably fine to keep working around carefully" rather than as the same stop-and-test obligation as a confirmed positive result until the sample clears it.

### Nailing zone / high-nailing
The manufacturer-defined strip on a shingle (usually the double-thickness common-bond area) where nails must be placed to hold the wind-resistance rating; "high-nailing" is placing nails above that zone.
**In use:** "Watch your nail placement on these — high-nailing here voids the wind warranty even if it looks fastened fine."
**Common misuse:** Assuming any nail that's visibly through the shingle and "holding" satisfies the spec — a nail above the nailing zone can hold today and still void the rating and warranty.

### Hip and ridge cap
Specially shaped or cut shingles installed over the hips and ridge lines of a roof to cap the intersection of two roof planes.
**In use:** "Stage the ridge cap bundles separately, they're not interchangeable with field shingles."
**Common misuse:** Substituting cut field shingles for purpose-made hip-and-ridge product to save material — the exposure, wind performance, and appearance aren't equivalent.

### Valley (open, closed-cut, woven)
The internal angle where two roof slopes meet, drained with one of three treatments — open (metal channel visible), closed-cut (shingles from one side cut at the valley line), or woven (shingles interlaced across the valley, uncut).
**In use:** "This one's a closed-cut valley, so trim 2 in back from the valley centerline, not full width."
**Common misuse:** Applying one valley method as a universal default — the roofer specifies which per job, and the wrong method changes both drainage performance and how much material staging differs.

### Drip edge
A metal flashing strip installed along the eaves and rakes to direct water away from the fascia and into the gutter, installed under or over the underlayment depending on the edge.
**In use:** "Drip edge goes under the felt at the eave, over it at the rakes — don't run it the same way on both."
**Common misuse:** Installing drip edge the same way on every edge of the roof, when eave and rake placement relative to the underlayment commonly differ by code and manufacturer instruction.

### Ice-and-water shield
A self-adhering waterproof membrane installed at eaves, valleys, and penetrations in climates with ice-damming risk, in place of standard felt underlayment.
**In use:** "Ice-and-water shield goes to 24 in past the interior wall line here, not just at the eave edge."
**Common misuse:** Treating it as an upgrade to add everywhere for extra protection rather than a climate- and code-driven spec with a defined extent — over-applying it isn't harmful but under-applying it in a required zone is a code and warranty failure.

### Deck sounding
Walking a newly exposed roof deck section to detect soft, springy, or delaminated sheathing by feel and sound before staging material or beginning dry-in.
**In use:** "Sound the whole section before you stage anything on it, don't just eyeball the plywood."
**Common misuse:** Relying on visual inspection of the deck's top surface alone — delamination and rot often show no visible surface sign and are only caught by weight and sound.

### Roof jack
A temporary bracket nailed to the deck on steep-slope work, supporting a plank for footing and material staging until removed and the nail holes patched.
**In use:** "Set jacks every other rafter on this pitch, we're staging bundles on the plank between them."
**Common misuse:** Spacing jacks by convenience (wherever's easiest to nail) instead of by the roofer's plan for the specific pitch and load — incorrect spacing under-supports the plank and the material staged on it.
