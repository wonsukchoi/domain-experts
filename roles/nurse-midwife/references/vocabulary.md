# Vocabulary

### Active-phase arrest
No cervical change for ≥4 hours with adequate contractions (or ≥6 hours with inadequate contractions despite oxytocin) at ≥6cm dilation with ruptured membranes, per ACOG/SMFM 2014.
**In use:** "We can't call this active-phase arrest yet — we're only 90 minutes into confirmed-adequate contractions at 6cm."
**Common misuse:** applied using the old 1cm-per-hour Friedman benchmark, or before 6cm dilation, which manufactures an arrest diagnosis — and often a cesarean referral — for a labor that's actually within normal limits.

### Montevideo units (MVU)
The sum of the pressure each contraction generates above baseline uterine tone, totaled over a 10-minute window, measured via an intrauterine pressure catheter.
**In use:** "MVU's running 215 over the last 10 minutes — that's adequate, so the arrest clock starts now, not at admission."
**Common misuse:** treated as interchangeable with "strong contractions by palpation." Palpation is a reasonable working estimate, but when an arrest diagnosis hinges on the exact adequacy threshold, only an objective MVU number actually clears it.

### Quantitative blood loss (QBL)
Directly measured blood loss — weighed pads and drapes, graduated collection devices — as opposed to estimated blood loss (EBL) by visual assessment.
**In use:** "EBL was called at 400mL, but QBL came back at 780 once everything was weighed — reassess before clearing her."
**Common misuse:** relying on EBL alone, which studies behind the CMQCC toolkit show under-calls true volume by roughly a third to half, worse the higher the actual loss.

### Active management of third stage of labor (AMTSL)
Prophylactic uterotonic administration, controlled cord traction, and uterine massage after birth, bundled together to reduce postpartum hemorrhage risk.
**In use:** "AMTSL's in — oxytocin's on board; we're not skipping controlled cord traction just because she wants delayed clamping."
**Common misuse:** shortened in practice to "just give oxytocin," dropping the cord-traction and massage components that also contribute to the hemorrhage-risk reduction.

### Physiologic birth
Labor and birth proceeding with minimal intervention, under continuous clinical monitoring, absent a specific indication to intervene.
**In use:** "This is a physiologic labor so far — we're watching closely, not doing nothing."
**Common misuse:** read as "hands-off" or "unmonitored." It describes a high threshold for intervention, not a low level of clinical attention.

### Bishop score
A scoring system (cervical dilation, effacement, station, consistency, position) that predicts how likely an induction is to succeed.
**In use:** "Bishop's a 4 — we're starting with a cervical ripening agent, not straight to oxytocin."
**Common misuse:** used as a gate for whether induction should be offered at all, rather than as the tool for choosing which induction method fits the current cervix.

### Shared decision-making / informed choice
A documented, decision-specific discussion of risks, benefits, and alternatives that results in a choice the patient actively makes, for that particular decision.
**In use:** "We've done informed choice on GBS prophylaxis specifically — she understands the numbers and is declining. That's not the same as her never being asked."
**Common misuse:** treated as satisfied by a single blanket consent form at intake, instead of a fresh conversation each time a new decision point (induction, monitoring, delayed clamping) actually arises.

### Full practice authority
The state practice-authority category under which a CNM can evaluate, diagnose, order tests, and prescribe — including controlled substances — without a mandatory collaborative agreement with a physician.
**In use:** "We're in a full-authority state, so this doesn't legally need an OB co-sign — it needs an OB consult because of the clinical picture, not the paperwork."
**Common misuse:** conflated with "consultation is never needed." Full practice authority removes the legal requirement for a supervisory agreement; it doesn't remove the clinical judgment to consult when risk factors actually warrant it.

### HELPERR
The ordered maneuver sequence for shoulder dystocia: Help, Evaluate for Episiotomy, Legs (McRoberts), suprapubic Pressure, Enter maneuvers (internal rotation), Remove the posterior arm, Roll to hands-and-knees.
**In use:** "Called it at delivery — going through HELPERR in order, McRoberts before we move to rotation maneuvers."
**Common misuse:** maneuvers attempted out of sequence, or repeated well past the point of futility, instead of escalating — both extend the head-to-body delivery interval that matters for outcome.

### Category I / II / III fetal heart tracing
The NICHD three-tier system classifying electronic fetal monitoring by risk of fetal acidemia.
**In use:** "This is Category II — not reassuring like Category I, but not the immediate-delivery indication of Category III either; let's try intrauterine resuscitation measures first."
**Common misuse:** Category II treated as one uniform risk level. It spans a wide range from near-normal to genuinely concerning, and the specific features present — not just the label — drive the response.

### Risk-based vs. culture-based GBS prophylaxis
The two CDC-endorsed pathways for deciding intrapartum antibiotic prophylaxis, used depending on whether a valid GBS culture result is available.
**In use:** "No valid culture — going risk-based: she's preterm and ROM is over 18 hours, and either one alone triggers prophylaxis."
**Common misuse:** assuming prophylaxis only applies with a positive culture result, missing the risk-based triggers that apply specifically when culture status is unknown or unavailable.

### Nuchal cord
The umbilical cord wrapped around the fetal neck, present in roughly a quarter of births.
**In use:** "Nuchal cord, loose, reduced over the head without issue — not the cause of the variable decelerations we saw earlier."
**Common misuse:** automatically blamed for any intrapartum fetal heart rate abnormality. Most nuchal cords are loose and incidental, not the driver of a Category II or III tracing.

### Obstetric anal sphincter injury (OASIS)
A 3rd- or 4th-degree perineal laceration involving the anal sphincter complex, sometimes the rectal mucosa.
**In use:** "Rectal exam shows a palpable sphincter defect — this is OASIS, not a routine 2nd-degree repair."
**Common misuse:** the sphincter-specific exam skipped after a difficult delivery because the visible perineal tear looks routine, missing a defect that only a deliberate rectal exam would catch.
