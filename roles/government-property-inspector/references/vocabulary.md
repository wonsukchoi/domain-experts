### Government-furnished property (GFP)
Property owned by the government and provided to a contractor for use in performing a contract, remaining government-owned throughout.
**In use:** "This tooling is GFP — the contractor has custody, but the government retains ownership."
**Common misuse:** Treating GFP as the contractor's asset because it's in the contractor's physical possession and daily use.

### Contractor-acquired property (CAP)
Property purchased or otherwise acquired by a contractor using contract funds, which becomes government property upon acquisition even though the contractor bought it.
**In use:** "That equipment was bought with contract funds, so it's CAP — still government property, not the contractor's own asset."
**Common misuse:** Assuming property becomes the contractor's own simply because the contractor purchased it, rather than checking the funding source and contract terms.

### FAR 52.245-1 (Government Property clause)
The standard Federal Acquisition Regulation clause establishing a contractor's responsibilities for accounting, maintaining, and disposing of government property in its possession.
**In use:** "The property management system requirements here all trace back to FAR 52.245-1's specific outcome areas."
**Common misuse:** Treating property management as a general best-practice area rather than a specific, clause-driven set of enforceable requirements.

### Property management system
The contractor's documented process and records for tracking, maintaining, and controlling government property, evaluated against defined outcome areas (records, physical inventory, utilization, maintenance, subcontractor control, disposition, and others).
**In use:** "Their property management system passed on records accuracy but failed on subcontractor control."
**Common misuse:** Evaluating the system as a single overall pass/fail rather than checking each required outcome area independently.

### Physical inventory
The actual, in-person count and verification of government property, performed on a defined cycle and reconciled against the contractor's property records.
**In use:** "Physical inventory sample found a 6% discrepancy rate against the records."
**Common misuse:** Treating a sample inventory's findings as limited to just the sampled items rather than extrapolating the discrepancy rate to estimate exposure across the full population.

### Loss, damage, or destruction (LDD) report
The formal report a contractor must file when government property is lost, damaged, or destroyed, typically within a specific timeframe from discovery.
**In use:** "The LDD report for these two units is six months late relative to when they should have been discovered missing."
**Common misuse:** Treating a late LDD report as cured by eventually filing it, rather than recognizing the lateness itself as a separate violation from the underlying loss.

### Disposition (property disposition)
The formal process for removing government property from active use — including screening for reutilization elsewhere in government inventory before scrap, sale, or transfer.
**In use:** "Before that idle equipment can be scrapped, it has to go through the disposition screening process first."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a contractor can independently decide to scrap or repurpose no-longer-needed property without going through required screening and authorization.

### Reutilization screening
The process of checking whether other government programs or agencies need property before it's approved for disposal, required as part of the disposition process.
**In use:** "Screening turned up no other program that needs this equipment, so disposal can proceed."
**Common misuse:** Skipping the screening step because the contractor's own program has no further use for the item, without checking broader government need.

### Subcontractor control
The prime contractor's responsibility to track and account for government property in the possession of its subcontractors, as part of its overall property management system.
**In use:** "This subcontractor's holdings weren't in the prime's property record — that's a subcontractor control gap."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a prime contractor's property accountability ends at its own facility, when it extends to property held by subcontractors under flow-down terms.

### Property administrator
The government official (often from an agency like DCMA) responsible for overseeing contractor property management system compliance and approving or reviewing disposition requests.
**In use:** "Disposition of this item needs property administrator sign-off before it proceeds."
**Common misuse:** Treating property administrator involvement as optional or informal rather than a required approval step in specific processes like disposition.
