# Red flags

Smell tests to catch before they become an incident report.

### 1. Load overhangs the fork face by more than ~6 inches with no load-center recalculation logged

**Usually means:** the operator (or the person who staged the pallet) is reading the truck's rated capacity as a flat weight limit and never checked the actual load center against the plate's reference distance — the most common precursor to an over-moment lift.

**First question:** "What's this load's actual center of gravity from the fork face, and what does the truck's plate rate at that distance?"

**Data to pull:** the load's weight and dimensions, the truck's data plate (rated capacity, reference load center, axle-to-fork distance), and the resulting de-rated capacity calculation.

### 2. Truck traveling with forks raised more than a few inches off the floor

**Usually means:** the operator raised the load for visibility or to save a step at the destination instead of lowering it for transit — this is the single most common way stability margin gets traded for convenience, and it's routinely visible from across the floor.

**First question:** "Why is this load traveling at height instead of at travel height?"

**Data to pull:** standard operating height for that truck class/site, frequency of raised-travel observations in recent safety walks, whether a specific obstruction (uneven floor, low load) justified it this one time.

### 3. Attachment installed (clamp, side-shifter, boom, extension) with no attachment-specific capacity plate referenced

**Usually means:** the operator is running the base truck's nameplate capacity, which no longer applies once an attachment adds weight and typically shifts the load center further from the mast.

**First question:** "What does the attachment's own capacity plate say at this load's center, not the truck's base plate?"

**Data to pull:** attachment manufacturer's derating chart, attachment weight, resulting combined load center.

### 4. Rack level with no visible or legible load-capacity placard

**Usually means:** the rack was installed or reconfigured without the RMI/ANSI MH16.1 placard being posted or replaced, or an existing placard was damaged/painted over — the level's true capacity is now unknown to anyone staging it.

**First question:** "Has this level's actual rated capacity been confirmed with facilities/rack engineering since it was last reconfigured?"

**Data to pull:** rack installation or reconfiguration records, manufacturer capacity documentation for that rack model, current staged weight on the level and bay.

### 5. Staged weight on a beam level within 10% of its posted capacity and still receiving new pallets

**Usually means:** individual putaways are being cleared against the incoming pallet's weight alone, without anyone summing what's already on that span — the level fills past its margin one "it's just one more pallet" decision at a time.

**First question:** "What's the running total on this span right now, including what's already there?"

**Data to pull:** WMS or inventory system's current weight-on-location total, placard capacity, weight of the pallet about to be placed.

### 6. Operator running a route through a blind corner or cross-aisle without sounding the horn

**Usually means:** familiarity with a repeated route has eroded the habit — the operator has run this corner hundreds of times without a pedestrian appearing and has stopped treating it as a blind spot.

**First question:** "How many pedestrian near-misses or struck-by incidents has this specific corner had in the last year?"

**Data to pull:** incident/near-miss log for that location, mirror/camera coverage at the corner, observed horn-use rate during a walk-through.

### 7. Pedestrian walkway markings faded or obscured in a zone with regular forklift traffic

**Usually means:** floor marking maintenance hasn't kept pace with actual traffic patterns, and pedestrians and operators are negotiating right-of-way informally rather than by a visible, agreed rule — this is where struck-by incidents cluster.

**First question:** "When were these markings last repainted, and does the marked path match where people actually walk?"

**Data to pull:** facilities maintenance schedule for floor markings, a traffic observation count of pedestrian paths versus marked lanes, recent near-miss reports from that zone.

### 8. Operator resuming full duty immediately after a tip, near-tip, or contact incident on the strength of a currently-valid 3-year certification

**Usually means:** the standing certification is being treated as blanket clearance, when OSHA's trigger-event rule requires a fresh evaluation after any such incident regardless of days remaining before the calendar renewal.

**First question:** "Has this operator had a post-incident evaluation, separate from their standing certification date?"

**Data to pull:** incident report, evaluation record (or absence of one) since the incident, date of last general certification versus date of the incident.

### 9. Truck operating on a rated grade with the load oriented downgrade

**Usually means:** the operator prioritized a faster route or turn-around over the manufacturer's loaded-orientation procedure, which specifically exists to keep the load from sliding or the truck from tipping nose-down on a slope.

**First question:** "What's this truck's posted maximum grade rating, and is the load oriented per the manufacturer's procedure for this direction of travel?"

**Data to pull:** truck's grade-rating spec, ramp's actual grade, direction of travel, load orientation observed.
