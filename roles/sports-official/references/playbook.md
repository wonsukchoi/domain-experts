# Playbook

Filled operational tools a crew actually runs. Adapt the numbers to the sport and level; the structure and thresholds are the point.

## 1. Pregame conference checklist (3-person basketball crew, filled example)

Run 5-10 minutes before the teams take the floor, every game, no exceptions for "we've worked together before."

```
CREW: [Lead], [Trail], [Center] — assignor: [name]
VENUE NOTES: shot clock horn shared with buzzer — confirm distinct sound test
RULE SET: NCAA Men's, restricted-area review active (final 2:00 only)
COVERAGE:
  - Lead: baseline-to-baseline strong side, primary on post play
  - Trail: top of key through three-point line, primary on ball-handler drives
  - Center: opposite baseline, primary on weak-side rebounding position
SPECIAL SITUATIONS:
  - Last 2:00, restricted-area calls reviewable — crew chief goes to monitor,
    standard = clear and obvious, target under 2:00 elapsed
  - Administrative technical for bench decorum: warning at first outburst,
    technical on repeat from same person
  - Injury timeout protocol: closest official waves in trainer, other two hold
    position and manage players
COMMUNICATION: verbal "I have him" / "help" only — no hand signals for
  coverage handoffs, reserve those for administrative calls
```

**The two items a rushed pregame skips, and shouldn't:** the horn/buzzer sound test (indistinguishable sounds cause blown clock calls that are entirely preventable) and the specific technical-foul threshold for bench conduct (a vague "we'll know it when we see it" produces inconsistent thresholds across the three officials).

## 2. Dissent escalation ladder (with scripted thresholds)

| Rung | Trigger | Response | Script |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1. Warning | First vocal complaint about a judgment call, no personal target | Verbal warning, keep moving | "That's enough, coach — let's play." |
| 2. Card/technical | Complaint continues past the warning, OR first instance of personal/insulting language, OR visible show-up (arms up, walking toward official) | Card (soccer) or technical foul (basketball), stated to both benches | "Technical, blue bench — [official] to scorer's table." |
| 3. Second card/technical | Any dissent after rung 2, regardless of severity | Second technical/card — ejection follows automatically in most rule sets | State the rule citation triggering automatic ejection |
| 4. Direct ejection (skip rungs) | Physical contact, threat, or conduct targeting an official personally rather than a call | Immediate ejection, no warning owed | Short, factual statement for the report — not an argument in the moment |

**Rule:** never skip from rung 1 to rung 3 to "send a message" — it looks arbitrary next to the game's own record of what was and wasn't tolerated earlier, and it is the single fastest way to convert one argument into a bench-wide standoff. Never let rung 1 repeat indefinitely either — the second identical complaint from the same person is rung 2, not a second warning.

## 3. Review-protocol time budgets (by sport, target vs. what blows the budget)

| Sport / review type | Stated target | What legitimately extends it | What's a mechanics failure if it happens |
|---|---|---|---|
| NBA replay center review | ~1:30 | Multiple angles needed for a spot-check (out-of-bounds, basket interference) | Crew re-debating the original judgment instead of applying the review standard |
| NCAA basketball monitor review | under 2:00 | Measuring exact foot/ball position frame-by-frame | Going to monitor without first declaring what's under review |
| NFL replay assist / booth review | ~60-90 seconds for booth-initiated; longer for coach's challenge | Determining exact ball spot for a line-to-gain measurement | Referee narrating uncertainty on-mic instead of a clear ruling |
| Soccer VAR check | silent check adds no time in the vast majority of cases; on-field review ~60-90 seconds | Off-side line calibration requiring frame-accurate marking | VAR intervening on a subjective/borderline call rather than a "clear and obvious error" |
| Baseball replay (crew chief challenge) | ~1:30-2:00 average | Neighborhood-play/transfer-rule angles requiring multiple camera cuts | Crew chief accepting a call reversal on "closer look, might be different" rather than clear and convincing evidence |

**Standard across all of them:** the review answers one question — was there clear and obvious/conclusive evidence the on-field ruling was wrong — not "what would I call now, cold, from a better angle." A review that reopens the whole judgment call rather than checking the original evidence is a review that will run long and still get argued afterward.

## 4. Postgame incident report (filled template — ejection)

```
GAME: [Team A] vs [Team B], [date], [venue]
CREW: [Lead/Referee — name], [Trail/Umpire 2 — name], [Center/Umpire 3 — name]
INCIDENT TYPE: Ejection
GAME TIME: 3rd quarter, 6:42 remaining, score 41-38 (Team A)

SEQUENCE:
  6:58 — Team B player fouled on drive, no basket, foul called (personal, 3rd
    on player). Player visibly frustrated, no verbal dissent.
  6:51 — Team B head coach shouts from bench toward Trail official,
    "that's a joke, you're terrible" — directed personally, not at the call.
    RULING: Technical foul, Rung 2, per escalation protocol. Stated to
    scorer's table and both benches.
  6:42 — Same coach, immediately following the technical shot, stands and
    continues shouting toward Trail official, gesturing.
    RULING: Second technical foul — automatic ejection per Rule 10-2.
    Coach directed to locker room by arena security escort.

RULE CITATIONS: Technical foul — unsporting conduct (Rule 10-1); ejection —
  second technical foul, same individual, same game (Rule 10-2, automatic).

FREE THROWS / ADMINISTRATIVE RESULT: 2 technical free throws awarded to
  Team A (both attempts, 1 made) plus possession. Score after sequence: 42-38.

CREW ASSESSMENT: Escalation followed protocol — warning was not owed at
  rung 2 given the personal target of the first outburst; second outburst
  within 9 seconds of the first technical met automatic-ejection criteria
  without further warning.

FILED BY: [Crew chief name] · SUBMITTED: same day, to [conference/league
  officiating coordinator]
```

**Why same-day filing matters:** a report written from memory three days later loses the exact sequencing (which comment triggered which response) that is the only thing that makes an ejection defensible on appeal — "he was ejected for arguing" is not defensible; "second technical within 9 seconds of the first, per Rule 10-2" is.
