# Red flags

Smell tests an assigner, crew chief, or veteran partner catches early. Format: what it usually means, the first question to ask, and the data to pull before drawing a conclusion.

### A favorable call for one team follows within a possession/at-bat of a blown call against them

**Usually means:** a make-up call — the official is unconsciously (or consciously) rebalancing, which converts a single random error into a visible pattern. It is rarely deliberate bias; it is almost always overcorrection in the moment.

**First question:** "Was there an actual infraction on the second call, independent of the first one?"

**Data to pull:** the video of both plays side by side; the time gap between them; whether the same official made both calls or a partner made the second one (partner-made "correction" calls are a different, more concerning pattern than self-correction).

### One team's foul/card count is 30%+ higher than the other with no pace or possession-count explanation

**Usually means:** either a genuine and defensible discrepancy (one team is fouling more, physically), or a standard that has drifted — tightening against one team's style of play (physical post play, aggressive pressing) while the other team's equally physical actions go unflagged.

**First question:** "Normalize for possessions/plays — is the rate actually different, or is raw count just tracking who had the ball more?"

**Data to pull:** foul/card counts per possession or per defensive snap, not raw totals; a play-by-play sample check of 5-10 uncalled plays from the "clean" team to see if the same contact went unflagged.

### An official's technical-foul or card rate this game is well above their season average

**Usually means:** the official's own composure or game management broke down, not that players suddenly got worse-behaved — this is a self-assessment flag as much as a player-conduct one.

**First question:** "Walk me through the sequence before the first technical/card — what was said, and did the response match the escalation ladder or skip a rung?"

**Data to pull:** timestamped sequence of warnings vs. cards issued that game vs. the official's season rate; whether rungs were followed in order.

### A replay/monitor review runs well past its stated time budget

**Usually means:** the crew is re-litigating the original judgment call frame-by-frame instead of checking for a specific, clear, objective error — reviews that reopen "what would I call now" rather than "was there conclusive evidence of an error" run long and often still get appealed afterward.

**First question:** "What specific evidence were we looking for, and did we find it inside two looks, or are we searching for a reason to overturn?"

**Data to pull:** review start/end timestamps; the specific question the crew chief declared before starting the review; how many camera angles were actually needed to answer that question versus how many were watched.

### The same player or coach draws a favorable no-call from the same crew across multiple games

**Usually means:** most often coincidence in a small sample, occasionally a genuine crew-consistency problem (a "reputation call" — a star player getting the benefit of a marginal read because of who they are, not what happened on the play).

**First question:** "Pull the last three games this crew worked with this player — is the no-call rate on similar contact actually different, or does it look different because we remember the close ones?"

**Data to pull:** cross-game log of similar-play calls involving that player/coach across the season, not anecdote from a single broadcast or highlight reel.

### An assigner's grades for one official trend downward for 3+ consecutive assignments with no rule changes in the interim

**Usually means:** fatigue, complacency, or a mechanics habit (drifting out of coverage zone, delayed whistle) that hasn't been named yet — a single bad grade is normal variance; a trend is a signal.

**First question:** "Is the drop concentrated in one call type (judgment calls, e.g.) or spread across everything, including objective calls like boundary lines?"

**Data to pull:** grade-by-category breakdown across the last 3-5 games, not just the composite score; video clips flagged in each report to look for a repeated mechanics issue.

### Stoppage/added time at the end of a half is systematically longer when the home team is behind and shorter when it's ahead, across a season

**Usually means:** referee bias correlated with crowd pressure rather than a deliberate decision — documented in officiating research as a real, measurable effect at the aggregate level even when no single game looks suspicious.

**First question:** "What's this official's average added-time differential (home-behind vs. home-ahead) across the season, not just this match?"

**Data to pull:** added-time logs across the season split by score situation and home/away; stoppage events (injuries, substitutions, celebrations) that should explain the variance on legitimate grounds.

### A crew chief overrules a partner's call without going to the monitor, when a review was available

**Usually means:** a protocol violation — crew chief authority covers running the review process and applying its standard, not substituting personal judgment for a partner's live look without evidence.

**First question:** "Was there a review available for this call type, and if so, why wasn't it used before the ruling changed?"

**Data to pull:** the league's reviewable-play list for that call type; whether the correction was based on video evidence or the crew chief's own unaided view from a different angle.

### A newly certified official's no-call rate on contact drops sharply right after being told to "let them play"

**Usually means:** overcorrection — replacing a nuanced standard (advantage/disadvantage, degree of contact) with a blanket policy of silence, which is its own game-management failure even though it looks like the opposite problem.

**First question:** "Show me three plays this game where contact occurred and nothing was called — did the contact affect the play, or was it truly incidental?"

**Data to pull:** video of the no-calls in question against the sport's actual standard (advantage/disadvantage, restricted-area rule, etc.), not against the vague instruction that prompted the change.
