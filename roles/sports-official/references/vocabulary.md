# Vocabulary

Terms generalists flatten into "the ref made a call" that practitioners keep sharply separate.

### Judgment call vs. rule-application call

A **judgment call** requires perceiving something inherently graded — contact, intent, degree, timing (block/charge, holding, traveling). A **rule-application call** applies a fixed fact to a fixed rule with no perceptual judgment involved (boundary line, shot-clock violation, possession arrow).

**In use:** "Don't argue the block/charge with me for ten minutes — that's a judgment call and I made it. If you want to argue the shot clock, pull the video, because that one's just a fact."

**Common misuse:** treating every close call as equally arguable. A rule-application call is either right or wrong and can be corrected on evidence; a judgment call is a professional read that a different, equally competent official could have called the other way — arguing it as if it were provably wrong misunderstands what kind of call it is.

### Advantage (advantage/disadvantage principle)

The referee's authority (IFAB Law 5 in soccer, analogous provisions elsewhere) to let play continue after a foul if stopping would penalize the team fouled *against*, because they retain or gain a good opportunity by playing on.

**In use:** "I saw the foul, but she's through on goal — advantage, play on. If that breaks down in the next couple seconds, we come back for the original foul."

**Common misuse:** assuming "playing advantage" means the foul was ignored. The foul is still recorded for disciplinary purposes (a card can still be issued when play next stops); advantage defers the stoppage, not the disciplinary consequence.

### Primary vs. secondary coverage area

**Primary coverage** is the zone/matchup an official is mechanically assigned to watch first in a given crew formation; **secondary coverage** is what they help with only after primary responsibilities are covered. Assigned by the crew's mechanics manual, not improvised per play.

**In use:** "I had primary on the post feed — I can't also be the guy calling the three-point line on the weak side. That's why we have a trail official."

**Common misuse:** "ball-watching" — following the ball outside one's assigned zone, which is exactly the failure mode coverage areas exist to prevent, since it leaves the actual primary responsibility uncovered.

### Selling the call

The visible, physical decisiveness of a signal or verbal ruling, scaled to the moment — full extension, clear pause, unhurried delivery on a big or controversial call.

**In use:** "That's the game-winner — sell it. Big mechanic, hold it, let everyone see it before you turn and walk away."

**Common misuse:** confusing "selling" with theatrics or arrogance. It is about removing ambiguity from the delivery of a correct-but-contestable call, not performing for the crowd — an official who "sells" a call they privately know was a guess is doing something different and worse.

### Restricted area / no-charge semicircle

A marked arc under the basket (radius varies by rule set — commonly 3 feet in NCAA/high school, 4 feet in the NBA) inside which a defender cannot draw an offensive charge regardless of otherwise-legal positioning, to prevent rim-protection contact from being adjudicated as a foot-position dispute at full speed.

**In use:** "Doesn't matter that he was set — his foot was inside the arc, so it's a block by rule, not a judgment call."

**Common misuse:** treating the arc as a "usually" rule that yields to a well-set defender. It is a bright-line rule specifically because foot position is easy to verify and intent/setup is not — the arc overrides an otherwise-legal-looking stance.

### Make-up call

A ruling, conscious or not, that favors the team disadvantaged by a prior blown call — intended to "even things out."

**In use:** "Don't even think about it on the next possession — no make-up calls. If it's a foul, it's a foul; if it's not, it's not, regardless of what happened at the other end."

**Common misuse:** assuming make-up calls fix credibility problems. They convert a single, forgettable random error into a visible, remembered pattern, which is strictly worse for the crew's credibility than the original mistake.

### Mechanics

The codified positioning, signal vocabulary, and coverage-responsibility system a crew is trained to run — distinct from "the rules," which govern what's legal in the game itself.

**In use:** "That wasn't a rules problem, it was a mechanics problem — he was out of his primary and missed the actual foul because he was watching the ball."

**Common misuse:** using "mechanics" and "rules" interchangeably. An official can know the rulebook perfectly and still blow calls through bad mechanics (wrong position, wrong angle, ball-watching) — and conversely can have flawless mechanics undermined by a rules-knowledge gap.

### Bang-bang play

A play where two events (a tag and a base touch, a shot and a buzzer, a fumble and a recovery) occur close enough in time that no angle can definitively separate them without slow-motion review.

**In use:** "That's a bang-bang play at first — full speed, that's an out ninety-five times out of a hundred, and replay confirmed it."

**Common misuse:** treating every close call as bang-bang. Many close-looking plays have a clean, unambiguous angle from the right position; "bang-bang" specifically describes plays where even a perfect angle can't resolve the timing without frame-by-frame review.

### Assigner

The person or body that assigns officials to specific games/levels and evaluates their performance across a season — the officiating profession's analogue to a manager, distinct from the league or the teams being officiated.

**In use:** "My assigner bumped me up to varsity after the grades came back above 90% for three straight months."

**Common misuse:** assuming the assigner and the league office are the same entity, or that a single bad grade from an assigner ends an assignment path — it's the trend across games, not one outlier, that changes assignments.

### Clear and obvious error (review standard)

The evidentiary bar a replay/monitor/VAR review applies before overturning an on-field ruling: the video must show a definite, unambiguous mistake, not merely a plausible alternative read.

**In use:** "The angle we have is inconclusive — that's not clear and obvious, so the call on the field stands."

**Common misuse:** treating review as a chance to re-referee the play from scratch with a better angle. The standard exists precisely so that a marginal, arguable read isn't overturned just because a slower, closer look invites a different guess — it protects the original judgment call unless the evidence is decisive.

### Protest vs. appeal

A **protest** challenges whether a rule was correctly applied or whether an ineligible situation occurred (wrong rule cited, ineligible player), filed through the governing body, often before the next game. An **appeal** (in the officiating-evaluation sense) challenges a grade or assignment decision through the assigner's own process. They use different rulebooks and go to different people.

**In use:** "You can't protest a judgment call — protests are for rule misapplication, not for disagreeing with a block/charge read."

**Common misuse:** filing or threatening a "protest" over a disputed judgment call. Nearly every rulebook explicitly excludes judgment calls from the protest process; only rule-application errors and eligibility issues are protestable.

### L2M (Last Two Minute Report)

The NBA's public postgame accuracy review of officiating calls (and notable non-calls) in the final two minutes of games decided by five points or fewer — an unusually transparent, published postmortem mechanism, uncommon across sports.

**In use:** "The L2M graded that a correct no-call, even though it looked bad live — worth pulling up before assuming the crew missed it."

**Common misuse:** assuming an L2M "incorrect call" grade implies bias or incompetence rather than an error rate the report itself frames as expected at live speed; the report's value is the trend across a season and the transparency, not any single grade as a verdict on an official's competence.
