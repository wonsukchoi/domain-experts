# Vocabulary

### Tally (pipe tally)
The running, individually-measured record of every joint and stand of drill pipe run into or pulled out of the hole, summed to give the true string length.
**In use:** "Tally says 8,215 — read that against the counter before we call it TD."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a paperwork copy of the driller's electronic depth counter, when it's the independent check the counter is validated against, not the other way around.

### Stand
Two or three joints of drill pipe pre-connected and racked as a single unit (a "triple" for three joints) to speed tripping.
**In use:** "87 racked, expecting 88 — hold before the last connection breaks."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to be a fixed, uniform length across a string; actual stand lengths vary joint to joint and are individually tallied, not treated as identical units.

### Red zone / line of fire
The zone around a suspended load, a line under tension, or moving rig-floor equipment where a dropped or released load could reach a person.
**In use:** "Load's swinging — stay out of the red zone till the driller calls you in."
**Common misuse:** Judged by load weight ("it's not that heavy") instead of by position — a light load moving unpredictably is still a line-of-fire hazard.

### Fish / fishing job
Equipment or pipe left, lost, or stuck in the wellbore that must be retrieved before drilling can continue.
**In use:** "If the tally's really short, we've got a fish and a fishing string to run."
**Common misuse:** Assumed from a tally mismatch before the count is actually rechecked — most short-tally alarms are miscounts, not fish, and chasing the wrong one wastes rig time.

### Scaled distance
The blasting-industry method for setting a minimum safe standoff distance, calculated as K × √(maximum charge weight per delay), not a flat number of feet.
**In use:** "180 lb per delay today — that's 738 feet at our K-factor, not yesterday's line."
**Common misuse:** Reused from the previous shot without recalculating for the current shot's actual charge weight per delay.

### Shot firer
The certified individual who owns the blast design, loading sequence, and clearance call at a quarry or mine blast site.
**In use:** "Nobody re-enters until the shot firer calls it cleared."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to be interchangeable with "whoever's in charge on site" — the clearance call is a specific certified authority, not a general supervisory one.

### IDLH (Immediately Dangerous to Life or Health)
The OSHA-defined exposure concentration above which a person could not escape without irreversible harm or death; for H2S this is 100 ppm.
**In use:** "That reading's IDLH territory — full evacuation, no unprotected re-entry."
**Common misuse:** Treated as the threshold for the first response, when the OSHA action level (10 ppm) and ceiling (20 ppm) trigger evacuation and PPE response long before IDLH.

### MSHA Part 46 / Part 48
The two federal training regimes for mine safety: Part 46 covers surface and small underground mines, Part 48 covers underground and surface metal/nonmetal mines — each sets minimum new-miner and task-specific training hours.
**In use:** "He's got his 24 hours logged under Part 46 for this task, he's clear to work it unsupervised."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a one-time orientation instead of an hours requirement tracked per task category, with separate hour floors for new tasks.

### BHA (bottom hole assembly)
The lower portion of the drill string — bit, mud motor, stabilizers, drill collars — whose measured length is subtracted from total depth to get drill pipe length in the hole.
**In use:** "BHA's 95 feet, so pipe in the hole is TD minus that."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to be a fixed standard length across wells; it's assembled and measured per well, and using a remembered figure instead of the current BHA report's number is how a tally gets built on a wrong base.

### RigPass / WellCAP
Cross-operator baseline safety orientation and well-control training programs (IADC) required before working unsupervised on many land and offshore rigs.
**In use:** "RigPass card's current — he's cleared for the floor, not for well-control response yet."
**Common misuse:** Conflated with full task certification; RigPass is a baseline orientation, not a substitute for task-specific hours or well-control (WellCAP) qualification.

### Trip (tripping pipe)
The process of pulling the drill string out of the hole (POOH) or running it back in (RIH), typically to change a bit or BHA component.
**In use:** "We're on a bit trip — POOH to surface, then RIH on the new bit."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a routine mechanical process where pace is the only variable; trip speed itself is a hazard control (too fast can swab the well and cause a kick), not just an efficiency metric.

### Kick
An unplanned influx of formation fluid or gas into the wellbore, often triggered by tripping too fast (swabbing) or inadequate mud weight.
**In use:** "Slow the trip down through that zone — we don't want to swab a kick."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to be purely a driller's mud-weight problem; trip speed, which the floor crew directly controls, is an equally live contributing factor.
