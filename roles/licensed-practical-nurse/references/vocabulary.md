# Vocabulary

### Task list (authorized functions)
The specific, enumerated set of procedures and decisions a state's Nurse Practice Act permits an LPN/LVN to perform, separate from what the employing facility's policy additionally allows or restricts.
**In use:** "IV push meds are on our state's task list, but this facility's policy still keeps that RN-only — policy is the tighter one here, so it governs."
**Common misuse:** Treating the state task list alone as sufficient authorization, without checking whether the facility's own policy is more restrictive.

### Supervised practice
The legal requirement that an LPN/LVN practice under the direction of an RN, physician, or other authorized provider, rather than independently — a structural feature of the license, not a statement about competence.
**In use:** "I'm confident about what's going on with him, but I still need the RN or MD to sign off — that's supervised practice, not a trust issue."
**Common misuse:** Treating supervision as optional once the LPN/LVN feels sure of the clinical picture, rather than as a fixed legal boundary independent of confidence.

### Focused reassessment
Data collection targeted at a specific, already-identified concern (a vital-sign recheck, a wound check, a symptom follow-up) as opposed to a comprehensive/admission assessment, which most states reserve to the RN.
**In use:** "I did a focused reassessment on his breathing and confusion — the full comprehensive assessment is the RN's when she gets here."
**Common misuse:** Using "assessment" loosely to describe both, which blurs the scope line the term exists to mark.

### Stop & Watch
An INTERACT early-warning tool (spelling the mnemonic S-T-O-P-A-N-D-W-A-T-C-H) used in long-term care to flag a resident's subtle change in condition before it becomes an emergent one, triggering same-shift notification on any single item.
**In use:** "He tripped two Stop & Watch items tonight — confusion and warmth — that's enough to call, I don't need a third."
**Common misuse:** Waiting for multiple triggered items to accumulate before notifying, when the tool is designed to act on any single one.

### Change in condition (CIC)
A facility's formally defined trigger — a specific, documented shift in a resident's status from their baseline — that starts a required assessment-and-notification sequence, distinct from routine variation.
**In use:** "That's a change in condition, not just a bad night — start the notification sequence."
**Common misuse:** Applying the term to any deviation from a fixed number rather than a deviation from *this resident's own* documented baseline.

### INTERACT (Interventions to Reduce Acute Care Transfers)
A long-term-care quality-improvement program providing standardized tools (Stop & Watch, SBAR-NH, Care Paths) to identify, evaluate, and manage a resident's change in condition, aimed at reducing avoidable hospital transfers.
**In use:** "Pull the INTERACT Care Path for fever — it'll walk the workup before we decide on transfer."
**Common misuse:** Treating INTERACT tools as transfer-prevention checklists to justify keeping someone in place, rather than as structured decision aids that can just as easily support transferring.

### Avoidable hospitalization
An unplanned hospital transfer that, in retrospect, could plausibly have been managed in place with timely recognition and intervention — a tracked outcome under CMS's SNF Value-Based Purchasing program, not a general term for "a transfer someone disagreed with."
**In use:** "We're tracked on avoidable hospitalizations now — that's part of why we call the on-call doc before we call the ambulance."
**Common misuse:** Using the facility's readmission metric as a reason to delay a transfer that's actually clinically indicated — the metric is a documentation-and-judgment prompt, not a veto on sending someone out.

### F-tag
A specific numbered deficiency citation in CMS's State Operations Manual, Appendix PP, used by state surveyors to cite a skilled nursing facility for a regulatory violation (e.g., F-580, notification of changes).
**In use:** "That's an F-580 issue — the family wasn't notified within the required window."
**Common misuse:** Using "F-tag" generically for any survey criticism, rather than the specific numbered regulation actually cited.

### Five rights of delegation
The NCSBN framework — right task, right circumstance, right person, right direction/communication, right supervision/evaluation — governing what may be handed off to unlicensed or lower-scope staff.
**In use:** "Right circumstance fails here — he's flagged on Stop & Watch, this isn't a delegate-the-vitals resident tonight."
**Common misuse:** Applying the five rights only downward (to CNAs) and forgetting they apply upward too, when accepting a task delegated from an RN.

### Read-back
Repeating a verbal or telephone order back to the prescriber, verbatim, before ending the call, as the verification step required before the order is considered valid to act on.
**In use:** "Give me a second — let me read that back before I hang up."
**Common misuse:** Treating a familiar on-call physician's order as self-executing without a documented read-back, because the interaction feels routine.

### POLST (Physician/Provider Orders for Life-Sustaining Treatment)
A portable, actionable medical order set — distinct from an advance directive — reflecting a patient's current wishes for resuscitation, hospitalization, and other interventions, signed by a provider.
**In use:** "Check the POLST before you call 911 — it may already say do-not-hospitalize."
**Common misuse:** Confusing a POLST with a general advance directive; a POLST is an active medical order a clinician can act on directly, not a statement of prior wishes requiring interpretation.

### Braden Scale
A validated scoring tool (typically 6–23) predicting pressure-injury risk from sensory perception, moisture, activity, mobility, nutrition, and friction/shear; lower scores mean higher risk.
**In use:** "He's a Braden of 11 — that's high risk, we need the turning schedule tightened, not just noted."
**Common misuse:** Documenting a Braden score without adjusting the actual care plan (turning frequency, surface, nutrition consult) to match the risk level it indicates.

### Unlicensed assistive personnel (UAP)
Facility staff, most commonly CNAs, who perform delegated tasks under a licensed nurse's direction and retained accountability, without independent scope of practice.
**In use:** "The UAP can take the vitals, but I'm the one who decides what an abnormal reading means."
**Common misuse:** Treating a UAP's report of a delegated finding as closing the loop, rather than as data the delegating nurse still has to interpret and act on.
