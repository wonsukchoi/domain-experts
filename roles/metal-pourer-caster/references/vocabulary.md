# Vocabulary

### Inoculation (melt treatment)
The addition of an inoculant material to molten iron (commonly ductile or gray iron) to promote favorable graphite structure formation during solidification, with a limited effective duration after treatment.
**In use:** "Inoculation fade is eight to ten minutes on this alloy — we're past that now, so we're checking nodularity, not just assuming it's fine."
**Common misuse:** Assuming inoculation benefit persists indefinitely as long as the melt stays at proper temperature — the treatment's effect decays on its own time-based schedule, independent of temperature, and pouring after the fade window loses the benefit even with perfect temperature.

### Nodularity
A quality metric for ductile iron measuring the percentage of graphite present in the desired round/nodular form versus a degraded (flake or irregular) form, directly affecting the casting's mechanical properties.
**In use:** "Nodularity came back at seventy-eight percent — below our eighty-five minimum, likely from the extended time since inoculation."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a casting's temperature and pour behavior looking normal confirms adequate nodularity — nodularity depends on treatment timing (fade), which isn't visible in the pour process itself and requires a specific metallographic check to verify.

### Pour rate
The speed at which molten metal is introduced into a mold cavity, a controlled parameter matched to the specific gating/mold design rather than a generic "careful and steady" pace.
**In use:** "This gating system's designed for a specific fill rate — pouring faster than that risks turbulence, slower risks a cold shut."
**Common misuse:** Treating pour rate as a matter of operator feel or general caution rather than a parameter tied to the specific mold/gating design — different gating systems are engineered for different fill rates, and a pace that's appropriate for one can be wrong for another.

### Cold shut / misrun
Casting defects where molten metal solidifies before fully filling the mold cavity or before two streams of metal properly fuse, typically caused by pour temperature being too low or pour rate too slow.
**In use:** "That incomplete fill in the thin section is a classic cold shut — check actual pour temperature, not just what the furnace read at tap."
**Common misuse:** Diagnosing a cold shut/misrun defect against furnace tap temperature alone — pour temperature (measured at the actual point of pour, after any delay-driven decay) is what matters, and it can differ meaningfully from tap temperature.

### Steam explosion risk (moisture in molten metal contact)
A catastrophic, violent reaction occurring when moisture contacts molten metal, rapidly converting to steam and causing an explosive expansion — a safety hazard distinct from and more severe than any quality concern.
**In use:** "We're not skipping the drying check on this ladle for any reason — that's not a quality shortcut, that's a real explosion risk."
**Common misuse:** Treating ladle/tool drying verification as a routine quality step that can be shortened under time pressure — the consequence of a moisture-related steam explosion is categorically different (catastrophic, potentially fatal) from a quality defect, and the verification step should never be treated as optional or "probably fine."

### Refractory (ladle lining)
The heat-resistant lining material inside a ladle or furnace that contains molten metal, subject to wear, cracking, and buildup that can affect both pour quality and ladle integrity.
**In use:** "Checking refractory condition before this pour — even though the ladle worked fine last time, wear can develop between uses."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a ladle's refractory condition from its last successful use — wear and cracking can develop between uses in ways that aren't always predictable or visible without a fresh inspection.

### Pour temperature (point-of-pour verification)
The actual temperature of molten metal at the moment it's poured into the mold, which can differ meaningfully from furnace tap temperature due to heat loss over any elapsed time and handling.
**In use:** "Furnace read twenty-six fifty at tap, but we're twelve minutes out now — checking actual temperature at the ladle before we pour."
**Common misuse:** Treating furnace/tap temperature as representative of pour-time temperature regardless of elapsed time — temperature decays continuously from the moment metal leaves the furnace, and any meaningful delay requires direct re-verification at the point of pour.

### Degassing (melt treatment)
A melt treatment process removing dissolved gas (commonly hydrogen in aluminum alloys) from molten metal before casting, which — like inoculation — has a limited effective duration after treatment before re-absorption or benefit loss occurs.
**In use:** "Degassing was done fifteen minutes ago — checking the spec's fade window before we assume it's still effective."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a degassing treatment's benefit is permanent once applied — like inoculation, degassing effectiveness can diminish over time as the melt re-absorbs gas from the atmosphere or other sources, making elapsed time since treatment a relevant, separate variable from temperature.

### Gating system
The network of channels (sprue, runners, gates) designed to direct molten metal into a mold cavity at a specific rate and flow pattern, engineered to work with a specific pour rate.
**In use:** "This gating's designed for a controlled, moderate fill — pouring it like we would a simpler open-top mold would create turbulence it's not designed to handle."
**Common misuse:** Applying the same general pouring technique across different gating system designs — each gating design is engineered around an intended fill behavior, and technique needs to match the specific design, not be applied uniformly regardless of mold complexity.
