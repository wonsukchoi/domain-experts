# Playbook

Filled sequences for the four call types that make up most of a transit/railroad officer's shift. Each is a step order with thresholds an agent can execute directly, not a description of one.

## 1. Track-protection request (person, object, or vehicle fouling the track)

| Step | Action | Who confirms | Typical elapsed time |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Declare emergency stop on the radio: location, track/direction, nature of hazard | Rail/transit control center acknowledges verbally | 5–10 sec |
| 2 | Control center holds all movement toward the section | Control center states train ID(s) held and where | 5–15 sec |
| 3 | If roadbed entry is needed, request power isolation for the named track section | Control center confirms breaker open | 60–90 sec (varies by system/substation layout) |
| 4 | Officer reads back the isolation confirmation verbatim before stepping onto the roadbed | Officer, logged with exact time | — |
| 5 | On-roadbed action (rescue, arrest, inspection) | — | — |
| 6 | Officer clears the roadbed and notifies control center | Control center inspects and restores power/movement | 3–10 min typical for a short-duration incident |
| 7 | Control center formally releases the track to service | Logged time = incident-clear time for the report | — |

**Fallback if control center is unreachable or protection can't be confirmed:** treat the situation as an active emergency-stop-only condition — hold position at the platform/roadbed edge, do not enter, use PA/verbal direction to move the person to a refuge point, and escalate to a supervisor and the next available control channel. Never substitute "the train hasn't come" for a confirmed isolation.

**Distinguishing emergency stop from routine protection:** an emergency stop is a verbal, immediate hold used for an unplanned hazard (this playbook's use case). Routine blue-flag/blue-light protection is the planned process used before scheduled maintenance or investigation work and follows a written request/permit rather than a verbal declaration — don't use the slower routine process when someone is on the roadbed now.

## 2. Unattended item at a station

1. **Do not touch, move, or open the item.** Establish a stand-off perimeter: minimum 25 ft on an open platform, wider (50+ ft) if any behavioral indicator is present or the area is enclosed.
2. **Check for behavioral indicators**, in order of weight: visible wires or electronic components; leaking or unusual odor (chemical, almond, sulfur); unusual weight for size or unusual sound; placement against a structural column, near an egress point, or at a high-occupancy time; no plausible owner within visual range.
3. **Zero indicators + owner locatable nearby** → routine lost-property handling, no evacuation.
4. **One or more indicators, or no owner found after a page/announcement** → notify control center to consider a partial platform hold, request EOD/bomb squad, and begin a rolling evacuation from the perimeter outward — do not wait for EOD arrival to start moving people back.
5. **EOD clears or renders safe** → control center restores full service; officer logs total time item was unattended-to-cleared for the after-action report.

## 3. Fare-evasion contact

| Contact outcome | Default action | Escalate to arrest when |
|---|---|---|
| First contact, no ID issue, no warrant | Verbal warning or civil summons | — |
| Repeat contact (documented 2+ prior summonses at this system) | Summons, note pattern in system | 3rd+ documented evasion in a rolling 12 months, per agency's own escalation policy |
| Active warrant returns on ID check | — | Arrest on the warrant, not the fare offense |
| Physical resistance to a summons | De-escalate, disengage if safe, summons by mail/ID | Only if resistance itself rises to an arrestable offense (assault, flight with ID unknown) |

**Fallback position order** if the person has no ID and refuses to identify: (1) attempt voluntary ID via any means available (transit card registration, witness); (2) if genuinely unidentifiable and offense is fare-only, detain briefly for ID purposes proportionate to a summons-level offense, not a felony-level detention; (3) release with a to-be-determined summons only if department policy and state law support it — otherwise this step requires supervisor sign-off.

## 4. Chronic trespass/intrusion location (CPTED escalation)

1. Pull the location's incident count for the trailing 90 days. **Three or more track-intrusion or trespass calls at the same 500-foot segment in 90 days** is the threshold for escalating past enforcement-only response.
2. Cross-reference with the engineering/facilities department: fencing gaps, lighting outages, a desire-line (worn path) crossing the right-of-way, or a nearby transfer point without a legal crossing.
3. File a CPTED referral naming the specific defect (not "increase patrols") — e.g., "72-foot fence gap at MP 14.2, worn path confirmed by satellite imagery and three witness statements."
4. Continue enforcement response in parallel; CPTED fixes run on a facilities timeline (weeks to months), not a shift timeline.
5. Re-pull the 90-day count after the fix is installed to confirm the intrusion rate actually dropped — a fence fixed at the wrong gap doesn't reduce next quarter's count and means the desire-line analysis was wrong, not that CPTED failed.
