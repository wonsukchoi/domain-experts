### Blue flag / blue light protection
A formal signal-and-communication protocol (patterned on 49 CFR 218 Subpart B) that halts train movement and confirms power isolation over a defined track segment before anyone works or stands on the roadbed.
**In use:** "We're not fouling that track until Control reads back blue flag protection on Section 4."
**Common misuse:** Treating it as a physical lantern placed by the officer — it's a control-center-confirmed communication state, not a device the officer controls unilaterally.

### Fouling the track
Being positioned close enough to the rail that a passing train's dimensions (or a worker's tools/equipment) could make contact — not the same as physically standing on the rail itself.
**In use:** "He wasn't on the rail, but he was fouling the track — close enough that a train couldn't have passed him safely."
**Common misuse:** Assuming only someone standing directly on the rail counts as being in danger; the fouling zone extends well beyond the rail head.

### Third rail
The energized conductor rail (typically 600–750V DC depending on the system) that supplies traction power to trains without overhead catenary.
**In use:** "Confirm the third rail's isolated before anyone steps down — we don't assume it's off because there's no train coming."
**Common misuse:** Inferring the rail is de-energized from the absence of a visible or scheduled train; only a control-center confirmation establishes that.

### Trespasser (FRA definition)
Anyone on railroad property without authorization or license, at a location other than a public grade crossing — a broader category than the criminal-law term "trespass" and the basis for federal casualty statistics.
**In use:** "That's a trespasser fatality for FRA reporting purposes even if we never file a criminal trespass charge."
**Common misuse:** Conflating the FRA reporting category with whether a criminal charge was filed — the statistic counts the location and circumstance, not the disposition.

### Positive Train Control (PTC)
A federally mandated system (Rail Safety Improvement Act of 2008) that prevents train-to-train collisions, overspeed derailments, and unauthorized incursions into established work zones through automated train control.
**In use:** "PTC will stop two trains from colliding on this line, but it won't detect a person on the roadbed."
**Common misuse:** Assuming PTC functions as a general safety net that also protects pedestrians or vehicles fouling the track — it does not detect people.

### Hi-rail vehicle
A road-legal vehicle fitted with retractable steel wheels that allow it to travel directly on the rails, used for right-of-way patrol and maintenance access between stations.
**In use:** "Put the hi-rail out on the Fairview segment — there's no road access to that stretch."
**Common misuse:** Assuming any patrol SUV can access the right-of-way; only hi-rail-equipped and rail-authorized vehicles can enter the track structure itself.

### Roadway worker protection (RWP)
The set of rules and communication requirements governing how anyone (officer, maintenance crew, inspector) may occupy or work near an active track, distinct from general workplace safety rules.
**In use:** "Our RWP briefing covers what track authority we're operating under before the crew steps out."
**Common misuse:** Treating RWP as optional paperwork for a "quick" scene — the protection status is what makes entry legal, not a formality layered on top of it.

### Concurrent jurisdiction
The overlapping authority of a railroad/transit police department and a municipal police agency over the same geographic area, common where a station or right-of-way sits inside a city.
**In use:** "This is concurrent jurisdiction — we can take the platform portion, PD can take the street side, and we coordinate the handoff."
**Common misuse:** Treating concurrent jurisdiction as a boundary dispute to be resolved before acting, rather than a default cooperative arrangement that both agencies can act under simultaneously.

### VIPR (Visible Intermodal Prevention and Response) team
A TSA-led, joint deployment of federal, transit, and local law enforcement resources for randomized, high-visibility security sweeps at transit and rail facilities.
**In use:** "We've got a VIPR deployment at Central Station this evening — K9 and TSA screening at the mezzanine."
**Common misuse:** Referring to a department's own routine K9 or uniformed patrol as "VIPR" — the term specifically denotes the joint TSA-coordinated deployment, not any visible security presence.

### CPTED (Crime Prevention Through Environmental Design)
A design-based approach to reducing opportunity for crime or unauthorized access through physical changes — lighting, sightlines, fencing, landscaping — rather than through enforcement alone.
**In use:** "The repeat intrusions at MP 14.2 are a CPTED problem — that fence gap, not a patrol-frequency problem."
**Common misuse:** Using "CPTED" as a synonym for general environmental design or aesthetics rather than the specific security-through-design discipline tied to measurable incident locations.

### Grade crossing
A location where a public or private road crosses railroad tracks at the same level, distinct from a trespass location, which by definition is not an authorized crossing point.
**In use:** "That's a grade-crossing collision, not a trespasser strike — different FRA reporting category and different root-cause analysis."
**Common misuse:** Using "grade crossing" loosely to describe any point where a person crosses the tracks, including unauthorized foot paths that are not designated crossings.

### Special agent (railroad police)
The formal title, in many state statutes and railroad company usage, for a sworn officer employed by the railroad itself rather than a government agency, holding statutory police powers tied to the employer's property.
**In use:** "He's a special agent for the freight line — sworn statewide on their right-of-way, not a contract security guard."
**Common misuse:** Assuming "special agent" implies federal law enforcement (as with the FBI/DEA usage); in the rail context it denotes a privately employed but publicly sworn officer.

### Emergency stop (radio declaration)
A verbal, immediate order any qualified officer or employee can issue to a rail/transit control center to halt all train movement toward a specified section, used for unplanned hazards.
**In use:** "I declared an emergency stop the second the witness said someone was on the roadbed — we don't wait for the formal protection request in that situation."
**Common misuse:** Confusing it with routine blue-flag protection, which is the slower, planned process for scheduled track entry and not appropriate when someone is on the roadbed right now.
