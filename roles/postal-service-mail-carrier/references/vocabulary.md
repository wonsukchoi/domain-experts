# Vocabulary

### DPS (Delivery Point Sequencing)
Machine-sorted mail arriving already arranged in the exact walking or driving order of the route.
**In use:** "DPS came in heavy today, but the flats still need hand-casing before I can leave."
**Common misuse:** Assuming "casing" describes the whole route's mail; in practice DPS mail needs no manual sequencing at all — casing is specifically the non-machinable remainder.

### Casing
Manually sorting non-DPS mail into delivery-point order using the case (sorting cabinet), done before leaving the station.
**In use:** "Casing ran long this morning — three trays of flats plus all the forwards."
**Common misuse:** Confusing casing with delivering; casing is entirely an in-office activity that happens before the carrier ever leaves the building.

### IMD / MDD (Intelligent Mail Device / Mobile Delivery Device)
The handheld scanner that records each delivery, attempt, or exception as the official proof-of-service record.
**In use:** "No scan on that stop means no record it was attempted — that's what the investigation will ask about."
**Common misuse:** Treating a scan as optional bookkeeping rather than the actual legal record used to resolve lost-mail claims and misdelivery disputes.

### Route evaluation / Count and Inspection
The formal USPS process that measures a route's office and street time based on current volume and delivery-point count, setting the route's standard until the next evaluation.
**In use:** "Standard's still 7.5 hours from the last count, but that was before Phase 2 went in — time to request another one."
**Common misuse:** Treating the evaluated time as a permanent, self-updating figure rather than a snapshot that goes stale as volume and point count change.

### T6 / auxiliary (reserve) carrier
A substitute carrier who covers other carriers' routes on their scheduled days off, typically rotating across several routes rather than owning one.
**In use:** "T6 is covering Route 12 Saturday — make sure the route book's current before they head out."
**Common misuse:** Assuming any trained carrier can safely run any route with no route-book review, ignoring that hazard knowledge (dogs, access issues) is address-specific and doesn't transfer automatically.

### DOIS (Delivery Operations Information System)
The USPS system tracking a route's volume, delivery time, and workload history over time.
**In use:** "DOIS shows this route's been trending 15% over standard for six weeks straight."
**Common misuse:** Treating day-to-day DOIS variance as noise to shrug off, rather than the data source that justifies requesting a formal route re-evaluation.

### CBU (Cluster Box Unit)
A centralized, locked bank of individual mailboxes serving multiple addresses from one physical location.
**In use:** "That CBU's lock is jammed again — that's twelve addresses without delivery until it's fixed, not just one."
**Common misuse:** Treating a CBU delivery like a single stop; a fault there affects every address assigned to that unit simultaneously, which changes how urgently and how it should be reported.

### Hold mail / forwarding order
A customer-initiated, dated system instruction to withhold delivery for a period or redirect it to a new address.
**In use:** "System shows an active hold through the 14th — nothing goes in that box until it lifts."
**Common misuse:** Relying on memory of a customer's stated plans ("they said they'd be back") instead of checking the current dated system record, which can change independently of what was said in person.

### Delivery point
A single mail-receiving location — one mailbox, slot, or CBU compartment — the base unit that route time and route evaluation are built on.
**In use:** "450 delivery points on this route, but that's not the same as 450 addresses — the apartment building alone is 40 points."
**Common misuse:** Equating "address" with "delivery point"; one address can be many points (multi-unit buildings) or several addresses can share one point (multiple names on a single box).

### Attempted delivery
A scanned record showing delivery was tried but not completed, distinct from a successful delivery scan.
**In use:** "Scan it attempted, not delivered — the gate was locked and no one answered."
**Common misuse:** Treating every non-delivery as the customer's fault or absence, when "attempted" also covers a genuinely blocked access point, an unsafe condition, or a full receptacle.
