# Spa management working vocabulary

Terms a spa manager uses precisely and a generalist blurs together. Format: definition → how a practitioner says it → common misuse.

**Room-hour utilization** — Booked treatment-room hours divided by total available room-hours (rooms × operating hours), the core capacity metric for a spa since the room, not the therapist, is the fixed constraint.
In use: "We're at 61% utilization — before we hire, let's find out whether that's a demand gap or a scheduling-fragmentation gap."
Misuse: tracking therapist headcount or booking count alone as the capacity signal, without normalizing against actual room-hours available, which hides whether the real constraint is rooms or staff.

**Fragmentation loss** — Room-hour time lost between bookings because service durations don't align cleanly with the scheduling grid, distinct from time lost to genuinely low demand.
In use: "That 45 minutes a day per room isn't lack of demand — it's fragmentation from six different service durations that don't turn over cleanly."
Misuse: treating any utilization gap as a demand problem requiring more marketing, without first checking whether it's actually a scheduling-mismatch problem requiring menu standardization instead.

**Retail attach rate** — Retail product revenue as a percentage of service revenue, tracked because retail sales don't consume the room-hour constraint and represent close to pure incremental margin.
In use: "Retail attach is 6% against a 15-20% industry benchmark — that's revenue sitting on the table with zero capacity cost to capture it."
Misuse: treating retail as a minor add-on rather than a primary growth lever precisely because it's the one revenue category not bounded by room-hour capacity.

**Employee vs. independent contractor classification** — A legal determination (not a business preference) based on behavioral control (who sets the schedule/method), financial control (who provides tools/sets prices), and the relationship of the parties — misclassifying a worker who meets the employee test as a contractor creates back-tax and penalty exposure.
In use: "She works exclusively here, on our schedule, in our room, at our set prices — that's an employee regardless of what the 1099 says."
Misuse: assuming a signed independent-contractor agreement settles the classification question — the legal test looks at actual working conditions, not contract labels.

**Scope of practice (licensing)** — The specific set of services a licensed esthetician, massage therapist, or cosmetologist is legally authorized to perform under their state license, which varies meaningfully by state and license type.
In use: "Her esthetician license doesn't cover that modality in this state — we can't put it on her service list even if she's been trained on it elsewhere."
Misuse: assuming a certification or training course substitutes for the actual state-licensed scope of practice required to legally perform and bill for a service.

**No-show vs. late cancellation** — A no-show is a booked client who doesn't arrive or notify; a late cancellation is a client who cancels within a policy-defined window (commonly under 24 hours) too late to realistically rebook the slot. Both convert a booked, non-revenue room-hour into a cost equivalent to a staffed hour.
In use: "Our no-show rate runs 9% and late-cancellation another 6% — that's 15% of room-hours at risk before we even talk about actual demand."
Misuse: tracking only outright no-shows while ignoring late cancellations, which understates the actual share of room-hour capacity at risk from the same underlying behavior.

**Standard service duration (menu design)** — A limited set of treatment lengths (e.g., 50 and 80 minutes) chosen to align with the room-turnover schedule, as opposed to an expanded menu offering many different durations.
In use: "We're folding the 65 and 90-minute services into the 50/80 standard tiers — the variety wasn't worth the scheduling fragmentation it created."
Misuse: expanding the menu with more duration options to seem more flexible to clients, without accounting for the scheduling-gap cost each additional distinct duration creates.

**Day-part demand curve** — The pattern of booking demand across the operating day (e.g., a lunch-hour and after-work peak with a mid-afternoon lull), used to decide where a waitlist buffer or promotional pricing belongs.
In use: "The 2-4pm slot runs 40% utilization on weekdays — that's where a promotional rate makes sense, not across the whole day."
Misuse: applying a single utilization target or pricing strategy across the whole operating day, ignoring that the demand curve isn't flat and the fix belongs at the specific under-booked day-part.

**Waitlist buffer** — A small number of standby bookings held for peak-demand slots, sized to the historically observed no-show rate, used to protect against empty room-hours without fully overbooking.
In use: "Peak Saturday slots get a one-slot waitlist buffer given our 9% no-show rate — enough to fill a likely gap without risking a walk-in conflict."
Misuse: either ignoring no-show risk entirely (leaving predictable empty room-hours) or overbooking aggressively without data, which risks turning away a paying client when the no-show doesn't materialize.

**Sanitation/turnover protocol** — The documented, state-health-code-compliant procedure for cleaning and resetting a treatment room between clients, distinct from routine housekeeping.
In use: "The turnover log needs a signed entry every time, not just at shift change — that's what an inspector will actually check."
Misuse: treating sanitation logging as a paperwork formality rather than the actual compliance record a health department inspection or client dispute would rely on.
