# Geoscientist — Vocabulary

### OOIP (Original Oil in Place)
The total estimated volume of oil in a reservoir before any production, computed from bulk rock volume, porosity, and water saturation.
**In use:** "OOIP for Compartment A comes out to 10.7 MMstb before we apply a recovery factor."
**Common misuse:** treating OOIP itself as "reserves" — it isn't; reserves are OOIP times the recovery factor, and reporting OOIP as reserves overstates recoverable volume by whatever the recovery factor would have discounted (often 60-80%).

### Net pay
The portion of a gross logged interval that meets porosity, water-saturation, and shale-volume cutoffs for productive reservoir rock.
**In use:** "Gross interval is 48 feet, but net pay after cutoffs is only 22 feet."
**Common misuse:** using gross interval thickness in volumetric calculations instead of net pay, which inflates the bulk rock volume and everything downstream of it.

### SPE-PRMS (Petroleum Resources Management System)
The industry-standard framework (SPE/WPC/AAPG/SPEE) for classifying hydrocarbon volumes by confidence level (1P/2P/3P) and commercial maturity.
**In use:** "This compartment doesn't have enough well control to book as 1P — it stays 2P until we get production data."
**Common misuse:** treating "reserves" as a single fixed number rather than a category-dependent range; conflating "resources" (may not be commercially viable) with "reserves" (commercially viable under current conditions).

### Recovery factor (RF)
The fraction of oil or gas in place that is technically and economically recoverable, given the reservoir's drive mechanism and rock properties.
**In use:** "We're using a 35% recovery factor from the analog field's solution-gas-drive performance."
**Common misuse:** applying an analog field's RF to a new compartment without checking that the drive mechanism, rock quality, and fluid properties actually match — a mismatch here silently propagates through the entire reserve estimate.

### Vshale (shale volume)
The fraction of a logged interval's rock matrix that is shale, typically derived from a gamma-ray log.
**In use:** "We cut anything above Vshale 0.4 — that's mostly non-reservoir shale, not pay."
**Common misuse:** ignoring Vshale entirely and relying on porosity alone to define net pay, which misses shaly intervals that have porosity but won't produce.

### Water saturation (Sw)
The fraction of pore space occupied by water rather than hydrocarbon, typically calculated from resistivity logs via Archie's equation.
**In use:** "Sw comes out to 32% from the resistivity log using core-calibrated Archie parameters."
**Common misuse:** using textbook default Archie parameters (a, m, n) without core calibration, which can shift Sw by 10+ percentage points in shaly or unconventional rock.

### Stratigraphic correlation
The process of tying equivalent rock units (formation tops, marker beds) across multiple wells or outcrops to build a subsurface framework.
**In use:** "The correlation between A-1 and A-2 is solid — same marker bed, consistent structural dip — but A-3 has two competing ties."
**Common misuse:** treating a correlation as an established fact once it "looks clean," rather than as the best-supported hypothesis pending additional data.

### Structural closure
A geometric high point in a rock layer (from folding or faulting) capable of trapping migrating hydrocarbons, if sealed.
**In use:** "The 640-acre closure on the structure map is the trap — but we still need a fault-seal analysis before booking anything against it."
**Common misuse:** assuming a mapped closure automatically implies a working hydrocarbon trap, without evaluating whether the bounding seal (fault or stratigraphic) actually holds pressure.

### Recurrence interval
The average time between occurrences of a geologic event (earthquake, flood) of a given magnitude, expressed as a long-run statistical average, not a schedule.
**In use:** "The fault has a paleoseismic recurrence interval of about 250 years — that's a 0.4% annual probability, not a due date."
**Common misuse:** communicating a recurrence interval as if the event is "overdue" once the interval has elapsed since the last occurrence — the interval describes an average, not a fixed cycle.

### Material balance
A reservoir-engineering method that uses production, pressure, and PVT data to independently estimate original hydrocarbons in place, used to cross-check volumetric (static) estimates.
**In use:** "Once we had six months of production, the material-balance number came in about 12% below the volumetric — worth investigating before we finalize the 2P case."
**Common misuse:** relying solely on the static volumetric estimate indefinitely, even after enough production history exists to run a material-balance cross-check that could reveal the static model is wrong.

### Facies
A body of rock with characteristics (grain size, sedimentary structures, fossil content) reflecting a specific depositional environment, distinguished from adjacent rock of different origin.
**In use:** "The fining-upward gamma-ray signature is consistent with a fluvial point-bar facies."
**Common misuse:** extrapolating one facies interpretation across a wide area without checking whether the depositional system (e.g., a meandering river) is known to be laterally discontinuous at that scale.

### Bulk rock volume
The total volume of reservoir rock within a mapped closure, before porosity or fluid-saturation adjustments — the first step in a volumetric calculation.
**In use:** "Bulk rock volume is 14,080 acre-feet — that's before we apply porosity and water saturation."
**Common misuse:** confusing bulk rock volume with hydrocarbon volume; bulk rock volume includes the rock matrix and all pore fluids, not just the oil or gas fraction.
