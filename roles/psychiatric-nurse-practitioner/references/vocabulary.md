### Adequate trial
A medication given at a therapeutic dose for a sufficient duration (generally ≥4–6 weeks for antidepressants) with documented adherence — the minimum bar before a drug can be judged as "not working."
**In use:** "We can't call this treatment-resistant yet — she's only had two weeks at 50mg, that's not an adequate trial."
**Common misuse:** Generalists use "the medication failed" after any short course, regardless of dose or duration, conflating impatience with pharmacological non-response.

### Augmentation vs. switching
Augmentation adds a second agent to a partially effective regimen; switching replaces the current agent entirely. The choice depends on whether there was partial response (favors augmentation) or no response (favors switching).
**In use:** "She's had a 30% PHQ-9 drop on sertraline — that's partial response, so we augment with bupropion rather than switch."
**Common misuse:** Treating any incomplete response as an automatic switch, discarding partial gains instead of building on them.

### Collaborative practice agreement (CPA)
The formal document (required in reduced/restricted-practice states) defining the working relationship between a PMHNP and a collaborating physician, including chart-review percentage and prescribing limits.
**In use:** "Our CPA requires 10% chart co-review monthly and physician sign-off on new controlled-substance schedules."
**Common misuse:** Treating the CPA as a formality rather than an operative document that defines what the NP can and cannot do unilaterally in that state.

### Black box (boxed) warning
The FDA's strongest label warning, indicating a serious or life-threatening risk — for antidepressants, increased suicidal thinking/behavior in patients under 25.
**In use:** "The boxed warning doesn't mean don't prescribe — it means she needs weekly contact for the first four weeks."
**Common misuse:** Reading a boxed warning as a contraindication rather than a mandated monitoring intensity.

### MDQ (Mood Disorder Questionnaire)
A 13-item bipolar-spectrum screening tool; positive at ≥7 yes-items plus at least moderate co-occurring functional impact.
**In use:** "MDQ came back positive — 8 yeses with moderate impact — so we're holding antidepressant monotherapy pending a fuller mood history."
**Common misuse:** Treating a positive MDQ as a bipolar diagnosis rather than a screen that changes the next step in the workup.

### C-SSRS (Columbia-Suicide Severity Rating Scale)
A structured suicide-risk assessment tracking ideation, intensity, and behavior, used to standardize risk documentation across visits.
**In use:** "C-SSRS today is passive ideation, no plan, no intent — that's a tier-1 risk, not an ED referral."
**Common misuse:** Running it once at intake and never repeating it, when risk tier can change visit to visit.

### AIMS (Abnormal Involuntary Movement Scale)
A structured exam for detecting and quantifying tardive dyskinesia in patients on long-term antipsychotics.
**In use:** "AIMS score went from 2 to 6 since last visit — that's a tardive dyskinesia trend, not incidental twitching."
**Common misuse:** Skipping AIMS because the patient "doesn't look like they have movement problems" — early TD is subtle and scored, not eyeballed.

### Therapeutic drug monitoring
Scheduled blood-level checks (lithium, valproate, clozapine's ANC) used to keep a drug in its safe and effective range, not just at intake.
**In use:** "Trough is 0.9 mEq/L, right in range — no dose change needed, recheck in 3 months per protocol."
**Common misuse:** Ordering a level only when something seems wrong, instead of on the scheduled interval that catches asymptomatic drift.

### Metabolic monitoring
The baseline/12-week/annual panel (weight, waist circumference, BP, fasting glucose, fasting lipids) required for patients on second-generation antipsychotics.
**In use:** "He's due for his 12-week metabolic panel — weight's up 6% from baseline, that's above the action threshold."
**Common misuse:** Treating metabolic monitoring as optional paperwork rather than the mechanism that catches drug-induced diabetes and dyslipidemia before it's symptomatic.

### Serotonin syndrome
A potentially life-threatening drug reaction from excess serotonergic activity, presenting as a triad of mental-status change, autonomic instability, and neuromuscular findings (clonus, hyperreflexia).
**In use:** "New clonus and agitation after adding tramadol to her SSRI — this needs to be ruled in or out as serotonin syndrome today, not at the next visit."
**Common misuse:** Confusing it with generic anxiety or agitation and missing the neuromuscular exam findings that distinguish it.

### PDMP (Prescription Drug Monitoring Program)
A state-run database of controlled-substance fills across all prescribers and pharmacies, queried before writing or refilling a controlled-substance script.
**In use:** "PDMP shows a fill from another prescriber three days ago — we need to have that conversation before writing anything today."
**Common misuse:** Querying it only for new patients rather than at every controlled-substance encounter, missing patterns that develop over time.

### Treatment-resistant depression (TRD)
A depressive episode that has not responded to at least two adequate trials of antidepressants from different classes.
**In use:** "This qualifies as TRD — two adequate trials, both at therapeutic dose for six-plus weeks, both documented non-response."
**Common misuse:** Applying the label after one inadequate trial, which understates how much room is left in first-line treatment.

### Full practice authority
A state designation under which a PMHNP can evaluate, diagnose, prescribe (including controlled substances, per state rules), and manage treatment without a mandated physician agreement.
**In use:** "We're in a full-practice-authority state, so I don't need a co-signature on this med change — but I still document the reasoning the same way."
**Common misuse:** Assuming full practice authority changes the standard of clinical reasoning or documentation required — it changes the liability structure, not the medicine.

### Discontinuation syndrome
A cluster of symptoms (dizziness, paresthesia, irritability, "brain zaps") from abrupt cessation of a short-half-life antidepressant, distinct from relapse by its rapid onset.
**In use:** "Symptoms started two days after she stopped venlafaxine cold — that's discontinuation syndrome, not a depression relapse."
**Common misuse:** Mistaking rapid-onset discontinuation symptoms for a return of the underlying disorder and restarting or escalating treatment unnecessarily.
