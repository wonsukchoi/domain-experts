# Insurance Claims and Policy Processing Clerk — Vocabulary

### Proof of loss
A signed statement from the policyholder formally documenting the claimed loss — amount, cause, and circumstances — required within a state-mandated or policy-specified window after the date of loss.
**In use:** "The proof-of-loss statement is still outstanding and we're inside the 60-day window — flag this one, don't let it sit with the photo requests."
**Common misuse:** Treating it as equivalent to the initial claim narrative or first notice of loss — it's a distinct, later, formally signed document with its own deadline.

### First notice of loss (FNOL)
The initial report of a loss to the carrier, starting the claims process — distinct from proof of loss, which comes later and is a separate formal requirement.
**In use:** "FNOL came in yesterday; the file's still in intake, we haven't gotten to completeness review yet."
**Common misuse:** Using "FNOL" and "the claim" interchangeably when documenting timeline — FNOL is a specific event/timestamp, not the whole process.

### Endorsement
A change to an existing policy's terms — adding/removing coverage, changing a limit, correcting an error — that modifies the contract without replacing it entirely.
**In use:** "This is an endorsement request to raise the dwelling coverage limit, not a new policy — check for open claims before processing."
**Common misuse:** Confusing an endorsement with a renewal — a renewal is a new policy period; an endorsement modifies the current one mid-term.

### Cause of loss (peril code)
The specific coded classification of what caused the loss (fire, wind, theft, burst pipe, etc.), used for both claims routing and statistical reporting.
**In use:** "The cause-of-loss code says wind, but the narrative describes a plumbing failure — that's a mismatch worth a clarifying question."
**Common misuse:** Treating the code as a formality to fill in rather than a claim that should match the narrative and photos.

### Reservation of rights
A carrier's written notice that it's investigating a claim while preserving its right to later deny coverage based on a specific policy provision — an adjuster-level decision, not something this role drafts, but a term this role will see referenced on files it processes.
**In use:** "This file has a reservation-of-rights letter attached from the adjuster — don't close it out of the queue as resolved."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a reservation of rights means the claim is being denied — it means coverage is still under investigation, not decided.

### Subrogation
A carrier's right to recover claim payment from a third party responsible for the loss, pursued after the policyholder is paid.
**In use:** "Flag this file for subrogation review — the narrative names another driver as at-fault."
**Common misuse:** Confusing subrogation with the claim's own coverage determination — subrogation is about recovering money after paying the policyholder, a separate downstream process this role routes to but doesn't decide.

### Named insured
The person(s) or entity specifically listed on the policy as covered — distinct from anyone who happens to be affected by the loss or files the claim.
**In use:** "The claim was submitted by the tenant, but the named insured on this policy is the property owner — verify the relationship before processing."
**Common misuse:** Assuming the person who calls in or submits the claim is automatically the named insured.

### Total loss threshold
The point at which repair cost relative to actual cash value triggers a mandatory total-loss designation on a vehicle — an adjuster-level calculation, but a term this role will see on routed auto files.
**In use:** "This file's already flagged total-loss by the adjuster — route status updates accordingly, don't send a repair-timeline update."
**Common misuse:** This role doesn't calculate the threshold itself; the misuse is assuming a repair estimate lower than ACV automatically means "repairable" without the adjuster's percentage-threshold math (see `claims-adjuster` for that calculation).

### Cycle time
The elapsed time from claim intake to resolution, tracked as an operational benchmark distinct from any regulatory deadline.
**In use:** "This file's approaching the 10-day cycle-time benchmark with no adjuster contact yet — that's a status-update trigger, not yet a compliance issue."
**Common misuse:** Conflating cycle-time benchmarks (internal operational targets) with statutory unfair-claims-practices deadlines (external regulatory requirements) — missing the former is inefficiency; missing the latter can be a violation.

### SIU (Special Investigations Unit)
The carrier's internal fraud-investigation team, which claims are referred to when specific documented indicators are present — not a default escalation path for general unease.
**In use:** "Route this to SIU with the specific indicator noted — repeat contractor across three claims this month, not just 'this seems off.'"
**Common misuse:** Treating any coding mismatch or inconsistency as automatic grounds for an SIU referral rather than a routine clarifying question first.

### Loss run
A report summarizing an insured's claims history over a period, used in underwriting and renewal decisions.
**In use:** "Pull the loss run on this address before processing the endorsement — three prior claims in 12 months might affect how the underwriter wants this handled."
**Common misuse:** Confusing a loss run (historical claims summary) with a single claim's file history.
