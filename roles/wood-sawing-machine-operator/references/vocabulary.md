# Vocabulary

### Kickback
A severe saw safety hazard where a workpiece binds against the blade and is thrown back toward the operator at high speed, resulting from a specific, preventable combination of conditions rather than random chance.
**In use:** "Kickback risk on this cut comes from the missing riving knife, not bad luck — that's a known, preventable condition."
**Common misuse:** Treating kickback as an unpredictable, inherent risk of sawing that "just happens sometimes" — it results from identifiable, preventable conditions (misalignment, dull/wrong blade, missing safety devices, poor feed technique), and addressing those conditions directly prevents it.

### Riving knife (splitter)
A safety device positioned behind a table saw blade that keeps the kerf (cut) open, preventing the workpiece from pinching the blade and causing kickback.
**In use:** "Riving knife stays in for every rip cut — there's no cut where removing it is actually the safer or easier choice."
**Common misuse:** Removing the riving knife for a specific cut perceived as "not needing it" (a shallow cut, a small piece) — the device protects against a binding mechanism that can occur across a wide range of cut types, not just obviously risky ones.

### Rip cut vs. crosscut
Two fundamentally different cut types relative to wood grain — a rip cut runs parallel to the grain (along the length of the fibers), a crosscut runs perpendicular to the grain — each requiring a different blade tooth configuration for clean, safe cutting.
**In use:** "Switching to the rip blade — fewer, larger teeth designed to clear material fast along the grain, not the crosscut blade's finer tooth pattern."
**Common misuse:** Using a single "general purpose" or wrong-type blade for both rip and crosscut operations — each cut type has different fiber-shearing requirements, and a mismatched blade both produces worse cut quality and increases binding/kickback risk.

### Clear span (defect-free length)
The length of material along a board or panel free of defects (knots, checks, cracks), which determines the actual maximum length of a structurally sound part obtainable from that specific piece of stock.
**In use:** "This board's got two clear spans — neither one's long enough for the forty-inch blank we need."
**Common misuse:** Planning a cut based on the board's total length rather than its actual clear span around defects — a board can be long enough in total length while having no single defect-free section long enough for the required part.

### Blade tension and tracking (band saw)
Two adjustable parameters specific to band saws — tension (how tightly the blade is stretched) and tracking (the blade's alignment on the wheels) — that together determine whether the blade cuts straight or drifts during a cut.
**In use:** "Checking tension and tracking before this precision cut — a running saw doesn't confirm either one is actually set correctly."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a band saw that's running and cutting is properly tensioned and tracked — insufficient tension or poor tracking causes the blade to drift during a cut, producing a non-straight result even with a correctly set fence.

### Blade drift (band saw)
The tendency of a band saw blade to wander from a straight cutting line during a cut, typically caused by inadequate blade tension or poor tracking, distinct from a fence-alignment issue.
**In use:** "That curve in the cut is drift, not a fence problem — the fence was set correctly, the blade wandered."
**Common misuse:** Diagnosing a non-straight band saw cut as a fence alignment issue by default — drift caused by tension/tracking problems produces a similar-looking result but requires a completely different fix.

### Anti-kickback pawls
Spring-loaded devices, often paired with a riving knife, that dig into the workpiece if it starts moving backward toward the operator, physically arresting a kickback event in progress.
**In use:** "Pawls caught that one before it could throw the piece back — that's exactly what they're there for."
**Common misuse:** Treating anti-kickback pawls as redundant or unnecessary when a riving knife is already present — the two devices address related but distinct aspects of kickback prevention, and removing either reduces the overall protection.

### Feed technique (saw)
The method by which a workpiece is guided and supported through a cut, affecting whether the material can twist, pinch, or bind against the blade.
**In use:** "Keeping consistent pressure against the fence through the whole cut — letting the piece wander partway through is how a bind starts."
**Common misuse:** Treating feed technique as unimportant once blade and safety device setup are correct — improper feeding (inconsistent pressure, allowing twist) can create a binding condition regardless of how well the machine itself is set up.

### Defect-avoidance cut planning
The practice of sequencing cuts from a board or panel to route around visible material defects, prioritizing structural/aesthetic integrity over pure dimensional yield.
**In use:** "This board only yields a shorter clear piece once we route around the knot — diverting it to a non-structural part instead of forcing the structural blank through the defect."
**Common misuse:** Treating cut planning as a purely geometric yield-maximization exercise, blind to defect location — a layout that maximizes usable area without checking each piece's actual clear span around defects can produce dimensionally correct but structurally compromised parts.
