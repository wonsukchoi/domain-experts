# Sociologist — Red Flags

### A group-level correlation is being used to explain an individual case
- **Usually means:** ecological fallacy — someone is reading "neighborhoods with trait X have outcome Y" as "this person has trait X, so they have outcome Y."
- **First question:** "Is this claim about the group's average, or about this specific individual — and does the data support the second?"
- **Data to pull:** the individual-level data, if it exists; if it doesn't, flag the claim as unsupported at the individual level regardless of how strong the group-level correlation is.

### Intercoder kappa below 0.60 on a code already in production coding
- **Usually means:** the code's operational boundary is ambiguous, not that coders need more training.
- **First question:** "Where do the two coders' readings diverge — is it one recurring type of case, or scattered?"
- **Data to pull:** the disagreement transcripts themselves, sorted by which specific passages triggered the split.

### A qualitative "theme" is reported with no sample size or coding process described
- **Usually means:** either a single coder's impression presented as a finding, or a cherry-picked set of quotes chosen to illustrate a point decided in advance.
- **First question:** "How many transcripts were coded, by how many coders, and what fraction showed this theme?"
- **Data to pull:** the codebook and the code-frequency table across the full sample.

### A generational/cohort claim is made from a single cross-sectional survey
- **Usually means:** the pattern is likely an age effect (people at this life stage always look this way) or a period effect (something happened to everyone recently), misattributed to cohort.
- **First question:** "Is there a second survey wave or an older cohort at the same age to compare against?"
- **Data to pull:** repeated cross-sections or a panel, to attempt an age-period-cohort decomposition.

### A between-group outcome gap (marital status, union membership, neighborhood) is reported without addressing selection
- **Usually means:** the groups differ on more than the category itself — people who marry, unionize, or move aren't a random draw from the population.
- **First question:** "What predicts who ends up in each group, and could that same thing predict the outcome?"
- **Data to pull:** covariates available for matching, or a natural experiment / instrument if one exists.

### Response rate on a survey component is below ~50% with no nonresponse analysis
- **Usually means:** whoever answered may differ systematically from whoever didn't, biasing every downstream estimate in an unknown direction.
- **First question:** "Do respondents and non-respondents differ on any variable we have for both (age, address type, prior-wave response)?"
- **Data to pull:** frame-level demographics for the full sampled population, compared against the responding subset.

### A construct (social capital, class, community cohesion) is operationalized with no stated alternative considered
- **Usually means:** the operationalization was chosen for data convenience, not theoretical fit, and the finding may be an artifact of that specific choice.
- **First question:** "What would this finding look like under the next-most-plausible operationalization?"
- **Data to pull:** a sensitivity check re-running the analysis with at least one alternative measure of the same construct.

### Qualitative sample size was fixed before fieldwork began (e.g. "we will interview exactly 30 people")
- **Usually means:** saturation logic was replaced with a quantitative-style pre-registered n, which may stop data collection before new themes stop emerging, or continue well past the point where they did.
- **First question:** "Did the last 3-5 interviews produce any code that wasn't already in the codebook?"
- **Data to pull:** a code-emergence-by-interview-number chart across the full field period.

### A mixed-methods report presents quant and qual findings that actually disagree, framed as if they agree
- **Usually means:** the write-up smoothed over a real tension to present a cleaner narrative.
- **First question:** "Where specifically do the two data sources point in different directions, and was that reported?"
- **Data to pull:** the qual findings that don't fit the quant headline, and vice versa.

### A stratification/mobility finding is reported without specifying the time window and cohort it covers
- **Usually means:** mobility rates vary substantially by era (labor-market structure, education-cost regimes) and the finding may not generalize outside the studied window.
- **First question:** "What birth cohort and calendar years does this cover, and has mobility in this population shifted across other windows?"
- **Data to pull:** the same statistic computed for at least one earlier cohort/period for comparison.
