# Sociologist — Vocabulary

### Ecological fallacy
Inferring an individual-level relationship from a group-level (aggregate) correlation.
**In use:** "We can't say this specific low-income student is at higher dropout risk just because their school's aggregate dropout rate is high — that's an ecological inference."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a synonym for "aggregation is bad" generally, rather than the specific error of crossing from group-level to individual-level claims.

### Atomistic fallacy
The mirror-image error: inferring a group- or structural-level pattern from individual-level data or anecdotes.
**In use:** "Three interviewees mentioning discrimination doesn't establish an institutional pattern — that's an atomistic inference running the wrong direction."
**Common misuse:** Rarely named at all; generalists usually only know "ecological fallacy" and misapply that label to this opposite error.

### Operationalization
The process of specifying how an abstract construct will be measured with concrete data.
**In use:** "We're operationalizing 'social capital' as bridging-tie count from the network module, not bonding-tie density — that choice will affect which neighborhoods look high-capital."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a mechanical, theory-neutral step, when the choice of operationalization is itself a substantive theoretical claim.

### Saturation
The point in qualitative data collection where new interviews or observations stop producing new codes or themes.
**In use:** "We hit saturation around interview 34 — the last six didn't surface anything the codebook didn't already have."
**Common misuse:** Confused with hitting a pre-set sample size target; saturation is about information yield, not a headcount.

### Intercoder reliability
A quantitative measure of agreement between two or more independent coders applying the same codebook to the same data.
**In use:** "Kappa on the 'identity loss' code came in at 0.31 — we need to revise the definition before scaling to the full sample."
**Common misuse:** Skipped entirely, with "the coders discussed and agreed" substituted for a computed reliability statistic — discussion-to-consensus doesn't demonstrate the codebook is unambiguous to an independent reader.

### Cohen's kappa
A chance-corrected agreement statistic; unlike raw percent agreement, it subtracts the agreement expected by chance given each coder's marginal rate of applying the code.
**In use:** "Raw agreement looked fine at 67%, but kappa was only 0.31 once we corrected for how often each coder applies the code overall."
**Common misuse:** Substituted with raw percent agreement, which overstates reliability whenever a code is applied to a small minority (or large majority) of cases — chance agreement is high in both situations.

### Grounded theory
A methodology for generating theory inductively from data, through iterative coding and constant comparison, rather than testing a theory specified in advance.
**In use:** "We're running this as grounded theory — the codebook isn't fixed at the start, it evolves as categories emerge from the interviews."
**Common misuse:** Used as a label for any qualitative study, including ones that actually test a pre-specified hypothesis — grounded theory specifically means the theory itself is an output, not an input.

### Thick description
Detailed, context-rich qualitative reporting that conveys not just behavior but the meaning and social context surrounding it.
**In use:** "The methods section needs thick description of the plant-closure context — without it, readers outside this region won't know what 'losing the job' meant locally."
**Common misuse:** Conflated with simply writing at length; thickness refers to interpretive depth and context, not word count.

### Social stratification
The hierarchical arrangement of individuals or groups by unequal access to resources, status, or power, typically studied via class, race, gender, or education.
**In use:** "The stratification finding here is about relative mobility — whether rank order changes across generations — not just whether absolute income rose."
**Common misuse:** Used loosely as a synonym for "inequality," collapsing the distinction between the level of inequality and the structure/persistence of the hierarchy.

### Social mobility (absolute vs. relative)
Absolute mobility measures whether people do better than their parents in real terms; relative mobility measures whether their rank in the distribution changed.
**In use:** "Absolute mobility can rise across the whole distribution — everyone getting richer — while relative mobility stays flat because rank order didn't change."
**Common misuse:** The two are reported interchangeably, when a policy conclusion ("mobility improved") often depends entirely on which one is meant.

### Selection effect
A systematic difference between who ends up in a group being studied and who doesn't, that is itself a cause of the outcome being measured.
**In use:** "Before crediting the mentorship program with the retention gain, check whether more-motivated employees selected into it in the first place."
**Common misuse:** Narrowed to mean only self-selection by individuals; institutional selection (who gets assigned, admitted, or recruited) is the same problem and just as common.

### Reflexivity
The researcher's explicit accounting for how their own position (identity, relationship to participants, prior assumptions) shapes data collection and interpretation.
**In use:** "The reflexivity statement notes the interviewer was a former plant employee — that likely affected rapport and disclosure in ways a stranger-interviewer wouldn't produce."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a pro forma disclosure paragraph rather than a substantive discussion of how the researcher's position plausibly shaped specific findings.

### Age-period-cohort (APC) problem
The statistical identification problem that arises because age, survey period, and birth cohort are perfectly collinear (period = age + birth year), making it impossible to fully separate their effects without additional assumptions or data structure.
**In use:** "With only two survey waves we can't run a real APC decomposition — we don't have enough periods to separate cohort from period effects."
**Common misuse:** Attempted with data too thin (few cohorts, few periods) to identify the three effects separately, then reported with the same confidence as a well-identified decomposition.

### Purposive sampling
Deliberately selecting participants because they have characteristics relevant to the research question, rather than sampling randomly.
**In use:** "We used purposive sampling to ensure the interview sample included both workers who found comparable-skill reemployment and those who didn't."
**Common misuse:** Confused with convenience sampling (whoever's easiest to reach); purposive sampling requires a deliberate, stated logic for who's included and why.

### Triangulation
Using multiple data sources or methods to examine the same question, treating convergence as stronger evidence and divergence as informative rather than as noise to be resolved away.
**In use:** "The survey and interview findings triangulate on the same mechanism — that's stronger evidence than either alone."
**Common misuse:** Applied only when the methods agree; when they disagree, the disagreement itself gets dropped from the write-up rather than reported as a finding about measurement or mechanism.
