# Receptionist — Vocabulary

### Warm transfer
Announcing a caller to the person receiving the transfer before connecting them, versus a "cold" or "blind" transfer where the call just rings through with no context.
**In use:** "Give me a warm transfer on that one — the caller's already frustrated and shouldn't have to re-explain."
**Common misuse:** Treated as optional politeness rather than a functional handoff — a cold transfer on an already-escalated call often re-escalates it when the receiving person has to ask "sorry, what's this about?"

### Gatekeeping
Screening calls/visitors on behalf of an executive or department, deciding what gets through immediately versus deferred.
**In use:** "Part of the job is gatekeeping for the VP — not every call needs to interrupt her."
**Common misuse:** Used as a synonym for "being unhelpful" or "blocking access," when the actual function is prioritization, not obstruction — a good gatekeeper still gets urgent things through fast.

### Tailgating
A security term for someone gaining building access by following an authorized person through a door or past a badge check without being individually verified.
**In use:** "That's how the auditor flagged us last year — someone tailgated through the badge-only door right behind an employee."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to require malicious intent; most tailgating is an authorized person holding the door for someone out of politeness, not an intruder sneaking in.

### FIFO (first-in, first-out) queue handling
Answering calls or serving visitors in the order they arrived, absent an overriding priority flag.
**In use:** "Line 1's been holding longer, so it's FIFO — Line 1 first unless Line 2 is the emergency extension."
**Common misuse:** Applied rigidly even when a clear priority signal (VIP line, safety concern, visibly distressed visitor) should override strict arrival order.

### Screening question
A single, minimal question used to route a call or visitor correctly without conducting a full intake.
**In use:** "One screening question — is this about billing or support? — gets them to the right department without me trying to solve it myself."
**Common misuse:** Turned into a multi-question interrogation, which frustrates the caller and duplicates work the receiving department will redo anyway.

### De-escalation
Deliberately lowering the emotional intensity of an interaction before attempting to solve the underlying problem.
**In use:** "I de-escalated first — named that she was frustrated — before I even tried to find out who she needed."
**Common misuse:** Confused with "agreeing" or "apologizing on behalf of the company" — de-escalation is about tone and pacing, not conceding fault.

### Visitor-management system (VMS)
Software that pre-registers expected visitors, prints badges, and logs sign-in/sign-out times, often tied to a host employee's calendar invite.
**In use:** "The VMS flagged him as pre-registered, so badging in took ten seconds instead of a phone call to confirm."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a replacement for judgment — a VMS match confirms a name was entered somewhere, not that the person standing at the desk is who they claim to be.

### Escalation path
The predefined sequence of who to contact, in order, when a situation exceeds what the front desk can resolve (safety issue, angry executive-level complaint, media inquiry).
**In use:** "Press calls go straight to the escalation path — comms director, not me."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to be common knowledge; a receptionist who's never been walked through the actual escalation path will improvise one under pressure, which is how the wrong person ends up handling a sensitive call.

### Hold-queue visibility
The phone system feature showing how many calls are waiting and how long each has been on hold.
**In use:** "Hold-queue visibility is why I knew Line 1 had been waiting 40 seconds before I even looked at the visitor."
**Common misuse:** Ignored in practice even when available — some receptionists answer whichever line rings loudest rather than checking actual wait time.

### Badge exception
A deliberate, logged deviation from standard sign-in/badge procedure, made for a specific documented reason.
**In use:** "That's a badge exception, not a skipped procedure — I logged why and got a verbal confirm from the host first."
**Common misuse:** Used retroactively to justify skipping the procedure entirely rather than as a documented, judgment-based deviation with a paper trail.

### Sign-in log
The record — paper or digital — of who entered the building, when, and who they were there to see.
**In use:** "Pull the sign-in log for the past three weeks — see if this visitor's name shows up requesting different people each time."
**Common misuse:** Kept inconsistently (skipped during busy periods) which defeats its value as a security and pattern-detection record exactly when it's most needed.
