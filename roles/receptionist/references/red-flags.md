# Receptionist — Red Flags

### Visitor claims an appointment that isn't on any calendar or list
- **Usually means:** honest scheduling mismatch (wrong day, wrong location, appointment made verbally and never logged) — occasionally a social-engineering attempt to gain building access.
- **First question:** "Who scheduled this with you, and do you have a confirmation email or their direct number?"
- **Data to pull:** today's expected-visitor list, the named employee's calendar if visible, a callback to the named employee's extension.

### Visitor pushes back hard on badge/sign-in procedure ("I do this every week, you know me")
- **Usually means:** genuine frequent visitor who finds the repetition annoying — but familiarity is exactly the social pressure that causes procedure to be skipped when it shouldn't be.
- **First question:** "I hear you — it'll take 10 seconds, mind signing in while I badge you?"
- **Data to pull:** none needed if truly a known, badged-before visitor; escalate to a supervisor if pressure continues past one polite redirect.

### Caller refuses to state a reason for the call
- **Usually means:** privacy-conscious caller with a legitimate but sensitive reason (HR complaint, legal matter) — or a sales call trying to bypass gatekeeping.
- **First question:** "I don't need details, just a general category so I route you correctly."
- **Data to pull:** none — route to a general voicemail or the most likely department if they still won't say.

### Call volume on hold exceeds 3+ lines simultaneously
- **Usually means:** either a system-wide issue prompting many people to call at once (outage, event, mass mailing that listed the wrong number) or simply an unusually busy period.
- **First question:** "Is this call volume about one specific thing?" (ask the first caller you reach)
- **Data to pull:** check if an internal announcement, system status page, or recent mass email correlates with the timing.

### Visitor or caller mentions a safety concern (threat, injury, unsafe condition)
- **Usually means:** exactly what it sounds like — this is never a routine-routing situation.
- **First question:** none — escalate immediately per the organization's safety protocol, do not attempt to triage further.
- **Data to pull:** n/a — time-sensitive, escalate first, document after.

### Same visitor name shows up requesting access to multiple different employees across visits
- **Usually means:** could be a legitimate vendor/contractor with several contacts — could also be a pattern worth flagging (unauthorized solicitation, someone probing for building access).
- **First question:** "What company are you with, and who initiated today's visit?"
- **Data to pull:** sign-in log history for that name/company over the past few weeks.

### Delivery person or courier asks to be let past the desk instead of leaving the package
- **Usually means:** most often genuine (heavy/signature-required item) — occasionally a bypass attempt.
- **First question:** "I can sign for it here, or call up to have someone come down — which works?"
- **Data to pull:** none needed; default to keeping deliveries at the desk unless a specific employee confirms otherwise by phone.

### A caller identifies as press, a regulator, or law enforcement
- **Usually means:** exactly what it sounds like, and the organization almost always has a specific person who's supposed to handle this, not the receptionist.
- **First question:** "Can I get your name and a callback number, and I'll connect you with [designated contact] directly?"
- **Data to pull:** the organization's designated media/legal/regulatory contact — never answer substantive questions from the desk.

### Multiple hang-ups from the same number in a short window
- **Usually means:** either a caller who keeps getting disconnected/misrouted, or automated/spam dialing.
- **First question:** if they reach a live person on a later attempt, "I see a few calls from this number — were you trying to reach someone specific?"
- **Data to pull:** caller-ID log if the phone system retains one.
