# Vocabulary

### Terrazzo
A matrix of a binder (Portland cement or resin) embedded with graded aggregate (marble, glass, or quartz chips), ground and polished to expose the aggregate in a flat, continuous plane.
**In use:** "This is bonded epoxy terrazzo, not stamped concrete — the aggregate is graded and seeded, then ground, not just broadcast on top."
**Common misuse:** calling any polished, aggregate-flecked floor "terrazzo," including stained or stamped concrete overlays with no actual ground matrix.

### Divider strip
A metal or plastic strip set into the substrate before the pour that separates pattern fields; on cementitious terrazzo it functions as a shrinkage-control joint, on epoxy it functions mainly as a design element.
**In use:** "Run the strips on 4-ft centers here — we're cementitious, we need the shrinkage relief, not just the pattern."
**Common misuse:** treating strips as purely cosmetic pattern lines and spacing them by the design alone on a cementitious job, ignoring the shrinkage-control function.

### Chip / aggregate grade
Marble, glass, or quartz particles sized and numbered by industry grade — #0 through #2 for standard terrazzo, #3 through #8 for Venetian, #00/#000 for micro-aggregate.
**In use:** "Spec calls for #1 standard chip at 3/8 in — that's not going to read as Venetian scale no matter how we seed it."
**Common misuse:** using "chip" and "aggregate" as generic synonyms for gravel or crushed stone without reference to the grade number that actually determines topping thickness and finish scale.

### Seeding
Broadcasting and working chips into the still-wet binder, by hand or blower, to reach a target density before the mix sets.
**In use:** "Seed to refusal, then roll it — we want tight, even coverage, not 70% fill with binder showing through."
**Common misuse:** treating seeding as a purely decorative sprinkle rather than a density-controlled step that determines how even the final ground surface will look.

### Grout coat (double grout)
A thin cement or resin slurry, matched or contrasted in color, applied after the first rough grind to fill voids and pinholes exposed by that pass, before continuing to finer grits.
**In use:** "First grind opened some pinholes along the east wall — lay a matching grout coat there before we go to the 200-grit pass."
**Common misuse:** confusing this with tile grout; a terrazzo grout coat is a fill-and-seal step within the ground matrix itself, not a joint filler between separate units.

### Grit progression / grind schedule
The ordered sequence of coarse-to-fine diamond abrasive passes (roughly 40–80 → 100–200 → 400–3000+) used to expose aggregate, flatten, and polish the surface.
**In use:** "Don't jump from 80 to 400 — you'll leave holidays in the field that only show under raking light."
**Common misuse:** skipping intermediate grits to save a pass, which leaves a scratch pattern invisible under overhead shop lighting but obvious once the floor is sealed and occupied.

### Honing vs. polishing
Honing stops the grit progression at a mid-range grit for a matte or satin finish; polishing continues through fine and ultra-fine grits (typically resin-bond pads) for a reflective gloss.
**In use:** "Owner wants honed, not glossy — stop the sequence at the 200-grit pass and seal from there."
**Common misuse:** using "polished" as a generic word for "finished," rather than a specific, higher gloss level reached only by continuing the grit sequence further.

### Relative humidity (RH) testing / moisture vapor emission rate (MVER)
Quantitative measures of moisture moving through a concrete substrate — in-situ probes (ASTM F2170) for RH, anhydrous calcium chloride dome test (ASTM F1869) for MVER — used to decide whether a vapor-impermeable resin system can be installed safely.
**In use:** "RH came back at 90% at 2 in depth — we're not setting epoxy on this slab until it's mitigated or re-tested."
**Common misuse:** eyeballing a slab as "dry" by touch or a quick handheld surface moisture meter instead of running the standard probe test at the required depth and equilibration time.

### Bonded / monolithic / sand-cushion / rustic terrazzo
System types distinguished by how the terrazzo relates to the substrate: bonded (adhered to an existing surface via a bonding coat), monolithic (poured directly onto and bonded to the structural slab, no isolation layer), sand-cushion (floats on a sand/reinforcement layer for crack isolation from the substrate), rustic (aggregate exposed but left unpolished).
**In use:** "This renovation is over a slab with active cracking — we're going sand-cushion for the isolation layer, not monolithic."
**Common misuse:** using "monolithic" loosely to mean "seamless-looking" rather than the specific system in which the terrazzo bonds directly to the structural slab with no isolation layer between them.

### Lippage
A measurable height difference between adjacent surfaces at a joint, most commonly at a divider strip or panel edge.
**In use:** "We've got about 1/16 in of lippage across that strip run — regrind before it goes to final polish, not after."
**Common misuse:** using "lippage" loosely for any unevenness in general rather than the specific, measured edge-height differential it refers to.

### Densifier vs. sealer/finish
A densifier (typically lithium or sodium silicate) chemically hardens the ground cementitious surface; a sealer or finish is a separate topical or penetrating product applied afterward to control staining and set the sheen level.
**In use:** "Densify first, hold the reaction window, then seal — you can't seal over a densifier that hasn't finished reacting."
**Common misuse:** treating densifier and sealer as interchangeable or as a single combined step, skipping the reaction hold time between them.

### Terrazzo base (cove base)
A terrazzo wall base at the floor-wall transition, formed, ground, and polished to match the field — an integral element of the floor system, not a separate applied trim.
**In use:** "Cove base gets its own hand-grind pass at the wall — the walk-behind machine can't reach the corner."
**Common misuse:** assuming the base is comparable to tile or vinyl cove trim rather than a matched, integrally ground terrazzo element requiring its own grind sequence.

### Metal-bond vs. resin-bond diamond pads
Metal-bond pads are used for aggressive early coarse grinding on unopened, ungraded surfaces; resin-bond pads are used at the fine/polish end of the sequence, after the surface is already flat and exposed.
**In use:** "We're still opening the field, stay on metal-bond until we've got a full, even chip pattern showing."
**Common misuse:** switching to resin-bond pads too early, which glaze over an unopened or uneven surface instead of cutting it.

### Terrazzo mockup / sample panel
A small physical sample poured, ground, and polished to the actual specified chip blend, binder color, and gloss level, approved on-site before the full chip lot is ordered.
**In use:** "Get the mockup approved before we order the full chip lot — the architect needs to see the actual grey-white ratio at final polish, not a chart."
**Common misuse:** treating a printed chip or color chart as equivalent to a real ground-and-polished mockup, which can look substantially different once actually ground and wet-polished.
