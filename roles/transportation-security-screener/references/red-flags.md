# Transportation Security Screener — Red Flags

### AIT anomaly re-appears in the same zone after a pat-down resolution attempt
- **Usually means:** the pat-down didn't actually cover the exact ATR-flagged zone (procedural gap); second most likely is a genuine unresolved item (insulin pump, ostomy bag, prosthetic) that needs an alternate resolution method, not a repeat pat-down.
- **First question:** did the pat-down cover the precise ATR-flagged zone, or the general area around it?
- **Data to pull:** the ATR alarm-zone diagram against the logged pat-down coverage for this screening.

### ETD swab positive with no corroborating alarm from AIT, WTMD, or X-ray
- **Usually means:** a false positive from a common substance (nitroglycerin medication, fertilizer residue, glycerin-based lotion); second most likely is trace contamination from unrelated prior contact.
- **First question:** does the passenger have a documented medical reason for a nitrate-based substance?
- **Data to pull:** the retest swab result, cross-checked against the station's known false-positive substance list.

### Carry-on X-ray anomaly stays ambiguous after a re-scan from a second angle
- **Usually means:** object orientation genuinely obscures a benign item (tightly packed electronics, dense food); less commonly, deliberate stacking or wrapping to defeat single-angle imaging.
- **First question:** does the anomaly's shape or position match a known concealment method, or is it consistent with ordinary dense contents?
- **Data to pull:** none beyond a physical open-bag inspection — by definition, imaging alone hasn't resolved this signal.

### Passenger's stated bag contents don't match what the physical search finds
- **Usually means:** genuine forgetfulness about an item packed by someone else or days earlier (common, especially for restricted-item ounce limits most travelers don't track); occasionally deliberate misdirection.
- **First question:** is the mismatch about an incidental item, or one that's independently prohibited or significant on its own?
- **Data to pull:** none beyond the search itself, unless the same traveler has a mismatch logged on a prior screening at this station.

### Two or more independent alarms, different modalities, same passenger, same screening
- **Usually means:** statistically less likely to be coincidence than a single alarm; treat as a compounding pattern, not two separately additive events.
- **First question:** are the alarms in unrelated zones/items, or could one plausibly explain the other?
- **Data to pull:** both alarm resolution records logged together, with supervisor notification per the station's multi-alarm protocol.

### An individual screener's TIP (Threat Image Projection) misses cluster in one threat category, 3+ times in a rolling evaluation window
- **Usually means:** fatigue or a genuine skill gap concentrated on that category (e.g., consistently missing sheet explosives but not weapons); rarely a monitor/display calibration issue.
- **First question:** are the misses concentrated on one threat category or spread evenly across categories?
- **Data to pull:** the individual's TIP score history broken out by threat-item category, not the aggregate miss rate alone.

### WTMD alarm clears on a second walk-through with nothing removed and no explanation offered
- **Usually means:** a marginal metal item that registered inconsistently, or a sensitivity fluctuation; SOP treats this as unresolved regardless of the second clear, since intermittent alarms don't self-resolve.
- **First question:** was a full pat-down completed on the first alarm, or did the lane rely on the unalarmed second pass?
- **Data to pull:** the lane's WTMD calibration/certification log, and confirmation of whether pat-down-on-first-alarm was actually performed.

### Passenger declines private screening but resists a specific pat-down zone at the point of contact
- **Usually means:** a legitimate pain, injury, or sensitivity issue (common); rarely a deliberate attempt to avoid detection at that zone.
- **First question:** can the passenger identify a specific reason, and does a documented alternate resolution method apply?
- **Data to pull:** the station's reasonable-accommodation procedure for the condition described.

### A prohibited item is found alongside a bag-contents declaration that doesn't match, at a station running above its per-lane throughput target
- **Usually means:** the declaration mismatch is unrelated unfamiliarity with current rules far more often than deception; the throughput pressure is what makes the combination worth flagging, since it's exactly when a resolution shortcut is tempting.
- **First question:** does station protocol require supervisor notification for this item class regardless of queue pressure?
- **Data to pull:** the lane's wait time or queue length logged at the time of the find, to confirm full resolution was still followed despite the pressure.

### A restricted item is just under its published threshold (e.g., a liquid container labeled exactly 3.4 oz, a spray canister labeled exactly 4 fl oz)
- **Usually means:** genuine compliant packaging far more often than deliberate near-threshold gaming; manufacturers routinely package to the published limit.
- **First question:** does the container's actual fill level match the printed label, or does it appear over-filled relative to the stated volume?
- **Data to pull:** none beyond visual/volume confirmation — this is a low-severity signal on its own and rarely warrants escalation absent another alarm.
