# Transportation Security Screener — Vocabulary

### AIT (Advanced Imaging Technology)
The millimeter-wave body scanner used at primary screening; displays a generic outline with a marked anomaly location, not an image of the passenger's body.
**In use:** "AIT flagged a single anomaly, left ankle — targeted pat-down only, not full-body."
**Common misuse:** Assuming AIT shows the operator an actual body image; every deployed unit uses the ATR generic-outline privacy algorithm, which only marks location, not appearance.

### ATR (Automated Target Recognition)
The software layer on AIT that generates the generic outline and marks the anomaly's location with a highlighted zone.
**In use:** "ATR put the box on the ankle, not the whole leg — pat-down stays targeted to that zone."
**Common misuse:** Treating the ATR marker as a diagnosis of what the object is; it only indicates location, not identity or threat level.

### WTMD (Walk-Through Metal Detector)
The primary metal-detection modality at the checkpoint, calibrated to a certified sensitivity threshold.
**In use:** "WTMD alarmed twice with nothing removed on the second pass — that's still an unresolved intermittent alarm."
**Common misuse:** Treating a second unalarmed walk-through as resolution of the first alarm.

### ETD (Explosives Trace Detection)
A swab-and-analyzer method that detects trace chemical residue consistent with explosives.
**In use:** "ETD came back positive with no corroborating alarm elsewhere — check the false-positive list before escalating."
**Common misuse:** Treating a single positive ETD result as confirmed explosive presence rather than a trace chemical hit that needs a retest and context.

### TIP (Threat Image Projection)
A system that randomly inserts fake threat images into the live X-ray feed to measure and train each screener's detection rate by threat category.
**In use:** "His TIP score dropped this month — misses concentrated on sheet explosives, so he's back in that training module."
**Common misuse:** Treating TIP score as a general productivity or speed metric rather than a calibrated detection-accuracy measure.

### Sterile area
The security-controlled part of the airport beyond the checkpoint, where every person and item has passed screening.
**In use:** "The item never entered the sterile area — passenger routed it back to checked baggage before boarding."
**Common misuse:** Using "sterile area" interchangeably with the SIDA (Secured Identification Display Area, the airfield/ramp side) — a different zone with different access rules entirely.

### SSI (Sensitive Security Information)
A federal information-protection category covering TSA SOPs, alarm-resolution sequencing, and calibration detail — restricted by regulation, though not classified.
**In use:** "I can't detail the exact resolution sequence for that alarm type — that's SSI."
**Common misuse:** Assuming SSI material is publicly available because it isn't marked "classified"; it's legally restricted from public release under separate authority.

### Prohibited item vs. restricted item
The distinction between items barred entirely from the sterile area (most knives, firearms in carry-on) and items allowed only under specific conditions (liquids ≤3.4 oz, self-defense spray ≤4 fl oz checked-bag only).
**In use:** "The spray isn't prohibited outright — it's restricted to checked baggage under 4 ounces; hers is 6, so it's fully prohibited."
**Common misuse:** Treating every disallowed carry-on item as fully banned from air travel, when many just require checked-bag routing instead of surrender.

### SPOT (Screening of Passengers by Observation Techniques)
A former TSA program using trained officers to flag behavioral indicators for closer screening; its evidentiary basis was found insufficient by GAO in 2013.
**In use:** "The behavioral flag routes him to closer screening — it's not grounds on its own to deny him access."
**Common misuse:** Treating a behavioral flag as equivalent in reliability to a physical or alarm-based finding.

### Alarm resolution vs. alarm explanation
The distinction between physically confirming the cause of an alarm (resolution) and accepting the passenger's stated account of the cause (explanation).
**In use:** "He explained it was his belt — resolution still means the pat-down proceeds either way."
**Common misuse:** Stopping at the explanation because it sounds plausible, without completing the physical procedure.

### Layers of security
The aviation-security model in which checkpoint screening is one of several independent layers (intelligence, PreCheck-style vetting, canine teams, federal air marshals, hardened cockpit doors), each covering gaps the others miss.
**In use:** "Checkpoint screening isn't the only layer stopping a threat — that's why one covert-test miss at the checkpoint doesn't mean the whole system failed."
**Common misuse:** Treating checkpoint screening as the sole line of defense, which both overstates what a single test result proves about total system failure and understates how much rides on getting the checkpoint layer right, since it's the layer exercised on every traveler.

### Risk-based security
The philosophy of allocating more scrutiny to less-known travelers and less to vetted ones (e.g., expedited screening for pre-vetted programs), rather than applying uniform maximum scrutiny to everyone.
**In use:** "She's in the pre-vetted program, so she's routed to the expedited lane — that's risk-based allocation working as designed, not a shortcut."
**Common misuse:** Assuming risk-based security means lower standards across the board, rather than scrutiny reallocated toward higher-uncertainty travelers.
