# Interviewer — Vocabulary

### Screener
The initial set of questions that determines whether a respondent qualifies for the full study, before the substantive questionnaire begins.
**In use:** "She passed the screener on age and region but got disqualified on the product-usage question."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a formality to get through quickly — a poorly-administered screener (rushed, leading) lets in respondents who don't actually qualify, contaminating the sample before the real questionnaire even starts.

### Skip pattern
The programmed or scripted logic that routes a respondent to different follow-up questions based on an earlier answer, so respondents don't get asked questions that don't apply to them.
**In use:** "The skip pattern sends non-owners straight to Q22, past the whole ownership-experience battery."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to be self-evident from context, so an interviewer manually skips a question they think doesn't apply — the actual programmed skip logic may differ from an interviewer's intuition about what "obviously" applies.

### Straight-lining
Selecting the same response option down an entire grid or battery of rating-scale questions, regardless of each item's actual content — a data-quality red flag, not a valid response pattern.
**In use:** "Case 412 straight-lined the whole 15-item satisfaction battery at 'somewhat agree' — flagging for review."
**Common misuse:** Confused with a respondent who genuinely feels the same way about every item — occasionally true, but common enough as a satisficing signal that it's checked, not assumed benign.

### Break-off
An interview that starts but terminates before completion, as distinct from a refusal (which never starts) or a completion (which finishes).
**In use:** "We had 40 completes and 6 break-offs this week, three of which broke off at the same question."
**Common misuse:** Lumped together with refusals in casual reporting — the two have different causes and different fixes; a refusal problem is a recruiting issue, a break-off problem is often a questionnaire-design issue.

### Quota cell
One specific combination of sampling characteristics (e.g. "female, age 35-54, Northeast region") with its own target completion count, used to ensure the achieved sample matches a desired demographic distribution.
**In use:** "Cell 4 is 8 respondents short with two field days left — we need to escalate."
**Common misuse:** Treated as flexible once the overall sample size target is met — filling total N without hitting individual cell targets defeats the purpose of quota sampling, which is controlling the sample's composition, not just its size.

### Disposition code
The categorical outcome assigned to each contact attempt (complete, refusal, break-off, non-working number, language barrier, etc.), used to calculate response rates and diagnose field-period problems.
**In use:** "Pull the disposition-code breakdown before we decide whether to extend the field period."
**Common misuse:** Coded loosely ("not answered" used for both a genuine no-answer and an explicit refusal) — imprecise disposition coding makes response-rate calculations and problem diagnosis unreliable.

### Standardized interviewing
The practice of administering every interview with identical wording, tone, and probing technique, so that response differences reflect true differences between respondents rather than differences in how the question was asked.
**In use:** "Standardized interviewing means reading the clarification exactly as scripted, even if you could explain it more clearly in your own words."
**Common misuse:** Mistaken for a suggestion or best practice rather than the methodological foundation the survey's validity depends on — "clearer" interviewer-invented wording is a bias source, not an improvement.

### Interviewer effect
Systematic variation in survey responses attributable to who conducted the interview (tone, wording deviation, respondent's perception of the interviewer) rather than to true differences in respondent opinion.
**In use:** "We're seeing an interviewer effect — completion rates and response distributions differ more between our interviewers than they should."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to be about interviewer identity/demographics affecting responses (a real but separate phenomenon) rather than about script-adherence variance, which is the day-to-day version this role controls directly.

### Respondent fatigue
Declining engagement and response quality over the course of a long interview, most visible in later questions or batteries.
**In use:** "The straight-lining starts around Q40 — that's consistent with respondent fatigue in a 45-minute survey."
**Common misuse:** Used as a blanket excuse for any late-interview data-quality issue without checking whether the pattern actually correlates with question position, versus being present from the start (which points to a different cause — respondent disengagement or interviewer rushing).

### Field period
The defined window of calendar time during which interviewing for a study takes place, after which no more completes count toward the study.
**In use:** "We're on day 7 of a 10-day field period and two cells are still open."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a soft deadline that can quietly slip — field-period length is often tied to downstream analysis timelines and client deliverable dates, so an unplanned extension has real costs beyond just more calling hours.

### Incidence rate
The proportion of the general population (or sample frame) that qualifies for a study after screening — a low-incidence study requires many more contact attempts per completed interview.
**In use:** "This is a 4% incidence study, so expect roughly 25 screening calls per qualified completion."
**Common misuse:** Confused with the response rate (the proportion of qualified, contacted respondents who complete) — incidence is about how rare the target population is; response rate is about how willing qualified people are to participate. A study can have high incidence and low response rate, or the reverse, and the field strategy differs for each.
