# Vocabulary

Terms generalists flatten together that a land surveyor keeps sharply separate — the value is in the misuse, not the definition.

## Retracement survey vs. original survey

An **original survey** creates a new boundary where none existed (e.g., a first-time PLSS section subdivision, or a new subdivision plat). A **retracement survey** re-locates a boundary that was already created, using the original survey's evidence — it does not get to redesign where the line "should" be.

**In use:** "This isn't an original survey — we're retracing the 1955 plat, so the found monuments control, not a fresh set of measurements."

**Common misuse:** treating a retracement like an original survey and computing a "best fit" position from measurements alone, ignoring the monument evidence the retracement exists to find.

## Senior rights vs. junior rights

When two surveys of adjoining land conflict, the parcel conveyed **first in time** holds **senior rights** to its full described boundary, even if that means the **junior** (later-conveyed) parcel's description has to yield — the junior deed is presumed to have been written with knowledge of the senior boundary already in place.

**In use:** "The 1962 parcel has senior rights on this line — the 1978 deed's call for '200 feet' yields to wherever the 1962 boundary actually falls."

**Common misuse:** assuming the most recent or most detailed survey automatically controls, rather than checking which conveyance came first in time.

## Aliquot part (PLSS)

A fractional subdivision of a Public Land Survey System section described by repeated halving (e.g., the NE¼ of the SW¼ of Section 14) rather than by metes and bounds — the boundaries are defined by proportional measurement between section corners, not by an independent survey of that specific line.

**In use:** "It's described as an aliquot part, so the boundary comes from proportioning between the section corners, not from an independent metes-and-bounds call."

**Common misuse:** treating an aliquot description as if it names fixed distances, when the actual boundary is whatever proportion of the (possibly irregular) section the aliquot fraction produces.

## Single proportion vs. double proportion

**Single proportion** restores a lost corner using record distances along one line only, between the two nearest found corners — used only for outer-boundary corners. **Double proportion** uses record distances on both the line and the intersecting line, between four found controlling corners — required for interior corners.

**In use:** "That's an interior quarter corner, not an outer boundary point — it needs double proportion, using both lines."

**Common misuse:** defaulting to single proportion for convenience on an interior corner, discarding half the available evidence.

## Metes and bounds vs. PLSS description

A **metes and bounds** description defines a boundary by a specific sequence of bearings and distances from a point of beginning, independent of any government survey grid. A **PLSS description** locates land by reference to the township/range/section grid and aliquot parts — its boundaries derive from that grid's monuments, not from an independently stated bearing chain.

**In use:** "The deed reads like metes and bounds, but check whether it's actually tied to the section corner — if so, the PLSS grid controls the starting point, not the stated point of beginning alone."

**Common misuse:** treating every legal description as self-contained metes and bounds, missing that a PLSS tie makes the grid's monuments part of the controlling evidence.

## Positional uncertainty vs. precision ratio

**Positional uncertainty** (ALTA/NSPS) is a per-corner allowable error budget (0.07 ft + 50 ppm of distance to the nearest related monument), checked corner by corner. **Precision ratio** is a whole-traverse figure (perimeter divided by linear misclosure), checked once for the entire closed figure. A survey can pass one and fail the other.

**In use:** "Positional uncertainty checks out at every corner, but the overall precision ratio is only 1:8,000 — that course still needs re-observation."

**Common misuse:** treating a passing positional-uncertainty check as proof the whole traverse meets its precision-ratio standard, or vice versa.

## Angular misclosure vs. linear misclosure

**Angular misclosure** is the difference between the sum of measured interior angles and the theoretical sum for a polygon with that many sides. **Linear misclosure** is the vector distance the traverse fails to close by after angles and distances are both used to compute latitudes and departures — a separate check that can fail even when angular closure passes cleanly.

**In use:** "Angular closure was clean at 8 seconds, but linear closure only came out to 1:6,500 — the distances, not the angles, are the problem here."

**Common misuse:** treating angular closure alone as sufficient quality assurance and skipping the linear closure computation.

## Compass Rule (Bowditch) adjustment

A method of distributing a traverse's linear misclosure across each course's latitude and departure, proportional to that course's length relative to the perimeter — it forces mathematical closure but does not identify which course actually carried the error.

**In use:** "Compass Rule will force this to close on paper, but it won't tell us which course was actually wrong — that's a separate diagnosis."

**Common misuse:** treating a completed Compass Rule adjustment as evidence the survey is correct, rather than as bookkeeping that masks where the real error is.

## Monument vs. corner

A **monument** is the physical object set or found marking a position (a pipe, rebar, stone, or concrete marker). A **corner** is the legal point in space the monument is evidence of — a monument can be moved, destroyed, or replaced while the corner (the legal point) persists, which is exactly why proportionate measurement and monument-hierarchy rules exist.

**In use:** "The monument's gone, but the corner still exists in law — we're restoring its position by proportion, not creating a new corner."

**Common misuse:** using "monument" and "corner" interchangeably, obscuring that a corner's legal position can outlive the physical object marking it.

## Occupation line

A physical line of use — a fence, hedge, cultivated edge, or driveway — that may or may not coincide with the record boundary; itself the primary evidence considered in unwritten-rights doctrines like acquiescence and adverse possession.

**In use:** "The occupation line has run along this fence for over thirty years — that's the fact pattern to hand to the client's attorney, not something to plat over."

**Common misuse:** conflating an occupation line with the legal boundary, either by assuming it's automatically the true line or by ignoring it entirely because it doesn't match the record.

## Unwritten rights (acquiescence, agreement, estoppel, adverse possession)

A family of distinct, state-law doctrines under which a boundary's legal location can shift away from the record description based on parties' conduct over time — **acquiescence** (mutual recognition of a line over a statutory period), **agreement** (an express boundary settlement, sometimes unrecorded), **estoppel** (one party's conduct induced reasonable reliance by another), and **adverse possession** (hostile, open, continuous possession meeting statutory elements). Each has different elements and a different statutory period by state.

**In use:** "This could be acquiescence or adverse possession depending on the neighbors' actual conduct — that's a legal determination, not something the survey itself resolves."

**Common misuse:** treating "unwritten rights" as one doctrine with one test, or treating the surveyor's identification of the evidence as a legal conclusion about who wins.

## Table A item (ALTA/NSPS)

One of the numbered optional certification items (1 through 21 in the 2021 standard) a client can request beyond the mandatory minimum survey — each item has its own required source and scope, and an uncontracted item cannot be certified.

**In use:** "Client didn't contract for Item 11 utilities — don't certify utility locations even if some are visible in the field."

**Common misuse:** certifying an item that wasn't contracted for, or certifying a contracted item from an insufficient source.

## Record vs. measured (bearing/distance)

**Record** values are what the governing deed or plat states. **Measured** values are what the current survey's instruments actually observed. The two are reported separately on a retracement plat, and a discrepancy between them is itself a finding — not an error to silently resolve to one or the other.

**In use:** "Record calls 200.00 feet; measured comes out to 200.14 — both go on the plat, with the discrepancy noted, not just the number we like better."

**Common misuse:** reporting only the measured value, or only the record value, and discarding the comparison that a future retracement will need.

## Corner record (monument record)

A filed document — required by some states, optional in others — describing the evidence found or set at a specific corner: monument type, condition, ties to reference points, and the surveyor who filed it. Distinct from the plat itself, which shows the whole parcel.

**In use:** "File a corner record on that restored section corner — the next surveyor who comes looking for it in twenty years needs to know what we actually found and how we restored it."

**Common misuse:** treating the plat as a sufficient permanent record of monument evidence, when a corner record is what specifically survives for the next retracement.

## State plane coordinate system vs. geodetic control

A **state plane coordinate system** is a localized, low-distortion projection used for practical boundary and construction coordinates within a state or zone. **Geodetic control** is the underlying national reference framework (the National Spatial Reference System) that state plane coordinates are ultimately tied back to — establishing and densifying that framework is the geodetic surveyor's specialty, not the land surveyor's; the land surveyor consumes it as a tie-in.

**In use:** "Tie the boundary corners to the nearest state plane control monument — we're using the framework, not establishing it."

**Common misuse:** treating "geodetic" and "state plane" as synonyms, or assuming a boundary retracement requires the same order of control-network precision a geodetic densification project does.
