# Fashion Designer — Vocabulary

### Marker efficiency
The percentage of fabric actually used by nested pattern pieces versus total fabric consumed, after accounting for the layout ("marker") needed to cut them.
**In use:** "This marker's only running 74% — the curved yoke pieces are eating a lot of waste, let's see if squaring them costs us anything on fit."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a fixed property of a fabric or garment type rather than something that changes with pattern-piece shape and can often be improved without a fit tradeoff.

### Tech pack
The factory-facing document specifying construction, measurements, materials, and tolerances for a style.
**In use:** "The tech pack doesn't have a tolerance column on the sleeve length — send it back before it goes to the factory."
**Common misuse:** Confused with a line sheet (buyer-facing sales document) — a tech pack is a manufacturing instruction set, not a marketing artifact.

### Grade rule
The fixed measurement increment applied to a base pattern at each size step to generate the full size run.
**In use:** "The grade rule on chest is +1.5 inches per size — check the XXL against that before we cut."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to be linear and uniform across all measurement points and all size ranges, when plus-size and petite grading often use different increments than the core size range.

### Keystone (pricing)
A pricing convention where retail price is roughly double wholesale price (and wholesale is a multiple of COGS), used as a fast sanity check on viability.
**In use:** "At $42 wholesale and $14.73 COGS we're at 2.85x — comfortably past keystone, there's room."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a fixed rule that applies identically across all channels — luxury, direct-to-consumer, and off-price models routinely use different multiples.

### Sell-through
The percentage of units received by a retailer (or produced by a brand) that actually sell within a defined period, typically at full price.
**In use:** "That wrap dress is only pacing at 41% sell-through against a 55% line floor — it's a cut candidate for next season."
**Common misuse:** Confused with sales volume — a style can sell a large absolute quantity while still having weak sell-through if it was overordered.

### Tolerance (garment measurement)
The acceptable range around a spec measurement within which a sample or bulk garment is considered a pass.
**In use:** "It's a quarter-inch over spec on the chest, but that's within our ±0.375 tolerance — approve it."
**Common misuse:** Ignored entirely, treating every spec number as an exact target and rejecting samples that are commercially within an acceptable range.

### CMT (Cut, Make, Trim)
A contract-manufacturing arrangement where the factory cuts, sews, and trims a garment from fabric and materials the brand supplies or specifies, as distinct from full-package production.
**In use:** "This is a CMT quote, so our fabric cost is separate from the $4.10 labor line."
**Common misuse:** Conflated with full-package (FP) manufacturing, where the factory also sources fabric/trim — the cost structure and who bears fabric-price risk differ materially between the two.

### Open-to-buy
The dollar amount a retailer has budgeted to purchase inventory in a given period, which caps how many styles/units a line can realistically place.
**In use:** "We can't add three more styles to the line — the buyer's open-to-buy for this category is basically flat versus last season."
**Common misuse:** Treated as the designer's problem to ignore, when line-planning decisions (SKU count, price points) are directly bounded by what buyers can actually spend.

### Line plan
The full set of styles, colorways, and price points a brand is developing and presenting for a given season.
**In use:** "The line plan's got 34 styles across 3 price tiers — merchandising wants that down to 28."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a simple list rather than a portfolio-allocation decision balancing sampling budget, buyer attention, and price-tier coverage.

### Proto (prototype/first sample)
The first physical sample made from a pattern, used to check fit, construction, and fabric behavior before further sample rounds.
**In use:** "The proto came back tight through the shoulder — that's a pattern issue, not a factory cutting issue."
**Common misuse:** Confused with the final production sample (a later-stage sample confirming bulk-ready construction) — a proto is an early fit/design check, not a pre-production signoff.

### Fabric consumption
The actual yardage or area of fabric required to produce one garment, accounting for marker efficiency, as distinct from the raw pattern-piece area.
**In use:** "Pattern area is 1.85 square yards, but at 74% marker efficiency our fabric consumption comes out to 1.53 yards per unit."
**Common misuse:** Estimated from pattern-piece area alone without applying marker efficiency, understating real fabric cost.

### Cost-plus pricing
A pricing method that sets wholesale (or retail) price as a multiple of confirmed cost-of-goods, as opposed to pricing based on perceived value or competitor benchmarking.
**In use:** "We're using cost-plus here — 2.5x on COGS gets us to the $42 wholesale target."
**Common misuse:** Applied using an unconfirmed sample-quantity cost rather than a bulk quote, producing a wholesale price that doesn't hold once real production costs come in.
