# Photographer — Vocabulary

### Exposure triangle
The three interdependent controls of exposure — aperture, shutter speed, ISO — where adjusting one requires a compensating adjustment in another to maintain the same overall exposure.
**In use:** "I opened the aperture a stop for shallower depth of field, so I dropped the ISO a stop to compensate — same exposure, different tradeoff."
**Common misuse:** Treated as three independent settings to "get right" individually, rather than a single tradeoff system where every gain (faster shutter, wider aperture) costs something specific elsewhere.

### Reciprocity
The principle that equivalent exposure can be achieved through different combinations of aperture, shutter, and ISO, as long as the total light captured is the same.
**In use:** "f/8 at 1/250 and f/5.6 at 1/500 are reciprocal — same exposure, different depth-of-field and motion-freeze tradeoff."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to mean the settings produce identical images; they produce identical exposure (brightness), not identical depth of field or motion rendering.

### Key light / Fill light / Lighting ratio
The key light is the primary light source shaping the subject; the fill light softens the shadows it creates. The lighting ratio is the measured brightness difference between key and fill, expressed as a ratio (e.g., 4:1).
**In use:** "Bring the fill up half a stop — we're at 8:1 and the brief wants something closer to 4:1."
**Common misuse:** "Good lighting" described only in adjectives (moody, soft, dramatic) without the measured ratio behind it, which makes the setup non-reproducible on a reshoot or by a second photographer.

### Rights-managed (RM) vs. Royalty-free (RF)
Rights-managed licensing scopes usage to a specific medium, duration, and geography, priced accordingly; royalty-free licensing sells broader, less-restricted usage rights for a flatter one-time fee, typically at lower per-use value.
**In use:** "This is a rights-managed license — six months, U.S. only. If the client wants ongoing global use, that's a different (and higher) fee, not an extension of this one."
**Common misuse:** Conflated with "free to use however" — royalty-free still has license terms (attribution, no resale, no exclusive claims); it isn't unlimited-rights ownership.

### Work-for-hire
A contractual arrangement in which the commissioning client, not the photographer, owns the copyright to the resulting images outright.
**In use:** "This is work-for-hire, so the brand owns the copyright — that's priced at a premium over a standard licensed usage fee, because I'm not retaining any future licensing revenue on these images."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to be the default arrangement for any paid shoot; absent an explicit work-for-hire agreement, the photographer typically retains copyright and licenses usage rather than transferring ownership.

### Usage scope
The specific medium, duration, geography, and exclusivity terms under which licensed images may be used — the basis for usage-fee pricing.
**In use:** "Before I quote a number, I need the usage scope: is this digital-only or print too, how long does it run, and is it exclusive to your category?"
**Common misuse:** Left unspecified at the time of quoting, which leads to a fee that doesn't match the client's actual (often broader) intended use once the campaign launches.

### Model release / Property release
A signed document granting permission to use an identifiable person's (model release) or privately owned property's (property release) likeness in commercially distributed images.
**In use:** "We can't license this shot commercially without a signed model release for everyone recognizable in frame, including the background extras."
**Common misuse:** Assumed unnecessary for editorial or "just social media" use; commercial licensing intent, not the publication venue, is what typically triggers the release requirement.

### Coverage
The practice of capturing multiple angles, exposures, or takes of a single moment to guard against any one frame being technically unusable.
**In use:** "We're light on coverage for the ring exchange — only one frame, and if that one's soft focus we have nothing."
**Common misuse:** Confused with simply shooting a high volume of frames; coverage specifically means redundancy on high-risk, irreplaceable moments, not overall frame count.

### Sunny 16 rule
A baseline exposure guideline for full-sun daylight: aperture f/16, shutter speed approximately the reciprocal of the ISO (e.g., 1/100 at ISO 100).
**In use:** "No light meter on hand, but it's full sun — Sunny 16 gets me close enough to dial in from there."
**Common misuse:** Applied in mixed, shaded, or artificial lighting, where the rule's core assumption (direct unobstructed sunlight) doesn't hold and the resulting exposure will be wrong.

### Histogram
A graph showing the distribution of tonal values (shadows to highlights) in a captured image, used to verify exposure independent of screen brightness.
**In use:** "The LCD looked fine in the shade, but the histogram showed the highlights were clipped — checked it before moving on."
**Common misuse:** Ignored in favor of the rear-screen preview, which is unreliable across varying ambient light conditions (a screen looks darker in bright sun, brighter in a dim room, regardless of actual exposure).

### Dynamic range
The span between the darkest recoverable shadow detail and the brightest recoverable highlight detail a sensor can capture in a single exposure.
**In use:** "This scene's dynamic range exceeds the sensor's capacity — either bracket and blend, or decide which end (shadow or highlight) to sacrifice."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to be unlimited or a purely post-processing problem; a scene that exceeds the sensor's dynamic range loses real information at capture that no amount of editing recovers.

### Non-destructive editing
An editing workflow (typically RAW-based, with adjustment layers or metadata-only edits) that preserves the original file data, keeping every adjustment reversible until final export.
**In use:** "Keep this in RAW with layered adjustments — don't flatten and export until the client's confirmed the final look."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to apply automatically just because a RAW file was shot; if edits are baked in and the original overwritten, the workflow is no longer non-destructive regardless of the source format.

### Color grading vs. color correction
Color correction fixes technical inaccuracies (white balance, exposure) to make an image look neutrally accurate; color grading is a deliberate stylistic choice applied on top of a corrected base.
**In use:** "Correct the white balance first — get it neutral — then we'll grade for the warm, filmic look the brand wants."
**Common misuse:** Used interchangeably; skipping correction and grading directly on top of an inaccurate base compounds the original technical error into the stylistic result.

### Bracketing
Capturing the same composition at multiple exposure settings (e.g., -1, 0, +1 stop) to guarantee at least one usable exposure or to blend a wider dynamic range.
**In use:** "High-contrast scene, so I bracketed three stops — we'll pick the best single frame or blend for the full range in post."
**Common misuse:** Skipped on ambiguous-lighting shots to save time, which removes the safety margin exactly when it's most needed.

### Contact sheet / Culling
A contact sheet is a compiled overview of all captured frames from a shoot; culling is the process of narrowing that full set down to the selects worth further editing.
**In use:** "First cull pass is purely technical — sharp focus, no blink, no clipped highlights — before we even look at composition."
**Common misuse:** Culling done in a single pass mixing technical and aesthetic judgment simultaneously, which is slower and less consistent than separating "is this frame usable" from "is this the best frame."
