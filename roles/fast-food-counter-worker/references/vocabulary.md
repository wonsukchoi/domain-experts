# Vocabulary

Terms generalists misuse or flatten together, kept sharply separate by a front-counter practitioner.

### Read-back

The practice of stating a guest's full order — item, size, and every modifier — back to them verbally before it's sent to the kitchen, distinct from simply repeating the total price.
**In use:** "I did the read-back on that one, she confirmed no onions before it ever hit the kitchen display."
**Common misuse:** Treating "repeating the total" or "the kiosk screen shows it" as equivalent to a read-back — both skip the actual confirmation of item-level modifiers, which is where entry errors actually live.

### Order accuracy

The percentage of orders that leave the register or window matching exactly what the guest ordered, tracked separately from speed-of-service and from food-quality complaints.
**In use:** "Accuracy's at 94% this shift — that's the number that actually predicts next week's complaints, not how fast the line moved."
**Common misuse:** Conflating a fast shift with an accurate one; a station can hit every speed target while quietly failing on modifiers that don't show up until the guest is already gone.

### Order-to-window / order-to-bag time

The measured interval from order placement to handoff at the drive-thru window or counter, distinct from kitchen production time (which is the cook's number) and from the order-taking phase alone.
**In use:** "Order-to-window's at 210 seconds — check whether that's order-taking or the handoff step before you touch staffing."
**Common misuse:** Attributing a rising order-to-window number automatically to kitchen slowness, when the delay is frequently in order-taking or payment instead.

### Attach rate

The share of orders that include at least one prompted add-on (upsize, combo conversion, dessert) from the suggestive-sell script, tracked per register login over a shift.
**In use:** "Attach rate's at 32% today, right around target — no need to push a second prompt to chase it higher."
**Common misuse:** Treating attach rate as a number to maximize without limit, rather than a target band; chasing it past one prompt per order slows the line for a return that doesn't offset the delay.

### Authorization ceiling

The maximum dollar value of a comp, refund, or remake a front-counter worker can approve using their own login, above which a manager-on-duty's approval is required.
**In use:** "That refund's above my ceiling — I need the MOD to key that one in, not me."
**Common misuse:** Assuming "I'm the one dealing with the guest" grants authority to approve anything needed to end the complaint, regardless of the store's stated ceiling.

### Modifier

A change to a standard menu item entered as a discrete, kitchen-display-readable line (no pickles, extra sauce, substitute side), distinct from a free-text note that a kitchen display may not parse or display consistently.
**In use:** "Enter that as a modifier line, not a note — the note field doesn't always show up on the kitchen screen."
**Common misuse:** Typing a substitution into an open comment field because it's faster, when only a structured modifier reliably reaches whoever is building the order.

### Allergen deferral

The act of declining to answer an allergen question from memory and instead checking a reference card/app or referring the guest to a manager.
**In use:** "That's a deferral, not a guess — I told her I'd check before answering the peanut-oil question."
**Common misuse:** Treating a confident-sounding answer given without checking the card as acceptable because it turned out to be correct; the process failure is the same regardless of the outcome.

### Big 9 (major allergens)

The nine allergens with mandatory disclosure under U.S. federal food-labeling law: milk, eggs, fish, crustacean shellfish, tree nuts, peanuts, wheat, soybeans, and sesame.
**In use:** "Sesame's one of the Big 9 now — that bun needs the same disclosure treatment as the peanut items."
**Common misuse:** Assuming the allergen list is unchanged from an older training cycle; sesame was added under the FASTER Act effective January 2023 and some staff still work from an outdated list of eight.

### Bank (drawer starting cash)

The fixed cash amount a register or a worker's assigned drawer starts a shift with, counted in before the first sale and counted out at handoff against the same figure.
**In use:** "Bank's $150 — count it before you log in, not after the first transaction rings."
**Common misuse:** Confusing a bank-count discrepancy found at shift start with a sales variance from that shift's transactions; they're different problems caught at different points.

### Standard remedy

The pre-authorized fix for a routine order-error complaint (remake the item, replace a missing component, refund the specific item) that a front-counter worker can apply without escalation, distinct from a negotiated or improvised resolution.
**In use:** "Standard remedy on that one — missing sauce, handed her a cup, no ticket change needed."
**Common misuse:** Improvising a bigger give (a free combo, a gift card) to placate a guest instead of applying the standard remedy first and only escalating if it's declined.

### Speed-of-service (SOS)

The full measured interval a guest experiences from order start to receiving their food, distinct from order-to-window time (which is the drive-thru-specific version) and from kitchen ticket time (production only).
**In use:** "SOS looks bad chain-wide this week, but check whether it's an order-taking problem or a kitchen one before assuming it's on us."
**Common misuse:** Using SOS and order-to-window interchangeably; SOS is the broader guest-facing metric and can include steps (menu-board legibility, payment friction) the counter worker doesn't fully control alone.

### Kiosk-assist

A front-counter task where the worker corrects or completes a guest's self-service kiosk order rather than taking it directly, still requiring the same read-back discipline before it's sent.
**In use:** "She flagged me for kiosk-assist — guest couldn't find the modifier screen, so I confirmed it verbally before submitting."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a kiosk order needs no human confirmation because the guest entered it themselves; the same modifier-entry failure mode applies regardless of who typed it.

### No-call escalation vs. standard escalation

A "standard escalation" is calling the MOD because a remedy was declined or exceeds the worker's ceiling; escalation is mandatory and immediate, regardless of ceiling or guest tone, whenever illness, injury, or a foreign object is mentioned.
**In use:** "That's not a standard escalation, she said she got sick — that goes straight to the MOD, ceiling doesn't matter here."
**Common misuse:** Applying the same wait-and-see judgment used for a routine complaint to an illness or injury mention, when the content of the complaint — not its size — is what triggers immediate escalation.
