# Funeral home management working vocabulary

Terms a funeral home manager uses precisely and a generalist blurs together. Format: definition → how a practitioner says it → common misuse.

**General Price List (GPL)** — The itemized price disclosure document required by the FTC Funeral Rule (16 CFR 453), which must be presented before any discussion of merchandise or arrangements, and disclosed over the phone if requested.
In use: "We hand the GPL over first, every time, before we even start talking about casket options."
Misuse: treating the GPL as a reference document available "if a family asks" rather than something proactively presented before the arrangement conversation begins — the rule requires it up front, not on request.

**Cash-advance item** — A good or service the funeral home purchases from a third party on the family's behalf (flowers, obituary notices, officiant fees, death certificates) and passes the cost through, with any markup required to be disclosed.
In use: "The floral markup needs a disclosure line on the GPL — right now we're billing $315 against a $274 vendor cost with nothing stated."
Misuse: assuming a cash-advance item can carry an undisclosed markup as long as the total is "reasonable" — the FTC rule requires disclosure of the markup's existence, not just a defensible price.

**Guaranteed vs. non-guaranteed preneed contract** — A guaranteed contract locks the price for services/merchandise regardless of cost increases between signing and time of need, with the funeral home (or its funding vehicle) absorbing the difference. A non-guaranteed contract leaves the family responsible for any price increase at time of need.
In use: "This trust doesn't support a guarantee under state law — we tell the family plainly it's non-guaranteed, not soften the language."
Misuse: using "guaranteed" loosely in a sales conversation without confirming the underlying funding vehicle and state statute actually support a price lock.

**Trust-funded vs. insurance-funded preneed** — Trust-funded preneed places the family's payment into a state-regulated trust account, invested and reconciled against future cost; insurance-funded preneed assigns a life insurance policy's death benefit to cover the funeral cost.
In use: "The trust's investment performance has lagged the GPL price increases for that package — we need to reconcile before quoting a guarantee off it."
Misuse: treating the two funding mechanisms as interchangeable when their risk profile differs — trust performance is investment-market-dependent, insurance-assignment value is fixed at the policy's face amount.

**Statement of Funeral Goods and Services Selected** — The itemized, signed document recording exactly what the family selected and at what price, required by most state funeral boards as the arrangement conference's binding record.
In use: "Every selection on this file traces back to a signed line on the Statement — that's what protects us if the billing is ever questioned."
Misuse: treating a verbal agreement or an informal invoice as sufficient documentation of what was selected, without the signed statement to back it up.

**Basic Services Fee (non-declinable)** — The GPL line item covering the funeral home's overhead, staff time, and coordination costs, which applies to every case regardless of which other services or merchandise are selected, and cannot be waived.
In use: "The Basic Services Fee applies even to a direct cremation with no other selections — that's not negotiable, it's on every GPL by rule."
Misuse: presenting the Basic Services Fee as if it's just another optional line item a family could decline, rather than the required floor charge it actually is.

**Cremation-with-service vs. direct cremation** — Direct cremation involves no visitation, viewing, or ceremony — cremation only, at the lowest price point. Cremation-with-service adds a memorial service, visitation, or ceremony option around the cremation.
In use: "Before assuming direct cremation is the family's preference, walk them through the cremation-with-service option — many haven't been shown it."
Misuse: treating a family's initial mention of "cremation" as automatically meaning direct cremation with no service, without presenting the full range of options the GPL requires disclosing.

**First call** — The funeral home's initial response to a death, typically involving removal of the deceased, which most states require to involve or be authorized by a licensed funeral director.
In use: "First call went out at 2 a.m. — confirm the on-call director signed off on the removal per state requirement."
Misuse: treating first-call response as a logistics task any staff member can handle, without checking the state's specific licensed-involvement requirement for removal.

**Break-even case volume** — The number of cases, weighted by category mix, a funeral home needs annually to cover its fixed costs, recalculated whenever the mix between traditional, cremation-with-service, and direct cremation shifts.
In use: "Our break-even moved when cremation share crossed 50% — the old volume target doesn't hold at this mix anymore."
Misuse: tracking total case count alone as a health indicator without recalculating break-even against the current category mix, which can mask margin erosion behind a stable case count.

**Apprenticeship / licensure requirement (state-specific)** — Most states require a period of supervised apprenticeship under a licensed funeral director/embalmer before an individual can independently conduct arrangements or embalming; requirements vary meaningfully by state.
In use: "She's still in her apprenticeship year — arrangements need a licensed director co-signing or present, per this state's board rule."
Misuse: assuming licensing requirements are uniform across states, or that an apprentice can independently handle arrangements the same way a fully licensed director can.
