# Red flags

Smell tests a veteran vegetation applicator catches before the tank is even mixed, or in the first look at a complaint. Format for each: what it usually means, the first question to ask, and the data to pull.

### Wind reading below 3 mph at spray time
- **Usually means:** a temperature inversion is forming or already present — stable air is holding fine droplets aloft instead of letting them disperse, and once the inversion breaks they can move as a concentrated cloud well off the treated site.
- **First question:** "Is this actually calm, or is it inversion-calm — what's the sky and dew point doing?"
- **Data to pull:** on-site anemometer log, temperature/dew-point spread, cloud cover, and time of day (dawn/dusk inversions are the most common miss).

### Wind above the label's stated ceiling, but the crew "topped off the tank anyway"
- **Usually means:** schedule pressure overrode the label; the ceiling exists because the label's assumed droplet size can't guarantee containment above it, regardless of how coarse the nozzle actually is.
- **First question:** "What was the logged wind reading at the exact time of the last pass, not the average for the day?"
- **Data to pull:** weather log timestamps against application timestamps; nozzle/droplet-size spec in use; any downwind complaint correlated with that window.

### Same MOA group used three or more consecutive seasons on one segment
- **Usually means:** a resistant population (commonly marestail, waterhemp, or ryegrass depending on region) is being selected for, and continued use is accelerating the shift rather than controlling it.
- **First question:** "Show me the treatment history for this exact segment for the last three years — same product each time, or same MOA group under different trade names?"
- **Data to pull:** application log by product and WSSA group per segment; any escapes or "control failures" noted in prior-year follow-up.

### Non-target browning or leaf cupping on adjacent crop within days of an ROW application
- **Usually means:** auxin-herbicide vapor drift (2,4-D or dicamba-family) onto a volatility-sensitive crop, especially under a still evening followed by a warm morning — or physical drift if wind was marginal at spray time.
- **First question:** "What formulation was used near that field edge — ester or amine — and what was the overnight low the night after application?"
- **Data to pull:** formulation and volatility class used within a quarter mile of the affected field; weather log for the 24–48 hr window after application; photos and timeline of symptom onset.

### Catch-test result more than 5% off the target GPA
- **Usually means:** nozzle wear, a partial clog, or a pressure setting drifted from spec — silent until it shows up as under- or over-application across an entire block.
- **First question:** "When was this rig last calibrated, and did anything change since — nozzles, tips, pump pressure?"
- **Data to pull:** individual nozzle catch volumes (not just the average), pressure gauge reading, nozzle age/wear against manufacturer replacement interval.

### REI signage missing or entry logged before the interval expired
- **Usually means:** either the posting step was skipped under time pressure, or someone treated the REI as advisory rather than a toxicology-based floor.
- **First question:** "What time did the last nozzle shut off, and what does the label say the REI is for this exact formulation and task?"
- **Data to pull:** application start/stop timestamps, label REI for the specific product and activity, entry log or witness statement for anyone in the treated area since.

### Ester formulation staged for a zone inside the water buffer distance
- **Usually means:** the crew is treating the buffer as a distance rule alone and missing that the label also restricts which formulation is approved near water — an aquatic label isn't interchangeable with a terrestrial one at the same rate.
- **First question:** "Is this specific product's label approved for use at this distance from water, or only the aquatic-labeled version of the same active ingredient?"
- **Data to pull:** product label's aquatic-use statement, distance from the treated zone to the nearest water feature (including dry/intermittent channels), which tank load was mixed for that zone.

### Multi-product tank mix loaded without a jar test
- **Usually means:** time pressure skipped a five-minute check before committing a full tank — the failure mode (gelling, separation, reduced activity) usually isn't visible until the sprayer is already clogging or under-performing in the field.
- **First question:** "Was this combination jar-tested with today's actual water source, or assumed compatible from a prior job?"
- **Data to pull:** jar-test record (or absence of one), water hardness/pH of the source used, product compatibility chart for the specific combination.

### Applicator certification lapsed or missing the state's right-of-way category
- **Usually means:** either a paperwork lapse that's genuinely administrative, or someone is applying restricted-use products without the required category endorsement — the latter is a compliance exposure regardless of how competent the handler actually is.
- **First question:** "Can you produce the current certification card and confirm which category it covers?"
- **Data to pull:** state certification database record, category and expiration date, whether any restricted-use product on this job requires a category the certificate doesn't list.

### Complaint that "everything is dead" on a site where selective control was specified
- **Usually means:** a non-selective product was used where the spray plan called for a selective one — often a cost or convenience substitution made without updating the plan or client.
- **First question:** "What product actually went in the tank versus what the spray plan specified for this segment?"
- **Data to pull:** use log versus spray plan for the segment, tank-mix record for the day, photos of the affected area against the intended target list.

### Boom height set noticeably above or below the label's specified height
- **Usually means:** the crew is compensating for uneven terrain or trying to increase swath coverage, both of which push the actual droplet-size-at-target outside the label's assumed range and increase drift.
- **First question:** "What boom height is this label calibrated for, and what's it actually running at on this pass?"
- **Data to pull:** label's specified boom height/nozzle angle, measured boom height in the field, terrain notes for the segment.

### Hard water source used for a glyphosate mix with no conditioning agent
- **Usually means:** calcium, magnesium, or iron in hard water is antagonizing the glyphosate molecule before it ever reaches the plant, reducing efficacy independent of rate or timing.
- **First question:** "What's the water hardness on this source, and is AMS or an equivalent conditioner in the tank?"
- **Data to pull:** water hardness test result, conditioner used and rate, any prior efficacy complaints tied to the same water source.
