# Vocabulary

Terms generalists conflate that a vegetation applicator keeps sharply separate. Each: definition, how a practitioner actually uses it in a sentence, and the common misuse to avoid.

### REI vs. PHI

**REI** (restricted-entry interval) is the time after application during which entry to the treated area is restricted absent PPE — it protects handlers, co-workers, and the public. **PHI** (pre-harvest interval) is the minimum time between application and harvest of a food crop — it's about residue on the harvested product, not entry safety, and mostly doesn't apply to right-of-way work at all.

*Practitioner usage:* "There's no PHI question on this job, there's no crop being harvested — the number that matters here is the 12-hour REI before anyone walks the ROW again."

*Common misuse:* using the two terms interchangeably, or citing a PHI when the actual constraint on a non-crop site is REI.

### Label rate vs. "label is the law"

**Label rate** is the specific application rate (and rate range, if the label gives one) printed on the product label for a given site and target. "**Label is the law**" is the broader doctrine (FIFRA §12(a)(2)(G)) that the entire label — rate, site, REI, buffer, PPE — is legally enforceable, not just the rate.

*Practitioner usage:* "The client wants it heavier for a tougher kill — the label rate is a ceiling, not a suggestion, and 'the label is the law' means I can't go over it because a client asked."

*Common misuse:* treating "label rate" as the only enforceable number on the label while treating REI, buffers, or PPE requirements on the same label as optional guidance.

### Selective vs. non-selective herbicide

**Selective** herbicides kill certain plant types (broadleaf, grass, woody) while sparing others, based on differences in plant physiology the active ingredient exploits. **Non-selective** herbicides kill essentially any green plant they contact, regardless of species.

*Practitioner usage:* "Triclopyr's selective to broadleaf and woody — it'll take out the brush and leave the fescue standing, which is exactly what the IVM plan wants here."

*Common misuse:* defaulting to a non-selective product because it's simpler or cheaper on a site where the plan calls for preserving a grass or groundcover layer, then treating the resulting bare ground as an unrelated problem next season.

### Mode of action (MOA) / site of action vs. trade name

**Mode of action** (used interchangeably with site of action in most field usage) is the specific biochemical process an herbicide disrupts, grouped into numbered WSSA classes (e.g., Group 4 synthetic auxins, Group 9 EPSP-synthase inhibitors). **Trade name** is the commercial product name, and multiple trade names can share the same MOA group.

*Practitioner usage:* "Don't let the different trade name fool you — that's still Group 9, same MOA as what we sprayed here last year, so it doesn't count as a rotation."

*Common misuse:* assuming a rotation happened because the product name changed, when the active ingredient and MOA group are the same.

### Drift vs. volatility (vapor drift)

**Drift** (physical or particle drift) is spray droplets carried off-target by wind during or shortly after application. **Volatility** (vapor drift) is the herbicide vaporizing off the treated surface hours to days later and moving as a gas, independent of wind at spray time.

*Practitioner usage:* "Wind was fine when we sprayed — the injury three days later on the beans is a volatility question, which means the formulation choice, not the spray-day weather, is what we need to look at."

*Common misuse:* clearing a formulation of blame because "the wind was in spec," when the complaint is about vapor drift that happens after the wind reading stopped mattering.

### Droplet size classification (VF/F/M/C/VC/UC)

The ASABE S572.3 standard classifies spray droplets from Very Fine (VF) through Extremely Coarse/Ultra Coarse (UC), each category carrying a different drift potential — finer droplets drift farther and evaporate faster; coarser droplets drift less but can reduce coverage on some targets.

*Practitioner usage:* "The label calls for Coarse or larger on this product — swap the tips before we run it, the ones on there now are rated Fine."

*Common misuse:* selecting a nozzle for spray pattern or cost alone without checking it against the label's required droplet-size category for that specific product.

### GPA (gallons per acre) vs. product rate per acre

**GPA** is the total spray-mix volume (water plus product plus adjuvants) applied per acre — a calibration output. **Product rate per acre** is the amount of active product alone specified by the label, independent of how much water it's carried in.

*Practitioner usage:* "The label rate is 1 qt per acre of product — that's fixed regardless of whether we're running 15 or 20 GPA, but the GPA changes how much water we add to hit that rate."

*Common misuse:* conflating the two and assuming a higher GPA means a stronger application — GPA is carrier volume, not concentration or dose.

### Buffer zone vs. Application Exclusion Zone (AEZ)

A **buffer zone** is a label-specified minimum distance from a sensitive feature (commonly water) within which a standard formulation cannot be used. The **Application Exclusion Zone (AEZ)**, a WPS term, is the area immediately around application equipment that must be kept clear of people during application itself — a real-time exclusion, not a fixed distance from a permanent feature.

*Practitioner usage:* "The buffer keeps us off standard triclopyr near the creek regardless of who's around; the AEZ is about nobody standing in front of this boom right now while it's running."

*Common misuse:* treating the two as the same concept — a site can be outside every buffer zone and still require an active AEZ during the pass itself.

### Integrated Vegetation Management (IVM) vs. brush control

**IVM** is a multi-year management approach that combines mechanical, chemical, and cultural methods to shift a right-of-way toward a stable, low-growing plant community requiring less frequent treatment. **Brush control** is the single-visit act of killing or cutting back woody growth, with no implied strategy for what grows back.

*Practitioner usage:* "This year's selective foliar pass is part of the IVM plan for the corridor — the brush control itself is one tool in it, not the whole program."

*Common misuse:* treating a single successful brush-control pass as evidence the vegetation program is "done," when the community composition shift IVM is aiming for takes multiple cycles to establish.

### Tank mix vs. sequential application

A **tank mix** combines multiple products in one tank for a single pass. A **sequential application** applies products separately, on different passes or dates, often to manage compatibility or timing (e.g., a pre-emergent followed later by a post-emergent).

*Practitioner usage:* "These two aren't compatible in the jar test — we're going sequential instead, pre-emergent this week and the post-emergent in three weeks."

*Common misuse:* forcing a tank mix for scheduling convenience on a combination that failed compatibility testing, rather than accepting the extra pass a sequential application requires.

### Resistance vs. tolerance

**Resistance** is a heritable trait a specific population has evolved through selection pressure from repeated exposure to one MOA — it develops over time in a treated area. **Tolerance** is a species' inherent, non-evolved insensitivity to a given herbicide, present from the start regardless of spray history.

*Practitioner usage:* "That's not resistance, that species was always tolerant to Group 9 — resistance is what we're watching for in the marestail on this same stretch after three years of it."

*Common misuse:* labeling any herbicide "failure" as resistance, when it may simply be applying a product against a species it was never labeled to control (tolerance), which points to a product selection error rather than a rotation problem.

### Applicator certification category vs. general certification

**General certification** qualifies a handler for restricted-use products broadly under a state's program. A **category endorsement** (e.g., Category 6, Right-of-Way Pest Control, under 40 CFR Part 171) is a specific credential required for a particular use pattern, and a general certification alone does not cover every category.

*Practitioner usage:* "I'm certified, but not in Category 6 yet — I can't be the certifying applicator of record on this ROW job until that's added."

*Common misuse:* assuming any valid pesticide-applicator certification covers every job type; category-specific restricted uses require the matching category on the card.
