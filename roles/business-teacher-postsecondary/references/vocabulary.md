# Vocabulary

### Case method
An instructional approach where students analyze a real or realistic business situation with incomplete information and arrive at a supported recommendation, rather than being given a single worked example to memorize.
**In use:** "We're running this as a case, not a lecture example — I want each of you to argue a position from the dataset, not repeat back what I present."
**Common misuse:** Running "case discussion" as a guided walk-through where the instructor reveals the single intended answer at the end — that's a lecture wearing a case's format; the method's value comes from students producing and defending their own reasoning from ambiguous material, not from confirming a predetermined conclusion.

### Reasoning-based grading
A grading approach that scores the rigor, evidence use, and internal consistency of a submission's argument, independent of which specific conclusion it reaches.
**In use:** "Two students recommended opposite entry modes here and both scored in the top tier — the rubric grades the trade-off analysis, not which option they picked."
**Common misuse:** Assuming reasoning-based grading means "anything goes" or that no submission can score poorly — a poorly reasoned argument for any conclusion, including the instructor's own preferred one, should score low; the rubric removes the answer key, not the standard.

### Case currency
The state of a case's factual content (named companies, market conditions, regulations, figures) relative to the present, checked separately from whether the case's underlying teaching point still holds.
**In use:** "The framework this case teaches is still solid, but the case currency is off — two of the five competitors it names have since changed pricing models."
**Common misuse:** Treating a case as "good" or "bad" as a whole based on age alone — a case's teaching framework can remain sound for years while specific facts within it go stale; the fix is usually a targeted addendum on the changed facts, not discarding or fully rewriting the case.

### Framework vs. opinion
The distinction between a citable, generalizable analytical model taught as course content and an instructor's personal experience, anecdote, or preference offered alongside it.
**In use:** "That last point about negotiation tactics was my own experience from industry, not something in the assigned framework — treat it as one data point, not a rule."
**Common misuse:** Assuming that because an instructor has real industry experience, their practitioner opinions carry the same generalizable weight as a peer-reviewed or widely adopted framework — expertise makes the anecdote worth hearing, not automatically representative beyond the specific situation it came from.

### Preserving ambiguity
Deliberately retaining incomplete information, uncertain third-party responses, and genuine trade-offs in a case's dataset, so that more than one course of action can be defended from the same facts.
**In use:** "I left the regulatory approval timeline as a range instead of a fixed date — that uncertainty is part of what the students need to reason through, not a detail I forgot to pin down."
**Common misuse:** Adding uncertainty or missing information to every case regardless of what it's meant to teach — an introductory case meant to demonstrate a single framework cleanly is better served by a more constrained dataset; ambiguity should track the case's teaching goal, not be maximized on principle.

### Transferability (of a framework)
The degree to which a framework taught through one case's specific facts can be applied by a student to a different, unseen business situation of the same general type.
**In use:** "If they can only apply the five forces model to the exact airline example we used in class, we haven't taught transferability — the retail case on the exam checks whether it actually transferred."
**Common misuse:** Confusing detailed familiarity with one case for transferability — a student who can discuss the taught case's company in depth may still be unable to apply the same framework to a new company, since deep case-specific knowledge and a portable analytical skill are not the same outcome.

### Cold call (case pedagogy)
An instructor's practice of calling on a student without advance notice to open or advance case discussion, used to require preparation and active reasoning rather than passive listening.
**In use:** "I'll cold call to open the case — come prepared to state and defend a recommendation, not just summarize the facts."
**Common misuse:** Using cold calling only to check whether students read the case (a factual-recall check) rather than to surface and test their reasoning — the pedagogical value is in requiring a defended position under some pressure, not in verifying attendance to the reading.

### Capstone case
A culminating case assignment, typically placed near the end of a course or program, designed to require synthesis of multiple frameworks taught earlier rather than application of a single one.
**In use:** "This capstone case doesn't map to one chapter — it needs the pricing framework from week 4 and the market-entry framework from week 9 together."
**Common misuse:** Treating a capstone case as simply a longer or harder version of an earlier single-framework case — its distinguishing design goal is requiring integration across multiple previously taught frameworks, and a capstone that only exercises one framework more extensively hasn't achieved that goal even if it's more difficult.
