# Red flags

### A grading rubric that lists a single correct conclusion for an open business decision
- **Usually means:** submissions that reach a different, well-reasoned conclusion from the same incomplete dataset will be marked down regardless of reasoning quality.
- **First question:** does the rubric score the reasoning and evidence use, or does it score whether the final decision matches one predetermined answer?
- **Data to pull:** the rubric's scoring criteria, a sample of graded submissions that reached different conclusions.

### A case being taught without checking whether a named company, market, or regulation in it has changed since it was written
- **Usually means:** students may be reasoning from an outdated picture of the market presented as if it were still current.
- **First question:** when was this case last checked against current conditions for the specific companies/market/regulations it names?
- **Data to pull:** the case's original write-up date, current status of the named companies/market/regulatory conditions.

### An instructor's personal anecdote or past employer's practice presented in the same tone as a taught, citable framework
- **Usually means:** students may treat one practitioner's experience as an established, generalizable principle.
- **First question:** was this point flagged as personal experience, or presented as if it carries the same evidentiary weight as a named framework?
- **Data to pull:** the lecture material or notes for how the point was introduced, whether a citable source or framework backs it.

### A case dataset with no missing information, uncertain third-party responses, or competing objectives
- **Usually means:** the case teaches students to expect a completeness of information that real business decisions don't provide.
- **First question:** can more than one course of action be defended using only the data given, or does one option clearly dominate once all the data is read?
- **Data to pull:** the case dataset, a check of whether at least 2 distinct recommendations can be argued from it.

### Grading feedback that states only the score or grade, without identifying which parts of the reasoning were strong or weak
- **Usually means:** the student can't tell whether their analytical process needs improvement or only their specific conclusion was disfavored.
- **First question:** does the feedback address the reasoning path (framing, evidence use, trade-off treatment), or only the bottom-line grade?
- **Data to pull:** the feedback given on a sample of submissions.

### A framework introduced in lecture with no case or applied exercise requiring students to use it
- **Usually means:** students may be able to name and describe the framework without being able to apply it to an unfamiliar situation.
- **First question:** is there a scheduled case or exercise requiring application of this specific framework before the course moves to the next topic?
- **Data to pull:** the course syllabus/schedule, whether an applied assignment is linked to this framework.

### A course run primarily through case discussion with no named, transferable framework attached to the recurring pattern being discussed
- **Usually means:** students may become skilled at discussing the specific companies covered without a generalizable principle they can carry to a new, unseen situation.
- **First question:** is there an underlying framework being built across these case discussions, or is each case discussed as a standalone situation?
- **Data to pull:** the course's case sequence and whether a common framework is named and referenced across them.

### Multiple graders scoring the same assignment without a shared, documented rubric
- **Usually means:** the same quality of reasoning could receive different grades depending on which grader's informal standard was applied.
- **First question:** did all graders use the same written rubric criteria, or did each apply their own sense of what counts as strong reasoning?
- **Data to pull:** the rubric document, a grade-distribution comparison across graders on a shared sample of submissions.

### A case known by the instructor to be outdated is taught anyway with no addendum or verbal note to students
- **Usually means:** the instructor is aware of the currency problem but hasn't corrected for it before the students encounter the case.
- **First question:** why wasn't an addendum or note prepared, given the instructor's own awareness that something in the case has changed?
- **Data to pull:** any instructor notes acknowledging the change, whether that information reached the students before or during the case discussion.
