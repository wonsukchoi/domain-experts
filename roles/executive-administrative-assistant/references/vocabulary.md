# Executive Administrative Assistant — Vocabulary

### Gatekeeping
The judgment function of deciding what reaches the executive directly, what gets redirected, and what gets deferred to a batch review.
**In use:** "I'm gatekeeping the inbox this week while she's traveling — anything not time-sensitive waits until Monday."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a purely defensive/blocking function. Real gatekeeping also actively routes things forward faster when they matter — it's a triage function, not a wall.

### Delegated authority
A specific, bounded decision-making power granted by the executive to the EA, with defined limits (dollar amount, decision type, expiration).
**In use:** "My delegated authority on travel rebooking tops out at $500 — this one needs to go to her."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a fixed personality trait ("I have a lot of authority") rather than a specific, auditable boundary that should be logged and reviewed, not assumed.

### Standing meeting
A recurring calendar commitment (1:1, team sync, board prep) that has priority-tier protection against being displaced by ad hoc requests.
**In use:** "That's a standing meeting — I can move the vendor call, not this one."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to be permanently untouchable regardless of context; a standing meeting should still be reviewed periodically for whether it's still earning its calendar slot.

### Buffer time
Unscheduled time deliberately held between commitments to absorb overruns, travel delays, or prep needs.
**In use:** "I built in 30 minutes of buffer before the board call so she's not walking in from the airport."
**Common misuse:** Cut first when a calendar gets tight, on the assumption it's "free" time — buffer is a risk-mitigation resource, not slack to be filled.

### Priority rubric
An explicit, named ranking of requester types or request categories used to resolve scheduling conflicts consistently rather than case-by-case.
**In use:** "Per the rubric, a Tier-1 request bumps a Tier-3 meeting automatically — I don't need to ask."
**Common misuse:** Applied rigidly even when a lower-tier request carries a harder external constraint that should elevate it for that specific case.

### Escalation
The act of surfacing a decision to the executive because it falls outside delegated authority or requires judgment only they can make.
**In use:** "I'm escalating this one — it's over my rebooking limit and it affects a client commitment."
**Common misuse:** Used as a synonym for "asking a question," diluting its signal value. An escalation should come bundled with options and a recommendation, not an open-ended "what do you want to do?"

### Per diem
A fixed daily allowance for expenses like meals, set by policy, against which actual spending is checked.
**In use:** "Day two's dinner is $35 over the meal per diem — flagging it since it included a client."
**Common misuse:** Confused with a strict cap that can never be exceeded; most per-diem policies allow documented exceptions, they just require the exception to be called out, not silently absorbed into a total.

### Confidentiality boundary
The defined line for who is authorized to receive specific information, based on role rather than personal trust or perceived need-to-know.
**In use:** "That's inside the confidentiality boundary — even though he's a board member, this particular deal hasn't been cleared for board-level disclosure yet."
**Common misuse:** Treated as identical to "keeping secrets from people I don't trust," when it's actually about role-based authorization regardless of how trustworthy a given person seems.

### Reconciliation (expense)
The process of matching submitted receipts against approved bookings or policy limits, line by line, before submitting a final report.
**In use:** "I reconciled the trip — two line items are over cap and flagged individually."
**Common misuse:** Treated as just adding up totals; the actual value is in the line-by-line variance check, not the sum.

### Precedent (in delegated-authority context)
A prior decision that, once logged, can be used to justify handling a similar future case without re-escalating.
**In use:** "We set precedent on this exact vendor-response scenario last quarter — I can handle it without asking again."
**Common misuse:** Stretched to cover cases that are similar but not actually identical, quietly expanding delegated authority beyond what was explicitly granted.

### Buffer conflict
A scheduling situation where the time between two commitments is technically nonzero but insufficient for realistic transition (travel, prep, cognitive reset).
**In use:** "Technically there's ten minutes between those two calls, but that's a buffer conflict given one is in-person and one is virtual."
**Common misuse:** Not distinguished from a hard double-booking; buffer conflicts are subtler and more common, and just as damaging to execution quality.

### Intake process
The standard channel and screening step through which external requests reach the executive's calendar or attention.
**In use:** "New vendor contacts go through the intake process — I screen before anything reaches her inbox directly."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to be bureaucratic friction rather than a deliberate filter; bypassing it is often exactly what a lower-priority requester is trying to do.
