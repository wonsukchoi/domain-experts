# Red flags — what a cardiologist notices instantly

Smell tests during a cardiology consult or clinic visit. Format per flag: signal, what it usually means, first question, what to pull.

**Troponin elevated but the story doesn't fit ACS (sepsis, tachyarrhythmia, recent seizure, GI bleed).**
Usually means: type 2 MI (supply-demand mismatch) rather than type 1 (plaque rupture) — treating the precipitant matters more than treating the coronary tree.
First question: "Is there a demand-mismatch driver active right now, and does the troponin trend track it?"
Pull: the trigger event's timeline against serial troponin values; check for a rising-then-plateauing pattern consistent with a resolving precipitant rather than an evolving MI.

**A "negative" stress test in a patient whose symptoms are still limiting.**
Usually means: either the pretest probability was low enough that a negative test is genuinely reassuring, or the wrong modality was chosen (submaximal exercise, inadequate imaging window) and the test never adequately stressed the myocardium.
First question: "Did the patient reach target heart rate / adequate stress, and does the negative result change what I do next?"
Pull: the stress-test report's heart-rate/METs achieved and imaging adequacy notes, not just the summary impression line.

**Heart failure patient on only one or two of the four GDMT pillars more than 3 months post-diagnosis.**
Usually means: sequential titration that stalled — beta-blocker started, "we'll add the MRA next visit," and the visit never came, or got skipped for a lab value that didn't actually warrant holding.
First question: "What's the specific reason each missing pillar hasn't been started, and is that reason still true today?"
Pull: current K+, eGFR, and BP against each drug's actual hold threshold (not a rounded-up internal rule of thumb).

**Ejection fraction reported as "improved" or "normalized" and GDMT discontinued.**
Usually means: GDMT-responsive recovery (HFrEF with improved EF), where the improvement is a consequence of the drugs, not evidence they're no longer needed — stopping them has a documented relapse rate.
First question: "Was this EF recovery achieved on GDMT, and has anyone shown it's sustained off therapy?"
Pull: TRED-HF-type evidence in the chart (none usually exists) before agreeing to any taper; the default is continue, not stop.

**"Sole discretion" style clinical shorthand — chest pain called "atypical" without documenting which features are absent.**
Usually means: a pattern-matching label substituting for the actual history elements the HEART score needs (character, radiation, exertional relationship, associated symptoms).
First question: "What specific feature makes this atypical, and did we ask about it directly?"
Pull: the documented history against each HEART "history" anchor point; vague labels don't score.

**CHA₂DS₂-VASc ≥2 and anticoagulation withheld for "fall risk."**
Usually means: fall risk is overweighted relative to actual evidence — the number of falls needed to outweigh anticoagulation's stroke-prevention benefit is very high (often cited around 300 falls/year in a single patient), rarely met in practice.
First question: "Has this patient actually had a fall causing a bleed requiring intervention, or is this a hypothetical risk?"
Pull: documented fall history with injury severity, and the HAS-BLED score broken into its actual components rather than a global impression.

**DAPT stopped early (before 12 months post-ACS) for a scheduled minor procedure.**
Usually means: a procedural convenience is being weighed against stent thrombosis risk without a documented bridging or timing plan.
First question: "Can this procedure wait until the DAPT window closes, or does it truly require interruption?"
Pull: procedure bleeding-risk category and time since stent (thrombosis risk is highest in the first month, materially elevated through 12).

**Angiographically severe lesion recommended for stenting without any ischemia correlate.**
Usually means: the oculostenotic reflex — treating the image, not the physiology.
First question: "Is there a positive stress test, wall-motion abnormality, or FFR ≤0.80 tied to this specific vessel?"
Pull: the FFR/iFR value if measured; if not measured, why not, given it's available in the same setting.

**Blood pressure or potassium reading used to stop a GDMT drug entirely rather than adjust the dose.**
Usually means: a single out-of-range value triggering an all-or-nothing response instead of a titration response.
First question: "Is this value isolated or trending, and does it cross the drug's actual discontinuation threshold or just its titration-caution threshold?"
Pull: the last 2-3 readings of the relevant lab/vital, not the single most recent one.

**Syncope worked up with an extensive cardiac battery in a patient whose history is classic vasovagal.**
Usually means: reflexive over-testing after being taught to distrust gestalt, applied to a presentation gestalt actually handles well (situational trigger, prodrome, young patient, normal exam/ECG).
First question: "Does this history meet San Francisco Syncope Rule or Canadian Syncope Risk Score low-risk criteria?"
Pull: the documented prodrome, trigger, and post-episode recovery pattern against the validated low-risk criteria before ordering echo/EP referral.
