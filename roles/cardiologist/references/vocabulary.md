# Vocabulary

Terms of art generalists (and non-cardiology clinicians) reliably misuse. Format per term: definition, practitioner usage, common misuse.

## Type 1 vs. type 2 myocardial infarction

- **Definition:** type 1 MI is plaque rupture/erosion with thrombus formation; type 2 MI is myocardial injury from oxygen supply-demand mismatch (tachyarrhythmia, severe anemia, hypotension, hypoxia) without plaque rupture.
- **Practitioner usage:** "Troponin's up in the septic patient with a fib RVR — that's a type 2 pattern; treat the sepsis and rate, not a cath lab activation."
- **Common misuse:** treating any troponin elevation as an indication for dual antiplatelet therapy and cath referral, regardless of the clinical context producing it.

## Ejection fraction (EF)

- **Definition:** the percentage of end-diastolic left-ventricular volume ejected per beat; a measure of systolic pump function, not a complete description of heart failure physiology.
- **Practitioner usage:** "HFpEF at EF 55% has a completely different treatment algorithm than HFrEF at EF 25% — SGLT2i is the only pillar with the same class of evidence across both."
- **Common misuse:** treating "normal EF" as "no heart failure" — HFpEF is a distinct, common, and morbid diagnosis with a normal or near-normal EF.

## FFR (fractional flow reserve) and iFR

- **Definition:** the ratio of pressure distal to a stenosis versus proximal (aortic) pressure, measured with a pressure wire during coronary angiography, quantifying the lesion's physiologic (not anatomic) significance. iFR is a resting-state variant without vasodilator induction.
- **Practitioner usage:** "It's 70% by eyeball but FFR is 0.86 — defer, it's not causing ischemia."
- **Common misuse:** assuming angiographic percent stenosis and FFR track linearly; a visually moderate lesion can be physiologically severe and vice versa, especially in diffuse disease.

## GDMT (guideline-directed medical therapy)

- **Definition:** in heart failure with reduced EF, specifically the four drug classes with independent mortality benefit — ARNI (or ACEi/ARB), evidence-based beta-blocker, MRA, SGLT2i — each titrated toward target dose.
- **Practitioner usage:** "She's on three of four pillars at target dose; the SGLT2i is the last piece and there's no renal contraindication, so it goes on today, not next visit."
- **Common misuse:** using "GDMT" loosely to mean "on some heart medication," or counting a drug as "started" when it's at a sub-therapeutic dose that was never titrated up.

## HFrEF vs. HFmrEF vs. HFpEF

- **Definition:** heart failure classified by EF band — reduced (≤40%), mildly reduced (41-49%), preserved (≥50%) — each with meaningfully different GDMT evidence bases.
- **Practitioner usage:** "HFmrEF gets a partial-evidence extrapolation from the HFrEF trials, not the full four-pillar mandate — SGLT2i and MRA have the strongest signal in that band."
- **Common misuse:** applying the full HFrEF GDMT algorithm uniformly across all three bands as if the trial evidence were equally strong in each.

## CHA₂DS₂-VASc vs. HAS-BLED

- **Definition:** CHA₂DS₂-VASc estimates stroke risk in atrial fibrillation to guide the anticoagulation decision; HAS-BLED estimates bleeding risk to identify modifiable risk factors, not to veto anticoagulation.
- **Practitioner usage:** "HAS-BLED of 3 just means fix the blood pressure and stop the ibuprofen — it's not a reason to leave a CHA₂DS₂-VASc of 4 unanticoagulated."
- **Common misuse:** treating a high HAS-BLED score as a stopping condition symmetrical to CHA₂DS₂-VASc, when the two scores share risk factors and a high HAS-BLED is meant to prompt correction, not avoidance.

## Door-to-balloon time

- **Definition:** the interval from a STEMI patient's arrival at a PCI-capable facility to balloon inflation (first device) in the culprit vessel; guideline target ≤90 minutes.
- **Practitioner usage:** "We're at 62 minutes door-to-balloon on this one — activate the lab straight off the ECG next time and we'll get it under an hour routinely."
- **Common misuse:** treating door-to-balloon as a hospital quality-metric abstraction rather than a proxy for myocardial salvage — every 30-minute delay measurably increases infarct size and mortality.

## Appropriate Use Criteria (AUC)

- **Definition:** ACC/professional-society consensus ratings (appropriate / may be appropriate / rarely appropriate) matching diagnostic tests and procedures to specific clinical scenarios, based on pretest probability and prior workup.
- **Practitioner usage:** "This is a rarely-appropriate stress test per AUC — asymptomatic, low pretest probability, already had one two years ago that was negative."
- **Common misuse:** treating AUC as a billing/reimbursement checkbox rather than an actual pretest-probability tool that should change ordering behavior.

## Oculostenotic reflex

- **Definition:** Topol and Nissen's term for the reflex to treat (stent) any angiographically visible stenosis, independent of physiologic significance or symptom correlation.
- **Practitioner usage:** "That's oculostenotic reflex talking — get an FFR before we commit to a stent on an asymptomatic 70% lesion."
- **Common misuse:** assuming visual severity alone (without ischemia testing) is sufficient justification for revascularization in a stable patient.

## Guideline-directed vs. patient-directed therapy in shared decision-making

- **Definition:** guidelines specify the evidence-based default; shared decision-making is the explicit process of presenting genuine equipoise choices (e.g., rate vs. rhythm control in AFib, PCI vs. CABG vs. OMT in multivessel disease) and incorporating patient values into the final choice.
- **Practitioner usage:** "Both are guideline-concordant here — this is a shared-decision conversation, not a right-answer question."
- **Common misuse:** presenting a guideline default as the only medically acceptable option when the guideline itself frames the situation as a genuine choice between reasonable alternatives.
