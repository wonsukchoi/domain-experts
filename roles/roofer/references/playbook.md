# Playbook

Filled sizing templates a roofer or foreman runs before and during a job. Every figure below is a worked example following common code sections and typical manufacturer specs — always verify the actual product's installation instructions and the local jurisdiction's code amendments before ordering material or signing off on a job.

## 1. Nailing pattern vs. wind-zone rating

Method: pull the local wind-zone requirement first, then match the shingle product's tested nailing pattern to it. Nail count and placement are part of the wind-resistance rating (ASTM D3161 or D7158, depending on the manufacturer's test method) — not interchangeable with a "standard" habit.

**Filled example — 28-square job, 110 mph wind-zone requirement:**

| Pattern | Nails/shingle | Nails/square (≈80 shingles) | Total (28 squares) | Typical wind rating unlocked* |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Standard | 4 | 320 | 8,960 | Lower-wind-zone rating only |
| Enhanced (high-wind) | 6 | 480 | 13,440 | High-wind-zone rating |

*Illustrative pattern common to many architectural shingle lines — the specific nail count required for a specific wind rating is set by that product's installation instructions, not by this table.

**Cost delta:** 4,480 additional nails ≈ $15 in fastener cost; ≈1.5 crew-hours added across a 28-square job. Compare that against the cost of a full re-nail after a wind claim — the enhanced pattern is never the expensive option.

**Nailing zone check (every course):** nails go in the manufacturer's defined common-bond nailing zone — typically a band roughly 1 in above the shingle's cutout notches, in the double-thickness overlap area. High-nailing (above the zone, into single-layer material) is the single defect most often cited in manufacturer warranty denials. Snap a chalk line to the zone; don't eyeball it.

## 2. Slope-to-system decision table

| Slope | System | Underlayment | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| ≥ 4:12 | Standard steep-slope shingle | Single layer, felt or synthetic | Standard install per most manufacturer instructions |
| 2:12 – 4:12 | Low-slope shingle application | Double layer, or full ice-and-water-shield coverage | Longer water dwell time than steep-slope even with same shingle product; code (e.g. IRC R905.2.8.2) requires the upgraded underlayment |
| < 2:12 | Shingles not applicable | N/A | Default to modified bitumen, single-ply membrane (TPO/EPDM), or standing-seam metal — systems designed for standing water, not sheet shedding |

**Default rule:** measure slope on site, don't infer it from the original roof's shingle type — a prior installer may have already installed the wrong system on a marginal-slope section (porch, dormer, bay window roof), and that error doesn't get inherited into the new job.

## 3. Attic ventilation net free area (NFA) calculation

Method: NFA required = attic floor area ÷ ratio (300 if intake/exhaust balanced and a vapor retarder is present, 150 if not). Split required NFA roughly 50/50 between intake (soffit) and exhaust (ridge/roof vents), then verify against actual product NFA-per-linear-foot ratings and against physical blockage (insulation baffles, paint-clogged vents).

**Filled example — 2,400 sq ft attic floor, balanced system with vapor retarder (1:300):**

- Required total NFA = 2,400 ÷ 300 = 8 sq ft = 1,152 sq in.
- Split: ≈576 sq in intake, ≈576 sq in exhaust.
- Existing soffit vent (older strip type): 2.5 sq in NFA/lf × 140 lf = 350 sq in — **226 sq in short** of the 576 sq in intake requirement.
- Replacement soffit vent (continuous type): 9 sq in NFA/lf × 140 lf = 1,260 sq in — clears requirement with margin.
- Ridge vent: 18 sq in NFA/lf × 60 ft ridge = 1,080 sq in — clears the 576 sq in exhaust requirement once intake is fixed.

**Verification step (not optional):** pull back insulation at 3–4 soffit locations to confirm baffles are present and vents aren't buried — a vent's rated NFA on the spec sheet is worthless if blown-in insulation covers the intake path in the attic.

## 4. Ice barrier along-slope length

Method: code figure (e.g. IRC R905.1.2) states the ice barrier must extend from the eave edge to a point at least 24 in inside the interior face of the exterior wall, measured horizontally. That horizontal distance is *not* the membrane length to order — convert to along-slope distance, and add any eave overhang to the horizontal distance before converting.

**Formula:** along-slope length = (overhang + 24 in) × slope factor, where slope factor = √(rise² + run²) ÷ run (rise and run in the same units, e.g. 6:12 pitch → √(12²+6²)/12).

**Filled example — 6:12 pitch, 18 in overhang:**

- Horizontal distance to cover = 18 in overhang + 24 in code minimum = 42 in = 3.5 ft.
- Slope factor (6:12) = √(12² + 6²) / 12 = √180 / 12 ≈ 1.118.
- Along-slope length = 3.5 ft × 1.118 ≈ 3.91 ft ≈ **47 in**.
- A single 36-in-wide membrane roll falls **11 in short** of that 47-in requirement — order a second course, lapped a minimum 4 in over the first, running upslope from the eave course.

**Reference table for common pitches (18 in overhang, 24 in code minimum):**

| Pitch | Slope factor | Along-slope length | Fits one 36-in roll? |
|---|---|---|---|
| 4:12 | 1.054 | ≈44 in | No — 2nd course needed |
| 6:12 | 1.118 | ≈47 in | No — 2nd course needed |
| 8:12 | 1.202 | ≈50 in | No — 2nd course needed |
| 12:12 | 1.414 | ≈59 in | No — 2nd course needed |

**Takeaway:** on nearly any pitch with a typical residential overhang, a single 36-in course does not satisfy the 24-in-past-the-wall code requirement once overhang and slope are accounted for — assume a second course is needed and confirm, rather than assuming one course is enough and finding out at the next freeze-thaw cycle.

## 5. Fall-protection plan and anchor sizing

**Trigger height:** 6 ft on construction work generally, including residential steep-slope roofing (OSHA 29 CFR 1926.501(b)(13)) — fixed, not judged per worker or per job.

**System selection, in order of preference for a steep-slope residential job:**

1. Guardrail system around the work area, where the roof geometry allows a practical perimeter.
2. Safety net system, where guardrails aren't practical.
3. Personal fall arrest system (PFAS) — harness, shock-absorbing lanyard, and a rated anchor — as the default for most steep-slope residential work where 1 and 2 aren't practical.
4. A written, site-specific fall protection plan, only when conventional methods (1–3) are documented as infeasible for that specific roof geometry — never a substitute chosen for convenience.

**Anchor point rating:**

| Anchor type | Required rating |
|---|---|
| Non-certified, single-use anchorage | 5,000 lb per attached worker |
| Certified/engineered, reusable anchorage | Designed by a qualified person, minimum 2x safety factor against the maximum arrest force expected |

**Pre-mobilization checklist (every job, every day the crew is above trigger height):**

- Anchor points marked and rated before the crew's first worker steps above 6 ft.
- Harness and lanyard inspected for cuts, UV degradation, and stitching integrity — not just presence.
- Rescue plan named and understood by the crew (how a fallen, suspended worker gets down), not assumed to be "call 911 and figure it out."
- No anchor substitutions (vent pipes, straps around framing, prior job's leftover anchor) accepted without re-verifying the rating.
