# Red flags

Smell tests a roofing foreman catches on a walk-through or a warranty-claim review. Format for each: what it usually means, the first question to ask, and the data to pull before signing off on a decision.

## 1. Nail heads visible above the shingle's sealant strip

**Usually means:** high-nailing — the nail gun was set or angled to place fasteners above the defined nailing zone, into single-layer material instead of the double-thickness bond area. This is the defect manufacturers cite most often when denying a wind or workmanship warranty claim.

**First question:** "Was the nailer's fence/guide set to the zone on this course, or did the installer eyeball it?"

**Data to pull:** a sample of pulled shingles from the affected slope showing nail placement relative to the cutout notches; the installer's own photos (if any) from install day; the manufacturer's specific nailing-zone diagram for the product installed.

## 2. Wind damage or shingle blow-off within the first 1–2 years of install

**Usually means:** either an under-spec nailing pattern for the actual local wind zone, or correct nail count placed wrong (high-nailing) — rarely a manufacturing defect at this age. Rule out installation before assuming the shingle itself failed.

**First question:** "What wind-zone rating was this roof specified for, and what nailing pattern does that require versus what's actually in the roof?"

**Data to pull:** local wind map rating at time of install; manufacturer's installation instructions for the product's nailing pattern by wind zone; a pulled-shingle sample showing actual nail count and placement.

## 3. Ice dam with icicles forming beyond the eave line in the first winter after install

**Usually means:** either insufficient ice barrier extent (short course, or code minimum not converted to along-slope length), inadequate attic insulation/air-sealing letting heat escape to the roof deck, or both. Rarely a shingle-quality issue.

**First question:** "How far up-slope does the ice-and-water shield actually run, measured from the eave edge — and does that match the code minimum converted for this roof's overhang and pitch?"

**Data to pull:** installation records or as-built photos of ice-barrier course count and lap; attic insulation R-value and any visible air-leak paths (can lights, bath fans, attic hatch) near the affected area; roof pitch and overhang measurements.

## 4. Frost or moisture visible on the underside of the roof deck in winter

**Usually means:** attic ventilation imbalance — usually insufficient intake relative to exhaust, or a vapor retarder missing/damaged at the ceiling plane — letting warm, humid house air condense on the cold deck. Rarely a roofing-material defect.

**First question:** "What's the actual net free area of intake versus exhaust in this attic, and is the ceiling's vapor retarder intact?"

**Data to pull:** soffit and ridge/roof vent product NFA ratings and linear footage; a physical check for blocked soffit vents (insulation, paint); attic floor area to recompute the required NFA at the applicable ratio.

## 5. Ridge vent installed with no soffit vent, or soffit vents visibly blocked by insulation

**Usually means:** the installer treated ventilation as an exhaust checklist item without auditing intake — a long ridge vent over no functioning intake doesn't move more air, and in some conditions reverses to pull conditioned air from the house through ceiling penetrations.

**First question:** "Where is the intake air for this ridge vent actually coming from?"

**Data to pull:** soffit vent presence and NFA rating; a visual or camera check inside the attic at the soffit-to-roof-deck transition for insulation blocking the vent chute; presence/absence of baffles.

## 6. Roof slope under 2:12 with standard shingle product installed

**Usually means:** either the slope was never measured (a prior roof's system was copied without checking), or a low-slope shingle exception was applied incorrectly to a slope that's actually below the shingle floor entirely. This is a materials-vs-physics mismatch, not a workmanship defect that better installation fixes.

**First question:** "What is the measured slope on this specific section, and does the installed system match the code and manufacturer minimum for that slope?"

**Data to pull:** slope measurement (rise/run) taken directly on the section in question, not assumed from the main roof; manufacturer's minimum-slope specification for the installed shingle product; code section governing low-slope shingle application in the jurisdiction.

## 7. Single ice-and-water-shield course at the eave with any meaningful roof overhang

**Usually means:** the installer ordered material against the flat code figure (e.g., 24 in past the interior wall line) without adding the overhang distance or converting to along-slope length — a common shortfall on any roof with more than a minimal eave overhang.

**First question:** "What's the overhang distance and roof pitch, and was the membrane run calculated from those, or just cut to the code number as if it were a flat measurement?"

**Data to pull:** overhang measurement; roof pitch; the along-slope length calculation (overhang + code minimum, × slope factor) compared against the actual membrane coverage installed.

## 8. Crew working above 6 ft on a steep-slope roof without visible harnesses or anchor points

**Usually means:** either no fall-protection plan exists for the job, or one exists on paper but isn't being followed on site — both are stop-work conditions, not a documentation gap to fix later.

**First question:** "Where's the job's fall-protection plan, and why isn't what's on the roof right now matching it?"

**Data to pull:** the written fall-protection plan (if any) for the job; anchor point locations and ratings; harness/lanyard inspection log.

## 9. Anchor point is a vent pipe, exhaust stack, or a strap improvised around framing

**Usually means:** the crew treated "something solid-looking" as equivalent to a rated anchor — it isn't, and most improvised anchors have never been load-tested to the 5,000 lb (or engineered 2x safety factor) standard a real anchor point requires.

**First question:** "What is this anchor point rated to hold, and who verified that rating?"

**Data to pull:** manufacturer spec or engineering documentation for the anchor product actually in use; a physical inspection of what the harness lanyard is actually clipped to.

## 10. Granule loss or bald patches concentrated in a line along the nail course, not general wear

**Usually means:** repeated overdriven nailing cut through the shingle mat along that course, exposing the substrate to accelerated wear at exactly the fastener line — a nail-gun pressure-calibration issue, not normal aging.

**First question:** "Has the nailer's air pressure been checked against the manufacturer's spec for this shingle thickness?"

**Data to pull:** nailer pressure setting logs (if kept); a sample of shingles from the affected course showing nail penetration depth; manufacturer's specified pressure range for the product.

## 11. Valley or step flashing reused from tear-off, or missing at a roof-to-wall transition

**Usually means:** a cost- or time-saving shortcut on tear-off/reflash — reused flashing has often already deformed or corroded past its service life, and a missing step/kick-out flashing at a wall transition is one of the most common sources of a leak that shows up as interior wall damage far from the actual entry point.

**First question:** "Was this flashing replaced as part of the tear-off, or reused — and is there a photo from before the new shingles went down?"

**Data to pull:** tear-off scope in the original work order (flashing replacement should be a named line item, not assumed bundled into "reroof"); a visual inspection of flashing condition at valleys and wall transitions before covering with new courses.
