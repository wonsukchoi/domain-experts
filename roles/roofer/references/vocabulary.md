# Vocabulary

Terms generalists conflate that roofers keep sharply separate. Each: definition, a sentence a practitioner would actually say using it, and the common misuse.

## Nailing zone vs. nailing line

**Nailing zone** is the manufacturer-defined band on a shingle — typically just above the cutout notches, in the double-thickness overlap area — where fasteners must land to achieve the tested wind rating and warranty coverage. **Nailing line** is sometimes used loosely to mean the same thing, but some crews use it to mean "wherever we snapped the chalk line today," which isn't the same as the manufacturer's zone if the chalk line was set wrong.

*Practitioner usage:* "Snap the chalk to the zone printed on the shingle spec sheet, not to whatever line worked on the last job — different product, different zone."

*Common misuse:* treating "nailing line" as interchangeable with "nailing zone" when the actual chalk line hasn't been verified against the current product's spec.

## High-nailing vs. correct nailing pattern

**High-nailing** is placing fasteners above the defined nailing zone, into single-layer shingle material instead of the double-thickness bond area — usually done to make the nail-gun angle easier on a steep roof. **Correct nailing pattern** refers to both placement (in the zone) and count (matching the wind-zone requirement).

*Practitioner usage:* "That whole slope is high-nailed — pull a sample and you'll see the heads sitting an inch above where they belong."

*Common misuse:* assuming a roof with the right nail *count* automatically has the right nail *placement* — a six-nail pattern that's all high-nailed still fails the warranty inspection.

## Net free area (NFA) vs. vent size

**Net free area** is the actual open, unobstructed area a vent provides for airflow, expressed per linear foot or total square inches — it's smaller than the vent's physical opening because of louvers, mesh, or baffles. **Vent size** (the physical dimension of the product) is not the same number and shouldn't be used in the ventilation calculation.

*Practitioner usage:* "That soffit vent looks like a 4-inch strip, but its NFA rating is only 2.5 square inches per linear foot — that's the number that goes in the calc."

*Common misuse:* using a vent's physical opening size, or a marketing claim like "high airflow," in place of its actual rated NFA when sizing a system.

## 1:150 vs. 1:300 ventilation ratio

Both express required ventilating area as a fraction of attic floor area. **1:300** applies when intake and exhaust are balanced and a vapor retarder is present at the ceiling plane. **1:150** is the more conservative default required when either condition isn't met.

*Practitioner usage:* "No vapor retarder in this attic, so we size to 1:150, not 1:300 — the more forgiving ratio only applies when the other conditions are actually met."

*Common misuse:* defaulting to 1:300 on every job as "the modern number" without checking whether the balance and vapor-retarder conditions that ratio assumes are actually present.

## Steep-slope vs. low-slope roofing

**Steep-slope** roofing (generally 4:12 and steeper) relies on shingle lap and gravity to shed water quickly. **Low-slope** (roughly 2:12 to 4:12 for shingle-compatible systems, and anything flatter than that for membrane systems) has longer water dwell time on the surface and needs a different underlayment strategy or an entirely different system.

*Practitioner usage:* "That porch roof is 3:12 — it's low-slope, not steep-slope, even though it's the same shingle as the main roof. Double underlayment or full ice-and-water coverage, not the standard single layer."

*Common misuse:* treating slope as a continuous spectrum where "close enough to 4:12" gets the steep-slope treatment — the underlayment requirement is a threshold, not a gradient.

## Ice barrier vs. underlayment (general)

**Ice barrier** (ice-and-water shield) is a specific self-adhered membrane required by code in ice-dam-prone jurisdictions, sized to extend a defined distance past the interior wall line. **Underlayment** is the broader term for any water-resistant layer under the shingles, including standard felt or synthetic underlayment that isn't a self-adhered ice barrier.

*Practitioner usage:* "The eaves get ice barrier, the rest of the field gets standard synthetic underlayment — don't run ice-and-water-shield over the whole roof, it's not a general-purpose upgrade and it's not designed to be walked on as often as field underlayment."

*Common misuse:* calling any underlayment "ice and water shield," or assuming a roof with underlayment everywhere has satisfied the specific ice-barrier code requirement without checking whether it's the right membrane in the right location.

## Wind-resistance rating (ASTM D3161 vs. D7158)

**ASTM D3161** and **ASTM D7158** are two different test standards manufacturers use to rate shingle wind resistance, and they aren't directly comparable numbers even when both are expressed in mph — they use different test methods and different nailing patterns as part of the test. A shingle's rating on the wrapper only applies when installed with the specific pattern the test used.

*Practitioner usage:* "Don't compare this shingle's D7158 rating straight across to that one's D3161 rating like they're the same scale — check which standard and which nailing pattern each rating assumes."

*Common misuse:* treating any "mph" figure on a shingle wrapper as directly comparable across products without checking which test standard and nailing pattern produced it.

## Fall restraint vs. fall arrest

**Fall restraint** physically prevents a worker from reaching an edge or fall hazard at all (a fixed-length lanyard sized so a fall can't occur). **Fall arrest** allows the worker to reach the hazard and stops a fall in progress (via a shock-absorbing lanyard and rated anchor) — arrest systems must account for the forces of an actual fall, restraint systems don't.

*Practitioner usage:* "That lanyard's too long for restraint — if he can reach the edge, we're in fall-arrest territory and need the anchor rated for arrest forces, not just restraint."

*Common misuse:* using a restraint-length lanyard as if it provides arrest protection, or vice versa — sizing an arrest system's anchor as if only restraint forces apply.

## Anchor point rating vs. "something solid"

**Anchor point rating** is a certified or engineered load capacity (5,000 lb per worker for a non-certified single-use anchorage, or an engineered value with a 2x safety factor for reusable systems). "Something solid" is a visual judgment — a vent stack, a strap around a truss — with no verified rating behind it.

*Practitioner usage:* "I don't care how solid that vent pipe looks, it's not an anchor point until it's rated — clip into the engineered anchor or don't clip in at all."

*Common misuse:* treating structural appearance (thick pipe, doubled framing) as a substitute for a documented rating.

## Starter strip vs. first course

**Starter strip** is a purpose-made product (or a modified first course) installed along the eave to back up the gaps between the first visible course's tabs and provide the sealant line the wind rating depends on. **First course** is the first row of full shingles installed over the starter — the two are different products with different jobs, not the same thing under two names.

*Practitioner usage:* "Don't skip the starter strip to save a bundle — the wind rating on the first course assumes that sealant backup is there underneath it."

*Common misuse:* using an inverted or trimmed full shingle as "starter" without the correct adhesive placement, which looks similar from the ground but doesn't provide the same wind performance.

## Exposure vs. coverage

**Exposure** is the portion of each shingle course left visible to the weather (e.g., 5-5/8 in on a standard architectural shingle). **Coverage** is how many layers of material end up over any given point on the deck as a result of that exposure and shingle length (standard exposure gives double coverage; some products are designed for triple coverage). The two numbers are related but not interchangeable.

*Practitioner usage:* "Don't shortcut the exposure to stretch a bundle further — you're not just changing how much shows, you're cutting the coverage layers the product's wind and water rating assumes."

*Common misuse:* adjusting exposure to save material without recognizing that it changes coverage, which changes the tested performance of the system.
