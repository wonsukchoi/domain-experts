# Vocabulary

Terms generalists conflate or use loosely that operators keep sharply separate — the separation is usually where the actual craft judgment lives.

### Camera operator vs. Director of Photography (DP/Cinematographer)

The DP designs the visual language of the production — lighting, lens choice, camera movement style, overall look — and is accountable to the director for it. The operator executes that design physically, frame by frame, take by take, often without designing any of the lighting or lensing themselves.

**In use:** "The DP lit this for a 4-stop ratio key-to-fill and called a slow push on the 35 — my job is making sure that push lands exactly on her line and doesn't drift off the mark."

**Common misuse:** calling the operator "the DP" or "the cameraman" interchangeably on a scripted set, which erases a real division of labor and, on a union show, is also a jurisdictional distinction (IATSE Local 600 covers both, as separate classifications with separate rate cards).

### T-stop vs. f-stop

f-stop is a purely geometric ratio (focal length ÷ aperture diameter). T-stop is that same ratio corrected for the specific lens's actual light transmission, accounting for glass absorption and coating losses — cinema lenses are calibrated and marked in T-stops precisely because exposure consistency across a lens change matters more than theoretical geometry.

**In use:** "Both lenses read f/2.8 on paper, but the zoom is actually T3.1 and the prime is T2.8 — that's why the wide shot looked a third of a stop darker before we corrected."

**Common misuse:** treating f-stop and T-stop as interchangeable when swapping lenses mid-scene, which produces an exposure jump that's invisible on a waveform monitor calibrated to the wrong assumption but visible to the eye in the cut.

### Headroom vs. noseroom (lookspace)

Headroom is the vertical space between the top of a subject's head and the top of the frame. Noseroom (lookspace) is the horizontal space left in the direction a subject is looking or moving, distinct from simple rule-of-thirds framing.

**In use:** "Give her more noseroom camera-left — she's about to turn and address the room, and right now she's looking almost straight into the frame edge."

**Common misuse:** applying a single fixed headroom/noseroom rule across every shot size — the correct amount shrinks as the shot gets tighter (see `references/playbook.md`), so a wide-shot rule of thumb applied to a close-up reads as a framing mistake.

### 180-degree rule vs. axis of action

The axis of action (the "line") is the invisible line connecting two subjects, or a subject and its direction of travel, established the moment a scene's geography is set. The 180-degree rule is the practice of keeping every subsequent camera position on one side of that line so screen direction and eyelines stay consistent when shots are cut together.

**In use:** "We're on the north side of the line for this whole exchange — if the B-camera drifts past the actors' sightline axis, that shot's unusable against the reverse."

**Common misuse:** treating "the line" as a permanent, scene-independent rule rather than something that resets every time the scene's geography changes (a character walks through a door, a car turns) — operators who don't re-establish the axis after a geography change end up "crossing the line" without realizing a new line now exists.

### Reframe vs. re-compose vs. correction

A reframe is a planned, anticipated adjustment to follow expected motion (a subject standing up, a car turning a corner). A re-compose is a deliberate mid-shot change to a fundamentally different composition (widening for a second subject entering). A correction is an unplanned reaction to unexpected drift — necessary sometimes, but visible when overused.

**In use:** "That was a reframe, not a correction — we rehearsed her standing on that line."

**Common misuse:** calling every camera movement mid-take a "reframe," which obscures whether the movement was anticipated (fine) or reactive (a flag worth reviewing).

### Frame rate vs. shutter angle vs. shutter speed

Frame rate is how many images are captured per second. Shutter angle (film-derived convention, still used on digital cinema cameras) or shutter speed (video convention, in fractions of a second) governs how long each individual frame is exposed — independent of frame rate, though the 180-degree-shutter convention ties the two together by default.

**In use:** "We're shooting 24fps with a 180-degree shutter for the dialogue scenes, but I want 90 degrees on the chase — same frame rate, crisper motion."

**Common misuse:** assuming frame rate alone determines the "look" of motion; two shots at the same frame rate with different shutter angles produce visibly different motion blur, which is exactly how the *Saving Private Ryan* Omaha Beach sequence (45–90° shutter) reads differently from the rest of the film.

### A-camera vs. B-camera (vs. C-camera) operator

A-camera is the primary coverage camera on a multi-camera scripted setup, typically operated by the more senior operator and usually the one whose framing the DP is watching most closely. B-camera (and C-camera, on larger setups) captures simultaneous coverage from a second angle — not a "backup," a genuinely different, simultaneously-shot angle used to reduce the number of takes needed for full coverage.

**In use:** "B-camera, hold the wide the whole take — we're only doing this blocking twice, so get the master and the two-shot in the same pass."

**Common misuse:** treating B-camera as lower-priority or a safety net; on a scene with limited takes (stunts, difficult performances, expensive setups), B-camera's footage is often just as likely to make the cut as A-camera's.

### Safe area (broadcast-safe vs. multi-format-safe)

Broadcast-safe area historically meant keeping essential content inside a margin from the 4:3 or 16:9 frame edge to survive older CRT overscan. Multi-format-safe (the modern equivalent) means keeping essential content inside the narrowest aspect ratio the footage will be reversioned to — usually a vertical or square social crop from a 16:9 or wider capture.

**In use:** "Forget broadcast-safe, nobody's watching this on a CRT — what matters is whether she's still in frame after the 9:16 crop."

**Common misuse:** assuming "safe area" is a solved, legacy problem from the tube-TV era; it's a live concern again, just for a different downstream format.

### ND (neutral density) filter vs. variable ND

A neutral density filter reduces the amount of light reaching the sensor without altering color, used to maintain a desired aperture/T-stop and shutter angle combination in bright conditions. A variable ND is a single filter that can be dialed to different densities on the fly, common on run-and-gun and ENG work where lighting conditions change faster than filter swaps allow.

**In use:** "We're in full sun at T2.0 for the depth of field the DP wants — that's a 1.2 ND minimum, and I'd rather have the variable on for when we move into that shaded doorway."

**Common misuse:** treating ND purely as an exposure tool; the real reason it matters on a cinema set is that it lets the operator hold a specific T-stop (for the DP's chosen depth of field) and a specific shutter angle (for the desired motion blur) simultaneously, rather than trading one off against the other.
