# Red flags — signals a brownfield redevelopment specialist notices instantly

Smell tests with thresholds. Any one alone can be a fixable gap; two or more on the same acquisition or project is a pattern worth an immediate legal/technical review before proceeding.

### Acquisition scheduled to close with no completed AAI-compliant Phase I ESA on file
- **Usually means:** the buyer is about to lose landowner liability protections that cannot be established retroactively after closing — this is the single most common and most consequential brownfield mistake.
- **First question:** "Has the Phase I's interviews, records search, and site visit all been completed within the required 180-day window before this closing date?"
- **Data to pull:** Phase I ESA report and its completion dates for each required component, closing date, AAI compliance checklist (see `references/playbook.md` §1).

### A Phase I ESA on file is older than the required currency window relative to the planned closing date
- **Usually means:** relying on it for liability protection at closing may not actually establish the AAI defense, even if its findings are still technically accurate.
- **First question:** "Which specific components of this Phase I (interviews, records search, site visit) fall outside the 180-day window, and has an update been scoped?"
- **Data to pull:** Phase I ESA date-of-completion breakdown by component, planned closing date, cost/timeline to update the stale components.

### Remediation budget assumes the unrestricted-residential cleanup standard without checking it against the actual approved reuse plan
- **Usually means:** the project may be paying for remediation depth/extent the reuse plan doesn't actually require, if a commercial/industrial or restricted-residential standard with engineering controls would legally support the same footprint's intended use.
- **First question:** "Does every square foot planned for residential use actually require the unrestricted standard, or does part of the footprint's design allow a less restrictive standard?"
- **Data to pull:** site plan by footprint/parcel, cleanup-standard-to-reuse matrix (see `references/playbook.md` §2), Phase II delineation data by depth and location.

### An institutional control or deed restriction exists on the property with no corresponding disclosure in the purchase agreement or financing application
- **Usually means:** a lender or future buyer discovering this after closing turns a routine disclosure into a deal-threatening surprise and potential claim.
- **First question:** "Where in the transaction documents and financing application is this restriction explicitly stated?"
- **Data to pull:** recorded deed restriction/covenant, purchase and sale agreement language, financing application disclosure section, institutional control disclosure checklist (see `references/playbook.md` §4).

### A funding application submitted to a source whose eligibility depends on an earlier grant/enrollment that hasn't been secured yet
- **Usually means:** the application is likely to be rejected as premature, wasting the time spent preparing it, because the funding stack wasn't sequenced by dependency.
- **First question:** "What does this specific funding source's eligibility criteria require to already be in place, and is that condition actually met yet?"
- **Data to pull:** funding source's published eligibility requirements, current status of any prerequisite grant/enrollment/plan approval, funding stack sequencing worksheet (see `references/playbook.md` §3).

### An engineering control (vapor barrier, sub-slab depressurization system) with no assigned maintenance responsibility or recorded covenant
- **Usually means:** the control's long-term effectiveness depends on maintenance that may never happen without a specific, recorded, assigned obligation — a gap that surfaces years later as a re-emerged exposure pathway.
- **First question:** "Who is contractually and specifically obligated to maintain this system, and is that obligation recorded against the property?"
- **Data to pull:** engineering control design documents, any maintenance covenant or agreement, property ownership/management structure.

### A property acquisition involves a buyer affiliated with (common ownership, prior business relationship) the party that caused the original contamination
- **Usually means:** the BFPP defense may not be available regardless of how thorough the Phase I is, since BFPP status requires no affiliation with a liable party.
- **First question:** "Is there any ownership, familial, or business relationship between the buyer and any prior owner/operator of this site?"
- **Data to pull:** corporate ownership history of both buyer and prior owners/operators, chain-of-title records, any shared officers/directors.

### Community engagement on the redevelopment plan begins only after the remediation approach and site plan are finalized
- **Usually means:** objections that could have been addressed while the design was still flexible now require reopening decisions that are harder and costlier to change, and may jeopardize funding sources requiring community input as an eligibility condition.
- **First question:** "What community engagement has occurred so far, and at what stage relative to the remediation/reuse plan's finalization?"
- **Data to pull:** community engagement log and dates, remediation plan finalization date, any funding source's community-engagement eligibility requirements.

### Purchase price for a known-contaminated property is set as if the site were clean
- **Usually means:** this can undermine the good-faith element of a BFPP defense, which looks at whether the price reflects the contamination's presence, among other factors.
- **First question:** "Does the appraisal or purchase price analysis explicitly account for the known contamination and anticipated remediation cost?"
- **Data to pull:** appraisal report, purchase price negotiation history, comparable clean-site pricing for context.
