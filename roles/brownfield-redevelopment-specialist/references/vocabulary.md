# Brownfield redevelopment working vocabulary

Terms a brownfield redevelopment specialist uses precisely and a generalist blurs together. Format: definition → how a practitioner says it → common misuse.

**CERCLA (Superfund)** — The Comprehensive Environmental Response, Compensation, and Liability Act, the federal law imposing strict, joint-and-several, and retroactive liability for contamination cleanup costs on current and past owners/operators, regardless of fault.
In use: "CERCLA liability doesn't care that we didn't cause this contamination — without a landowner defense, we could still be on the hook for cleanup costs."
Misuse: assuming liability requires having caused or contributed to the contamination — CERCLA liability attaches to ownership/operation status itself, independent of fault, unless a specific defense applies.

**Bona fide prospective purchaser (BFPP)** — A CERCLA liability defense available to a buyer who acquires property after knowing of the contamination, provided they complete All Appropriate Inquiries beforehand, aren't affiliated with a liable party, and maintain specific post-acquisition compliance obligations.
In use: "We're buying this knowing it's contaminated — BFPP is our defense, which means the AAI has to be done right before closing, not after."
Misuse: assuming BFPP protection is automatic simply because the buyer didn't cause the contamination — it requires completing AAI and maintaining ongoing compliance obligations, not just innocence at the time of purchase.

**Innocent landowner defense** — A CERCLA liability defense for a buyer who did NOT know and had no reason to know of contamination at the time of purchase, having conducted appropriate inquiry beforehand — distinct from BFPP, which applies when the buyer DOES know about contamination before purchase.
In use: "This is an innocent landowner situation, not BFPP — the Phase I came back clean and nobody had reason to suspect otherwise at closing."
Misuse: conflating innocent landowner with BFPP — they apply to opposite factual situations (unknowing vs. knowing purchaser) and require different documentation to establish.

**All Appropriate Inquiries (AAI)** — The EPA-defined due diligence standard (40 CFR Part 312) a prospective purchaser must complete before acquisition to qualify for the BFPP or innocent landowner defenses, typically satisfied through a Phase I ESA meeting ASTM E1527 standards.
In use: "AAI isn't just 'get a Phase I' — it's specific currency requirements on interviews, records, and site visit that have to be met relative to the closing date."
Misuse: treating any Phase I ESA, regardless of age or completeness, as automatically satisfying AAI — specific components have currency windows (commonly 180 days) that must be met relative to the acquisition date.

**Recognized environmental condition (REC)** — A Phase I ESA finding indicating the presence or likely presence of a hazardous substance release, or a material threat of one, at the property.
In use: "The Phase I flagged a REC for the historical dry-cleaning operation — that triggers a Phase II to actually delineate the contamination."
Misuse: treating an REC finding as itself proof of contamination requiring remediation — it's a trigger for further investigation (Phase II), not a confirmed contamination finding on its own.

**Institutional control vs. engineering control** — An institutional control is a legal/administrative restriction (deed restriction, land use covenant) limiting property use to manage residual contamination risk. An engineering control is a physical measure (vapor barrier, cap, sub-slab depressurization system) that physically interrupts an exposure pathway.
In use: "The deed restriction is the institutional control preventing residential use here; the vapor barrier is the engineering control managing the vapor intrusion pathway if that ever changes."
Misuse: treating the two as interchangeable — an engineering control requires ongoing physical maintenance, while an institutional control requires ongoing legal/administrative enforcement; conflating them misses what kind of ongoing obligation actually exists.

**Risk-based cleanup standard** — A cleanup standard calibrated to a specific intended land use and its associated exposure pathways, as opposed to an unrestricted standard calibrated to the most conservative (typically residential, unlimited exposure) scenario.
In use: "We're remediating to the commercial risk-based standard for this footprint, not the unrestricted standard, because the design excludes residential use there."
Misuse: assuming a risk-based standard is a lesser or lower-quality cleanup — it's a legally valid, use-specific standard, not a shortcut, provided the land use restriction that justifies it is actually in place and enforced.

**Voluntary Cleanup Program (VCP)** — A state-run program allowing a property owner/developer to voluntarily investigate and remediate contamination under state regulatory oversight, typically in exchange for a liability assurance letter or covenant not to sue upon completion.
In use: "We're enrolling in the state VCP to get regulatory sign-off and the liability assurance letter once cleanup's done — that's what makes this financeable."
Misuse: treating VCP enrollment as merely a formality or funding source — it's the mechanism that produces the regulatory closure document lenders and future buyers actually rely on.

**Brownfield (EPA definition)** — Real property where expansion, redevelopment, or reuse may be complicated by the presence or potential presence of a hazardous substance, pollutant, or contaminant — a regulatory/funding-eligibility term, not simply "any formerly industrial site."
In use: "This qualifies as a brownfield under EPA's definition, which is what makes it eligible for assessment and cleanup grant funding."
Misuse: using "brownfield" loosely for any old industrial building regardless of actual contamination status — funding and liability-protection mechanisms are tied to the specific regulatory definition, not colloquial usage.

**Vapor intrusion** — The migration of volatile chemical vapors from contaminated soil or groundwater into the indoor air of an overlying or nearby building, a distinct exposure pathway from direct soil or groundwater contact.
In use: "Even though the soil contact pathway is capped by the deed restriction, vapor intrusion into any building on this footprint still needs its own engineering control."
Misuse: assuming an institutional control addressing soil contact (like a deed restriction against digging) also addresses vapor intrusion — they're separate exposure pathways requiring separate controls.
