# Underground Loader (LHD) Operator — Vocabulary

### LHD (Load-Haul-Dump)
An articulated, low-profile loader with a front bucket that digs, trams, and dumps blasted rock in one machine, diesel or battery-electric, ride-on or tele-remote capable.
**In use:** "Send the LHD up to four-level east, that round's ready to muck."
**Common misuse:** Calling any underground loader an "LHD" regardless of whether it's articulated load-haul-dump equipment versus a fixed-position loader; the articulated frame and haul function are what define the class.

### Mucking
Loading blasted rock (muck) out of a heading, as distinct from drilling, blasting, or ground support work.
**In use:** "We're still mucking the east drift, won't be ready for the drill jumbo until noon."
**Common misuse:** Using "mucking" loosely for any underground cleanup task; it specifically means loading out blasted material with a loader.

### Bootleg
A missed hole — explosive that failed to detonate in a blast round and remains live in the muck pile or the face.
**In use:** "Fired count's one short of drilled — treat it as a bootleg until the blaster confirms otherwise."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a visual inspection of the muck pile can rule out a bootleg; the only reliable check is reconciling the drilled-versus-fired hole count against the shot report.

### Scaling
Removing loose rock from the back and ribs with a bar or mechanical scaler after a blast, before ground support or mucking proceeds.
**In use:** "Scaling's only 60% done on that heading — hold the LHD on remote until it's signed off."
**Common misuse:** Treating "scaled" and "supported" as synonyms; scaling removes loose material, support (bolts, mesh, shotcrete) is a separate, subsequent step, and both must be complete for ride-on entry.

### Back
The roof or overhead surface of an underground opening.
**In use:** "There's a fresh crack in the back near the face — flag it before the next round."
**Common misuse:** Using "back" and "roof" interchangeably across coal and hard-rock contexts without noting that ground control plan requirements differ by commodity and mine.

### Ore pass
A vertical or steeply inclined excavated opening that ore is dumped into on one level and drawn out at a lower level, used to transfer material by gravity between levels.
**In use:** "Ore pass on three is nearly full — reroute to the waste pass until the truck clears it."
**Common misuse:** Confusing an ore pass (a gravity transfer opening between levels) with a drawpoint (the extraction point at the base of a stope where broken ore is accessed), which are distinct structures serving different stages of the material flow.

### Drawpoint
The opening at the base of a stope or draw column where broken ore is extracted by the LHD, as distinct from the ore pass it may feed into.
**In use:** "Drawpoint 12 is hung up — won't flow until it's blasted loose."
**Common misuse:** Treating drawpoint capacity issues (a hang-up in broken ore) as the same problem as ore-pass capacity issues (a downstream transfer point backing up); the causes and fixes differ.

### Remote-control (tele-remote) tramming
Operating the LHD via radio or line-of-sight remote control from outside the machine, used when the operator cannot legally or safely be positioned inside the cab.
**In use:** "Run it remote from the muck bay until the back's fully supported."
**Common misuse:** Treating remote control purely as a productivity drag; it exists as the compliance mode for mucking unsupported ground, not merely a slower option chosen out of excess caution.

### Fill factor
The percentage of a bucket's rated volumetric capacity actually achieved on a given pass, which varies with fragmentation and material stickiness.
**In use:** "Fill factor's down around 80% on this blocky ore — don't expect rated bucket tonnage per pass."
**Common misuse:** Assuming rated bucket capacity equals actual tons per pass; the fill factor and the material's loose bulk density both discount the rated number.

### Swell factor
The percentage volume increase of rock after blasting compared to its in-situ (unbroken) volume, due to voids created by fragmentation.
**In use:** "At 40% swell, that round's in-situ tonnage doesn't change, but the loose volume the LHD has to move does."
**Common misuse:** Applying swell factor to change the tonnage calculation; swell changes loose volume and bulk density, not the total mass of ore in the round.

### Oversize / secondary breakage
Blasted rock too large for the bucket, ore pass grizzly, or crusher, requiring a rock breaker or a secondary ("popping") shot before it can be handled normally.
**In use:** "Two oversize boulders at the face — call for the rock breaker before we can finish this round."
**Common misuse:** Treating a single oversize occurrence and a recurring oversize pattern as the same issue; a recurring pattern points to the drill-and-blast design, not the muck pile.

### DPM (diesel particulate matter)
Fine particulate emitted by diesel engines, regulated in underground metal/nonmetal mines by an 8-hour total-carbon exposure limit under 30 CFR §57.5060.
**In use:** "DPM's trending up in that heading — check the filter and cut the idle time before it becomes a compliance issue."
**Common misuse:** Treating a DPM exceedance as solved by better personal respiratory protection alone, when the regulation targets the measured exposure and the underlying source (ventilation, idle time, filter condition).

### Misfire
A blast hole that fails to detonate as intended, governed by the mine's approved procedure under 30 CFR Part 57, Subpart J, rather than by operator judgment call.
**In use:** "That's a misfire situation — barricade it and get the blaster, don't muck around it."
**Common misuse:** Treating "misfire" as informal shorthand for any blast-related delay; it specifically means a hole that didn't detonate and triggers a defined regulatory procedure.

### Payload
The actual weight of material carried in the bucket on a given pass, tracked against the machine's rated capacity via an onboard scale where fitted.
**In use:** "Payload's reading 15% over rated on that last pass — ease off, that's what's chewing through tires and brakes."
**Common misuse:** Equating a full-looking bucket with a payload within spec; visual fill and weight-based payload diverge with material density.

### Ramp grade
The slope of a haul ramp, expressed as a ratio (e.g., 1:7) or percentage, that governs the mine's design maximum for loaded LHD travel and braking requirements.
**In use:** "That temporary bypass ramp is steeper than 1:7 — derate payload and speed until it's regraded."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a machine's factory brake rating applies unchanged at any grade and any payload; rated braking capacity is grade- and payload-dependent, not a flat spec.
