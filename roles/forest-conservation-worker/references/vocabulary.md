# Vocabulary

Terms generalists conflate that field crews and foresters keep sharply separate. Each: definition, how a practitioner actually uses it in a sentence, and the common misuse to avoid.

## Stocking vs. density (TPA)

**Density** (trees per acre, TPA) is a raw count of live trees on the ground. **Stocking** is a proportion — the percent of survey plots meeting a minimum live-tree threshold, or live count against the target density — and is what a contract's survival clause actually measures.

*In use:* "We planted at 605 TPA, but the survey came back at 74% stocking — density tells you what went in the ground, stocking tells you what's still alive."

*Common misuse:* reporting the planting-time density as if it answers the survival question; a unit can be planted at full target density and still fail stocking six months later.

## J-root / L-root vs. root-bound (circling roots)

**J-root** and **L-root** describe a planting-technique defect — the root system folded sideways or upward because the hole or dibble slit was too shallow or narrow at the time of planting. **Root-bound** (circling or girdling roots) is a nursery-stock defect — the seedling was held too long in an undersized container before it ever reached the field.

*In use:* "This zone's failure is J-rooting, not root-bound stock — the defect only shows up in the compacted rows, not across the whole delivery."

*Common misuse:* treating any root deformity found at audit as a single category and blaming the planting crew when the defect was baked in at the nursery, or vice versa.

## Bare-root vs. containerized stock

**Bare-root** seedlings are lifted from a nursery bed with no soil around the roots, lighter and cheaper to transport but more sensitive to root desiccation and hole quality. **Containerized** stock ships with a soil plug intact, more forgiving of a slightly imperfect hole but heavier, slower to plant, and vulnerable to root-binding if held too long before shipping.

*In use:* "We're bare-root on this unit for cost, which means the crew has maybe 15 minutes of exposed-root time before survival starts dropping — containerized would buy us more slack but blow the budget."

*Common misuse:* assuming stock type doesn't change the planting-window discipline required, or ordering containerized stock as a fix for a planting-technique problem when the defect audit shows J-rooting, which containerized stock doesn't prevent.

## Fireline vs. fuel break vs. control line

**Fireline** is a cleared strip built during an active fire assignment to stop fire spread at that location. **Fuel break** is a pre-planned, permanent or semi-permanent strip of reduced fuel load built before a fire occurs, meant to slow spread and give suppression crews a place to hold. **Control line** is the general term covering both fireline and any other boundary (roads, streams, dozer line) used to contain a fire.

*In use:* "The ridge fuel break bought us time, but we still had to tie fresh fireline into it on the north flank where the break didn't extend."

*Common misuse:* using "fireline" for a feature built weeks before any fire started — that's a fuel break — or assuming an existing fuel break needs no fresh fireline tied in just because it's on the map.

## Flame length vs. fireline intensity

**Flame length** is a directly observable measurement (base of the flame to the tip) crews use to size line width in the field. **Fireline intensity** is a calculated value (rate of spread × fuel consumed × heat content) used in fire-behavior modeling and prediction, not something read off the fire by eye.

*In use:* "Flame length's running 6 feet on this flank — call it in, but don't quote intensity numbers over the radio, nobody can act on that in real time."

*Common misuse:* citing fireline intensity as if it were a field-observable number a crew uses to size line on the fly; it's a modeling input, and the field decision runs off flame length.

## LCES

Shorthand for Lookouts, Communication, Escape routes, Safety zones — the four conditions established before engaging fire and reassessed continuously as conditions change.

*In use:* "Fire's backing downslope now, which means our escape route just moved uphill through green fuel — re-set LCES before anyone goes back to the line."

*Common misuse:* treating LCES as a checklist completed once at shift briefing rather than a live condition; the classic failure is an escape route that was valid at the start of the shift and hasn't been reassessed as fire position changed.

## 10 Standard Fire Orders vs. 18 Watch Out Situations

The **10 Standard Fire Orders** are affirmative operating rules (know the weather, keep informed on fire status, base actions on current fire behavior). The **18 Watch Out Situations** are specific hazard conditions (fire not scouted, unburned fuel between crew and fire, no escape route) that, when present, are a signal to stop and reassess rather than a general reminder to be careful.

*In use:* "We've got Watch Out Situation four right now — unburned fuel between us and the fire — that's a stop, not a note in the log."

*Common misuse:* treating the 18 situations as a list to acknowledge rather than individual stop-work triggers; each one independently correlates with the fatality case histories behind the fire orders.

## Foliar vs. basal bark vs. cut-stump herbicide application

**Foliar** application sprays leaves during active growth for systemic uptake. **Basal bark** applies herbicide in an oil carrier around the lower stem of a standing, uncut plant, absorbed through bark. **Cut-stump** applies concentrated herbicide directly to a freshly cut stump face, timed to dormant season for most woody resprouters.

*In use:* "This privet stump-sprouts hard, so we're cutting and treating the stump within five minutes — foliar next spring would just mean spraying a thicket that's already resprouted."

*Common misuse:* picking application method by convenience (whichever sprayer is on the truck) rather than by species biology and season, which is the single biggest driver of "the herbicide didn't work" complaints.

## Pre-commercial vs. commercial thinning

**Pre-commercial thinning** removes excess stems from a young stand with no merchantable value in the removed wood, done purely to concentrate growth on the remaining trees. **Commercial thinning** removes merchantable-size stems that get sold, usually in an older stand, generating revenue alongside the stand-improvement effect.

*In use:* "This stand's at 800 stems an acre and eight years old — that's a pre-commercial thin, nobody's buying four-inch pulpwood at this stage."

*Common misuse:* assuming any thinning operation should generate revenue, or delaying a pre-commercial thin waiting for merchantable size, which lets the stand's growth concentrate on the wrong stems in the meantime.

## Site prep: mechanical vs. chemical vs. burn

Three distinct methods to clear a site of competing vegetation and logging debris before planting: **mechanical** (disking, chopping, raking), **chemical** (herbicide applied to competing vegetation pre-plant), and **burn** (broadcast burning of slash and debris). Sites commonly use a combination, not one exclusively.

*In use:* "We're chemical site-prep only on this unit — too steep for the disk, and the burn permit window closed before we could get to it."

*Common misuse:* treating "site prep" as a single undifferentiated step in a contract instead of naming which method, since the method drives both the planting-window timing and what competing vegetation will look like at year one.

## Browse damage vs. planting mortality

**Browse damage** is deer, rabbit, or other wildlife feeding on seedlings after a successful planting — the tree went in correctly and died or was damaged afterward. **Planting mortality** covers seedlings that failed from the planting event itself (J-root, desiccation, poor hole). Both show up as a dead seedling at survey; the cause and the fix are unrelated.

*In use:* "Half this failure is browse, not planting mortality — check for clipped terminal buds before blaming the crew's technique."

*Common misuse:* attributing all first-year mortality to planting technique without checking for browse sign, which sends the fix toward retraining a crew when the actual fix is fencing or repellent.
