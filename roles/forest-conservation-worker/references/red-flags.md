# Red flags

Smell tests a crew lead or forester catches in the first walk-through of a unit or a burn assignment. Format for each: what it usually means, the first question to ask, and the data to pull before deciding.

## 1. First-year stocking survey below 80% for the whole unit

**Usually means:** the failure is clustered in one sub-area (skid trail, drought pocket, browse-heavy edge) rather than spread evenly, or the stock itself was root-bound at delivery — a unit-wide replant order issued before checking which is far more expensive than it needs to be.

**First question:** "Break the plot data down by sub-area — is the failure clustered or scattered?"

**Data to pull:** plot-level survival by sub-area, GPS overlay of equipment traffic/skid trails against the survey grid, original crew-zone assignment log, nursery lift/ship date for the stock used.

## 2. A planter's daily count is well above 1,200 trees/day on rough or compacted ground

**Usually means:** hole depth or root spread is being shortcut to hit count under piece-rate pressure, not superior technique — flat, loose-ground rates don't transfer to rough terrain.

**First question:** "Pull 20 random trees from that planter's row today and check root position."

**Data to pull:** planting-quality audit sample (straight/J-root/L-root classification), per-planter daily tally, terrain and soil-compaction notes for that row.

## 3. Fireline built to one width regardless of fuel type along its length

**Usually means:** the crew sized the line to the flame length and fuel model at the start point and didn't re-check as fuel type changed along the run.

**First question:** "What's the current flame length and fuel model on this section, and does the line width scale with it?"

**Data to pull:** current fire-behavior observation by section, NWCG fuel model assigned per section, measured line width at several points along the run.

## 4. Herbicide "reapplication" requested within days of the initial treatment

**Usually means:** the first application missed the species' active-growth or dormancy window (mode-of-action/timing mismatch), or spray drift damaged non-target crop trees and the reapplication request is actually mislabeled damage control.

**First question:** "Was the initial application inside the label's temperature and growth-stage window for this species?"

**Data to pull:** application log (date, product, rate, weather at time of spray), target species' phenology stage on the application date, off-target damage survey on adjacent stock.

## 5. Crew describes a Watch Out Situation as "we were just being careful" instead of stopping work

**Usually means:** normalization of deviance around fireline safety protocol — treating a listed hazard condition as a caution level rather than the stop-work trigger it's designed to be.

**First question:** "Which of the 18 Watch Out Situations applied here, and did the crew lead sign off to continue?"

**Data to pull:** IRPG checklist completion for the shift, radio log around the time in question, incident safety officer notes if one was assigned.

## 6. A full-unit replant is ordered immediately after a failed stocking survey

**Usually means:** the survey wasn't broken down by sub-area before the seedling order went in, wasting stock and labor replanting acreage that was already above threshold.

**First question:** "Show me the plot-level survival map before we order seedlings — is the failure uniform or clustered?"

**Data to pull:** sub-area survival breakdown, root-defect audit results by sub-area, cost comparison of partial vs. full replant.

## 7. An invasive species is declared "controlled" after a single treatment pass

**Usually means:** the treatment killed visible above-ground growth but missed the seed bank or a resprout-capable root system — a one-pass declaration ignores species-specific regrowth biology.

**First question:** "What's the resprout or germination count at 6 and 12 months post-treatment, not at 6 weeks?"

**Data to pull:** follow-up monitoring transect at 6 and 12 months, species-specific dormancy/seed-bank data, treatment date against the species' growth cycle.

## 8. A fuel break is sized only to the day's wind forecast

**Usually means:** the design didn't account for gust factor or a possible wind shift, which is the most common way an otherwise adequate-looking fuel break gets breached.

**First question:** "What wind speed was used to size this break — sustained or gust — and what's the shift risk in the forecast window?"

**Data to pull:** spot weather forecast, nearest RAWS station data, as-built fuel-break width against the NWCG guideline for the fuel model present.

## 9. A crew consistently finishes acreage well under the budgeted number of days

**Usually means:** planters are walking wider spacing than the contract specifies to hit a daily count, undershooting target density — or the crew is genuinely efficient; the difference matters and isn't visible from the schedule alone.

**First question:** "Spot check actual tree-to-tree spacing on a transect against the specified spacing."

**Data to pull:** measured in-ground spacing sample, seedlings actually used against seedlings ordered, calculated TPA from a plot count.

## 10. Container stock shows circling or girdling roots at the first-year audit

**Usually means:** the stock was root-bound before it ever left the nursery — held too long in an undersized container — rather than a planting-technique defect in the field.

**First question:** "How long was this stock held after lifting, and was it root-pruned or shifted to a larger container at the nursery?"

**Data to pull:** nursery lift/ship date, container size and stock type, root-inspection photos from the audit sample compared against bare-root J-root rates from the same unit.
