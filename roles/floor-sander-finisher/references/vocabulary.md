# Vocabulary

Terms generalists conflate that a floor finisher keeps sharply separate, because the difference changes what you do next.

### Sanding vs. screening

**Sanding** removes material with a drum/edger/orbital machine and a grit progression, cutting down to bare or near-bare wood. **Screening** (also called buffing) abrades only the very top of the cured finish film with a screen or pad on a floor buffer, to promote mechanical adhesion for the next coat — it removes almost no wood.

**In use:** "This floor's only worn in the traffic lanes — we don't need to sand it, a screen-and-recoat gets you a fresh topcoat for half the price."

**Common misuse:** calling a between-coat screening pass "sanding," which leads someone to run overly aggressive equipment or grit on a step that only needs to scuff the surface.

### Moisture content (MC) vs. relative humidity (RH)

**MC** is the percentage of water in the wood itself, measured with a moisture meter. **RH** is the percentage of water vapor in the surrounding air. Wood MC moves toward the level implied by the room's temperature and RH (its equilibrium moisture content, EMC) but lags behind — a room can read comfortable on a hygrometer while the wood itself is still well outside range.

**In use:** "Room's reading 45% RH, that's fine, but the wood under that rug is still sitting at 11% — check the meter, not just the room."

**Common misuse:** monitoring only room RH with a hygrometer and assuming the wood itself is in range, without ever putting a moisture meter on the flooring.

### Cupping vs. crowning

**Cupping** is when a board's edges sit higher than its center, almost always from the underside or edges being wetter than the top/center. **Crowning** is the opposite — center higher than edges — and on a refinished floor it's frequently the aftermath of sanding a cupped floor flat before the moisture evened out.

**In use:** "That crown along the wall isn't a new problem, it's last year's cupping — someone sanded it flat before the moisture dropped, and now it's finished drying unevenly."

**Common misuse:** treating crowning as a new, unrelated defect instead of checking whether it's a cupping episode that was sanded flat too early.

### Flash time vs. recoat time vs. full cure

**Flash time** is how long a coat needs before it's safe to apply the next one without trapping solvent. **Recoat time/window** is the manufacturer's stated range — often bounded on both ends — during which the next coat will bond properly without screening. **Full cure** is when the film has finished cross-linking and reached its final hardness, commonly measured in days to weeks, not hours.

**In use:** "It's dry to the touch, which just means flash is done — we're nowhere near the 14-day full cure this rug needs."

**Common misuse:** treating "dry to the touch" or "walkable" as equivalent to full cure, and putting rugs or furniture pads down early enough to imprint a still-soft film.

### Oil-modified urethane vs. moisture-cure urethane vs. waterborne

**Oil-modified urethane** cures by solvent evaporation and oxidation, ambers over time, and has a moderate recoat window. **Moisture-cure urethane** cross-links using ambient humidity, is far more sensitive to RH swings during application and cure, and has strong solvent odor. **Waterborne** finishes dry fast via water evaporation, stay clear/non-ambering, and are the most humidity-tolerant of the three during cure.

**In use:** "This is a moisture-cure job, not standard oil-modified — if that storm drops the humidity in here, the cure schedule changes, not just the smell."

**Common misuse:** lumping all non-waterborne finishes together as "oil-based," missing that moisture-cure urethane has a distinct humidity sensitivity oil-modified doesn't share.

### Shiners

Spots the sander missed — old finish or a shallow scratch left behind — invisible under normal work lighting and only visible once a raking (low-angle) light crosses the floor, often not caught until the finish is already on.

**In use:** "Kill the overheads and bring in the trouble light at a low angle before we stain — that's the only way you'll catch the shiners."

**Common misuse:** declaring a floor fully sanded because it looks even under normal room lighting, skipping the raking-light check that actually reveals missed spots.

### Picture framing (perimeter gap pattern)

A pattern where gaps open specifically around the room's perimeter while the field stays tight, from boards fastened differently near the walls or from the expansion gap at the wall line taking up seasonal movement that the field distributes elsewhere.

**In use:** "Those gaps are picture-framing around the perimeter, not random shrinkage — that's a seasonal movement pattern, not defective boards."

**Common misuse:** assuming perimeter-only gapping means bad material, when it's frequently a normal movement pattern tied to how the perimeter is fastened relative to the field.

### Water-popping

Wetting bare, sanded wood (usually an open-grain species like oak) before staining to raise the grain and open the pores, producing deeper, more even stain absorption than staining dry wood directly.

**In use:** "This oak's going to blotch if we stain it dry — water-pop it first and let it dry fully before you put stain down."

**Common misuse:** skipping water-popping on a blotch-prone species to save a step, or over-wetting the floor and not letting it dry completely before staining, which raises the grain unevenly and requires another light sanding pass.

### Tack cloth vs. vacuum-only dust removal

A **tack cloth** is a resin-impregnated cloth that picks up fine dust by tackiness. Some tack cloths leave a light residue that can interfere with waterborne finish adhesion; **vacuum-only** dust removal (sometimes followed by a barely-damp microfiber pass for waterborne jobs) avoids that residue risk entirely.

**In use:** "Skip the tack cloth on this one, it's going under waterborne — just vacuum and do a final pass with a barely damp cloth."

**Common misuse:** using a standard tack cloth on every job regardless of finish type, without checking whether it's rated compatible with waterborne systems.

### Grit-sequence skip rule

The practice of never skipping a full grit level when progressing through a sanding sequence (e.g., 36 to 60 to 80/100, never 36 straight to 100), because each step's job is removing the previous step's scratch pattern, not just refining the surface further.

**In use:** "We're not skipping straight to 100 to save a pass — the 36-grit scratches will still be under there."

**Common misuse:** treating grit numbers as a single continuum where "higher is just finer," rather than understanding each step must fully clear the prior step's scratch depth before advancing.
