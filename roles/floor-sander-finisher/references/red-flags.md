# Red flags

Smell tests a lead finisher catches during the job or shortly after, before a defect becomes a callback.

### Board edges read 1/16 in or more higher than centers (cupping) before sanding

- **Usually means:** subfloor or wood moisture content is elevated relative to the field average — a leak, high crawl-space humidity, or a slab with no vapor barrier.
- **First question:** "Has there been a leak, appliance failure, or persistent damp area anywhere near this section, even one that's since been fixed?"
- **Data to pull:** moisture-meter readings on the wood and subfloor at the cupped section versus the field average; any plumbing or HVAC service history for the room.

### Sanding the floor flat produces a crowned (center-high) board within weeks

- **Usually means:** the floor was sanded flat while still cupped and wetter than its surroundings; as it finished drying, the previously high edges — now sanded down — ended up lower than the center.
- **First question:** "What was the moisture differential logged before this floor was sanded, and was it actually inside the stabilization threshold or just close?"
- **Data to pull:** pre-sand moisture log for the section; time elapsed between sanding and the crowning appearing (faster appearance points to a larger differential that was pushed through anyway).

### Craters or fisheye spots appear in the wet finish film

- **Usually means:** silicone or oil contamination in the wood pores (furniture polish, pet dander, wax residue not fully removed by sanding) repelling the finish locally.
- **First question:** "What was this floor cleaned or polished with before the job, and did we run a test patch before committing the whole floor?"
- **Data to pull:** job history / prior maintenance products used on the floor; test-patch results if one was done; whether the contaminated area corresponds to a specific traffic pattern (near a door, under a rug) consistent with polish transfer.

### White haze or blush appears in the finish film, worse in humid weather

- **Usually means:** moisture or condensation interfered with cure — humidity too high during application/cure, or (for moisture-cure/conversion finishes) the cure reaction itself was disrupted by excess ambient moisture.
- **First question:** "What was the shop temperature and relative humidity during application and for the first several hours after?"
- **Data to pull:** environmental log for the application window; product TDS's stated humidity ceiling; whether any doors/windows were opened mid-cure, introducing a humidity spike.

### Faint cross-grain scratch lines visible under raking light after the final coat

- **Usually means:** the edger's scratch pattern (which runs across the grain near walls and in corners) wasn't fully blended into the field by hand-sanding or buffing before finish went down.
- **First question:** "What grit did the final hand-sand or buffer pass on the edger areas actually use, and was it checked under raking light before staining or finishing?"
- **Data to pull:** grit-sequence log for edger vs. drum passes; whether a raking-light inspection was documented before finish application.

### Washboard ridges or chatter marks in the sanded surface

- **Usually means:** the drum sander was allowed to dwell in one spot, has a dull or unevenly worn belt, or is vibrating from an imbalanced drum.
- **First question:** "Was the sander kept moving continuously across this section, and when was the belt/drum last serviced?"
- **Data to pull:** operator's pass log for the section; sander maintenance/belt-change record.

### A second or third coat delaminates or peels shortly after application

- **Usually means:** the recoat window was exceeded without screening before the new coat, or the prior coat was contaminated (dust, oil) before recoating.
- **First question:** "How many hours had passed since the previous coat, and was the surface screened before this one went on?"
- **Data to pull:** coat-by-coat timing log; screening step confirmation; ambient temperature/humidity at each coat (both affect how fast the window closes).

### Board gaps open up beyond roughly 1/8 in within the first year after installation or a major refinish

- **Usually means:** the flooring was installed or the site was too wet relative to its eventual in-service moisture level, and the wood has since dried and shrunk.
- **First question:** "What was the wood-to-subfloor moisture differential logged at install, and did the material get its full acclimation period on site?"
- **Data to pull:** install-day moisture log; acclimation duration logged before installation; regional seasonal EMC range for the install location.

### Visible airborne dust with no local exhaust running during a sanding pass

- **Usually means:** the sander's dust collection isn't connected, isn't running, or the filter/bag is full — beyond the health issue, fine wood dust is a documented combustible-dust hazard (NFPA 664).
- **First question:** "Is the dust collector actually on and pulling, and when was the filter or bag last checked?"
- **Data to pull:** dust-collection equipment log; filter/bag change record; any open flame or spark source (space heater, power tool) in the same room.

### Oily rags left balled up in a trash can or pile after an oil-based application

- **Usually means:** standard spontaneous-combustion precautions weren't followed — linseed and other drying oils in oil-based finishes oxidize exothermically as they cure, and a balled-up rag traps that heat instead of dissipating it.
- **First question:** "Where are today's finish rags right now, and are they in water or laid flat to dry, or just in the trash?"
- **Data to pull:** site safety checklist / end-of-day rag disposal confirmation.

### Waterborne finish specified directly over an existing oil-based or waxed finish with no abrasion or test patch

- **Usually means:** the job is being treated as a simple recoat without checking whether the existing film and the new chemistry are actually compatible — a common cause of adhesion failure and fisheye.
- **First question:** "What's actually on this floor right now, and did a test patch confirm the waterborne coat bonds before the whole floor was committed?"
- **Data to pull:** solvent-rag test result identifying the existing finish; test-patch outcome if one was run.

### Customer wants furniture or an area rug back within 48-72 hours of the final coat

- **Usually means:** the timeline is being planned against "dry" or "walkable," not the product's actual full-cure schedule, which commonly runs 7-14 days (waterborne) to roughly 30 days (oil-based/oil-modified) before the film can take a rug or a furniture pad without permanent marking.
- **First question:** "Is the plan built around this product's walkable time or its full-cure time — and does the customer know those are different dates?"
- **Data to pull:** the finish's TDS cure schedule; the customer's actual move-back-in date versus that schedule.
