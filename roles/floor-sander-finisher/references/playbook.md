# Playbook

Filled sequences and calculations a refinish crew actually runs, not descriptions of them.

## 1. Grit progression, full refinish (worked, 3/4-in red oak strip, existing oil-based poly)

| Pass | Grit | Purpose | Machine |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | 36 | Strip old finish, remove surface unevenness, cut through any minor cupping remnants | Drum sander, with the grain |
| 2 | 60 | Remove the 36-grit scratch pattern | Drum sander, with the grain |
| 3 | 80-100 | Remove the 60-grit scratch pattern; final drum pass for an oil-based finish | Drum sander, with the grain |
| 4 | matching edger sequence (36/60/80-100) | Bring perimeter and corners to the same scratch depth as the field | Edger |
| — | 100-120 hand/buffer pass | Blend edger's cross-grain scratches into the field | Buffer with sanding screen, with the grain |

**Waterborne exception:** go one step finer on the last pass (100→120, sometimes 120→150) before a waterborne topcoat. Waterborne film has less amber tone to mask scratch depth than oil-based poly, so scratches that were invisible under an amber finish show under a clear one.

**Rule never to break:** don't skip a full grit level (e.g., 36 straight to 100). Each step's job is removing the previous step's scratch pattern — skip one and that pattern survives underneath the final grit, invisible under work lighting, visible under raking light or a clear topcoat sheen.

**Drum technique notes:**
- Never let the drum sit still while spinning and in contact with the floor — an instant gouge, not a gradual one.
- Overlap each pass by roughly 2-4 in.
- Only angle the first (coarsest) pass diagonally (roughly 7-15°) to knock down an uneven or lightly cupped floor faster; every subsequent pass runs straight with the grain, because a diagonal scratch from a later grit is much harder to remove and is exactly what shows as chatter under sheen.

## 2. Moisture-differential check (worked)

**Targets (NWFA):** wood flooring in the 6-9% MC range in most of the continental US; installed flooring within 4% of subfloor MC for strip flooring under 3 in wide, within 2% for wide plank (>3 in).

**Worked reading set, 900 sf red oak strip job:**

| Location | Wood MC | Subfloor MC | Differential |
|---|---|---|---|
| Main field (average of 6 spot checks) | 8% | 9% | 1 pt — in range |
| Kitchen-adjacent zone (former leak) | 11% | 14% | 5 pts — over the 4-pt threshold, and subfloor is above the 9% ceiling outright |

**Decision:** main field proceeds to sanding. Kitchen zone is deferred — dehumidify/fan for 5-7 days, re-test both wood and subfloor, and only sand once the differential is back under 4 points and the subfloor is back under roughly 9-12% depending on regional norms. Sanding it flat at 5 points over threshold removes material from boards that are still going to keep drying and shrinking — the edges you cut down today are not where the board will settle once it's done moving.

## 3. Finish coverage and coat-count calculation (worked)

**Formula:** sq ft of floor ÷ manufacturer's coverage rate (sq ft/gal) × number of coats = gallons required. Add 10-15% for waste/touch-up, round up to available can sizes.

**Worked, 800 sf, waterborne satin @ 550 sf/gal, 3 coats (1 seal + 2 topcoats):**

```
800 sf ÷ 550 sf/gal = 1.4545 gal per coat
1.4545 gal/coat × 3 coats = 4.36 gal total
4.36 gal × 1.13 (13% waste buffer) = 4.93 gal
→ order 5 × 1-gal cans
```

**Oil-modified urethane comparison (same 800 sf, typically ~450 sf/gal, 2 coats over a stained/sealed floor):**

```
800 sf ÷ 450 sf/gal = 1.778 gal per coat
1.778 gal/coat × 2 coats = 3.56 gal
3.56 gal × 1.13 = 4.02 gal
→ order 4 × 1-gal cans
```

## 4. Recoat and cure schedule templates

**Waterborne, same-day 3-coat schedule at 70°F / 45% RH (within typical product spec):**

```
AM: sand, vacuum, tack
10:00a  Seal coat
1:00p   Coat 2   (2-3 hr recoat window at these conditions)
4:00p   Coat 3 (final)

Day 2  (~24 hr): walkable in socks/soft soles
Day 3:           furniture back, felt pads required
Day 14:          full cure — area rugs OK from this point
```

**Adjustment for cooler/damper shop conditions (60°F / 65% RH):** recoat window roughly doubles — plan 2 coats/day instead of 3, or push the third coat to the next morning. Always check the product's TDS table for the actual adjustment factor; this is a planning heuristic, not a substitute for the spec sheet.

**Oil-modified urethane, 2-coat schedule:**

```
Day 1: stain (if applicable), let dry per product spec (commonly 24 hr)
Day 2: Coat 1 (seal/first coat), AM
Day 3: Screen lightly, vacuum, tack, Coat 2 (final), AM
       (recoat window for oil-modified poly commonly runs 12-24 hr —
        if the gap exceeds ~48 hr, screen before recoating even if
        the schedule above was followed, since ambient conditions vary)

Day 4-5: walkable
Day 6-8: furniture back, felt pads
Day 30-ish: full cure — area rugs and heavy traffic pads
```

**Before the first oil-based/oil-modified coat in an occupied structure:** confirm gas pilot lights (water heater, furnace) are extinguished or the space is isolated/ventilated, and any oily rags go into a water-filled metal container or are laid flat outdoors to dry — never balled up in a trash can.

## 5. Gap-filling with sanding dust (worked)

Collect dust from the 60-80 grit pass (fine enough to blend, coarse enough to still be visibly wood, not the final 100-120 grit dust which is too fine and lightens the mix). Mix with a slow-set finish or dedicated filler to a peanut-butter consistency, work into gaps with a putty knife across the grain, let flash per the filler's schedule, then proceed to the final sanding pass — the fill needs to be sanded level with the same final grit as the rest of the floor, not left proud.
