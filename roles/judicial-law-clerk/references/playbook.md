# Bench memo and draft opinion playbook

Filled worksheets for the recurring judicial-clerk tasks: standard-of-review framework, record-citation discipline, and the steelman-both-sides worksheet.

## 1. Standard of review by procedural posture

| Posture | Standard | What it means for the analysis |
|---|---|---|
| Motion to dismiss (12(b)(6)) | De novo | Accept well-pleaded facts as true, draw reasonable inferences for non-movant; legal sufficiency only |
| Summary judgment | De novo | No deference to district court's legal conclusion; view record facts in light most favorable to non-movant; genuine dispute of material fact defeats it |
| Findings of fact after bench trial | Clear error | Substantial deference — reverse only if left with definite and firm conviction a mistake was made |
| Discretionary rulings (evidentiary, case management, sanctions) | Abuse of discretion | Reverse only if no reasonable judge could have so ruled, or ruling rests on legal error/clearly erroneous fact |
| Agency factual findings (administrative appeal) | Substantial evidence | Uphold if a reasonable mind could accept the evidence as adequate, even if reviewing court might have decided differently |
| Statutory/constitutional interpretation | De novo | No deference; pure question of law |

Rule: state the standard in the first section of the memo, before any merits discussion — it determines what has to be shown and how much deference the analysis owes the ruling below.

## 2. Record citation discipline

Every factual assertion in a bench memo or draft opinion needs a specific citation, in this form:

```
[Factual assertion]. (R.[page], or R.[page]:[line] for transcript; Ex.[number] for exhibits.)
```

- ❌ "The witness testified she wasn't aware of the reviews." (no citation — unusable)
- ✅ "The HR director testified she had not reviewed the performance write-ups before the termination decision. (R.88.)"

Rule: a fact without a record citation doesn't go in the memo — "I recall the brief saying..." is not a substitute for pulling the actual record page.

## 3. Steelman-both-sides worksheet

Before drafting a recommendation, fill this for every material issue:

```
Issue: <the specific legal/factual question>

Party A's strongest argument (even if not their brief's best-articulated version):
  - <argument>
  - <supporting record cite / authority>

Party B's strongest argument (even if not their brief's best-articulated version):
  - <argument>
  - <supporting record cite / authority>

What the district court's opinion / prior brief did NOT address:
  - <gap>

Clerk's assessment (clearly marked as clerk's view, not binding conclusion):
  - <reasoning, with the standard of review applied>
```

Never skip filling both sides even when one seems obviously weaker — the point is surfacing what the judge hasn't seen yet, not confirming what's already assumed.

## 4. Citation validity check

For every case cited (in either party's brief, or independently found), verify before relying on it:

- [ ] Has it been reversed, vacated, or overruled?
- [ ] Has it been questioned or distinguished by a later case in the same jurisdiction, especially on the specific point it's cited for?
- [ ] Has the underlying statute or rule it interprets been amended since the decision?
- [ ] Is it still binding in this jurisdiction (not just persuasive from another circuit)?

A case cited in a brief that hasn't been re-verified at drafting time is not confirmed good law — verify independently regardless of which party cited it or how confidently.

## 5. Recusal/conflict screening checklist (case intake)

- [ ] Judge's prior employment, financial interests, or family relationships checked against parties/counsel in the case
- [ ] Clerk's own prior employment, pending job offers, or personal relationships checked against parties/counsel
- [ ] Any check flagged escalated to the judge immediately, before substantive work begins on the case
- [ ] Screening repeated if new parties or counsel are added mid-case (e.g., intervenors, substituted counsel)

Run this at assignment, not after memo drafting has already begun — a conflict discovered late wastes the work and complicates disclosure.
