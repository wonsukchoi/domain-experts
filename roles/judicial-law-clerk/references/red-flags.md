# Red flags — signals a judicial law clerk notices instantly

Smell tests with thresholds. Any one alone can be a fixable gap in a draft memo; two or more together on the same case is a pattern worth flagging to the judge before the memo or draft opinion is finalized.

### A brief's cited authority hasn't been independently re-verified for current validity
- **Usually means:** the brief's citations may be stale — a case can be overruled, distinguished, or superseded by statute after the brief's research was done, and neither party may have caught it.
- **First question:** "Has every case this brief relies on been Shepardized/KeyCited as of today, not just accepted from the brief's citation?"
- **Data to pull:** citation-validity check results for each cited authority (see `references/playbook.md` §4), date the brief was filed versus today's date.

### A factual assertion in a draft memo has no specific record citation
- **Usually means:** the assertion may be recollection or inference rather than something the record actually supports, which is unusable in a memo meant to survive scrutiny.
- **First question:** "What specific record page or transcript line supports this exact sentence?"
- **Data to pull:** the draft memo's factual claims cross-referenced against record citations (see `references/playbook.md` §2).

### The standard of review isn't stated before the merits analysis begins
- **Usually means:** the analysis may be built on the wrong deference level, which can invalidate the whole memo once the correct standard is identified.
- **First question:** "What's the controlling standard of review for this specific procedural posture, and does the analysis actually apply it?"
- **Data to pull:** procedural posture of the case, standard-of-review table (see `references/playbook.md` §1), draft memo's analysis structure.

### A draft memo only presents one side's argument in its strongest form
- **Usually means:** the memo may leave the judge exposed to a strong counterargument that hasn't been surfaced, especially if it happens to align with the memo writer's or judge's initial inclination.
- **First question:** "What's the strongest version of the other side's argument, even if their brief didn't articulate it well?"
- **Data to pull:** steelman-both-sides worksheet (see `references/playbook.md` §3) for the material issues in the case.

### A circuit split or genuinely unresolved legal question is presented as settled law
- **Usually means:** the judge may be deciding under an assumption of settled doctrine when the actual state of the law is contested, which matters for how the opinion is written and what risk of reversal/further review exists.
- **First question:** "Is this actually resolved in this circuit, or is there a split/unresolved question the memo is smoothing over?"
- **Data to pull:** case law research across circuits on the specific question, any pending certiorari petitions or en banc proceedings on point.

### A new case assignment hasn't been screened for a recusal or conflict issue
- **Usually means:** significant work could be invested before a disqualifying conflict is discovered, wasting effort and complicating disclosure.
- **First question:** "Has this case been run through the recusal/conflict screening checklist at assignment?"
- **Data to pull:** recusal/conflict screening checklist (see `references/playbook.md` §5), parties and counsel list for the case, judge's and clerk's relevant prior relationships.

### Deliberative material (draft opinions, bench memo reasoning, chambers discussion) discussed outside chambers
- **Usually means:** a confidentiality breach, even if inadvertent or seemingly minor (e.g., mentioning a case's likely outcome to a friend or former colleague), which the clerk's non-disclosure obligation covers absolutely and permanently.
- **First question:** "Was this discussed with anyone outside chambers, in any form, including implication of how a case is trending?"
- **Data to pull:** nature and recipient of the disclosure, timing relative to the case's pendency (the obligation doesn't end when the case closes).

### A draft opinion advances a doctrinal theory the judge hasn't actually endorsed
- **Usually means:** significant drafting effort may be wasted if the judge's actual reasoning diverges from the theory once reviewed, since the draft is a tool for the judge to adopt or redirect, not the clerk's own position to advance.
- **First question:** "Has the judge actually indicated this is the theory they want to proceed on, or is this the clerk's own preferred framing?"
- **Data to pull:** any chambers conference notes or judge's stated inclination, draft memo's recommendation section versus the judge's actual direction.
