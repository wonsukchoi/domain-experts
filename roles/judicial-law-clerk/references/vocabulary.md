# Judicial law clerk working vocabulary

Terms a judicial law clerk uses precisely and a generalist blurs together. Format: definition → how a practitioner says it → common misuse.

**De novo review** — Review with no deference to the lower court/agency's conclusion — the reviewing court decides the question fresh, typically applied to pure questions of law (statutory interpretation, summary judgment, motions to dismiss).
In use: "Summary judgment is reviewed de novo — we're not deferring to the district court's read of the record, we're re-examining it ourselves."
Misuse: applying de novo review's lack-of-deference to a factual-finding posture (like post-bench-trial findings), where clear error, not de novo, is the correct standard.

**Abuse of discretion** — A deferential standard applied to discretionary rulings (evidentiary calls, case management, sanctions), reversed only if no reasonable judge could have so ruled, or the ruling rests on a legal error or clearly erroneous factual finding.
In use: "The evidentiary exclusion is reviewed for abuse of discretion — even if we might have ruled differently, that's not enough to reverse."
Misuse: treating "I would have decided this differently" as sufficient grounds for reversal under an abuse-of-discretion standard — the bar is materially higher than de novo disagreement.

**Clear error** — The standard applied to a trial court's factual findings (typically after a bench trial), reversed only if the reviewing court is left with a definite and firm conviction a mistake was made.
In use: "The finding on damages is reviewed for clear error — we don't reweigh the evidence, we ask if the finding is plausible given the whole record."
Misuse: reweighing the evidence directly as if conducting a fresh factual inquiry, rather than asking the narrower question of whether the existing finding is plausible on the record.

**Bench memo** — An internal, confidential memorandum a clerk prepares for the judge analyzing the legal issues, record, and recommended disposition of a motion or appeal, distinct from a draft opinion (which is written for eventual public issuance).
In use: "The bench memo lays out both sides' strongest arguments and a recommendation — the judge decides whether and how to turn that into an opinion."
Misuse: drafting a bench memo in the style of a public-facing opinion (persuasive, one-directional) rather than as an internal analytical tool meant to inform the judge's independent decision.

**Pretext (McDonnell Douglas framework)** — In employment discrimination law, the third step of the burden-shifting framework where a plaintiff shows the employer's stated non-discriminatory reason for an action is not the real reason, often through evidence like after-the-fact justification, shifting explanations, or procedural irregularities.
In use: "The performance documentation was compiled after the termination decision — that's textbook after-the-fact pretext evidence, not just a weak defense."
Misuse: treating a dispute over whether an employee's performance was actually poor as the pretext question — pretext is about whether the stated reason was the *real* reason, which can be shown through timing and process irregularities even if the underlying performance claim isn't directly disputed.

**Shepardizing / KeyCiting (citation validity check)** — The practice of checking whether a cited case has since been overruled, reversed, distinguished, questioned, or superseded by statute, using a citator service (Shepard's on Lexis, KeyCite on Westlaw).
In use: "Shepardize every case in the brief before relying on it — one of appellee's three cited cases was distinguished by a later en banc decision they didn't catch."
Misuse: assuming a case cited in a party's brief has already been verified as current good law by that party — briefs go stale, and independent verification at drafting time is the clerk's own responsibility.

**Recusal vs. disqualification** — Recusal is a judge voluntarily withdrawing from a case due to a potential conflict; disqualification is the formal, often statutorily-governed process (e.g., 28 U.S.C. § 455) by which a judge is required to step aside, sometimes on motion by a party.
In use: "This isn't a discretionary recusal call — the financial interest triggers mandatory disqualification under § 455."
Misuse: treating the two terms as interchangeable — disqualification can be legally mandatory in ways recusal (as a voluntary act) is not, and misidentifying which applies changes the procedural obligations involved.

**Standard of review** — The degree of deference an appellate or reviewing court gives to a lower court or agency's decision, determined by the type of ruling being reviewed (factual finding, legal conclusion, discretionary call), not by the case's subject matter.
In use: "Don't analyze the merits yet — what's the standard of review for this specific ruling, and does that change what we even need to show?"
Misuse: assuming the standard of review is uniform across an entire case — a single appeal can involve different standards for different rulings within it (e.g., de novo for a legal question, abuse of discretion for an evidentiary ruling in the same appeal).

**Record citation** — A specific reference to the exact location in the official trial or administrative record (transcript page/line, exhibit number) supporting a factual assertion, as distinct from a general characterization of what happened.
In use: "Don't just say the witness testified she wasn't aware — cite the exact transcript page where she said it."
Misuse: relying on a party's brief's characterization of the record instead of pulling and citing the actual record location, which risks propagating a brief's mischaracterization or convenient omission.

**Deliberative confidentiality** — The absolute and ongoing obligation to keep chambers discussions, draft opinions, and the reasoning behind pending decisions confidential, extending beyond the case's resolution and beyond the clerk's term.
In use: "That case closed two years ago, but the deliberative confidentiality obligation doesn't expire — still can't discuss how the judge reasoned through it."
Misuse: assuming the confidentiality obligation ends when the case is decided or when the clerkship ends — it's a permanent, not time-limited, obligation.
