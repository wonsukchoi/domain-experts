# Vocabulary

### Warp and weft
In woven fabric, the warp is the set of lengthwise threads held under tension on the loom; the weft (or fill) is the thread woven crosswise through the warp.
**In use:** "The streak runs down the warp direction — that's a single-end tension issue, not a weft/pick problem."
**Common misuse:** Confusing which direction a defect runs relative to warp versus weft — a warp-direction (lengthwise) defect points to a specific end's tension, while a weft-direction (crosswise, repeating along the length) defect points to a specific pick or mechanical cycle event, and misreading the direction sends troubleshooting to the wrong system.

### Let-off tension
The controlled tension applied to each warp end as it feeds off the warp beam during weaving, set individually or in groups depending on the loom's tensioning system.
**In use:** "That end's let-off tensioner slipped — it's feeding slack instead of holding 250 grams like the rest of the beam."
**Common misuse:** Assuming let-off tension is set once and stays fixed for the whole run without monitoring — a slipping or worn individual tensioner can develop mid-run, producing a defect that wasn't present at start-up.

### Gauge (knitting)
The number of needles or stitches per unit width on a knitting machine, determining the fabric's stitch density and directly affecting both appearance and fit.
**In use:** "This job's set to a 12-gauge — check the needle selection cam matches that before running the full order."
**Common misuse:** Treating gauge as purely a finished-product specification rather than a machine setup parameter to verify before running — a subtle gauge/needle-selection mismatch at setup can produce a full run that's visually plausible but off-spec on close measurement.

### Heddle
A wire or cord component on a loom, through which a warp end passes, that raises or lowers to create the weave pattern's shed (the gap the weft passes through).
**In use:** "Defect repeats every so many picks — check the heddle at that position in the draft, not the yarn."
**Common misuse:** Overlooking heddles as a source of a repeating defect and defaulting to a yarn-quality explanation — a specific damaged or misthreaded heddle produces a defect tied to the loom's mechanical cycle, not the yarn.

### Weave draft
The specification defining how warp ends are threaded through heddles and how the loom's shedding mechanism operates, determining the resulting weave pattern.
**In use:** "Double-check the draft against the job ticket before threading — a threading error here shows up as a systematic pattern flaw across the whole run."
**Common misuse:** Treating draft setup as a one-time task to complete rather than a configuration to verify against the specific job's specification — an incorrect draft produces a consistent, run-length defect that a casual visual check of "does it look woven" won't catch.

### Pick
A single pass of the weft thread across the loom, through the warp shed, in weaving.
**In use:** "That repeating flaw shows up every 40 picks — points to a specific mechanism in the loom's cycle, not random yarn variation."
**Common misuse:** Confusing "pick" (a single weft pass) with "pick count" (picks per inch, a fabric density measurement) — the two terms describe different things and mixing them up misstates either a defect's interval or the fabric's actual density spec.

### Stitch density
The number of stitches per unit area in knitted fabric, analogous to pick count in woven fabric, determining fabric weight and drape.
**In use:** "Stitch density measured off-spec even though the roll looked fine visually — the gauge setting drifted somewhere in the run."
**Common misuse:** Relying on visual inspection alone to judge stitch density — a subtle gauge drift can be visually unremarkable but still measurably off the job's specification, which is why a measured check matters beyond a visual pass.

### Selvedge
The finished, self-bound edge of a woven fabric that prevents the weft from unraveling, distinct from a cut edge.
**In use:** "Measuring the defect's position from the left selvedge — that's the fixed reference point for tracking a specific warp end."
**Common misuse:** Using an arbitrary or shifting reference point to locate a defect's cross-width position instead of a fixed landmark like the selvedge — without a consistent reference, tracing a defect back to a specific end or needle becomes unreliable.

### Shuttle, rapier, and air-jet looms
Different mechanical systems for inserting the weft thread across the warp — shuttle looms carry the weft on a physical shuttle, rapier looms use a rigid or flexible arm, air-jet looms use compressed air to propel the weft.
**In use:** "This defect pattern's consistent with an air-jet nozzle timing issue, not something we'd see on the rapier looms."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a defect diagnosis or fix transfers directly between different loom types — each weft-insertion mechanism has its own characteristic failure modes, and troubleshooting one type using another type's known issues can misdirect the investigation.

### Yarn break rate
The frequency of yarn breaks per unit time or unit production, tracked to distinguish isolated incidents from a developing mechanical or material trend.
**In use:** "Break rate's climbing on the third shift — that's not random, something's changing on that machine or in this yarn lot."
**Common misuse:** Treating each yarn break as an independent, isolated event to simply repair and move past — without tracking rate and location over time, a developing trend (worn component, degrading yarn lot) goes unnoticed until it produces a larger failure.
