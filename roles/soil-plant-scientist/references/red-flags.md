# Soil and Plant Scientist — Red Flags

### Soil-test P below 15 ppm (Bray-P1) on a field with no buildup history on record
- **Usually means:** chronic under-application over multiple years, or a newly rented/acquired field with unknown fertility history.
- **First question:** what's this field's manure and fertilizer application history for the last five years?
- **Data to pull:** prior soil-test records, fertilizer purchase and application logs, manure-application records if livestock is/was on the operation.

### pH below 5.5 on a field targeting corn or soybean (target range 6.0-6.8)
- **Usually means:** years of ammonium-based nitrogen application without offsetting lime (acidifying), or naturally acidic parent material never limed.
- **First question:** when was lime last applied, and at what rate?
- **Data to pull:** lime application history, buffer-pH lab result to size the correction.

### Soil-test K trending down more than 20 ppm across two consecutive test cycles despite a maintenance-rate K program
- **Usually means:** actual K removal exceeds the grain-only removal rate assumed in the plan (silage or hay harvest instead of grain), or the soil's CEC is low enough that applied K is leaching before crop uptake.
- **First question:** has harvest type changed (grain to silage/hay), and what's the field's CEC?
- **Data to pull:** harvest-type records, CEC value from the most recent soil test.

### Yield goal set more than 15% above the field's trailing 5-year actual yield-monitor average
- **Usually means:** the goal was copied from a neighboring field, a whole-farm average, or a marketing target rather than built from this field's own history.
- **First question:** what does the field-level yield-monitor data actually show, not the farm-wide number?
- **Data to pull:** field-specific yield-monitor history for the last five seasons.

### CEC below 8 meq/100g with a single large spring K application planned
- **Usually means:** high leaching risk before the crop can take up the applied K, especially ahead of a wet spring.
- **First question:** can the K be split pre-plant/sidedress, or switched to a slower-release source?
- **Data to pull:** soil texture and CEC report, planned application timing and rate.

### Base saturation Ca:Mg ratio far outside the 4:1-6:1 range (e.g., below 2:1) cited as the reason for a corrective lime/gypsum program
- **Usually means:** a naturally Mg-heavy soil (dolomitic limestone parent material) being mistaken for a problem requiring correction — modern trial data shows the ratio itself rarely limits yield if absolute Ca and Mg levels are each independently sufficient.
- **First question:** are the absolute Ca and Mg levels sufficient on their own, regardless of the ratio?
- **Data to pull:** full base-saturation breakdown (not just the Ca:Mg ratio), absolute Ca and Mg ppm values.

### Phosphorus soil-test level above 60 ppm (Bray-P1) on a field near a water body or under tile drainage
- **Usually means:** legacy manure application has built soil P well past agronomic need, into an environmental runoff-risk zone.
- **First question:** what's the field's Phosphorus Index or state-equivalent runoff-risk rating?
- **Data to pull:** P-Index assessment, tile-drainage map, distance to the nearest water body.

### A recommendation table applied to a soil-test report from an unfamiliar or out-of-state lab without checking the extraction method
- **Usually means:** a Mehlich-3 result is being read against a Bray-P1-calibrated table (or vice versa), which can produce a rate wrong by a wide margin since the two methods extract different chemical fractions.
- **First question:** which extraction method did this lab actually use, and does the recommendation table match it?
- **Data to pull:** the lab report's methodology section.

### Sampling density below one composite sample per 2.5 acres on a field with visible yield-map variability
- **Usually means:** the composite sample is averaging out real within-field fertility variation, producing a flat-rate recommendation that over-fertilizes the field's low-need zones and under-fertilizes its high-need zones.
- **First question:** does a yield-monitor map show enough within-field variability to justify a grid- or zone-sampling upgrade?
- **Data to pull:** current sampling grid density, yield-monitor variability map if available.

### Lime applied within 60 days of planting with an expectation of full pH correction that season
- **Usually means:** the reaction-time requirement (typically 6-12 months for full effect, depending on particle fineness) was underestimated when the plan was sequenced.
- **First question:** is this a maintenance lime application, or a correction for a significant pH deficit that needed a longer runway?
- **Data to pull:** lime source fineness (mesh size), application date relative to planting date.
