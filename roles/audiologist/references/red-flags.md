# Audiologist — Red Flags

### Sudden sensorineural hearing loss — onset over ≤72 hours, unilateral, ≥30 dB across 3 contiguous frequencies
- **Usually means:** Idiopathic sudden sensorineural hearing loss (most common), less commonly viral, vascular, or autoimmune cause.
- **First question:** Exactly when did the loss start, and has it been evaluated by a physician yet — this is time-sensitive for oral/intratympanic steroid treatment, which loses effectiveness the longer it's delayed.
- **Data to pull:** Onset timeline, audiogram confirming the 30 dB/3-frequency threshold, any recent viral illness or vascular risk factors.

### Significant interaural asymmetry — PTA or word-recognition score differing meaningfully between ears
- **Usually means:** Unilateral pathology (acoustic neuroma, unilateral Meniere's, noise/trauma to one ear) rather than the bilateral symmetric pattern typical of presbycusis or ototoxicity.
- **First question:** Is there any associated tinnitus, vertigo, or facial numbness on the affected side?
- **Data to pull:** Full audiometric comparison between ears, word-recognition scores, ABR if retrocochlear pathology is suspected.

### Word-recognition score substantially worse than the pure-tone audiogram predicts
- **Usually means:** Retrocochlear pathology (e.g., vestibular schwannoma) or a central auditory processing problem, rather than straightforward cochlear hearing loss.
- **First question:** Was the mismatch confirmed on rescreen, and is it unilateral?
- **Data to pull:** Repeat word-recognition testing, ABR results, imaging referral status.

### Conductive hearing loss with a normal-appearing eardrum and no clear history
- **Usually means:** Otosclerosis, ossicular chain discontinuity, or a middle-ear mass — not always visible on otoscopy alone.
- **First question:** Family history of hearing loss, especially conductive loss beginning in early adulthood?
- **Data to pull:** Tympanogram type, acoustic reflex results, ENT referral status.

### Air-bone gap present but bone-conduction thresholds also elevated (mixed pattern) misread as pure sensorineural
- **Usually means:** A real conductive component is being overlooked because the overall severity label (driven by the elevated AC) looks like a routine sensorineural loss.
- **First question:** Was the ABG calculated explicitly at each frequency, or was only the PTA severity label reviewed?
- **Data to pull:** Frequency-by-frequency AC and BC thresholds, not just the averaged PTA.

### Hearing aid dispensed without real-ear verification
- **Usually means:** The fitting is programmed to the audiogram on paper but has not been confirmed to actually deliver the prescriptive target in this patient's ear canal.
- **First question:** Was REM performed, and at what input levels?
- **Data to pull:** REM printout showing target vs. measured insertion gain across at least soft/average/loud input levels.

### Vertigo episodes lasting seconds and provoked by head position
- **Usually means:** Benign paroxysmal positional vertigo (BPPV) — canalith debris in a semicircular canal.
- **First question:** Does rolling over in bed or looking up reliably trigger it?
- **Data to pull:** Dix-Hallpike test result, nystagmus direction and latency if positive.

### Vertigo with central neurological symptoms (diplopia, dysarthria, limb weakness, severe headache)
- **Usually means:** A central (brainstem/cerebellar) cause rather than a peripheral vestibular one — this is a medical emergency differential (e.g., stroke), not a routine audiology referral.
- **First question:** Are any of these symptoms present now, and how did they start?
- **Data to pull:** None from audiology — refer for immediate medical evaluation before continuing the vestibular workup.

### Progressive bilateral hearing loss in a patient on an ototoxic medication
- **Usually means:** Drug-induced ototoxicity (e.g., aminoglycosides, platinum-based chemotherapy, high-dose loop diuretics) — often high-frequency first and may be permanent if the medication continues unmonitored.
- **First question:** What medication, what dose, and how long has the patient been on it?
- **Data to pull:** Baseline vs. current audiogram if available, medication timeline, communication with the prescribing physician.

### Child failing a newborn hearing screen or with delayed speech/language milestones
- **Usually means:** Congenital or early-onset hearing loss requiring urgent diagnostic audiometry — early intervention timelines matter significantly for language development outcomes.
- **First question:** Has diagnostic ABR or behavioral audiometry been completed, and at what age?
- **Data to pull:** Screening results, diagnostic testing timeline against recommended intervention windows (commonly cited as identification by 3 months, intervention by 6 months — confirm against current early-hearing-detection-and-intervention guidelines).
