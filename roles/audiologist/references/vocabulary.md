# Audiologist — Vocabulary

### Air-bone gap (ABG)
The difference between air-conduction and bone-conduction thresholds at a given frequency, expressed in dB.
**In use:** "There's a 35 dB air-bone gap across the low frequencies, so this is conductive, not sensorineural."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a single number for the whole audiogram rather than calculated per frequency — a gap that's significant at 500 Hz but closes at 4000 Hz points to a different (and more specific) middle-ear pathology than a uniform gap.

### Pure-tone average (PTA)
The average of air-conduction thresholds at 500, 1000, and 2000 Hz (sometimes including 4000 Hz), used as a summary severity index.
**In use:** "PTA is 52.5 dB, moderate loss — but check the ABG before deciding what that means clinically."
**Common misuse:** Used as if it fully describes the hearing loss — it discards both the shape (sloping vs. flat) and the type (conductive vs. sensorineural vs. mixed), both of which change the management plan.

### Word-recognition score (WRS)
The percentage of monosyllabic words correctly repeated at a comfortable, audible presentation level, measuring how well speech is understood once it's been made loud enough to hear.
**In use:** "WRS of 88% is consistent with intact cochlear function, given the conductive-loss classification."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to always track pure-tone severity proportionally — a poor WRS relative to a mild pure-tone loss is a specific red flag for retrocochlear pathology, not a testing artifact to be dismissed.

### Conductive hearing loss
Hearing loss caused by a problem in the outer or middle ear (wax, effusion, otosclerosis, ossicular discontinuity) that impedes sound transmission to the inner ear.
**In use:** "Normal bone conduction with a significant air-bone gap — this is conductive, refer to ENT before amplification."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to always be visible on otoscopy — otosclerosis in particular can present with a completely normal-looking eardrum.

### Sensorineural hearing loss (SNHL)
Hearing loss caused by damage to the cochlea (inner ear) or the auditory nerve pathway, where both air- and bone-conduction thresholds are elevated together.
**In use:** "Both AC and BC are elevated by roughly the same amount with minimal gap — that's sensorineural."
**Common misuse:** Treated as automatically permanent and untreatable in all cases — some causes (e.g., sudden sensorineural hearing loss from certain etiologies) are time-sensitive to treat medically, not simply candidates for a hearing aid.

### Mixed hearing loss
A combination of conductive and sensorineural components — bone conduction is elevated (a real sensorineural component exists) but there's also a meaningful air-bone gap on top of it.
**In use:** "Mixed loss — the sensorineural piece won't reverse, but the conductive piece is still worth an ENT workup."
**Common misuse:** Collapsed into "sensorineural" because the BC threshold is elevated, missing the treatable conductive component riding on top of it.

### Real-ear measurement (REM)
Probe-microphone measurement of the actual sound level produced by a hearing aid inside a specific patient's ear canal, used to verify the fitting against a prescriptive target.
**In use:** "REM shows we're 7 dB under target for soft speech — adjust the low-input gain before dispensing."
**Common misuse:** Skipped in favor of trusting the fitting software's simulated/predicted output, which does not account for that patient's individual ear-canal acoustics.

### NAL-NL2 / DSL v5.0
Prescriptive fitting formulas that calculate target hearing-aid gain by frequency and input level from a patient's audiogram, used as the verification target during real-ear measurement.
**In use:** "Programmed to NAL-NL2, but we still need to verify it's actually hitting that target in the ear."
**Common misuse:** "Programmed to [formula]" is treated as equivalent to "verified" — the formula generates a target; REM confirms whether the aid is actually meeting it in that ear.

### Tympanometry
A test of middle-ear mechanical function (compliance and pressure) using a probe tone, classified into types (A, As, Ad, B, C) based on the resulting curve shape.
**In use:** "Type As tympanogram is consistent with a stiffened middle-ear system — raises suspicion for otosclerosis."
**Common misuse:** Interpreted as a hearing test on its own — tympanometry measures middle-ear mechanics, not hearing sensitivity, and doesn't substitute for an audiogram.

### Acoustic reflex
The involuntary contraction of the stapedius muscle in response to a loud sound, tested to help localize a hearing-loss site and screen for certain retrocochlear or facial-nerve pathologies.
**In use:** "Absent acoustic reflexes on that side, combined with the WRS mismatch, is enough to push us toward an ABR referral."
**Common misuse:** Ordered and reported without being integrated into the differential — an absent reflex is a data point that changes the referral urgency, not a routine box to check.

### Auditory brainstem response (ABR)
An electrophysiological test measuring the auditory nerve and brainstem's response to sound, used for retrocochlear workup, newborn/difficult-to-test hearing assessment, and estimating hearing thresholds objectively.
**In use:** "Given the WRS mismatch, an ABR is warranted to rule out a retrocochlear lesion before we finalize this as routine sensorineural loss."
**Common misuse:** Treated as interchangeable with a standard audiogram — ABR estimates thresholds and screens neural pathway integrity but is not a full behavioral hearing evaluation.

### Otoacoustic emissions (OAEs)
Sounds generated by healthy outer hair cells in the cochlea in response to a stimulus, measurable in the ear canal and used for newborn screening and quick cochlear-function checks.
**In use:** "OAEs are present bilaterally, so cochlear outer-hair-cell function looks intact — the problem is more likely retrocochlear or middle-ear."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a full hearing test — present OAEs indicate cochlear function but say nothing about the neural pathway beyond the cochlea, and OAEs can be absent even with normal hearing if there's a mild conductive component.

### Presbycusis
Age-related, typically bilateral, symmetric, high-frequency-predominant sensorineural hearing loss.
**In use:** "Gradual, symmetric, high-frequency pattern in a 75-year-old — consistent with presbycusis, routine workup timeline."
**Common misuse:** Used as a default explanation for any older adult's hearing loss without confirming the pattern is actually gradual and symmetric — a sudden or asymmetric loss in an older adult is not "just presbycusis" and needs the sudden/asymmetric workup pathway instead.

### Ototoxicity
Hearing (or balance) damage caused by a medication or chemical exposure, often dose-dependent and sometimes irreversible.
**In use:** "Progressive bilateral high-frequency loss starting after chemotherapy initiation — screen for ototoxicity and flag to the oncology team."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to always be reversible once the causative medication stops — some ototoxic damage (e.g., from platinum-based chemotherapy or aminoglycosides) is permanent, which changes the urgency of catching it early with monitoring audiograms.
