# Exercise Trainer — Vocabulary

### 1RM (One-Repetition Maximum)
The maximum load a person can lift for a single complete repetition of an exercise with correct form.
**In use:** "We're not testing a true 1RM this cycle — we'll estimate it from a 5-rep set and program off that."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a fixed, permanently-known number rather than something that changes with training status and needs periodic re-estimation.

### DOMS (Delayed-Onset Muscle Soreness)
Dull, diffuse muscle soreness that peaks 24–72 hours after unaccustomed or high-intensity exercise, distinct from acute injury pain.
**In use:** "That's DOMS from Tuesday's new leg volume, not an injury — light movement will help it clear."
**Common misuse:** Used as a catch-all label for any post-exercise discomfort, including sharp or mid-set pain that doesn't fit the DOMS timeline and should be treated as a possible injury instead.

### RPE (Rate of Perceived Exertion)
A subjective scale (commonly 6–20 Borg, or a simplified 0–10) a client uses to report how hard an effort felt, used alongside or instead of a percentage-of-1RM prescription.
**In use:** "Take that last set to an RPE of 8 — you should have about 2 reps left in the tank."
**Common misuse:** Treated as an objective, comparable-across-clients measure — RPE is self-reported and calibration varies significantly by individual and training experience.

### Progressive overload
The principle that a training stimulus (load, volume, or intensity) must gradually increase over time for continued adaptation.
**In use:** "We're progressing load by about 5% a week on this block — anything faster and recovery won't keep up."
**Common misuse:** Applied as "more is always better" rather than "more, within what the body can adapt to" — ignoring recovery capacity turns progressive overload into overtraining.

### Deload
A planned period of reduced training volume or intensity (commonly 30–40% reduction) inserted periodically to allow recovery before the next progression block.
**In use:** "This is week 4 — deload week, then we start the next progression block at a slightly higher baseline."
**Common misuse:** Skipped as unnecessary when a client is "still making progress," which is often exactly the situation a deload is meant to protect.

### Periodization
Structuring a training program into planned phases (blocks) with varying volume, intensity, or focus over weeks or months, rather than repeating the same session indefinitely.
**In use:** "We're in the hypertrophy block right now — strength block starts in three weeks."
**Common misuse:** Confused with simply "having a program" — periodization specifically means the plan varies systematically over time, not that a plan exists at all.

### Movement compensation
A deviation from ideal movement mechanics that the body uses to work around a mobility, stability, or strength limitation.
**In use:** "The knee valgus on her squat is a compensation for weak glute medius, not a cue problem."
**Common misuse:** Corrected with verbal instruction alone ("don't let your knees cave") without addressing the underlying limitation the compensation is signaling.

### PAR-Q+ (Physical Activity Readiness Questionnaire Plus)
A standardized pre-participation screening questionnaire used to identify individuals who should seek physician clearance before starting or increasing exercise intensity.
**In use:** "He flagged 'yes' on the joint-condition question, so he needs clearance before we load that knee."
**Common misuse:** Treated as intake paperwork to file rather than a screening gate that changes what programming is appropriate.

### Overhead-squat assessment
A standardized movement screen (arms overhead, bodyweight squat) used to observe compensations across the ankle, knee, hip, spine, and shoulder in one movement pattern.
**In use:** "Her overhead squat showed forward lean and heel rise — that's pointing at ankle mobility, not a core issue."
**Common misuse:** Scored as pass/fail rather than recorded as specific, location-by-location compensation findings that inform which corrective exercise to assign.

### Training age
How long a client has been consistently training, used to calibrate how aggressively load or volume can progress (novices adapt faster; advanced trainees need smaller, slower increments).
**In use:** "He's got a low training age even though he's 45 — we can progress him faster than the rep-range chart alone would suggest."
**Common misuse:** Conflated with chronological age — a novice 50-year-old and a novice 20-year-old both start with fast initial adaptation, just with different absolute-load ceilings.

### Corrective exercise
An exercise specifically selected to address a movement-screen finding, distinct from a general-fitness exercise chosen for conditioning or strength.
**In use:** "The banded lateral walk is corrective for her knee valgus, not just a warmup filler."
**Common misuse:** Assigned once and never reassessed, so there's no evidence it's actually resolving the compensation it was chosen for.
