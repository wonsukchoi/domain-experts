# Phlebotomist — Vocabulary

### Order of draw
The CLSI-specified sequence for filling multiple tube types in a single venipuncture, designed to prevent additive carryover between tubes.
**In use:** "Cultures first, then the citrate tube, then everything else — order of draw isn't optional just because the requisition lists them differently."
**Common misuse:** Treating requisition listing order as draw order — they're unrelated documents serving different purposes.

### Additive carryover
Trace contamination of one tube's additive into a subsequently drawn tube via the needle or stopper, capable of skewing that tube's results.
**In use:** "Carryover from a clot-activator tube into a coag tube drawn right after it is exactly what order of draw prevents."
**Common misuse:** Assuming carryover only matters for grossly visible contamination — even trace amounts affect sensitive assays like coagulation testing.

### Positive patient identification
Confirming a patient's identity using two independent identifiers (typically name plus DOB or MRN) before specimen collection, matched against both the patient and the requisition/label.
**In use:** "Two identifiers, spoken by the patient when possible — that's positive ID, not just glancing at the wristband."
**Common misuse:** Treating a single identifier (just a room number, or just a name without DOB/MRN cross-check) as sufficient.

### Hemolysis
Rupture of red blood cells releasing intracellular contents (notably potassium) into serum/plasma, visible as pink-to-red discoloration and capable of falsely elevating certain analyte results.
**In use:** "This tube's grossly hemolyzed — the potassium and LDH are unusable, but glucose on the same tube is probably fine."
**Common misuse:** Rejecting an entire panel for hemolysis when only specific hemolysis-sensitive analytes are actually affected.

### Discard tube
A tube drawn and discarded (not sent for testing) before a coagulation tube, when using a winged/butterfly collection set, to clear dead-space air from the tubing and preserve accurate fill volume.
**In use:** "Citrate's first in the sequence and we're on a butterfly set — draw a discard tube first."
**Common misuse:** Skipping the discard tube because "blood is already flowing," which under-fills the citrate tube by the volume of displaced tubing air.

### Fill volume / draw volume
The actual amount of blood collected into a tube, measured against that tube's stated nominal (vacuum-designed) draw volume, expressed as a percentage.
**In use:** "2.6 out of 2.7 mL — that's 96% fill, well above the 90% minimum for a citrate tube."
**Common misuse:** Eyeballing a tube as "looks full" instead of checking the actual fill line against the stated minimum, especially for citrate tubes where the ratio matters precisely.

### Specimen rejection criteria
The documented set of conditions (underfill, hemolysis, clotting, mislabeling, wrong tube type) under which a lab will refuse to process a specimen and require recollection.
**In use:** "This one hits two rejection criteria — underfilled and no time-of-draw on the label."
**Common misuse:** Treating rejection criteria as a judgment call on the phlebotomist's competence rather than an objective, documented threshold that protects result accuracy.

### CLSI (Clinical and Laboratory Standards Institute)
The standards-development organization whose guidelines (e.g., GP41 for venous specimen collection) define widely adopted best practices for specimen collection, though individual labs' SOPs govern actual practice and may be stricter.
**In use:** "CLSI GP41 is where the order-of-draw sequence comes from — but check the lab's own SOP if it differs."
**Common misuse:** Treating CLSI guidelines as binding regulation rather than a widely adopted standard that facility policy implements (and sometimes modifies).

### Wrong blood in tube (WBIT)
A specimen-identification failure where blood from one patient ends up labeled and processed as another patient's specimen — one of the most serious phlebotomy errors, capable of causing a fatal transfusion reaction if undetected.
**In use:** "WBIT is exactly what positive patient ID and bedside labeling exist to prevent."
**Common misuse:** Underestimating the severity — this isn't a quality-control inconvenience, it's a patient-safety sentinel event.

### Butterfly / winged collection set
A blood-collection device with a short, flexible tube between the needle and the tube holder, typically used for small, fragile, or difficult-to-access veins.
**In use:** "Small veins, going with a butterfly set — remember the discard tube if citrate's first."
**Common misuse:** Forgetting that winged sets introduce tubing dead-space air, which affects the first tube drawn (especially citrate) differently than a straight-needle draw.

### Tourniquet time
The duration a tourniquet remains applied before and during a draw; prolonged application (typically over ~1 minute) can cause hemoconcentration and affect certain lab values.
**In use:** "Tourniquet's been on almost two minutes — release and let it rest before trying again, or the potassium and other values will run high."
**Common misuse:** Leaving a tourniquet on through multiple failed attempts without releasing, compounding both patient discomfort and result-accuracy risk.

### Analyte stability window
The maximum time a specimen can sit at a given temperature before a specific test's result becomes unreliable due to degradation.
**In use:** "Glucose without a fluoride tube has maybe an hour before glycolysis starts dragging the number down — get it to the lab fast."
**Common misuse:** Assuming all analytes share the same stability window; it varies significantly by test and by tube additive.
