# Vocabulary

Terms generalists misuse. Format per term: definition, how a practitioner actually uses it, the common misuse.

### Ejection Fraction (EF)
The percentage of end-diastolic LV volume ejected per beat, properly derived from a traced biplane Simpson's method of discs, not a visual impression.
**In use:** "EF by biplane Simpson's is 38% — it looked more like mid-40s on the freeze-frame, but the trace doesn't lie."
**Common misuse:** treating any number labeled "EF" on a report as equally reliable regardless of whether it came from a trace or a glance.

### ACT (Activated Clotting Time)
A point-of-care coagulation assay used to titrate heparin in real time during catheter-based procedures, targeted to a regimen-specific range, not a fixed universal number.
**In use:** "ACT's 195 on unfractionated heparin alone, we're targeting 250-300 — give another 2,000 units before we cross into the vessel."
**Common misuse:** confusing ACT with PTT or INR, or assuming one in-range value early in a long case still holds an hour later without a recheck.

### Ankle-Brachial Index (ABI)
The ratio of ankle systolic pressure to brachial systolic pressure, screening for peripheral arterial disease; values above 1.4 indicate noncompressible vessels, not health.
**In use:** "ABI's 1.5 — that's noncompressible, not normal, reflex to a toe-brachial index."
**Common misuse:** reporting >1.4 as reassuring instead of flagging arterial calcification that makes the test itself unreliable.

### Cigarroa formula
The contrast-dosing formula (5 mL × weight in kg / serum creatinine in mg/dL, capped at 300 mL) used to estimate the maximum safe contrast volume for a given patient's renal function before a cath lab case.
**In use:** "Calculated max is 225 for this patient — we're not treating 300 as the number that matters here."
**Common misuse:** treating 300 mL as a universal ceiling regardless of the patient's actual calculated, often lower, number.

### PSV (Peak Systolic Velocity)
The maximum blood-flow velocity during systole on spectral Doppler, the primary quantitative input for grading arterial stenosis severity.
**In use:** "ICA PSV is 280 with a ratio over 4 — that's a ≥70% stenosis by SRU criteria, not just 'looks narrowed' on grayscale."
**Common misuse:** grading stenosis by grayscale plaque appearance alone without the velocity criteria, or trusting a velocity taken at an uncorrected Doppler angle.

### E/e' ratio
The ratio of early mitral inflow velocity (E) to early diastolic mitral annular tissue velocity (e'), used as one of several inputs estimating LV filling pressure.
**In use:** "Average E/e' is 16, but this patient has LVH — check the lateral e', septal alone overstates it here."
**Common misuse:** using septal e' in isolation in LVH, LBBB, or paced patients, where it's known to be unreliable, and calling the result diastolic dysfunction on that basis alone.

### IVUS / OCT (Intravascular Ultrasound / Optical Coherence Tomography)
Catheter-based intravascular imaging that measures true vessel and lumen dimensions from inside the artery, used when angiography's silhouette view can't establish a true reference diameter.
**In use:** "The vessel's diffusely diseased on angio, there's no clean reference segment — pull IVUS before we size the stent."
**Common misuse:** treating angiography alone as sufficient sizing information when the reference segment is itself diseased and therefore falsely small-looking.

### FFR / iFR (Fractional / Instantaneous Wave-Free Ratio)
Pressure-wire-derived physiologic measurements of whether a coronary stenosis is actually flow-limiting, independent of its angiographic percentage.
**In use:** "It's 70% by eye, but FFR is 0.86 — that's not flow-limiting, hold off on stenting."
**Common misuse:** assuming an angiographically intermediate lesion needs intervention without a physiologic measurement to confirm it's actually restricting flow.

### Door-to-balloon time
The interval from a STEMI patient's first medical contact (not cath lab arrival) to balloon inflation/wire crossing in the culprit vessel, a quality benchmark targeted at 90 minutes or less.
**In use:** "We're at 62 minutes door-to-balloon, clock started at ED presentation, not when he hit our table."
**Common misuse:** starting the clock at cath lab arrival instead of first medical contact, which understates the true interval.

### Bubble study (agitated saline contrast echocardiography)
Injection of agitated saline microbubbles during echo to detect intracardiac or intrapulmonary right-to-left shunting, read by both presence and timing of bubbles in the left heart.
**In use:** "Bubbles crossed within 3 cycles of RA opacification with a good Valsalva release — that's consistent with a PFO, not a pulmonary shunt."
**Common misuse:** calling any left-heart bubble appearance a PFO without checking the cycle-count timing that distinguishes an intracardiac shunt from a pulmonary one.

### IAC accreditation
Intersocietal Accreditation Commission certification of an echocardiography or cath lab facility against a standards checklist, re-surveyed periodically, not a one-time credential.
**In use:** "Our IAC re-survey is in six months — that's when the QA logs and physician CME hours actually get audited."
**Common misuse:** treating accreditation as a static wall certificate rather than an ongoing documentation and peer-review obligation.

### RCIS / RCS / RDCS
Cardiovascular Credentialing International's Registered Cardiovascular Invasive Specialist and Registered Cardiac Sonographer credentials, and ARDMS's Registered Diagnostic Cardiac Sonographer credential — distinct subspecialty certifications, not interchangeable titles.
**In use:** "She's RCIS for the cath lab and RDCS for echo call — two separate certifying exams, not one generic 'cardiac tech' license."
**Common misuse:** using the credentials interchangeably as if any one of them covers competency across invasive, echo, and vascular subspecialties.
