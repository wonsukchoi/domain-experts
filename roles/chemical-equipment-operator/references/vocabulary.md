# Vocabulary

### Thermal runaway
A self-accelerating condition where an exothermic reaction's rate of heat generation exceeds the system's rate of heat removal, causing temperature (and often reaction rate) to climb faster and faster until control is lost.
**In use:** "That temperature excursion during addition is the early signature of a thermal runaway starting — we stop addition now, not once it's already accelerating."
**Common misuse:** Treating any elevated temperature reading as equivalent risk — a stable, slightly elevated temperature is a different situation than an actively rising excursion, and only the latter carries the runaway signature that demands an immediate stop.

### Exotherm
The heat released by a chemical reaction, which must be removed by the system's cooling capacity at a rate matching or exceeding its generation rate to keep temperature controlled.
**In use:** "This reaction's exotherm is well-characterized at this concentration — we know the cooling jacket's margin at the standard addition rate."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a reaction's total heat release is the only relevant number — the *rate* at which that heat is generated (controlled by addition rate) matters as much as the total quantity, since cooling systems have a rate capacity, not just a total capacity.

### Batch record
The documented, real-time record of a specific batch's charge quantities, sequence, process parameters, and any deviations, serving as the production and quality traceability document.
**In use:** "Batch record shows the deviation at t=45 minutes — that's what quality reviews before releasing this lot."
**Common misuse:** Treating the batch record as paperwork to complete after the fact rather than a real-time record of what actually happened — a record reconstructed after a batch is finished loses its value as an accurate account of any deviation that occurred.

### Lockout/Tagout (LOTO)
A safety procedure ensuring hazardous energy sources (electrical, mechanical, chemical, pressure) are isolated and verified de-energized before equipment is serviced or entered.
**In use:** "LOTO's not optional because it looks purged — we verify per procedure before anyone opens that vessel."
**Common misuse:** Treating LOTO as necessary only when a hazard is visibly obvious — the entire purpose of the procedure is to catch a residual hazard (a vapor space, an unpurged segment, stored pressure) that isn't visually apparent.

### Addition rate control
Controlling the rate at which a reagent is added to a reaction based on the reactor's actual, measured response (typically temperature), as opposed to a fixed time- or volume-based schedule.
**In use:** "We're on rate control against reactor temp, not the clock — if temp climbs faster than expected, addition slows regardless of where we are in the schedule."
**Common misuse:** Treating a fixed addition schedule as inherently safe because it was "planned that way" — a schedule is only safe under the conditions it was validated for, and actual reactor response is the real-time check that those conditions still hold.

### Reaction pathway
The specific sequence of chemical steps a reaction follows to reach its product, which can differ entirely depending on conditions like reagent addition order, pH, or temperature at the time of addition.
**In use:** "Adding B before A opens a different reaction pathway entirely — that's not the same reaction just run in a different order."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a reaction's outcome depends only on which reagents are present, not the order or conditions of their combination — many reactions are genuinely pathway-dependent, where sequence determines which product actually forms.

### Impurity profile
The specific pattern and concentration of unwanted byproducts or contaminants in a chemical product, which can differ even between two batches with the same overall yield or visual/physical appearance.
**In use:** "Same yield, same appearance, but the impurity profile's different after that sequence deviation — that's what the analytical test is for."
**Common misuse:** Assuming yield and visual/physical inspection are sufficient to confirm product quality — a deviation can shift which byproducts form without affecting yield or appearance, and only a targeted analytical test catches that.

### Secondary containment
A physical barrier or containment system (dikes, containment basins, double-walled tanks) designed to hold a release from a primary vessel or line, sized for the worst credible single-point failure.
**In use:** "Containment's rated for the largest single tank's full volume — that's the design basis, not an average-case estimate."
**Common misuse:** Assuming containment readiness is a one-time design fact rather than something requiring ongoing verification — a containment system's actual functional capacity (clear drains, intact liner, correct valve positions) needs periodic re-verification, not just confidence in the original design.

### Process deviation
Any departure from a batch record's specified sequence, quantity, timing, or condition during production, requiring evaluation for its actual consequence before the batch is released.
**In use:** "That's a documented deviation — sequence was off by one step. We're not releasing until the analytical result comes back."
**Common misuse:** Treating "deviation" as synonymous with "defect" or dismissing a deviation as inconsequential because the batch otherwise looks normal — a deviation is a trigger for evaluation, and its actual consequence (none, minor, or significant) is determined by that evaluation, not assumed in either direction beforehand.

### Cooling capacity margin
The difference between a cooling system's actual heat removal capability and the heat generation rate required by a process at its specified conditions, representing the buffer available before a control failure.
**In use:** "Our margin at the standard addition rate is already tight — that's why even a small rate increase pushed us over the temperature limit."
**Common misuse:** Assuming cooling capacity margin is fixed and doesn't need re-checking — ambient conditions, cooling system degradation (fouling, reduced flow), or a reagent/concentration change can all erode a margin that was previously adequate.
