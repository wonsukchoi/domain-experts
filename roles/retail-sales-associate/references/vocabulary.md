# Vocabulary

Terms generalists conflate or use loosely that practitioners keep sharply separate.

## Conversion rate vs. traffic

**Conversion rate** is transactions ÷ visits (foot traffic), the share of people who walked in who bought something. **Traffic** is the raw visit count, usually from a door counter independent of the POS. A sales drop can happen with either falling and the fix is different for each.

*In use:* "Traffic's flat, conversion's down four points — this is a floor problem, not a marketing problem."

*Common misuse:* using "traffic" and "conversion" interchangeably, or blaming a sales drop on traffic without checking whether conversion actually moved.

## UPT (units per transaction) vs. ATV (average transaction value) vs. AUR

**UPT** is total units sold ÷ transactions — basket size in item count. **ATV** (also ATS, average transaction size) is total sales ÷ transactions — basket size in dollars. **AUR** (average unit retail) is ATV ÷ UPT — the average price per item sold, which separates "customers are buying fewer items" from "customers are buying cheaper items."

*In use:* "ATV's down but AUR's flat — that's a UPT problem, we're not adding on, not a pricing problem."

*Common misuse:* reporting only ATV and concluding the fix is pricing-related, when the actual cause is basket size (UPT) or vice versa — the two require different interventions.

## Sell-through rate

Units sold ÷ units received (or ÷ beginning-of-period stock) over a defined window, usually tracked against a markdown cadence. Distinct from conversion rate — a SKU can have a healthy conversion rate on the store overall while sitting at a poor sell-through because it's overbought relative to demand.

*In use:* "This style's at 28% sell-through two weeks past the first markdown trigger — that's an inventory call, not a floor-coaching one."

*Common misuse:* treating low sell-through on a specific SKU as a sign the sales team isn't pushing it, when the more common cause is an open-to-buy or placement problem upstream of the floor.

## Shrinkage (shrink)

The gap between recorded inventory and actual physical inventory, expressed as a percentage of sales. Industry average runs roughly 1.5–1.6% of sales (NRF National Retail Security Survey), split across external theft, internal (employee) theft, organized retail crime (ORC), vendor fraud, and administrative/process error — the last category is frequently underestimated.

*In use:* "Before we talk employee theft, rule out administrative error — receiving miscounts alone can account for a point of shrink in a department this size."

*Common misuse:* treating "shrink" as a synonym for "theft" — administrative error and process gaps are a substantial, often majority, share of most stores' shrink number.

## Sweethearting

An employee-fraud pattern where a cashier deliberately fails to scan or under-charges for items for a friend, family member, or accomplice, often disguised inside otherwise-normal transactions.

*In use:* "The no-sale and void counts on that register track together almost 1:1 — that's the sweethearting signature, not random price checks."

*Common misuse:* labeling any register with an elevated void rate as sweethearting — most elevated void rates have an innocent explanation (price-check-heavy department, new associate still learning the system); the tell is the correlation pattern, not the raw rate alone.

## Wardrobing

A return-fraud pattern where a customer buys an item, uses it once (often for a single event — a dress for a wedding, a tool for one job), and returns it as unworn for a full refund.

*In use:* "Occasion dresses are our highest wardrobing risk category — the return-window clustering right after a weekend is the tell."

*Common misuse:* assuming wardrobing is rare or negligible; in occasion-wear and single-use-tool categories specifically, it's a material and measurable share of returns, not an edge case.

## Suggestive selling vs. upselling vs. cross-selling

**Suggestive selling** is the umbrella term for any in-the-moment recommendation. **Upselling** specifically moves the customer to a higher-priced version of the same item category (the better jacket, not a jacket plus something else). **Cross-selling** suggests a different, complementary item alongside the one already chosen (the scarf with the jacket).

*In use:* "We cross-sell well at the fitting room but rarely upsell at the shelf — worth testing whether showing the premium version first changes that."

*Common misuse:* using "upsell" for any add-on suggestion, which blurs two techniques with different scripts, different price psychology, and different failure modes.

## Open-to-buy (OTB)

The dollar or unit budget still available to purchase new inventory in a period, after accounting for what's already on order and planned sell-through — a merchandising/buying control, not a floor-selling concept, but the reason a popular SKU may be unavailable to reorder mid-season.

*In use:* "We're out of OTB for this category until next month — reordering that SKU isn't an option regardless of how well it's selling."

*Common misuse:* floor staff assuming a stockout is simply a reordering oversight, when it's frequently a deliberate OTB constraint set upstream by buying.

## Planogram

The diagram specifying exact product placement, facing count, and shelf position for a department, issued by merchandising/visual and audited for compliance separately from sales performance.

*In use:* "Sell-through's soft on that SKU — first check it's actually in its planogram position before assuming it's a demand problem."

*Common misuse:* treating planogram compliance as a cosmetic/visual-merchandising-only concern; misplacement measurably suppresses sell-through independent of demand.

## BOPIS (buy online, pick up in store)

An omnichannel fulfillment path where a customer orders online and collects in-store, which counts toward store sales/traffic reporting differently than a walk-in sale depending on the POS configuration — a frequent source of conversion-rate miscalculation if pickup transactions aren't excluded from the walk-in traffic denominator.

*In use:* "Check whether BOPIS pickups are counted in the door-traffic number — if they are, our walk-in conversion rate is understated."

*Common misuse:* comparing conversion rate across stores or periods without confirming BOPIS transactions are counted consistently in both the traffic and transaction figures.
