# Vocabulary

### Brown-out
Temporarily pulling a specific apparatus or unit out of service — usually for a shift or a defined period — because staffing can't fill it, distinct from closing a station permanently.
**In use:** "We browned out Ladder 22 for the shift instead of running it two short."
**Common misuse:** Treated as interchangeable with a permanent station closure, or as a routine cost-saving move rather than the last-resort option it should be.

### Kelly Day
A scheduled day off built into a 24/48-style shift rotation specifically to bring a firefighter's average workweek under the FLSA §7(k) 212-hour/28-day threshold.
**In use:** "He's on a Kelly Day Thursday, so don't count him against the callback list."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a discretionary vacation day rather than a scheduling mechanism tied to a specific hours cap.

### §7(k) exemption
The Fair Labor Standards Act provision letting fire departments use a 28-day work period with a 212-hour threshold before overtime is owed, instead of a standard 40-hour workweek.
**In use:** "He's at 205 hours this cycle — one more callback puts him over 7(k), which changes the OT math, not the safety call."
**Common misuse:** Assuming firefighters earn overtime after 40 hours in a week the way most employees do.

### Minimum staffing
The SOP- or CBA-set floor number of personnel required before a specific apparatus can be placed or kept in service.
**In use:** "We're at three on the engine — that's below minimum staffing, not just short-handed."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a flexible target that can be waived on a slow shift rather than a number tied to response-time and tactical-capability standards.

### RIT (Rapid Intervention Team/Crew)
A dedicated crew staged and fully equipped at an incident, not yet committed to fire operations, ready to rescue a downed or trapped firefighter.
**In use:** "Ladder 14 is standing RIT — don't pull them for exterior overhaul."
**Common misuse:** Assigning RIT personnel to other tasks because they "look like they're just standing by."

### ISO PPC (Public Protection Classification)
A 1–10 insurance-industry rating driven by a department's staffing, training, equipment, and water-supply data, which directly affects municipal and homeowner insurance rates.
**In use:** "Our training-hour logs feed the PPC score directly — that's not paperwork for its own sake."
**Common misuse:** Treated as disconnected administrative overhead rather than the same operational records the captain already maintains for readiness.

### Company officer
The direct supervisor of a single apparatus and crew — captain or lieutenant — distinct from a battalion or district chief who supervises multiple companies.
**In use:** "That's a company-officer call, not something that needs to go up to battalion."
**Common misuse:** Using "officer" loosely for any ranked member, which blurs who is actually accountable for a specific crew's decisions.

### Advanced inspection (PPE)
The NFPA 1851-required detailed annual inspection of turnout gear, distinct from a routine post-incident visual check or wipe-down.
**In use:** "That gear's due for its advanced inspection this month, not just a rinse."
**Common misuse:** Treating a routine post-call check as satisfying the annual advanced-inspection requirement.

### Rehab (NFPA 1584)
A formal operational period at an incident where crews are pulled off task for vital-sign monitoring, rest, and rehydration.
**In use:** "Second bottle's done — that crew goes to rehab before they go back to work."
**Common misuse:** Skipped or treated as optional because a crew self-reports feeling fine.

### Mutual aid vs. automatic aid
Mutual aid is a discretionary, request-based agreement between departments; automatic aid is pre-programmed into dispatch to respond regardless of a specific request, often as closest-unit response across a jurisdiction line.
**In use:** "That's an automatic-aid response — Station 22 rolls on this box regardless of who calls it."
**Common misuse:** Using the two terms interchangeably when the dispatch trigger and the underlying legal agreement are actually different.

### Two-in/two-out
The OSHA 29 CFR 1910.134 requirement that two members be staged outside, ready to effect rescue, before two members enter an IDLH atmosphere — with a narrow, time-boxed known-rescue exception.
**In use:** "We don't have two-in/two-out yet — hold the entry team at the door."
**Common misuse:** Applied as a general staffing minimum for any task, rather than the specific interior-entry trigger the rule actually governs.

### Line-of-duty death (LODD) — cardiac/overexertion category
The single leading statistical cause of on-duty firefighter deaths in most reporting years per USFA data, ahead of burns or structural collapse.
**In use:** "Our fatigue and rehab policies exist because cardiac events, not fire, are what actually kill the most firefighters on duty."
**Common misuse:** Assuming fireground trauma is the dominant LODD risk, which skews safety attention toward tactics and away from fitness, rest, and rehab.

### Company journal / e-log
The shift's contemporaneous, time-stamped operational record — treated as a discoverable document in litigation or an LODD investigation, not an informal notebook.
**In use:** "Log the staffing decision and the reason now, not a summary at the end of shift."
**Common misuse:** Written after the fact as an outcome summary ("shift went fine") rather than documenting the decision and its rationale at the time it was made.

### Pre-incident plan (PIP)
A pre-built tactical reference for a specific target hazard — occupancy layout, known hazards, water supply, key contacts — reviewed and updated on a set cycle.
**In use:** "Pull the PIP for that warehouse before we roll — it's got the hazmat storage location marked."
**Common misuse:** Created once and filed away rather than re-walked and updated on the department's set review cycle.
