# Hoist and Winch Operator — Vocabulary

### Layer-derated capacity
The maximum line pull a drum hoist can safely exert at a specific wrap layer, lower than the nameplate (bare-drum, layer-1) rating because effective drum radius grows with each added layer and the hoist is torque-limited.
**In use:** "Nameplate says 10,000, but we finish this lift on layer 4 — the layer-derated capacity there is 7,780."
**Common misuse:** Checking the load only against the nameplate figure, without calculating which layer will be active at the critical point of the specific lift.

### Bare-drum rating
The capacity figure stamped on a hoist's nameplate, measured at the first (innermost) layer of rope on the drum, where effective radius is smallest and line pull is highest.
**In use:** "That's the bare-drum rating — it only holds at layer one, not once we're three layers deep."
**Common misuse:** Treating the nameplate number as the equipment's capacity under all conditions rather than as the best-case, first-layer figure.

### Two-blocking
Running the load block (or hook block) into the upper block, sheave, or boom tip because hoisting continued past the point where no more travel is available — a failure that parts the rope, overloads the sheave assembly, or transfers load into the structure, and does not self-arrest.
**In use:** "We two-blocked it on the last job because the A2B was jumpered — parted the rope on the upper sheave."
**Common misuse:** Assuming the hoist will simply stall or stop safely if the block reaches the top, rather than recognizing it as an active structural-failure mode.

### Anti-two-block (A2B) device
A limit switch or equivalent sensing device that cuts hoist-up motion before the load block reaches the upper block, sheave, or boom tip, giving the operator an automatic stop independent of visual judgment.
**In use:** "A2B's tripping at the same hook height every time — that's a reeving problem, get it looked at, don't jumper it."
**Common misuse:** Bypassing or disabling the device when it trips inconveniently, converting a preventable nuisance into the exact failure the device exists to stop.

### Holding brake
A brake (mechanical or load brake) rated specifically to hold a suspended load stationary without creep, as distinct from the brake or motor that controls active lifting and lowering.
**In use:** "Lifting brake's fine, but confirm the holding brake's rated for this load before we leave it hanging over the shift change."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a brake that lifts and lowers smoothly is automatically rated to hold the same load indefinitely, without checking its separate holding-capacity figure.

### Creep
Slow, unintended downward drift of a suspended load held on a brake, indicating the brake's holding capacity is inadequate or worn for the current load.
**In use:** "We're seeing about an inch of creep over ten minutes — that load's coming down or getting blocked, not staying on the brake alone."
**Common misuse:** Dismissing minor observed drift as normal settling rather than treating any measurable creep as a signal to stop relying on the brake alone.

### Fleet angle
The angle between a rope's straight-line path from the first fixed sheave to the drum and a line perpendicular to the drum's axis, which determines whether the rope lays in even layers or cross-winds.
**In use:** "Fleet angle's opening up past two degrees near the drum flange — that's why it's piling instead of laying flat."
**Common misuse:** Treating fleet angle as a fixed spec of the installation that never needs rechecking, when it changes across the drum's travel and needs verification at both extremes.

### Cross-winding
Rope riding up and over a previous wrap instead of laying flat beside it, usually from excessive fleet angle or worn drum grooves, which invalidates the layer-capacity chart's assumption of ordered spooling.
**In use:** "Drum's cross-winding on the outboard end — stop and re-spool under tension before we load it again."
**Common misuse:** Continuing to operate through visible cross-winding on the assumption that the rope will "settle" once more wraps go on, when it typically compounds instead.

### Level-wind
A mechanical guide that traverses rope evenly across a drum's width in sync with drum rotation, keeping wraps ordered without relying on fleet angle alone.
**In use:** "Level-wind's lagging the drum by half a wrap — that's going to cross-wind even though our fleet angle measures fine."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a level-wind mechanism removes the need to check fleet angle or inspect spooling, rather than treating it as a component that itself needs inspection.

### Line pull
The tensile force a hoist or winch can exert on the rope at a given drum layer and rope speed, the operative capacity figure that changes with layer as opposed to a single fixed rating.
**In use:** "Rated line pull drops to under 8,000 once we're on the fourth layer — that's the number to check, not the nameplate."
**Common misuse:** Using "line pull" and "rated capacity" interchangeably without specifying which drum layer the figure applies to.

### Proof load / load test
A test load applied above rated capacity (commonly stated as a percentage above 100% per the applicable ASME B30 standard) at commissioning or after major repair, to verify the equipment and its braking system before return to service.
**In use:** "Unit's due for its load test after the gearbox replacement — it doesn't go back into service until that's current."
**Common misuse:** Treating a routine operational inspection (checking function at rated load) as equivalent to a full proof-load test, when they verify different things at different intervals.

### Rope safety factor
The ratio between a wire rope's breaking strength and its actual working load, set higher for personnel-carrying hoist service than for materials-only service under mine-hoisting standards.
**In use:** "This shaft's rigged for man-riding, so the rope safety factor requirement is higher than what we'd run for rock only."
**Common misuse:** Applying a materials-only rope safety factor to equipment that also carries personnel, or assuming one factor applies universally regardless of service type.

### Duty cycle
The pattern of load, speed, and rest a hoist or winch experiences in normal service — continuous single lifts, frequent stop-start cycles, or sustained tension under dynamic loading — which differs materially between mine, marine, and construction contexts.
**In use:** "This winch's duty cycle is sustained tension under vessel motion, not a single lift-and-set like a material hoist — different wear pattern entirely."
**Common misuse:** Assuming inspection intervals and wear expectations transfer directly from one context's duty cycle to another's without adjustment.
