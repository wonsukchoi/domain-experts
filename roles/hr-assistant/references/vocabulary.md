# HR assistant working vocabulary

Terms an HR assistant uses precisely and generalists conflate. Format: definition → how a practitioner says it → common misuse.

**Personnel file vs. medical file** — The personnel file holds general employment records (offer letter, reviews, disciplinary notes); the medical file is a physically or logically separate, access-restricted file required under ADA/GINA for any health, disability, or genetic information, no matter how minor the document seems.
In use: "That accommodation note goes in the medical file, not the personnel file — doesn't matter that it's one sentence."
Misuse: treating "medical file" as a synonym for "sensitive stuff we're careful with" rather than a legally required separate file with its own access restrictions.

**Effective date (benefits) vs. hire date** — The effective date is when benefits coverage actually starts, governed by the plan document's waiting-period clause; the hire date is simply the day employment began, and the two are rarely the same date.
In use: "His hire date is January 6th, but his medical coverage effective date is March 1st per the 30-day waiting period."
Misuse: defaulting the HRIS effective-date field to the hire date because it's the value already on screen, instead of computing it from the plan document.

**Adverse action (background check)** — The formal, statutorily sequenced process (pre-adverse notice, wait period, final notice) that must run before a hiring decision based partly on a background-check report goes final; it is a compliance sequence, not a synonym for the decision itself.
In use: "We're in the adverse-action wait period on this one — nothing final until day 5 clears."
Misuse: using "adverse action" loosely to mean "we decided not to hire them," collapsing the required notice sequence into a single decision moment.

**E-Verify case vs. I-9 completion** — Two linked but separate actions: I-9 Section 2 is the employer's physical document review, while an E-Verify case is a separate electronic submission run on the same 3-business-day clock for participating employers — completing one does not complete the other.
In use: "Section 2's done, but I still need to create the E-Verify case before end of day to stay inside the window."
Misuse: assuming a completed I-9 automatically means the E-Verify case was also created, or running them sequentially instead of in parallel and blowing the shared deadline.

**Rehire eligibility flag** — A personnel-file field set at termination indicating whether the company would rehire the person, distinct from and not derivable from the reason for separation alone (a layoff and a for-cause termination can both carry either flag depending on circumstances).
In use: "Termination reason was 'position eliminated,' but check the rehire flag separately before confirming eligibility to the verification requester."
Misuse: inferring the rehire flag from the separation reason instead of checking the field itself.

**Requisition (req) vs. job posting** — A requisition is the internal, approved authorization to hire for a role, including its budget and priority status; a job posting is the external-facing listing derived from it — a posting can exist without an active, approved requisition behind it in a poorly maintained ATS.
In use: "There's a live posting, but let me confirm the req is still open and approved before we schedule more interviews against it."
Misuse: treating an open job posting as proof that headcount is approved and funded.

**Reference check vs. employment verification** — Employment verification confirms objective facts (title, dates, rehire eligibility) per a standard script; a reference check solicits a subjective assessment of the person's performance and is typically declined by HR in favor of routing to a former manager who volunteers to speak informally.
In use: "I can do the employment verification right now — for an actual reference, that's not something I'm authorized to give."
Misuse: answering a reference-check-style question ("would you recommend them") under the authority of a routine verification request.

**Onboarding vs. orientation** — Orientation is a single Day-1 event covering logistics and paperwork; onboarding is the full 90-day (or longer) sequence covering role clarity, team integration, and early performance — treating the two as interchangeable produces a Day-1-only checklist mistaken for a retention program.
In use: "Orientation covered forms and badges; onboarding runs through the 90-day review."
Misuse: calling the Day-1 checklist "onboarding" and considering the job done once it's checked off.

**Waiting period** — The plan-document-defined span of continuous employment (commonly 30 or 60 days) that must elapse before benefits coverage becomes effective, distinct from any eligibility criteria about hours or employment status.
In use: "He's benefits-eligible by classification, but he's still inside the 30-day waiting period, so coverage doesn't start yet."
Misuse: conflating "eligible" (meets the classification criteria) with "effective" (the waiting period has actually elapsed).

**Reverification (I-9)** — The requirement to re-examine and re-document an employee's work authorization before a List A or List C document's stated expiration date, applicable only to documents that actually expire, not a general "redo the I-9" requirement.
In use: "Her EAD expires in January, so reverification is due before then — the rest of the I-9 doesn't need to be touched."
Misuse: reverifying an entire I-9 or List B identity document when only a specific, expiring work-authorization document requires it.

**Segregated file** — Any personnel-record category (medical, I-9, background-check) that regulation requires to be stored separately from the general personnel file with restricted access, not merely a suggested best practice for tidiness.
In use: "That's a segregated-file item — it doesn't go in the same cabinet as the general files, regardless of how thin the folder is."
Misuse: treating file segregation as an organizational nicety rather than a compliance requirement tied to specific document categories.

**Escalation** — The deliberate, same-day routing of a request's interpretive component to the HR specialist or manager, logged in writing, not a synonym for "I couldn't answer it" or a sign of failure to resolve something independently.
In use: "I answered the factual half and escalated the eligibility judgment — logged both in the ticket."
Misuse: treating every escalation as a personal shortfall instead of the correct outcome for a question that was never this role's to decide.
