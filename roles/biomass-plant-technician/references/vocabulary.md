# Vocabulary

### Fuel moisture content (biomass)
The percentage of water weight in biomass fuel, which varies substantially by source, storage condition, and weather — a primary driver of combustion behavior that must be actively monitored rather than assumed from a general specification.
**In use:** "This delivery's testing at forty-two percent moisture — twelve points above our thirty percent design basis, so combustion parameters need adjusting."
**Common misuse:** Assuming all "biomass" fuel behaves similarly regardless of actual moisture content — the term covers a wide range of actual moisture variability by source and handling, and combustion parameters need to match the actual delivered fuel's characteristics, not a general assumption.

### Bridging (fuel handling)
A mechanical blockage in a hopper, feeder, or conveyor where irregular biomass fuel particles interlock and form a stable arch or bridge, stopping fuel flow despite the equipment operating normally otherwise.
**In use:** "That's bridging in the feed hopper, not a combustion issue — the fuel's physically stuck, not burning wrong."
**Common misuse:** Treating a fuel feed interruption as a combustion or control system problem by default — biomass fuel's irregular particle shape makes bridging a common, distinct mechanical issue requiring physical intervention, not combustion troubleshooting.

### Slagging and fouling
The buildup of molten or softened ash deposits on boiler heat transfer surfaces, more aggressive and less predictable in biomass combustion than in many fossil fuels due to biomass ash's typically higher alkali content and lower melting point.
**In use:** "Steam temperature trend's showing fouling building up faster than the standard schedule assumes — this fuel batch's ash chemistry is more aggressive."
**Common misuse:** Assuming slagging/fouling accumulates at a predictable, fuel-independent rate the way some fossil fuel ash might — biomass ash chemistry varies by source, and fouling rate needs to be monitored against actual conditions, not assumed constant.

### Air/fuel ratio (combustion)
The relative proportion of combustion air to fuel supplied to a boiler, which must be adjusted for actual fuel moisture and composition to maintain complete combustion and emissions compliance.
**In use:** "Increasing the air ratio for this wetter fuel batch — otherwise we won't get complete combustion and emissions will climb."
**Common misuse:** Holding a fixed air/fuel ratio calibrated for one fuel moisture level regardless of actual incoming fuel variability — biomass fuel's moisture variability directly requires corresponding air/fuel ratio adjustment to maintain combustion quality.

### Continuous Emissions Monitoring System (CEMS)
Instrumentation that continuously measures and records a combustion facility's stack emissions, providing real-time data to verify ongoing compliance rather than relying on periodic spot checks.
**In use:** "CEMS data shows the CO spike correlating exactly with when the wetter fuel batch started feeding — that's our confirmation of cause."
**Common misuse:** Treating emissions monitoring as a periodic compliance check appropriate for a stable fuel source — biomass fuel variability means emissions can shift meaningfully between fuel batches, making continuous, active monitoring more valuable than infrequent spot checks.

### Fuel characterization (biomass)
The practice of sampling and testing an incoming biomass fuel batch's actual moisture content and composition before or during use, rather than assuming it matches a general specification or design basis.
**In use:** "Characterizing this delivery before we run it through — the source and storage conditions were different from our usual supplier, so we're not assuming it matches spec."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a new fuel delivery matches a general "biomass" specification without testing — actual variability by source, storage, and weather conditions can be substantial, and combustion parameter decisions should be based on the actual tested batch, not an assumption.

### Heating value (biomass fuel)
The amount of energy released per unit mass of fuel when burned, which decreases as moisture content increases since energy is consumed evaporating the water rather than contributing to useful heat output.
**In use:** "Wetter fuel means lower effective heating value per pound — that's why we're accepting reduced steam output rather than trying to force full rated capacity."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a given mass or volume of biomass fuel delivers consistent heating value regardless of moisture content — wetter fuel genuinely contains less usable energy per unit mass, and expecting unchanged output at unchanged feed rate ignores this real physical relationship.

### Design basis (boiler fuel specification)
The fuel characteristics (moisture content, composition) a boiler's combustion system was originally designed and calibrated around, serving as a reference point rather than a guarantee of what will actually be delivered.
**In use:** "Design basis is thirty percent moisture, but that's a reference point for calibration, not a promise about what shows up at the gate."
**Common misuse:** Treating the design basis moisture content as the actual, ongoing characteristic of delivered fuel — the design basis is a calibration reference, and actual fuel needs to be checked against it, not assumed to match it.
