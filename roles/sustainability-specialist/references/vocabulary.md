### Scope 1 emissions
Direct greenhouse gas emissions from sources owned or controlled by the reporting company (e.g., on-site fuel combustion, company vehicles).
**In use:** "Scope 1 covers our natural gas combustion and fleet vehicle emissions."
**Common misuse:** Including purchased electricity in Scope 1 rather than correctly categorizing it as Scope 2.

### Scope 2 emissions
Indirect emissions from the generation of purchased electricity, steam, heating, or cooling consumed by the reporting company.
**In use:** "Scope 2 is calculated both location-based and market-based given our renewable energy certificate purchases."
**Common misuse:** Reporting only one Scope 2 method (location-based or market-based) when the company holds RECs or PPAs and both figures are expected.

### Scope 3 emissions
Indirect emissions occurring in a company's value chain (both upstream and downstream) that are not owned or controlled by the reporting company — often the largest and least precisely measured category.
**In use:** "Scope 3 purchased goods and services is our largest category, estimated using a spend-based method."
**Common misuse:** Presenting Scope 3 figures with the same precision and confidence as metered Scope 1/2 data without flagging the estimation methodology and its lower certainty.

### Location-based method (Scope 2)
A Scope 2 calculation approach using average emission factors for the electricity grid where consumption occurs, regardless of any renewable energy purchases.
**In use:** "Location-based Scope 2 uses the regional grid average, independent of our REC purchases."
**Common misuse:** Confusing location-based with market-based, or reporting only one when both are expected under GHG Protocol Scope 2 guidance.

### Market-based method (Scope 2)
A Scope 2 calculation approach reflecting the emissions from electricity a company has specifically chosen to purchase, accounting for renewable energy certificates (RECs) or power purchase agreements (PPAs).
**In use:** "Market-based Scope 2 is lower than location-based because 30% of our electricity is covered by RECs."
**Common misuse:** Treating market-based Scope 2 as the only relevant figure and omitting the location-based figure, which is also generally expected for transparency.

### Organizational boundary (equity share / financial control / operational control)
The methodology a company uses to determine which entities' emissions to include in its consolidated footprint — based on ownership percentage, financial control, or operational control.
**In use:** "We use financial control as our boundary approach, consistently applied across all subsidiaries and joint ventures."
**Common misuse:** Switching boundary methodology between entities to include or exclude specific emissions sources depending on which result looks more favorable.

### Science Based Targets initiative (SBTi)
An organization that validates corporate emissions-reduction targets against required trajectories aligned with limiting global warming to 1.5°C or well-below 2°C, per sector-specific pathways.
**In use:** "The target needs SBTi validation, which means checking it against the required minimum annual reduction rate for our sector."
**Common misuse:** Describing a target as "science-based" simply because it sounds ambitious, without validating it against SBTi's actual required trajectory calculation.

### Double materiality
An assessment approach evaluating both financial materiality (how sustainability issues affect the company's financial performance) and impact materiality (how the company affects people and the environment) — required under some frameworks like the EU's CSRD/ESRS.
**In use:** "CSRD requires double materiality, so we need both the financial-impact lens and the company's-impact-on-the-world lens in this assessment."
**Common misuse:** Running only a financial (single) materiality assessment under a framework that specifically requires the double materiality approach.

### Spend-based method (Scope 3)
A Scope 3 estimation approach calculating emissions by multiplying procurement spend in a category by an average emission factor (emissions per dollar spent), rather than using actual supplier-specific activity data.
**In use:** "We used a spend-based method for purchased goods — $12M in spend times an industry-average factor."
**Common misuse:** Presenting a spend-based estimate without flagging it as a lower-confidence method compared to supplier-specific or activity-based calculations.

### Emission factor
A coefficient used to convert activity data (e.g., fuel consumed, electricity used, dollars spent) into greenhouse gas emissions, typically specific to a fuel type, region, and year.
**In use:** "We're using the current regional grid emission factor rather than last year's or a generic national average."
**Common misuse:** Defaulting to a generic, non-region-specific, or outdated emission factor when a more current and specific one is available and would materially change the result.

### GRI, SASB, TCFD/ISSB, CSRD/ESRS
Major sustainability reporting frameworks: GRI (broad stakeholder-focused reporting), SASB (industry-specific financially material topics), TCFD/ISSB (climate-related financial risk disclosure, now consolidated into IFRS S1/S2), and CSRD/ESRS (the EU's mandatory sustainability reporting directive and its detailed standards).
**In use:** "This report needs to satisfy both SASB's industry-specific metrics and CSRD's double materiality requirement."
**Common misuse:** Treating these frameworks as interchangeable rather than recognizing each has distinct scope, materiality approach, and (in CSRD's case) mandatory legal requirements.
