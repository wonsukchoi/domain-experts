# Farm management working vocabulary

Terms an operator uses precisely. Format: definition → how a practitioner says it → common misuse.

**Basis** — The difference between the local cash price and the futures market price for a commodity, reflecting local supply/demand, transportation cost, and storage conditions.
In use: "Futures are at $5.20, but our local basis is −$0.35, so the actual cash price we'd get here is $4.85."
Misuse: quoting the futures price as if it's the price a producer will actually receive locally — basis can swing the realized price meaningfully in either direction.

**Forward contract** — An agreement to sell a specified quantity of a commodity at a set price for future delivery, locking in price before harvest/sale regardless of what the market does later.
In use: "We forward-contracted 60% of expected corn bushels at $5.00 before planting to cut price risk on the larger acreage."
Misuse: treating a forward contract as risk-free — it locks price but doesn't remove yield risk; a shortfall in actual production against the contracted quantity can force buying grain on the open market to fulfill it.

**Actual Production History (APH)** — A multi-year yield record for a specific farm/field used to set crop insurance coverage levels and guarantees.
In use: "Our APH corn yield is 175 bu/acre, so at 75% coverage the insurance guarantee kicks in below about 131 bu/acre."
Misuse: assuming APH automatically reflects current-year potential; it's a trailing average and can understate yield potential after a genetic or practice improvement, or overstate it after a run of unusually good years.

**Revenue Protection (RP) vs. Yield Protection (YP) crop insurance** — RP insures against a drop in revenue from either a price decline or a yield shortfall (or both); YP insures against yield shortfall only, regardless of price movement.
In use: "We carry RP, not YP, because our biggest exposure is a simultaneous price drop and average yield, not just a yield miss."
Misuse: assuming yield insurance protects against a price collapse — YP pays out on yield shortfall only, not on price risk.

**Break-even price** — The price per unit of production needed to cover total cost of production (fixed + variable), the reference point for whether a marketing decision is locking in a profit or a loss.
In use: "Our break-even on corn this year is $4.35/bu — anything forward-contracted above that is a locked-in margin, not just a locked-in price."
Misuse: comparing a contracted price only to last year's price or to a neighbor's price rather than to this operation's own current break-even cost.

**Working capital** — Current assets minus current liabilities; the liquid buffer available to cover a bad year's operating expenses without new borrowing.
In use: "Working capital covers about four months of operating expenses — thin enough that a delayed harvest sale would strain cash flow."
Misuse: conflating working capital with total net worth or land equity; land equity doesn't help cover this month's input bill if it can't be quickly converted to cash.

**Debt-to-asset ratio** — Total liabilities divided by total assets, a standard solvency benchmark in agricultural lending (commonly considered healthy under ~40%, elevated risk above ~50-60%).
In use: "We're at 38% debt-to-asset, which is why the lender didn't blink at financing the new irrigation system."
Misuse: treating a single snapshot ratio as sufficient without also checking the trend direction and the working-capital/liquidity position alongside it.

**Marginal return (on an input)** — The additional revenue generated by one more unit of an input (seed, fertilizer, feed) relative to its additional cost — the correct basis for an input decision, as opposed to total yield or gain alone.
In use: "The extra $18/acre of nitrogen only bought 4 more bushels at $5.00 — that's $20 of extra revenue for $18 of extra cost, barely worth it, and the next increment likely isn't."
Misuse: choosing the input level that maximizes total yield rather than the level where marginal revenue still exceeds marginal cost — the highest-yield option is rarely the most profitable one.

**Cash rent vs. share rent (crop share lease)** — Cash rent is a fixed per-acre payment to the landowner regardless of yield or price outcome; a share lease splits both costs and revenue (and therefore price/yield risk) between operator and landowner by an agreed percentage.
In use: "We moved this parcel to a 50/50 share lease so the landowner absorbs some of the price risk with us this year."
Misuse: treating cash rent as risk-free for the operator — it fixes the cost side, but all price and yield risk on the resulting crop still sits entirely with the operator, more so than under a share arrangement.

**Prevented planting** — A crop insurance provision paying an indemnity when weather or field conditions prevent planting a crop by the insurance-defined final planting date, distinct from a yield-shortfall claim on a crop that was planted.
In use: "The field never dried out enough to plant by the final planting date, so we filed a prevented-planting claim instead of a yield claim."
Misuse: assuming any planting delay qualifies — the provision has specific date and condition thresholds defined in the policy, not simply "it was a wet spring."

**Price-taker** — A market participant whose individual production or sales decisions have no meaningful effect on the market price they receive, as distinct from a price-maker who can influence price through their own volume or market power.
In use: "We can affect our cost and yield, but we're a price-taker on the bushel — the plan has to work at whatever price the market sets, not the price we'd like."
Misuse: planning around an assumed favorable price as if the operation had some ability to secure it through production choices alone, rather than treating price as an external variable to hedge or plan around.

**Enterprise budget** — A detailed cost-and-return projection for a specific crop or livestock line, itemizing all variable and fixed costs against expected revenue, used to compare profitability across production options.
In use: "The soybean enterprise budget shows a $180/acre margin at current prices, versus $286 for corn — but that's before weighing the diversification benefit."
Misuse: comparing crops or livestock lines only on gross revenue or yield rather than the full enterprise budget's margin, which accounts for the different cost structures.

**Yield drag** — A reduction in expected yield from a practice or input choice made for a different reason (e.g., a drought-tolerant variety trading some yield potential for resilience), distinct from a yield loss caused by an adverse event.
In use: "The drought-tolerant hybrid carries maybe a 3% yield drag in a normal year, but it's the right trade if there's real drought risk this season."
Misuse: treating any yield below the top-performing variety as a mistake, without weighing the risk-reduction benefit that caused the deliberate trade-off.
