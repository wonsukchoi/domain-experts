# Vocabulary

Terms generalists flatten together that a structural ironworker keeps sharply separate — the value is in the misuse, not the definition.

## Connector vs. bolting-up (finish) crew

A **connector** lands steel off the hook, aligns holes with a drift pin or spud wrench, and drives the minimum bolts needed to satisfy the release requirement — a temporary, structural-enough state, not a finished one. The **bolting-up (finish) crew** comes through afterward and brings each connection to its specified final pretension. Calling every ironworker on site "the connector crew" erases which stage a given connection is actually in.

**In use:** "Connector's already two bays ahead — bolting-up crew's still working C–D from yesterday, don't assume anything past that line is pretensioned."

**Common misuse:** treating any bolted connection as finished because "the ironworkers already did it," without checking whether it's connector-stage (snug/pinned) or bolting-up-stage (pretensioned).

## Snug-tight vs. pretensioned

**Snug-tight** is the tightness reached by an ironworker's ordinary spud-wrench effort or a few impact-wrench hits — plies in firm contact, nothing more. **Pretensioned** is a distinct, later state reached by one of the specified methods (turn-of-nut, calibrated wrench, DTI washer, twist-off bolt) and verified against that method's own numeric criterion.

**In use:** "That joint's snug, not pretensioned — we still owe it a turn-of-nut check before it counts as slip-critical."

**Common misuse:** calling a bolt "torqued" or "tight" once it's snug, without running the actual pretensioning method the connection drawing specifies.

## Connector exemption (15–30 ft band)

The provision letting a connector work between 15 and 30 ft (or two stories, whichever is less) above a lower level without individual tie-off, conditional on fall-protection equipment being provided and the site erection plan addressing connector procedures. It is a bounded, conditional exception — not a blanket exemption from fall protection at any height.

**In use:** "We're at 22 ft, connector exemption applies, but the harness and lanyard still have to be on — the plan requires it even though tie-off's optional here."

**Common misuse:** treating "connector" as a role that never needs fall protection, instead of a role with a specific, height-bounded, equipment-conditional exception.

## Controlled decking zone (CDZ) vs. controlled access zone (CAZ)

A **CDZ** is a steel-erection-specific zone, bounded to no more than 90 ft by 90 ft from the leading edge and no more than 3,000 sq ft of unsecured decking, where trained workers doing leading-edge decking or connecting may work without individual fall arrest. A **CAZ** is the broader general-construction concept for restricting access near a hazard, without those decking-specific numeric ceilings.

**In use:** "This is a CDZ, not a general CAZ — it's capped at 90 by 90 and 3,000 square feet unsecured, not whatever boundary the GC's access plan uses elsewhere on site."

**Common misuse:** applying generic controlled-access-zone boundary assumptions to a CDZ, missing that the CDZ has its own numeric size and unsecured-decking limits.

## Turn-of-nut, calibrated wrench, DTI washer, twist-off (TC) bolt

Four distinct, RCSC-recognized methods for verifying a bolt has reached its pretension: **turn-of-nut** rotates the nut a specified angle past snug; **calibrated wrench** uses a torque wrench verified against a tension-measuring device; **DTI washer** uses feeler-gauge refusal at compressed protrusions; **twist-off (TC) bolt** shears its own splined tip at a factory-set tension. They are not interchangeable improvisations of each other — each has its own acceptance criterion and its own verification record.

**In use:** "Drawing calls for turn-of-nut on this joint — a calibrated wrench reading isn't a substitute, and neither is just running the impact gun a few extra seconds."

**Common misuse:** treating any of the four as generically "torquing the bolt" and skipping the specific numeric check the chosen method requires.

## Slip-critical joint vs. bearing-type joint

A **slip-critical joint** is fully pretensioned with a specified faying-surface treatment because the connection cannot be allowed to slip under service load (moment connections, load reversal, fatigue-prone members). A **bearing-type joint** is finished at snug-tight, common on ordinary shear connections not subject to those conditions.

**In use:** "This one's bearing-type — snug-tight and done. The moment connection two bays over is slip-critical, that one gets the full turn-of-nut and Class B surface prep."

**Common misuse:** assuming every high-strength bolted connection needs full slip-critical pretensioning (wasting crew time), or the reverse — leaving a genuinely slip-critical joint at snug-tight because "it's bolted."

## Plumbing-up

The process of adjusting guy-cable turnbuckles to bring an erected frame within its specified plumb tolerance before final bolt-up locks the geometry in place. Once bolts are pretensioned, whatever plumb state existed at that moment is permanent.

**In use:** "Hold the finish bolting on that column line until plumbing-up's confirmed — we tighten those bolts, that's the plumb we're stuck with."

**Common misuse:** rushing or skipping plumbing-up because the deck is already down, treating plumb as a cosmetic step rather than the geometry the final bolt-up will permanently fix.

## Raising gang

The erection crew sequence — hooker-on (rigs the load at the ground/staging area), connector (lands and temporarily bolts aloft), bolting-up crew (final pretension) — each stage owning a distinct scope and a distinct connection status.

**In use:** "Raising gang's moved on to the next bay — bolting-up's the crew that still owes this one its turn-of-nut."

**Common misuse:** using "raising gang" to mean any ironworker on site, collapsing the distinction between which stage — and which connection status — a given crew member actually owns.

## Eccentric (cantilevered) connection

A connection whose geometry — an overhanging beam end, an off-center load path — creates a moment that the standard 2-bolt release minimum may not be enough to stabilize, triggering a competent person's determination of how many additional bolts are required before release.

**In use:** "That's a cantilevered tip, not a simple span — competent person needs to call the bolt count before we release, two won't necessarily cover it."

**Common misuse:** applying the general 2-bolt rule to any connection without checking whether its geometry is eccentric enough to require the competent-person review the standard calls for.

## Anchor rod (anchor bolt) assembly

The full column base-connection assembly — rods, base-plate weld, and foundation — designed as a unit to resist a minimum eccentric gravity load (300 lb at 18 in from the column's outer face) that an ironworker standing and working on top of an unbraced column imposes. A minimum of 4 rods is required regardless of column size.

**In use:** "Don't grind that rod to fit — if it doesn't line up, it's a fabrication nonconformance, and the whole assembly's rated capacity is what's at stake, not just the one hole."

**Common misuse:** treating anchor-rod count and fit as a dimensional nuisance to solve in the field, rather than a load-rated assembly with a specified minimum count and design load.

## Double connection

Two members framing into opposite sides of the same column web or bracket at the same elevation, where the standard bolt pattern for each can leave no wrench clearance for the other — resolved by a seat, clip, or blind-bolting detail the connection drawing specifies, not by field improvisation.

**In use:** "This is a double connection at the column — check the detail for the seat angle before anyone tries to force bolts in blind from underneath."

**Common misuse:** treating a double connection as a routine fit-up problem to solve with whatever clearance trick works in the moment, instead of pulling the connection drawing's specified solution.

## Positioning device system vs. personal fall-arrest system (PFAS)

A **positioning device system** holds a worker in place at a work position (e.g., a connector's positioning lanyard), allowing hands-free work with a limited free-fall distance by design — it is not built to arrest a full fall. A **PFAS** is designed specifically to stop a fall in progress and limit arrest forces. They serve different functions and aren't interchangeable based on which one happens to be on hand.

**In use:** "That's a positioning lanyard, not fall arrest — fine for holding him at the connection, not a substitute for PFAS once he's working past 30 feet."

**Common misuse:** treating a positioning-device lanyard as adequate fall-arrest protection at heights or in configurations it wasn't designed to arrest a fall from.
