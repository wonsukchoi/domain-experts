# Hydroelectric operations working vocabulary

Terms a production manager uses precisely. Format: definition → how a practitioner says it → common misuse.

**Acre-foot** — The standard unit for water storage/volume in reservoir management: the volume of water needed to cover one acre to a depth of one foot (~326,000 gallons), used to quantify storage, obligations, and drawdown consistently.
In use: "We have 280,000 acre-feet in storage against 161,600 acre-feet of remaining binding obligations — that leaves 118,400 acre-feet for generation."
Misuse: discussing reservoir levels only as a percentage of capacity or a water-surface elevation without converting to acre-feet when comparing against volume-denominated obligations (irrigation contracts, ecological flow requirements), which can obscure the actual available margin.

**Multi-objective reservoir operation** — Managing reservoir releases and storage against the full set of competing legal and operational obligations (irrigation, flood control, ecological flow, generation) rather than optimizing for power generation alone.
In use: "The operating plan has to clear irrigation delivery and ecological minimum flow before any water is treated as available for generation optimization."
Misuse: treating generation output as the primary objective function and other water uses as constraints to work around at the margin, rather than co-equal or often legally superior claims on the same resource.

**Ecological (minimum) flow requirement** — A legally or regulatorily mandated minimum water release, often varying by season and tied to specific ecological needs (fish spawning, migration timing), distinct from a flexible or discretionary release target.
In use: "The 40 cfs minimum flow requirement isn't just a floor — it steps up during the spawning window, which changes our release math for that period."
Misuse: treating the minimum flow requirement as a single flat number year-round rather than checking for seasonal or biologically-timed variation in the actual requirement.

**Flood control storage** — Reservoir capacity reserved specifically for absorbing storm inflow to reduce downstream flood risk, a standing operational commitment rather than a discretionary buffer available for generation optimization when convenient.
In use: "We can't count that flood control reservation as available storage for summer generation — it's a standing commitment tied to storm-season risk."
Misuse: treating reserved flood control capacity as flexible storage that can be used for generation whenever storm risk seems low, undermining the reservation's actual risk-management function.

**Dam safety risk tolerance** — The categorically more conservative risk standard applied to dam safety-relevant inspection, monitoring, and maintenance decisions, reflecting the catastrophic and irreversible consequence of structural failure, as distinct from routine equipment maintenance risk tolerance.
In use: "This is a dam-safety item — it gets funded and scheduled on a different basis than routine equipment maintenance, regardless of this quarter's budget pressure."
Misuse: applying the same cost-benefit tradeoff logic used for routine equipment maintenance to a dam safety-relevant decision, missing the fundamental asymmetry between deferred routine maintenance cost and the consequence of a structural failure.

**Scenario-based hydrologic planning** — Planning reservoir operations against a range of plausible inflow scenarios (wet, median, dry) rather than committing to a single median-forecast operating plan.
In use: "The operating plan holds up under the dry scenario at 78% of median storage — that's the case that actually mattered this year."
Misuse: building and committing to an operating plan based only on the median (expected) inflow forecast, leaving no prepared response for a drought or flood scenario that a wider range would have anticipated.

**Water rights / water-use priority** — The legal framework establishing which claims on a shared water resource (irrigation, municipal, ecological, generation) take priority, often established by seniority or specific statutory/regulatory allocation, independent of what would maximize generation revenue.
In use: "The irrigation district's water right predates the hydroelectric license here — their delivery isn't a negotiable constraint, it's a senior legal claim."
Misuse: treating all water uses as equally negotiable operational tradeoffs, missing that some claims (senior water rights, legally mandated ecological flows) aren't discretionary constraints the facility can choose to deprioritize.

**Seepage/movement monitoring** — Continuous instrumented monitoring of dam structural indicators (water seepage rate, structural movement, spillway condition) designed to catch slow-developing deterioration before it becomes an acute safety issue.
In use: "Seepage rate at monitoring point 4 has increased 15% over the last two readings — that gets investigated now, not at the next scheduled inspection."
Misuse: relying on periodic scheduled inspections alone rather than continuous instrumented monitoring, missing gradual deterioration trends between inspection cycles.

**Reservoir drawdown** — The planned or actual reduction in reservoir storage level over a period, driven by releases for generation, irrigation, ecological flow, or flood control, and evaluated against total available storage above binding obligations.
In use: "The proposed 200,000 acre-foot drawdown exceeds what's actually available above our remaining seasonal obligations by 81,600 acre-feet."
Misuse: planning a drawdown target based on generation revenue goals alone without first netting out the binding obligations that constrain how much storage is actually available for that purpose.

**Fish passage** — Infrastructure or operational measures (fish ladders, timed releases, bypass systems) enabling fish migration past a dam, often a specific regulatory and licensing requirement distinct from general ecological minimum flow.
In use: "Fish passage operations during the spring migration window take priority over generation scheduling for that specific facility segment."
Misuse: conflating fish passage requirements with general ecological minimum flow; fish passage often has its own specific, separately regulated operational requirements tied to migration timing.
