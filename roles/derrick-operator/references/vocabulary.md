# Vocabulary — Derrick Operator

### Trip tank
A small, calibrated tank plumbed to the well used specifically during trips to measure the exact volume of mud that goes into or comes out of the hole, independent of the larger active pit system.
**In use:** "Zero the trip tank before we pull the first stand so the fill numbers mean something."
**Common misuse:** Treated as interchangeable with the pit volume totalizer — the trip tank is precise for the small, controlled volumes of a trip; the PVT is for the larger active system and is comparatively coarse for catching a slow swab trend stand by stand.

### PVT (pit volume totalizer)
An instrumented, continuous readout of total active mud system volume across all connected pits, used mainly while circulating or drilling to catch a kick from a volume gain.
**In use:** "PVT's up six barrels in the last ten minutes with nothing logged going in — that's a flow check."
**Common misuse:** Read as a single absolute number rather than a trend; a PVT reading is only meaningful compared against its own recent baseline, since pit configuration and sensor calibration vary rig to rig.

### Swabbing
A reduction in bottomhole pressure caused by pulling pipe out of the hole too fast, which can pull formation fluid into the wellbore as if the pipe were acting like a piston.
**In use:** "We're getting a swab signature — trip tank's light every stand, not just the one."
**Common misuse:** Used loosely for "any time the well acts up while tripping," when the specific mechanism is pulling-induced pressure reduction, distinct from surge (the opposite effect, from running in too fast) or a static kick unrelated to pipe movement.

### Surge
An increase in bottomhole pressure from running pipe into the hole too fast, the mirror-image risk to swabbing, which can fracture a weak formation and cause lost circulation rather than an influx.
**In use:** "Slow the trip in through that depleted interval — we don't want to surge it and lose returns."
**Common misuse:** Conflated with swabbing as "trip speed is bad," when the two produce opposite pressure effects and opposite failure modes (kick vs. losses).

### Closed-end displacement
The volume of fluid a length of pipe physically displaces when run into or pulled from a full hole with the pipe's interior treated as sealed (relevant when tripping dry, without filling the pipe).
**In use:** "Use closed-end displacement for this calculation — we're not filling the string on the way out."
**Common misuse:** Confused with pipe capacity (the volume the pipe's bore can hold) — displacement and capacity are different numbers used in different calculations (trip-tank fill vs. mud-pumped-down-the-string volume).

### Setback (setback capacity)
The area on the rig floor, and the fingers on the derrick board that hold it, where stands of pipe are stood upright and stored during a trip; setback capacity is the structural maximum stand count or weight the derrick and substructure are rated for.
**In use:** "We're at 80 stands against a 90-stand rating — plan the trip point now, not at 90."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a soft, informal limit adjustable by how the board "looks," when it's a rated structural number from the rig's equipment documentation.

### Monkeyboard
The working platform partway up the derrick, at stand height, where the derrick operator stands to guide the top of the stand into the fingers during a trip.
**In use:** "Give me a minute to get set on the monkeyboard before you start pulling."
**Common misuse:** Used generically for "up in the derrick" — the monkeyboard is a specific fixed platform at a specific height tied to the rig's stand length, not an arbitrary elevated work position.

### Weight-up
The process of raising mud density (typically with barite) to a target program weight, calculated by mass balance against current volume and weight rather than judged by feel.
**In use:** "Weight-up to 10.5 calculates to about 270 sacks on a 500-barrel system — mix it through the hopper and check the balance."
**Common misuse:** Treated as "add barite until it feels right," ignoring that added barite also adds volume, which changes the calculation and means overshooting is common when done by eye.

### ECD (equivalent circulating density)
The effective mud weight exerted on the wellbore while circulating, which is higher than static mud weight because of the added pressure from friction as fluid moves up the annulus.
**In use:** "We're already tight on losses — don't weight up more than the program calls for or we'll push ECD past the fracture gradient."
**Common misuse:** Confused with static mud weight — ECD only exists while pumping, and a mud weight safe static can still exceed the formation's fracture gradient once circulating.

### Funnel viscosity
A field spot-check of mud's flow resistance, measured in seconds for a fixed volume (typically one quart) to drain through a Marsh funnel, used as a fast trend indicator between full lab tests.
**In use:** "Funnel viscosity's up five seconds since this morning with nothing added — check the shakers before we treat it chemically."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a precise rheological measurement on its own, when it's a fast trend check; a real diagnosis (gel strength, plastic viscosity, yield point) needs the full mud lab report.

### Flow check
A deliberate procedure — pipe picked up to a fixed point, pumps shut down, flow line observed — to determine whether the well is flowing on its own with no pumps running, the direct test for whether an influx is in progress.
**In use:** "Stopping the trip here to run a flow check before we rack another stand."
**Common misuse:** Skipped or shortened as "we'll just watch the pit for a minute" — a flow check specifically requires pumps off and a fixed reference point; watching pits while still circulating or tripping doesn't isolate the same signal.

### Trip sheet
The stand-by-stand written or electronic log of calculated versus actual mud volume during a trip, the primary record a well control review depends on after the fact.
**In use:** "Log every stand as we pull it — don't wait and fill the trip sheet in from memory at the end."
**Common misuse:** Treated as after-the-fact paperwork rather than a real-time control; a trip sheet completed retroactively can't catch a developing trend while the trip is still happening.

### Geronimo line (escape line / Gemini device)
A controlled-descent device rigged from the monkeyboard to the ground, used as emergency egress if the board becomes unsafe to leave by the normal route.
**In use:** "Test the Geronimo line from this board height before we start racking — not just at the start of the tour."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to be a fixed, one-time setup for the well, when board height and rigging can change with stand length or derrick configuration and need re-verification at the current position.
