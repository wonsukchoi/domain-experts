# Red flags

Smell tests worth catching before they show up in an incident report or a rejected load.

### Driver backing without a spotter or completed GOAL check more than once per shift
- **Usually means:** time pressure is overriding trained procedure, often following an earlier delay; second most likely, a spotter simply isn't budgeted for that route type.
- **First question:** "Was backing solo logged as an exception, or is it every stop on this route?"
- **Data to pull:** route observation or dashcam log, spotter staffing roster for the route, prior safety-audit notes for that route.

### Route or driver contamination rate trending above ~15% over a rolling 4 weeks
- **Usually means:** curbside screening discipline has slipped under time pressure, or set-out habits in the served area have changed (new residents, holiday volume).
- **First question:** "Pull the oops-tag count for this route — is it falling while contamination is rising?"
- **Data to pull:** MRF contamination audit reports by route/truck, oops-tag issuance counts, time-per-stop trend for the same period.

### Backup alarm or camera reported inoperative but the truck still dispatched
- **Usually means:** a maintenance backlog, or a normalization that treats the alarm/camera as optional once "it's worked fine without it" for a few shifts.
- **First question:** "How long has this truck run without a working alarm/camera, and was a spotter assigned as the compensating control?"
- **Data to pull:** vehicle maintenance log, dispatch assignment record, any near-miss reports for that truck or route in the period.

### One truck posting sharply lower contamination rejections than the rest of the fleet on the same route type
- **Usually means:** genuinely better screening discipline (rare enough to verify directly), or under-tagging — accepting borderline containers to protect route time and pushing the contamination risk downstream to the next audit.
- **First question:** "Is this driver's per-stop time meaningfully faster than the fleet average for the same stop count?"
- **Data to pull:** per-driver time-per-stop and tag-rate comparison across the fleet, MRF-reported contamination attributed to that route over the last several loads.

### Hopper or packer jam cleared without a documented lockout
- **Usually means:** the "it'll take 30 seconds" shortcut, most often under route-time pressure or because the lockout kit is inconvenient to reach from the cab.
- **First question:** "Walk me through exactly what you did with your hands and the control panel before you cleared that jam."
- **Data to pull:** lockout/tagout compliance log for the vehicle, packer jam-frequency history, whether the jam recurs (a recurring jam is a maintenance issue masquerading as a one-off).

### Route consistently finishing 30+ minutes over its time budget, attributed to "just a slow day"
- **Usually means:** either a genuine one-off (weather, an obstruction), or a systemic route-density problem — too many stops per mile, poor container placement, or a stop count set before the neighborhood's household count grew.
- **First question:** "Is the overage tied to one delay event, or is average seconds-per-stop drifting up across weeks?"
- **Data to pull:** route telematics time-per-stop trend, current stop count vs. the route's original design stop count, household growth data for the service area.

### Pedestrian or motorist near-miss during a backing maneuver not logged same-day
- **Usually means:** the incident is being treated as a non-event because nothing happened — exactly the reporting gap that hides a pattern until an actual injury occurs.
- **First question:** "When did this happen, and why wasn't it in today's log?"
- **Data to pull:** dashcam footage if equipped, verbal incident timeline from the driver and any spotter, comparable near-miss history for that intersection or route segment.

### Containers regularly overfilled (lids not closing, contents piled above the rim)
- **Usually means:** collection frequency mismatched to the neighborhood's actual generation rate, or residents using recycling and trash bins interchangeably to avoid extra-tag fees — raising both contamination risk and manual-handling injury risk from loose or heavy overflow.
- **First question:** "Is this overfill pattern specific to a few addresses or the whole route?"
- **Data to pull:** cart fill-rate data if RFID/scanner-equipped, service-request or complaint history for the affected addresses, the area's bulk-item or extra-tag policy.

### New or transferred driver assigned a high-density urban route within the first week
- **Usually means:** a staffing gap being filled without accounting for backing frequency and pedestrian density being highest exactly where inexperienced judgment is riskiest.
- **First question:** "What route did this driver train on, and does it match this route's backing frequency and pedestrian density?"
- **Data to pull:** driver tenure and route-history record, training completion log for backing/spotter procedure, injury or near-miss data by tenure bucket if the fleet tracks it.

### Contamination rejections blamed entirely on "residents don't sort right," with no route-level breakdown
- **Usually means:** a real resident-education gap in some areas, but also a common excuse that skips the harder question of whether curbside screening itself is catching what it should.
- **First question:** "Break the contamination down by route — is it uniform across the service area or concentrated in a few?"
- **Data to pull:** MRF contamination audit disaggregated by route/driver, oops-tag rates by route, any recent set-out education campaign and its target neighborhoods.
