# Vocabulary

Terms generalists conflate or misuse; practitioners keep them sharply separate.

## ASL vs. rear-loader vs. front-loader

**ASL (automated side loader)** picks up standardized carts with a hydraulic arm the driver operates from the cab, eliminating most manual lifting but introducing arm-proximity risk. **Rear-loader** is manually loaded at the back of the truck by a walking crew member, trading arm risk for manual-handling load and rear-hopper proximity risk. **Front-loader** services commercial dumpsters via a forked front-mounted lift, with far fewer, larger stops per day than residential routes.

**In use:** "This is an ASL route, so the crew risk is arm-proximity, not lifting — keep everyone clear of the reach envelope before you cycle it."

**Common misuse:** treating all three as interchangeable "garbage trucks" with the same hazard profile, when the controlling risk (arm proximity vs. manual handling vs. dumpster access) is different for each.

## GOAL (Get Out And Look)

The practice of physically exiting the cab and visually confirming a clear path before reversing when no spotter is present, repeated if enough time passes or conditions change.

**In use:** "No spotter on this one — GOAL it, and if that delivery van pulls in behind you, GOAL it again before you back."

**Common misuse:** treating a single GOAL check at the start of a stop as sufficient for the whole backing maneuver, rather than re-checking when conditions change mid-maneuver.

## Contamination rate

The percentage, by weight or volume, of non-recyclable or improperly prepared material in a load presented as recyclables. Distinct from a single container being "wrong" — it's a load-level (or fleet-level, route-level) aggregate figure that MRF contracts price in bands.

**In use:** "Our contamination rate crossed 20% last audit — that's surcharge territory, not just a few bad containers."

**Common misuse:** discussing contamination only at the individual-container level ("that one bin was dirty") without connecting it to the load-level threshold that determines whether the whole truck's contents get processed or landfilled.

## Oops tag / tag-and-leave

A notice left on a container identifying a specific contamination issue and explaining that the container was not collected, as opposed to collecting it anyway or leaving no explanation at all.

**In use:** "Tag it for bagged trash specifically — a blank rejection notice doesn't tell them what to fix next week."

**Common misuse:** using a generic "we couldn't collect this" tag instead of naming the specific contaminant, which removes the behavior-change signal the tag exists to provide.

## Spotter vs. backup camera/alarm

A **spotter** is a person providing real-time visual guidance and stop authority during a backing maneuver — an administrative control. A **backup camera/alarm** is an engineering control giving the driver a supplementary view and audible warning, but with no independent judgment or stop authority.

**In use:** "Camera's working fine, but this is a persistent-hazard stop — spotter's still mandatory, the camera doesn't replace it."

**Common misuse:** treating a functioning backup camera and alarm as sufficient on their own for high-risk backing situations, when they're designed to supplement a spotter, not substitute for one.

## Lockout/tagout (LOTO)

The procedure of isolating a machine's energy source, physically locking it, and tagging it before performing maintenance or clearing a jam — verified by attempting to operate the machine and confirming it doesn't respond.

**In use:** "Lock it out before you reach in, even if it's just a stuck cardboard flap — verify zero energy, don't assume it."

**Common misuse:** skipping the lockout step for jams perceived as quick or minor, treating LOTO as proportional to job size rather than as an unconditional prerequisite for reaching into the hazard zone.

## Route density vs. route length

**Route density** is stops per mile (or per hour) — the driver of collection economics. **Route length** is total distance or total stop count, which says little on its own about how efficiently a route can be run.

**In use:** "This route's long, but it's dense — 40 stops a mile. The sparse rural route with half the stops actually runs slower per stop."

**Common misuse:** judging route efficiency by stop count or mileage alone instead of density, which is what actually determines time-per-stop and cost-per-ton.

## Single-stream vs. dual-stream recycling

**Single-stream** collects all recyclables (paper, plastic, metal, glass) in one container, sorted later at the MRF — higher participation, higher contamination risk. **Dual-stream** separates fiber (paper/cardboard) from containers (plastic/metal/glass) at the curb, lowering contamination but requiring more collection infrastructure and resident compliance.

**In use:** "We went single-stream to boost participation, and contamination went up with it — that trade-off isn't optional, it's baked into the collection method."

**Common misuse:** treating a rising contamination rate after a single-stream conversion as a sorting or education failure alone, without recognizing it's a structural trade-off of the collection method itself.

## Tipping fee vs. processing rebate

**Tipping fee** is the cost paid to dispose of a load at a landfill or transfer station. **Processing rebate** is what a hauler receives (or is credited) for a load of recyclables accepted by a MRF. When a recyclables load gets rejected for contamination, the swing is from a rebate to a fee — a cost delta on both ends of the same load.

**In use:** "That load flipped from a rebate to a tipping fee once it got rejected — the contamination didn't just cost us the rebate, it cost us the fee on top of it."

**Common misuse:** thinking of a rejected recyclables load as merely "losing the rebate," missing that it also now incurs a disposal cost it wouldn't have if it had never been contaminated.

## Hopper vs. packer/compaction chamber

The **hopper** is the opening where material is loaded into the truck. The **packer** (or compaction chamber/blade) is the mechanism that compresses material into the body of the truck. Entanglement hazards differ by location: hopper incidents typically involve reaching in to clear a jam; packer incidents involve material shifting or a blade cycling unexpectedly.

**In use:** "The jam's at the packer blade, not the hopper opening — different lockout point, don't assume the hopper procedure covers it."

**Common misuse:** using "hopper" and "packer" interchangeably when describing where a jam or hazard is located, which can lead to the wrong lockout point being isolated.

## CFOI fatality rate

The Bureau of Labor Statistics' Census of Fatal Occupational Injuries expresses occupational risk as fatalities per 100,000 full-time-equivalent (FTE) workers, allowing comparison across occupations of very different sizes.

**In use:** "The all-worker average is around 3.7 per 100,000 — this occupation runs roughly 9-10x that, which is why it lands in BLS's top-10 list most years."

**Common misuse:** citing raw fatality counts instead of the rate, which makes a large occupation look disproportionately dangerous (or a small one look safe) relative to its actual per-worker risk.

## Struck-by vs. backing-over incident

**Struck-by** is the broader BLS category for being hit by a moving vehicle or object, including traffic while working outside the cab. **Backing-over** is the specific subtype where the collection vehicle itself, in reverse, strikes a pedestrian or co-worker — the leading transportation-incident subtype in this occupation.

**In use:** "Most of our transportation-incident fatalities are backing-over, not struck-by-traffic — that's why spotter discipline outranks hi-vis vests as the priority fix."

**Common misuse:** treating "struck-by" as synonymous with "hit by traffic," collapsing the backing-over subtype (preventable primarily through spotter/GOAL discipline) into general traffic-safety messaging that targets the wrong control.

## MRF (materials recovery facility)

The facility where mixed recyclables are sorted, by machine and by hand, into commodity streams for sale to processors. The MRF, not the hauler, applies the contamination audit that determines whether a load is accepted at standard terms, surcharged, or rejected outright.

**In use:** "The MRF's last audit flagged our route at 22% contamination — that's their number, not ours, and it's contractual."

**Common misuse:** assuming the hauler controls or sets the contamination threshold, when it's typically set by the MRF's contract terms and can vary by facility.
