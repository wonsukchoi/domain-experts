# Vocabulary

Working vocabulary that generalists reliably misuse. Format per term: definition, how a practitioner actually uses it, the common misuse.

## Student credit hour (SCH)

- **Definition:** the unit (one student enrolled in a one-credit course for a term) that most university budget formulas use to allocate resources back to departments.
- **Practitioner usage:** "Our two intro sections generate more SCH per semester than the entire upper-division major sequence combined — that's the number the dean actually looks at."
- **Common misuse:** treating SCH as a pure teaching-quality proxy. A large, well-enrolled gen-ed course can generate huge SCH with mediocre evaluations, and a small, excellent seminar generates almost none — SCH measures budget contribution, not teaching quality.

## Indirect costs / F&A rate

- **Definition:** the percentage a university adds on top of a grant's direct costs to cover facilities and administration; negotiated institution-wide, not set by the PI.
- **Practitioner usage:** "The award is $450,000 direct, but the university's F&A rate adds another roughly 50–60% on top for the total award to the institution — that overhead isn't mine to spend."
- **Common misuse:** assuming the PI controls or benefits directly from indirect-cost dollars; they fund the institution's infrastructure, not the lab's discretionary budget.

## Broader impacts (NSF)

- **Definition:** one of NSF's two co-equal merit-review criteria (alongside Intellectual Merit) — the proposal's benefit to society beyond the scientific result itself.
- **Practitioner usage:** "Reviewers flagged the broader-impacts section as generic — we need to name the specific intro-course lab this data will feed, not just say 'public engagement.'"
- **Common misuse:** treating broader impacts as a box-checking paragraph appended after the science is written, rather than a planned, budgeted activity integrated with the project.

## Tenure clock / stop-the-clock

- **Definition:** the fixed number of years (commonly six) before a mandatory tenure decision; "stop-the-clock" is a formal extension (e.g., for parental leave or documented hardship) that pauses the countdown.
- **Practitioner usage:** "She stopped the clock for a semester after her second child, so her dossier is due in year 6.5 of actual calendar time, not year 6."
- **Common misuse:** assuming a stop-the-clock extension is automatic or informal — it requires a formal request and institutional approval, and doesn't reset expectations for the amount of work in the dossier.

## Concept inventory

- **Definition:** a validated, standardized multiple-choice instrument whose wrong-answer options are built from documented student misconceptions, used to diagnose (not just grade) understanding before and after instruction.
- **Practitioner usage:** "The Geoscience Concept Inventory pre-test told us which specific misconceptions this cohort is bringing in, before we wrote a single learning objective."
- **Common misuse:** treating a concept inventory like a normal quiz and grading it for a course score — its value is the item-level pre/post comparison, not a single graded total.

## Course release

- **Definition:** a formal reduction in teaching load (fewer courses that term) granted in exchange for a specific research, administrative, or grant-funded obligation.
- **Practitioner usage:** "The grant buys out one course this fall — that's a course release, not a favor from the chair."
- **Common misuse:** assuming a course release is discretionary time; it's usually tied contractually to a specific deliverable (a grant's effort commitment, an administrative role) and is tracked against it.

## Carnegie classification (R1 / R2 / PUI)

- **Definition:** a university classification system describing research activity level — R1 (very high research activity), R2 (high research activity), and PUI (primarily undergraduate institution) carry materially different default teaching loads and tenure expectations.
- **Practitioner usage:** "A 2-2 teaching load and a funded-research tenure bar is standard at an R1; the same CV would look underloaded on teaching at a PUI with a 3-3 or 4-4 load."
- **Common misuse:** assuming teaching-load or research-productivity norms transfer across institution types; a strong R1 research record doesn't automatically read as a strong PUI tenure case, where teaching evidence carries more weight.

## Job Hazard Analysis (JHA)

- **Definition:** a documented, activity-by-activity risk assessment (hazard, likelihood, severity, mitigation) completed before a field or lab activity, typically required by a university's environmental health and safety office.
- **Practitioner usage:** "The JHA for the coastal transect flags rip current and tide-timing hazards separately from the outcrop-access hazards — they need different mitigations."
- **Common misuse:** conflating a JHA with a liability waiver. The waiver is a legal document signed by participants; the JHA is the planning document that determines what mitigations are actually in place before anyone signs anything.

## Data Management Plan (DMP)

- **Definition:** a required NSF proposal component describing how data generated by the project will be documented, stored, and made available after the grant ends.
- **Practitioner usage:** "The DMP names the specific repository (a data center, not 'available upon request') and the retention period, because reviewers check that it's concrete."
- **Common misuse:** writing generic language ("data will be shared with the community") without naming a specific repository, format, or timeline — reviewers read this as a compliance afterthought.

## External letter (tenure/promotion)

- **Definition:** a solicited evaluation letter from a senior scholar outside the candidate's institution, assessing the candidate's standing in the field, typically arm's-length from direct collaborators.
- **Practitioner usage:** "We need letters from people who know the subfield but haven't co-authored with her — a glowing letter from a frequent collaborator reads as weaker evidence than an arm's-length one."
- **Common misuse:** assuming any positive letter helps equally; committees discount letters from close collaborators or former advisors more than letters from arm's-length peers, because the latter carries less bias.

## h-index

- **Definition:** a single number where h papers have each been cited at least h times; a rough, field- and career-length-dependent measure of publication impact.
- **Practitioner usage:** "His h-index looks modest for his cohort, but two of his papers are the field's standard method citation — that carries more weight than the number alone."
- **Common misuse:** comparing h-index across subfields or career stages without adjustment, or using it as a substitute for reading the actual papers being cited.

## Panel review vs mail (ad hoc) review

- **Definition:** panel review convenes multiple reviewers together to discuss and rank proposals relative to each other; mail review collects independent written reviews without discussion. NSF programs vary in which they use, and some use both.
- **Practitioner usage:** "This program uses panel review, so a proposal that's individually strong but doesn't compare well against the specific pool in the room can still lose out — it's relative, not absolute."
- **Common misuse:** assuming a strong individual review score guarantees funding; panel ranking is comparative within that cycle's applicant pool, not an absolute bar.

## Adjunct / contingent faculty

- **Definition:** non-tenure-track instructional staff, often course-by-course or year-by-year contracts, distinct from tenure-track or permanent lecturer lines.
- **Practitioner usage:** "The intro sequence runs partly on adjunct instructors — their contract renewal timing needs to be locked in before fall registration opens, not after."
- **Common misuse:** using "adjunct" and "lecturer" interchangeably; a permanent lecturer line typically carries continuing-contract protections an adjunct appointment does not.
