### Airworthiness Directive (AD)
A legally mandatory corrective action issued by the FAA under 14 CFR Part 39 when an unsafe condition is found in an aircraft, engine, or component design, applicable to every aircraft of that type or configuration.
**In use:** "AD 2021-09-13 grounds N612MA until the fuel-nozzle inspection is complied with — it's not optional and it's not a manufacturer recommendation."
**Common misuse:** Treating an AD as advisory guidance similar to a manufacturer service bulletin, when compliance is a regulatory requirement with a mandatory deadline, not a best-practice suggestion.

### Whichever occurs first (dual threshold)
An AD or maintenance-program interval expressed as two independent limits (commonly flight hours and calendar time), where the requirement triggers at whichever limit is reached first, not at the later or averaged one.
**In use:** "The hour leg crossed 42 hours ago — the aircraft having 5 months left on the calendar leg doesn't matter under whichever-occurs-first."
**Common misuse:** Checking only one leg (usually the calendar date, because it's easier to read) and treating compliance on that leg as compliance overall.

### Continuous Airworthiness Maintenance Program (CAMP)
The certificate holder's own system, required under Part 121/135, for tracking and performing all required inspections and maintenance, including AD compliance, across its fleet.
**In use:** "This finding isn't about the aircraft — it's that Meridian's CAMP tracking dropped the hour leg for every tail that moved through the June migration."
**Common misuse:** Treating a CAMP tracking defect as a one-aircraft paperwork issue rather than as a system-level defect that exposes every aircraft that passed through the same process.

### Certificate Management Office / Team (CMO, POI/PMI/PAI)
The FAA office and the specific assigned inspectors — Principal Operations, Principal Maintenance, and Principal Avionics Inspector — who carry ongoing surveillance of a specific air carrier's certificate.
**In use:** "The header-size question goes to the PMI, not something the operations side signs off on."
**Common misuse:** Assuming any FAA inspector can make a finding or sign off across all three disciplines, rather than routing a discipline-specific question to the inspector actually assigned that principal role on the certificate.

### Safety Assurance System (SAS)
The FAA's current data-driven, risk-based surveillance model for Part 121/135 certificate holders, which assigns risk tiers that drive surveillance frequency and replaced the legacy Air Transportation Oversight System (ATOS).
**In use:** "This certificate's SAS risk tier is what set the minimum surveillance frequency for this cycle, not the inspector's personal judgment of how the operator is doing."
**Common misuse:** Treating surveillance frequency as inspector discretion rather than as an output the risk-tier model already assigned, which the inspector then executes against.

### Operations Specifications (OpSpecs)
The formal, legally binding authorizations attached to a certificate that define exactly what an operator is approved to do — routes, aircraft types, weather minimums, special authorizations.
**In use:** "Check the OpSpecs before the flight — this route requires an RNP authorization the certificate doesn't currently carry."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a certificate's general authority (e.g., "Part 135 on-demand") covers an operation without checking whether the specific OpSpecs actually list that route, aircraft, or authorization.

### Safety Management System (SMS) / Safety Risk Management (SRM)
A required organizational framework (14 CFR Part 5) with four pillars — Safety Policy, Safety Risk Management, Safety Assurance, Safety Promotion — for proactively identifying and managing operational risk; SRM is the pillar that assesses hazards before and after operational changes.
**In use:** "The SRM assessment for the new route should have been completed before the schedule change went live, not after."
**Common misuse:** Auditing SMS as a set of required documents that exist, rather than as a functioning detection loop — a hazard report closed with no risk assessment is a failure of the system, not a missing form.

### Compliance philosophy / compliance action
The FAA's post-2015 enforcement posture (FAA Order 2150.3) that resolves inadvertent, non-recurring deviations through corrective, non-punitive compliance action rather than automatically pursuing legal enforcement.
**In use:** "This is a first-time, documented systemic cause with prompt correction — compliance action, not a civil penalty."
**Common misuse:** Applying compliance-action leniency by default regardless of recurrence or intent, rather than treating it as a classification that has to be earned by evidence of an isolated, non-willful cause.

### Letter of Correction (LOC)
A formal administrative-action record requiring the certificate holder to complete and verify a specific corrective action, used for findings that don't rise to legal enforcement but need a documented, tracked record.
**In use:** "The fleet-wide audit and process fix go into the Letter of Correction as required corrective actions with a verification deadline."
**Common misuse:** Treating a Letter of Correction as informal or off-the-record, when it is a formal instrument that becomes part of the certificate's enforcement history and is checked on future findings.

### Enforcement Investigative Report (EIR)
The formal written record an inspector prepares documenting a finding's facts, regulatory citation, root-cause and recurrence evidence, and recommended enforcement classification.
**In use:** "The EIR needs the exact hours-elapsed math and the migration root cause, not just 'aircraft found overdue.'"
**Common misuse:** Writing the EIR as a narrative summary of impressions rather than as a citation-and-evidence record that has to support the recommended classification on its own.

### Voluntary Disclosure Reporting Program (VDRP) / Aviation Safety Action Program (ASAP)
FAA programs that generally forgo legal enforcement for a deviation the certificate holder discloses voluntarily, completely, and before FAA discovery, in exchange for a corrective action.
**In use:** "This doesn't qualify for VDRP — the inspector's own records sample found it first."
**Common misuse:** Treating an operator's willingness to disclose as sufficient for eligibility regardless of timing, when the entire test is whether disclosure preceded FAA-initiated discovery of the same item.

### Ground / grounding
The immediate restriction of an aircraft from revenue flight, whether inspector-directed on a finding or operator-initiated, pending resolution of a specific airworthiness item.
**In use:** "N612MA is grounded effective this finding, independent of whatever enforcement classification the paperwork gap ends up getting."
**Common misuse:** Conflating the grounding decision (an airworthiness question, answered now) with the enforcement classification (a culpability question, answered separately) as if one decision resolves both.

### Certificate suspension vs. certificate revocation
Two distinct legal enforcement actions against an operating or airman certificate — suspension is time-limited with reinstatement on completion of specified conditions; revocation permanently terminates the certificate, requiring a new application to operate again.
**In use:** "This is a suspension with a reinstatement condition, not a revocation — the certificate holder can fly again once the corrective action is verified."
**Common misuse:** Using "suspension" and "revocation" interchangeably in a finding or a public-facing statement, when they carry materially different consequences and different paths back to operating.

### Terminating action
A one-time corrective action (commonly a part replacement or modification) specified in an AD that, once completed, eliminates the need for further repetitive compliance inspections.
**In use:** "The terminating action retires the AD entirely — no more 800-hour inspections on that engine after the replacement."
**Common misuse:** Continuing repetitive inspection cycles indefinitely without evaluating whether the terminating action's one-time cost is lower than the cumulative cost and recurring compliance risk of continuing inspections.

### National Program Guidelines (NPG)
FAA guidance setting minimum surveillance activity counts (ramp inspections, records reviews, en route checks) an assigned inspector must complete per certificate per cycle, calibrated to the certificate's SAS risk tier.
**In use:** "The NPG minimum for this risk tier is what set this cycle's records-review requirement, not a discretionary extra."
**Common misuse:** Treating NPG-mandated surveillance activities as a ceiling an inspector can trim under schedule pressure, rather than as the minimum floor the risk-tier model requires.
