# Crane and Tower Operator — Vocabulary

### Sail area (wind-exposed area)
The surface area of a load (or rigging) that presents resistance to wind, expressed relative to the load's weight as a ratio (m² per tonne) to compare against a chart's reference figure.
**In use:** "This panel's 4.8 m²/t — four times the chart's reference ratio, so we derate the wind limit, not just check the flat number."
**Common misuse:** Judging wind risk by the load's weight or the chart's rated capacity alone, missing that a light, large-surface load can be wind-limited well before it's capacity-limited.

### In-service / out-of-service wind rating
The two wind thresholds an OEM chart specifies: in-service is the limit for active operation (lifting, slewing), commonly stated as a 3-second gust at the jib head; out-of-service is the higher threshold at which the crane, set to free-slew, is rated to withstand wind while not operating.
**In use:** "We're well under the in-service number now, but the storm forecast means we set free-slew before we're anywhere near out-of-service limits."
**Common misuse:** Treating the out-of-service (free-slew) rating as available margin during active operation, when it only applies once the jib is released to weathervane and lifting has stopped.

### Weathervaning (free-slew)
Releasing the jib's slew brake so the crane rotates freely with the wind rather than resisting it as a fixed structure, set ahead of a high-wind event or at shift-end when the crane is unattended.
**In use:** "Forecast's calling for gusts past our out-of-service number tonight — get her set to free-slew before we lock up."
**Common misuse:** Leaving the jib locked against rotation into an approaching wind event, which loads the mast and foundation as a fixed obstruction instead of letting the structure shed the load by rotating.

### Blind lift
Any pick or portion of a pick where the operator cannot directly see the load, the pick point, or the landing zone from the cab, requiring full reliance on a signal person's radio calls for that segment.
**In use:** "Last third of this swing is blind behind the parapet — that's a call-and-repeat for every motion from here, not just at the start."
**Common misuse:** Treating a single "clear" call at the start of a blind segment as certifying the entire segment, rather than requiring a distinct confirmed exchange for each motion within it.

### Signal person
A person qualified to direct crane movement by confirmed radio or hand signal when the operator's view is obstructed; on a tower crane, frequently the only source of information for an entire segment of a pick, not a supplementary check on what the operator can already see.
**In use:** "Operator's got zero sightline on the roof deck — signal person's radio is the only information we have for that landing."
**Common misuse:** Treating the signal person's role as a backup to the operator's own judgment, when for a genuinely blind segment there is no independent operator judgment to back up — the call is the only information available.

### Load share
The portion of a shared load's total weight that a single crane in a two-crane (tandem) lift actually carries, determined by the load's center of gravity relative to both cranes' pick points.
**In use:** "CG's 6 m from your hook and 4 m from mine on a 10 m spread — that puts 10,800 of the 18,000 on you, not an even split."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a two-crane lift splits the load roughly evenly between the two machines, rather than calculating the actual share from CG position, which can load one crane well above the other.

### Derated ceiling (multi-crane lift)
The working limit each crane in a shared lift is held to, set below its own chart capacity (commonly around 75%) to absorb the risk that the engineered center-of-gravity estimate differs from the load's actual behavior once airborne.
**In use:** "Your calculated share is 72% of chart capacity — that's inside our 75% ceiling, but there's not much room if the CG estimate is off."
**Common misuse:** Comparing each crane's share only to 100% of its own chart capacity, treating the full chart number as the safe ceiling instead of the derated one that accounts for CG uncertainty.

### Trolley radius
The working radius on a hammerhead (trolley-jib) tower crane, set by how far the trolley has traveled out along the horizontal jib — the operator's primary means of changing radius on that jib type, distinct from a luffing crane's radius change by raising or lowering the jib angle.
**In use:** "Trolley's at 40 m — that's the radius to chart, not the jib's full 60 m length."
**Common misuse:** Confusing the jib's total length with the load's actual working radius, when the trolley position along the jib — not the jib's maximum reach — is what the chart is read against.

### Luffing jib
A tower crane configuration that changes working radius by raising or lowering the angle of the jib itself, rather than traversing a trolley along a fixed horizontal jib.
**In use:** "We're luffing in to clear that rooftop unit, not trolleying — radius changes with jib angle on this rig."
**Common misuse:** Applying a hammerhead crane's trolley-radius chart logic to a luffing jib, when the controlling variable for radius (and therefore capacity) is jib angle, not trolley position.

### Freestanding height
The maximum height a tower crane's mast can reach without being tied or braced to the structure being built, an engineered limit specific to the installed mast section and foundation, not something the operator can extend by choice.
**In use:** "We're at freestanding height limit for this mast section — next lift of steel needs a tie-in confirmed first, not just more mast."
**Common misuse:** Treating freestanding height as a soft guideline that can be exceeded briefly for one more lift, when it's an engineered structural limit tied to the specific foundation and mast configuration installed.

### Counterweight configuration
The specific set of counterweight blocks physically installed on the crane's counter-jib, which — together with jib length — determines which chart page applies; changing it requires an engineered reconfiguration, not a field adjustment.
**In use:** "Confirm the block count matches the chart page before we read capacity off it — erection drawing said eight, but count what's actually on the counter-jib."
**Common misuse:** Reading capacity from a chart page matching the erection drawing's specified counterweight without confirming the as-built block count, when a missing or substituted block invalidates that chart page.

### Slewing
Rotating the crane's jib and counter-jib around the tower's vertical axis, the tower-crane equivalent of a mobile crane's "swing."
**In use:** "Slewing right, five degrees, on my mark" — the exact call-and-repeat phrasing used through a blind segment.
**Common misuse:** Using "swing" and "slew" interchangeably in radio calls without a fixed vocabulary, increasing the odds of a misheard or ambiguous instruction during a blind motion.

### Climb cycle
The engineered sequence of raising a tower crane's mast section by section (via an internal or external climbing frame) as the structure rises, a distinct operational state with its own load and wind restrictions separate from normal lifting.
**In use:** "We're mid climb-cycle tomorrow morning — no normal lifting on the schedule until the frame's re-secured and inspected."
**Common misuse:** Scheduling routine lifts during or immediately after a climb cycle without confirming the climbing frame has been re-secured and inspected, treating the climb as instantaneous rather than a distinct operational state.
