# Real estate broker working vocabulary

Terms a broker uses precisely; generalists (and juniors) tend to blur the pairs.

### Contingent vs. pending
Contingent = an offer is accepted but one or more contingencies (inspection, financing, appraisal, sale-of-buyer's-home) are still unresolved. Pending = all contingencies have been satisfied or waived; only closing mechanics remain.
**In use:** "It's contingent on the appraisal only now — inspection and financing already cleared."
**Common misuse:** treating "contingent" and "pending" as interchangeable stages of "basically sold." A contingent listing can still fall through; buyers and other agents track which contingency is outstanding, not just the label.

### Appraisal gap
The dollar difference between the contract price and the lender's appraised value, which the buyer must cover in cash (beyond the down payment) if the deal is to close at the contract price without renegotiation.
**In use:** "Contract's at $678,000, appraisal came in at $660,000 — that's an $18,000 gap, and the buyer's addendum only covers $10,000 of it."
**Common misuse:** confusing appraisal-gap coverage with the escalation-clause cap — they resolve two different risks (competing-offer price vs. lender valuation) and a strong number on one says nothing about the other.

### Escalation clause
An addendum stating a buyer will automatically increase their offer by a set increment above any verified competing offer, up to a stated cap.
**In use:** "Base offer $650,000, escalating $3,000 over any verified offer, capped at $685,000."
**Common misuse:** submitting one without a cap, or without a verification requirement — an uncapped or unverifiable escalation clause is unenforceable in practice and a liability in writing.

### Dual agency vs. designated agency
Dual agency: one broker (or, in some states, one agent) represents both buyer and seller in the same transaction with reduced duties to each (no confidential negotiating advice to either side). Designated agency: the brokerage represents both sides, but two different agents within it are each designated to a single party, preserving full advocacy for each — legal in some states, banned or restricted in others.
**In use:** "This brokerage allows designated agency, so I can still advise you on strategy — my colleague represents the seller, not me."
**Common misuse:** treating dual agency as "neutral, so nothing changes" — it removes specific advisory duties (won't advise on negotiating position against the other party) and must be disclosed and consented to in writing before it applies, not after.

### Exclusive right-to-sell vs. exclusive agency vs. open listing
Exclusive right-to-sell: broker earns commission on any sale during the term regardless of who found the buyer, including the seller themselves. Exclusive agency: broker earns commission unless the seller personally procures the buyer. Open listing: seller can list with multiple brokers and only owes commission to whichever one produces the buyer.
**In use:** "It's an exclusive right-to-sell, so even if your cousin buys it directly, the commission's still owed."
**Common misuse:** assuming all listing agreements work like exclusive right-to-sell by default — the three types allocate risk and marketing incentive very differently, and MLS cooperation is typically conditioned on the first.

### Days on market (DOM) vs. cumulative days on market (CDOM)
DOM = days since the current listing went active. CDOM = total days on market across relistings, including any period the listing was withdrawn and relisted at a new price.
**In use:** "DOM resets to zero on today's relist, but CDOM still shows 74 — buyers' agents pull CDOM, so the reset doesn't hide the history."
**Common misuse:** relisting solely to reset the public DOM counter and assuming buyers won't notice — CDOM and price-history fields in most MLS systems make this visible to any agent who checks.

### Comparative market analysis (CMA) vs. appraisal
CMA: a broker's opinion of value based on comparable sales, used to set or evaluate a list price — not a licensed valuation. Appraisal: a state-licensed appraiser's opinion of value, governed by USPAP, required by the lender before funding a loan.
**In use:** "My CMA supports $650,000, but the lender's appraisal is the number that actually has to clear for financing."
**Common misuse:** presenting a CMA to a buyer or lender as if it carries the same weight as an appraisal — it's persuasive for pricing strategy, not a substitute for the appraisal in the financing process.

### Earnest money deposit (EMD)
Buyer's good-faith deposit, held in escrow, applied to the purchase price at closing or forfeited/returned per the contract's contingency terms if the deal falls through.
**In use:** "EMD is 2% here — thinner than the 3% we saw on the last two competing offers, which signals less commitment."
**Common misuse:** treating EMD size as purely a function of the buyer's cash position rather than as a competitive-commitment signal that sellers and listing agents read directly when ranking offers.

### Appraisal contingency vs. financing contingency
Appraisal contingency: buyer can renegotiate or exit if the appraised value comes in below the contract price. Financing contingency: buyer can exit if their loan isn't approved, independent of appraisal outcome — a loan can be denied for reasons (debt-to-income, credit event) that have nothing to do with the appraised value.
**In use:** "They waived the appraisal contingency but kept the financing contingency — different risk, still covered."
**Common misuse:** assuming a waived appraisal contingency means the whole deal is now unconditional; the financing contingency is a separate, still-live exit unless it's also waived.

### Steering
Directing buyers toward or away from specific neighborhoods based on a protected characteristic (race, color, religion, sex, national origin, familial status, disability), even implicitly through curated inventory or coded neighborhood descriptions.
**In use:** "Pull the same search criteria for every buyer and let them choose — don't pre-filter by 'what fits their family.'"
**Common misuse:** assuming steering requires an explicit discriminatory statement; regulators and courts look at the pattern of listings shown across clients, not any single conversation.

### Net sheet
An itemized estimate of what a seller will actually receive (or a buyer will actually pay) at closing, after commission, payoff, prorations, and concessions.
**In use:** "Before you counter, let's run the net sheet — a $10,000 higher price with a $6,000 concession only nets $4,000 more."
**Common misuse:** comparing offers or counters on headline price alone without re-running the net sheet for each scenario — the ranking can flip once concessions and closing-cost splits are included.

### Seller concessions
Costs the seller agrees to cover on the buyer's behalf (closing costs, rate buydown, repair credit), negotiated as part of the offer rather than a price reduction.
**In use:** "Buyer's at full price but wants a $8,000 concession toward closing costs — that's a 1.2% price cut wearing a full-price sticker."
**Common misuse:** treating "full price with concessions" as equivalent to "full price" when reporting to a seller — the net-sheet impact is the same as a price reduction and should be presented as one.
