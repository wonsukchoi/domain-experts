## Axis-of-action diagram notes (filled example, Scene 22 chase)

Top-down sketch of the three street segments, camera positions marked at each setup, with the established axis line drawn through the direction of travel.

- **Segment 1 (approach):** axis runs west→east along the street; camera setups 1–5 all placed on the north side of the line (screen-right travel maintained).
- **Segment 2 (intersection turn):** the chase vehicle turns south — this is a motivated axis change (a turn through frame), not a violation. New axis established south→further-south for segment 3; panel 12 explicitly shows the turn to carry the audience across the line.
- **Segment 3 (exit):** camera setups 13–15 placed consistent with the new axis from panel 12 onward; sideswipe beat (panels 16–20) and stunt angle panels (21–30) all checked against this segment's axis before drawing.

Note: only one axis change occurs in the sequence, and it's carried by an on-screen turn (panel 12) rather than an unmotivated cut — this is the difference between an intentional axis reset and a continuity error.

## Coverage/schedule memo (filled example — see SKILL.md worked example for full text)

Delivered as a one-page memo to the director and 1st AD: panel counts and hour estimates broken out per scene by coverage type, the basis for each estimate (comparable prior scene), total hours against the budgeted prep window, and any schedule flag (e.g., a fourth street segment added after a location scout) stated as an explicit delta in panels and hours rather than folded silently into the existing estimate.
