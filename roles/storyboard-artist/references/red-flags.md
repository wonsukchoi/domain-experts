# Storyboard Artist — Red Flags

### Panel count for a scene was set with a flat panels-per-second ratio, not a beat/setup count
- **Usually means:** the estimate was built off a rule of thumb applied uniformly, which overdraws static dialogue and underdraws action or VFX-heavy beats.
- **First question:** how many distinct camera setups or staging beats does the script actually call for in this scene?
- **Data to pull:** the beat breakdown against the script, setup by setup.

### A multi-setup sequence was boarded without an axis-of-action diagram
- **Usually means:** nobody confirmed the 180-degree line before drawing, and a screen-direction flip won't surface until the panels are viewed in order.
- **First question:** has the axis of action been charted on a top-down diagram for this sequence, and does every panel respect the same screen side?
- **Data to pull:** the staging diagram, checked panel-by-panel against camera side.

### Drawing-hour estimate was quoted from a "typical panel," not the closest comparable scene
- **Usually means:** the schedule number is a guess, not a capacity claim, and is likely wrong in the direction of underbidding action or arrow-heavy sequences.
- **First question:** what's the closest comparable scene by coverage type and setup count, and how many hours did it actually take?
- **Data to pull:** the logged hours from the nearest comparable scene in the production's boarding log.

### First-pass panels were rendered to tight/finished fidelity before a pitch or director review
- **Usually means:** time is being spent polishing art that's likely to be redrawn once the beat itself is reviewed, rather than saving finish work for the approved pass.
- **First question:** has this sequence been pitched or reviewed yet, and if not, why is it past thumbnail fidelity?
- **Data to pull:** the revision log's fidelity-stage field against the pitch/review schedule.

### More than roughly a third of a sequence's panels changed after a single pitch or review
- **Usually means:** the beat structure itself has a problem that redrawing tighter won't fix — the story or staging needs to change before more panels are drawn against it.
- **First question:** is this a drawing note or a beat note, and has the story supervisor or director signed off on the beat before the redraw starts?
- **Data to pull:** the revision log's change count per sequence, and whether the note is tagged staging vs. story.

### No timed animatic or story reel exists for a schedule-critical sequence before it's called locked
- **Usually means:** panel-by-panel sign-off has been mistaken for a timing approval, and pacing problems that only surface when cut against dialogue or music haven't been caught yet.
- **First question:** has this sequence been cut into the reel against scratch audio or a temp track, and timed?
- **Data to pull:** the animatic/story reel's cut list and running time against the script's estimated screen time.

### Boards for a scene aren't marked with the script draft they were boarded against
- **Usually means:** the script is still in active revision and the boards can't be traced to a specific draft, risking rework built on a version that's already been changed.
- **First question:** which script draft date is stamped on this sequence's panels, and does it match the current draft?
- **Data to pull:** the panel revision log's draft-date field against the script's version history.

### An action or stunt sequence has fewer panels than its dialogue-scene neighbor of similar runtime
- **Usually means:** coverage density wasn't adjusted for scene type — action needs denser beat coverage for staging and impact timing than a static dialogue scene of the same length.
- **First question:** does the panel count reflect the number of staging/impact beats in this sequence, or was it estimated off the scene's runtime alone?
- **Data to pull:** the beat breakdown for both scenes, compared side by side.

### A VFX- or stunt-heavy panel has no camera-move arrows or impact markers
- **Usually means:** the panel is being treated as a static composition reference rather than the shot-execution document the DP, stunt coordinator, or animator actually needs.
- **First question:** does this panel communicate the camera move and the impact beat, or only the final frame's composition?
- **Data to pull:** the panel itself, checked against the shot list's camera-move and stunt notes for that setup.
