# Storyboard Artist — Vocabulary

### Coverage
The set of camera setups or panels needed to fully convey a scene's staging, action, and cuts — a function of how many distinct beats or setups the scene has, not its runtime.
**In use:** "The chase needs coverage on the sideswipe from two angles or the stunt team can't see the near-miss distance."
**Common misuse:** Treated as interchangeable with panel count set by a flat time-based ratio, rather than driven by the actual number of setups or staging beats in the scene.

### Axis of action (180-degree line)
The imaginary line through a scene's action or between two characters that determines consistent screen-left/screen-right camera placement across a sequence of setups; crossing it without a motivated reason flips the audience's sense of spatial relationship.
**In use:** "We're crossing the intersection at panel 12 — that flips the axis unless we motivate it with a car turning through frame."
**Common misuse:** Checked panel-by-panel in isolation instead of across the sequence in order, which is the only way a violation actually shows up.

### Animatic (story reel)
The approved boards cut into a timed sequence against scratch dialogue, temp music, or sound effects, used to test pacing before committing to expensive production (animation) or as a pre-shoot planning reference (live action).
**In use:** "Panel-by-panel the sequence looks fine — cut it into the animatic before we call the timing locked."
**Common misuse:** Skipped in favor of individual panel sign-off, when the animatic is the actual gate for whether the pacing works, not the panels alone.

### Beat board
A loose, low-fidelity set of panels used to work out a scene's story or staging structure before committing to full coverage — common in animation story pitches.
**In use:** "Don't board full coverage yet — beat-board it first so the story team can react to the structure, not the drawings."
**Common misuse:** Rendered to finished fidelity prematurely, defeating the purpose of a fast, disposable pass meant to be thrown away or heavily revised.

### Pitch
The session where a storyboard artist (often the story supervisor, on animation) presents boards to the director or story team, walking through the panels in sequence with dialogue performed live, to get feedback before the sequence is locked.
**In use:** "We're pitching Sequence 8 Thursday — hold the tight pass until after notes."
**Common misuse:** Confused with a simple hand-off or file delivery; a pitch is a performed, interactive review, and boards presented without a pitch often miss problems that only surface when read aloud in order.

### Panel rate / day rate
The freelance or studio compensation and scheduling unit for boarding work — a per-panel fee or a daily rate — used to estimate both cost and schedule for a sequence.
**In use:** "At the comparable scene's logged rate, this sequence is 25 hours, not the 12 the flat day-rate average would suggest."
**Common misuse:** Applied as a flat studio-wide average across all scene types, instead of adjusted for the specific scene's setup density and arrow/impact-marker load.

### Previs (previsualization)
Rough 3D or motion-based visualization of a sequence's camera and blocking, typically produced after boards for complex VFX, stunt, or animation-heavy sequences, distinct from the 2D storyboard panels that precede it.
**In use:** "Board it first for staging and coverage, then hand it to previs for the camera move choreography on the flip."
**Common misuse:** Conflated with storyboarding itself; previs is a separate, usually later, department that works from the boards, not a synonym for the boarding pass.

### Boarding to the script vs. boarding to the mix
Two different production stages a sequence can be boarded against: an early pass boarded directly from the written script, versus a later pass boarded or revised against a locked edit, temp mix, or scratch audio.
**In use:** "These are boarding-to-script panels — expect another pass once we're boarding to the mix and the dialogue timing is locked."
**Common misuse:** Treated as the same fidelity and permanence, when a script-stage board is expected to change and a mix-stage board is close to final.

### Shot list
The production document listing every camera setup for a scene in shooting or animation order, with lens, movement, and framing notes — the document a live-action storyboard typically maps directly onto.
**In use:** "Number the panels to match the shot list IDs so the AD can find setup 14C without flipping through the whole board."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to be generated automatically from the boards; in practice the shot list and the boards are cross-referenced and kept in sync by hand, and drift between them causes on-set confusion.

### Revision pass (redline)
A marked round of changes to existing boards, tracked with a version number and the reviewer's notes, distinct from a full re-board from scratch.
**In use:** "That's redline pass 3 on Sequence 8 — check the version number before you pull panels for the animatic."
**Common misuse:** Left untracked or unversioned, so downstream departments (editorial, previs, animation layout) end up working from stale panels without knowing a newer pass exists.

### Impact marker
A panel annotation (starburst, motion streak, or similar shorthand) indicating the frame of a collision, hit, or other sudden event, used to communicate timing to a stunt coordinator, VFX supervisor, or animator without requiring a separate written note.
**In use:** "Put an impact marker on the sideswipe panel so the stunt team knows exactly which frame is the hit."
**Common misuse:** Omitted on the assumption the written shot description covers it, leaving the exact timing of the beat ambiguous to whoever executes the shot from the panel alone.
