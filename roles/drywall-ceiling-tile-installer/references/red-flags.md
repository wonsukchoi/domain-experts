# Red flags

Smell tests a lead installer catches walking a job before board goes up, or auditing one that's already closed.

## 1. Fastener spacing wider than 12" o.c. on a rated wall's field

**Usually means:** the crew hit production pace by loosening spacing on a wall nobody will visibly inspect until a fire or a complaint — the listed assembly's spacing was never confirmed against the actual UL design.

**First question:** "What UL design number is this wall, and what's its field/perimeter spacing?"

**Data to pull:** the plan sheet's assembly callout, the matching UL design's fastener table, and a spot count of actual screws per linear foot on an open section.

## 2. Board type or manufacturer swapped on a labeled fire-rated wall

**Usually means:** a substitution made to match what was in stock or cheaper, on the assumption that any Type X board "counts the same" toward the rating.

**First question:** "Does this specific UL design name a generic board type, or a proprietary board by model number?"

**Data to pull:** the UL design listing's exact wording on substitution, the board delivery ticket/manufacturer stamp actually installed, and whether an engineer's letter or evaluation report was issued for the swap.

## 3. No cavity insulation in a wall specified for STC 50

**Usually means:** the framing sub priced the fire rating but not the acoustic target, treating them as the same line item when they're two separate requirements.

**First question:** "What assembly is this STC-50 number coming from, and does it include cavity insulation?"

**Data to pull:** the lease or spec page citing the STC number, the assembly's tested configuration, and whether insulation was actually ordered and installed in that cavity.

## 4. Moisture-resistant ("green") board used as tile backer inside a tub or shower surround

**Usually means:** someone treated "moisture-resistant" as "waterproof," using standard MR board where cement board or foam backer is required by code and manufacturer instruction.

**First question:** "Is this surface inside the wet zone — direct spray or standing water contact — or just a humid room?"

**Data to pull:** the tile schedule's substrate callout, the board stamp on the installed material, and the manufacturer's install guide for the specific tile system being used.

## 5. No control joint on a wall or ceiling run over 30 linear feet

**Usually means:** the run was measured room-by-room on the plan rather than as one continuous span, hiding a long run inside what looks like two short walls.

**First question:** "Walk me the actual continuous length of this run, corner to corner — not the room dimensions."

**Data to pull:** an as-built measurement of the run, the framing drawings for any structural expansion joints it should align with, and the manufacturer's span table for that specific assembly.

## 6. Continuous top track or stud spanning both faces of a demising wall

**Usually means:** the framing crew ran one continuous top track across what's supposed to be an acoustically or structurally separate wall between two units, creating a rigid bridge that carries sound (and sometimes load) straight across.

**First question:** "Is there a structural or acoustic break required at this top track, and is it actually there?"

**Data to pull:** the wall section detail from the drawings, a field check of the top track for continuity, and any prior STC test results for this wall type.

## 7. Unsealed electrical box or penetration in an acoustically rated wall

**Usually means:** the box was set before the acoustic requirement was flagged, or the crew treated sealing as a finish-coat cosmetic step rather than a tested requirement.

**First question:** "Was this penetration sealed with the specified non-hardening acoustic sealant, or just caulked at finish?"

**Data to pull:** the acoustic assembly's penetration-detail spec, a visual/probe check behind the cover plate, and the sealant product actually used against the spec'd product.

## 8. More than roughly 5% of visible fasteners show broken paper face or crater from overdriving

**Usually means:** screw-gun clutch depth is set too aggressive for the crew's pace, or an inexperienced installer is running production speed without dialing in depth per board thickness.

**First question:** "When was the clutch depth last checked against this board thickness?"

**Data to pull:** a fastener sample count across a representative section, and whether the crew is running the same gun setting across different board thicknesses on the same job.

## 9. Suspended ceiling grid with no seismic clips or compression posts in a high seismic design category

**Usually means:** the grid was installed to a standard gravity-only detail without checking the project's seismic design category, often because the ceiling package was subcontracted separately from the structural review.

**First question:** "What's this project's seismic design category, and does the grid detail match CISCA/ASTM E580 for it?"

**Data to pull:** the structural drawings' seismic design category callout, the ceiling grid submittal's bracing detail, and a field check of wall-angle attachment and compression-post spacing.

## 10. Double-layer board applied with joints aligned, not staggered

**Usually means:** the crew treated the second layer as extra mass without registering that stagger is part of what makes the assembly's tested rating and acoustic performance apply — aligned joints create a straight-through weak seam in both fire and sound terms.

**First question:** "Are the face-layer joints offset from the base-layer joints by the assembly's specified distance?"

**Data to pull:** the assembly's stagger requirement (commonly 16" or one full stud bay), and a field measurement of actual joint offset on an open section.
