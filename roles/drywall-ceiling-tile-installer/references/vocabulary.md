# Vocabulary

Terms generalists conflate that installers keep sharply separate — each with the misuse that actually causes rework or a failed inspection.

## Type X vs. Type C gypsum board

**Type X** board has glass-fiber reinforcement giving it roughly a 1-hour fire-resistance contribution at 5/8" thickness in a tested assembly. **Type C** board has additional glass fiber and vermiculite that keep it structurally intact longer under fire exposure, used where a design calls for it specifically (often steel-stud/ceiling membrane assemblies needing extra shrinkage resistance).

**In use:** "The ceiling membrane calls for Type C specifically — Type X will meet the hour rating on paper but isn't what this design was tested with."

**Common misuse:** treating "Type X" as synonymous with "any fire-rated board," and substituting Type C or vice versa because both show up as "fire-rated" on a supply house shelf tag.

## UL design number / listed assembly

A **UL design number** identifies one specific, laboratory-tested combination of framing, board, insulation, and fasteners as a single rated unit — not a rating that transfers to any wall built with similarly-rated components.

**In use:** "Pull the actual U411 sheet before you order board — don't just quote '1-hour, 5/8 Type X' from memory."

**Common misuse:** citing the fire-resistance hour rating alone ("it's a 1-hour wall") as if that describes the assembly, when the hour rating only applies to the exact tested combination behind the design number.

## STC vs. NRC

**STC** (sound transmission class) measures how much a wall or ceiling assembly blocks sound from passing *through* it. **NRC** (noise reduction coefficient) measures how much a surface (typically an acoustic ceiling tile) *absorbs* sound within the same room rather than blocking transmission to the next room.

**In use:** "A high-NRC ceiling tile will quiet the room, but it does nothing for the STC-50 requirement between these two suites — that's a wall assembly problem, not a tile problem."

**Common misuse:** specifying a high-NRC ceiling tile to solve a between-room sound-transmission complaint; NRC and STC address different acoustic problems entirely.

## Resilient channel vs. sound isolation clip

**Resilient channel (RC-1)** is a formed metal furring channel that decouples the board from the framing on one axis, installed horizontally. **Sound isolation clips** decouple in more directions and generally test higher, at greater cost and thickness build-up, and are specified where the STC target exceeds what channel alone reaches.

**In use:** "We're only after STC 50 on this one — resilient channel gets us there; save the clips for the recording-room wall."

**Common misuse:** installing resilient channel upside down or fastening through it into the framing (common jobsite error), which eliminates the decoupling and drops the assembly back to roughly its non-RC STC performance.

## Moisture-resistant board vs. cement/backer board

**Moisture-resistant board** ("green board" or "MR board") has a treated paper facing that tolerates ambient humidity — bathrooms, laundry rooms — but is not a waterproof substrate. **Cement board** and **foam backer board** are the substrates rated for direct water contact behind tile in tubs, showers, and steam enclosures.

**In use:** "Green board's fine for the powder room walls, but the shower surround is cement board — no exceptions."

**Common misuse:** calling any board with a green or blue-tinted facing "waterproof" and using it as tile backer inside the actual wet zone, where code and manufacturer instructions require cement or foam board instead.

## Control joint vs. expansion joint

A **control joint** accommodates minor, ongoing dimensional movement in a continuous run of board (thermal/humidity cycling) at a location the installer chooses within a span rule. An **expansion joint** is a structural feature already designed into the building (at a seismic or thermal break) that the drywall detail must simply not bridge.

**In use:** "The building's expansion joint at column line 4 already breaks the framing — our control joint on the drywall just needs to land on that same line, not somewhere else on the run."

**Common misuse:** treating the two as interchangeable, or placing a control joint anywhere convenient on a long run instead of at the actual structural break when one exists.

## Field spacing vs. perimeter spacing

**Field spacing** is the fastener interval across the open middle of a board. **Perimeter (edge) spacing** is the tighter interval required along the board's edges, where fire and acoustic assemblies most often specify a different number than the field.

**In use:** "Perimeter's 8 inches on this one, field's 12 — don't run the whole sheet at the field spacing to save fasteners."

**Common misuse:** applying one spacing number to an entire sheet, which under-fastens the edges exactly where a rated assembly's test data assumed tighter attachment.

## Butt joint vs. tapered edge

A **tapered edge** is the factory-recessed long edge of a gypsum board sheet, designed to receive tape and compound flush with the surrounding surface. A **butt joint** is the untapered short end of a sheet meeting another sheet's end, which has no recess and is the harder joint to finish flat.

**In use:** "Stagger the butt joints between courses — two butt joints stacked in the same stud bay is what telegraphs as a visible ridge later."

**Common misuse:** treating all joints as equivalent finishing work; butt joints need a wider, feathered compound spread (and sometimes a control-joint-style relief) to avoid a visible bump that tapered edges don't have.

## Demising wall vs. partition wall

A **demising wall** separates two independently leased or owned spaces (tenant-to-tenant, unit-to-unit) and typically carries both a fire-rating and an acoustic requirement tied to the lease. A **partition wall** subdivides space within a single tenant's or owner's area and, absent a specific code trigger, usually carries neither requirement.

**In use:** "This one's a demising wall between two tenants — pull the lease's STC clause before anyone frames it, not just the fire schedule."

**Common misuse:** applying partition-wall assumptions (standard board, standard stud spacing, no insulation) to a wall that's actually a demising wall because it "looks like just another interior wall" on the floor plan.

## Level 5 finish vs. Level 4 finish

**Level 4** finish (two coats of compound over tape and fasteners, standard for most flat/eggshell paint) is the default drywall finish spec. **Level 5** finish adds a full skim coat over the entire surface, required where critical lighting or a high-gloss paint would otherwise telegraph every joint and fastener as a visible flaw.

**In use:** "Client's putting wall-wash lighting down that corridor — spec Level 5 now, because going back to skim-coat after paint is a full redo."

**Common misuse:** assuming Level 4 is "good enough" finish on any job, without checking the lighting design or paint sheen the wall will actually receive — the finish level that looks fine under normal light can show every joint under raking wall-wash light.
