# Red flags

Smell tests a senior boilermaker catches during a job walk-down, weld inspection, or schedule review. Format for each: what it usually means, the first question to ask, and the data to pull before committing to a plan.

## 1. Rolled-joint leak recurring within 6 months of the last reroll at the same tube row

**Usually means:** the reroll was under-rolled below the 4% wall-reduction target, or sister tubes from the same retube batch share the same install defect — one confirmed under-roll is a strong signal the whole row needs a wall-gauge check.

**First question:** "What was the measured wall reduction on the failed joint, and was it checked on the adjacent tubes from the same retube?"

**Data to pull:** wall-gauge or drift-pin measurements from the failed joint and at least three adjacent joints; the original retube job traveler recording target vs. achieved expansion.

## 2. Weld assigned to a welder whose continuity log shows no use of that process/material combination in over 6 months

**Usually means:** the WPQ is technically on file but the welder's actual skill currency for this specific combination has lapsed — a qualification on paper is not the same as a qualification in practice at this interval.

**First question:** "When did this welder last run this process on this material and thickness, and is there a supervised continuity weld on record since?"

**Data to pull:** the welder's continuity log; the WPQ record's covered process/material/thickness/position range; any recent test coupon results if re-qualification was performed.

## 3. Repair proceeding under an "R" scope that doesn't list alterations, on a job that changes tube wall thickness or MAWP

**Usually means:** the crew is treating a design-basis change as routine repair work because the workmanship is within skill, without checking whether the Certificate of Authorization on file actually covers alterations.

**First question:** "Does this job restore the component to its original design condition, or does it change thickness, pressure rating, or configuration from the original data report?"

**Data to pull:** the original manufacturer's data report for the component; the current "R" Certificate of Authorization's stated scope (repairs only, or repairs and alterations).

## 4. Radiographic film shows a cluster of rounded indications concentrated in one section of an otherwise clean weld

**Usually means:** localized porosity or slag inclusion from a process upset during that pass (contamination, gas coverage loss, or a paused-and-restarted weld) rather than a uniform process issue — averaging against the rest of the weld's clean reading is the wrong read.

**First question:** "What's the aggregate length of indications in that cluster against the acceptance table for this weld thickness, evaluated over its own length, not the whole weld's average?"

**Data to pull:** the RT film and reader's report for that specific section; the applicable Code Section's acceptance-criteria table for the actual weld thickness.

## 5. Hydro test hold time cut short of the required minutes to hit a restart schedule

**Usually means:** schedule pressure compressed the inspection window the hold time exists to provide — a joint that would weep at minute 25 of a 30-minute hold reads as a pass if the test is stopped at minute 15.

**First question:** "What's the required hold time for this test pressure and repair type, and who approved cutting it short?"

**Data to pull:** the hydro test log with actual elapsed hold time; the applicable Code Section or NBIC provision's minimum hold time for this repair type.

## 6. PWHT (post-weld heat treatment) skipped on a repair weld above the thickness/P-number threshold requiring it

**Usually means:** schedule or equipment-availability pressure treated a Code-required stress relief as optional — the omission doesn't show up as a defect immediately, it raises cracking risk that surfaces later in service.

**First question:** "Does this material thickness and P-number require PWHT under the applicable Code Section, and if so, where's the time-temperature record for it?"

**Data to pull:** the WPS's PWHT requirement field; the actual heat-treatment chart/log if PWHT was performed; the material thickness and P-number of the repaired section.

## 7. Tube pulled and reinstalled without a fresh drift-pin check on the reinstalled joint

**Usually means:** the crew treated tube replacement as a fit-and-roll job without re-verifying the expansion on the actual reinstalled tube, assuming the same roller settings that worked on the last tube will produce the same result — wall thickness and hole tolerance vary tube to tube.

**First question:** "What's the measured wall reduction on this specific reinstalled tube, not the setting used on the previous one?"

**Data to pull:** the drift-pin or ID gauge reading for this specific joint; the tube's actual measured original wall thickness before rolling.

## 8. Second tube failure at the same drum ligament position within one operating cycle

**Usually means:** the root cause (chronic thinning, local chemistry, thermal stress concentration at that ligament) was never addressed by the first repair — replacing the tube again with the same spec repeats the same failure.

**First question:** "What did the metallurgical or wall-thickness analysis show as the cause of the first failure, and did this repair change anything based on that finding?"

**Data to pull:** the first failure's root-cause note; wall-thickness trend data at that specific ligament position across the last several inspections; water chemistry logs if corrosion is suspected.

## 9. "R" Certificate of Authorization expired or lapsed at the time a repair was performed

**Usually means:** an administrative renewal was missed — the repair itself may be sound, but it was performed without valid legal authorization, which the Authorized Inspector cannot sign off as a Code repair.

**First question:** "What's the Certificate of Authorization's expiration date, and was it current on the date this repair was performed?"

**Data to pull:** the Certificate of Authorization renewal record from the National Board; the repair's actual work-performed date from the job traveler.

## 10. Boiler returned to service before the National Board "R" report was filed and signed

**Usually means:** schedule pressure pushed restart ahead of paperwork completion — the repair may be physically sound but isn't yet a documented Code repair until the Authorized Inspector's sign-off is on file.

**First question:** "Is the R report signed and filed, or is the unit running on the expectation that it will be filed after the fact?"

**Data to pull:** the R report's signature and filing date against the unit's actual restart date and time.

## 11. Torque or turn-count used as the sole basis for stopping a tube-rolling pass

**Usually means:** the crew is relying on a proxy (expander clutch torque) instead of the actual measurement (ID gauge or drift pin) — torque response varies with tube hardness, lubrication, and expander wear, so it drifts from the true wall-reduction percentage over time.

**First question:** "What did the drift pin or ID gauge read on this joint, independent of the torque setting used?"

**Data to pull:** the direct ID/wall measurement for the joint in question; the expander's calibration record if torque was used as a cross-check.

## 12. Weld repair performed using a WPS qualified for a different material P-number or thickness range than the actual repair

**Usually means:** a "close enough" WPS was substituted under time pressure without confirming it covers the base metal group and thickness actually in front of the welder — a procedure qualified sound for one material group doesn't transfer its qualification to another.

**First question:** "Does the WPS on the job traveler list this exact base-metal P-number and thickness range, or was it selected because it was the closest one available?"

**Data to pull:** the WPS's stated P-number and thickness range; the actual base metal certification/mill test report for the component being repaired.
