# Mobile Heavy Equipment Mechanic — Vocabulary

### Case drain flow
The flow of hydraulic fluid leaking internally past a pump or motor's rotating group back to its case/housing, measured separately from the pump's rated output flow as a direct indicator of internal wear.
**In use:** "Case drain's reading 12% of rated flow — that pump's internally worn, replace it, don't just re-torque the fittings."
**Common misuse:** Skipping the case-drain test and calling a pump good or bad based on relief pressure alone, which misses pumps that hold full pressure while losing significant flow.

### Volumetric efficiency
The ratio of a pump or motor's actual delivered flow to its theoretical displacement at a given speed and pressure; it drops as internal clearances wear, independent of whether output pressure still tests normal.
**In use:** "Pressure's fine, but volumetric efficiency is down — that's why the cylinder's slow even though the gauge looks normal."
**Common misuse:** Treating a normal pressure reading as proof the pump is fine, when pressure and volumetric efficiency are different measurements that can diverge.

### Cylinder drift
The rate a hydraulic cylinder retracts or extends under load while the control valve is held in neutral, used to isolate an internal seal bypass from a valve or downstream leak.
**In use:** "Blocked the hose and it still dropped 3 inches a minute — that's the cylinder seal, not the valve."
**Common misuse:** Testing drift with the valve in neutral but not isolating the cylinder from the rest of the circuit, which can't distinguish a cylinder seal problem from a valve spool leak.

### ISO 4406 cleanliness code
A three-number code (e.g., 18/16/13) describing the particle count of a hydraulic fluid sample at three size ranges, used to set and verify a target cleanliness level per component sensitivity.
**In use:** "This circuit's got a proportional valve — target is 18/16/13, and the sample came back 21/19/17, so we're filtering before button-up."
**Common misuse:** Treating "the fluid looks clean" or "we changed the filter" as equivalent to a verified ISO code, when only a particle-count sample confirms the actual cleanliness level.

### Chain pitch elongation
The stretch in a track chain's link pitch as pins and bushings wear, measured as a percentage increase over new-chain pitch and used as the primary undercarriage wear metric.
**In use:** "Pitch is at 2.8% — we've got maybe one more PM cycle before this crosses the 3% replace line."
**Common misuse:** Judging chain wear by looking at the track sag or visual slack instead of measuring pitch elongation directly, which can miss wear that hasn't yet loosened visible sag.

### Hook wear
A sprocket tooth wear pattern where the tooth profile curls ("hooks") from prolonged engagement with an over-elongated chain, distinct from simple uniform tooth-tip wear.
**In use:** "That sprocket's already hooked — the chain was run way past its own wear limit before anyone measured it."
**Common misuse:** Treating any worn sprocket tooth as routine wear-and-tear, when a hooked profile specifically indicates the chain should have been replaced earlier and is now damaging the sprocket.

### Matched-set replacement
Replacing chain, bushings, and sprocket together rather than individually, because installing one new component against other already-worn mating components accelerates wear on the new part.
**In use:** "Chain's at 3% and the sprocket's hooking — this is a matched-set replacement, not just a chain swap."
**Common misuse:** Replacing only the most visibly worn component to save cost, then finding the new part wears out well ahead of its rated life because it's meshing against already-worn mates.

### Percent worn
An undercarriage measurement convention (e.g., Caterpillar's Undercarriage Management percent-worn scale) expressing pin, bushing, sprocket, and shoe wear as a percentage of the component's total wearable material, used to plan replacement timing across brands.
**In use:** "Undercarriage report says we're at 62% worn on the bushings — that's in the replacement window, not an emergency, not something to ignore either."
**Common misuse:** Reading percent-worn charts calibrated for normal ground conditions and applying them unadjusted to an abrasive site, where the same percentage arrives on far fewer hours.

### Break-even distance
The travel distance or cost point at which dispatching a mobile mechanic to a job site costs the same as towing the machine to the shop, used to decide field-repair vs haul on a per-incident basis rather than by fixed policy.
**In use:** "At 35 miles with the part on the truck, we're well under break-even for hauling — fix it here."
**Common misuse:** Applying a single fixed mileage rule company-wide instead of recalculating for each incident's actual idle-crew rate, parts availability, and repair scope.

### Idle-crew cost
The fully loaded cost of a crew and adjacent equipment standing unproductive while a machine is down, distinct from the machine's own owning/operating cost and usually the dominant number in an on-site-vs-haul comparison.
**In use:** "The pump itself is cheap, but idle-crew cost on this paving crew is $600 an hour — that's what actually drives the repair-location call."
**Common misuse:** Comparing repair options on parts and labor price alone, without pricing the cost of the crew sitting idle while the decision gets made.

### Structural repair procedure
An OEM-engineered process (specific weld prep, filler material, post-weld stress relief, or reinforcement plate) for repairing a crack in a load-bearing structural member, as opposed to a generic field weld.
**In use:** "That's a main-frame crack — it gets the OEM structural repair procedure, not a shop weld and a coat of paint."
**Common misuse:** Treating any crack repair as a routine weld job, when load-bearing structural members have specific OEM procedures that a generic weld can miss or even weaken further.

### Stop-work item
A defect (typically structural or brake/steering-related) severe enough that the machine must not operate until repaired, regardless of production schedule or downtime cost.
**In use:** "That's a stop-work item — I don't care what the schedule says, it's not moving until the crack's assessed."
**Common misuse:** Treating a stop-work call as negotiable against the job schedule, when it's meant to override the downtime-cost comparison entirely.
