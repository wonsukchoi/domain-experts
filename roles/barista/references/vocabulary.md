# Vocabulary

Terms generalists use loosely that practitioners keep sharply separated. Each: definition, a sentence a practitioner would actually say, and the common misuse.

### Extraction yield (EY%) vs. TDS%

**Extraction yield** is the percentage of the dry coffee's mass that ended up dissolved in the cup. **TDS** (total dissolved solids) is the concentration of dissolved solids in the liquid itself. EY = TDS% × (yield grams ÷ dose grams).

**In use:** "TDS is fine at 9%, but EY's only 16% — the ratio's too tight for this bean, not the shot too weak."

**Common misuse:** assuming "tastes weak" always means under-extracted (it can mean correctly extracted but over-diluted, i.e., low TDS at normal EY) or the reverse.

### Ristretto vs. normale vs. lungo

Ratio/time variants of the same shot: **ristretto** roughly 1:1–1:1.5 in ~15–20s, **normale** roughly 1:2 in 25–30s, **lungo** 1:3 or beyond, 35s+.

**In use:** "This origin's delicate — pull it lungo instead of masking it ristretto."

**Common misuse:** calling any small-volume shot a "ristretto" regardless of its actual dose:yield ratio, or treating lungo as simply "more shots stacked" rather than one shot pulled to a longer ratio.

### Channeling

Water breaking through a low-resistance path in the coffee puck instead of flowing evenly through it, caused by uneven distribution, undissolved clumps, or a cracked puck face — not by tamp pressure.

**In use:** "That fast, sour pull with the pale streak down one side is channeling, not a grind problem."

**Common misuse:** reflexively regrinding finer when the actual fix is redistributing the dose (WDT) before tamping.

### Dial-in

The ongoing process of adjusting grind size, and sometimes dose, until a shot's ratio, time, and taste land inside the target window for a specific bag on a specific day.

**In use:** "We're not dialed in yet on this bag — give me two more test shots before we open."

**Common misuse:** treating dial-in as a one-time setup done once per bag rather than a check repeated through the shift as the bag ages.

### Microfoam vs. foam

**Microfoam** is steamed milk textured to sub-millimeter, barely visible bubbles suitable for latte art and silky mouthfeel. **Foam** (cappuccino-style) is coarser, visibly bubbly, and deliberately drier.

**In use:** "Stretch less next time — that's foam, not microfoam."

**Common misuse:** using "microfoam" as a catch-all for any steamed/foamed milk rather than a specific bubble-size and texture target that differs from cappuccino foam on purpose.

### Stretching vs. texturing

**Stretching** is the early steaming phase (below ~100°F/38°C) where air is introduced to build foam volume. **Texturing** is the later phase where the pitcher is submerged deeper to fold existing foam smaller without adding more air.

**In use:** "You kept stretching too long — stop adding air past the first few seconds and just texture from there."

**Common misuse:** continuing to introduce air through the entire steam, which produces coarse foam instead of silky microfoam regardless of final temperature.

### WDT (Weiss Distribution Technique)

Stirring dosed grounds in the portafilter basket with fine pins or a needle tool before tamping, to break up clumps that would otherwise channel.

**In use:** "WDT it before you tamp — don't just tap the basket on the counter and call it distributed."

**Common misuse:** treating tapping or knocking the portafilter as an equivalent substitute; it settles grounds but doesn't break the clumps WDT is built to eliminate.

### Golden Cup vs. golden ratio

**Golden Cup** is the SCA's filter-coffee standard: 18–22% extraction yield at roughly 1.15–1.45% TDS. **Golden ratio** is the colloquial 1:2 espresso dose:yield convention — a different standard for a different brew method, not a restatement of the same number.

**In use:** "We brew filter to Golden Cup, but the espresso ratio's house style, it's not an SCA standard the way Golden Cup is."

**Common misuse:** quoting SCA's filter-coffee TDS range as if it applied to espresso, where TDS runs far higher (roughly 8–12%) at a comparable extraction yield because the brew ratio is much more concentrated.

### Dose vs. yield vs. ratio

**Dose** is the dry coffee grams loaded into the basket. **Yield** is the liquid grams that come out. **Ratio** is yield ÷ dose.

**In use:** "Dose is fine at 18g — it's the yield that's off, we're pulling 42g instead of 36g."

**Common misuse:** describing yield alone as "the shot size" with no reference to dose, which hides whether a bigger pour is a deliberately longer recipe or an accidentally over-extracted, diluted shot.

### Crema

The reddish-brown foam layer on espresso formed from emulsified CO2 and oils; its thickness and persistence signal freshness and brew pressure, not directly the shot's taste balance.

**In use:** "Crema looks great — doesn't mean the shot underneath isn't sour."

**Common misuse:** judging shot quality by crema appearance alone instead of tasting, especially with very fresh beans that produce heavy crema even on a badly channeled pull.

### Third-wave vs. specialty coffee

**Specialty** is the SCA's quality designation — green and roasted coffee scoring 80+ on a 100-point cupping scale. **Third-wave** describes a retail movement and aesthetic (single-origin focus, manual brew methods, sourcing transparency) that usually, but not always, uses specialty-grade beans.

**In use:** "Being a third-wave shop doesn't guarantee every bean on that shelf actually scored specialty."

**Common misuse:** using the two terms interchangeably as if "third-wave" were itself a quality guarantee rather than a style descriptor.

### Burr wear and alignment

Grinder burrs dulling or shifting out of alignment over tens of thousands of shots, which widens the particle-size distribution in ways no grind-setting adjustment can fully correct.

**In use:** "We've chased this shot for twenty minutes on the grinder — check burr alignment, this might not be a dial-in problem at all."

**Common misuse:** assuming every persistent inconsistency is a technique or bean issue when equipment wear is the actual cause, especially on a grinder overdue for burr replacement.

### Pre-infusion

A low-pressure soak phase before the machine ramps to full brew pressure, letting the puck saturate evenly and reducing channeling risk, particularly on inconsistent doses.

**In use:** "Turn on four seconds of pre-infusion before ramping to full pressure on this machine."

**Common misuse:** assuming pre-infusion substitutes for good distribution — it lowers channeling risk but doesn't replace WDT or consistent dosing as the primary fix.
