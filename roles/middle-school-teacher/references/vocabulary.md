# Vocabulary

Terms of art in middle-level education that generalists flatten or conflate. Each: definition, how a practitioner actually uses it, and the common misuse.

## Interdisciplinary teaming vs. departmentalization

**Teaming** is a structure where a fixed group of subject teachers (typically 3–5) shares the same roster of ~100–125 students and a common planning period specifically to coordinate. **Departmentalization** just means each teacher teaches one subject to rotating groups of students — high schools are departmentalized without necessarily teaming.

**In use:** "We're departmentalized like the high school, but teaming is what actually lets us catch a global pattern before it becomes a crisis — without the common planning time, we'd never compare notes."

**Common misuse:** assuming any schedule where students rotate between subject teachers is "teaming." Without a shared roster and protected common planning time, it's just departmentalization — no cross-subject comparison is actually happening.

## Advisory vs. homeroom

**Advisory** is a scheduled block with an explicit curriculum — organizational skills, goal-setting, SEL topics — delivered by a teacher who also monitors that small group's overall wellbeing. **Homeroom** is purely administrative: attendance, announcements, locker assignment.

**In use:** "Advisory this week is the planner audit lesson — it's not homeroom, there's an actual objective on the board."

**Common misuse:** scheduling advisory time and never planning it, which collapses it into homeroom (or worse, unstructured social time) by default.

## Formative vs. summative assessment

**Formative** assessment checks progress during learning (exit tickets, daily homework, practice sets) and is meant to inform instruction, not certify mastery. **Summative** assessment certifies what was learned at the end of a unit (tests, projects, performance tasks) and is what the mastery grade should be built from.

**In use:** "That zero was on a formative exit ticket — it goes in the completion tracker, not the mastery average."

**Common misuse:** averaging formative scores directly into the same grade column as summative ones, so a practice-stage zero distorts a mastery number it was never designed to represent.

## Standards-based grading vs. traditional point-based grading

**Standards-based grading** reports mastery per specific skill/standard (e.g., "3 of 4" on "solves multi-step equations"), independent of when in the unit it was demonstrated. **Traditional point-based grading** averages all scored work chronologically into one cumulative percentage, regardless of whether early scores reflect the same skill level as later ones.

**In use:** "On the standards report, M. is a 3 on multi-step equations — the two early formative misses don't drag that down once mastery was demonstrated on the summative."

**Common misuse:** treating a traditional average as if it already reflects current mastery, when it's actually weighted by when in the unit each score happened, not by what the student can do now.

## Common planning time

A protected, scheduled period (often 40–50 minutes daily or several times a week) during which a team's teachers meet — distinct from individual prep time, which is for one teacher's own grading/planning.

**In use:** "That's a common-planning conversation, not an email — I need the whole team's data in front of us at once."

**Common misuse:** using common planning time for logistics only (field trip forms, schedule swaps) and never for the data-comparison work it exists to protect, letting the actual diagnostic function of teaming go unused.

## Flexible block scheduling

A master-schedule structure that lets a team borrow and rearrange time across its own subjects and periods (e.g., extending Math by 20 minutes one day by shortening Science, with the team's agreement) rather than a fixed uniform bell schedule.

**In use:** "We flexed the block Thursday — Science gave Math 20 extra minutes for the unit test makeup, we'll return it Friday."

**Common misuse:** assuming block scheduling means uniformly longer periods for every class; the flexibility is the point, not the length.

## Looping

A team or teacher stays with the same cohort of students across multiple years (e.g., 6th into 7th grade) instead of receiving a new roster each year.

**In use:** "Because we looped with this cohort, I already know M.'s executive-function pattern from last year — the makeup-scheduling gap this quarter was new, not a repeat."

**Common misuse:** treating looping as automatically better; it also means a teacher's early misread of a student compounds for another year if not actively re-examined.

## Differentiation vs. accommodation vs. modification

**Differentiation** varies instructional method or process for the same learning goal (e.g., choice of format for showing understanding). **Accommodation** changes how a student accesses or demonstrates content without changing the expectation itself (extended time, read-aloud). **Modification** changes the expectation or content itself (fewer objectives, lower complexity).

**In use:** "The extended time is an accommodation — the assignment target didn't change. If we cut the number of skills assessed, that's a modification, and it needs to go through the IEP team, not just me."

**Common misuse:** calling any adjustment "differentiation," which blurs a teacher's instructional choice with a legally documented accommodation or modification that requires a paper trail.

## Adolescent egocentrism / imaginary audience

A developmental pattern (Elkind) where early adolescents overestimate how much peers are watching and judging them, making a public correction or public wrong answer carry a disproportionate social cost relative to the same event for a younger or older student.

**In use:** "That correction needs to happen at her desk, not from the front of the room — at this age the imaginary audience makes a public correction cost far more than the content mistake itself."

**Common misuse:** dismissing this as "kids being dramatic" rather than a predictable developmental stage that changes which classroom-management tactics actually work.

## The zero problem / 100-point-scale distortion

The mathematical effect where, on a 0–100 scale, a single zero folded into an average with otherwise-high scores produces a disproportionately large drop compared to the same performance pattern on a 4-point or standards-based scale.

**In use:** "Three A's and a zero average to a D on the 100-point scale — that's the zero problem, not a mastery signal."

**Common misuse:** treating the fix as "never give a zero," which erases legitimate accountability for genuine non-attempts, instead of fixing the underlying issue — whether the assignment belonged in the mastery average at all.

## Redo/retake policy

A stated school or team policy governing whether and how a student can resubmit or retest after an initial low or missing summative score, including any cap on the recorded score and eligibility conditions (good-faith first attempt required).

**In use:** "Policy caps a retake at 75% — that's what goes in the book, not the raw 80% he actually earned on the redo."

**Common misuse:** applying redo privileges ad hoc, teacher by teacher, instead of a documented team or school policy — which turns a fairness mechanism into a source of perceived favoritism.

## MTSS (Multi-Tiered System of Supports) at the middle level

A tiered framework (universal instruction, targeted small-group intervention, intensive individual support) for academic or behavioral needs, re-scoped at the middle level to run through the team structure rather than a single classroom teacher.

**In use:** "This is a Tier 2 case — the team is documenting the targeted intervention and the recheck date together, not just one teacher's classroom accommodation."

**Common misuse:** running MTSS as if it were still elementary's single-teacher process, without looping in the rest of the team that actually holds the cross-subject data needed to track it.

## Grade floor / minimum-grade policy

A school policy setting the lowest score a zero can be recorded as (commonly 50) specifically to blunt — not eliminate — the 100-point-scale distortion.

**In use:** "The floor gets us from a 435-point collapse to something closer to accurate, but it's still not the same as the student actually completing the makeup."

**Common misuse:** treating a grade floor as equivalent to fixing the underlying missing-work issue, when it's a partial mitigation of the scale's math, not a substitute for the makeup itself.
