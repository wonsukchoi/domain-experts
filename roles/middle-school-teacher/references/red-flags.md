# Red flags

Smell tests a middle-level team should catch inside one common-planning meeting. Format: what it usually means, the first question to ask, and the data to pull before anyone decides.

## 1. Grade or behavior drop in exactly one class, four-plus points different from the other classes that same week

**Usually means:** a classroom-specific factor — seating, a relationship friction point, the period's time of day, a recent schedule change — not a home or global issue.

**First question:** "What does this look like in the other four classes this same week?"

**Data to pull:** cross-team grade comparison for the same seven-day window, attendance for that specific period, seating-chart or recent-change log for that class.

## 2. A quarter average drops 10+ points below the assignment-level average, driven by one or two zeros

**Usually means:** a missing/late-work policy is being conflated with a mastery measurement — the zeros are compliance data wearing a mastery-grade costume.

**First question:** "Were these formative or summative assignments, and is the zero a true assessed score or an unresolved missing-work placeholder?"

**Data to pull:** assignment-category breakdown (formative vs. summative), completion tracker entry for each zero, retake/makeup eligibility and scheduling history.

## 3. Advisory consistently described by students (or a substitute) as "the class where nothing happens"

**Usually means:** advisory has drifted into unstructured free time or homework time instead of the organizational/SEL curriculum it exists to deliver.

**First question:** "What's this week's advisory objective, on the board, and did the planned activity actually run?"

**Data to pull:** advisory scope-and-sequence, lesson-completion log across all advisors on the team, student exit-survey responses about advisory.

## 4. Disengagement starting almost exactly at the elementary-to-middle or 6th-to-7th transition

**Usually means:** the jump from one teacher tracking everything to 4–6 teachers each tracking a slice has outpaced the student's organizational system — an executive-function gap, not a motivation or ability problem.

**First question:** "Can this student tell you, unprompted, what's due tomorrow across all their classes right now?"

**Data to pull:** planner/agenda audit, missing-assignment count broken out by subject, family report of the home homework routine.

## 5. Team meeting where each teacher names a different root cause for the same student

**Usually means:** nobody has actually compared the same week's data side by side yet — only anecdote has been exchanged.

**First question:** "Let's each pull this student's data for the same seven days right now — what do we actually see?"

**Data to pull:** attendance, grades, discipline referrals, and any nurse/counselor visits for that student across the full team for the identical week.

## 6. Discipline referrals cluster on one day of the week or one period

**Usually means:** a schedule-level cause — right after lunch, last period on a specific day, a recurring substitute-coverage gap — not a fixed student characteristic.

**First question:** "Pull referral timestamps for the last six weeks — is there a day or period cluster?"

**Data to pull:** referral log with date/period stamps, seating chart for that period, substitute-coverage calendar cross-referenced against the cluster dates.

## 7. Family reports the student engages with the subject at home, but classroom performance disagrees

**Usually means:** the assessment format itself (timed test, heavy reading load, written-only response) is a barrier unrelated to the content knowledge being measured.

**First question:** "How does this student perform on the same content presented in a different format — oral, project-based, untimed?"

**Data to pull:** performance across assessment formats for the same content, any active IEP/504 accommodations, work samples in multiple formats.

## 8. A single long-term project (3+ weeks out, one end date) tanks the grade despite strong daily formative scores

**Usually means:** a task-initiation/chunking gap, not a content gap — the project tested unaided multi-week planning, a skill that was likely never explicitly taught.

**First question:** "Was this project ever broken into checkpoint deadlines, or was there one due date at the end?"

**Data to pull:** checkpoint-completion record (if any existed), planner entries in the weeks leading up to the due date, comparison against the student's daily formative average.

## 9. Two or three students dominate every class discussion; the rest stay silent even on content they clearly know from exit tickets

**Usually means:** the room's social hierarchy — status-conscious early adolescents avoiding a public wrong answer — is suppressing participation, not a comprehension gap.

**First question:** "What's the cold-call or no-hands-up structure in this class, and is there a low-stakes way to check understanding without a public answer?"

**Data to pull:** participation log by student, gap between verbal participation and exit-ticket accuracy for the quiet students.

## 10. Grades or behavior shift sharply right after a mid-year schedule change (new team, new locker bay, lunch-period move)

**Usually means:** disruption to routine and peer proximity in a population that leans heavily on both — an adjustment reaction, not a new academic problem.

**First question:** "When exactly did this start, relative to the schedule change date?"

**Data to pull:** date-stamped grade and behavior trend line plotted against the exact schedule-change date.
