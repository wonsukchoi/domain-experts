# Playbook

Filled operational templates a middle-level teacher and team actually run — adapt the numbers, keep the structure.

## 1. Common planning team meeting (weekly, 40–50 min)

**Standing agenda — data-first, not anecdote-first:**

```
0:00–0:05  Logistics (schedule changes, sub coverage, field trips)
0:05–0:25  Flagged-student data review (see table below)
0:25–0:35  Classification + action assignment per flagged student
0:35–0:45  Interdisciplinary unit / advisory planning check-in
0:45–0:50  Parking lot for next week
```

**Flagged-student data table (filled example, team of 5, week of March 14):**

| Student | ELA | Math | Science | Soc. Studies | Exploratory | Attendance | Referral? |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| M. | 87% | 72.5%* | 91% | 84% | 90% | 3 days excused | none |
| J. | 61% | 58% | 64% | 55% | 70% | full | 1 (Tues, 6th period) |

\* flagged by automated <75% alert.

**Reading the table:** M. is subject-specific (one low cell, four steady) — route to the Math teacher first, no team-wide plan needed yet. J. is global (every cell down) — convene a full team discussion this meeting, not a single-teacher fix.

**Classification → action mapping:**

| Classification | Signal | First action | Owner |
|---|---|---|---|
| Subject-specific | 1 of 5 cells low | Instructional fix with that teacher (seating, format, relationship) | Subject teacher |
| Global | 4–5 of 5 cells low | Team meets this week; one shared family message | Team lead |
| Schedule-linked | Referrals/grades cluster on one day/period | Check transition routine, seating, sub-coverage calendar | Whoever owns that period |

## 2. Gradebook: category weighting (converts the zero problem into a structural fix)

**Example weighting (adjust per school policy):**

| Category | Weight | Includes | Zero policy |
|---|---|---|---|
| Formative/practice | 20% | Homework, exit tickets, daily checks | Missing → logged in completion tracker, excluded from average until made up; never averaged in as 0 |
| Summative/mastery | 70% | Unit tests, projects, performance tasks | True zero only if genuinely unassessed after all makeup options exhausted |
| Participation/completion | 10% | Planner checks, materials-ready checks | Tracks compliance separately from content mastery |

**100-point vs. 4-point scale comparison (same underlying performance):**

| Scenario | 100-point avg | 4-point avg (4=A,3=B,2=C,1=D,0=F) |
|---|---|---|
| Scores: A,A,A, one 0 | (95+92+90+0)/4 = 69.25 (D) | (4+4+4+0)/4 = 3.0 (B) |
| Scores: A,A,A, one 50-floor | (95+92+90+50)/4 = 81.75 (B-) | n/a (floor doesn't map cleanly) |

The 100-point scale turns one missing assignment into a full letter-grade collapse even when three of four scores are A's; a 4-point scale or a 50-floor both blunt (not eliminate) the effect — the actual fix is completing the missing assessment, per the SKILL.md worked example.

## 3. Advisory scope-and-sequence (one semester, filled example)

```
Weeks 1–2:  Norms + goal-setting (each student sets one academic, one
            personal goal; revisited weeks 6, 12, 18)
Weeks 3–4:  Planner/agenda audit (structured: pull out planner, check
            against actual assignments due, not a lecture on organization)
Weeks 5–6:  SEL unit — recognizing peer pressure vs. genuine choice
Weeks 7–8:  Goal check-in #1 (5 min per student, rotating small groups)
Weeks 9–10: Study-skills unit — chunking a long-term project into
            checkpoints (practiced on an actual upcoming assignment)
Weeks 11–12: Goal check-in #2
Weeks 13–14: SEL unit — conflict de-escalation without a public audience
Weeks 15–16: Transition prep (if end of year) or mid-year goal reset
Weeks 17–18: Goal check-in #3 + reflection on growth since Week 1
```

**Rule:** every advisory session has a one-line stated objective on the board before students arrive. If a substitute or observer can't tell what skill this session teaches within 60 seconds, it has drifted into unstructured time.

## 4. Interdisciplinary unit planning template (filled)

```
UNIT: "Systems Under Pressure" — 3-week cross-subject unit, grade 7
SUBJECTS: Science (ecosystems), Social Studies (economic systems),
  ELA (persuasive writing)
SHARED ESSENTIAL QUESTION: "When one part of a system fails, who
  absorbs the cost, and who decides?"
SCIENCE ANCHOR: Case study — a keystone-species removal and the
  cascading ecosystem effects (data: population counts over 10 years)
SOCIAL STUDIES ANCHOR: Case study — a supply-chain disruption and
  which groups absorbed the cost vs. which were insulated
ELA ANCHOR: Persuasive essay arguing who should bear responsibility
  in one of the two case studies, using evidence from both classes
CHECKPOINTS: Week 1 end — evidence log started in both content
  classes; Week 2 end — essay outline conferenced in ELA; Week 3 —
  final essay + a one-slide "system map" presented to the team
COMMON PLANNING TIME USE: 15 min/week cross-referencing student
  evidence logs so ELA can flag students whose content-class
  evidence is thin before the essay is due, not after it's graded
```

## 5. Redo/retake policy decision tree

```
Was the original attempt made in good faith?
├── No (blank, off-task copy, clear non-attempt)
│     → No redo privilege. Score stands. Log as a completion issue,
│       not a mastery issue, and route to the completion tracker.
└── Yes (genuine attempt, low score)
      → Was this a summative mastery check (unit test, project)?
        ├── Yes → Redo available. New score replaces old, capped at
        │          the passing threshold (school policy, e.g. 75%),
        │          UNLESS the school's stated policy allows full
        │          credit for a first-time redo — apply that policy,
        │          don't freelance an exception.
        └── No (formative/practice) → No redo needed; it was never
                   meant to be averaged into the mastery grade at all.
                   Fix the gradebook category, not the score.
```
