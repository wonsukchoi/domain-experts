# Vocabulary

### Exertional Heat Stroke (EHS)
Life-threatening hyperthermia (typically rectal temp ≥104–105°F) with central nervous system dysfunction, occurring during or after exertion.
**In use:** "His temp's 105.9 with confusion — that's EHS, get the tub, not the shade tent."
**Common misuse:** Treating it as a severe case of "heat exhaustion" and responding with passive rest and oral fluids instead of immediate cold-water immersion — the two conditions require different responses and confusing them costs the minutes that matter.

### SCAT6 (Sport Concussion Assessment Tool, 6th edition)
A standardized sideline tool combining symptom checklist, cognitive screen, and balance testing (mBESS), used to assess a suspected concussion at the time of injury.
**In use:** "SCAT6 was clean, but he's still getting held out — one clean score isn't a clearance."
**Common misuse:** Treating a normal or improved SCAT6 score as full clearance to return, rather than one data point in a required multi-day, serial-monitoring process.

### Second Impact Syndrome
Rare but often fatal cerebral swelling that can occur when an athlete sustains a second head injury before fully recovering from a first one.
**In use:** "That's exactly why there's no same-day return on any suspected concussion — second impact syndrome doesn't care how mild the first hit looked."
**Common misuse:** Using the term to describe any prolonged or severe concussion recovery — it specifically refers to the rare acute catastrophic swelling event, not a slow recovery timeline.

### Graduated Return to Sport (GRTS)
The staged, minimum-24-hour-per-stage progression (symptom-limited activity through full competition) an athlete moves through after a concussion, moving back a stage if symptoms recur.
**In use:** "He's on stage 3 of the GRTS — sport-specific drills, no contact yet."
**Common misuse:** Compressing stages under a schedule deadline (e.g., a playoff game), treating the 24-hour floor as a formality rather than the minimum window symptom recurrence needs to show up in.

### Ottawa Ankle Rules
A validated clinical decision rule (bony tenderness at the malleolus tips, or inability to bear weight for 4 steps) used to decide whether an ankle or midfoot injury needs an X-ray.
**In use:** "He failed the Ottawa rules — tender right at the tip of the lateral malleolus — so he's getting imaged before we tape anything."
**Common misuse:** Skipping the rule and deciding on imaging based on how much pain the athlete reports, which correlates poorly with actual fracture risk.

### Functional testing (hop test, Y-balance/star excursion)
Objective, sport-specific performance measures (single-leg hop distance/time, reach distance on a balance grid) compared against the uninjured side or a preseason baseline to decide return-to-play readiness.
**In use:** "Hop symmetry's at 88% — that's outside our 90% threshold, so he's not cleared yet."
**Common misuse:** Substituting a subjective "how does it feel" check for a measured symmetry percentage, especially under time pressure from a coach.

### Baseline testing
Preseason measurement (SCAT/ImPACT score, isokinetic strength, hop/balance performance) recorded for an individual athlete before injury, used as their personal reference range.
**In use:** "His baseline SCAT was already low on the balance component — don't compare him to the population norm, compare him to his own number."
**Common misuse:** Comparing post-injury results only to population norms when an individual baseline exists and would give a more accurate picture.

### WBGT (Wet Bulb Globe Temperature)
A composite heat-stress index (combining temperature, humidity, wind, and solar radiation) used to set practice modification thresholds, distinct from air temperature alone.
**In use:** "WBGT's 84 — that's into mandatory-break territory per the heat policy, regardless of what the thermometer on the scoreboard says."
**Common misuse:** Using plain air temperature or the "feels like" number from a weather app as a stand-in for WBGT when setting practice modifications.

### Cool-first, transport second
The exertional heat stroke field protocol of beginning on-site cold-water immersion immediately, with EMS staged but transport delayed until the athlete reaches a cooling target (~102°F).
**In use:** "Tub's here, EMS is staged — we cool first, transport second, per protocol."
**Common misuse:** Defaulting to immediate transport (the intuitive move) which delays the actual treatment — cooling — by the length of the ambulance ride, materially worsening outcomes.

### Chronic ankle instability
Persistent mechanical or functional laxity and recurrent sprain risk following an inadequately rehabilitated ankle ligament injury.
**In use:** "That's his fourth 'rolled ankle' this year — that's chronic instability, not bad luck."
**Common misuse:** Treating each recurrence as an isolated new injury rather than recognizing a pattern that points back to incomplete rehab or unaddressed peroneal weakness.

### Exertional sickling
A collapse in an athlete with sickle cell trait, triggered by intense exertion, caused by red blood cell sickling in the microvasculature — clinically distinct from muscle cramping and from heat stroke, requiring rapid oxygen and cooling rather than stretching.
**In use:** "He's SCT-positive and collapsed without cramping first — treat this as sickling, not a cramp, until proven otherwise."
**Common misuse:** Assuming any exertional collapse with muscle symptoms is a cramp and responding with stretching and salt, which delays the oxygen and cooling response sickling actually needs.

### Female Athlete Triad / RED-S (Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport)
An interrelated condition of low energy availability, menstrual dysfunction, and low bone density (Triad), broadened in current terminology (RED-S) to include effects beyond the original three components.
**In use:** "Third metatarsal stress fracture this year plus irregular cycles — that's a RED-S workup, not just a bone density problem."
**Common misuse:** Treating a stress fracture as a standalone training-load issue without screening for the underlying energy-availability and menstrual-history pattern.

### Compartment syndrome
Elevated pressure within a fascial muscle compartment that compromises blood flow and tissue viability — a surgical emergency when acute.
**In use:** "Pain out of proportion, tense compartment, pain on passive stretch — that's a compartment syndrome workup, not a bad bruise."
**Common misuse:** Attributing the disproportionate pain to the athlete's pain tolerance rather than escalating for a pressure check or physician evaluation.
