# B2B sales working vocabulary

Terms an AE uses precisely. Format: definition → how a practitioner says it → common misuse.

**MEDDIC (Metrics, Economic buyer, Decision criteria, Decision process, Identify pain, Champion)** — A qualification framework covering the specific metric the buyer cares about, who controls budget, what criteria they'll judge options by, how the decision actually gets made, the pain driving urgency, and who advocates internally — deeper than a fast initial filter like BANT.
In use: "We have pain and a champion, but no confirmed economic buyer engagement yet — that's the MEDDIC gap keeping this at best-case, not commit."
Misuse: treating MEDDIC as a checklist to fill in retroactively for a forecast call rather than an active qualification framework that should shape how the deal is actually run.

**BANT (Budget, Authority, Need, Timeline)** — A fast initial qualification filter for whether a deal deserves real time investment, explicitly a floor, not a full qualification method — it misses the nuance a deeper framework like MEDDIC would catch.
In use: "This passes BANT as a fast filter, but we still need MEDDIC-level qualification before investing serious cycles."
Misuse: treating BANT qualification as sufficient on its own for a complex enterprise deal, missing the deeper stakeholder and decision-process nuance a fuller qualification method surfaces.

**Champion** — An internal contact who will personally advocate for the deal when the seller isn't in the room, distinct from a contact who is merely responsive or friendly but won't actually push for approval.
In use: "She's engaged and friendly, but the champion test fails — she's never advocated for this internally when we're not on the call."
Misuse: treating any responsive, friendly contact as a champion; the actual test is whether they'll advocate for the deal in rooms the seller isn't in, not how pleasant the conversation is.

**Economic buyer** — The specific person with actual budget authority to approve the purchase, as distinct from a champion, technical evaluator, or end user who may be enthusiastic but doesn't control spend.
In use: "We have strong champion support, but the economic buyer has never been on a call — that's the specific gap keeping this out of 'commit.'"
Misuse: assuming a champion's enthusiasm implies economic buyer alignment, when the champion and the actual budget-holder can have very different priorities and levels of engagement.

**Mutual action plan (close plan)** — A jointly built document mapping every remaining step to close, with owners and dates on both the buyer's and seller's side, used as concrete evidence of real forward progress rather than seller-side hope.
In use: "The mutual action plan has dated steps through legal review and signature — that's what makes this a real commit, not just optimism."
Misuse: building a "close plan" that only the seller has seen or contributed to, which reflects seller hope rather than a genuine mutual commitment with buyer-side ownership and dates.

**Forecast categories (commit, best-case, pipeline)** — Distinct forecast confidence tiers defined by specific evidence thresholds (e.g., signed mutual action plan and economic buyer engagement for commit), not by how good the last call felt.
In use: "This is best-case, not commit — the evidence bar for commit specifically requires economic buyer engagement, which hasn't happened yet."
Misuse: calling a deal "commit" based on the rep's confidence or the champion's enthusiasm rather than the specific, defined evidence threshold each forecast category actually requires.

**Single-threading** — Relying on one contact as the entire relationship at an account, with no visibility into or relationship with other stakeholders who could influence or block the deal.
In use: "We're single-threaded on the VP — if she changes roles next month, we have no other relationship to fall back on."
Misuse: treating a strong single relationship as sufficient risk management for a deal, missing that a single point of contact creates real fragility regardless of how positive that one relationship currently is.

**Objection behind the objection** — The underlying concern (risk aversion, unclear ROI, an unconvinced stakeholder) that a stated objection ("too expensive," "not the right time") is often standing in for, found by asking follow-up questions rather than answering the literal statement.
In use: "The stated objection is price, but the objection behind the objection is that the champion hasn't gotten finance to validate the ROI numbers themselves."
Misuse: answering a stated objection literally (e.g., offering a discount for a price objection) without probing for the underlying concern, which often leaves the actual blocker unaddressed and produces a new objection later.

**ROI/business case (buyer-defensible)** — A quantified comparison of the cost of the status quo against the cost of the proposed solution, built collaboratively with the champion so they can defend it internally to their own finance or leadership without the seller present.
In use: "The revised ROI case uses finance's own validated rework estimate, not our vendor number — that's what lets the champion defend it internally."
Misuse: building an ROI case using only vendor-supplied estimates the champion hasn't validated internally, leaving them unable to defend the numbers to their own finance team when challenged.

**Win/loss review** — A structured post-mortem process examining both won and lost deals to find real patterns in what drove the outcome, as distinct from anecdotal impressions of "what's working" based on recent memorable deals.
In use: "The win/loss review showed deals without economic buyer engagement by stage 3 lose at 3x the rate of ones with it — that's a real pattern, not a hunch."
Misuse: relying on anecdotal impressions from recent, memorable deals to guide sales process changes rather than systematic win/loss data across a larger, more representative sample.
