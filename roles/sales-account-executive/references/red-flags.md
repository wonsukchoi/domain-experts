# Red flags — what an AE notices instantly

Smell tests with thresholds. Any single one can be innocent; two or three together are a pattern.

### A "commit" forecast with no signed mutual action plan, no economic buyer engagement, and no procurement process started
- **Usually means:** the deal is being called "commit" on enthusiasm rather than evidence, and leadership will plan around a number the underlying signals don't support.
- **First question:** "What's the concrete evidence — mutual action plan, economic buyer call, procurement paper — versus just a champion's stated confidence?"
- **Data to pull:** CRM stage/evidence fields, most recent contact with the economic buyer (if any), procurement/legal engagement status.

### A price objection met immediately with a discount, with no question asked about what's actually driving the concern
- **Usually means:** the real blocker (often unestablished ROI confidence, not the number itself) goes unaddressed, and discounting doesn't fix a problem that was never about price.
- **First question:** "What specifically is driving this price concern, and compared to what alternative?"
- **Data to pull:** the ROI/business case status for this deal, whether the champion has validated the numbers internally.

### A single contact as the entire relationship at an account, with no visibility into whether they can actually get the deal approved
- **Usually means:** single-threading — a deal can die with no warning if the one contact loses influence, changes roles, or was never actually able to drive approval.
- **First question:** "Who else at this account has been engaged, and does this contact actually have the authority or influence to get this approved?"
- **Data to pull:** stakeholder map for the account, engagement history with anyone beyond the primary contact.

### Pipeline activity (calls, demos) counted as progress with no new stakeholder engaged or next step scheduled with a date
- **Usually means:** activity is being confused with actual buying-process advancement — busy doesn't mean moving.
- **First question:** "What specifically advanced in the buying process this week, not just what activity happened?"
- **Data to pull:** stakeholder engagement timeline, scheduled next steps with dates versus completed activities.

### A deal qualification based on enthusiasm or a large logo rather than confirmed pain, budget, and timeline tied to a real event
- **Usually means:** effort may be invested in a deal that was never going to close, regardless of how well it's pitched.
- **First question:** "What's the confirmed pain tied to a business metric, the actual budget owner engaged, and the timeline tied to a real event — not just how excited this contact sounds?"
- **Data to pull:** MEDDIC/BANT qualification fields for the deal, evidence behind each.

### A proposal or pitch presented before a discovery conversation establishes the buyer's specific situation and metrics
- **Usually means:** a generic pitch signals the rep didn't listen, which is itself disqualifying to a sophisticated buyer.
- **First question:** "What specific discovery was done on this account's metrics and constraints before this pitch was built?"
- **Data to pull:** discovery call notes/summary, whether the pitch content is customized to what was actually learned.

### A commitment on product capability or delivery timeline made to close a deal with no internal confirmation it's achievable
- **Usually means:** overpromising to close trades a short-term win for a renewal and reference risk once the commitment doesn't hold.
- **First question:** "Has the relevant internal team (product, delivery, support) confirmed this specific commitment is real?"
- **Data to pull:** the specific commitment made, confirmation status from the team responsible for delivering on it.

### An objection answered literally on its first statement with no follow-up question to find the underlying concern
- **Usually means:** the stated objection is often a symptom, not the real blocker — answering it literally without probing further can leave the actual concern unaddressed, producing a new objection next call.
- **First question:** "What would need to be true for this stated concern to actually be resolved — is there something underneath it?"
- **Data to pull:** the objection as stated, any follow-up discovery done to find the underlying concern.
