# Vocabulary

Terms generalists conflate that institutional-foodservice practitioners keep sharply separate. Each: definition, a sentence a practitioner would actually say, and the common misuse.

## Ounce equivalent (oz eq) vs. actual ounce weight

**Ounce equivalent** is the USDA crediting unit for meat/meat-alternate and grain components in a reimbursable meal — it measures nutritional contribution against a standard (e.g., 1 oz eq of grain roughly equals 16g of creditable grain ingredient), not the food's raw weight on a scale.

*Practitioner usage:* "That chicken patty weighs 3 oz raw, but after cooking loss it only credits 2 oz eq — check the manufacturer's CN label before you plan portions around it."

*Common misuse:* assuming a food's scale weight and its oz-eq credit are the same number; they diverge for breaded, composite, or moisture-loss items.

## Offer versus Serve (OVS) vs. family style vs. à la carte

**OVS** lets a student or patient decline some reimbursable components (commonly select at least 3 of 5, one being a fruit or vegetable) while the kitchen must still produce and offer all components at minimum portion. **Family style** is self-service from shared platters, common in some long-term-care settings. **À la carte** is separately priced food outside the reimbursable meal pattern, with no meal-pattern obligation attached.

*Practitioner usage:* "We're OVS today, so the fruit cup still gets portioned for all 480 even though maybe 300 kids actually take it."

*Common misuse:* treating low uptake of a component under OVS as license to under-produce it — the compliance obligation is on what's offered, not what's chosen.

## Standardized recipe vs. house recipe

A **standardized recipe** is a tested, fixed-yield recipe card carrying oz-eq crediting that a nutrient analysis was built against — changing an ingredient or quantity invalidates that crediting. A **house recipe** (restaurant sense) is whatever the kitchen currently uses and can be adjusted at will with no external certification riding on it.

*Practitioner usage:* "That's not a house recipe I can tweak to taste — it's standardized, so a swap needs to come off the substitution list."

*Common misuse:* a cook with restaurant background treating the recipe card as a starting point rather than a fixed spec.

## HACCP Process 1 vs. Process 2 vs. Process 3

**Process 1** is no-cook food (salads, sandwiches assembled cold) — the control point is preventing cross-contact/contamination, not cook temperature. **Process 2** is same-day cook-and-serve — the control points are cook end-temp and hot-hold time. **Process 3** is complex food that's cooked, cooled, and reheated (cook-chill) — it adds the cooling-curve control point on top of Process 2's.

*Practitioner usage:* "The chicken salad is Process 1 — nobody's checking a cook temp on it, they're checking hand-washing and cold-holding."

*Common misuse:* applying a single generic "check the temperature" mindset across all three, missing that Process 1's actual risk is contact, not heat.

## Cyclical menu vs. static menu

A **cyclical menu** repeats on a fixed rotation (commonly 4 or 6 weeks) so the nutrient analysis for the whole cycle only needs certifying once. A **static menu** doesn't repeat on a schedule and would need re-certifying more often, which is why institutional settings almost never run one.

*Practitioner usage:* "We're in week 3 of the cycle — that's the same Tuesday menu we ran three weeks ago, so the nutrient numbers are already certified."

*Common misuse:* assuming "the same menu every few weeks" is a creativity failure rather than the compliance mechanism that makes certification affordable at all.

## Meal pattern vs. menu

The **meal pattern** is the regulatory minimum structure (component minimums, calorie ranges, sodium/fat targets) a meal must satisfy. The **menu** is the specific dishes chosen to satisfy it on a given day. Two very different menus can satisfy the identical meal pattern.

*Practitioner usage:* "The meal pattern needs 2 oz eq of meat/alt at lunch — today's menu happens to deliver that with chicken, next Tuesday it's with beans."

*Common misuse:* using "menu" and "meal pattern" interchangeably, which hides that a menu change still has to be checked against the pattern, not assumed compliant because a similar dish worked before.

## Therapeutic diet vs. texture-modified diet vs. IDDSI level

A **therapeutic diet** modifies nutrient content for a medical condition (renal, cardiac, diabetic). A **texture-modified diet** changes physical consistency (mechanical soft, pureed) typically for chewing/swallowing safety. **IDDSI levels** (0–7) are the standardized international scale used to specify exactly which texture/thickness a patient needs, replacing older facility-specific terms like "nectar-thick."

*Practitioner usage:* "She's IDDSI level 4 pureed, cardiac diet — that's two separate modifications stacked on the same tray, not one instruction."

*Common misuse:* treating "soft diet" as a single well-defined thing when the facility hasn't mapped its old terminology to IDDSI levels, so "soft" means different things to different staff.

## Production record vs. recipe card

The **recipe card** is the fixed, tested formula. The **production record** is the day-of document logging what was actually made, how much, any substitutions, and how many were served — it's the artifact an auditor checks against the recipe card and the nutrient analysis.

*Practitioner usage:* "The recipe card says 2 tsp salt at 50 portions — the production record shows what we actually used today at 480 portions and why."

*Common misuse:* keeping the recipe card current but not filling out the production record, which is the document that actually proves compliance on any given day.

## Reimbursable meal vs. à la carte item

A **reimbursable meal** meets the full meal pattern and draws federal/facility per-meal reimbursement. An **à la carte item** is sold separately, doesn't have to meet the meal pattern alone, and draws no reimbursement.

*Practitioner usage:* "That extra cookie is à la carte — it doesn't count toward or against today's meal-pattern compliance."

*Common misuse:* assuming every item on a cafeteria line carries meal-pattern obligations; only the reimbursable-meal components do.

## Cross-contact vs. cross-contamination

**Cross-contact** is the transfer of an allergen from one food/surface to another (e.g., a shared fryer or cutting board). **Cross-contamination** is the transfer of pathogens (bacteria, viruses). The prevention steps overlap (separate equipment, handwashing) but the failure mode and the harm are different — one causes an allergic reaction, the other causes foodborne illness.

*Practitioner usage:* "Wash and sanitize that board before the gluten-free batch — that's a cross-contact control, separate from the cross-contamination steps we already do for raw chicken."

*Common misuse:* using the two terms interchangeably, which can lead to treating an allergen control step as satisfied by a generic sanitizing step meant for pathogens.

## Danger zone / the 2-hour/4-hour rule

The **temperature danger zone** is 41°F–135°F, where bacteria multiply fastest. The **2-hour/4-hour rule** governs cumulative time food spends in that zone: under 2 hours, it can still be used or refrigerated; 2–4 hours, it must be used immediately with no cooling or re-holding; over 4 hours, it must be discarded — regardless of how it got there.

*Practitioner usage:* "That tray's been sitting since the delivery delay plus prep time — add it up, we're past 2 hours cumulative, so it goes straight to service now or gets tossed, not back in the cooler."

*Common misuse:* resetting the clock every time food moves between stations, instead of tracking cumulative danger-zone time across the whole day.

## Per-patient-day cost vs. per-meal cost

**Per-patient-day cost** (hospital/long-term-care) is the total food-and-labor allowance for all meals and snacks a patient receives in 24 hours. **Per-meal cost** (school/correctional) budgets a single meal against a per-meal reimbursement or per-diem figure. Confusing the two misstates whether a single expensive meal is actually a problem.

*Practitioner usage:* "Breakfast ran a little rich today, but we're still under the per-patient-day allowance once you net it against last night's lighter dinner."

*Common misuse:* flagging one meal as over-budget without checking whether the facility's actual cost unit is the day, not the single meal.

## Commodity foods (USDA Foods) vs. purchased foods

**USDA Foods** (commodities) are federally donated or entitlement-funded product allocated to schools and some institutions, credited at a set rate against the program's food-cost budget. **Purchased foods** are bought directly by the site at market price. The two often sit side by side on the same recipe card with different cost implications.

*Practitioner usage:* "The diced chicken on that recipe is a USDA commodity item — don't cost it at the market purchase price when you're reconciling this month's food-cost report."

*Common misuse:* costing commodity-sourced ingredients at retail/market price when reconciling budget variance, which overstates the actual cash food cost.

## Batch cooking vs. cook-chill

**Batch cooking** produces and serves the same day, at volume. **Cook-chill** cooks in batch, then rapidly cools and stores for reheating and service later (hours or days out) — it's a Process 3 HACCP category with its own cooling-curve control point that batch cook-and-serve doesn't need.

*Practitioner usage:* "This is going into the cook-chill program for Thursday's service, so it needs to hit the full cooling curve today, not just get cooked and held."

*Common misuse:* applying same-day hot-holding rules to a cook-chill batch, skipping the cooling-curve documentation that cook-chill specifically requires.
