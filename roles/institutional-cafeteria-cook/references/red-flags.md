# Red flags

Smell tests a kitchen manager or nutrition director should catch before a shift ends, not at the next audit. Format: what it usually means, the first question to ask, and the data to pull.

## 1. Steam-table item held >4 hours since cook-end, even at ≥135°F

**Usually means:** the site's hold-quality ceiling was never posted or trained, and staff are treating the bare food-safety minimum (≥135°F) as the only limit that matters. Texture and nutrient quality degrade well before the safety limit fails.

**First question:** "What time did this item leave the cook stage, and what's our written hold-quality ceiling for it?"

**Data to pull:** the hot-hold time-temperature log for that item, cook-end timestamp, the site's HACCP plan's stated hold-quality limit (distinct from the safety limit).

## 2. A diet order changed on a server's verbal say-so, no updated tray ticket

**Usually means:** a well-intentioned shortcut around the diet office, or a communication gap between the floor and the kitchen — either way, the tray now doesn't match the documented medical statement on file.

**First question:** "Where's the updated diet-office ticket or medical statement authorizing this change?"

**Data to pull:** the resident/patient/student's current diet order on file, the timestamp of any change request, who approved it.

## 3. Weekly nutrient analysis doesn't reconcile against the production records

**Usually means:** substitutions were made during the week and not logged, so the certified nutrient analysis for the cycle no longer describes what was actually served.

**First question:** "Pull every substitution made this week — which ones are on the approved list, and which aren't?"

**Data to pull:** production records for each day, the approved substitution list, the dietitian's original nutrient analysis for the cycle.

## 4. Cooling log shows food still above 70°F at the 2-hour checkpoint

**Usually means:** the batch size was too large for the cooling method (e.g., a deep hotel pan instead of shallow pans, or no ice-wand/blast chiller use), which is the single most common cooling-curve failure in institutional volume.

**First question:** "What method and pan depth was used to cool this batch, and was it split into shallow pans?"

**Data to pull:** the cooling log timestamps, batch volume, pan depth, cooling method used, ambient walk-in temperature during the cool.

## 5. A recipe scaled by straight linear multiplication above SF 6, no taste-adjustment note

**Usually means:** whoever scaled it treated the recipe card as pure arithmetic instead of applying the seasoning correction — the batch is very likely over- or under-seasoned relative to the base recipe.

**First question:** "Was this seasoning line corrected below linear and tasted before it went to hold?"

**Data to pull:** the production record's scaling worksheet, the recipe card's base seasoning line, any taste-adjustment note (or its absence).

## 6. An allergen ingredient substituted with a "close enough" commercial product, no matrix check

**Usually means:** a stockout was solved on the fly by whoever was on the line that day, without cross-referencing the allergen matrix — the new product's label may carry an allergen the original didn't.

**First question:** "Was this substitute's ingredient label checked against the allergen matrix before it went into the batch?"

**Data to pull:** the substitute product's ingredient label/allergen statement, the approved substitution list, the allergen matrix for any student/patient/resident receiving that item.

## 7. Offer-versus-Serve line is short a reimbursable component or under minimum portion

**Usually means:** either a production shortfall (ran out) that wasn't escalated, or a portioning drift where staff have been quietly under-portioning to stretch a batch — both break reimbursement eligibility for every tray served that period.

**First question:** "Were all five components available at or above their minimum oz-eq/cup-eq for the full service window?"

**Data to pull:** the day's production record against the certified meal pattern, portion-scoop size in use versus the recipe card's specified scoop, any mid-service shortage notes.

## 8. Repeated stockouts resolved with "chef's choice" substitutions, no dietitian sign-off

**Usually means:** the purchasing/inventory process is under strain (vendor issue, budget shortfall) and the kitchen has started absorbing that strain with uncertified swaps rather than escalating the root cause.

**First question:** "How many uncertified substitutions have happened this month, and is it the same two or three ingredients each time?"

**Data to pull:** the substitution log over the last 4–6 weeks, vendor fill-rate reports, purchasing/inventory records for the recurring shortfall items.

## 9. Receiving log shows deliveries accepted above 41°F (cold) with no corrective action noted

**Usually means:** receiving checks are being logged as a formality rather than actually gating acceptance — temperature abuse may already have started in transit before the food ever reaches the kitchen.

**First question:** "What was the corrective action when this delivery came in above temp — rejected, or accepted anyway?"

**Data to pull:** the receiving log, the vendor's delivery-temperature history, any rejected-delivery/credit paperwork (or its absence).

## 10. Per-meal food cost trending well above the reimbursement-derived target with no menu-mix explanation

**Usually means:** either a commodity price spike on one or two line items (isolated, easy fix) or systemic overproduction/waste (harder, needs a headcount-projection fix) — the two look identical on a blended cost line.

**First question:** "Break the overage down by ingredient line — is it one commodity spiking, or is it spread across the whole invoice?"

**Data to pull:** itemized invoices for the period, waste log, headcount-projected versus actual servings, prior-month price baseline per commodity.

## 11. Correctional/hospital master menu's certified calorie count sits below the facility's documented minimum, no RD memo

**Usually means:** a menu-cycle revision (cost-driven substitution, portion change) happened without re-running it past the registered dietitian who owns the master-menu sign-off — the calorie floor (e.g., a facility's documented general-population minimum) is a compliance requirement, not a target to trim quietly.

**First question:** "Who signed off on the current master menu's nutrient analysis, and when was it last re-certified?"

**Data to pull:** the master menu's dietitian-signed nutrient analysis, the date of the last revision, the facility's documented calorie-minimum policy.

## 12. Probe thermometer calibration log has gaps of more than one shift

**Usually means:** calibration is being treated as optional busywork rather than the thing that makes every other temperature reading in this list trustworthy — a mis-calibrated probe can make a genuinely unsafe hold read as compliant.

**First question:** "When was this probe last calibrated against ice-point or boiling-point, and by whom?"

**Data to pull:** the calibration log, the probe's last-known offset, which food temperature logs were recorded using that specific probe during the gap.
