# Floral Designer — Vocabulary

### Cost of goods (COGS)
The wholesale cost of the flowers, greenery, and hard goods that go into an arrangement — not including labor, delivery, or overhead.
**In use:** "Our COGS on this wedding is running 26%, so we've got room before we're eating into labor margin."
**Common misuse:** Clients (and some junior designers) assume "cost" means the total quote price; COGS is only the raw-materials slice, and confusing the two leads to underpricing.

### Focal flower
The largest or most visually dominant bloom in an arrangement, around which supporting flowers, filler, and greenery are composed.
**In use:** "The garden roses are our focal flower here — everything else is playing a supporting role in scale and color."
**Common misuse:** Generalists assume every expensive stem is "the focal flower"; the focal role is about visual weight and placement, not price.

### Filler
Smaller, textural flowers or foliage used to fill visual gaps between focal blooms and add movement, not to bulk up stem count cheaply.
**In use:** "We're using waxflower as filler here to break up the density between the garden roses."
**Common misuse:** Treating filler as a low-cost way to pad an arrangement's apparent size rather than as a deliberate textural/movement choice.

### Hard goods
Non-perishable items in a floral order — vases, compotes, stands, arch structures, candle holders — priced and often rented separately from the flowers.
**In use:** "The arch structure is a hard-goods rental line, separate from the floral dressing cost."
**Common misuse:** Folding hard-goods rental cost into the flower markup instead of pricing it as its own line, which obscures true margin.

### Vase life
The number of days a cut stem stays visually acceptable (turgid, uncolored petals, no wilting) under proper hydration and storage.
**In use:** "Dahlias have maybe a 3-4 day vase life even with perfect processing — we can't build those more than a day out."
**Common misuse:** Assuming vase life is fixed and identical across all flowers; it varies by variety, harvest maturity, and processing quality, not just "how long flowers usually last."

### Hydration/processing
The step of re-cutting a stem underwater (or immediately submerging the fresh cut) and placing it into a preservative solution, done on arrival before any design work.
**In use:** "Nothing goes into the design queue until it's been processed and had at least two hours of hydration."
**Common misuse:** Skipping or rushing processing under time pressure, treating it as optional prep rather than the step that determines how much usable vase life is left.

### Preservative solution
A mixed solution of biocide (prevents bacterial growth), acidifier (lowers water pH for better uptake), and sugar (feeds the stem) that cut flowers are placed into after processing.
**In use:** "Swap the bucket water for fresh preservative solution — it's been sitting since yesterday and the biocide's spent."
**Common misuse:** Assuming plain water with "flower food" packets sprinkled in achieves the same result as a properly mixed solution at the correct concentration.

### Ethylene sensitivity
A stem's vulnerability to ethylene gas (released by ripening fruit, decaying plant matter, and some flowers themselves), which accelerates wilting and petal drop.
**In use:** "Keep the carnations and delphinium away from that fruit bowl in the back cooler — they're both ethylene-sensitive."
**Common misuse:** Assuming all flowers are equally affected; sensitivity varies widely by species, and some flowers (like carnations) are both sensitive to and emitters of ethylene.

### Chilling injury
Physical damage (translucent spotting, browning, wilting) that occurs when cold-sensitive tropical stems are stored below their safe temperature threshold, typically around 50°F.
**In use:** "Those anthuriums are showing chilling injury — someone stored them in the main cooler instead of the tropical zone."
**Common misuse:** Treating "colder is always fresher" as a universal rule; tropical stems are damaged by standard floral-cooler temperatures that work fine for temperate flowers.

### Same-family substitution
Replacing an unavailable variety with one from the same botanical family or bloom-form category, preserving texture and scale rather than just color.
**In use:** "We can't get peonies this week, so I'm proposing a same-family swap to a large garden rose instead of a color-matched carnation."
**Common misuse:** Treating any similarly-colored flower as an acceptable substitute regardless of bloom form, which changes the arrangement's texture in ways clients notice.

### Centerpiece height class
A categorization of centerpiece design (low/compact, medium, tall) chosen based on room scale, sightlines, and guest interaction needs, not aesthetic preference alone.
**In use:** "At a 14-foot ceiling we need to go up to a tall height class or these centerpieces will read as an afterthought."
**Common misuse:** Choosing height class purely from a Pinterest reference photo without checking it against the actual room's ceiling height and table conversation needs.

### Strike
The post-event process of removing floral installations and collecting rented hard goods, distinct from teardown of non-floral event elements.
**In use:** "Strike starts at 10 PM once the reception's officially wrapped — we're only pulling our own rentals, not touching the DJ's gear."
**Common misuse:** Assuming "strike" means removing everything florals-adjacent, including client-owned keepsake arrangements that should stay.

### Design labor
The billed time spent physically constructing arrangements, distinct from consultation, sourcing, or administrative time, and priced as its own line separate from flower cost.
**In use:** "Design labor on this wedding ran 22 hours across three designers — that's a separate line from the flower markup."
**Common misuse:** Folding labor into the flower markup instead of pricing it explicitly, which hides the real cost driver when a job runs long.

### Wholesale confirmation
The point at which a grower or wholesaler has formally committed a specific quantity and grade of a variety to an order, as opposed to it merely appearing available on a catalog or price list.
**In use:** "That peony order isn't wholesale-confirmed yet — it's still just listed as available, which isn't the same thing three weeks out from a weather-sensitive bloom window."
**Common misuse:** Treating catalog/price-list availability as a locked commitment, when it's often provisional until the wholesaler confirms closer to the ship date.
