# Playbook

Filled templates and step sequences, not descriptions of them. Defaults, not laws — deviate consciously and document why.

## 1. ACSM preparticipation screening algorithm (filled decision table)

Run before any submaximal or maximal exertion test, fitness assessment involving exertion, or new-to-exercise lab activity.

| Step | Question | If NO | If YES |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Is the participant currently doing regular exercise (moderate intensity, ≥30 min/session, ≥3 days/week, for ≥3 months)? | Go to Step 2 as "inactive" | Go to Step 2 as "active" |
| 2 | Does the participant have known cardiovascular, metabolic (diabetes), or renal disease? | Go to Step 3 | Medical clearance required before testing at any intensity beyond light, regardless of activity status |
| 3 | Does the participant have any signs or symptoms suggestive of cardiovascular, metabolic, or renal disease (chest pain/discomfort, unusual dyspnea, dizziness/syncope, ankle edema, palpitations)? | Cleared to proceed at the desired intensity given activity status from Step 1 | Medical clearance required before testing, regardless of activity status |
| 4 | Desired exercise intensity for the session | If inactive + no disease/symptoms: clear for light-to-moderate intensity without clearance; vigorous intensity for a previously inactive participant still defaults to clearance first | If active + no disease/symptoms: clear for any intensity without further clearance |

Document the answer to all three questions and the resulting clearance decision on the participant's intake form before the test begins — a verbal "I feel fine" is not the documentation an incident review will accept.

## 3. Psychomotor skill check-off — calibration protocol

1. Before the first section's live grading, every instructor/TA who will grade the skill scores the same 2–3 recorded or live sample demonstrations independently, using the final checklist.
2. Compare scores item-by-item across graders — not just the total. A checklist item where graders disagree on 2+ of 3 samples gets rewritten to a more observable behavior before live grading starts (e.g., "compresses chest at a rate consistent with 100–120/min metronome" instead of "compresses at correct rate").
3. Run live grading with the calibrated checklist; every step is met/not-met, no partial credit unless the step is explicitly tiered with an observable anchor per tier.
4. After grading, compare pass rates across sections for the identical skill. A gap over ~15 percentage points between sections graded by different people, with no difference in student population, means recalibrate before the next check-off — don't wait for the next term.

## 5. Settled-vs-open classroom classification (filled examples)

Classify before teaching any topic likely to draw a content dispute.

| Topic | Classification | How to teach it |
|---|---|---|
| Progressive overload drives strength adaptation | Settled | Teach as consensus finding; cite the mechanistic and meta-analytic literature, don't stage a "debate" |
| Caloric deficit is required for fat loss | Settled | Teach as consensus finding; the open question next to it (which deficit size/diet composition is most adherent) is separate |
| Optimal periodization model (linear vs. undulating vs. block) for a given sport | Open | Present the competing evidence-based models, assign readings representing more than one, don't disclose personal preference as "the answer" |
| A specific supplement's marketed benefit sourced only from the manufacturer or an influencer | Neither — not evidence-based content | Do not present as an open scientific debate; evaluate the actual peer-reviewed evidence base for the specific claim, and say so explicitly if the evidence doesn't support it |
| HIIT vs. steady-state cardio for a specific population's cardiovascular outcome | Mixed | Teach the settled physiological mechanisms of each, then mark the transition to the genuinely open comparative-effectiveness question for that specific population |

## 6. Bloodborne pathogens exposure control — lab checklist

Required before any course session involving a fingerstick (lactate testing) or blood draw:

1. Written exposure control plan on file for the specific lab/course, reviewed annually (29 CFR 1910.1030(c)).
2. All students/staff performing the fingerstick trained on universal precautions and sharps handling before the session — training date logged.
3. Sharps container present, rated, and within reach at the point of use — not across the room.
4. Personal protective equipment (gloves at minimum) available and required for every fingerstick, no exceptions for "just a quick one."
5. Post-exposure protocol posted in the lab and reviewed verbally at the start of the term, not buried in a syllabus paragraph.
