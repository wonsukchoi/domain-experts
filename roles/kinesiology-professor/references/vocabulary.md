# Vocabulary

Terms of art in kinesiology/exercise-science academia that generalists misuse. Format per term: definition, how a practitioner actually uses it, the common misuse.

### CAAHEP vs. CoAES

- **Definition:** CAAHEP (Commission on Accreditation of Allied Health Education Programs) is the umbrella accrediting body; the Committee on Accreditation for the Exercise Sciences (CoAES) is the discipline-specific committee under it that writes and applies the standards for exercise science, exercise physiology, and personal fitness training programs.
- **In use:** "CAAHEP grants the accreditation, but CoAES is who actually wrote the standards we're being reviewed against."
- **Common misuse:** treating exercise-science program accreditation as a nice-to-have marketing credential rather than a standards-driven process with a defined review cycle and outcome thresholds.

### Preparticipation health screening algorithm

- **Definition:** ACSM's current (11th-edition *Guidelines*) decision process for whether medical clearance is needed before exercise, based on current activity level, known cardiovascular/metabolic/renal disease, symptoms, and desired intensity — it replaced the older three-tier low/moderate/high risk-stratification model.
- **In use:** "Run the algorithm, not a risk score — she's inactive with no symptoms, so light-to-moderate intensity clears without a physician referral."
- **Common misuse:** still applying the retired low/moderate/high risk-category framework, which the current reference text no longer contains.

### ACSM-EP (Certified Exercise Physiologist)

- **Definition:** an NCCA-accredited credentialing exam and certification for exercise physiologists, distinct from personal-training-level certifications in scope and required knowledge depth.
- **In use:** "The lab instructor needs to hold ACSM-EP or an equivalent exercise-physiology credential — a personal-training cert doesn't satisfy that course's faculty-qualification standard."
- **Common misuse:** treating any certification with "certified" in the name as interchangeable evidence of faculty qualification for a specific accredited course.

### Practicum vs. internship

- **Definition:** in most accredited program handbooks, a practicum is a lower-hour, often on-or-near-campus supervised experience earlier in the sequence; an internship is the higher-hour, off-site capstone placement — the two carry different minimum-hour and supervision requirements in the accreditor's outcomes reporting.
- **In use:** "That's practicum hours, not internship hours — it doesn't count toward the capstone placement total."
- **Common misuse:** using the terms interchangeably in a self-study report where the accreditor's form distinguishes them and expects different documentation for each.

### Credentialing-exam pass rate (and sit rate)

- **Definition:** the fraction of graduates who pass a national credentialing exam among those who sat for it — a distinct number from the sit rate (fraction of graduates who sat at all), since not every graduate is required to sit.
- **In use:** "11 of 19 who sat passed — 57.9% — but the sit rate itself, 61%, is right in line with prior cohorts, so the denominator isn't the story here."
- **Common misuse:** reporting the pass rate without stating the sit rate or cohort size, making a small-n swing look like a program-wide trend.

### PAR-Q+

- **Definition:** the Physical Activity Readiness Questionnaire (Plus), a standardized screening tool used to identify whether further screening or medical clearance is needed before exercise participation.
- **In use:** "Have them complete the PAR-Q+ before the assessment — a flagged answer routes to the full screening algorithm, not straight to the treadmill."
- **Common misuse:** treating a clean PAR-Q+ as blanket clearance for any intensity or population, rather than as the entry point into the fuller screening decision.

### Faculty qualifications (allied-health accreditation sense)

- **Definition:** the accreditor's requirement that the instructor of record for a specific course hold a credential or degree specialization matching that course's content, not just any terminal degree in a related field.
- **In use:** "He has a PhD in sport management, which is fine for the administration course, but it doesn't satisfy the faculty-qualification standard for the exercise physiology lab."
- **Common misuse:** assuming any terminal degree, regardless of subfield match, automatically satisfies a specialized accreditor's course-specific qualification standard.

### Psychomotor assessment / skill check-off

- **Definition:** grading a physical skill (CPR compressions, a spotting technique, a fitness-assessment protocol) against an observable-behavior checklist, as distinct from grading written or cognitive work.
- **In use:** "Check-offs are graded met/not-met against the calibrated checklist, not scored like an essay question."
- **Common misuse:** grading a skill demonstration with a single rater and no calibration, the way a paper gets read blind — which produces inconsistent pass/fail outcomes across sections.

### Affiliation agreement

- **Definition:** the signed document between the academic program and a practicum/internship site that names a qualified on-site supervisor of record and the terms of the placement, required before students can be placed and before hours count toward accredited outcomes.
- **In use:** "No affiliation agreement, no placement — even if the site manager verbally agreed to take a student."
- **Common misuse:** placing a student informally at a site based on a verbal agreement with a gym manager or coach who isn't a documented, credentialed supervisor of record.

### Self-study report

- **Definition:** the comprehensive document a program submits ahead of a CAAHEP/CoAES site visit, reporting outcomes data against the accreditor's own defined metrics and denominators.
- **In use:** "The self-study needs the outcomes table with n and the accreditor's cohort definition — not a paragraph about how proud we are of the program."
- **Common misuse:** writing it as institutional promotional narrative instead of evidence-based reporting against the accreditor's stated thresholds.

### Bloodborne Pathogens Standard / exposure control plan

- **Definition:** OSHA's 29 CFR 1910.1030, which requires a written exposure control plan, training, and engineering controls (sharps disposal, PPE) for any workplace or lab activity involving blood or other potentially infectious materials — including a fingerstick lactate test in an academic lab.
- **In use:** "The lactate-threshold lab needs an exposure control plan on file — it's a fingerstick, so it's in scope for 1910.1030 whether it's a hospital or a kinesiology classroom."
- **Common misuse:** assuming an academic kinesiology lab is exempt from OSHA's clinical bloodborne-pathogens rules because it isn't a clinical or hospital setting.

### Settled vs. open question (exercise-science pedagogy)

- **Definition:** a settled question has a discipline-wide consensus supported by mechanistic and meta-analytic evidence (progressive overload, caloric deficit for fat loss); an open question is a genuine, evidence-based disagreement without a consensus answer (which periodization model is optimal for a given population).
- **In use:** "Progressive overload is settled — I teach it as fact. Which periodization model beats the others for this specific sport is open — that's where I run the multiple-position model."
- **Common misuse:** treating a marketing or influencer-sourced supplement claim as though it belonged in the same "open debate" category as a genuinely evidence-based, discipline-recognized open question.

### NCCA accreditation (of the credentialing exam)

- **Definition:** third-party accreditation from the National Commission for Certifying Agencies confirming a certification exam meets defined psychometric and governance standards — the basis for treating a pass rate as a legitimate program-outcomes metric.
- **In use:** "We report the ACSM-EP pass rate because it's NCCA-accredited — a certificate-mill credential with no NCCA accreditation wouldn't count the same way in the self-study."
- **Common misuse:** assuming any certification with "certified" or "board" in its name carries the same evidentiary weight for outcomes reporting, regardless of whether it holds NCCA accreditation.
