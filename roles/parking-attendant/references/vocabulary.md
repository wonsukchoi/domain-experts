# Vocabulary

Terms generalists use loosely that a valet/parking operation keeps precise, because the precision is what a liability claim or an insurance audit actually turns on.

### Bailment

A legal relationship where one party (the bailor, the guest) temporarily transfers possession — not ownership — of property to another (the bailee, the valet operator) for a specific purpose, with an obligation to return it. Surrendering keys is the act that establishes it.

**In use:** "The second he handed over the keys, we were the bailee — the ticket stub isn't a receipt, it's evidence of when the bailment started."

**Common misuse:** treating the paper ticket itself as the source of liability ("we're only responsible because we gave them a ticket"). The liability attaches at key surrender and vehicle possession, with or without a ticket — a lost-ticket retrieval is still a bailment, just a harder one to prove.

### Care, custody, and control

The three elements a bailee is judged on: physical possession (custody), the ability to direct what happens to the item (control), and the duty owed while both are true (care). A gratuitous bailee (no fee, e.g. free courtesy parking) owes "slight care"; a bailee for hire (paid valet) owes at least "ordinary care."

**In use:** "This is a paid valet stand, not a courtesy drop-off — we owe ordinary care, which is a real bar, not a formality."

**Common misuse:** assuming "we didn't charge for parking" eliminates liability. Fee structure affects the standard of care owed, not whether a duty exists at all.

### Chain of custody (ticket-to-tag-to-vehicle)

The unbroken, verifiable link between a claim ticket number, the key tag bearing the same number, and the specific vehicle's location — the mechanism that lets any attendant, not just the one who parked it, retrieve the right car correctly.

**In use:** "Don't hand off on 'I remember where it is' — walk the chain: ticket 47, tag 47, section C row 2."

**Common misuse:** treating the chain as a formality once an attendant "knows the lot," which is exactly the overconfidence that produces a mismatch during a rush or a shift change.

### Dwell time

The expected duration a vehicle will remain parked before retrieval is requested — the variable that determines how deeply a vehicle can be stacked or blocked in without creating an unacceptable retrieval delay.

**In use:** "Zone C is overnight dwell, we can go three-deep there; Zone A is unpredictable restaurant dwell, keep it shallow."

**Common misuse:** confusing dwell time with total time on the lot. A car parked 8 hours with a known checkout time has more retrieval flexibility (predictable dwell) than a car parked 45 minutes with no notice before the guest wants it (unpredictable dwell), even though the second is shorter.

### Stacking / blocking-in

Parking one vehicle so it physically prevents another from leaving without first being moved — the mechanism that produces valet's density advantage, and the mechanism that produces its retrieval-time cost.

**In use:** "We're stacked three deep in that row — if 52 leaves before 51 and 50, that's two extra moves nobody planned for."

**Common misuse:** treating stacking as simply "parking tightly." The defining feature isn't tightness, it's the dependency it creates between vehicles' retrieval order.

### Retrieval time

The elapsed time from a guest's pickup request to the vehicle being delivered to them — the metric that actually determines guest experience and staffing adequacy, as opposed to the total number of cars processed over a shift.

**In use:** "Average retrieval is fine, but the 90th-percentile retrieval time during the 10:00 rush is what's driving complaints."

**Common misuse:** reporting only an average retrieval time. A stand can have a good average while a meaningful share of guests wait far longer during the actual peak minutes, which is what generates complaints and comped parking.

### Peak staffing ratio

The attendants-to-vehicles ratio needed specifically during arrival/departure surges (roughly 1:15 as an industry rule of thumb), as distinct from the steady-state ratio (roughly 1:25-30) appropriate the rest of the shift.

**In use:** "We're staffed for steady-state all night — that's fine until the banquet lets out, then we need the peak ratio for 45 minutes, not the whole shift."

**Common misuse:** applying one ratio across an entire shift, either overstaffing quiet hours or understaffing the specific peak window, instead of flexing headcount to the demand curve.

### Density gain (valet vs. self-park)

The measurable increase in vehicles fitted per unit of lot area that valet achieves versus self-park, because valet needs only a walking lane for a trained driver rather than a full door-swing-and-backout aisle for every guest — industry figures cited around 40-60% more capacity in the same footprint.

**In use:** "The client's paying for valet because the lot physically can't fit their guest list self-park — the density gain is the business case, not the curb appeal."

**Common misuse:** describing valet purely as a convenience or luxury amenity. For undersized lots it's a hard capacity constraint being solved, not a service upsell.

### Claim ticket

The numbered stub given to the guest at intake, which functions as both a retrieval credential and, in a dispute, as evidence tying a specific vehicle and timestamp to the intake condition record.

**In use:** "Get me the intake photos for ticket 47 before anyone says anything to the guest about the dent."

**Common misuse:** treating the ticket as merely a retrieval convenience. Its evidentiary function in a damage or loss dispute is at least as important as its logistics function.

### Incident report

A same-shift written record of a damage claim, delay, lost ticket, or other exception — guest and employee statements, vehicle identifiers, photos, and timestamps — filed while witnesses and details are still fresh.

**In use:** "File the report tonight, not tomorrow — by tomorrow the guest's story and the attendant's memory will have both drifted."

**Common misuse:** treating incident reports as only necessary when the guest formally complains. The value of a same-shift report is highest precisely for borderline incidents that could escalate later, when the report becomes the only contemporaneous account.

### Gratuitous bailee vs. bailee for hire

The two liability tiers in bailment law: a gratuitous bailee (no fee involved, e.g. a free courtesy shuttle lot) owes only "slight care"; a bailee for hire (a paid valet operation, the near-universal commercial case) owes at least "ordinary care" — a meaningfully higher legal standard.

**In use:** "We charge for this stand, so we're a bailee for hire — 'we did our best' doesn't clear the ordinary-care bar the way it might for a free lot."

**Common misuse:** assuming the distinction is about how much effort was put in, rather than a specific legal standard tied to whether a fee was charged for the custody service.
