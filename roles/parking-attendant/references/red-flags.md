# Red flags

Smell tests a valet supervisor catches before they become a claim, a lost car, or a lawsuit.

### 1. Intake ticket has no damage notes, or only "some wear" / "checked, OK"

**Usually means:** the walkaround was skipped or rushed under volume — the exact condition (a busy stand) that makes documentation matter most is when it gets cut.

**First question:** "Walk me through what you actually looked at on this car before you took the keys."

**Data to pull:** the last 20 intake tickets from that shift — count how many have specific, located damage descriptions versus generic or blank ones. A pattern of blanks during rushes, not just this one ticket, is the real problem.

### 2. A key hung on an unnumbered peg, or two keys sharing one tag number

**Usually means:** the chain of custody broke under time pressure — someone skipped the tag-to-ticket match to move faster, which is precisely how a wrong-car handoff happens later.

**First question:** "Which ticket number does this key belong to, and where's that written down?"

**Data to pull:** current key box against the stand log — every hung key should have exactly one matching open ticket. Any mismatch gets resolved before another handoff happens, not at end of shift.

### 3. Retrieval queue backing up past ~10-15 minutes during a known peak window

**Usually means:** the section is staffed at a steady-state ratio (roughly 1 per 25-30 cars) during a peak that needs the peak ratio (roughly 1 per 15) — an averaging error, not a work-ethic problem.

**First question:** "How many attendants do we have on this section right now, and how many vehicles are in it?"

**Data to pull:** vehicles in the affected zone, attendants currently assigned, average retrieval time per car this shift versus the stand's historical average — if retrieval time itself hasn't slowed, it's a staffing-ratio problem; if retrieval time has slowed too, check for a stacking/blocking-in configuration that's gridlocking the lot.

### 4. A damage claim comes in more than a few hours after the vehicle was returned

**Usually means:** either a genuine mark the guest didn't notice until better light/a car wash, or a mark that occurred after the vehicle left the lot (a second parking event, a scrape elsewhere) being routed back to the valet as the last controlled custody point.

**First question:** "Do we have intake and return-condition photos for this ticket number, and what's the timestamp gap between return and the claim?"

**Data to pull:** intake photos, return-time log entry, any photos taken at handoff (if the stand's policy includes a return walkaround) — the intake record is the operator's entire defense here, and its absence is read against the operator, not neutrally.

### 5. An attendant moves an unfamiliar or manual-transmission vehicle at normal speed

**Usually means:** the attendant is prioritizing looking competent over the deliberate creep-and-confirm step the situation calls for — this is the single highest-risk moment in an otherwise low-speed job.

**First question:** "Had you driven this exact drivetrain before you pulled out?"

**Data to pull:** incident/near-miss log filtered for manual-transmission or unusual-drivetrain vehicles — if these vehicles are overrepresented in stalls, rolls, or minor contact incidents relative to their share of total cars parked, the intake process needs a mandatory slow-move step, not more general caution reminders.

### 6. Stacking depth in a zone exceeds what the expected dwell time supports

**Usually means:** the lot chased the density gain (three-deep stacking) without matching it to a dwell time long enough to make unstacking planned rather than reactive — turning the capacity advantage into a retrieval-time liability.

**First question:** "What's the expected departure time for the car at the back of this stack, and do we actually know it, or are we guessing?"

**Data to pull:** dwell-time assumptions per zone versus actual departure timestamps logged over the last month — zones where actual departures are more unpredictable than assumed are candidates for shallower stacking.

### 7. The same claim-ticket number gets reused before the first vehicle tied to it has left the lot

**Usually means:** a ticket book or system reset error, or a rushed attendant grabbing the next available tag without checking it's actually free.

**First question:** "Is ticket #[X] still an open vehicle on the lot right now?"

**Data to pull:** open-ticket list against the physical key box — any duplicate number is an active mismatch risk and needs immediate reconciliation, not end-of-shift cleanup.

### 8. An attendant settles or apologizes for damage on the spot, before a supervisor compares the intake record

**Usually means:** good intentions creating a bad outcome — an informal on-the-spot admission or promise can be treated as the operator's position even if the intake record later shows the mark predates the valet's custody.

**First question:** "Has anyone compared this to the intake photos yet, or did we respond before checking?"

**Data to pull:** the intake record for that ticket, and whatever was said or offered to the guest in the moment — get it in writing in the incident report before it becomes the operator's de facto stance.

### 9. Guest arrives with no ticket and no ID matching the vehicle's registration

**Usually means:** most often a genuinely lost ticket from a legitimate guest; less often, an attempted theft testing whether the stand verifies before handing over keys.

**First question:** "Can this person positively describe or identify the specific vehicle, beyond make and color, before we pull any key?"

**Data to pull:** the stand log's list of still-on-lot vehicles cross-referenced by description, and whatever ID or reservation record ties the person to that vehicle — no key moves until this reconciles.
