# Playbook

Filled operational sequences a stand actually runs, with real thresholds and example numbers — not descriptions of what a good process would include.

## 1. Vehicle intake (every car, every time)

1. **Write the ticket first.** Time in, guest name/room or table if applicable, ticket number.
2. **Match the key tag to the ticket number** before touching the car — hang no key on an unnumbered peg, ever, even "just for a second."
3. **Walkaround, same sequence every time**: start front-left corner, move clockwise. Wide shot of each side, close-up of any existing mark (scratch, dent, cracked trim), plate visible in at least one frame, odometer/fuel gauge if the lot's policy tracks it.
4. **Log condition on the ticket stub or digital log**: "existing: 4-inch scuff rear bumper driver side, hairline crack lower windshield passenger corner." Vague notes ("some wear") are functionally the same as no note in a dispute.
5. **Ask about drivetrain/quirks only if visibly non-standard** (manual shifter, unusual electronic parking brake, keyless start you don't recognize) — confirm before moving the car, not while already rolling.
6. **Move the vehicle deliberately if unfamiliar**: creep forward/back 10 feet first to confirm clutch bite and brake feel before committing to a ramp, tight turn, or incline.

## 2. Density and stacking plan (set before the rush, not during it)

**Tiered zones by expected dwell time (filled example, hotel valet with attached restaurant):**

| Zone | Expected dwell | Placement | Stacking |
|---|---|---|---|
| A — restaurant/bar guests | 1-3 hrs, unpredictable exact departure | Nearest lane, minimal blocking | None to one-deep |
| B — event/banquet guests | Known end time (banquet schedule) | Mid-lot | Two-deep, pre-staged for the known letout |
| C — hotel overnight | 8+ hrs, retrieval time flexible | Furthest/tightest spots | Three-deep or bumper-to-bumper, unstacked only on checkout request |

**The math the zones are built on:** valet's real capacity gain over self-park comes from needing only a walking lane for one trained driver instead of a full door-swing-and-backout aisle for every guest — industry figures put this at roughly 40-60% more vehicles in the same footprint. That gain is borrowed against retrieval time: every car placed behind another means whoever's behind it waits for the unstack. Zone C absorbs the deepest stacking because its dwell time gives the stand hours of notice to unstack in advance; Zone A gets the shallowest stacking because its departures are the least predictable.

**Pre-staging for a known departure (banquet ends 10:00 PM, 84 cars in Zone B):** starting at 9:30 PM, begin moving the vehicles expected to leave first (identified by table/seating chart or guest request at drop-off) to the most accessible spots in the zone. This is the difference between a 30-second and a 5-minute retrieval for the same car — the car doesn't get faster, its position does.

## 3. Peak staffing calculation (filled example)

**Inputs:** 84 vehicles in a section, average 3.5 attendant-minutes per retrieval (walk + verify tag + drive + handoff), peak window is the first 30-40 minutes after a banquet ends, each attendant has ~40 productive minutes/hour after breaks and radio time.

**Step 1 — total work required:** 84 cars × 3.5 min = 294 attendant-minutes.

**Step 2 — capacity per attendant:** 40 productive minutes/hour.

**Step 3 — rule-of-thumb check:** peak staffing guideline is roughly 1 attendant per 15 vehicles during arrival/departure peaks (widening to 1 per 25-30 in steady state). 84 ÷ 15 ≈ 5.6 → staff 6.

**Step 4 — reconcile:** at 6 attendants × 40 min = 240 attendant-minutes/hour of capacity against 294 minutes of work, the section clears in about 1 hour 15 minutes — still over an hour, but the guideline exists because the *first* 20-30 minutes of that window is when most guests are queued simultaneously; understaffing to "the average works out" produces a visible line even if the total labor hours pencil out over a longer window.

**Step 5 — decision:** pull 2-3 attendants from self-park or a second shift to cover the 45-minute peak window specifically, then release them back once the queue clears rather than staffing the whole shift at peak level.

## 4. Damage-dispute resolution sequence

1. **Never negotiate or apologize-as-admission on the spot.** Acknowledge the guest's concern, state that it will be compared against the intake record, and escalate to a supervisor within the same interaction.
2. **Pull the intake record for that ticket number** — photos, written notes, timestamp.
3. **Compare specifically**: location on the vehicle, size/shape, whether it appears in the intake photos at all. A mark in a different location than claimed, or a mark visibly present in an intake photo, resolves most disputes without further escalation.
4. **If the intake record is missing or inconclusive**, this becomes a claims/insurance decision, not a stand-level one — route to the operator's liability coverage (NPA-member operators typically carry $1M legal liability / $5M general liability as a baseline) rather than deciding value on the spot.
5. **File the incident report the same shift** regardless of outcome: guest and employee statements, vehicle identifiers (VIN, plate, make/model/year/color), photos, timestamps, contact information. A report written the next day loses the witnesses and the freshness that make it useful.

## 5. Lost-ticket / no-ticket retrieval (fallback sequence, in order)

1. **Verify identity** (ID matching name on any reservation/registration tied to the vehicle) before touching the key box.
2. **Cross-reference by vehicle description** (make/model/color) against the stand log's list of what's still on the lot, not against memory.
3. **Locate the matching key tag by the vehicle's logged ticket number**, confirmed against the stand log, before pulling the key.
4. **Have the guest positively identify the vehicle** (not just point at "the black SUV") before handoff.
5. **Log the exception** — no-ticket retrievals are exactly the incidents that need a paper trail, since they bypass the primary chain-of-custody check.
