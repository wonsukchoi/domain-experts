# Working vocabulary

Terms of art practitioners use precisely, that generalists misuse. Load this when writing or reviewing content that needs to sound like an actual postmaster/mail superintendent, not a generalist describing one.

### Chain of custody
The documented, unbroken record of who had control of a mail item at each point from acceptance to delivery, especially critical for registered, certified, or high-value items.
**In use:** "Custody breaks at the transfer between processing and the delivery unit — that's where the investigation needs to focus."
**Common misuse:** Treated as relevant only for lost-item investigations after the fact — chain-of-custody documentation and controls exist to prevent breaks and to make investigation possible, and gaps in the documentation itself (not just a confirmed loss) are a finding worth addressing.

### Delivery point
An individual address or mail-receiving location on a route, the base unit for route planning and workload measurement.
**In use:** "This route's grown from 420 to 468 delivery points without a rebalance — that's an 11% increase the route plan hasn't caught up with."
**Common misuse:** Conflated with volume — delivery-point count and total volume are different numbers that can move independently (points growing while per-point volume declines is common as letter mail shifts to package delivery).

### Sortation
The automated or manual process of sorting mail by destination/route, typically the processing-stage bottleneck point in a facility.
**In use:** "Sortation unit 3 is running degraded — that's the actual bottleneck, not carrier performance."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to always keep pace regardless of equipment status — a degraded or down sortation unit can create a delay that looks, downstream, like a delivery/carrier performance issue if timing isn't traced back to its actual source stage.

### Registered / certified mail
Mail classes with enhanced chain-of-custody tracking and (for registered mail specifically) insurance/security handling, distinct from standard tracked packages.
**In use:** "That's registered mail — it gets the enhanced custody handling protocol, not the standard package tracking process."
**Common misuse:** Treated as equivalent to any tracked package — registered mail specifically carries additional legal/security handling requirements (often including dual-custody handling) beyond standard tracking.

### Peak season volume mix
The composition of mail classes (letters, flats, packages) during high-volume periods (notably year-end holidays), often meaningfully different from standard-day mix.
**In use:** "Peak mix skews heavily toward packages — that's a different bottleneck (parcel sortation, vehicle cargo capacity) than our standard-day letter-heavy mix."
**Common misuse:** Peak planning done by scaling standard-day capacity proportionally — if the mix shifts (more packages relative to letters), the actual constraint shifts too, and a proportional scale-up of standard-day capacity may not address the real peak-season bottleneck.

### Route rebalancing
Periodic adjustment of route boundaries and assignments to reflect current delivery-point count and volume, distinct from a one-time route design done at creation.
**In use:** "This route's overdue for rebalancing — carrier's been running over standard shift time for two months."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a one-time setup activity rather than an ongoing maintenance process — routes drift from their original design assumptions as addresses are added and volume mix shifts, and periodic rebalancing is what keeps route assignments matched to actual current workload.

### Dual custody
A handling protocol requiring two authorized individuals to jointly access or transfer high-value/tracked mail, designed specifically to address insider risk.
**In use:** "High-value parcels get dual-custody handling at every transfer point — no single person has unsupervised access."
**Common misuse:** Applied inconsistently or skipped for convenience during busy periods — the protocol's value depends on consistent application; an exception made "just this once" during a peak period is exactly when the control is most needed.

### On-time delivery performance
The percentage of mail delivered within the applicable service standard timeframe, tracked as the primary service-quality metric — always meaningful only alongside stage-level timing data.
**In use:** "On-time is down to 89%, but that number alone doesn't tell us where the delay is — pull the stage breakdown."
**Common misuse:** Treated as sufficient information on its own to diagnose a problem — the aggregate percentage tells you performance changed, not where in the process it changed, which is the information actually needed to fix it.
