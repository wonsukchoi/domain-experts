# Vocabulary

Terms of art a generalist either doesn't distinguish or gets backwards.

### Energy Release Component (ERC) vs. Burning Index (BI)

**ERC** is a number related to the available energy (BTU per square foot) in the flaming front at the head of a fire — driven mainly by the dryness of larger, slower-drying fuels (100-hr and 1,000-hr classes) and changes slowly, day to day. **BI** combines ERC with Spread Component (how fast a fire's perimeter is likely to grow) into a single number closely related to flame length in feet times ten — it reacts faster to short-term wind and fine-fuel changes than ERC does.

**In use:** "ERC's been climbing steadily for two weeks — that's the fuel-dryness story. BI jumped 15 points overnight because the wind forecast changed, not because the fuels got drier."

**Common misuse:** treating ERC and BI as interchangeable "fire danger" numbers, or reacting to a BI spike as if it reflects a fuel-dryness change when it's often a wind-forecast artifact.

### Fuel moisture timelag class

The time (in hours) a fuel of a given size takes to reach roughly two-thirds of the way toward equilibrium with ambient humidity: 1-hour (grass, needles, small twigs), 10-hour (branches to ~1 in.), 100-hour (~1-3 in.), 1,000-hour (larger downed logs). Larger classes integrate a longer weather window and change more slowly.

**In use:** "1-hour fuels came back up overnight with the dew, but 1,000-hour is still critically dry from three weeks with no rain — that's what's going to carry a crown fire if this ignites."

**Common misuse:** judging overall fire danger from how dry the ground litter (1-hr) looks, which can recover in a single humid night while the large-fuel component that actually sustains a major fire hasn't moved.

### Industrial Fire Precaution Level (IFPL)

A tiered (typically four-level) regulatory system used by wildland fire agencies to restrict logging/industrial forestland operations — hours, fire watch, permitted equipment — based on current fire-danger index readings, posted daily during fire season.

**In use:** "We're in IFPL III today — cable yarding is 2000 to 1300 only, and fire watch stays on continuously through the gust window."

**Common misuse:** treating IFPL as a single on/off "fire season" switch rather than a graduated system where each tier carries distinct, specific operational requirements that change independently of whether the tier itself changes.

### Crossover / "30-30-30"

The point at which temperature, relative humidity, and wind speed simultaneously cross thresholds associated with a sharp escalation in fire behavior (a commonly cited rule of thumb: temp ≥30°C, RH ≤30%, sustained wind ≥30 km/h; NWS Red Flag criteria are the regionally calibrated equivalent). Crossover conditions are when control lines that held the day before can fail and spot fires become likely.

**In use:** "We're not at crossover on the sustained wind, but the gusts are hitting the wind leg intermittently — that's still enough to upgrade the fire watch."

**Common misuse:** treating crossover as a fixed hard number rather than a regional/fuel-type-calibrated threshold, or requiring all three legs to be sustained simultaneously when intermittent gust crossover already changes fire behavior.

### Origin vs. point of origin vs. cause

**Origin** (or general area of origin) is the broader area where the fire is determined to have started, established from perimeter burn-pattern indicators working inward. **Point of origin** is the specific, narrower location within that area pinpointed by the finest-grained indicators. **Cause** is the ignition source and circumstances — a separate determination made only after origin is fixed, never assumed from it.

**In use:** "We fixed the general area of origin to about a quarter-acre pull-off, narrowed the point of origin to the gravel shoulder near the guardrail, but the cause is still undetermined — no accelerant or device evidence recovered."

**Common misuse:** collapsing "origin" and "cause" into one step, or announcing a cause before the origin work is complete, which taints the rest of the investigation with a pre-formed theory.

### Negative corpus

An invalid investigative methodology: concluding an incendiary (intentionally set) cause solely because no accidental ignition source was identified, rather than from affirmative physical evidence of an incendiary act. Rejected in modern fire-investigation standards for both structural and wildland fires.

**In use:** "We can't rule in an accidental cause, but that doesn't rule in incendiary either — without device or accelerant evidence, this stays undetermined."

**Common misuse:** using "we checked everything else and nothing fit" as if it were itself evidence of arson, when it's actually evidence of an incomplete or contaminated investigation.

### Defensible space (immediate / intermediate / extended zone)

The graduated fuel-management area around a structure, per NFPA 1144: immediate zone (0-5 ft, noncombustible materials only), intermediate zone (5-30 ft, reduced/spaced vegetation), extended zone (30-100+ ft, thinned canopy and surface fuel reduction). Distinct from a fuel break, which is a linear strategic feature, not a structure-centered zone system.

**In use:** "Zone 1's clean, but the extended zone has continuous ladder fuel from the deck to the tree line 70 feet out — that's the finding that matters for ember exposure."

**Common misuse:** treating "defensible space" as satisfied by clearing brush immediately around the house (zone 1 only) while ignoring the extended zone, which is where crown-fire and long-range ember exposure is actually mitigated.

### Prescribed fire vs. wildfire vs. wildland-urban interface (WUI) fire

**Prescribed fire** is an intentionally ignited fire under an approved burn plan with defined objectives and weather trigger points. **Wildfire** is any unplanned, unwanted fire on wildland, regardless of cause. **WUI fire** specifically describes a wildfire that reaches the zone where structures and wildland fuel intermix — the term describes exposure geography, not cause or ignition type.

**In use:** "This started as an escaped debris burn — it's a wildfire now, not a prescribed fire, the moment it left the planned unit boundary. It became a WUI incident once it crossed into the subdivision fuel break."

**Common misuse:** using "wildfire" and "forest fire" interchangeably with "wildland-urban interface fire," which specifically flags structure exposure and triggers a different response posture (structure protection resources, evacuation coordination) than a wildland-only incident.

### Spot fire vs. spotting distance

A **spot fire** is a new fire ignited outside the perimeter of the main fire by a wind-carried ember or firebrand. **Spotting distance** is how far that ember traveled — a function of fuel type, plume height, and wind speed, and the single biggest reason a fireline that "should have held" doesn't.

**In use:** "We lost the line not because the crew failed to hold it — spotting distance under those wind gusts was well past our anchor point before we could react."

**Common misuse:** attributing a line loss to crew performance or line construction when the actual cause was spotting distance exceeding the line's buffer, which is a fire-behavior/weather problem, not a tactics problem.

### Fire watch

A person or crew stationed to monitor an operating or recently shut-down equipment site for ignition, for a specified duration set by the current precaution level (e.g., 1-hour, 3-hour, or continuous during elevated-risk windows).

**In use:** "Fire watch stays on for three hours after the last saw shuts down — that's not a suggestion, it's the IFPL II requirement for this operation type."

**Common misuse:** treating fire watch duration as a single fixed number rather than a requirement that scales with the current precaution tier and can be independently extended for forecast wind gusts.

### Cause classification confidence levels

The graded set of conclusions a wildland cause determination can reach: natural (confirmed, e.g., lightning with strike-locator correlation), accidental (specific mechanism identified), human-caused/undetermined ignition source (human origin established, mechanism not), and undetermined (origin or cause evidence insufficient). Not a binary "natural vs. human" call.

**In use:** "The strike-locator data puts a confirmed lightning strike within 200 meters and two minutes of the estimated ignition time — that's a natural, confirmed classification, not just a probable one."

**Common misuse:** reporting a cause as more specific than the confidence level the evidence supports, or treating "human-caused" and "arson" as synonyms when the former only establishes that the ignition wasn't natural.
