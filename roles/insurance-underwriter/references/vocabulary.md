# Underwriting working vocabulary

Terms an underwriter uses precisely and generalists conflate. Format: definition → how a practitioner says it → common misuse.

**Loss ratio vs. combined ratio** — Loss ratio = incurred losses ÷ earned premium. Combined ratio adds the expense ratio (and policyholder dividends if any) to loss ratio; under 100% is an underwriting profit before investment income.
In use: "We're at 74% loss ratio but 103% combined — the expense load on this class is the problem, not the losses."
Misuse: quoting loss ratio alone as "profitable" or "unprofitable" without the expense ratio; a 70% loss ratio is comfortable at a 25% expense load and a loser at a 40% one.

**Permissible loss ratio** — 1 minus the expense ratio minus the target underwriting profit provision; the loss ratio a class or account needs to hit to be adequately priced, not a fixed industry number.
In use: "Permissible here is 65% given our expense load — 60.6% blended is fine, no rate action needed."
Misuse: treating 60% (or any single number) as universally "good" without checking it against the carrier's own expense structure for that class.

**Credibility (Z-factor)** — The weight given to an account's own loss experience versus the class manual/bureau rate, scaled by claim volume against a full-credibility standard.
In use: "Z is 11% on this account — the manual rate is doing almost all the work; don't let one bad year move the mod much."
Misuse: pricing a small account entirely off its own three-year history, or assuming credibility only matters for "problem" accounts — it governs every renewal's rate change, good or bad years alike.

**Experience mod (modification factor)** — A multiplicative debit or credit applied to the manual rate, derived from the credibility-weighted comparison of account experience to expected experience.
In use: "The account's experience mod came in at 1.045 — a 4.5% debit, not the 44% the raw loss ratio implied."
Misuse: confusing the workers'-comp-specific NCCI Experience Rating Plan mod (a regulated, filed calculation) with an informal in-house experience mod used for GL/property — they follow similar logic but aren't interchangeable or equally auditable.

**Increased limits factor (ILF)** — The multiplier applied to a basic-limits manual rate to price a higher liability limit, reflecting that severity — not frequency — drives the additional cost of higher limits.
In use: "ILF to $2M/$2M off our $1M/$1M base is 1.16 for this class."
Misuse: assuming ILFs scale linearly with limit (they don't — they flatten at higher limits because large-severity tail risk is already mostly captured lower in the limit structure).

**Schedule rating** — A discretionary, underwriter-applied credit or debit (commonly ±25%) layered on top of experience/manual rating to reflect account-specific risk-management quality not captured by loss history alone.
In use: "We're crediting 7% for the safety program, but it's due for re-verification — that credit shouldn't just carry forward."
Misuse: treating schedule credits as permanent once granted, rather than evidence that needs periodic re-verification.

**Deductible vs. self-insured retention (SIR)** — A deductible is subtracted from the carrier's payment on a claim the carrier still handles; an SIR means the insured (or a TPA on its behalf) handles and pays the claim up to the retention before the policy responds, and typically requires posted collateral.
In use: "At $50K we'd need this on an SIR basis with collateral — at $25K it stays a deductible, no collateral trigger."
Misuse: treating the two as interchangeable ways to "raise the retention" — the collateral and claims-handling implications for the insured are materially different even at similar dollar levels.

**Loss elimination ratio (LER)** — The share of expected losses removed from the carrier's layer by raising the retention from one level to another.
In use: "LER at $50K SIR is 35% — the premium credit should track that, not just the broker's ask."
Misuse: pricing a retention change off the premium reduction the broker wants rather than the actual loss-cost math the LER represents.

**Rate-on-line (ROL)** — Premium divided by the limit of the layer being priced; a shorthand for how expensive a layer of coverage or reinsurance is relative to its size.
In use: "0.76% ROL on the $5M xs $1M umbrella, held flat given the severity tail on this class."
Misuse: comparing ROL across layers with different attachment points or loss profiles as if it were a single scale — a low ROL high in a program can be adequately priced while the same ROL low in the program is not.

**Attachment point** — The loss dollar amount at which a given layer of coverage (or reinsurance) begins to respond.
In use: "The umbrella attaches at $1M — the primary GL limit has to be exhausted first."
Misuse: confusing attachment point with the policy's deductible; attachment describes where a layer starts, not a retained amount within a single layer.

**Treaty vs. facultative reinsurance** — Treaty reinsurance cedes risk automatically under a standing agreement covering a class of business, up to defined capacity; facultative reinsurance is negotiated risk-by-risk, used when a single risk exceeds treaty capacity, falls outside its terms, or the ceding company wants risk-specific terms.
In use: "This one's over our treaty's automatic capacity on a PML basis — we need a facultative certificate for the excess."
Misuse: assuming any risk within the treaty's stated TIV limit is automatically covered — cat-exposed property is often capped on a modeled-PML basis, a separate and usually tighter constraint than the TIV limit.

**Probable maximum loss (PML) vs. total insured value (TIV)** — TIV is the maximum possible loss (full replacement value); PML is the modeled loss at a given return period (e.g., 1-in-250-year), almost always well below TIV for a single event.
In use: "TIV is $18.4M but modeled 250-year PML is $11.4M — that's the number that matters for treaty capacity."
Misuse: using TIV as the basis for cat-capacity decisions instead of the modeled PML at the return period the treaty is actually written to.

**Adverse selection vs. moral hazard** — Adverse selection is a selection-stage problem: insureds who know they're higher risk are more likely to buy or renew, especially when price signals a broad increase. Moral hazard is a behavior-change problem: being insured changes how carefully the insured avoids loss.
In use: "The renewal increase is going to select against us — our better risks have real alternatives and will leave first."
Misuse: using the terms interchangeably; a low deductible mainly creates moral hazard (less incentive to prevent small losses), while a uniform rate increase across a heterogeneous book mainly creates adverse selection (the wrong accounts stay).

**Binding authority / referral** — The premium, limit, and risk-characteristic thresholds within which an underwriter can commit the carrier without sign-off; a referral escalates a risk that trips any threshold to a senior underwriter or committee.
In use: "Loss ratio and the PML breach both trip mandatory referral here, independent of the authority room I have on TIV."
Misuse: treating authority limits as soft guidance rather than triggers, or structuring a submission (splitting limits, adjusting reported TIV) to stay just under a threshold instead of referring.
