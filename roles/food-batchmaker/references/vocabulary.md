# Vocabulary

### Water activity (aw)
A measure (0 to 1.0) of how much water in a food product is actually available for microbial growth and chemical reactions, distinct from total moisture content.
**In use:** "Moisture's at 15% either way, but water activity is what tells us if this is actually shelf-stable — check the meter, not just the moisture spec."
**Common misuse:** Treating moisture percentage as an interchangeable substitute for water activity — two formulations can share a moisture percentage while differing significantly in water activity depending on how much sugar, salt, or other humectant is binding that water.

### Baker's percentage
A formulation convention (mainly in baking) expressing every ingredient as a percentage of the total flour weight (flour = 100%), making formulas easy to scale by weight.
**In use:** "This dough's at 60% hydration — that's the water weight as a percentage of flour weight, not of the total batch."
**Common misuse:** Confusing baker's percentage with percentage of total batch weight — a 60% hydration dough isn't 60% water by total formula weight, it's 60% of the flour weight specifically, and misreading the base changes every calculation downstream.

### Working time
The elapsed time between completing a mixing or preparation step and the product reaching its next process step (baking, packaging, chilling) — a process variable distinct from, and not automatically scaled with, batch size.
**In use:** "Working time went from 8 minutes at pilot scale to 35 at production — that's the actual driver of the density difference, not the formula."
**Common misuse:** Assuming working time scales proportionally with batch size the same way ingredient weights do — larger batches often take disproportionately longer to portion and move through downstream handling, and that extra time can matter for time-sensitive ingredients even when nothing about the formula itself changed.

### Double-acting leavening
A chemical leavening system (like most commercial baking powders) with two reaction phases — an initial fast reaction on contact with liquid, and a second, slower reaction triggered by oven heat.
**In use:** "We lost fast-reacting capacity to the extended dwell time before the oven — that's why the double-acting powder didn't give us the rise we expected."
**Common misuse:** Assuming all the leavening gas a formula's baking powder can produce is still available at bake time regardless of how long the dough or batter sat beforehand — the fast-reacting portion depletes over room-temperature dwell time, independent of the total amount added.

### Scale-up
The process of increasing a formulation from a smaller (pilot or lab) batch size to full production batch size, which can change process behavior (mixing shear, working time, heat transfer) even when ingredient ratios are preserved exactly.
**In use:** "Scale-up isn't just multiplying the recipe by ten — we're verifying the process timing at production scale before we call this formula finalized."
**Common misuse:** Treating scale-up as solved once ingredient ratios are correctly multiplied — process parameters (mix time, shear, dwell time) frequently need independent verification at the new scale, not just proportional ingredient math.

### Clean-in-place (CIP)
An automated or semi-automated cleaning process that sanitizes production equipment (tanks, lines, mixers) without full disassembly, used between batches — critical for allergen and cross-contamination control.
**In use:** "Full CIP cycle before and after the allergen batch — that's not optional, even with the schedule running behind."
**Common misuse:** Treating a quick rinse or partial CIP cycle as equivalent to a full validated cleaning cycle — allergen and pathogen control depends on the CIP cycle actually completing its full validated parameters, not just visually appearing clean.

### Allergen sequencing
Scheduling production runs so allergen-free or lower-risk products run before allergen-containing products on shared equipment, minimizing cross-contact risk and changeover burden.
**In use:** "We run the nut-free line items first thing in the morning, then the peanut-containing batch last, with full changeover after."
**Common misuse:** Treating allergen sequencing as a scheduling convenience that can flex when the day's plan changes — the sequencing exists specifically to manage cross-contact risk, and reordering it without the required full changeover reintroduces the risk it was designed to prevent.

### Functional endpoint (mixing)
The actual target state of a mix (a specific consistency, gluten development, or emulsification) that a mixing process is meant to reach, distinct from a fixed elapsed-time setpoint used as a proxy for it.
**In use:** "We're mixing to the functional endpoint — target consistency — not just running the timer, because this mixer's blade wear has changed how fast it gets there."
**Common misuse:** Treating an elapsed-time setpoint as the actual goal rather than a proxy for it — a time-based setpoint is only valid as long as equipment condition and batch characteristics match what the setpoint was originally calibrated against.

### Ingredient risk category
An informal but functionally real classification of a formulation's ingredients by how much consequence a weight deviation carries — bulk structural ingredients at the low-consequence end, allergen-declared/safety-critical/regulated ingredients at the high-consequence end.
**In use:** "Flour's within a kilogram either way, no problem — but the preservative needs to be dead-on, it's a different risk category entirely."
**Common misuse:** Applying a single uniform tolerance percentage across an entire formulation regardless of which ingredient it's applied to — the consequence of a given percentage deviation varies enormously by what the ingredient actually does in the formula.

### Batch record
The documented record of a specific batch's ingredients, weights, process parameters, and any deviations/corrective actions, serving as the production and food-safety traceability record.
**In use:** "Check the batch record before you release it — does it show mix time, water activity, and sign-off, or is something missing?"
**Common misuse:** Treating the batch record as paperwork completed after the fact rather than a real-time record — a batch record reconstructed after the batch is finished loses its value as an accurate traceability document.
