# Red flags

Smell tests a painting contractor catches during a walk-through or a bid review, before a crew shows up. Format for each: what it usually means, the first question to ask, and the data to pull before scheduling or pricing.

### Blistering appears 2–4 months after a repaint

- **Usually means:** moisture trapped behind the film — an active leak, poor drainage, or painting over a substrate that was above its moisture ceiling at application. Second most likely: a non-breathable coating applied over a substrate that needs to vent vapor.
- **First question:** what was the moisture meter reading before painting, and is there an active leak source nearby (flashing, gutter, grade)?
- **Data to pull:** pre-job moisture log if one exists, flashing/gutter inspection, warranty callback history for the same wall.

### Alligator/reticulated cracking on an exterior wall

- **Usually means:** an oil-based topcoat applied over existing latex (rigid film over a flexible one, splitting as the substrate moves) or an oil film that's simply aged past its flexibility limit. Second most likely: a single very thick coat that mud-cracked during cure.
- **First question:** what's the paint system history on this wall — oil or latex, and when was it last recoated?
- **Data to pull:** solvent-rub test result, prior invoice or spec sheet if available, a sample scrape to count film layers.

### Existing film chalks heavily (white powder transfers to a rubbed finger) within 2 years

- **Usually means:** dry film thickness was under-spec — a single coat where two were specified — or an interior-grade/low-grade product was used outdoors.
- **First question:** what was the coat count and spread rate on the last job?
- **Data to pull:** manufacturer TDS for the product used, invoiced gallon quantity checked against area ÷ spread rate.

### House built before 1978 with no lead documentation on file

- **Usually means:** the EPA RRP Rule's presumption-of-lead applies by default, and the job needs certified-firm work practices before any prep that disturbs painted surfaces past the threshold.
- **First question:** is there a certified inspector's negative test on file, or do we presume positive and proceed under RRP practices?
- **Data to pull:** EPA Lead-Safe certified firm number, renovator certification, signed owner opt-out waiver if owner-occupied with no children under 6 or pregnant occupants.

### Moisture meter reads above 15% on wood siding at the scheduled painting date

- **Usually means:** an active leak, poor site drainage, or insufficient dry-out time after a pressure wash.
- **First question:** is this a chronic wet spot, or residual moisture from washing two days ago?
- **Data to pull:** meter readings at multiple points and depths, days since last rain or wash, condition of adjacent flashing and caulk joints.

### Concrete slab tests above 80% RH (ASTM F2170) or above 3 lbs/1,000 sq ft/24 hr (ASTM F1869)

- **Usually means:** the slab is still curing or off-gassing moisture, or there's no vapor retarder beneath it.
- **First question:** how old is the slab, and is there a below-slab vapor barrier?
- **Data to pull:** pour date, RH probe or calcium chloride test results, vapor-barrier spec from the original pour.

### Client wants a recoat within hours of a prior coat that's still tacky or soft

- **Usually means:** the recoat window is being blown in the too-soon direction — applying before the first coat has enough mechanical cure to hold the next layer, which shows up later as poor intercoat adhesion.
- **First question:** what's the manufacturer's minimum recoat time at today's temperature and humidity, and how long ago was the last coat actually applied?
- **Data to pull:** TDS recoat-window table, logged ambient temperature/humidity at application time.

### New latex topcoat peels off in sheets within a season

- **Usually means:** latex was applied directly over an intact, glossy oil-based film with no deglossing or bonding primer step.
- **First question:** was the old film oil or latex, and was it scuff-sanded or primed before the new coat went on?
- **Data to pull:** adhesion tape test result, sheen level noted at prep, primer product used, if any.

### Application scheduled on a morning within 3°F of the dew point

- **Usually means:** condensation risk during cure, producing a milky/blushed finish or a weakened bond, even though the air temperature alone looks fine on the forecast.
- **First question:** what's today's dew-point spread at the actual application hour, not just the daytime high?
- **Data to pull:** hourly dew point and surface-temperature forecast, manufacturer's minimum spread requirement for the product.

### Bid is priced flat per square foot with no coat count stated

- **Usually means:** the estimator hasn't reconciled area against gallons needed at the manufacturer's spread rate, so the coat count "included" is whatever number keeps the headline price low.
- **First question:** how many coats and what total dry film thickness does this price assume?
- **Data to pull:** area ÷ spread rate per coat, gallons listed on the material take-off, TDS dry-film-thickness target for the specified product.

### Dust-wipe clearance test after a pre-1978 repaint comes back elevated

- **Usually means:** containment was undersized relative to the disturbance area, or cleanup used a standard shop-vac instead of HEPA filtration.
- **First question:** was containment sized to the full work area, and was HEPA vacuum used before the wet-wipe pass?
- **Data to pull:** containment photos/log, cleanup method and equipment used, the clearance-test lab report itself.
