# Vocabulary

Terms generalists conflate or use loosely that a painter keeps sharply separate — the value in each entry is the misuse column, not the definition.

### Dry film thickness (DFT) vs. wet film thickness (WFT)

**WFT** is the thickness of the coating immediately after application, measured with a notched gauge while it's still liquid. **DFT** is the thickness after solvent/water evaporates and the film cures — always thinner than WFT, by a ratio specific to the product's solids content (a coating at 40% volume solids applied at 10 mils WFT cures to roughly 4 mils DFT).

**In use:** "WFT read 8 mils going on, but this product's at 35% solids, so we're looking at under 3 mils dry — short of the 4-mil spec."

**Common misuse:** citing a WFT gauge reading as if it were the DFT the spec requires, or assuming "it looked thick going on" substitutes for either measurement.

### Recoat window

The manufacturer-specified time range, bounded by a minimum (enough cure to accept the next coat) and a maximum (before the film cures past the point of mechanical bonding and needs to be treated as a new substrate).

**In use:** "We're at day 16 on a 2-week recoat window for this acrylic — that wall gets scuff-sanded before the second coat, not just rolled over."

**Common misuse:** assuming "the longer it cures, the better it'll bond" — most systems bond to themselves mechanically within the stated window and need reactivation (sanding, sometimes a bonding primer) once that window closes.

### Adhesion failure vs. cohesive failure

**Adhesion failure** is separation at an interface — new coat from old film, or film from substrate. **Cohesive failure** is the paint film itself splitting internally, usually from age, UV embrittlement, or being applied too thick.

**In use:** "That's a cohesive failure, not adhesion — the film's splitting mid-layer, not lifting off the wood. Different fix: this is a thickness/UV problem, not a prep problem."

**Common misuse:** calling every instance of peeling paint "bad adhesion" — the two failure modes have different causes and different prep fixes, and treating a cohesive failure with an adhesion fix (more primer) doesn't address it.

### Alligatoring vs. checking vs. cracking

**Alligatoring** is a reticulated, wide-pattern crack network, typically from a rigid coat over a flexible one or an aged oil film losing flexibility. **Checking** is fine, shallow surface cracks, often from a thick coat or fast cure. **Cracking** (or "cross-grain cracking") is deeper, following the substrate's grain, usually a sign the paint has reached end of life on wood.

**In use:** "That's alligatoring, not checking — look at the pattern width. That's an oil-over-latex system, not a cure-rate issue."

**Common misuse:** using the three terms interchangeably as "the paint is cracking" — each pattern points to a different root cause and a different repair scope.

### Moisture content (MC) vs. relative humidity (RH)

**MC** is a percentage of a wood substrate's weight that is water, read with a pin or pinless meter. **RH** is the ambient or in-substrate air moisture, read with a probe (ASTM F2170 for concrete) — the two are different measurements for different materials and aren't interchangeable.

**In use:** "MC on the siding is fine at 12%, but we haven't run an RH probe on the slab yet — that's a separate test, separate substrate."

**Common misuse:** applying a wood MC threshold to a concrete reading, or treating a "feels dry" surface check as equivalent to either instrumented test.

### Presumed lead vs. confirmed lead

**Presumed lead** is the EPA RRP Rule's default status for any pre-1978 target housing or child-occupied facility with no test on file — work practices apply regardless of whether paint is actually lead-based. **Confirmed lead** (or confirmed negative) comes from a certified inspector's XRF or lab test.

**In use:** "We have no test on file, so this is presumed lead by rule — RRP practices apply whether or not it turns out positive."

**Common misuse:** treating "presumed" as optional or as a formality that can be skipped if the paint "doesn't look old" — the rule's default status carries the same work-practice obligations as a confirmed-positive test.

### Bonding primer vs. sealer vs. primer

A **primer** creates a uniform base and improves general adhesion. A **sealer** locks down a porous, chalky, or stained substrate so the topcoat doesn't get absorbed unevenly. A **bonding primer** is formulated specifically to grip a difficult, low-porosity surface (glossy oil, tile, PVC) that a standard primer won't adhere to.

**In use:** "Standard primer won't hold on that gloss oil trim — we need a bonding primer there, not just any primer off the shelf."

**Common misuse:** treating "primer" as one interchangeable product category and grabbing whichever can is on the truck, instead of matching the specific problem (porosity, staining, or low-adhesion surface) to the specific product type.

### Dew-point spread

The difference between the substrate/surface temperature and the dew point at application time — the number that predicts condensation risk, not the air temperature or humidity percentage alone.

**In use:** "Air temp looks fine at 68, but we're only 3 degrees off the dew point — that's a hold, not a go."

**Common misuse:** checking only the forecast's temperature and humidity percentage and skipping the dew-point calculation, which misses the actual condensation-risk signal on cool, humid mornings.

### Mil

A unit of thickness equal to 0.001 inch, the standard unit for both wet and dry film thickness in the trade.

**In use:** "Spec calls for 3.0 mils total DFT across two coats — that's 1.5 mils a coat, not 3 mils each."

**Common misuse:** confusing mil thickness with coat count — a single heavy coat applied to hit a 3-mil target isn't equivalent to two coats at 1.5 mils each, and often introduces its own failure (sagging, slow or incomplete cure, surface mud-cracking).

### Spread rate

The area one gallon of a specific product covers at its specified dry film thickness, printed on the can and detailed in the manufacturer's TDS.

**In use:** "Can says 400 square feet a gallon, but this stucco is going to eat 25% more than that — order accordingly, don't order off the label."

**Common misuse:** using the printed spread rate as a fixed number regardless of substrate texture or porosity, which commonly cuts real coverage by 20–30% on rough or unsealed surfaces.

### Feathering

Sanding the edge of a scraped or repaired area so it blends smoothly into the surrounding sound paint, leaving no ridge for the new coat to telegraph through.

**In use:** "Feather that scrape line out another two inches or it's going to show right through the topcoat once the light hits it."

**Common misuse:** treating feathering as a cosmetic nicety to skip under time pressure — an unfeathered edge telegraphs through the new coat and becomes a visible defect line, not just a durability issue.
