# Playbook

Filled reference tables and step sequences for scoping and executing a repaint or new-construction paint job. Numbers here are the kind pulled during a walk-through and bid — treat the specific figures as representative of manufacturer TDS ranges, not a substitute for the actual product's data sheet on a live job.

## 1. Substrate moisture — go/no-go thresholds

| Substrate | Test method | Ceiling before coating | What's above it usually means |
|---|---|---|---|
| Wood siding/trim | Pin-type moisture meter | ≤15% MC (most exterior acrylics; some manufacturers allow up to 18% for elastomeric systems) | Active leak, poor drainage, or insufficient dry-out after a wash |
| New/repaired stucco or masonry | Visual + pH strip + time | ≥30 days cure minimum, pH <10 | Alkalinity or incomplete cure will saponify oil-based coatings and blister latex |
| Interior drywall (new construction) | Pinless moisture meter | ≤12% MC | Recent taping/mudding, HVAC not yet running to dry the space |
| Concrete slab (qualitative screen) | ASTM D4263 plastic sheet, 16–24 hr | No visible condensation or darkening under the sheet | Slab is a vapor path; run the quantitative test next |
| Concrete slab (quantitative) | ASTM F1869 calcium chloride | ≤3 lbs/1,000 sq ft/24 hr for most coatings (5 lbs for some flooring systems — check TDS) | Slab still curing/off-gassing, or no vapor retarder underneath |
| Concrete slab (in-situ) | ASTM F2170 RH probe | ≤75–80% RH | Same as above; probe method is less sensitive to surface drying than calcium chloride |

**Rule of thumb:** a slab poured within the last 60–90 days is presumptively not ready regardless of surface feel — cure rate is roughly 1 month per inch of slab thickness to reach typical coating-ready RH, faster with a vapor barrier and good ventilation, slower without either.

## 2. EPA RRP compliance checklist (pre-1978 target housing / child-occupied facilities)

Trigger: any renovation disturbing painted surfaces beyond **6 sq ft per interior component** or **20 sq ft per exterior component** (or any window replacement) on a structure built before 1978.

1. **Determine lead status.** Certified inspector/risk-assessor test (XRF or lab sample) → documented negative clears the job of RRP; no test on file → presume positive by default.
2. **Confirm firm certification.** EPA Lead-Safe Certified firm number on file; work directed by an EPA/state-certified renovator (8-hour initial course, 4-hour refresher every 5 years).
3. **Owner opt-out (owner-occupied only, never child-occupied facilities):** signed waiver on file confirming no child under 6 and no pregnant occupant resides there — opt-out removes the work-practice mandate but not the disclosure requirement.
4. **Containment sized to the full disturbance area** — poly sheeting extending 10 ft from the building for exterior work, taped and weighted; interior work sealed room-to-room.
5. **Prohibited practices avoided:** no open-flame burning, no high-heat gun above 1,100°F, no dry power-sanding or dry abrasive blasting without HEPA local exhaust, no uncontained power-washing runoff.
6. **Cleanup:** HEPA vacuum first, then wet-wipe/wet-mop in a fixed sequence (ceiling to floor, center to edges), disposable containment bagged as waste.
7. **Clearance:** visual clearance exam at minimum; dust-wipe clearance testing where required by state rule or contract, before containment comes down.
8. **Recordkeeping:** retain the test results, firm certification, renovator certification, and cleanup verification for 3 years — this is the paper trail an inspector or insurer asks for first.

**Penalty exposure** if skipped: EPA civil penalties run up to the tens of thousands of dollars per violation per day (inflation-adjusted periodically) — this is the compliance stakes conversation to have with the client before quoting, not after a complaint.

## 3. Paint-system compatibility — what fails and why

| Existing film | New coat | Direct application result | Required prep |
|---|---|---|---|
| Latex | Latex | Bonds fine if film is sound and clean | Wash, scuff dull any gloss above eggshell |
| Oil/alkyd | Oil/alkyd | Bonds fine if film is sound | Scuff-sand, deglossing agent on high-gloss |
| Latex | Oil/alkyd | Fails over time — rigid oil skin over a flexible latex base splits in a reticulated (alligator) pattern as the substrate below keeps expanding/contracting; visible in 1–3 years, not at application | Avoid the combination; if unavoidable, full scrape and oil-based primer, expect a shorter system life |
| Oil/alkyd (intact, glossy) | Latex | Fails by adhesion — latex can't grip a non-porous, glossy oil film; peels in sheets within a season | Deglossing sand pass + bonding primer (acrylic bonding primer or shellac-based primer), then latex topcoat |
| Unknown/undocumented | Either | Unknown until tested | Solvent-rub test (denatured alcohol softens/removes latex, does not affect cured oil) or heat-gun softening test before committing the job |

## 4. Spread rate, coat count, and dry film thickness — the math to show a client

Formula: **area (sq ft) ÷ spread rate (sq ft/gal) = gallons needed per coat.**

Worked example, exterior acrylic, 400 sq ft/gal spread rate, 2-coat system targeting 3.0 mils total DFT (1.5 mils/coat):

| Area | Sq ft | Coats | Gal/coat (area ÷ 400) | Total gallons | Material cost @ $58/gal |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Siding | 2,400 | 2 | 6.0 | 12.0 | $696 |
| Trim (350 sq ft/gal, enamel) | 300 | 2 | 0.86 → round up to 1 | 2 | $124 (@ $62/gal) |

**Texture/porosity adjustment:** the can's stated spread rate assumes a smooth, sealed surface. Rough-sawn siding, unsealed masonry, or heavily-chalked existing film commonly cut real coverage by 20–30% — verify with a measured first-gallon coverage on site before ordering the full material list, don't just order off the label math.

**Single-coat check:** if a bid's material list implies fewer gallons than area ÷ spread rate × specified coat count, the bid is under-thickness before a brush touches the wall — this is the fastest way to audit a suspiciously cheap quote.

## 5. Dew point and application-window screening

Rule: **surface temperature must be at least 5°F above the dew point**, and most acrylic/alkyd systems require ambient RH below ~85% at application, rising RH forecast within the recoat window is a hold signal.

| Day | Surface temp (°F) | Dew point (°F) | Spread | Call |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Mon | 72 | 58 | 14°F | Go |
| Tue | 68 | 65 | 3°F | Hold — condensation risk during cure |
| Wed | 70 | 64 | 6°F | Go, marginal — recheck forecast morning-of |
| Thu | 75 | 60 | 15°F | Go |
| Fri | 66 | 63 | 3°F | Hold |

## 6. Recoat window — by system, approximate

| System | Minimum recoat (before next coat) | Maximum recoat window (before requiring scuff-sand/reprime) |
|---|---|---|
| Latex (exterior acrylic) | 4 hours at 70°F/50% RH | ~2 weeks — beyond this, treat as fully cured and re-scuff |
| Oil/alkyd | 24 hours at 70°F/50% RH | ~1 week — oil films cure by oxidation and lose mechanical "tooth" fast |
| Epoxy (2-part) | Product-specific, often 6–24 hours | Can be as short as 24–72 hours — missing an epoxy recoat window frequently requires solvent-wipe or abrasion, check TDS explicitly, this system is the least forgiving |

**Cold-weather note:** recoat times roughly double for every 20°F drop below 70°F — a coat that recoats in 4 hours at 70°F may need 8+ hours at 50°F; forcing the schedule at low temperature is a common cause of the "applied too soon" adhesion failures in red-flags.md.
