# Vocabulary

Terms generalists use loosely that a host keeps sharply separate, because the distinction changes what they do next.

## Cover vs. table vs. turn

A **cover** is one seated guest. A **table** is a physical unit of seating capacity (a "4-top" seats four). A **turn** is one full cycle of a table from seated to cleared and reset. Rotation and revenue math run on covers, not tables — a section with fewer, bigger tables can carry more covers than a section with more, smaller ones.

*In use:* "Section B only turned five tables tonight, but that's forty-two covers — don't compare turns across sections with different table sizes."

*Common misuse:* rotating "one table per section in order" and calling it fair, when a section with 2-tops is structurally getting fewer covers per turn than a section with 6-tops even at a perfectly even table-count rotation.

## Rotation vs. cover-share

**Rotation** is the sequencing rule for which section gets the next open table. **Cover-share** is covers seated in a section divided by that section's total seat capacity, tracked live. Rotation should be *driven by* cover-share, not by a simple round-robin — a round-robin ignores that sections differ in size and table mix.

*In use:* "C's at 88% cover-share, B's at 38% — the next multi-top goes to B regardless of whose 'turn' it technically is."

*Common misuse:* treating rotation as a fixed sequence (A, B, C, A, B, C) instead of a live ranking that responds to the actual numbers.

## Grace period vs. no-show

The **grace period** is the fixed window a host holds a late reservation's table before releasing it — a policy, stated in minutes. A **no-show** is the outcome once that window has expired with no arrival and no contact. A party ten minutes late during a fifteen-minute grace period is not yet a no-show.

*In use:* "They're at twelve minutes — still inside our fifteen-minute grace period, don't release the table yet."

*Common misuse:* calling any late arrival a "no-show," or conversely holding a table indefinitely past grace out of guilt, which is really a failure to make the release decision.

## Quote breach

The point at which a wait-listed guest's actual elapsed wait exceeds the time they were quoted, past a meaningful margin (commonly ~15%) rather than by a trivial minute or two. A breach is a trust event, not just a scheduling delay.

*In use:* "Ortega's eighteen minutes in on a fifteen-minute quote — that's a breach, seat them next regardless of rotation."

*Common misuse:* treating every wait that runs slightly long as equivalent, instead of tracking the percentage-over-quote that actually predicts how a guest will feel about it.

## Marrying tables

Physically pushing two tables together to create a larger unit for a party that doesn't match any single table's size — a temporary combine, split back apart once the party clears.

*In use:* "Marry B-3 and B-4 for the party of six, then split them back the second they pay out."

*Common misuse:* treating a married pair as permanent capacity for the rest of the shift, which quietly removes a section's ability to seat two smaller parties at once.

## Section vs. station

A **section** is the host's unit of seating and rotation — a group of tables. A **station** is the server's assigned working unit, which may or may not map one-to-one to a host's section (some houses split a section across two servers at peak, or double one server across two sections in a slow period). Confusing the two leads to seating decisions that don't match who's actually working the tables.

*In use:* "Section C is one rotation unit for seating, but it's two stations right now — check which server actually has that table before you send a large party."

*Common misuse:* assuming section boundaries and server assignments are always identical, especially during a shift change or a cut.

## Pacing / fire time

**Pacing** is the deliberate spacing of when tickets — especially large-party tickets — hit the kitchen, so the line isn't asked to fire too many complex courses at once. **Fire time** is the specific moment a course's ticket is sent to the kitchen, which the host influences indirectly through when and how a party is seated.

*In use:* "Hold them at the stand five minutes — that's not about the table, that's about not firing a third six-top in twenty minutes."

*Common misuse:* believing pacing is solely the kitchen's or server's problem once a party is seated, when the seating and hold decision at the door is what created the pacing conflict in the first place.

## Holdback

The percentage of total seats a house deliberately keeps unbooked as walk-in inventory, even on a fully-reserved night — a buffer against no-shows and turn-time variance, not lost revenue.

*In use:* "We're holding back fifteen percent tonight — don't book that last table online even though it shows open."

*Common misuse:* treating a fully-booked reservation sheet as 100% committed capacity and having nothing to offer the walk-in line, then scrambling when the inevitable no-shows create empty tables mid-service.

## VIP flag / guest note

A **VIP flag** marks a guest for discretionary treatment (best table, preferred server, a comp) independent of rotation order. A **guest note** is factual context attached to a reservation (allergy, anniversary, seating preference) that changes how the table is prepared, not who gets priority.

*In use:* "She's flagged VIP, seat her ahead of the sheet — but log it so B doesn't eat that loss twice in one week."

*Common misuse:* using "VIP" as a catch-all for any regular or any guest management wants to please, without tracking which section absorbs the rotation cost each time — the flag is meant to be an occasional exception, not a running policy for the same handful of guests every night.

## Walked (a guest) vs. walked (a table)

A guest who **walked** left without being seated, usually because the wait exceeded what they were willing to tolerate — a lost check, and a signal the quote or the wait itself failed. A table that **walked** (less common usage) refers to a party who left before ordering or paying, distinct from a normal turn.

*In use:* "We had two walks during the seven o'clock rush — both were quote breaches we didn't seat in time."

*Common misuse:* recording a walked guest as simply "didn't want to wait" without checking whether it was a quote-breach failure the host could have prevented by seating them sooner.
