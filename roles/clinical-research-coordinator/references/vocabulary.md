# Working vocabulary

Terms of art practitioners use precisely, that generalists misuse. Load this when writing or reviewing content that needs to sound like an actual clinical research coordinator, not a generalist describing one.

### Protocol deviation
Any departure from the approved study protocol, ranging from minor to significant, that must be documented and reported per the study's defined process regardless of the coordinator's own assessment of its severity.
**In use:** "That's a protocol deviation — log it and route it through the reporting process, even though the visit itself went fine."
**Common misuse:** Only reported when it seems clinically significant — the reporting requirement applies to any deviation, because the coordinator isn't positioned to fully judge downstream significance alone; that's what the review process is for.

### ALCOA (data integrity principle)
Attributable, Legible, Contemporaneous, Original, Accurate — the standard used to judge whether a recorded data point is trustworthy, independent of whether the value itself seems reasonable.
**In use:** "This value isn't ALCOA-compliant — we can't trace who recorded it or when, so it doesn't matter that the number looks plausible."
**Common misuse:** Judged only on "accurate" (does the number look right) while ignoring attributable/contemporaneous/original — a plausible-looking number with no traceable source is still a data-integrity failure.

### Screen fail
A prospective participant who is screened for a trial but found ineligible before enrollment, distinct from a withdrawal (someone who enrolled and later left).
**In use:** "Screen-fail rate on that specific criterion is running double the protocol assumption — that's worth flagging to the medical monitor."
**Common misuse:** Conflated with dropout/withdrawal — screen-fails happen before enrollment and reflect eligibility-criteria fit with the referred population; withdrawals happen after enrollment and reflect retention factors. Different diagnosis, different fix.

### Source data verification (SDV)
The process of confirming that data entered into the study database matches the original source (medical record, lab report, patient diary) it was drawn from.
**In use:** "SDV caught a transcription error on the lab value — good thing we can trace it back to the original report."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a formality/audit-prep step rather than an ongoing data-integrity control — SDV done only right before a monitoring visit, rather than continuously, means errors accumulate undetected in the interim.

### Informed consent (ongoing process)
The ethical and regulatory requirement that a participant understands and voluntarily agrees to participate, understood as continuous through the study, not satisfied permanently by an initial signature.
**In use:** "That amendment changes the risk profile materially — we need a consent re-confirmation conversation with active participants, not just an updated form on file."
**Common misuse:** Treated as complete once the initial form is signed — genuine informed consent requires the participant's understanding to be current, especially after new information (an amendment, a safety signal) that the original signature didn't cover.

### Principal Investigator (PI) vs. Sponsor vs. IRB
Three distinct roles with different authority: the PI owns clinical/scientific decisions at the site; the sponsor owns the protocol and funds the trial; the IRB (Institutional Review Board) is the independent ethics oversight body approving and monitoring the study for participant protection.
**In use:** "This eligibility question goes to the PI for clinical judgment, but the deviation itself still needs to be reported to the IRB per protocol regardless of the PI's read on it."
**Common misuse:** Treated as interchangeable — a coordinator needs to know which of the three has authority over a specific type of question (clinical judgment vs. protocol compliance vs. participant-protection oversight) to route it correctly.

### Enrollment funnel
The staged conversion from referral to screened to eligible to consented to enrolled, used to diagnose where a recruitment shortfall is actually occurring.
**In use:** "The funnel shows referrals are fine — the drop happens at eligibility, so more outreach won't fix this."
**Common misuse:** Skipped in favor of looking only at the final enrollment number — the aggregate shortfall doesn't reveal which stage is actually underperforming relative to the original protocol assumptions.

### Withdrawal (participant)
A participant who leaves the study after enrollment, distinct from a screen-fail (never enrolled) — tracked separately because its causes (burden, scheduling, adverse events, personal reasons) and fixes differ from screen-fail causes.
**In use:** "Withdrawal reasons this quarter are mostly scheduling conflicts, not adverse events — that points to a coordination fix, not a protocol or safety issue."
**Common misuse:** Aggregated with screen-fails into one generic "recruitment problem" — the two happen at different stages for different reasons and need separately diagnosed fixes.
