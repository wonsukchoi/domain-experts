# Vocabulary

Terms generalists conflate or use loosely that a paperhanger keeps sharply separate — the value in each entry is the misuse column, not the definition.

### Booking vs. relaxing

**Booking** is folding a pasted strip's ends to the center, paste-to-paste, immediately after pasting. **Relaxing** is the wait time after booking — typically 5–10 minutes per the manufacturer's spec — that lets the paper finish absorbing paste and expanding before it goes on the wall.

**In use:** "It's booked, but give it the full seven minutes to relax before we hang it — it's still taking up paste."

**Common misuse:** treating booking as just a folding technique for carrying the strip, and skipping the relax time once it's folded — the fold and the wait are two separate steps with two separate purposes.

### Paste-the-wall vs. paste-the-paper

**Paste-the-wall** applies adhesive directly to the wall with a roller, then hangs a dry strip — the standard method for non-woven wallcoverings. **Paste-the-paper** applies adhesive to the back of the strip with a brush or pasting machine, then books and hangs it — the traditional method for paper and fabric-backed vinyl.

**In use:** "This one's non-woven — we're pasting the wall, not booking paper today."

**Common misuse:** applying a paste-the-wall product's install method to a traditional paper backing, or vice versa; the two backings expand and handle differently and the wrong method produces bubbling or dimensional shift.

### Sizing vs. primer

A wallcovering **primer** (or "primer/sizer") is a nonrewettable acrylic-polymer coating applied to the wall before hanging, creating a barrier between the wall and the paste and a release layer for future stripping. **Sizing** in older trade usage meant a diluted paste coat with a similar purpose, but a modern acrylic primer/sizer product is not the same thing as diluting leftover wallpaper paste and rolling it on.

**In use:** "Don't thin the paste and call it sizing — we need the actual primer/sizer product, it's what lets this come off clean in ten years."

**Common misuse:** using a standard interior paint primer, or a homemade diluted-paste "size," as a substitute for a manufacturer-specified wallcovering primer.

### Straight match vs. drop (offset) match vs. random match

**Straight match** means the pattern lines up at the same height across every seam. **Drop match** (or offset match, e.g. "24/12 in") means the pattern only lines up at a stated vertical offset between adjacent strips, usually alternating. **Random match** has no repeat to register at all — any two lengths hang next to each other cleanly.

**In use:** "It's a 24/12 drop match, so every other strip starts from a different point in the repeat — don't cut them all the same."

**Common misuse:** ordering or cutting a drop-match pattern as if every strip starts from the same point in the repeat, which produces a visibly misaligned pattern at every other seam.

### Pattern repeat

The vertical distance between one point in a printed pattern and the next identical point — the number that determines how much of a roll's total length is lost to matching rather than covering wall.

**In use:** "24-inch repeat on an 8-foot wall means every cut rounds up to a 10-foot drop — that's 16 inches of match waste per strip before we've hung a thing."

**Common misuse:** estimating roll quantity from a roll's total printed square footage without adjusting for the repeat, which understates the order on any pattern with a repeat over roughly 12 inches.

### Railroading

Hanging a wallcovering sideways — pattern running horizontally across the wall instead of floor-to-ceiling — typically used above door and window openings or on wide expanses to avoid a vertical seam falling in an awkward spot, and historically to use up scrap.

**In use:** "We'll railroad the strip over the doorway header instead of piecing in two short drops — cleaner and no extra seam."

**Common misuse:** assuming railroading is only a technique for stripe or plain patterns; directional prints (florals, scenes with an obvious up/down) can't be railroaded without the pattern reading sideways.

### Two-piece corner

The technique of cutting a strip at a corner, wrapping a small overlap (15–20 mm) onto the adjacent wall, snapping a fresh plumb line on that wall, and starting the next strip square to the new line rather than continuing the same strip around the corner.

**In use:** "That inside corner's 10 mm out of plumb top to bottom — two-piece it, don't wrap a full strip."

**Common misuse:** wrapping a full-width strip around any corner "because it looks straight," which carries the corner's actual plumb error (often invisible to the eye at a glance) into every subsequent strip.

### Selvage

The unprinted or trim-marked edge strip along the length of a roll, left by the printing/coating process, that gets trimmed off before or during hanging — on double-cut seams, both adjacent strips' selvage-side edges are overlapped and cut through together.

**In use:** "Leave the selvage on until we double-cut this seam — we need the overlap to cut through both layers clean."

**Common misuse:** trimming the selvage off every strip by default before checking whether the seam technique (butt vs. double-cut) actually needs it left on.

### Vinyl-to-vinyl adhesive (seam sealer)

A specialized adhesive applied at seams and edges of vinyl-faced wallcoverings, distinct from the wall-hanging paste, formulated to bond vinyl surface to vinyl surface where standard wall paste won't hold.

**In use:** "Edges are curling on that vinyl seam — that needs seam sealer, the wall paste underneath already set and won't re-bond vinyl to vinyl."

**Common misuse:** trying to re-adhere a lifting vinyl seam with more of the original wall paste, which doesn't bond well to the vinyl face the way it bonds to the wall substrate.

### Type I / Type II / Type III (ASTM F793)

A classification of wallcovering durability and use characteristics — light residential duty, standard commercial duty, and heavy commercial/high-traffic duty respectively — used to match product to a room's traffic and abrasion exposure.

**In use:** "This is a hospital corridor — spec Type II at minimum, Type III if it's a gurney-traffic hallway, not a residential-grade product."

**Common misuse:** treating "vinyl wallcovering" as one uniform durability tier and specifying by look or price rather than the ASTM type rating for the space's actual traffic.
