# Red flags

Smell tests a technician catches in the first few minutes of a job. Format for each: what it usually means, the first question to ask, and the data to pull before adjusting anything.

## 1. Chromatic tuner reads every note within ~1 cent of theoretical equal temperament on a piano

**Usually means:** the instrument was tuned to a flat table instead of aurally stretched — most often a novice or an app-only tuning with no inharmonicity compensation, less often an ETD used in "theoretical" mode without an aural check.

**First question:** "Did whoever tuned this check the octaves and chords by ear, or trust the app's per-note readout?"

**Data to pull:** this piano's measured stretch/inharmonicity curve from an ETD's aural-style calculation; any prior tuning log; who tuned it last and with what tool.

## 2. Piano loses 5-10+ cents of pitch across the whole scale within 4-8 weeks of a professional tuning

**Usually means:** an RH swing in the room or case, most commonly a heating-season drop below ~35% RH; less likely on an older piano, a slipping tuning-pin block.

**First question:** "What's the relative humidity been doing in this room over the last two months?"

**Data to pull:** hygrometer/data-logger reading (install one if none exists); pinblock torque/string-holding check if drift repeats after RH is stabilized; HVAC or heating schedule for the room.

## 3. Guitar buzzes on open chords and first position but plays clean above the 5th fret

**Usually means:** too little neck relief (nearly straight or back-bowed neck), less often a nut slot cut too low.

**First question:** "What's the gap at the 7th-8th fret with a capo at the 1st fret and the low E fretted at the neck-body joint?"

**Data to pull:** feeler-gauge relief measurement (target ~0.010"-0.012"), nut slot height at the 1st fret, string gauge and tuning (dropped tunings need more relief, not less).

## 4. Woodwind won't respond or cracks below the break, but plays fine in the upper register

**Usually means:** a leaking pad on a lower-register tone hole (often the register key or a ring key) — reed problems almost always affect the whole range, not just one register.

**First question:** "Has a leak light or paper pull-through been run on the pads below the break?"

**Data to pull:** leak-light result per suspect pad, spring tension at that key, cork/felt condition and approximate age.

## 5. Brass instrument valves feel gummy or slow to return immediately after fresh valve oil

**Usually means:** valve port or casing misalignment, or worn felt/cork bumpers changing valve travel — oil can't fix a mechanical alignment problem and often just masks it briefly.

**First question:** "Do the valve ports line up cleanly when sighting down the casing with the valve depressed?"

**Data to pull:** port alignment check (rotational and vertical), casing roundness/dent check along the travel path, bumper felt/cork thickness against spec.

## 6. A wood instrument develops a crack or open seam in winter

**Usually means:** RH dropped below the wood's safe range (commonly under ~30-35%) faster than the wood could equalize — not a manufacturing defect and not caused by how the instrument was played.

**First question:** "What was the humidity in the room the week before this appeared, and is there any humidity control in that room?"

**Data to pull:** hygrometer/data-logger history if available, heating-season timeline, case storage habits (left open near a vent, stored in a car, etc.).

## 7. Piano action feels heavier or lighter to the owner after a regulation the shop considers standard

**Usually means:** touchweight was set to a generic spec without measuring this action's actual friction and hammer weight, or hammer mass changed (new hammers) without rebalancing the action.

**First question:** "What were the measured down-weight and up-weight before and after, on a calibrated scale?"

**Data to pull:** touchweight readings across the scale, friction trend (down-weight minus up-weight), hammer/key mass data if hammers were replaced.

## 8. Fretted instrument intonates correctly at the 12th fret but reads sharp or flat higher up the neck

**Usually means:** saddle compensation was set without checking fret placement or scale-length accuracy, or old/dead strings with an inconsistent core-to-winding ratio.

**First question:** "Is the sharp/flat behavior the same across all strings, or worse specifically on the wound strings?"

**Data to pull:** strobe-tuner readings at the 1st, 12th, and 19th/24th frets per string, string age and gauge, measured saddle compensation distance per string.

## 9. Repair estimate on a student-grade instrument runs 40%+ of its fair resale value

**Usually means:** the instrument has reached the point where repair no longer makes economic sense against a comparable replacement — occasionally it's an heirloom the owner wants repaired regardless of resale math.

**First question:** "Is this instrument being kept for playability, or does it have value to you beyond that?"

**Data to pull:** current comparable resale pricing, itemized repair estimate, serial/production data if rarity or authenticity is in question.

## 10. Piano still sounds off specifically on the unisons, not on the single-strung bass notes

**Usually means:** the unison strings aren't perfectly matched to each other (false beats within the unison) from string wear or a mismatched replacement string — the temperament may be fine and the complaint is a mis-set or bad unison, not the tuning itself.

**First question:** "Play the note and listen — is the beat between the three strings of that unison, or between this note and the octave above or below it?"

**Data to pull:** unison-by-unison aural check, string condition and age, any recently replaced individual strings (mismatched wire gauge or age behaves differently from its neighbors).
