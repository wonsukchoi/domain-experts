# Vocabulary

Terms generalists conflate that technicians keep sharply separate. Each: definition, how a practitioner actually uses it, and the common misuse.

## Stretch tuning vs. equal temperament

**Equal temperament** is the abstract division of the octave into twelve equal 100-cent semitones. **Stretch tuning** is the deliberate widening of octaves beyond that theoretical 2:1 ratio to match a piano's inharmonic partials — applied on top of the temperament, not a substitute for it.

*In use:* "The temperament's fine — check F3 to F4 again — it's the stretch that's off, the ninth doesn't beat clean against the fourth."

*Common misuse:* assuming "equal temperament" means every octave is a literal frequency doubling; on a real piano, a correctly tuned octave almost never is.

## Cent

A logarithmic unit of pitch, 1/100 of an equal-tempered semitone, 1200 to the octave. It's the standard unit for describing how far a note deviates from a reference pitch.

*In use:* "This piano's C8 needs to sit about 29 cents sharp of theoretical, not dead on."

*Common misuse:* treating "0 cents" as an unqualified synonym for "correctly tuned" — it only means correct relative to the specific reference each note is being compared against.

## Railsback curve

The historically measured curve describing how a well-tuned piano's actual pitch deviates from theoretical equal temperament across the keyboard — flat in the bass, sharp in the treble — as a consequence of string inharmonicity. It's a descriptive average, not a fixed target for every piano.

*In use:* "Don't tune to the printed Railsback numbers blind — pull this piano's own stretch off the ETD, its scale design isn't average."

*Common misuse:* applying a generic published Railsback curve to every piano as if stretch amount doesn't vary with scale design, string length, and tension.

## Inharmonicity

The physical property of a real (non-ideal) string: its overtones (partials) run progressively sharper than a pure integer-multiple harmonic series, because string stiffness resists bending more at higher vibration modes. It's the underlying cause of stretch tuning.

*In use:* "Short, thick bass strings are more inharmonic than long ones — that's why this baby grand needs more stretch at the bottom than a full-size grand."

*Common misuse:* treating inharmonicity as a defect or sign of a bad string, rather than a normal physical property every real string has to some degree.

## Regulation vs. tuning vs. voicing (piano)

**Tuning** sets pitch. **Regulation** sets the mechanical geometry of the action (key dip, let-off, touchweight, and related travel distances) that governs how the key's motion reaches the string. **Voicing** shapes the hammer felt's density and hardness to control tone quality. The three are independent adjustments that happen to sit in the same instrument.

*In use:* "The pitch is fine — what she's feeling as 'sluggish' is a regulation problem, the let-off's too deep, not a tuning problem."

*Common misuse:* calling any piano complaint a "tuning" issue by default, or asking for "a tuning" when the actual complaint (heavy touch, uneven tone) is regulation or voicing.

## Touchweight (down-weight / up-weight)

**Down-weight** is the static force required to press a key from rest to the keybed. **Up-weight** is the force required for the key to return to rest on its own. The difference between them is the action's friction.

*In use:* "Down-weight's at 52 grams, up-weight's only 16 — that's 36 grams of friction, way over spec; check the key bushings before we touch the leading."

*Common misuse:* quoting only down-weight as "the touchweight," which hides a friction problem that would make two actions with identical down-weight feel completely different to play.

## Let-off (escapement)

The small gap deliberately left between the hammer and the string just before contact at full key travel, so the hammer can rebound freely instead of blocking against the string.

*In use:* "It's blocking on repeated notes because the let-off's nearly closed — give it another sixty-fourth."

*Common misuse:* confusing let-off with key dip; a key can have correct total travel and still block if the let-off distance itself is wrong.

## Neck relief vs. action height

**Neck relief** is the slight forward bow (concave curvature) intentionally left in a fretted-instrument neck so vibrating strings have clearance in the middle of their length. **Action height** is the height of the string above the frets, measured separately at a reference fret (commonly the 12th).

*In use:* "Don't lower the action to fix that buzz — check relief first, it's probably back-bowed, not just low."

*Common misuse:* adjusting action height (saddle/bridge) to fix a buzz that's actually a relief problem, which fixes the symptom in one position and reintroduces it elsewhere on the neck.

## Leak test (pad seating)

A check for whether a woodwind pad forms a complete, even seal against its tone hole under normal spring tension, using a leak light or a paper/feeler pull-through at multiple points around the pad's circumference.

*In use:* "It seats fine when I hold the key shut by hand, but the pull-through drags unevenly under spring tension — that's a spring problem, not a pad problem."

*Common misuse:* checking a pad's seal only by eye or only under hand pressure, which misses leaks that appear specifically under the instrument's actual playing spring tension.

## Intonation (fretted instrument)

The accuracy of pitch across the full length of the fingerboard, set by adjusting saddle/bridge compensation so a fretted note matches its corresponding harmonic at the same fret. Distinct from "tuning," which only sets the open-string pitch.

*In use:* "Tuning's dead on open, but intonation's off — the 12th-fret note reads eight cents sharp of the harmonic, the saddle needs to move back."

*Common misuse:* treating "in tune" (correct open-string pitch) and "correctly intonated" (correct pitch relationship across the whole neck) as the same thing — a guitar can be perfectly one and badly the other.

## RH / equilibrium moisture content (EMC)

**RH** (relative humidity) is the ambient moisture measurement. **EMC** is the moisture content wood settles toward at a given RH and temperature over time — wood doesn't respond to RH instantly, it lags toward equilibrium, which is why sudden swings (not just low or high absolute RH) cause the most damage.

*In use:* "The room's back to 45% RH now, but the soundboard hasn't caught up yet — give it two weeks before judging the crown."

*Common misuse:* assuming a wood instrument responds to a humidity change immediately, and either judging it too soon or blaming a fix for "not working" before the wood has had time to equalize.

## False beat / false unison

A **false beat** is an audible wow or pulsing from a single string's own imperfect vibration (a stiff spot, corrosion, or manufacturing flaw), independent of any other note. A **false unison** is when two or three strings meant to sound one pitch together beat against each other for this reason, not because they're mistuned relative to each other.

*In use:* "That's not a tuning problem, that string's got a false beat in it — it'll never sit still against its unison partners, it needs replacing."

*Common misuse:* re-tuning a false unison repeatedly, assuming operator error, when the actual defect is in the string itself and no tuning adjustment will resolve it.

## Valve alignment (brass)

The rotational and vertical positioning of a piston or rotor valve so its internal ports line up flush with the instrument's outer tubing at rest and at full depression, allowing unrestricted airflow through the horn.

*In use:* "It's not sluggish from lack of oil — the port's a quarter-turn off at the bottom of the stroke, the guide's worn."

*Common misuse:* treating any valve slowness as a lubrication problem and re-oiling repeatedly instead of checking alignment and bumper wear, which oil cannot correct.
