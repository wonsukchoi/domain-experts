# Vocabulary

### Number needed to screen (NNS)
The number of people who must be screened over a given period to prevent one adverse outcome (e.g., one cancer death).
**In use:** "FIT screening for colorectal cancer runs an NNS around 900 over ten years — that's the number to put next to the cost, not the sensitivity figure."
**Common misuse:** Treating a test's sensitivity/specificity as the program's real-world benefit, when NNS (which folds in prevalence, uptake, and follow-through) is the number that actually predicts population impact.

### Lead-time bias
Apparent survival-time improvement from screening that is entirely an artifact of earlier diagnosis, with no change to the actual date of death.
**In use:** "Before we credit the program with a survival-time gain, subtract the lead time — otherwise we're just measuring how much earlier we found it, not whether anyone lived longer."
**Common misuse:** Reporting a screened cohort's longer post-diagnosis survival as proof the screening extends life, without checking whether the death date itself moved.

### Length-time bias
Screening's tendency to preferentially detect slower-growing, less aggressive disease because it spends longer in a detectable pre-symptomatic window, making screen-detected cases look like they have better prognosis than the disease generally does.
**In use:** "The screen-detected tumors are smaller and slower on average — that's length-time bias doing its usual work, not necessarily the screening catching anything earlier in its natural history."
**Common misuse:** Concluding screening improves prognosis because screen-detected cases do better, without accounting for the fact that fast-growing, worse-prognosis cases are systematically underrepresented in what screening catches.

### Overdiagnosis
Detection of a condition that would never have caused symptoms or death in the person's lifetime, so treating it provides no benefit and only exposes them to treatment harm.
**In use:** "Some fraction of the thyroid nodules this program is finding are overdiagnosis — cancers that would have sat there for decades unnoticed — and every one of them gets a surgery with real complication risk."
**Common misuse:** Treating "detected" and "clinically meaningful" as synonyms, and counting every detected case as a program success rather than netting out the overdiagnosed fraction.

### Sensitivity/specificity vs. positive predictive value (PPV)
Sensitivity and specificity are properties of the test, fixed regardless of population. PPV — the chance a positive result is a true positive — depends on the population's prevalence and swings with it.
**In use:** "The test's 95% sensitivity looks great on paper, but at this population's 0.3% prevalence the PPV is still under 10% — most positives here will be false."
**Common misuse:** Quoting a test's sensitivity/specificity as if it tells you how much to trust a positive result, when PPV (which needs the local prevalence) is the number that actually answers that question.

### Herd immunity threshold
The vaccination coverage level above which an infectious agent's reproduction number drops below 1 in that population, protecting even the unvaccinated.
**In use:** "Measles needs roughly 95% coverage to hold herd immunity — a school sitting at 88% isn't close, it's structurally exposed."
**Common misuse:** Treating herd immunity as a fixed universal number (often mis-cited as "around 70% for everything") rather than a pathogen-specific threshold set by that disease's reproduction number.

### Population attributable fraction (PAF)
The share of disease incidence in a population that would be eliminated if a specific risk factor were removed, accounting for both the risk factor's relative risk and its prevalence.
**In use:** "Individually, the highest-risk smokers have the biggest personal risk, but obesity's PAF is larger here because it's so much more prevalent — that's where population-level intervention pays off first."
**Common misuse:** Prioritizing interventions by the risk factor with the highest relative risk in an individual, rather than the highest attributable fraction across the population, which depends equally on prevalence.

### Medical Removal Protection (MRP)
An OSHA-mandated provision (e.g., under the lead standard, 29 CFR 1910.1025) requiring a worker be removed from exposure, with rate and benefits protected for a defined period, once specified biological monitoring triggers are met.
**In use:** "The three-test average hit the MRP trigger even though no single BLL crossed 60 — removal isn't optional once that average threshold is met."
**Common misuse:** Checking only the single-value trigger and missing the separate rolling-average trigger that most exposure standards also carry.

### Permissible Exposure Limit (PEL) vs. Action Level
The PEL is the maximum airborne concentration a worker can legally be exposed to (typically as an 8-hour time-weighted average); the Action Level is a lower concentration that triggers monitoring and program requirements before the PEL is ever approached.
**In use:** "We're under the PEL, but we crossed the action level three months ago, so periodic monitoring and medical surveillance enrollment were already required starting then."
**Common misuse:** Treating "under the PEL" as equivalent to "no obligations triggered," when the Action Level (usually half the PEL) independently triggers monitoring duties well before the PEL matters.

### Epidemic curve (point-source, propagated, mixed)
A histogram of case onset dates used to infer transmission pattern: a point-source curve is a single tight peak roughly one incubation period wide; a propagated curve shows successive peaks spaced about one incubation period apart as person-to-person transmission continues.
**In use:** "The curve is one sharp peak two days wide — that's consistent with a single shared meal, not ongoing transmission; we should be interviewing about that one event, not contact-tracing forward."
**Common misuse:** Reading any case-count spike as evidence of an outbreak's shape without actually plotting onset dates, or misreading a mixed curve (initial point-source plus secondary spread) as pure point-source and stopping the investigation too early.

### Case definition (confirmed / probable / suspect)
A fixed, stated set of clinical, lab, and epidemiologic criteria that determines who counts as a case during a specific investigation, tiered by certainty of diagnosis.
**In use:** "We're counting probable cases in this line list too, but they're broken out separately from confirmed — don't let the combined total drive the trigger for closing the investigation."
**Common misuse:** Changing which tier of case definition gets counted partway through an investigation without a dated changelog, which silently invalidates before/after trend comparisons.

### DALY / QALY
Disability-Adjusted Life Year and Quality-Adjusted Life Year are the two standard units for combining length and quality of life into a single health-impact measure; DALYs count years lost to a condition (higher is worse), QALYs count years gained in good health from an intervention (higher is better).
**In use:** "The program buys about 40 QALYs a year in this population at current uptake — that's the number the cost-per-QALY line should be dividing by, not raw cases prevented."
**Common misuse:** Using DALYs and QALYs interchangeably in a single readout, or reporting cases prevented without converting to either unit, which makes cross-program cost-effectiveness comparison impossible.

### Fitness-for-duty determination
A physician's formal, binding judgment on whether an individual can safely perform the essential functions of a specific job or duty, distinct from a general clinical diagnosis or treatment opinion.
**In use:** "The determination is 'not fit for duty pending re-evaluation in 30 days' — that's a duty-specific finding, not a statement about the underlying diagnosis."
**Common misuse:** Treating a fitness-for-duty note as advisory guidance the employer or the individual can weigh alongside other factors, when it is a binding determination the physician's license stands behind.

### Aeromedical waiver / Special Issuance
An FAA process (under 14 CFR Part 67) allowing a pilot with a disqualifying medical condition to be certified anyway, subject to specific monitoring and reporting conditions defined case by case.
**In use:** "The applicant needs a Special Issuance for the cardiac history — standard certification isn't available, but a waiver with annual stress-test reporting is."
**Common misuse:** Treating a waiver as a one-time exception rather than an ongoing conditional certification with its own monitoring requirements that can be revoked if conditions aren't met.

### Sentinel surveillance
A surveillance design using a limited, pre-selected set of reporting sites (not the whole population) to detect trends efficiently, trading complete case ascertainment for speed and lower cost.
**In use:** "Our sentinel sites are showing a 20% uptick in influenza-like illness — that's a trend signal, not a population-wide case count, so don't report it as total cases."
**Common misuse:** Quoting sentinel-surveillance figures as if they represent total population case counts, when they're designed only to detect trend direction from a non-representative sample of sites.
