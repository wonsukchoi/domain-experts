# Marriage and family therapy working vocabulary

Terms a marriage and family therapist uses precisely and a generalist blurs together. Format: definition → how a practitioner says it → common misuse.

**Identified patient (IP)** — The family member presented as "the problem" or the reason treatment was sought, often not where the actual systemic pattern originates.
In use: "The daughter is the identified patient here, but the systemic assessment points to the marital conflict as a likely driver of her symptoms."
Misuse: treating the identified patient's presenting symptom as automatically the correct and complete treatment target, rather than as the entry point for assessing the broader system.

**Triangulation / detouring** — A structural family therapy concept describing when conflict or tension between two family members (often parents) gets redirected through or onto a third person (often a child), who may develop symptoms that stabilize the original dyad's conflict by giving them a shared focus.
In use: "The daughter's symptoms may be functioning as a detouring pattern — giving the parents a shared concern that displaces attention from their own conflict."
Misuse: assuming triangulation whenever a child has symptoms and parents have any conflict — it's a specific hypothesis to test via timeline correlation and family assessment, not an automatic conclusion from co-occurring facts.

**Multipartiality** — The technical stance of actively working to see, validate, and align with each family member's perspective without favoring one side, distinct from simple neutrality (which can read as disengaged) or true impartial detachment.
In use: "Multipartiality means actively validating each parent's experience in the same session, not just staying quiet and letting them argue it out."
Misuse: confusing multipartiality with passive neutrality — it's an active practice of engaging with and validating multiple perspectives, not simply refraining from taking a side.

**No-secrets policy** — An explicit policy, stated at intake, describing how the therapist will handle information disclosed by one family member individually that's relevant to the conjoint work, established before any individual/collateral sessions occur.
In use: "The no-secrets policy was stated at intake — when the affair came up in her individual session, we already had a framework for how to handle it."
Misuse: introducing a no-secrets expectation reactively after a secret has already been disclosed — the policy's value depends on being stated in advance, since introducing it after the fact can feel like a betrayal regardless of its content.

**Conjoint session vs. individual/collateral session** — A conjoint session includes multiple family members/partners together; an individual or collateral session involves one member alone, used for confidential disclosure, individual assessment, or targeted work (e.g., IPV screening).
In use: "We're screening for safety concerns in individual sessions with each partner before doing any conjoint work."
Misuse: assuming assessment done conjointly is equivalent to assessment done individually — some information (particularly around safety) is structurally unlikely to surface in front of the other party.

**Coercive control** — A pattern of behavior in an intimate relationship involving intimidation, isolation, and control over a partner's autonomy, distinct from mutual high-conflict disagreement, and a contraindication for standard conjoint couples therapy as primary treatment.
In use: "This isn't just high-conflict — the pattern of financial control and isolation from friends and family indicates coercive control, which changes the appropriate treatment approach entirely."
Misuse: treating any high-intensity couple conflict as equivalent to coercive control, or conversely dismissing coercive control indicators as "just conflict" — the distinction changes whether conjoint therapy is safe and appropriate at all.

**Genogram** — A structured, multi-generational diagram of a family's relationships, patterns, and significant events, used to identify recurring relational patterns across generations relevant to the current presenting concern.
In use: "The genogram shows a multigenerational pattern of triangulating the eldest child during marital stress — this isn't new to this generation."
Misuse: treating the genogram as merely a family tree for record-keeping rather than an active clinical assessment tool for identifying recurring systemic patterns.

**Enmeshment vs. disengagement (family systems)** — Enmeshment describes overly close, poorly differentiated family boundaries where individual autonomy is limited; disengagement describes overly rigid boundaries with limited emotional connection or communication — both are boundary-pattern concepts distinct from simple closeness or distance.
In use: "The family system reads as enmeshed around the parents' conflict — the daughter has limited space to have her own emotional experience separate from monitoring theirs."
Misuse: using "enmeshed" as a generic synonym for "close" or "involved" — it specifically describes a boundary problem where differentiation is compromised, not simply a family that spends time together or cares about each other.

**Differentiation of self (Bowenian)** — A Bowen family systems concept describing an individual's capacity to maintain their own sense of self and manage their own anxiety while staying emotionally connected to the family system, rather than being reactively fused to or cut off from it.
In use: "Part of the work here is helping the daughter develop more differentiation — staying connected to the family without her emotional state being wholly reactive to the parents' conflict."
Misuse: treating differentiation as synonymous with emotional distance or independence from family — it specifically describes maintaining connection while also maintaining one's own separate sense of self, not disengaging from the relationship.

**Danger assessment (IPV)** — A validated screening instrument or protocol assessing an individual's risk of serious harm or lethality within an intimate partner relationship, distinct from a general conflict-severity assessment.
In use: "The danger assessment findings are what determine whether conjoint work is safe to continue, not just how intense the arguments sound in session."
Misuse: relying on general impressions of conflict intensity as a substitute for a structured danger/safety assessment — lethality risk factors (weapon access, strangulation history, escalating frequency) require specific screening, not inference from general conflict tone.
