# Vocabulary

### ARAR (Applicable or Relevant and Appropriate Requirement)
A federal or state environmental standard, cleanup level, or permit requirement that a CERCLA remedy must meet — either because it directly applies (applicable) or is close enough in purpose and substance to be relevant and appropriate.
**In use:** "The state's groundwater standard is more stringent than the federal MCL, so it's the ARAR that drives the cleanup level here, not the MCL."
**Common misuse:** treating the federal MCL as automatically the governing number — a stricter state standard, or a to-be-considered (TBC) guidance value in the absence of an enforceable standard, frequently sets the actual cleanup level.

### Threshold criteria vs. balancing criteria
Under CERCLA's nine-criteria framework, protectiveness and ARAR compliance are threshold criteria an alternative must pass to be considered further; the other seven (five balancing, two modifying) are used to rank alternatives that already passed.
**In use:** "Alternative 2 doesn't clear the threshold criteria, so it's out of the comparison regardless of how well it scores on cost."
**Common misuse:** scoring all nine criteria on the same numeric scale and averaging — the threshold criteria are a gate, not a graded input.

### Conceptual Site Model (CSM)
The working hypothesis of how contamination moves from a source, through a pathway, to a receptor at a specific site — the basis for determining whether an exposure pathway is complete.
**In use:** "The CSM shows a completed pathway to the municipal well, which is why MNA alone won't satisfy the protectiveness criterion here."
**Common misuse:** treating a contamination extent map as equivalent to a CSM — a map showing where the chemical is doesn't establish that it reaches a receptor.

### Monitored Natural Attenuation (MNA)
A remedy relying on natural physical, chemical, or biological processes to reduce contamination, selected only with a monitoring program that confirms an actual declining trend.
**In use:** "MNA is defensible here on 3 years of declining-trend data with no completed pathway — it wouldn't have been a year ago."
**Common misuse:** proposing MNA as the low-cost default without the multi-year trend data most agencies require before treating "natural attenuation is occurring" as a demonstrated fact rather than a hope.

### Institutional control (IC) / Engineering control (EC)
Non-physical (deed restriction, zoning overlay, groundwater use prohibition) or physical (cap, fence, vapor barrier) measures that limit exposure where contamination remains above unrestricted-use levels.
**In use:** "The remedy relies on an IC restricting groundwater use — confirm it's recorded against the parcel before we call the ROD implemented."
**Common misuse:** treating a control cited in a planning document as equivalent to a control actually implemented — an IC that was never recorded, or an EC that was never inspected post-construction, provides no real protection despite appearing in the remedy description.

### Five-Year Review
The CERCLA-mandated periodic reassessment (§121(c)) of whether a remedy remains protective, required whenever contamination is left on site above levels allowing unrestricted use and unlimited exposure.
**In use:** "This site is due for its second Five-Year Review — pull the recorded ICs and the last three years of monitoring before drafting the protectiveness statement."
**Common misuse:** treating the review as a paperwork renewal rather than a substantive check of whether site conditions, land use, and controls still match the remedy's original assumptions.

### Reference reach / reference condition
A geomorphically or ecologically comparable, minimally-impacted stream reach or wetland used to set quantitative design targets and performance standards for a restoration project.
**In use:** "Channel dimensions are designed to the reference reach's entrenchment ratio and width/depth ratio, not an arbitrary 'natural-looking' template."
**Common misuse:** designing to a generic aesthetic of what a "restored" stream or wetland should look like, without a stated reference site — this leaves no falsifiable basis for judging whether post-construction monitoring shows success or failure.

### Rosgen stream classification
A geomorphic classification system (A through G stream types) categorizing channels by entrenchment, width/depth ratio, sinuosity, and slope, used to select a design template and predict channel evolution.
**In use:** "The existing reach classifies as an incised F4 — the design target is to restore it to its likely historic C4 form."
**Common misuse:** applying a classification-derived template without confirming the reference reach shares the same valley type and watershed drainage area — the same stream type can require different dimensions in different physiographic settings.

### Compensatory mitigation (restoration / enhancement / preservation / creation)
Actions taken to offset unavoidable wetland or stream impacts, categorized by the type and amount of new ecological function generated, which sets the credit ratio.
**In use:** "Preservation-only acreage generates far fewer credits per acre than restoration, since no new function is being created."
**Common misuse:** applying a flat credit ratio across restoration, enhancement, and preservation — banking instruments specify different ratios per activity type precisely because they create different amounts of new function.

### Habitat Equivalency Analysis (HEA) / Resource Equivalency Analysis (REA)
Natural resource damage assessment methods that scale the size of a restoration project to the value or quantity of ecological services lost, using discounted service-acre-years (HEA) or in-kind units (REA).
**In use:** "HEA puts the interim service loss at 420 discounted service-acre-years, which sets the restoration project's required scale, not the acreage of the original injury."
**Common misuse:** scaling restoration acreage 1:1 to the acreage of the injured habitat, instead of to the discounted service-loss calculation, which accounts for both the severity and duration of the interim loss.

### AACE cost estimate class (Class 1-5)
A cost-estimate accuracy classification (adapted to environmental projects via ASTM E2516) ranging from Class 5 (conceptual, widest range) to Class 1 (definitive, narrowest range), tied to how much design detail exists when the estimate is prepared.
**In use:** "This is a Class 4 estimate at the FS stage — expect the number to move -30%/+50% once we're into remedial design."
**Common misuse:** presenting an early-stage estimate's point value without its class or accuracy range, which invites the reader to treat a conceptual number as a firm budget.

### Record of Decision (ROD)
The formal document (or amendment) in which the lead agency selects and justifies a CERCLA remedy, documenting the nine-criteria comparison and the ARARs the remedy must meet.
**In use:** "The ROD names the groundwater use restriction as the sole institutional control — confirm it was actually recorded before we treat that commitment as satisfied."
**Common misuse:** treating the ROD as the end of the planning process rather than the start of an implementation and long-term-stewardship obligation that the Five-Year Review will later check against.
