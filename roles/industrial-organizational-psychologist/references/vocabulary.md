# Industrial-Organizational Psychologist — Vocabulary

### Adverse impact
A substantially different selection, promotion, or termination rate for members of a protected group compared to the group with the highest rate, typically screened using the 4/5ths rule.
**In use:** "The assessment shows adverse impact against Black applicants — ratio's 0.667, and it's statistically significant, not just a small-sample flag."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a synonym for intentional discrimination; it's a statistical pattern that can arise from a validated, job-related tool and still needs a business-necessity defense.

### Four-fifths (4/5ths) rule
The Uniform Guidelines' practical screen: a selection rate for any group below 80% of the rate for the highest-scoring group is treated as evidence of possible adverse impact.
**In use:** "20% divided by 30% is 0.667 — that's below the 4/5ths threshold, so we run the significance test next."
**Common misuse:** Applied as a legal bright line rather than a screening threshold; courts and regulators also weigh statistical significance and practical significance.

### Criterion-related validity
Evidence that scores on a predictor (test, interview) correlate with an actual outcome measure (job performance, tenure) — predictive (scores collected before hire) or concurrent (scores and performance collected at the same time, on incumbents).
**In use:** "We ran a concurrent criterion-related validity study — n=85 incumbents, corrected r=.43 against six-month sales performance."
**Common misuse:** Concurrent studies (on current employees) are treated as equivalent to predictive studies (on applicants) without acknowledging the restriction-of-range difference between the two samples.

### Content validity
Evidence that a test's content directly mirrors the tasks and requirements of the job, established through a job analysis rather than a statistical correlation.
**In use:** "The typing test has content validity — it's literally the data-entry task from the job analysis, timed the same way."
**Common misuse:** Claimed for tools (personality inventories, cognitive tests) that measure underlying constructs rather than direct task replicas — those need criterion or construct validity instead.

### Construct validity
Evidence that a test measures the underlying psychological trait or construct it claims to (e.g., conscientiousness), established through convergent/discriminant evidence, not job-task mirroring.
**In use:** "Before we lean on this personality scale, we need construct validity evidence that it actually measures conscientiousness and not just social desirability."
**Common misuse:** Used interchangeably with criterion-related validity; construct validity is about what the test measures, not whether that measurement predicts performance.

### Validity generalization (VG)
Meta-analytic evidence that a predictor's validity holds across jobs and settings, allowing transportability of validity evidence without a new local study.
**In use:** "Local N is only 22 — we're leaning on validity generalization from the Schmidt-Hunter GMA meta-analysis instead of reporting that unstable local r."
**Common misuse:** Cited to justify a tool for a job family the underlying meta-analysis never actually covered.

### Range restriction
Reduced variance in a validation sample because the people in it were already selected on the predictor (or a correlated one), which attenuates the observed correlation below its true value.
**In use:** "Raw r was .28, but that's on incumbents who already passed the assessment — corrected for range restriction it's .43."
**Common misuse:** Ignored entirely, leaving validity coefficients compared across studies with very different degrees of pre-selection as if they were on the same scale.

### Reliability (of a measure)
The consistency of a measurement — test-retest, internal consistency, or interrater — which sets a mathematical ceiling on the validity coefficient that measure can show.
**In use:** "Interrater reliability on these performance ratings is .60 — that caps what any predictor can correlate with, regardless of how good the test is."
**Common misuse:** Conflated with validity; a highly reliable test can still be invalid (consistently measuring the wrong thing), and low criterion reliability can make a valid predictor look weak.

### KSAOs
Knowledge, Skills, Abilities, and Other characteristics — the job-analysis-derived attributes a selection tool is built to measure.
**In use:** "The KSAO list for this role came straight off the task inventory, not a generic competency library."
**Common misuse:** Written from a job title or a borrowed competency model rather than from an actual task analysis of the specific position.

### Job analysis
The systematic study of a job's tasks, responsibilities, and required KSAOs, forming the evidentiary foundation for content validity and test specifications.
**In use:** "We can't defend this test's content validity — the job analysis it's based on is six years old and the role's changed since."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a one-time formality rather than something that needs updating when the job changes materially.

### Banding
Grouping test scores within a statistically defined range (often based on the standard error of measurement) and treating scores within a band as functionally equivalent, rather than ranking by exact score.
**In use:** "We're banding at ±1 SEM around the cut score instead of a hard cutoff — it reduces adverse impact at the margin without discarding the validated ranking."
**Common misuse:** Confused with quota-based selection; banding still selects on the assessment, it just stops over-interpreting small, unreliable score differences as meaningful.

### Disparate treatment vs. disparate impact
Two distinct legal theories: disparate treatment is intentional differential treatment of a protected group; disparate impact is a facially neutral practice that produces a differential outcome regardless of intent.
**In use:** "This isn't a disparate treatment claim — nobody was treated differently on purpose — it's a disparate impact question about whether the test itself is job-related."
**Common misuse:** Used interchangeably, which matters because the legal defenses differ — disparate impact can be defended with job-relatedness/business-necessity evidence; disparate treatment generally cannot.

### Transportability
The argument, and its supporting evidence, that a validity study conducted for one job/organization applies to another sufficiently similar job/organization without a new local study.
**In use:** "The transportability argument here rests on the job analyses showing 80%+ task overlap between the two roles."
**Common misuse:** Asserted informally ("it's basically the same job") without the documented task-comparison evidence the Uniform Guidelines actually require.

### Standard error of measurement (SEM)
The estimated amount of error in an individual's observed test score, used to judge whether score differences near a cutoff are meaningful or noise.
**In use:** "Two candidates three points apart, but that's well within one SEM — treat them as tied for this decision."
**Common misuse:** Ignored when setting cut scores, producing hard cutoffs that split statistically indistinguishable candidates into pass/fail.

### Differential prediction
A situation where a predictor's regression relationship with the criterion (slope or intercept) differs by subgroup, meaning the same test score implies different predicted performance for different groups.
**In use:** "Overall validity looks fine, but we haven't checked differential prediction — the regression line could still differ by subgroup underneath that aggregate number."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to be covered by an adverse-impact check; differential prediction is a separate analysis on the predictor-criterion relationship itself, not on selection rates.
