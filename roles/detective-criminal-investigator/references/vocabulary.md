# Detective / Criminal Investigator — Vocabulary

### Probable cause
The legal threshold required to obtain a warrant or make an arrest — a totality-of-circumstances standard, not a percentage-confidence test.
**In use:** "We've got probable cause for the search warrant, but we're nowhere near proof beyond a reasonable doubt yet."
**Common misuse:** Treated as roughly "more likely than not" (a 51% threshold), when it's actually a lower, non-quantified standard assessed on the combined weight of facts, not any single fact meeting a percentage bar.

### Totality of circumstances
The *Illinois v. Gates* standard for evaluating probable cause — the combined weight of all known facts, not a checklist where each fact must independently clear a bar.
**In use:** "That one fact is weak on its own, but under totality of circumstances with the other three, it supports the warrant."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a single weak or ambiguous fact automatically sinks probable cause, rather than assessing it in combination with everything else known.

### Tunnel vision
The investigative failure mode of forming a case theory and then unconsciously seeking only confirming evidence while discounting or not pursuing disconfirming evidence.
**In use:** "Before we charge, walk me through what we did to try to disprove this theory — I don't want tunnel vision on the record."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a character flaw or sign of bad faith, when it's a structural cognitive bias any investigator is susceptible to without a documented disproof step.

### PEACE model
A structured, non-accusatorial interview method (Preparation and Planning, Engage and Explain, Account, Closure, Evaluate) focused on gathering an account before challenging it.
**In use:** "We ran the interview PEACE-style — full account first, then we went back through the inconsistencies."
**Common misuse:** Conflated with simply "being polite" during questioning, when it's a specific structural sequence distinct from accusatorial interrogation.

### Reid technique
A widely taught accusatorial interrogation method that presents the interviewer's conclusion of guilt early and uses permitted forms of deception about evidence to elicit admissions.
**In use:** "That confession came out of a Reid-style interview with no independent corroboration — we can't treat it as case-closing on its own."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to be the neutral, standard, risk-free approach, when the interrogation-psychology literature documents elevated false-confession risk in vulnerable interviewees under this method.

### Chain of custody
The documented, unbroken record of who possessed a piece of evidence and when, from collection through use in court.
**In use:** "I need the chain of custody on that phone from the moment we seized it, not just from when the lab logged it in."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to only matter once evidence reaches a lab, when a gap at the point of initial collection or transport is just as damaging.

### Corroboration
Independent evidence that supports a claim (such as a confession or eyewitness account) without relying on that same claim as its own proof.
**In use:** "We have a confession, but no corroboration — no non-public detail, no physical evidence tying him to the scene."
**Common misuse:** Treating a confession as self-corroborating simply because it's detailed or confidently delivered.

### CSLI (cell-site location information)
Historical records of which cell tower/sector a device connected to at a given time, used to approximate a device's location.
**In use:** "CSLI puts his phone in this sector at 11:50, which is a 1.8-mile radius, not a street address."
**Common misuse:** Presented or received as GPS-level precision (a specific address or room), when it typically resolves only to a tower's coverage area.

### Franks challenge
A defense motion under *Franks v. Delaware* arguing that a probable-cause affidavit contained a knowing or reckless false statement or material omission, potentially invalidating the resulting warrant.
**In use:** "If we leave that fact out of the affidavit and it surfaces later, that's a live Franks challenge."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to require an outright lie in the affidavit, when a material omission of a known fact can also support the challenge.

### Exculpatory evidence
Any evidence that could reasonably tend to weaken the case against a suspect, not only evidence that affirmatively proves innocence.
**In use:** "That inconsistent witness statement is exculpatory — it goes in the file and gets disclosed, whether or not it changes our theory."
**Common misuse:** Narrowed to mean only evidence that definitively clears someone, missing the broader *Brady*-material scope of anything that could undercut the prosecution's case.

### Confirmation bias
The unconscious tendency to notice, weight, and recall evidence that supports an existing belief more readily than evidence that contradicts it.
**In use:** "Let's get a second set of eyes on this file — I want a confirmation-bias check before we finalize the theory."
**Common misuse:** Framed as deliberate dishonesty, when it typically operates unconsciously in how evidence gets noticed and weighted, not as an intentional choice to ignore facts.

### Corpus delicti
The legal principle that a crime must be proven to have actually occurred before a conviction can rest on a confession alone.
**In use:** "We can't charge on the confession alone — we still need corpus delicti that a crime actually happened here."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to literally mean "the body," a holdover from homicide cases, when the principle applies to proving any offense occurred, not a physical corpse.
