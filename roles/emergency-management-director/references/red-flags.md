# Red flags — what an Emergency Management Director notices instantly

Smell tests with thresholds. Format per flag: signal → what it usually means → the first question a veteran asks → the data to pull.

### Shelter arrivals exceeding 150% of the planned compliance-rate estimate within the first 6 hours of opening
- **Usually means:** the public messaging undersold self-sufficiency options (hotel/family) or overstated storm severity relative to what residents can judge for themselves, or a competing shelter closed/never opened and traffic diverted here.
- **First question:** "Is this one site over capacity, or is every site running the same overage?" (One site = local messaging or access problem; all sites = the compliance-rate assumption itself was wrong.)
- **Data to pull:** intake logs by site and hour, status of all other designated shelters, any last-minute public messaging changes or road closures that funneled evacuees to one location.

### Local emergency declaration signed but no Preliminary Damage Assessment fieldwork scheduled within 5 days of safe site access
- **Usually means:** the declaration was treated as the finish line instead of the trigger; nobody assigned PDA logistics, or the state liaison relationship wasn't activated in time.
- **First question:** "Who owns scheduling the joint PDA team, and what's blocking it?"
- **Data to pull:** declaration timeline log, state EM agency correspondence, damage assessment team roster and assigned dates.

### ICS-214 unit logs with a gap longer than one operational period (12 hrs) during active response
- **Usually means:** either nothing happened in that window (unlikely during active response) or logging discipline broke down — usually at a shift change where the outgoing lead didn't brief documentation duties to the incoming one.
- **First question:** "Walk me through what happened in that window from memory, right now, before it fades further."
- **Data to pull:** shift-change ICS-201 briefings, radio/dispatch logs for the same window as a cross-check, whoever was Planning Section Chief during the gap.

### A debris removal or emergency protective measures contract mobilized without a dated pre-mobilization procurement justification
- **Usually means:** the emergency (non-competitive) procurement exception was used correctly operationally but not documented correctly — the justification memo was written after the fact to match the contract, which reads as fabricated to a PA auditor even when the underlying decision was sound.
- **First question:** "Do we have a memo dated before mobilization, and who signed it?"
- **Data to pull:** procurement file timestamps, purchase order date vs. contractor mobilization date, emergency procurement policy on file.

### EMAC or mutual aid resources deployed before a reimbursement/cost agreement is executed
- **Usually means:** the operational urgency was real and the ask was right, but the paperwork sequence was skipped under pressure — this is the single most common cause of a disallowed mutual-aid cost.
- **First question:** "Is the signed agreement in hand yet, and if not, who's chasing it today, not after demobilization?"
- **Data to pull:** EMAC REQ-A form, executed reimbursement agreement, deployment start date compared against agreement execution date.

### Non-federal cost-share obligation not appropriated by the Board within 30 days of declaration approval
- **Usually means:** the Board or CFO is waiting for federal funds to arrive first, misunderstanding that PA reimburses against money already spent, not in advance.
- **First question:** "Has Finance modeled the cash-flow gap between when we spend the match and when FEMA reimburses the 75%?"
- **Data to pull:** budget amendment status, reserve fund balance, PA disbursement timeline for comparable prior declarations in this state.

### A tabletop or functional exercise After-Action Report with zero findings, or all findings rated "minor"
- **Usually means:** the exercise was scripted to succeed — no meaningful injects, evaluators grading participant performance instead of the plan, or a facilitator who is also the plan's author reluctant to expose its gaps.
- **First question:** "What inject was designed to fail, and what happened when it did?"
- **Data to pull:** the Situation Manual and inject list, evaluator assignments (check they weren't also plan authors), the exercise objectives as originally written.

### Improvement Plan items with no named owner or no deadline more than 45 days after an incident or exercise
- **Usually means:** the AAR was produced to close out the exercise/incident administratively, not to actually change anything — unowned findings have close to zero implementation rate.
- **First question:** "Who owns each open item, by name, and what's the date?"
- **Data to pull:** the Improvement Plan tracker, comparison against the prior AAR's IP items to check for repeats (a repeated finding is the strongest signal the process itself is broken).

### Continuity of Operations Plan invoked with an order of succession only one deep for an essential function
- **Usually means:** the COOP plan was written to satisfy a compliance checklist, not tested against the actual scenario where the primary and the first backup are both unavailable (a regional event often takes out both).
- **First question:** "If the named successor is also affected by this event, who's next, and do they know it?"
- **Data to pull:** the COOP essential functions annex, last COOP exercise/drill date, current contact roster for succession positions.

### Incident Commander with more than 7 direct reports during an active operational period
- **Usually means:** span of control wasn't adjusted as the incident grew — a structure sized for the initial response wasn't scaled with additional Branches/Divisions as the operation expanded.
- **First question:** "What's stopping us from standing up a Branch or Division right now to split this load?"
- **Data to pull:** current ICS organization chart, time since last org-chart revision, Planning Section's span-of-control assessment from the last operational period briefing.

### Two different evacuation zone maps or shelter lists circulating from different agencies at the same time
- **Usually means:** the Joint Information Center wasn't actually joint — a partner agency (state DOT, a neighboring municipality, a school district) pushed its own public communication without clearing it through the single messaging channel.
- **First question:** "Which map is the JIC's approved version, and how fast can we get the conflicting one corrected publicly?"
- **Data to pull:** JIC-approved messaging log with timestamps, the conflicting source's publication time, and the JIC's public correction issued afterward.
