# Emergency management working vocabulary

Terms an EM director uses precisely and generalists misuse. Format: definition → how a practitioner says it → common misuse.

**Unified Command** — A structure where multiple agencies or jurisdictions with legal authority over parts of the incident jointly set objectives and priorities in one Incident Action Plan, each retaining command of their own personnel.
In use: "We stood up Unified Command with the Sheriff, Fire, and the state DOT liaison — one IAP, three chains of command underneath it."
Misuse: treating Unified Command as one person (the EM director) giving orders to everyone. It's shared decision-making, not a rank above the agency heads.

**Incident Commander (IC) vs. EOC Director** — The IC runs the tactical, field-level response (a single incident or Unified Command). The EOC Director/EM Director runs the Emergency Operations Center — coordination, resource support, policy interface — and does not direct field tactics.
In use: "The Fire Chief is IC on the wildfire; I'm running the EOC getting him resources and keeping the Board briefed."
Misuse: assuming the EM director is "in charge" of the incident in a tactical sense. The EOC supports the incident; it doesn't command it.

**Span of control** — The number of people or elements one supervisor directly manages; NIMS recommends a 1:3–1:7 ratio, with 1:5 as the optimal target.
In use: "The Ops Section Chief is at 1:9 right now — time to activate a Branch and split the load."
Misuse: letting an org chart grow flat during a large incident because "everyone already knows their job" — span of control failures are a leading cause of missed coordination, not a paperwork nicety.

**Preliminary Damage Assessment (PDA)** — A joint local/state/FEMA field survey quantifying damage after an incident, used to test eligibility for a Presidential major disaster declaration against a per-capita damage threshold.
In use: "PDA came back at $38.4M — we clear the per-capita indicator with plenty of margin, so the Governor's request is well supported."
Misuse: treating the PDA as a formality that always leads to a declaration. A PDA that comes in near the threshold, not comfortably over it, can still be denied.

**Per-capita impact indicator** — FEMA's dollar-per-resident damage threshold (published annually, indexed to a construction cost index), applied at both the statewide and county level, used as the quantitative gate for declaration requests.
In use: "The county indicator this year is roughly $4.72 — at 310,000 residents that's about $1.46M; we need real margin above that, not a narrow clear."
Misuse: quoting a stale year's indicator, or comparing county damage against the (much lower per-capita, much larger denominator) statewide indicator instead of the county-level one.

**Public Assistance (PA) Categories A–G** — FEMA's damage classification: A = debris removal, B = emergency protective measures (the two "emergency work" categories, often eligible for accelerated or enhanced cost share), C = roads/bridges, D = water control facilities, E = buildings/equipment, F = utilities, G = parks/recreational/other — the five "permanent work" categories.
In use: "Category A and B costs get tracked and can move fast; C through G go into long-form project worksheets and take longer to finalize."
Misuse: lumping all PA costs together as "FEMA money," obscuring that emergency work (A/B) and permanent work (C–G) have different documentation timelines and sometimes different cost-share treatment.

**Individual Assistance (IA) / Individuals and Households Program (IHP)** — A separate FEMA assistance track (not PA) for individuals/households, capped per household (an inflation-indexed maximum, roughly $40k+ in recent years) covering temporary housing and essential needs — distinct from public infrastructure reimbursement.
In use: "PA covers our roads and buildings; IA is what gets a household with storm-damaged housing a temporary rental assistance grant — separate declaration request, separate dollars."
Misuse: conflating IA with PA, or assuming a jurisdiction automatically gets IA designation alongside PA — they're requested and evaluated separately.

**Cost share / non-federal match** — The percentage of eligible PA costs the federal government does not cover; standard is 75% federal / 25% non-federal, sometimes enhanced (occasionally to 90% or a temporary 100% on emergency work) by a separate, discretionary request.
In use: "We're budgeting the standard 75/25 — if the enhanced share for Category A/B comes through, that's upside, not the plan."
Misuse: assuming an enhanced cost share is the default, or budgeting as if it's approved before FEMA confirms it in writing.

**EMAC (Emergency Management Assistance Compact)** — The interstate mutual aid system (all 50 states plus territories) for requesting personnel and equipment across state lines during a declared emergency, with built-in reimbursement mechanisms.
In use: "We filed an EMAC request for a generator strike team — budgeting 48 hours to boots-on-ground."
Misuse: treating EMAC as instant, or as a substitute for exhausting closer local/regional mutual aid and internal resources first.

**Mutual aid vs. EMAC vs. Direct Federal Assistance** — Three distinct escalation tiers: mutual aid is pre-arranged local/regional agreements (fastest); EMAC is interstate, state-to-state (hours to days); Direct Federal Assistance is a federal agency directly performing work when no lower tier can (typically slowest to arrange, but sometimes the only option for specialized capability).
In use: "We're covered on mutual aid for the first period, EMAC crews land day 2, and we're not requesting Direct Federal Assistance unless the airlift capability isn't available any other way."
Misuse: calling any inbound outside help "FEMA resources" — most mutual aid and even EMAC assistance isn't a federal asset at all.

**Continuity of Operations (COOP)** — A plan ensuring essential government/organizational functions continue during a disruption, including orders of succession, delegations of authority, and an alternate facility.
In use: "COOP activates at the 12-hour mark if the EOC building itself is unusable — alternate site is the community college, three deep on succession for every essential function lead."
Misuse: writing a COOP plan with a succession list one name deep, which fails in exactly the regional event most likely to trigger COOP in the first place.

**Operational period / Incident Action Plan (IAP)** — A set time block (commonly 12 or 24 hours) with its own written objectives, resource assignments, and safety message, approved at the start of each period.
In use: "This operational period's IAP has debris clearance on the two arterial roads as the top Operations objective."
Misuse: running an incident off a single Day 1 plan without refreshing the IAP each period as conditions change.

**After-Action Report / Improvement Plan (AAR/IP)** — HSEEP-structured documents: the AAR analyzes what happened against objectives; the IP converts findings into corrective actions with owners and deadlines.
In use: "AAR flagged the comms gap at shift change; IP item assigns the Planning Section Chief to update the shift-change checklist by next quarter."
Misuse: producing an AAR as a narrative summary with no IP, or an IP with findings but no named owner — either version has a low real-world implementation rate.

**Debris monitoring** — Third-party (or trained staff) verification of debris removal volumes at collection and disposal, via load tickets and site photos, required for PA reimbursement of Category A costs.
In use: "Monitoring firm reconciled load tickets to disposal tonnage before we submitted the Category A project worksheet."
Misuse: relying on the hauling contractor's own volume reporting without independent monitoring — a common basis for PA disallowance on audit.
