# Private Detective and Investigator — Vocabulary

### Permissible purpose
The FCRA-defined legal justification (employment, tenancy, credit, insurance underwriting, licensing) required before pulling a consumer report or credit-header data on a subject.
**In use:** "We can't run that as a consumer report — the client doesn't have a permissible purpose, so we're limited to public-records-only research."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a formality to document after the fact, rather than a gate that determines whether the search can legally happen at all.

### One-party consent / two-party consent
State-law classification of whether recording a conversation requires only one participant's consent (often the investigator's own) or all participants' consent.
**In use:** "This state is two-party consent, so we can't record the call even though our client is one of the parties on the line."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to be uniform nationally, or assumed based on the client's home state rather than the state where the recording actually occurs.

### Skip trace
The process of locating a subject who has moved or is avoiding contact, using converging public-records and database sources rather than a single lookup.
**In use:** "The skip trace turned up three possible addresses — we corroborate before billing surveillance hours at any of them."
**Common misuse:** Treated as synonymous with a single database query, when it's properly a corroboration process across multiple independent sources.

### Pretext / pretexting
Using a false cover story to obtain information or confirm a location, legal within narrow bounds (e.g., confirming someone's employment by posing as a prospective employer's HR contact is often permissible; impersonating law enforcement or a government official is never permissible).
**In use:** "The pretext call confirmed the subject still works there — we didn't ask anything beyond employment confirmation."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a blanket-legal technique rather than one with hard boundaries (impersonating officials, obtaining financial records under GLBA) that carry criminal exposure.

### Corroboration
Confirming a finding through at least one additional independent source before reporting it as established rather than probable.
**In use:** "One utility hit is a lead. We need corroboration — vehicle registration or a second independent source — before we call this a confirmed address."
**Common misuse:** Skipped under time pressure, with a single-source finding reported with the same confidence as a corroborated one.

### Chain of custody
Documented, unbroken record of who has held and accessed a piece of evidence (photo, recording, document) from collection to delivery, required for it to be usable in litigation.
**In use:** "The video file's chain of custody starts at the camera's timestamp and ends at the client handoff — no unlogged copies in between."
**Common misuse:** Applied only to physical evidence, when digital files (photos, recordings) require the same documented handling to survive an authenticity challenge.

### Consumer report
An FCRA-covered report on a consumer's creditworthiness, character, or reputation, which triggers specific disclosure and permissible-purpose requirements when compiled by a third party for certain use cases.
**In use:** "If this report is going to be used for employment purposes, it's a consumer report under FCRA and needs a signed disclosure — public-records research alone doesn't."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to only mean a formal credit score, when FCRA's definition is broader and covers background-check content used for the covered purposes.

### Represented party
An individual who has retained an attorney in an active legal matter, triggering bar-rule restrictions on direct contact by opposing parties (and their agents, including investigators).
**In use:** "The subject is a represented party in the divorce — any contact needs to go through opposing counsel, not a direct approach."
**Common misuse:** Overlooked when the investigator is retained by one side and doesn't realize the ethical restriction applies to their contact with the other side too.

### Public vantage point
A surveillance observation location on public property or with legitimate public/invited access (sidewalk, public parking lot, a location the investigator has permission to be), as opposed to trespass.
**In use:** "We held the surveillance from the public vantage point across the street — never stepped onto the subject's property."
**Common misuse:** Stretched to include locations with ambiguous access (a shared apartment-complex hallway, a business's back lot) without confirming actual public or authorized status.

### Bond / licensing
The state-mandated license and surety bond required to legally operate as a private investigator, varying significantly by state in requirements and renewal cadence.
**In use:** "Our bond covers claims up to $10,000 — that's part of what a client is paying for when they hire a licensed investigator over an unlicensed one."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a bureaucratic formality rather than the mechanism that gives a client recourse and gives the investigator standing to gather admissible evidence.

### Locate confirmation
The specific, narrow act of confirming a subject's current address or whereabouts, distinct from a full background investigation.
**In use:** "The client only paid for a locate confirmation — full background research is a separate, additional-scope engagement."
**Common misuse:** Conflated with a comprehensive background check, leading to scope creep when a client assumes "finding someone" includes everything else.
