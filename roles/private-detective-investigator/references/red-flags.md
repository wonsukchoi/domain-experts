# Private Detective and Investigator — Red Flags

### Client requests audio recording without confirming jurisdiction's consent law
- **Usually means:** the client (or the investigator, if rushed) is defaulting to a one-party-consent assumption that may not hold in a two-party-consent state.
- **First question:** "Which state's law governs where the recording will actually take place — not where the client lives?"
- **Data to pull:** state wiretap/eavesdropping statute text; confirm the physical location of both parties at time of recording, not just one.

### Client can't articulate a specific, legally permissible purpose for a background check
- **Usually means:** the request is personal curiosity, a custody/relationship dispute proxy, or a purpose that would trigger FCRA if a consumer report is pulled.
- **First question:** "What decision will you make based on this report, and does that decision fall under FCRA's permissible-purpose categories?"
- **Data to pull:** signed purpose-and-use statement from client before running any credit-header or FCRA-covered search.

### Located-subject conclusion rests on a single data-broker/people-search hit
- **Usually means:** the address is stale, purchased-list data, or belongs to a relative/former address still indexed.
- **First question:** "What's the source date on this hit, and what's the second corroborating source?"
- **Data to pull:** utility connection date, vehicle registration date, most recent social-media activity timestamp.

### Client asks for GPS tracking of a vehicle not titled to them or a consenting party
- **Usually means:** the client is trying to track a spouse, ex-partner, or third party without legal standing to consent to tracking that vehicle.
- **First question:** "Whose name is on the title and registration, and do they know about and consent to this tracking?"
- **Data to pull:** DMV registration record; state GPS-tracking consent statute (varies — several states require registered-owner or co-owner consent).

### Surveillance vantage point requires stepping onto private property
- **Usually means:** the only clear sightline is from a location without public or invited access.
- **First question:** "Is there a public easement, sidewalk, or client-authorized-access vantage point that gets 80% of the same coverage?"
- **Data to pull:** property-line/easement records for the observation location.

### Field log shows gaps or was reconstructed after the shift rather than written in real time
- **Usually means:** the investigator lost visual, took an unlogged break, or is filling in details from memory under deposition-prep pressure later.
- **First question:** "Was this written contemporaneously, and are the gap periods marked explicitly?"
- **Data to pull:** timestamped photo/video metadata cross-checked against the written log's stated times.

### Report characterizes the subject's intent or state of mind rather than observed facts
- **Usually means:** the investigator is drawing a conclusion the observed evidence doesn't fully support, creating a credibility target for opposing counsel.
- **First question:** "What was directly observed, versus what is being inferred?"
- **Data to pull:** rewrite the finding as an observation statement (what/when/where) and check whether the conclusory language adds anything the facts don't already show.

### Engagement scope expands mid-investigation without a written amendment
- **Usually means:** the client is verbally asking for something (a new subject, a new method like audio) beyond the signed engagement letter.
- **First question:** "Is this within the original scope, or does it need a written amendment and a legality check before I proceed?"
- **Data to pull:** original engagement letter's scope section; compare against the new request line by line.

### Criminal-records search returns no hits and the finding is reported as "clean record"
- **Usually means:** the search covered available public databases, which have systematic gaps (sealed records, expungements, jurisdictions not covered by the aggregator).
- **First question:** "What jurisdictions and record types did this search actually cover, and what's known to be excluded?"
- **Data to pull:** the specific databases queried and their documented coverage limitations.
