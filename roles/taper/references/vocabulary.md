# Vocabulary

### Level of finish
The GA-214 scale (0–5) defining exactly which elements — joints, fasteners, and at Level 5, the entire face — receive how many coats of compound.
**In use:** "Corridor's Level 5, storage room's Level 4 — don't skim the storage room, that's wasted labor."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a general quality adjective ("good finish" vs. "rough finish") instead of the specific contract scope it defines; generalists assume higher is always better rather than matched to paint sheen and lighting.

### Hot mud
Job-site term for setting-type joint compound (Durabond, Easy Sand), named for the exothermic chemical reaction as it hardens — distinct from drying-type compound, which hardens by water evaporating.
**In use:** "Use 90-minute hot mud on the fill coat so we can finish-coat before we leave the floor."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to be interchangeable with any "fast" compound; the number in the name (20/45/90) is the working time in minutes before it sets, and using the wrong grade for the task either sets before the crew finishes spreading it or holds up the schedule.

### Photographing
A joint, fastener, or seam that becomes visible through the finished paint under raking or angled light, even though it looked flat under normal work lighting.
**In use:** "That corridor's photographing bad under the can lights — we skipped the wide feather coat."
**Common misuse:** Confused with a physical defect visible in all lighting; photographing is specifically a raking-light phenomenon and the fix (wider feather, higher finish level) differs from fixing a genuinely uneven surface.

### Feathering
Tapering a coat of compound progressively thinner from the joint outward so there's no perceptible edge for light to catch.
**In use:** "Widen that feather to 16 inches on the finish coat, this wall's getting semi-gloss."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a cosmetic nicety that can be shortened under schedule pressure; the feather width is what determines whether the joint photographs, not the number of coats alone.

### Butt joint
A joint between two non-tapered (factory-cut) board ends, as opposed to the factory-tapered long edges of a sheet.
**In use:** "Back-block that butt joint before board goes up, it'll ridge otherwise."
**Common misuse:** Finished with the same technique and feather width as a tapered joint; butt joints have no recessed taper to absorb the tape and compound thickness, so they need wider feathering and often back-blocking to avoid a visible ridge.

### Skim coat
A thin, uniform coat of compound applied over an entire wall or ceiling face, not just the joints — the defining extra step of Level 5 finish.
**In use:** "This whole room's getting a skim coat, designer wants zero texture under the accent lighting."
**Common misuse:** Used loosely to mean "one more coat over the joints"; a skim coat specifically covers the full face, and calling a wide finish coat over joints a "skim coat" misrepresents the scope actually delivered.

### Bazooka / banjo
Mechanical taping tools — the bazooka (mud tube) embeds tape and compound in one pass along a joint; the banjo is a smaller hand-fed version — used to speed tape embedding on production commercial runs.
**In use:** "Run the bazooka down that corridor ceiling, hand-taping 400 feet will kill the schedule."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to replace hand-finishing entirely; these tools embed the tape coat only — fill, finish, and feather coats are still applied by hand or flat box regardless of how the tape went up.

### Corner bead
Metal, vinyl, or paper-faced trim installed at outside corners before finishing to protect the edge and give the compound a straight line to feather to.
**In use:** "Paper-faced bead on this corner, it's getting a level-5 skim and metal will show through eventually."
**Common misuse:** Selected purely on cost rather than location; metal bead dents and eventually shows through a smooth skim-coated finish in high-traffic areas where paper-faced or a more impact-resistant bead type holds up better.

### All-purpose compound
A drying-type joint compound formulated as a single product for both taping and topping coats, as opposed to separate taping and topping formulations.
**In use:** "We're running all-purpose start to finish on this job, no separate topping."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to be a lower-quality shortcut compared to a dedicated taping-then-topping system; all-purpose is a legitimate standard system for many jobs, but it is not a substitute for setting-type compound when the schedule or joint type calls for one.

### Working time
The number in a setting-type compound's name (e.g., "Durabond 90") indicating approximately how many minutes the mixed compound stays workable before it begins to set.
**In use:** "Mix a small batch of 20 if you're only patching that one spot, no point fighting a 90 for five minutes of work."
**Common misuse:** Confused with total cure time or paintability time; working time is only how long the mud can be spread before hardening — it still needs additional time after that before it's fully hard enough to sand or recoat.

### Level 2 finish
The GA-214 tier calling for a tape coat plus one additional coat over joints and fasteners, with no third coat and no full-face treatment.
**In use:** "Garage only needs a Level 2, it's not getting painted with any sheen."
**Common misuse:** Conflated with Level 3 or treated as "basically Level 4 but cheaper"; Level 2 is a specific, lower coat count intended for areas that won't be under close visual inspection, not a discount version of a higher level.

### Wet sponge
A finishing technique using a damp sponge instead of dry sanding to smooth compound between or after coats, primarily to control dust.
**In use:** "Wet-sponge the occupied floors, we can't be running sanders with tenants working next door."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to produce an identical result to dry sanding; wet sponging generally leaves a slightly less flat finish than a properly dry-sanded coat and is a tradeoff for dust control, not a strict upgrade.
