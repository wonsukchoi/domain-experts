# Vocabulary

Terms students and generalists conflate that a history instructor keeps sharply separate — the value is in the misuse each entry corrects.

## Sourcing

The practice of interrogating a document's origin — who wrote it, when, for whom, and why — before evaluating what it claims. The foundational move in Wineburg's historical-thinking framework.

**In use:** "Before we ask what this pamphlet says about the massacre, source it — who published it, and what did the Sons of Liberty need Bostonians to believe?"

**Common misuse:** reading a primary source for its stated content first and treating authorship/audience/purpose as background trivia to mention afterward, rather than the lens that determines how much weight the content deserves.

## Corroboration

Comparing multiple independent sources on the same event to see where they agree and where they diverge — divergence is often the historically interesting finding, not a problem to resolve into one clean narrative.

**In use:** "The two eyewitness accounts disagree on who fired first — that disagreement is the finding, not something to average away."

**Common misuse:** treating corroboration as a search for a single "correct" combined story, rather than mapping where and why accounts genuinely conflict.

## Contextualization

Situating a document or event within the conditions of its own time — what was normal, contested, or assumed by contemporaries — rather than isolating it as a standalone artifact.

**In use:** "You can't read this 1850s pro-slavery pamphlet's economic argument without the context of the cotton boom it's defending."

**Common misuse:** supplying textbook background facts as filler rather than using context to explain why the document says what it says, in the terms available to its author.

## Presentism

Evaluating past actors, decisions, or societies primarily by the moral, legal, or scientific standards of the present, rather than by what was normative or knowable in their own moment.

**In use:** "That reading is presentist — ask what his contemporaries who opposed him actually argued, using the terms available in 1850, not ours."

**Common misuse:** conflating "explaining a past actor's reasoning in its own context" with "excusing or endorsing" that reasoning — contextualizing is not the same as approving.

## Anachronism

Placing an idea, object, technology, or awareness into a period before it existed or was available — a factual/analytical error distinct from presentism's evaluative bias, though the two often appear together.

**In use:** "Crediting the Founders with 'seeing' the abolition movement decades before it organized is an anachronism — that political awareness didn't exist yet."

**Common misuse:** using "anachronism" and "presentism" interchangeably — anachronism is misplacing a fact in time; presentism is judging by the wrong era's values.

## Historiography vs. history

**History** is the study of past events. **Historiography** is the study of how historians have interpreted and argued about those events over time — the debate about the debate.

**In use:** "The paper needs a historiography paragraph — not just what happened at Gettysburg, but how the 'Lost Cause' interpretation shaped how it was written about for a century."

**Common misuse:** using "historiography" to mean "the history of the event" rather than "the history of how the event has been interpreted and argued over."

## Primary vs. secondary source

A **primary source** was produced during or immediately after the event by someone with direct knowledge of it (a letter, a government record, a photograph). A **secondary source** analyzes or interprets primary sources after the fact (a scholarly monograph, a textbook).

**In use:** "A 1960s oral-history interview about the 1930s is still primary — it's a firsthand account, even though it was recorded decades later."

**Common misuse:** assuming "old" means primary and "recent" means secondary — age is not the distinguishing feature; the author's direct relationship to the event is.

## Periodization

The scholarly act of dividing history into named eras or periods (Antebellum, Progressive Era, Cold War) — a historian's argument about where meaningful breaks occur, not a neutral, given fact.

**In use:** "Where you draw the line between 'Reconstruction' and 'Redemption' is itself a historiographical claim about when the period's defining project ended."

**Common misuse:** treating period boundaries as fixed, objective facts rather than as interpretive choices that different historians draw differently for different arguments.

## Thesis-driven argument vs. narrative

A **narrative** recounts what happened in sequence. A **thesis-driven argument** stakes a specific, debatable claim about causation, significance, or interpretation and sustains it with evidence.

**In use:** "This draft is a strong narrative but not yet an argument — tell me what you're claiming that another historian could push back on."

**Common misuse:** believing that a well-researched, chronologically accurate narrative satisfies an argument-based assignment, because the facts are correct — accuracy and argument are graded on different axes.

## DBQ (document-based question)

An assessment format (popularized by AP History and SHEG's "Reading Like a Historian" curriculum) that gives students a small set of primary sources and asks them to construct an argument using sourcing, corroboration, and contextualization skills applied to those specific documents.

**In use:** "The DBQ format is right for teaching sourcing skills in the survey — it's the wrong format for the senior seminar, where independent archival research is what's being assessed."

**Common misuse:** treating DBQ practice as sufficient preparation for independent research, when it teaches document analysis on a curated set, not the harder skill of finding and selecting sources in the first place.

## Uncoverage

Lendol Calder's term for deliberately choosing depth over breadth in survey-course design — teaching fewer topics with real primary-source engagement rather than summarizing more topics at surface level.

**In use:** "We're running an uncoverage model this term — 7 topics instead of 20 — so students actually practice sourcing instead of just hearing about more events."

**Common misuse:** treating uncoverage as "doing less work" or lowering rigor, when it's a deliberate tradeoff that increases the depth of skill practiced per topic.

## Historical empathy

Reconstructing how a past actor's choices made sense given what they knew, valued, and faced at the time — distinct from sympathy or moral endorsement.

**In use:** "Historical empathy means explaining why a moderate abolitionist in 1850 might have opposed immediate emancipation on strategic grounds — not agreeing with that position."

**Common misuse:** conflating historical empathy (understanding the reasoning) with defending or excusing the actor's choices — understanding the logic is not endorsing it.

## Survey course vs. methods/seminar course

A **survey** covers a broad chronological or thematic sweep for a general audience, most of whom take no further history course. A **methods/seminar course** trains majors in independent research, historiography, and original argument construction, usually capped at a small enrollment.

**In use:** "The survey teaches sourcing on a curated document set; the seminar requires finding and evaluating your own sources from scratch — that's the actual skill jump between the two."

**Common misuse:** assuming the two course types differ only in difficulty level, rather than in what skill each is designed to certify (exposure vs. independent research capability).

## Contingent faculty / adjunctification

The shift of undergraduate teaching load, particularly gen-ed survey sections, onto non-tenure-track instructors hired term-to-term rather than tenure-line faculty — a staffing pattern, not an individual instructor's qualification level.

**In use:** "70% of our gen-ed history sections are taught by contingent faculty this year — that's a staffing-model problem for rubric consistency, not a comment on any one instructor's teaching."

**Common misuse:** treating adjunctification as a proxy for teaching quality, when the concern is structural — grading consistency and course-design continuity across sections taught by different people each term, often without shared planning time.
