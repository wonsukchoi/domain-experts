# Red flags

Smell tests an experienced history instructor catches early. Format: what it usually means, the first question to ask, the data to pull.

## 1. Essay scoring 85+ with a primary-source quote that can't be located at its cited page

**Usually means:** a fabricated or AI-generated citation, or (less often) a mis-cited page/edition — both defeat similarity-detection tools because the surrounding prose is original.

**First question:** "Can you produce the physical page, archival database record, or page image where this quote appears?"

**Data to pull:** the cited source at the cited page and its index; the original database or archive record if digital; any prior drafts or research notes showing where the quote was found.

## 2. A 15-week survey syllabus lists 20+ topics with no primary-source assignment

**Usually means:** a default coverage-model syllabus inherited from a textbook's table of contents, not a deliberate breadth choice tied to an articulation or accreditation requirement.

**First question:** "Is this breadth required by a transfer-articulation agreement, or is it the textbook's chapter list by default?"

**Data to pull:** the articulation agreement's named topics (if any); the syllabus's assignment list cross-referenced against topic count; time-on-task estimate per topic.

## 3. A paper narrates events chronologically with no arguable claim in the first or last paragraph

**Usually means:** the student (or the assignment prompt itself) conflated "explain what happened" with "argue why it mattered" — the most common structural failure in undergraduate history writing.

**First question:** "What is this paper arguing that a reasonable historian could disagree with?"

**Data to pull:** the paper's opening and closing paragraphs; the assignment prompt's wording (does it ask for an argument or a summary?).

## 4. Multiple papers share an unusual, non-generic historiographical framing or the same citation error

**Usually means:** a shared tutoring service, solution/paper-bank source, or a shared AI-drafting session — more reliable a signal than a shared correct fact, which converges independently on well-documented events.

**First question:** "Does the specific interpretive framing, not just the underlying facts, match across these papers?"

**Data to pull:** side-by-side paragraph comparison of the shared framing or error; timestamps if submissions are staggered; the exact phrasing, not paraphrase.

## 5. TAs grading the same essay question differ by more than 20% of the category's point value

**Usually means:** no rubric-anchoring session happened before grading opened, or the thesis-gate criterion wasn't applied consistently.

**First question:** "Show me the rubric and the anchor essays we scored together before grading started."

**Data to pull:** a random 10% sample of TA-graded essays for the same question, re-scored by the instructor; the specific rubric category where scores diverge.

## 6. A Turnitin similarity score is high but driven almost entirely by block-quoted primary sources

**Usually means:** the similarity algorithm is flagging properly quoted and cited primary-source material, not plagiarized analysis — a false positive common in history papers specifically because of heavy primary-source quotation.

**First question:** "What percentage of the flagged text is inside quotation marks and cited, versus unquoted analysis matching another source?"

**Data to pull:** Turnitin's source-match breakdown; the paper's own quotation marks and citations cross-referenced against the flagged passages.

## 7. A paper judges a historical actor entirely by present-day moral or legal standards with no period-context engagement

**Usually means:** presentism — evaluating the past by current norms rather than by what was knowable or normative in the actor's own moment — the default lens for an untrained reader of history.

**First question:** "What did this actor's contemporaries who disagreed with them actually argue, using the terms available at the time?"

**Data to pull:** the paper's engagement (or lack of it) with contemporaneous counter-arguments; whether cited secondary sources address period context directly.

## 8. Enrollment or persistence in an upper-division seminar drops the term after a survey-course "uncoverage" redesign

**Usually means:** the depth-first survey redesign under-delivered the breadth of exposure majors expected before choosing a seminar topic, not that the incoming cohort is less engaged.

**First question:** "What share of this term's seminar enrollees came through the redesigned survey versus the prior breadth-model survey, and does the enrollment gap track that split?"

**Data to pull:** seminar enrollment by prior-survey cohort; survey-to-major conversion rate before and after the redesign.

## 9. A single paper mixes Chicago/Turabian, MLA, and APA citation formats

**Usually means:** the paper was assembled from multiple sources (a tutoring service, an AI draft, a recycled section from a course in another discipline) rather than written straight through in one citation system.

**First question:** "Walk me through how you built this paper's citations, section by section."

**Data to pull:** which sections use which format; whether the mixed sections correspond to different argument segments or sources.

## 10. A grade appeal frames the dispute as "the professor's historiographical bias" rather than a specific graded criterion

**Usually means:** the student is contesting an interpretive framing choice rather than identifying a specific rubric criterion where they believe the score is wrong — sometimes valid, but needs to be pinned to evidence, not asserted as bias.

**First question:** "Which specific piece of evidence or interpretation in your paper do you believe was penalized, and against which rubric criterion?"

**Data to pull:** the graded rubric with criterion-by-criterion comments; the specific paragraph in dispute.

## 11. A department's gen-ed history sections are taught 70%+ by contingent (non-tenure-track) faculty

**Usually means:** persistent understaffing relative to gen-ed enrollment demand, which structurally erodes rubric consistency and grading depth across sections unless anchoring sessions are mandated department-wide, not left to individual instructors.

**First question:** "Is there a shared rubric and anchoring process across all sections of this gen-ed course, or does each instructor grade independently?"

**Data to pull:** section-by-section grade distributions for the same gen-ed course; whether a shared syllabus/rubric exists across sections; contingent-to-tenure-line staffing ratio for the course.

## 12. An adjunct or lecturer is assigned 5+ preps or sections in a term with no grading support

**Usually means:** assessment mix will drift toward whatever grades fastest (multiple-choice recall, shorter papers) rather than what tests sourcing and argument skill, without anyone deciding that tradeoff on purpose.

**First question:** "Has the assignment mix (primary-source-based papers vs. recall-based quizzes) changed since the load increased, and was that a deliberate pedagogical call?"

**Data to pull:** assignment-type mix by term for the instructor's sections; grading turnaround time; office-hour attendance as a proxy for available support capacity.
