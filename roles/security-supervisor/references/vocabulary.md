# Vocabulary

### Post order
The site-specific written document defining an officer's duties, checkpoints, access-control rules, and force-authorization limits for a given post; it is the legal baseline a court measures officer conduct against.
**In use:** "The officer didn't wave the vehicle through against policy — check the post order, that's exactly what it authorizes for tenant move-ins."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a generic job description or training handout instead of the specific, dated, signed-acknowledgment document that defines the duty of care actually owed at that post.

### Pass-down log
The written handoff between outgoing and incoming shifts, recording what happened, what's unresolved, and what the next shift needs to watch.
**In use:** "Before you post up, read the pass-down — there's an open facilities ticket on the elevator sensor from last night."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a formality signed without reading rather than the primary control that prevents information loss across a 24/7 rotation.

### Use-of-force continuum
The staged model (presence, verbal commands, soft empty-hand control, intermediate weapons, deadly force) used to judge whether an officer's response was proportional to the resistance encountered.
**In use:** "The report shows active resistance and soft empty-hand control — that's proportional, one step up the continuum, not excessive."
**Common misuse:** Applied as a rigid ladder officers must climb one rung at a time in real time, rather than a post-incident standard for judging whether the jump taken was reasonable given the resistance offered.

### Foreseeability
The legal standard, central to negligent-security claims, asking whether a property owner or security provider knew or should have known a given type of harm was likely, based on prior similar incidents or the totality of circumstances.
**In use:** "Two attempted break-ins in the same garage in fourteen months makes a third one foreseeable — that's the number that matters in a claim, not whether anyone got hurt."
**Common misuse:** Conflated with "predictable in hindsight" — foreseeability in this legal sense is about a documented pattern that existed *before* the incident, not a judgment made after the fact.

### Prior-similar-incidents test
The specific foreseeability test many jurisdictions apply, requiring that the plaintiff show a comparable crime type occurred on or near the property before the incident at issue.
**In use:** "Counsel's going to pull our incident log for the trailing two years — make sure it's complete before they do."
**Common misuse:** Assumed the incidents must be identical; courts generally require similarity of type and location, not an exact match.

### Negligent security
A premises-liability claim alleging that inadequate security measures — staffing, lighting, access control — allowed a foreseeable crime to occur.
**In use:** "Cutting the overnight post right after two garage incidents is exactly the fact pattern negligent-security counsel look for."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to require an actual injury on-site to be viable; a claim can proceed on inadequate-measures grounds even where the specific harm alleged didn't happen to the plaintiff at that exact spot before.

### Guard tour / checkpoint system
GPS- or NFC-based technology that verifies an officer physically visited a specific checkpoint at a specific time, producing an auditable patrol record.
**In use:** "Pull the tour report before you tell the client the garage was checked every twenty minutes — verify it, don't just repeat the schedule."
**Common misuse:** Treated as an attendance/productivity metric rather than what it actually is — the evidentiary record that a control was executed, which matters most after an incident, not during a performance review.

### Bill rate vs. pay rate
Bill rate is what the client is invoiced per hour for a post; pay rate is what the officer is paid; the difference, minus payroll burden (taxes, insurance, workers' comp — typically ~25–35% loaded onto pay rate), is the branch's margin.
**In use:** "The margin on that post is thin — $28 billed, $18 paid plus thirty percent burden, that's about $13 an hour before overhead."
**Common misuse:** Margin calculated as bill rate minus pay rate only, ignoring payroll burden — overstates true margin by the burden percentage.

### Span of control
The number of officers or posts one supervisor can oversee well across briefing, audit, and incident response — not just headcount on an org chart.
**In use:** "Adding a fourth site to this supervisor's span means incident response time doubles on the two farthest posts — that's the real cost, not the org chart."
**Common misuse:** Set purely by budget (one supervisor per N officers) without accounting for site geography, shift overlap, or incident frequency, which change how many posts one person can actually cover well.

### PERC / guard card
State-issued licensing credential (naming and requirements vary by state — e.g., Private Employment Registration Card, guard card, security officer license) required before an individual can legally work an armed or unarmed security post.
**In use:** "Confirm the guard card's current before you post someone, not after — an unlicensed officer on post voids the contract's insurance in most states."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to be a one-time hire-day check; cards expire and require renewal, and an expired card mid-assignment is a live compliance failure, not a paperwork lag.

### Workplace violence prevention plan
A site-specific written plan (increasingly a legal requirement in states like California) addressing how threats, restraining orders, and known aggressor risks are communicated to and handled by on-site personnel, including security.
**In use:** "HR closed the restraining-order file, but did that ever make it into the WVPP briefing for the officer on that lobby post?"
**Common misuse:** Treated as an HR/legal document that lives in a policy binder, disconnected from the actual shift briefing that would let an officer recognize and respond to the named risk in real time.

### Exempt / non-exempt (FLSA)
The Fair Labor Standards Act classification determining whether a supervisor is entitled to overtime pay; exemption requires meeting a salary threshold plus a duties test (directing two or more full-time employees, meaningful input on hiring/firing) — there is no security-specific exemption category.
**In use:** "Being called 'supervisor' doesn't make the role exempt — check the duties test and the current salary threshold before assuming no overtime is owed."
**Common misuse:** Assumed the title alone (or a flat weekly salary) establishes exemption; both the salary level and the duties test have to be met, and the federal threshold has moved and been litigated recently enough that it should be checked, not assumed.

### Chain of custody
The documented, unbroken record of who possessed a piece of physical or video evidence from collection to handoff, required for the evidence to hold up if the incident becomes a legal matter.
**In use:** "Log who pulled that footage, when, and who it was handed to — a broken chain of custody can get the evidence excluded entirely."
**Common misuse:** Applied loosely to mean "we kept the footage somewhere," when it specifically requires a documented, gap-free transfer record, not just retention.

### Cross-trained officer / cross-site pooling
An officer qualified and briefed on more than one site's post orders, allowing coverage gaps to be filled from a shared pool rather than through overtime or an unfamiliar fill-in.
**In use:** "Before you call anyone in on overtime, check the cross-trained pool — is anyone from the Riverside site available tonight?"
**Common misuse:** Assumed any officer can "cover" any post interchangeably; cross-training means the officer has actually been briefed on that specific site's post order, not just that they hold a general license.
