# Vocabulary

Terms generalists conflate that sterile processing technicians keep sharply separate.

### Cleaning vs. disinfection vs. sterilization

**Cleaning** is physical removal of soil and organic material (the prerequisite for everything downstream). **Disinfection** kills most pathogenic microorganisms but not necessarily bacterial spores. **Sterilization** is the destruction of all microbial life, including spores, validated to a defined probability (commonly a sterility assurance level of 10⁻⁶).

*In use:* "It came out of the washer disinfected, not sterile — it still needs the autoclave before it's tray-ready."

*Common misuse:* calling a washer-disinfector cycle "sterilizing" the instruments, or assuming a visibly clean instrument has been disinfected without checking the actual cycle log.

### Biological indicator (BI) vs. chemical indicator (CI) vs. physical monitor

**BI** is a live spore test (commonly *Geobacillus stearothermophilus*) that proves an actual kill occurred. **CI** is a chemical strip or integrator that changes state on exposure to a sterilization parameter (per ISO 11140 Class 1–6), proving exposure, not lethality. **Physical monitors** are the sterilizer's own gauges and printouts, proving the machine reached its set parameters, not that the load was penetrated correctly.

*In use:* "CI passed and the printout's clean, but that's parameter confirmation — we still hold the implant tray for the BI."

*Common misuse:* treating a passed CI as equivalent evidence to a passed BI, especially for implant loads where only the BI result satisfies the release requirement.

### Event-related sterility vs. time-related (shelf-life) sterility

**Event-related** sterility policy holds that a package remains sterile until an event compromises the packaging barrier — a tear, moisture exposure, a seal failure, rough handling — regardless of elapsed time. **Time-related** (older model) assigned a fixed expiration date regardless of package condition.

*In use:* "It's eight months old but the wrap's intact and it's been on a covered shelf — under event-related policy that's still sterile; we don't discard on the date."

*Common misuse:* discarding intact, properly stored packages because a date "expired," or conversely trusting a damaged pack because it's "not expired yet" — both invert what actually determines sterility.

### Immediate-use steam sterilization (IUSS) vs. routine terminal sterilization

**IUSS** ("flash") is an abbreviated steam cycle, typically unwrapped and without a full drying phase, intended for a specific emergency (e.g., a dropped instrument with no substitute available). **Terminal sterilization** is the standard wrapped/contained cycle with full drying, intended for storage and later use.

*In use:* "We flashed the one dropped retractor for this case — that's IUSS. The rest of the set went through terminal sterilization overnight like normal."

*Common misuse:* using IUSS as a standing solution for chronic instrument shortages instead of an occasional true emergency — the drying step it skips is exactly what protects the tray on transport.

### Spaulding classification: critical vs. semicritical vs. noncritical

**Critical** items enter sterile tissue or the vascular system and require sterilization. **Semicritical** items contact mucous membranes or non-intact skin and require at minimum high-level disinfection. **Noncritical** items touch only intact skin and require low-level disinfection or cleaning.

*In use:* "It's a semicritical device by contact, but the IFU only validates high-level disinfection for it — we don't force a sterilization method the manufacturer never tested."

*Common misuse:* applying the classification rigidly without checking whether the specific device's IFU actually supports the "expected" reprocessing method for its category — some devices in a critical-contact role still can't tolerate standard sterilization and require an IFU-specified alternate method.

### A0 value

A calculated lethality value (per ISO 15883) expressing the equivalent disinfection effect of a thermal washer-disinfector cycle at a reference temperature — commonly a target of A0 ≥ 600, roughly equivalent to 90°C for one minute, though the same A0 can be reached at a lower temperature held longer.

*In use:* "The cycle log shows A0 of 3000 for that load — well above our 600 target, no issue there."

*Common misuse:* assuming a higher temperature alone guarantees adequate disinfection without checking the actual A0 the cycle achieved, which depends on both temperature and hold time together.

### Bioburden

The quantity and type of viable microorganisms and organic material present on an instrument before processing — the starting condition cleaning and sterilization have to overcome, not a synonym for "dirty-looking."

*In use:* "Lumened instruments carry heavier bioburden per surface area than a solid retractor — that's why the brush size and soak time differ."

*Common misuse:* judging bioburden by visible soil alone; an instrument can look clean and still carry a bioburden load high enough to shield organisms from a marginal cycle, especially in a lumen or box lock.

### Instrument tracking (tray tracking)

A barcode or RFID system that ties a specific tray, its individual instruments, its sterilization cycle, and the patient/case it was used in together into one traceable record.

*In use:* "Tray tracking shows this set went through Sterilizer #4's Wednesday load and into Tuesday's ortho case — that's how we scoped the recall to exactly those patients."

*Common misuse:* treating tray tracking as a convenience/inventory tool only; its real value is enabling a precise, defensible recall scope instead of a department-wide "when in doubt, recall everything" response.

### Retained surgical item

An instrument, sponge, or other item unintentionally left inside a patient after surgery — the specific failure mode instrument counts and count-discrepancy protocols exist to prevent.

*In use:* "The count discrepancy wasn't resolved before the tray went out — that's now a retained-item risk, not a paperwork note."

*Common misuse:* treating an unresolved count discrepancy as acceptable if the missing item is "probably" in the trash or the wash — probability isn't resolution; the item has to be physically located or the case has to be informed before closure.
