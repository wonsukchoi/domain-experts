# Vocabulary

### NPDES (National Pollutant Discharge Elimination System)
The Clean Water Act permit program governing point-source discharges to surface waters.
**In use:** "We're discharging under the general NPDES permit, but at this flow we should look at whether we need an individual permit instead."
**Common misuse:** treating "NPDES permit" as a single universal document — general permits and individual permits carry materially different limits and monitoring requirements for the same facility type.

### Potential to Emit (PTE)
The maximum capacity of a source to emit a pollutant under its physical and operational design, before considering any federally enforceable limit that restricts it.
**In use:** "Their PTE for NOx is 95 tons per year uncontrolled, but the permit caps it at 38 as a synthetic minor limit."
**Common misuse:** confusing PTE with actual measured emissions — PTE is a ceiling based on capacity, not a report of what was actually emitted.

### Synthetic minor source
A facility whose potential to emit would otherwise classify it as a major source, but which accepts a federally enforceable permit limit to stay below the major-source threshold.
**In use:** "We can keep this a synthetic minor if the new line doesn't push PTE over the accepted limit — otherwise we're into full Title V territory."
**Common misuse:** assuming "minor source" status is permanent regardless of operational changes — any change that could increase PTE needs to be checked against the synthetic minor limit before it happens, not after.

### Secondary treatment standards
The federal minimum performance requirements (40 CFR 133.102) for municipal wastewater treatment — 85% removal AND numeric effluent limits for BOD5 and TSS, both required simultaneously.
**In use:** "We're clearing the 30 mg/L limit but only hitting 82% removal — that's still a violation of the standard."
**Common misuse:** treating percent removal and effluent concentration as redundant checks — they can diverge, and both must be met.

### Conceptual Site Model (CSM)
The working hypothesis of how contamination moves from a source through a pathway to a receptor at a specific site.
**In use:** "Before we scope remediation, we need a CSM that actually traces the plume to the residential wells, not just a contamination map."
**Common misuse:** treating a contamination extent map (where the chemical is) as equivalent to a CSM (how it gets to someone) — a map without pathway analysis can't justify a cleanup decision.

### Monitored Natural Attenuation (MNA)
A remediation approach relying on natural physical, chemical, or biological processes to reduce contamination, paired with a long-term monitoring program to confirm the trend.
**In use:** "MNA is defensible here because we have three years of declining concentration trend and no completed exposure pathway."
**Common misuse:** proposing MNA as a low-cost default without the multi-year trend data regulators require to approve it — it's a data-supported decision, not a cost-driven default.

### Mixed Liquor Suspended Solids (MLSS)
The concentration of biological solids in an activated sludge aeration basin, the operational lever for adjusting treatment capacity to loading.
**In use:** "We can absorb some of this loading increase by running MLSS 15% higher, but we need to watch sludge volume index so it doesn't bulk."
**Common misuse:** assuming MLSS can be raised indefinitely to handle more loading — beyond a point it causes settling problems (bulking) in the clarifier, trading one failure mode for another.

### Sludge Retention Time (SRT) / Solids Age
The average time solids remain in the biological treatment system, which controls whether slow-growing organisms (like nitrifiers) can establish.
**In use:** "Ammonia's creeping up because SRT dropped below the nitrification threshold after we increased wasting."
**Common misuse:** conflating SRT with hydraulic retention time (HRT) — they're mechanically different (SRT depends on wasting rate, HRT on flow through basin volume) and drive different failure modes.

### New Source Review (NSR) / Prevention of Significant Deterioration (PSD)
Clean Air Act permitting programs triggered when a new source or major modification would significantly increase emissions, requiring best-available-control-technology analysis before construction.
**In use:** "This expansion looks like a major modification under PSD — we need a BACT analysis before we can even apply."
**Common misuse:** assuming NSR/PSD only applies to brand-new facilities — a modification to an existing source can trigger the same review if the emissions increase crosses the applicable threshold.

### Discharge Monitoring Report (DMR)
The periodic (usually monthly) self-reported compliance data a permittee submits to the regulator under NPDES.
**In use:** "Pull the last 12 months of DMRs before you size anything — that's the real variability, not the vendor's nominal spec."
**Common misuse:** treating a single DMR snapshot as representative of plant performance, instead of pulling enough history to see the actual variance and any seasonal pattern.

### Best Available Control Technology (BACT)
The air-pollution control technology determination required for major sources under PSD, balancing energy, environmental, and economic impacts against emissions reduction.
**In use:** "BACT for this boiler size is low-NOx burners plus SCR, per the last five permits issued in this state — a generic scrubber recommendation won't hold up."
**Common misuse:** treating BACT as a fixed technology list — it's case-by-case and shifts with the state's recent permitting precedent (the "BACT clearinghouse" record), not a static standard.

### Vapor intrusion
The migration of volatile contaminants from subsurface soil or groundwater into the indoor air of an overlying or nearby building.
**In use:** "We can't default to MNA here — there's a completed vapor intrusion pathway to the daycare next door."
**Common misuse:** assuming vapor intrusion risk only applies directly above a plume — lateral migration can put a completed pathway under a building that isn't directly over the contamination source.
