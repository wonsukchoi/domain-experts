# Anesthesiologist Assistant — Vocabulary

### Anesthesia Care Team (ACT)
The model where a physician anesthesiologist directs one or more non-physician providers (CAA or CRNA) delivering the anesthetic.
**In use:** "This hospital only runs an Anesthesia Care Team model — there's no solo CAA practice option here."
**Common misuse:** Treating "ACT" as a staffing convenience rather than the specific legal structure that is the entire basis for CAA scope of practice existing at all.

### Medical direction
The CMS billing and practice arrangement where an anesthesiologist directs 2–4 concurrent cases, satisfying seven specific documented steps.
**In use:** "We're at medical direction on four rooms right now — a fifth case opening converts all of them to supervision."
**Common misuse:** Assuming "medical direction" describes any physician oversight, when it specifically means the seven-step, ≤4-room CMS arrangement with its own billing modifier (QK).

### Medical supervision
The billing/practice category that applies once an anesthesiologist is directing more than four concurrent cases, or not meeting the seven-step requirement.
**In use:** "Once room five opened, we were legally in medical supervision, not direction, for that window."
**Common misuse:** Treating supervision and direction as interchangeable synonyms for "a physician is involved," when they carry different real-time-availability guarantees and different billing modifiers (AD vs. QK).

### Immediately available
The medical-direction requirement that the directing anesthesiologist can respond to an emergency without delay, without requiring continuous physical presence in the room.
**In use:** "He's immediately available — he's in the workroom next door, not in another building."
**Common misuse:** Reading "immediately available" as equivalent to a CRNA's independent-practice authority in states where CAAs have no independent scope at all — the phrase describes the anesthesiologist's obligation, not an expansion of the CAA's authority.

### ASA Physical Status Classification
A six-tier (I–VI) system rating a patient's baseline systemic disease burden before anesthesia, used for risk communication and case scheduling.
**In use:** "She's an ASA III with poorly controlled hypertension — that changes our induction agent choice."
**Common misuse:** Treating ASA class as a complete risk score; it doesn't capture airway difficulty or OSA risk, which are separate, equally decision-relevant axes.

### STOP-BANG
An 8-item validated screening tool for obstructive sleep apnea risk (Snoring, Tiredness, Observed apnea, blood Pressure, BMI, Age, Neck circumference, Gender).
**In use:** "STOP-BANG came back 5 out of 8 — I want her in a monitored bed overnight, not routine same-day discharge."
**Common misuse:** Skipping the screen on patients who look otherwise healthy, on the assumption that a low ASA class rules out significant OSA risk.

### MAC (Minimum Alveolar Concentration)
The alveolar concentration of an inhaled anesthetic at which 50% of patients don't move in response to a surgical incision; the standard unit for volatile agent dosing and depth.
**In use:** "We're running 0.8 MAC and he's still tachycardic to the same stimulus — check depth before treating it as pain."
**Common misuse:** Treating MAC as a fixed target number rather than a population-average threshold that varies with age, other drugs on board, and the specific stimulus.

### Capnography (ETCO2)
Continuous measurement of exhaled carbon dioxide, confirming ventilation adequacy and, via waveform shape, a range of physiologic and mechanical events.
**In use:** "The waveform's still well-formed, just rising — that's not a sampling problem, that's a patient-side process."
**Common misuse:** Reading only the number and ignoring waveform morphology, which is often what distinguishes a real physiologic trend from an equipment or sampling artifact.

### Malignant hyperthermia (MH)
A rare, life-threatening hypermetabolic reaction to volatile anesthetics or succinylcholine in genetically susceptible patients, treated with dantrolene.
**In use:** "ETCO2's climbing with unchanged vent settings and his temp's up 0.8 in ten minutes — that's an MH pattern, not a depth problem."
**Common misuse:** Waiting for a fully developed triad (hypercarbia, tachycardia, hyperthermia) before acting, rather than treating two consistent signs with a well-formed waveform as sufficient to start the protocol.

### Local anesthetic systemic toxicity (LAST)
Cardiovascular and CNS toxicity from excessive systemic absorption of local anesthetic, treated with lipid emulsion.
**In use:** "Metallic taste and tinnitus right after the block — stop the injection, this is early LAST."
**Common misuse:** Attributing early symptoms (perioral numbness, tinnitus) to anxiety or a vasovagal response and continuing the injection instead of stopping immediately.

### Look-alike/sound-alike (LASA) drug error
A medication error caused by visual or auditory similarity between two drugs' names, labels, or packaging, rather than by dosing miscalculation.
**In use:** "That's a classic LASA pair — succinylcholine and neostigmine look almost identical unlabeled."
**Common misuse:** Assuming medication error risk scales with drug unfamiliarity, when LASA swaps of well-known drugs are the more common real-world failure.

### Dantrolene
The specific antidote for malignant hyperthermia, dosed by weight and repeated on a fixed interval until reversal.
**In use:** "First dose is 2.5 mg/kg — round up to the next whole vial, don't underdose to hit an exact number."
**Common misuse:** Under-dosing by rounding down to save drug/cost, when protocol explicitly rounds up to the nearest whole vial.

### Emergence
The transition period from anesthetic depth back to consciousness, ending in extubation and handoff.
**In use:** "Emergence is one of the two required-presence windows for medical direction — confirm he's actually in the room, not just aware it's happening."
**Common misuse:** Treating emergence as a routine, lower-attention phase compared to induction, when both carry the same medical-direction presence requirement and both carry real complication risk (laryngospasm, delayed emergence, aspiration).
