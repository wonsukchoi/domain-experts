# Vocabulary

### Backtiming
Counting down from a fixed future post (a legal ID, a network join, a hard newscast start) rather than counting up from when a break began.
**In use:** "I'm backtiming off the :58 legal ID, so I've got ninety seconds of talk left before I need to be into the sweeper."
**Common misuse:** Generalists treat it as a synonym for "finishing on time" in general, rather than the specific practice of continuously recalculating remaining seconds against a fixed post while still talking.

### Legal ID
The station identification required at or near the top of the hour under 47 CFR §73.1201 — call sign plus community of license, not just any mention of the station's name or slogan.
**In use:** "That was just a liner, not the legal ID — we still need call letters and city before the top of the hour."
**Common misuse:** Assuming any on-air station mention satisfies the requirement, when it specifically requires the call sign and licensed community, not a branding phrase.

### Voice-tracking
Recording only the spoken breaks (not the music) ahead of time, which automation then stitches live between songs at one or more destination stations, often same-day and across markets.
**In use:** "I'm voice-tracking four stations this afternoon — same breaks, but I'm swapping the local references for each market before I send them."
**Common misuse:** Treating it as a synonym for any prerecorded show; it specifically means live-sounding breaks assembled by automation around a shared music log, which is what makes generic tracks detectable as canned.

### PPM (Portable People Meter)
Nielsen Audio's passive metering device that logs continuous, minute-by-minute tuning exposure automatically, replacing the older paper diary panel in most metro markets.
**In use:** "Our AQH is soft in that quarter-hour on the PPM data — people are physically tuning away, not just forgetting to write us down."
**Common misuse:** Assuming PPM behaves like the diary method (stated recall of favorite stations); PPM rewards continuous engagement second-by-second and punishes boring stretches the diary would never have caught.

### Cume / TSL
Cume is the number of unique listeners tuning in over a period; time-spent-listening (TSL) is how long each of them stays once tuned in.
**In use:** "Cume's fine, we're getting sampled — it's a TSL problem inside the noon hour specifically."
**Common misuse:** Treating any ratings decline as one undifferentiated "the numbers are down" problem instead of diagnosing which of the two is actually falling, since the fixes (promotion vs. in-show pacing) are different.

### Hot clock
The station's minute-by-minute template dictating exactly where music, talk, spot breaks, and imaging fall within each hour.
**In use:** "The hot clock only gives me a two-minute talk window there — I can't run the full interview in that slot."
**Common misuse:** Confusing it with the playlist; the hot clock governs spot load and break placement as much as it governs music, and a host who ignores it throws off every slot behind it.

### Sweeper
A short produced audio element — often with music or effects underneath — used to bridge between songs or brand a transition, distinct from a spoken-only liner.
**In use:** "Drop the sweeper before the news join, not a bare liner — we need the produced element there."
**Common misuse:** Using "sweeper" and "liner" interchangeably; a liner is spoken-word only, a sweeper is produced with a music or sound-effect bed.

### Liner
A short, scripted, spoken-only brand phrase read live or pre-voiced and dropped into automation.
**In use:** "Just hit the liner into the spot break, we don't have time for a full sweeper."
**Common misuse:** Treating any short spoken phrase as a liner regardless of whether it's produced with music underneath, which is actually what separates it from a sweeper.

### Board op / on the board
The person actively running the console (levels, carts, mic switching) during a broadcast; on many shifts the on-air talent runs their own board (a "combo" operation) rather than having a separate operator.
**In use:** "It's a combo shift tonight, I'm on the board myself, so watch your pacing — there's no one else riding levels."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a board op is always a separate role; in most modern formats the announcer runs their own board, and treating levels as someone else's job is how dead air happens.

### Network join / satellite join
The scheduled, automated switch from local programming to a syndicated or network feed (national news, syndicated talk) at an exact clock time.
**In use:** "The join is hard at 1:00:00 — automation switches regardless of whether I'm still talking."
**Common misuse:** Treating it as a flexible cue the host can drift into; it is a fixed automation event, and running past it gets the host cut off mid-sentence, not gently faded.

### Dead air
Unintentional silence on air, as distinct from a deliberate comedic or dramatic pause.
**In use:** "That was three seconds for effect, that's not dead air — dead air is what happens if I lose my train of thought for ten."
**Common misuse:** Labeling any brief pause as dead air; the industry concern is unintentional silence that runs long enough (commonly flagged by automation and engineers past roughly ten seconds) to read as a technical failure, not a one- or two-second comic beat.

### Safe harbor
The 10pm–6am local window under FCC indecency enforcement where the standard narrows from indecency to obscenity only.
**In use:** "We're inside safe harbor at 11, but if this repeats on the delayed feed into daytime it's a different call."
**Common misuse:** Treating safe harbor as a blanket exemption from all content rules; it only narrows the standard, and it stops applying the moment the same content airs (live or via delay/repeat) outside the window.

### Sponsorship ID
The FCC requirement (47 CFR §73.1212) to disclose on air when airtime, mentions, or consideration were provided in exchange for payment or any other consideration — the modern payola-disclosure rule.
**In use:** "That client sent us the tickets, so the mention needs a sponsorship ID, not just a casual shout-out."
**Common misuse:** Assuming only direct cash payment for airplay triggers the rule; any consideration (merchandise, travel, favors) in exchange for airtime or mentions requires disclosure, not just money changing hands for music rotation.
