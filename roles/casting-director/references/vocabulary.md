# Casting Director Vocabulary

### Breakdown
The character description and submission instructions posted (typically via Breakdown Express) to talent agents and managers for a role.
**In use:** "The breakdown's drawing nothing — pull it and rewrite the age range before we lose another day."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a job posting written once and left alone; a thin submission pool usually means the breakdown itself needs a rewrite, not just more waiting.

### Avail
An informal check of an actor's availability for a role, with no commitment on either side.
**In use:** "Get me an avail on her before we go any further with the offer."
**Common misuse:** Treated as interchangeable with a hold — an avail confirms nothing is blocking a conflicting offer, it only confirms the actor is currently free.

### Hold
A tentative commitment from an actor (through their rep) not to accept other offers for a window of time, short of a signed deal.
**In use:** "We've got a hold on him through Friday — need the deal closed before then or he's free to take the other offer."
**Common misuse:** Placed without an expiration date and then treated as durable; an undated hold protects the actor's options, not the production's.

### Option
A studio or network's contractual right to extend an actor's deal for additional seasons or episodes at pre-negotiated terms.
**In use:** "The option-year escalator is 5% — that's the number that has to be in the deal memo, not 'standard.'"
**Common misuse:** Assumed to be a formality that gets settled later; unpriced options are exactly what turns cheap in season one into expensive by season four.

### Quote
An actor's established rate from their most recent comparable work, used as a negotiating floor.
**In use:** "Her quote's $75k an episode from her last series regular gig — that's the number we're negotiating against, not scale."
**Common misuse:** Treated as fixed and non-negotiable; a quote is a starting position, adjustable against billing, backend, and the specific value of this project to the actor.

### Favored nations
A billing or pay-parity clause guaranteeing one performer terms no worse than another's.
**In use:** "Keep the three supporting leads favored nations on billing — nobody's ego blows up the shoot over card order."
**Common misuse:** Applied across performers of clearly different marquee value or role size, where it functions as a way to suppress a bigger name's rate rather than protect genuine peers.

### Test / test-option deal
The full deal (including option-year terms) an actor negotiates before booking a pilot or new series role, since a booked pilot role converts automatically into the negotiated series terms if picked up.
**In use:** "Get the test deal done before she reads for the network — we lose all our leverage on option years the second she's booked."
**Common misuse:** Deferred until after casting to "not slow things down"; deferring it is exactly what collapses the CD's negotiating leverage on the terms that matter most over a multi-season run.

### Series regular / recurring guest star / day player
Tiers of a performer's contractual commitment: series regular (contracted for the show's run, appears most episodes), recurring guest star (contracted per-appearance across multiple episodes), day player (single-episode, daily rate).
**In use:** "Bump her from recurring to series regular next season — the option-year math only works at that tier."
**Common misuse:** Used loosely as synonyms for "how much they're in the show," when each tier carries a distinct deal structure, exclusivity obligation, and cost basis.

### Chemistry read
A callback pairing finalists opposite an already-cast actor (usually the lead) to test how the pairing actually plays, not how either performer reads solo.
**In use:** "She read great solo, but we need the chemistry read against [lead] before this goes any further."
**Common misuse:** Skipped under schedule pressure on the assumption that two strong solo reads will combine into a strong pair — chemistry is its own variable, not the sum of two individual reads.

### Self-tape
An audition recorded by the actor (or their team) and submitted digitally, standard for first-round auditions industry-wide since the shift accelerated in 2020.
**In use:** "First round's self-tape only — we're not bringing people in until the producer-session list is locked."
**Common misuse:** Judged as directly comparable to an in-person read without accounting for takes, lighting, and coaching that a controlled tape allows and a live room doesn't.

### Pre-read
An initial audition, typically with the casting director or an associate only, before a role advances to a producer session.
**In use:** "She's through the pre-read — put her on the producer-session list for Thursday."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a formality to clear everyone through; the pre-read is the actual triage step, and passing weak reads through it just moves the real decision (and the wasted time) upstream to the producers.

### Backend / points
Contingent compensation tied to a project's performance (syndication, streaming thresholds, box office), paid in addition to or instead of a higher up-front fee.
**In use:** "Offer her points instead of the extra $25k an episode — it caps our exposure now and gives her real upside if the show hits."
**Common misuse:** Presented as equivalent in value to guaranteed fee; backend is genuinely contingent, and a rep evaluating it correctly will price it at a discount to the guaranteed alternative.

### Billing
The contractual terms governing how and where a performer's name and image appear in credits and marketing (position, card size, "and"/"with" placement).
**In use:** "Billing's not settled — she wants a separate card before the title, not shared with the ensemble."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a detail to finalize after the money is agreed; billing is frequently worth as much to an actor's career as the fee, and negotiating it last gives away leverage that should have moved with the money.

### Scale
The minimum day or episodic rate set by the applicable union agreement (SAG-AFTRA), the negotiating floor for any signatory production.
**In use:** "Budget's tight — we're offering scale-plus-ten on the smaller roles, not scale flat."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to be a single fixed number across all projects and years; scale changes with each new union contract cycle and varies by performer classification (day player, series regular, background).

### Open call vs. submission-restricted breakdown
Whether a breakdown accepts direct actor submissions (open call) or only submissions routed through agents and managers (restricted).
**In use:** "This one's agents-and-managers only — we don't have the bandwidth to triage an open call for a series regular."
**Common misuse:** Open calls used reflexively for every role to "not miss anyone," when for a heavily represented tier of role it mostly just multiplies the unusable-submission volume without improving the actual pool.
