# Chiropractor — Playbook

## Technique selection by risk factor

| Risk factor present | Preferred approach | Avoid | Why |
|---|---|---|---|
| Osteoporosis / known low bone density | Instrument-assisted (e.g., Activator-type) or low-force mobilization | HVLA thrust | Reduced fracture threshold at the segment |
| Anticoagulant therapy | Mobilization, soft-tissue work | HVLA thrust to cervical spine | Elevated bleeding/vascular-injury risk |
| Cervical instability sign (e.g., positive Sharp-Purser) | Refer for imaging before any manipulation | Any cervical manipulation | Instability contraindicates thrust technique outright |
| Hypermobile segment (adjacent to a restricted one) | Stabilization exercise, not manipulation of the hypermobile level | HVLA on the hypermobile segment | Manipulating an already-mobile segment adds risk without benefit |
| Age > 65 with no other risk factor | Standard technique with lower-force variant as first choice | — | Conservative default; escalate force only if lower-force trial doesn't progress |
| No risk factors, clear mechanical finding | Standard HVLA appropriate | — | No indication to under-treat a straightforward presentation |

## Reassessment checkpoint — decision rule

At each checkpoint, compare against baseline on **both** self-report and at least one objective measure (orthopedic test result, ROM, or a validated disability index):

- Both improved and objective finding resolved → discharge, or step down to a maintenance interval only if a specific functional risk (e.g., occupational loading) justifies it.
- Self-report improved, objective finding still positive → continue plan as scheduled; do not discharge on symptom relief alone.
- Neither improved after the full defined trial (6–12 visits, condition-dependent) → reassess working diagnosis, consider imaging, or refer out.
- Objective finding worsening at any point, regardless of self-report → stop current approach, reassess, consider referral.

## Informed consent note (cervical manipulation, filled example)

> I have explained to the patient that cervical spinal manipulation carries a rare but serious risk of vertebral or carotid artery injury, which can in turn cause stroke. This risk is not eliminated by screening but is reduced by ruling out headache, dizziness, visual disturbance, slurred speech, or other neurological symptoms prior to treatment, which was done today with a negative result. The patient was given the opportunity to ask questions and consented to proceed. Alternative approaches (mobilization, soft-tissue work, referral) were discussed.
