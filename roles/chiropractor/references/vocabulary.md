# Chiropractor — Vocabulary

### Subluxation
In chiropractic use, a segmental dysfunction — a joint with restricted or altered motion, found on palpation and correlated with a symptom pattern.
**In use:** "The restriction at L4-L5 correlates with her reported pain on flexion, so that's the segment I'm targeting."
**Common misuse:** Treating "subluxation" as a discrete, visible, always-present lesion independent of any symptom or functional correlation — the finding only has clinical meaning when it explains something the patient is experiencing.

### Manipulation vs. mobilization
Manipulation is a high-velocity, low-amplitude (HVLA) thrust taking a joint past its passive range of motion; mobilization is a slower, lower-force technique staying within the passive range.
**In use:** "Given her anticoagulant use, I switched from manipulation to mobilization for the cervical segment."
**Common misuse:** Using the terms interchangeably — the risk profile and technique selection differ substantially between the two.

### HVLA (high-velocity, low-amplitude thrust)
The classic chiropractic adjustment technique: a fast, short-distance thrust applied to a joint.
**In use:** "I used HVLA at L4-L5 after confirming no contraindications."
**Common misuse:** Treating HVLA as the only or default technique regardless of patient risk factors — it's one option among several, selected by risk profile.

### Cavitation
The audible or palpable "pop" during joint manipulation, caused by gas-bubble release in synovial fluid, not bones "cracking into place."
**In use:** "The cavitation you heard is a normal part of the adjustment, not evidence the joint was 'out.'"
**Common misuse:** Presenting cavitation to a patient as proof a misalignment was corrected — it's a mechanical byproduct, not a diagnostic or therapeutic marker on its own.

### Vertebrobasilar insufficiency
Reduced blood flow through the vertebral and basilar arteries supplying the brainstem, which can present with dizziness, visual changes, or slurred speech and is a contraindication to cervical manipulation if suspected.
**In use:** "The dizziness with neck rotation raised concern for vertebrobasilar insufficiency, so I referred before considering cervical work."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a negative screening test fully rules out dissection risk — screening reduces but does not eliminate the risk, which is why informed consent still names it explicitly.

### Segmental dysfunction
A joint or spinal segment showing abnormal motion (restricted or excessive) relative to adjacent segments.
**In use:** "There's a segmental dysfunction at C5-C6 with reduced rotation compared to the segments above and below."
**Common misuse:** Diagnosing segmental dysfunction purely from imaging without a hands-on motion exam — it's a functional finding, not a structural one visible on X-ray.

### Instrument-assisted manipulation
A technique using a spring-loaded or mechanical device to deliver a precise, low-force impulse to a segment, as an alternative to manual HVLA thrust.
**In use:** "Given her osteoporosis, I used instrument-assisted adjustment instead of manual thrust."
**Common misuse:** Treating instrument-assisted technique as strictly inferior to manual HVLA — for certain risk profiles it's the more appropriate, not the lesser, choice.

### Maintenance care
Ongoing periodic visits after the resolution of the presenting complaint, intended to sustain a functional or preventive benefit.
**In use:** "We've resolved the acute episode; maintenance care at a monthly interval is appropriate given her occupational loading."
**Common misuse:** Scheduling maintenance visits as an open-ended default for every patient regardless of documented benefit or functional rationale — it should be tied to a specific, stated reason, not habit.

### Red flag vs. yellow flag
A red flag is a finding suggesting serious underlying pathology requiring urgent referral; a yellow flag is a psychosocial or behavioral factor (fear-avoidance, job dissatisfaction, depression) associated with poorer treatment response but not requiring emergency referral.
**In use:** "No red flags on exam, but her fear-avoidance score is high — that's a yellow flag worth addressing in the care plan."
**Common misuse:** Treating yellow flags as irrelevant to physical treatment planning — they predict outcome and should shape the plan, even though they don't require the same urgency as a red flag.

### Kemp's test
An orthopedic test for lumbar facet-joint or nerve-root involvement, performed by extending and rotating the spine toward the symptomatic side.
**In use:** "Kemp's test reproduced her right-sided pain, consistent with a facet-mediated presentation."
**Common misuse:** Treating a single positive orthopedic test as diagnostic on its own — it's one data point combined with history and other exam findings, not a standalone diagnosis.

### Active vs. passive range of motion
Active ROM is the range a patient can move a joint under their own muscular effort; passive ROM is the range achievable when the examiner moves the joint for the patient.
**In use:** "Her active flexion is limited by pain, but passive flexion is nearly full — that points toward a muscular guarding pattern rather than a joint restriction."
**Common misuse:** Recording only one of the two and drawing a mechanical conclusion — the gap between active and passive ROM is itself diagnostically informative.

### Evidence-based practice (in a chiropractic context)
Integrating the best available research evidence with clinical expertise and patient preference — a standard the field has moved toward but that varies in application across practitioners and techniques.
**In use:** "The care plan follows evidence-based visit-frequency guidelines rather than an open-ended schedule."
**Common misuse:** Invoking "evidence-based" as a blanket credibility claim without specifying which guideline or study the plan is actually drawing from.
