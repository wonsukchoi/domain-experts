# Red flags — what a CEO notices instantly

Smell tests with thresholds. Any single one can be innocent; two or three together are a pattern.

### Runway under 9-12 months with no active fundraising process or path to profitability underway
- **Usually means:** the company is closer to a forced decision (raise on bad terms, cut deeply, or fail) than the team's day-to-day urgency reflects.
- **First question:** "What specifically happens to spend and hiring if we're not closed on a raise in 90 days?"
- **Data to pull:** current cash balance, trailing 3-month net burn trend, fundraising pipeline stage and timeline.

### A strategy document or plan with more than 3-4 stated top priorities
- **Usually means:** the organization hasn't actually chosen — a list that tries to do everything is a wish list, not a strategy, and resourcing will end up thin across all of it.
- **First question:** "If we could only fund two of these, which two, and what happens to the others?"
- **Data to pull:** the strategy doc itself, headcount/budget allocation across the listed priorities.

### Board materials that read as uniformly positive with no flagged risk or miss
- **Usually means:** information is being filtered/softened on the way up, or the board relationship has drifted into presentation mode rather than governance.
- **First question:** "What's the worst piece of news this quarter that isn't in this deck?"
- **Data to pull:** the board deck itself, any skip-level or informal signal that contradicts its tone.

### A newly announced priority with no corresponding change to comp, promotion criteria, or budget allocation
- **Usually means:** the incentive structure still rewards the old behavior, so the new priority is unlikely to actually change what people do day to day.
- **First question:** "What does someone get promoted or paid more for today, and does that match what we just said matters most?"
- **Data to pull:** current comp/promotion criteria documentation, budget allocation by initiative before and after the announcement.

### Calendar time allocation that doesn't match stated top priorities
- **Usually means:** the real priorities are whatever's consuming the CEO's actual attention, regardless of what's stated — and the organization reads the calendar, not the speech.
- **First question:** "Looking at the last 4 weeks of my calendar, does the time allocation match what I've told the company matters most?"
- **Data to pull:** calendar time-block analysis by category over the trailing month.

### A major, hard-to-reverse decision (key hire, large capital commitment, structural reorg) made without a documented view of its reversibility and urgency
- **Usually means:** a one-way door decision may be getting the same fast, low-process treatment appropriate only for a two-way door — or the reverse, an easily reversible call is stuck in unnecessary process.
- **First question:** "If this turns out to be wrong, how long and how expensive is it to reverse — and does the process this decision got match that?"
- **Data to pull:** decision log/rationale if one exists, actual time and approval layers the decision went through.

### A strategic bet continuing past its planned review point with no fresh evidence cited for continuing it
- **Usually means:** inertia or reluctance to admit a wrong call, rather than a deliberate double-down based on new data.
- **First question:** "What evidence, gathered since the last review, specifically supports continuing this rather than just not wanting to stop?"
- **Data to pull:** the bet's original success criteria and timeline, actual metrics against them at the current date.

### Customer or frontline feedback that consistently differs from what's reported up through management layers
- **Usually means:** information is degrading or being softened as it travels up — a structural signal problem, not a one-off miscommunication.
- **First question:** "What does the last unfiltered customer or frontline conversation say that the last management report didn't?"
- **Data to pull:** direct customer/frontline contact notes (skip-levels, support tickets, sales call notes) versus the corresponding management summary.
