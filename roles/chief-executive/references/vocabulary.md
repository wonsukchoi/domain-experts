# Executive working vocabulary

Terms a CEO uses precisely. Format: definition → how a practitioner says it → common misuse.

**Runway** — The number of months a company can operate at its current net burn rate before cash runs out, calculated as cash on hand divided by monthly net burn.
In use: "We're at $18M cash and $1.5M monthly burn — 12 months of runway before this new hire request, 10.6 after."
Misuse: quoting runway based on gross burn or without netting out revenue, overstating how much time is actually available; also treating runway as static rather than recalculating it every time burn changes.

**One-way door vs. two-way door decision** — A one-way door decision is difficult or costly to reverse (a key hire, a major structural reorg, a large capital commitment); a two-way door decision is cheap and fast to reverse if it turns out wrong. The distinction, not the decision's size alone, should determine how much process and centralization it gets.
In use: "This is a two-way door — let's let the team ship it and iterate, not run it through three approval layers."
Misuse: treating decision size (dollar amount, visibility) as the determinant of process weight rather than actual reversibility — a small but hard-to-reverse decision deserves more care than a large but easily-undone one.

**Net burn vs. gross burn** — Gross burn is total cash outflow in a period; net burn subtracts any revenue collected in the same period, giving the true rate at which cash reserves are depleting.
In use: "Gross burn is $2.1M, but with $600K of revenue collected, net burn is $1.5M — that's the number that determines actual runway."
Misuse: reporting or planning against gross burn alone, understating how much runway revenue is already buying.

**Strategy tax (opportunity cost of a resourcing decision)** — What a company gives up elsewhere by committing scarce capital, headcount, or attention to a specific initiative, since resources allocated to one bet are unavailable to another.
In use: "Approving this headcount request has a real strategy tax — it's the same budget that would otherwise close the core product's staffing gap."
Misuse: evaluating a resourcing request only on its own merits ("is this a good opportunity?") without asking what else that same resource would otherwise fund.

**Skip-level** — A direct conversation between the CEO (or another senior leader) and employees two or more layers below them in the org chart, bypassing the intermediate management layer specifically to get less-filtered signal.
In use: "The skip-levels this month surfaced a morale issue on the platform team that hadn't come up in any manager 1:1."
Misuse: treating skip-levels as a one-off morale-check exercise rather than a recurring structural channel for getting information that naturally gets softened as it travels up through management layers.

**OKRs (Objectives and Key Results)** — A goal-setting framework pairing a qualitative objective with a small number of measurable key results, used to cascade company-level strategy into team-level, checkable execution.
In use: "Our Q3 OKR ties directly to the strategy — if the key results don't move, the strategy isn't actually being executed, whatever the team reports."
Misuse: writing OKRs as a compliance ritual disconnected from the actual stated strategy, or setting key results that are activity metrics (tasks completed) rather than outcome metrics (what actually changed).

**Incentive-behavior alignment** — The check of whether an organization's actual compensation, promotion, and resourcing structures reward the behavior a stated priority requires, as distinct from whether the priority was simply announced.
In use: "We said quality matters more this quarter, but the bonus structure still rewards ship speed alone — that's an incentive-behavior mismatch we need to fix before the message will land."
Misuse: assuming an announced priority changes behavior on its own, without auditing whether the underlying incentive structure was actually updated to match.

**Return on invested capital (ROIC) thinking** — Evaluating a resource-allocation decision by the return generated relative to the capital or headcount committed, used to compare fundamentally different opportunities (a new product line vs. fixing an existing one) on a common basis.
In use: "On a rough ROIC basis, fixing the core product's staffing gap has a faster, more certain payback than the new product line pilot right now."
Misuse: comparing opportunities only on absolute upside (total market size, headline revenue potential) without normalizing for the capital or headcount required to pursue each one.

**Reversibility-weighted process** — Matching the amount of deliberation, approval layers, and time a decision gets to its actual reversibility and urgency, rather than to its visibility or dollar size alone.
In use: "This is reversible and cheap to undo — let's not put it through the same review as the lease commitment."
Misuse: applying uniform decision process regardless of reversibility, which either slows down easily-reversible calls unnecessarily or rushes truly hard-to-reverse ones.

**Communication half-life** — The observation that a strategy or priority stated once, even clearly, decays in organizational understanding over time and across levels, requiring repeated restatement in different forms to actually take hold company-wide.
In use: "I know I'm sick of repeating this strategy, but by communication half-life, half the company is probably just hearing it clearly for the first time."
Misuse: assuming a single all-hands announcement means the organization now understands and will act on a strategic priority.
