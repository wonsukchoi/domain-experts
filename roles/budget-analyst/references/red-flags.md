# Red flags — what a budget analyst notices instantly

Smell tests with thresholds. Any one can be innocent; two or three together on the same request is a pattern.

### A unit's actual spend runs 8%+ under its appropriation for two consecutive fiscal years on the same line
- **Usually means:** a position that can't be filled, a program that quietly ended, or a line padded above real need — not disciplined underspending.
- **First question:** "Which specific position or activity drove this both years, and is it the same one?"
- **Data to pull:** position control roster (months vacant by position, both years), variance report driver notes for both cycles.

### A decision package prices a new facility, program, or position group at a full fiscal-year cost when the confirmed start date is mid-year
- **Usually means:** the department defaulted to "budget year = cost year" instead of checking the actual construction/hiring timeline, or is padding the ask knowing a cut is coming.
- **First question:** "What's the confirmed occupancy or start date, from the entity actually running the project — not the department's internal target?"
- **Data to pull:** construction schedule or procurement timeline from the executing agency (Public Works, IT, procurement), hiring lead-time assumption used.

### The requested increase is a single round number ("$500K more") with no workload count behind it
- **Usually means:** the ask was set by comparison to another department's increase or by what leadership will tolerate, not by an actual driver.
- **First question:** "What workload measure — cases, calls, square footage, membership count — is this dollar figure built from?"
- **Data to pull:** the workload/volume driver field of the decision package; comparable prior-year actuals for the same driver.

### A department requests the same dollar increase every cycle regardless of workload trend
- **Usually means:** the increase has become an entitlement baked into planning rather than a re-justified number — classic incremental-budgeting drift that zero-based review exists to catch.
- **First question:** "If workload were flat this year, would the request still be the same number?"
- **Data to pull:** 3-5 year trend of request vs. adopted vs. actual, workload driver trend over the same period.

### Personnel costs are requested under an "operating" or "contracted services" line instead of the personnel category
- **Usually means:** an attempt to route around a headcount cap or hiring freeze, or a genuine misclassification that will still distort personnel-cost trend reporting either way.
- **First question:** "Is this an employee (even if titled 'contractor') performing an ongoing function under direction and schedule set by the department?"
- **Data to pull:** the underlying contract or position description; compare control test factors (direction, schedule, duration) against the jurisdiction's classification policy.

### An ongoing operating cost (staffing, routine maintenance, utilities) is requested inside a capital project budget
- **Usually means:** the department is using one-time capital authority to avoid the operating budget's tighter scrutiny — creating a cliff the year the capital funding ends.
- **First question:** "What happens to this cost the fiscal year after the capital project closes out?"
- **Data to pull:** capital project budget detail line-by-line, Capital Improvement Plan (multi-year) to see if the operating impact was ever projected forward.

### Unrestricted general fund balance is at or drifting toward 2 months (~16.7%) of operating expenditures with no jurisdiction-specific rationale for the level
- **Usually means:** the jurisdiction is treating GFOA's stated floor as its target, without having tested it against its own revenue volatility.
- **First question:** "What's our revenue mix (property tax vs. sales/tourism-driven), and what would a 2-quarter downturn scenario do to this cushion?"
- **Data to pull:** revenue source mix over the last 5 years, historical revenue volatility (largest single-year decline), current reserve policy document and its stated rationale (if any).

### A variance line exceeds the jurisdiction's own review threshold (commonly 5-10%, policy-set) with no driver memo attached
- **Usually means:** either the explanation was never required and enforced, or the unit is hoping an unexplained variance rolls into next year's baseline unexamined.
- **First question:** "Has this specific line tripped the threshold before, and was a memo filed that time?"
- **Data to pull:** variance report history for the line, any prior driver memos on file.

### A budget amendment reclassifies spending across appropriation categories (e.g., personnel to capital, or across funds) without going back through formal adoption
- **Usually means:** someone treated a legally binding appropriation as an internal working estimate — an Antideficiency-Act-class problem in federal/state contexts, a charter or code violation in most local ones.
- **First question:** "Who approved this shift, and under what authority — was it processed as a formal amendment or just re-coded?"
- **Data to pull:** the amendment/transfer log, the jurisdiction's own threshold for what requires governing-body approval versus administrative transfer authority.

### An agency claims obligating authority from its full-year appropriation before checking what's currently apportioned (federal/state contexts with apportionment controls)
- **Usually means:** confusion between appropriation (Congress/legislature authorized) and apportionment (OMB/central budget office released for the current period) — a real risk of an Antideficiency-Act violation if obligations exceed what's apportioned for the period.
- **First question:** "What's apportioned for obligation in the current quarter or period, separate from the annual total?"
- **Data to pull:** the current apportionment schedule (Category A/B/AB lines under OMB Circular A-11), obligation activity to date against it.
