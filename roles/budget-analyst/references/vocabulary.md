# Budget analyst working vocabulary

Terms a budget office uses daily and precisely. Format: definition → how a practitioner says it → common misuse. Public-sector (government) context; corporate FP&A analogs noted where they diverge.

**Appropriation vs. apportionment vs. allotment** — Appropriation = the legislature's/council's legal authorization to spend up to a set amount for a stated purpose. Apportionment (federal/some state usage) = the budget office's (OMB's) release of that authority for a specific period or program, per OMB Circular A-11. Allotment = a further internal subdivision, often to a department or quarter.
In use: "We've got the annual appropriation, but only Q2's apportionment is released — we can't obligate against Q3 yet."
Misuse: treating "it's appropriated" as "it's spendable right now." The apportionment (or allotment, at the local level) is the actual gate.

**Obligation vs. outlay (federal/state usage)** — Obligation = a binding commitment to pay (a signed contract, a purchase order). Outlay = the actual cash disbursement, which can happen well after the obligation.
In use: "We obligated the full $2M on the paving contract in Q3, but outlays won't hit until the work is billed over the next three quarters."
Misuse: reading "unspent" (low outlays) as "uncommitted" — a heavily obligated budget can have very little left to allocate even with outlays barely started.

**Encumbrance** — A local/state-government accounting reservation of budget authority against a purchase order or contract not yet paid, so the same dollar can't be double-committed.
In use: "That $40K is encumbered against the paving PO — it shows as spent in the budget system even though the invoice hasn't come in."
Misuse: confusing an encumbrance with an actual expenditure when reading a "budget vs. actual" report — an encumbrance-heavy line can look overspent on a cash basis and be fine on a budgetary basis, or vice versa depending on the report's basis.

**Zero-based budgeting (ZBB)** — Every dollar re-justified from zero each cycle via ranked decision packages, rather than starting from last year's number (Pyhrr, Texas Instruments, early 1970s; Georgia state government under then-Gov. Carter).
In use: "We're running ZBB on Parks & Rec this cycle since their base hasn't been re-examined in six years."
Misuse: applying it agency-wide every single year regardless of resourcing — full-scope ZBB every cycle is a multi-week-per-department exercise; most practitioners rotate a subset.

**Incremental budgeting** — Building next year's budget as last year's adopted amount plus/minus a percentage adjustment, without re-examining the base.
In use: "This is a straight incremental roll — 3% COLA on last year's number, no package required."
Misuse: using it as the default for every line by habit, which is exactly how a chronically vacant position's savings never gets trued down.

**Decision package** — A self-contained request for a new or changed program/position, priced and ranked against every other unit's packages, the operational unit of ZBB review.
In use: "Their community center package ranked #1 of 4 — fully justified, it's the one we fund first if revenue comes in short."
Misuse: bundling a new program's cost into the baseline continuation submission instead of a standalone package, which hides it from decomposition and phase-in review.

**Fund balance (unrestricted) / reserve** — The accumulated, spendable-with-discretion portion of a government fund's net resources; GFOA recommends a floor of ≥2 months (~16.7%) of general fund operating revenues or expenditures.
In use: "We're at 18% unrestricted fund balance — above the GFOA floor, but our revenue's tourism-tax-heavy, so I'd want us closer to 25%."
Misuse: treating the GFOA figure as a universal target rather than a floor to be adjusted for the jurisdiction's own revenue volatility.

**Variance (favorable/unfavorable) — timing vs. structural** — A budget-to-actual gap. Timing variance reverses on its own (a delayed invoice, a hire that slipped a quarter); structural variance persists because the baseline itself is wrong.
In use: "The maintenance line's favorable variance is structural — same vacant position, second year — so we're trueing the base down, not just noting it."
Misuse: reporting every favorable variance as "good budget management" without checking whether it will recur, which lets a bad baseline compound year over year.

**Baseline / base budget** — The continuation cost of an existing program at current service level, before escalation, workload change, or new initiatives are layered on.
In use: "Strip out the new center — what's the actual base for existing Parks operations this year?"
Misuse: assuming last year's adopted appropriation equals the correct base, when actuals show the base itself needs trueing up or down first.

**Reprogramming / transfer authority** — Moving budget authority between purposes, programs, or categories after adoption; federal practice under OMB Circular A-11 sets specific notification/approval thresholds, and most state/local codes set their own thresholds for what an executive can shift administratively versus what requires governing-body approval.
In use: "That's a reprogramming above our administrative threshold — it needs Council action, not a memo."
Misuse: assuming any in-year shift is fine as long as the fund total doesn't change; category and purpose restrictions bind independently of the total.

**Capital Improvement Plan (CIP)** — A multi-year (typically 5-6 year) rolling plan of capital projects, budgeted and adopted separately from the annual operating budget, updated annually.
In use: "The community center is Year 2 of the CIP — the operating cost it creates has to land in the FY27 operating budget, not the capital fund."
Misuse: treating the CIP as a place to also carry a project's future operating costs, which hides the ongoing budget impact until the capital funding runs out.

**Full-cost vs. marginal (incremental) cost allocation** — Full-cost allocates a proportional share of indirect/overhead costs to a program or grant (required for federal grant compliance under 2 CFR 200); marginal costing prices only the additional cost a specific unit of activity causes.
In use: "For the federal grant claim we allocate full cost per our approved cost allocation plan; for the internal fleet chargeback we price marginal cost per mile so departments aren't overcharged for shared fixed costs."
Misuse: using full-cost allocation for an internal chargeback decision, which can make an in-house service look artificially more expensive than an outside vendor quoted on marginal cost alone.

**Continuing resolution (CR)** — Federal (and some state) practice of funding government at the prior year's level, or a formula rate, when a new appropriation hasn't been enacted by the fiscal year start.
In use: "We're operating under a CR at the prior-year rate — no new starts, no rate changes until the real appropriation passes."
Misuse: assuming a CR simply means "same budget as last year" without checking its specific anomaly/new-start restrictions, which are usually tighter than a flat continuation.

**Distinguished Budget Presentation Award (GFOA)** — A voluntary peer-reviewed recognition for a budget document meeting GFOA's criteria as a policy document, financial plan, operations guide, and communications device.
In use: "We restructured the narrative section to meet the Distinguished Budget criteria this cycle, not just to pass the audit."
Misuse: treating it as a compliance requirement rather than a voluntary quality bar — no jurisdiction is required to pursue it, and not pursuing it says nothing about budget quality on its own.
