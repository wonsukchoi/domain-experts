# Vocabulary

Terms generalists conflate or use loosely that a tire repairer/changer keeps sharply separate — each with a definition, a sentence a practitioner would actually say, and the common misuse.

### Repairable injury vs. non-repairable injury

**Repairable injury** is a puncture within the tread/crown area, ≤1/4 in (6mm) diameter, with no secondary damage on inner-liner inspection. **Non-repairable** is anything failing any one of those three conditions — location, size, or secondary damage.

**In use:** "It's non-repairable — not because of the size, it's a shoulder hit."

**Common misuse:** treating "non-repairable" as a size-only judgment call ("it's small, it'll be fine") when location alone already disqualifies it.

### Plug vs. patch vs. combination repair

**Plug** fills the puncture channel from the tread side. **Patch** seals the inner liner from the inside over the injury. **Combination repair** does both in one unit, installed only after demounting and inspecting the inner liner — the only method meeting the USTMA/TIA repair standard.

**In use:** "That's not a repair, that's a plug — we need to pull it and do a combination unit if it qualifies."

**Common misuse:** calling a plug-only fix "the repair" on the invoice, or assuming a patch alone (no plug stem) fully seals a puncture channel against later corrosion of the steel belts.

### Load range vs. ply rating

**Load range** is the modern designation (B, C, D, E, F...) for a tire's load-carrying capacity at a specified inflation pressure. **Ply rating** is the older nomenclature (4-ply, 6-ply, 8-ply...) describing the equivalent strength in cotton-ply construction terms, even though modern tires don't literally use that many plies. The two map directly (Load Range C ≈ 6-ply, Load Range E ≈ 10-ply) but only one — load range plus its rated inflation — is the number that matters for a capacity decision.

**In use:** "Don't quote him '6-ply,' quote him Load Range C at 50 psi and show him what that carries versus the E he's got now."

**Common misuse:** treating ply rating as a literal physical ply count in a modern tire, or assuming two tires with the same "ply rating" name carry the same load without checking the load range table.

### Speed rating

A letter code (Q, S, T, H, V, W, Y...) denoting the maximum sustained speed a tire is rated to carry its full rated load. It is a laboratory-tested structural limit, not a suggestion tied to typical driving speed.

**In use:** "Vehicle's speed capability is capped at the lowest-rated tire on it — doesn't matter that the other three are V-rated if one's an S."

**Common misuse:** assuming speed rating only matters for drivers who actually drive at the rated speed — heat buildup and structural margin at highway speed are affected well below the rated ceiling too.

### Direct TPMS vs. indirect TPMS

**Direct TPMS** uses a physical pressure sensor inside each wheel transmitting pressure and a sensor ID to a receiver. **Indirect TPMS** infers pressure loss from wheel-speed differences through the ABS system, with no physical sensor. Direct systems need a relearn (stationary sequence or scan tool) after service; indirect systems need a reset through the vehicle's menu.

**In use:** "Check which system it has before you touch it — indirect just needs a menu reset, direct needs the sensor relearn or the light's coming right back."

**Common misuse:** running a direct-system relearn procedure on an indirect vehicle (or vice versa) because "TPMS" sounds like one universal process.

### Shoulder vs. sidewall vs. crown/tread area

**Crown/tread area** is the flat, road-contacting portion. **Shoulder** is the transition zone where tread meets sidewall. **Sidewall** is the flexing, unsupported portion between shoulder and bead. Repair standards treat crown as repairable (within limits) and shoulder/sidewall as never repairable, because the cord structure and flex behavior differ by zone.

**In use:** "It's a half-inch into the shoulder — that's not tread anymore, that's the transition zone, and it's out."

**Common misuse:** describing an injury as "on the edge of the tread" as though that's still inside the repairable zone — the shoulder is the boundary, not a buffer that counts as tread.

### Run-flat / self-supporting tire vs. standard tire

A **run-flat (self-supporting)** tire has reinforced sidewalls that carry the vehicle's weight for a limited distance and speed after a full pressure loss. A **standard tire** collapses onto the rim under the same condition. Run-flats generally cannot be repaired even for injuries that would qualify under the standard size/zone rule, once they've been driven in the deflated state.

**In use:** "Even if the puncture's textbook-perfect in the crown, this is a run-flat that's been driven flat — it's done, per the manufacturer's own policy."

**Common misuse:** applying the standard repairable-injury rule to a run-flat that's been driven at low or zero pressure, without checking whether that specific manufacturer prohibits repair regardless of injury location.

### DOT date code

The last four digits of a tire's DOT stamp, formatted week-week-year-year (e.g., `3221` = week 32 of 2021), identifying manufacture date. Distinct from tread wear — a tire can pass a tread-depth check and still be aged past a manufacturer's recommended service life.

**In use:** "Tread's fine, but it's a 2018 tire — that's age, not wear, and it's a separate conversation."

**Common misuse:** relying on tread depth alone to judge whether a tire needs replacing, without checking the date code for dry rot or belt-separation risk.

### GAWR vs. GVWR

**GAWR** (Gross Axle Weight Rating) is the maximum load a single axle is rated to carry. **GVWR** (Gross Vehicle Weight Rating) is the maximum for the entire vehicle, front and rear axles combined. Tire load-capacity decisions are made against GAWR per axle, not GVWR divided evenly — front and rear axles often carry different rated loads.

**In use:** "Don't divide GVWR by four — pull the rear GAWR off the placard and divide that by the two rear tires."

**Common misuse:** dividing GVWR evenly across all tires as if every axle carries an equal share of the vehicle's total rated weight.

### Torque sequence (star/cross pattern) vs. sequential tightening

**Torque sequence (star/cross pattern)** tightens lugs in a crossing order (e.g., 1-3-5-2-4 on a 5-lug) in staged passes to final spec. **Sequential tightening** — going around the wheel in physical order — seats the wheel unevenly against the hub even at identical final torque numbers, because early-tightened lugs can pull the wheel slightly off-center before the last ones are reached.

**In use:** "Same torque wrench, same final number — but do it in the star pattern or you'll warp that rotor eventually."

**Common misuse:** assuming that reaching the same final ft-lb reading on every lug is sufficient regardless of the order they were tightened in.

### Combination repair unit

A single manufactured repair product combining a plug stem (fills the puncture channel) and a patch (bonds to the inner liner), installed as one piece after buffing and cementing — as distinct from separately sourced plug rope and patch stock.

**In use:** "Use the combination unit rated for that hole diameter, not a standalone patch with a loose plug stuffed through it."

**Common misuse:** assembling a plug and a patch from separate, non-matched products and treating it as equivalent to a manufacturer-rated combination unit.
