### Fair market value (FMV)
The price a willing buyer would pay a willing seller, neither under compulsion, both having reasonable knowledge of relevant facts — the standard value premise for donation, estate, and gift tax purposes.
**In use:** "Fair market value for this donation appraisal is $16,500, reconciled from three comparable auction sales."
**Common misuse:** Using fair market value for an insurance or lending appraisal, where replacement cost or liquidation value is the actual applicable premise.

### Replacement cost (replacement value)
The cost to replace an item with a new item of similar kind and quality — the standard value premise for insurance appraisals.
**In use:** "Replacement cost for this equipment is what the insurance policy will pay out, not its current fair market value."
**Common misuse:** Confusing replacement cost with fair market value, which reflects what a used item would actually sell for, not what a new equivalent would cost.

### Orderly liquidation value (OLV)
The estimated value realizable in an orderly sale process over a reasonable period of time, typically used as the basis for asset-based lending collateral valuation.
**In use:** "The lender's collateral valuation is based on orderly liquidation value, not fair market value in continued use."
**Common misuse:** Using fair market value in continued use for a lending collateral appraisal, overstating what the asset would realize if the borrower defaulted and the asset needed to be sold.

### Forced liquidation value (FLV)
The estimated value realizable in a forced or urgent sale within a compressed timeframe, materially lower than orderly liquidation value.
**In use:** "In a bankruptcy forced-sale scenario, forced liquidation value is the appropriate premise, well below the orderly liquidation figure."
**Common misuse:** Conflating forced liquidation value with orderly liquidation value — the compressed timeline in a forced sale produces a materially lower number.

### Marketable cash value
Fair market value minus the estimated costs of sale (commissions, closing costs), sometimes used in divorce or litigation contexts to reflect what a party would actually net from a sale.
**In use:** "Marketable cash value nets out the auction house's commission from the fair market value figure."
**Common misuse:** Using fair market value interchangeably with marketable cash value in a context (like divorce settlement) where the actual net proceeds after sale costs is the relevant figure.

### Qualified appraisal / qualified appraiser
IRS-defined requirements under Form 8283 for noncash charitable donations over $5,000, specifying appraiser credentialing/experience standards and appraisal timing (completed no more than 60 days before the donation).
**In use:** "This donation needs a qualified appraisal — confirm the appraiser's credentials and that the report is dated within the 60-day window."
**Common misuse:** Assuming any competent value opinion satisfies the IRS requirement, without confirming the specific qualified-appraiser credentialing and timing rules are met.

### Provenance
The documented history of an item's ownership, exhibition, and authentication, used to establish attribution and support value for art, antiques, and collectibles.
**In use:** "Provenance includes the original gallery receipt and a documented exhibition history, supporting the attribution."
**Common misuse:** Treating provenance as optional supporting detail rather than a threshold requirement for establishing authentication before valuation proceeds.

### Cost approach (for machinery/equipment)
A valuation method estimating value as the current replacement cost new, less accumulated depreciation from physical deterioration, functional obsolescence, and economic obsolescence.
**In use:** "Cost approach is more reliable here given how thin the resale market is for this specific type of industrial equipment."
**Common misuse:** Applying a straight-line depreciation estimate without separately considering functional obsolescence (outdated technology) or economic obsolescence (reduced demand for the equipment's output), which can be more significant value drivers than physical wear alone.

### Comparable sale (personal property)
A documented sale of a similar item (same maker/artist, period, condition) used to support a value conclusion via the sales comparison approach.
**In use:** "This comparable is a strong match — same artist, similar period, comparable condition."
**Common misuse:** Using a comparable from a different maker, period, or significantly different condition without documenting why it's still relevant, weakening the value conclusion's support.

### USPAP (Uniform Standards of Professional Appraisal Practice), Personal Property
The section of USPAP governing personal property appraisal practice, covering scope of work, value premise selection, and reporting requirements distinct from the real property sections.
**In use:** "The report follows USPAP's Personal Property standards, including the stated value premise and intended use."
**Common misuse:** Applying real property USPAP conventions (like standardized comparable adjustment grids) directly to personal property appraisal without accounting for the different, often thinner and more qualitative comparable data available for unique items.
