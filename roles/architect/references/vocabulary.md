# Architect — Vocabulary

### FAR (Floor Area Ratio)
The ratio of total building floor area to lot area, set by zoning — a zoning constraint, separate from any building-code area limit.
**In use:** "We're at 3.0 FAR on a 6,000 SF lot, so 18,000 SF is the ceiling regardless of what the building code would otherwise allow."
**Common misuse:** treating FAR as a building-code concept, or assuming it's satisfied just because the building-code allowable-area calc passed.

### Allowable area (IBC)
The maximum floor area permitted per floor for a given occupancy classification and construction type, before sprinkler/frontage increases — a building-code concept, separate from zoning's FAR.
**In use:** "Base allowable for V-B, Group B is 9,000 SF, but the sprinkler and frontage increases bring it to 27,000 — we're well inside that."
**Common misuse:** confusing allowable area with FAR-driven zoning area, or forgetting the increases apply only when the qualifying conditions (sprinklers, frontage) are actually met.

### Occupant load factor
The square-feet-per-occupant value assigned by IBC Table 1004.5 to a specific use classification, used to calculate required egress capacity.
**In use:** "At 150 SF/occupant for Group B, that 6,000 SF floor is a 40-person occupant load."
**Common misuse:** eyeballing occupant load from how full a room looks instead of applying the code table's factor for the space's actual use classification.

### Egress width
The minimum clear width of exit components (doors, stairs, corridors) required per occupant, calculated by multiplying occupant load by a code-specified inches-per-occupant factor.
**In use:** "40 occupants at 0.3 in/occupant is a 12-inch minimum — our two 44-inch stairs give us more than seven times that."
**Common misuse:** sizing egress width to "look adequate" rather than computing it from the occupant load and code factor, especially after a late-stage occupancy reclassification.

### Construction type (IBC Type I-V)
A code classification of a building's structural and envelope materials (I being most fire-resistive, V least), which sets allowable height, area, and required fire ratings.
**In use:** "We're Type V-B, unprotected, unsprinklered — the sprinkler system is what unlocks the area increase we're relying on."
**Common misuse:** treating construction type as a design preference rather than a code-driven classification that constrains allowable area and height before any increases are applied.

### Value engineering (VE)
A structured cost-reduction process comparing a design element's cost against its function, distinct from an unstructured "just cut the budget" request.
**In use:** "This VE item swaps the curtain wall for a punched-window system — that's a finish/systems tradeoff, not a code-minimum reduction, so it's a legitimate VE target."
**Common misuse:** applying VE language to a reduction that actually cuts into a code-minimum dimension (egress width, fire rating), where there's no margin left to "value engineer" away.

### RFI (Request for Information)
A formal, written, drawing-referenced construction-phase question from the contractor to the design team, logged and tracked — distinct from an informal field conversation.
**In use:** "That's an RFI, not a phone call — log it against Sheet A-401 and I'll respond in writing by end of day."
**Common misuse:** treating a verbal field answer as equivalent to an RFI response; it carries no documentation if the resulting condition is disputed later.

### Change order (CO)
A documented modification to the contract sum and/or schedule, executed after a field condition, owner request, or design change alters the original scope.
**In use:** "CO-004 is a net $11,200 increase with zero schedule impact — that column line had six days of float."
**Common misuse:** stating a change order's cost impact without a corresponding schedule-impact finding, missing that a cost-neutral change can still sit on the critical path.

### Schematic Design (SD) / Design Development (DD) / Construction Documents (CD)
The three sequential design phases between programming and permit submission, each with a distinct deliverable and typical fee-percentage allocation.
**In use:** "We're at 50% CD — consultant coordination needs a full overlay review before we go further, not just at 100%."
**Common misuse:** treating the phases as purely a billing milestone rather than a deliverable-completeness gate — advancing to the next phase's fee percentage without the prior phase's deliverable actually being coordinated.

### Long-lead item
A procurement item (curtain wall, elevators, specialty structural steel) whose fabrication lead time is long enough that releasing it late in design, rather than early, adds direct schedule risk.
**In use:** "The curtain wall has a 20-week lead time — we release it for fabrication at 60% CDs or it becomes the critical path."
**Common misuse:** waiting until 100% CDs to release any procurement item regardless of its individual lead time, when the risk is item-specific, not phase-wide.

### Construction Administration (CA)
The design team's post-permit phase responsibilities — RFI response, submittal review, site observation — distinct from construction management, which the architect does not perform.
**In use:** "CA includes biweekly site visits and RFI turnaround, but we're not managing the contractor's means and methods — that's outside our scope."
**Common misuse:** assuming CA responsibilities include means-and-methods or safety oversight, which is the contractor's responsibility, not the architect's.

### Separated construction (IBC 508.4)
A method of accommodating multiple occupancy classifications in one building by providing a fire-rated barrier between them, rather than treating the whole building as the most restrictive occupancy.
**In use:** "The 2-hour fire barrier between the M and B occupancies is what lets us use separated construction instead of the non-separated method's more restrictive allowable-area math."
**Common misuse:** assuming any floor/ceiling assembly qualifies as the separation, without checking it meets the specific fire-rating requirement IBC 508.4 sets for the occupancy pairing.
