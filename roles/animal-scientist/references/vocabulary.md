# Animal Scientist — Vocabulary

### EPD (Expected Progeny Difference)
A prediction of how an animal's offspring will perform, on average, relative to the offspring of an average animal in the same breed genetic-evaluation database — expressed in the trait's own units (lb, %, days).
**In use:** "His weaning-weight EPD is +58, so his calves should wean about 13 lb heavier than calves from a bull with a +45 EPD."
**Common misuse:** treating an EPD as a prediction for one specific calf's actual weight rather than an average across many calves in many herds — a high-EPD bull can still throw a light calf out of a poor-milking cow.

### TDN (Total Digestible Nutrients)
An energy-value measure expressed as a percentage of feed dry matter, representing the sum of digestible protein, fiber, nitrogen-free extract, and fat (fat weighted 2.25×).
**In use:** "The finishing ration needs to run about 75% TDN to hit target gain."
**Common misuse:** treating TDN as interchangeable with crude protein — a ration can meet a TDN target while badly missing protein, or vice versa; they're separate binding constraints.

### Heritability (h²)
The fraction of observed variation in a trait attributable to genetic differences, on a 0-1 scale, for a given population and environment.
**In use:** "Fertility's heritability runs low, around 0.10, so we're not going to move calving rate much through sire selection alone."
**Common misuse:** treating heritability as a fixed biological constant rather than population- and environment-specific — h² estimated in one herd/environment doesn't transfer cleanly to another.

### Selection index
A single composite score combining multiple EPDs, each weighted by its economic value to the specific production system, used to rank breeding candidates.
**In use:** "He ranks higher on the terminal index even though his weaning-weight EPD is lower, because his calving-ease numbers pull more weight in this herd."
**Common misuse:** assuming a generic published index applies to any operation — the weights are built around a specific marketing endpoint (terminal vs. maternal) and don't transfer across production systems.

### Least-cost formulation
A linear-programming method that finds the lowest-cost ingredient mix satisfying every nutrient requirement as a constraint simultaneously.
**In use:** "Corn price is up, so we need to re-run the least-cost formulation, not just swap in more hay."
**Common misuse:** confusing "least-cost" with "cheapest ingredients" — the solution can include a more expensive ingredient if it's the only way to clear a binding nutrient constraint at lower total cost.

### Feed-conversion ratio (FCR)
The ratio of feed dry matter consumed to weight gained, over a defined period — lower is more efficient.
**In use:** "FCR crept from 6.2 to 6.8 this month; before we touch the ration, let's check the scale and the health log."
**Common misuse:** comparing FCR across different growth stages as if it should stay constant — FCR rises (gets less efficient) as an animal approaches mature or market weight, by biology, not by formulation failure.

### Calving ease direct (CED)
An EPD predicting a sire's tendency to produce calves born with less assistance, expressed as a percentage difference in unassisted-birth probability relative to breed average.
**In use:** "We need positive calving-ease-direct EPDs on any bull going over first-calf heifers."
**Common misuse:** confusing calving-ease direct (the calf's birth-ease contribution) with calving-ease maternal (a daughter's future ability to calve easily) — they're different traits with different EPDs.

### Dry matter (DM) basis
Feed values expressed after removing moisture content, allowing fair comparison of ingredients with different water content (e.g., silage vs. dry hay).
**In use:** "The ration is 68% TDN on a DM basis — remember to adjust as-fed amounts for the silage's actual moisture."
**Common misuse:** mixing as-fed and DM-basis numbers in the same calculation, which silently overstates nutrient delivery for high-moisture feeds like silage.

### Antagonistic trait correlation
A genetic relationship where selecting for one trait tends to move a correlated trait in an undesired direction (e.g., growth and calving ease).
**In use:** "Growth and birth weight are antagonistically correlated, so heavy selection for growth alone tends to push birth weights up too."
**Common misuse:** assuming all traits are independent and can be selected for individually without tradeoffs — most economically relevant traits have at least one antagonistic correlation worth checking.

### Body condition score (BCS)
A subjective, standardized visual/palpation score (commonly 1-9 scale in beef cattle) estimating an animal's fat reserves, used as a proxy for nutritional status.
**In use:** "She's a BCS 4 at calving — thin enough that rebreeding is going to be a challenge without extra energy in the ration."
**Common misuse:** treating a single BCS reading as diagnostic on its own rather than tracking it over time — a trend (declining, stable, improving) carries more information than one score.

### Proper-use factor
The fraction of standing forage production that can be harvested by grazing without degrading plant vigor or soil cover, varying with range condition.
**In use:** "This pasture's in fair condition, so we're using a 45% proper-use factor instead of the 50% default."
**Common misuse:** applying a flat 50% proper-use factor to every site regardless of current range condition, which locks already-degraded land into continued decline.

### Binding constraint
In a least-cost formulation, a nutrient requirement met exactly at its minimum (or maximum), meaning the solution can't reduce cost further without violating that specific nutrient.
**In use:** "Protein's the binding constraint here — if soybean meal price drops, the ration cost drops too, but if it rises, we have to reformulate."
**Common misuse:** assuming every nutrient in a formulation is equally sensitive to ingredient price changes — only the binding constraints actually determine cost sensitivity.
