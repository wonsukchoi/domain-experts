# Moving Crew Supervisor — Vocabulary

### Cube sheet (Table of Measurements)
The estimator's item-by-item conversion of a household's contents into cubic feet, used to derive the estimated shipment weight before the crew ever sees the job.
**In use:** "The cube sheet says 6,000 lbs — anything the crew loads that isn't on it needs its own paperwork."
**Common misuse:** Treating it as a rough guess rather than the document the entire estimate, and the 110% ceiling calculation, is anchored to.

### Bill of Lading (BOL)
The signed contract of carriage for the shipment — states the estimate type, weight or cube basis, tariff rate, and valuation option, and is the legal reference document for the whole move.
**In use:** "Nothing goes on the truck that isn't covered by what's on the BOL or a signed additional-service order."
**Common misuse:** Confusing it with a receipt or inventory list — it's the contract, and its estimate-type field is what determines the payment rules at delivery.

### Binding estimate
A fixed total price agreed before the move, regardless of actual weight (absent additional services).
**In use:** "This one's binding at $6,400 — the reweigh doesn't change what's owed unless the customer adds items."
**Common misuse:** Assuming "binding" means the crew can add items without new paperwork just because the price is locked — added items still need a signed additional-service order.

### Non-binding estimate
An estimate based on projected weight, where final charges are based on actual weight at delivery, subject to the 110% collect-on-delivery payment ceiling.
**In use:** "It's non-binding, so we run the 110% cap against the actual reweigh before we collect anything at the door."
**Common misuse:** Treating "non-binding" as meaning the customer owes whatever the actual weight comes out to, in full, at delivery — the ceiling caps what's collectable today, not what's ultimately owed.

### Hundredweight (CWT)
The linehaul billing unit — the tariff rate is priced per 100 lbs of shipment weight.
**In use:** "Tariff's $120 per cwt, so 6,900 lbs is 69 times $120 for the linehaul line."
**Common misuse:** Applying the cwt rate to gross truck weight instead of net shipment weight (gross minus tare).

### Tare weight / gross weight / net weight
Tare is the empty truck's weight, gross is the loaded truck's weight, and net (gross minus tare) is the actual billable shipment weight.
**In use:** "Tare was 32,000, gross came back 38,900 — net is 6,900, that's the number that goes on the bill."
**Common misuse:** Billing off the gross weight directly, which includes the truck itself and any fuel variance since the last tare weighing.

### Reweigh
A second, customer-witnessable weighing of the loaded shipment; if the second weight is lower than the first, the lower figure governs billing.
**In use:** "Customer wants to watch the reweigh — that's their right, and if it comes in lower we bill the lower number."
**Common misuse:** Treating a reweigh as something the crew can decline to offer, or something that only benefits the company.

### 110% rule
The federal cap (49 CFR 375.405) on collect-on-delivery payment for a non-binding-estimate shipment: no more than 110% of the estimate's total charges may be demanded at delivery, with any remainder billed no sooner than 30 days out.
**In use:** "Actual came in over — cap it at 110% of the estimate, release the shipment, and bill the balance later."
**Common misuse:** Applying the 110% multiplier to the *actual* weight's cost instead of the *original estimate's* total — it's a ceiling on the estimate, not a markup on the real bill.

### Hostage load
Refusing to release a shipment at delivery until the customer pays an amount above the legal payment ceiling — a serious consumer-protection violation, not a collections tactic.
**In use:** "We don't hold a load over the cap, ever — that's a hostage-load complaint waiting to happen."
**Common misuse:** Assuming any dispute over final price justifies withholding the shipment; only a payment below the legally due amount (estimate total, or capped amount for non-binding) does.

### Additional-service order
A signed document authorizing and pricing an item or service added after the BOL was signed, separate from and not folded into the original weight-based estimate.
**In use:** "The safe's not on the cube sheet — get the additional-service order signed before it goes on the dolly."
**Common misuse:** Treating a verbal "go ahead and load it" as sufficient — without a signature, the added weight is both uncollectable and, if it pushes the shipment over the estimate's ceiling, undocumented exposure.

### Accessorial (long carry, shuttle, stair carry)
A billable service beyond standard curb-to-curb loading — extra distance from truck to door, a shuttle vehicle for inaccessible streets, or a flight of stairs.
**In use:** "That's a 150-foot carry from where the truck can park — it's a long-carry accessorial, and it should've been on the original survey."
**Common misuse:** Charging an accessorial that wasn't disclosed at the original survey without documenting why it wasn't caught then — undisclosed accessorials are a common source of delivery-day disputes.

### Released Value Protection
The default, no-cost valuation option: the company's liability for loss or damage is capped at $0.60 per pound per article, regardless of the item's actual value.
**In use:** "Customer's on released value — a 20-lb lamp claim caps at $12, even if it cost $400."
**Common misuse:** Assuming the low cap means damage documentation doesn't matter — the crew's own liability for an unrecorded pre-existing scratch isn't limited by the customer's valuation choice.

### Full Value Protection (FVP)
A valuation option, usually at additional cost, under which the company is liable for repair, replacement, or cash settlement at current value for lost or damaged items, subject to any stated deductible.
**In use:** "This shipment's under FVP with a $250 deductible — pull the origin photos before agreeing to a settlement number."
**Common misuse:** Treating FVP as meaning "the company pays whatever the customer claims" — it's still subject to the stated deductible and requires the company's standard damage-verification process.

### High-Value Inventory (HVI) form
A supplementary list of items the customer declares above a per-article value threshold, requiring individual documentation beyond the standard inventory sheet.
**In use:** "The jewelry box and the original painting both go on the HVI form, with their own condition notes."
**Common misuse:** Only listing items the crew visually judges as expensive, rather than what the customer has actually declared — the form exists to capture the customer's stated value, not the crew's guess.

### NIOSH Lifting Index (LI)
The ratio of an actual load's weight to the Recommended Weight Limit computed by the Revised NIOSH Lifting Equation for that specific lift's geometry and grip; LI above 1.0 indicates elevated injury risk for that lift.
**In use:** "Scored it out — LI's around 4.8 on that safe, we're not attempting it as a two-person carry."
**Common misuse:** Using a single item's "usual" LI from memory instead of rescoring it for today's actual distance, grip, and path — the same dresser can score very differently going up a narrow stairwell versus across a flat driveway.

### Team lift
A lift performed by two or more people specifically to bring an over-threshold single-person load under a safe effective weight per person, as opposed to simply having help nearby.
**In use:** "This one's a team lift — three on it, not two, given the stairs."
**Common misuse:** Calling any lift with a second person present a "team lift" even when the load and grip haven't actually been redistributed to bring each person's share under a safe limit.
