# Working vocabulary — braided-funding nonprofit program management

Terms a program director uses precisely, with the misuse a generalist commonly makes.

**Braided funding vs. blended funding** — Braided = multiple restricted funding streams supporting one program while each stream's eligible costs and reporting stay separately tracked and traceable. Blended = funds pooled such that individual sources are no longer separately identifiable in reporting (rare, and only allowed where the funder explicitly permits it).
In use: "Case-manager salaries are braided across HUD CoC and the county block grant — we track effort separately even though the client experiences one program."
Misuse: using the terms interchangeably. Treating braided funds as blended (not tracking which dollar paid for what) is exactly what a Single Audit cost-allocation finding looks like.

**Indirect cost rate (de minimis vs. NICRA)** — The percentage applied to a direct-cost base to recover shared organizational overhead (finance, HR, IT, facilities). The 10% de minimis rate (2 CFR 200.414) is available without negotiation; a Negotiated Indirect Cost Rate Agreement (NICRA) is negotiated with a cognizant federal agency and can be higher or lower.
In use: "We're on the 10% de minimis rate; a cost study puts our real rate closer to 13.5%, so every federal dollar under-recovers by 3.5 points."
Misuse: assuming the de minimis rate always favors the organization. For an agency with genuinely low overhead, a NICRA study could set the rate below 10%.

**MTDC (modified total direct costs)** — The base against which an indirect rate is applied; excludes items like capital equipment, the portion of subawards above $25,000, and a few other carve-outs defined in 2 CFR 200.1.
In use: "Indirect is 10% of MTDC, not 10% of the total award — don't apply the rate to the whole grant number."
Misuse: applying the indirect rate to the full award amount instead of the narrower MTDC base, which overstates recoverable indirect and creates a reconciliation problem at closeout.

**Admin cap (HUD CoC / ESG)** — A hard ceiling, separate from the indirect-rate calculation, on how much of a specific federal award can be charged as administrative cost (7% under 24 CFR 578.63 for CoC, a similar cap under ESG regulations).
In use: "Indirect at our rate would be $167K on this grant, but the admin cap only lets us recover $126K — the other $41K has to come from somewhere else."
Misuse: assuming the organization's negotiated or de minimis indirect rate automatically applies in full to every grant; program-specific admin caps can override it downward regardless of the org's actual rate.

**Drawdown (reimbursement basis)** — The act of requesting payment from a grant after costs have already been incurred, as opposed to receiving funds in advance.
In use: "We drew down $145K against February expenses on March 8th — the cash won't land until roughly the 15th."
Misuse: budgeting as if the full grant award is available cash on the date of the award letter, rather than modeling the drawdown lag against the organization's cash reserve or line of credit.

**Match / cost-share** — A funder requirement that the grantee contribute a specified cash or in-kind amount (volunteer hours, donated space, other funding) alongside the grant.
In use: "The county block grant requires a 10% cash match — we're meeting it with the foundation grant's unrestricted portion, logged monthly."
Misuse: treating match as automatically satisfied by "general agency activity" without a documented, contemporaneous log — undocumented match is grounds for the funder to recapture the corresponding grant amount.

**HUD APR (Annual Performance Report)** — The standardized outcome report HUD Continuum of Care grantees submit, drawing on HMIS data, covering metrics like exits to permanent housing and length of stay.
In use: "Our APR shows 82% exits to permanent housing against an 80% contractual floor — comfortable, but two points of headroom isn't much given this quarter's harder intake mix."
Misuse: treating the APR metric as the complete definition of program success rather than one funder-facing lens that can create incentives (e.g., against accepting harder cases) worth tracking separately in CQI data.

**CQI (continuous quality improvement)** — An internal, ongoing review process tracking program quality and client outcomes beyond what any single funder requires, used to catch and correct problems before they show up as a funder-reported miss.
In use: "CQI flagged a rising median time-to-re-engagement before it showed up as a lower APR retention number — we had a corrective plan in place before the funder asked."
Misuse: treating CQI as a compliance form completed after the fact rather than a live monitoring process; a CQI process only earns funder trust if it demonstrably catches problems before the funder does.

**Caseload ratio** — The number of clients per full-time-equivalent direct-service staff member, set against a staffing model appropriate to program acuity (e.g., ~1:20 for intensive PSH case management per CSH guidance).
In use: "We're carrying a 23.7:1 caseload against a 20:1 model — 1.19x, inside tolerance but trending the wrong way."
Misuse: setting the caseload target against budgeted FTE count rather than vacancy-adjusted actual FTE, which builds an unrealistic target into the program design from day one.

**Restricted vs. unrestricted net assets** — Restricted = funds legally designated for a specific purpose, program, or time period by the donor/funder. Unrestricted = funds the board can direct to any organizational purpose.
In use: "The foundation grant is unrestricted, which is the only reason we can use part of it to cover the indirect-recovery gap on the capped grants."
Misuse: treating "unrestricted" as synonymous with "surplus" or "uncommitted" — most nonprofits have unrestricted funds fully allocated to real operating needs before any gap-filling happens.

**Single Audit (Uniform Guidance, 2 CFR 200 Subpart F)** — The compliance audit required of any organization expending $750,000+ in federal awards in a fiscal year, testing internal controls and compliance with each program's specific requirements.
In use: "We cross $750K in federal expenditure this year for the first time — Single Audit applies, and cost allocation is the area most likely to draw a finding."
Misuse: assuming a clean financial-statement audit means Single Audit compliance is also clean; Single Audit tests program-specific compliance requirements a standard financial audit doesn't cover.

**Logic model** — A structured map from inputs and activities to outputs and outcomes for a program, used both to design the program and to justify what is and isn't in scope when a funder proposes adding requirements.
In use: "The logic model doesn't include job-placement outcomes — if the funder wants that reported, it's a scope change with a cost, not a form update."
Misuse: writing the logic model once for the grant proposal and never using it again, instead of as a living reference for scope conversations with funders and the board.
