# Vocabulary

### DFW rate
The percentage of enrolled students who receive a D, F, or Withdrawal in a course — the standard gateway-course health metric.
**In use:** "Calc I posted a 31% DFW this fall against a three-term baseline of 22-24%, and the placement cut score didn't move, so we're auditing the course before touching admissions."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a single undifferentiated number and blamed on "student preparation" without splitting it by cause (failed-final vs. early-withdrawal vs. cumulative-low-grades), which point at completely different fixes.

### Gateway course / gatekeeper course
A high-enrollment, often multi-section course (College Algebra, Calculus I, Discrete Math) that sits at the entry point of a degree pathway and disproportionately determines whether students continue in STEM.
**In use:** "This is a gateway course — a redesign here moves the needle on the whole program's completion numbers, not just one class's grades."
**Common misuse:** Used interchangeably with "hard course," when the defining feature is structural position in the pathway, not difficulty — a gateway course can be redesigned to be rigorous and still pass more capable students.

### Corequisite (support) model
Placing an underprepared student directly into the credit-bearing course with a concurrent support section, instead of a standalone remedial prerequisite course taken first.
**In use:** "She's in the corequisite band on the diagnostic — put her in the credit-bearing section with the Thursday support block, not a semester of prerequisite algebra first."
**Common misuse:** Applied as a blanket policy for every underprepared student regardless of gap size — corequisite support is designed for a moderate, broad gap, not a severe one that genuinely needs a full prerequisite term.

### Placement cut score
The diagnostic-test threshold above which a student is placed into a given course without additional prerequisites or support.
**In use:** "The cut score hasn't been revalidated against actual course outcomes in three years — pull the placement-to-grade scatter before we trust it."
**Common misuse:** Treated as fixed once set, rather than as a predictive instrument that needs periodic revalidation against real pass/fail outcomes as the course itself changes.

### Norming (grading)
A calibration process where multiple graders independently score the same sample of exams before full-scale grading, to align interpretation of the rubric.
**In use:** "Norm on 5 exams per TA before anyone touches the full stack — a rubric that reads the same to me and reads differently to four TAs isn't actually one rubric yet."
**Common misuse:** Skipped because "the rubric is written clearly," conflating a clear rubric document with graders having actually converged on how to apply it.

### Rubric (proof-based)
A scoring instrument that assigns points to specific required logical steps or components of a proof, not to matching a single expected solution's wording.
**In use:** "The rubric gives 2 points for a logically equivalent definition, phrased however the student phrased it — not for matching the lecture's exact wording."
**Common misuse:** Written at the topic level ("partial credit for a reasonable attempt at injectivity") instead of the logical-step level, which is exactly the ambiguity norming is needed to resolve.

### Inquiry-Based Learning (IBL)
A pedagogy where students construct proofs or solve problems with minimal direct instruction, then present and defend their work to the class, with the instructor facilitating rather than lecturing.
**In use:** "This is a Moore-method-style IBL section — students present their proofs at the board and the class critiques them; my job is refereeing the critique, not delivering it."
**Common misuse:** Equated with "no lecture" or "group work," when the defining and evidence-backed feature is structured, individually accountable student work with instructor-facilitated critique — unstructured group time without that accountability structure isn't IBL and doesn't carry the same outcomes.

### CAS (computer algebra system)
Software (Mathematica, Maple, or similar) capable of performing symbolic manipulation, differentiation, integration, and equation-solving.
**In use:** "This is a modeling exam — CAS is allowed, because the skill being tested is setting up the right equation, not solving it by hand."
**Common misuse:** Banned or allowed as a blanket department policy rather than per-assessment, based on whether the specific skill being tested is the manipulation itself or something upstream/downstream of it.

### Recitation section
A smaller, TA-led session attached to a large lecture course, typically for problem-solving practice and questions, distinct from the lecture itself.
**In use:** "Move the proof-writing practice into recitation where the TA can give real-time feedback — lecture time isn't where that skill gets built."
**Common misuse:** Used as unstructured overflow office-hours time rather than a planned session with its own problem set and learning objective.

### Common final
A single shared final exam given across all sections of a multi-section course, graded against one rubric regardless of instructor.
**In use:** "The common final is what certifies students for Calc II — a section that grades softer on it breaks the prerequisite guarantee for the next course."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to guarantee consistency by itself; without a norming pass, a common exam graded by different people still produces different section averages.

### Mastery-based / standards-based grading
An assessment structure where a grade is built from demonstrated competency on discrete, individually-assessed skills or standards, often with reassessment opportunities, rather than a single weighted average of exam scores.
**In use:** "He didn't clear the related-rates standard on the first attempt, but he can reassess it next week without it dragging down the exam average he already earned on the other standards."
**Common misuse:** Confused with simply "allowing retakes" — the defining feature is that the grade is decomposed by discrete skill, not that points can be recovered.

### Productive struggle
The instructional design principle that students learning durably need to work through difficulty on appropriately challenging problems, as distinct from being shown a solution before attempting one.
**In use:** "Don't rescue them the moment they're stuck on part (b) — this problem is designed for the struggle; step in only if they're stuck on the wrong thing entirely."
**Common misuse:** Used to justify giving students no support at all or problems far above their level, rather than problems calibrated to be difficult but solvable with the tools already taught.

### Formative vs. summative assessment
Formative: low- or no-stakes checks used to give feedback and catch gaps while there's still time to act (in-class problems, ungraded quizzes). Summative: high-stakes assessments that certify what was learned (midterms, finals).
**In use:** "We have plenty of summative data from the final, but almost no formative checkpoint before week 10 — that's why struggling students weren't caught in time to get support."
**Common misuse:** Grading every formative check heavily, which converts it into a second summative assessment and defeats its purpose of surfacing gaps without penalty.

### Item bank
A maintained, tagged repository of exam and homework questions with known difficulty and topic coverage, used to assemble common exams or track item performance over time.
**In use:** "Pull the injectivity proof question from last fall's item bank entry — we already have its per-TA norming notes attached."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a static archive rather than a maintained resource — questions that consistently show grading ambiguity or poor discrimination should be flagged or retired, not reused indefinitely.
