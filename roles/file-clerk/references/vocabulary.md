# File Clerk — Vocabulary

### Terminal-digit filing
A numeric filing scheme that indexes by the *last* two digits of an ID first (primary section), then the middle two (secondary), then the first two (tertiary) — distributing new files evenly across 100 physical sections instead of clustering them at the high end of a straight-numeric range.
**In use:** "We switched to terminal-digit filing once we passed 10,000 active cases — straight-numeric was jamming the back of the room every quarter."
**Common misuse:** Assuming "terminal-digit" means files are sorted by their full number in order — it's the opposite; the *last* digits drive the primary sort, which is exactly what makes it counterintuitive to file by without training.

### Straight-numeric filing
Filing by the full ID number in ascending order — simple to explain but concentrates new-file volume at the high end of the range as the ID sequence grows.
**In use:** "Straight-numeric is fine at our volume — we're not going to hit the congestion problem terminal-digit solves for another few years."
**Common misuse:** Defaulting to it for high-volume active-filing rooms out of habit, without checking whether the congestion problem it creates has already started.

### Charge-out
The log entry created when a file physically leaves its storage location — who took it, when, and when it's expected back.
**In use:** "That file's not misfiled, it's charged out to escrow since Tuesday — check the log before you re-search the shelf."
**Common misuse:** Treating charge-out logging as optional for a "quick look" — the file taken without a log entry is the one that becomes untraceable.

### Cross-reference sheet
A placeholder filed under an alternate name or ID pointing to a record's primary filed location, used when a record could reasonably be searched more than one way.
**In use:** "File the cross-reference under her maiden name too — the old case correspondence still uses it."
**Common misuse:** Only creating a cross-reference after a failed search reveals the need, rather than at filing time when the alternate identifier is already known.

### Misfile rate
The percentage of a sampled/audited population of files found shelved in the wrong location relative to the master index.
**In use:** "This quarter's misfile rate came in at 3.6%, above our 2% target — we need to trace the pattern before retraining."
**Common misuse:** Treating the raw rate as the finding, rather than the starting point for root-cause analysis by found-location pattern.

### ROT (redundant, obsolete, trivial)
A classification applied during a records cleanup/digitization project to content that's safe to dispose of — duplicates, superseded versions, or content with no business or legal value.
**In use:** "About a third of this legacy file room classified as ROT during the pre-digitization pass — we're not scanning any of it."
**Common misuse:** Applying "obsolete" to anything old, without checking whether an old record is still within its retention period or under a legal hold.

### Legal hold
An instruction to suspend normal destruction/purge of specific records because litigation is reasonably anticipated or underway, overriding the retention schedule for the affected content regardless of its scheduled purge date.
**In use:** "That series has a hold on it from the Meridian matter — it doesn't purge this cycle no matter what the schedule says."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a hold only affects records already involved in litigation, rather than the full custodial scope legal specifies (which can include related series a records clerk wouldn't guess at without checking the hold notice).

### Purge date
The date on which a record series becomes eligible for destruction under the organization's retention schedule.
**In use:** "The purge date passed in March, but the manifest didn't go out until we confirmed no holds were active on that series."
**Common misuse:** Treating a purge date as a deadline the records must be destroyed by, rather than as the earliest date they're *eligible* — a hold can extend it indefinitely.

### Index verification
The process of checking that a scanned/digitized document's metadata (index fields — date, ID, record type) was captured correctly, either by full review or sampling.
**In use:** "We index-verify 100% of the first batch on any new digitization project — spot-checking from day one hides a bad template."
**Common misuse:** Sampling from the very first batch of a new digitization workflow, which can miss a systemic template error that a full first-batch check would catch immediately.

### Basic filing rules (ARMA)
A standardized set of alphabetic-filing conventions (unit-by-unit indexing order, how to handle prefixes, numbers, and abbreviations) published by ARMA International, used to keep filing consistent across staff and time.
**In use:** "New hires get the ARMA basic filing rules on day one — otherwise everyone develops their own convention for hyphenated names."
**Common misuse:** Assuming filing rules are self-evident or universal — different organizations adopt different conventions, and inconsistency between staff (not the rules themselves) is usually the actual misfile driver.

### Retrieval time
The elapsed time from a file request to the file being in the requester's hands — the primary performance measure for a filing system, as distinct from filing speed.
**In use:** "Filing speed doesn't matter if retrieval time blows up every time someone needs last year's records."
**Common misuse:** Optimizing a filing system for how fast new records go in, without measuring how long it takes to get an existing record back out.

### Out-of-file tracking
The general practice of knowing, at any moment, which records are not in their normal storage location and why (charged out, in transit, pending refiling).
**In use:** "Our out-of-file tracking caught it — that record's been sitting in the scan queue for two days, not lost."
**Common misuse:** Conflating "not on the shelf" with "misfiled" — a well-tracked system distinguishes a record that's accounted for elsewhere from one that's genuinely lost.
