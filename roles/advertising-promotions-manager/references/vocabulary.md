# Media planning & buying working vocabulary

Terms a media buyer uses precisely. Format: definition → how a practitioner says it → common misuse.

**Effective frequency** — The range of exposures per person within a flight where a message is likely to register (below the low end) without triggering fatigue or annoyance (above the high end); commonly 3-7 for many categories, but audience- and category-dependent.
In use: "We're planning to 4x this audience over 21 days — that's inside the effective range for a considered-purchase category like this one."
Misuse: treating "more frequency is always better" as true, or treating the 3-7 range as a universal constant rather than a category-calibrated starting point.

**Reach vs. frequency** — Reach is the number (or %) of unique people exposed at least once; frequency is the average number of times each person was exposed. A media plan trades off between the two at a fixed budget.
In use: "We chose to cap reach at 70% of the addressable audience so we could hit 5x frequency on the core segment instead of spreading thin at 2x."
Misuse: reporting reach and frequency as if maximizing both simultaneously were possible at a fixed budget — they trade off against each other.

**CPM (cost per thousand impressions)** — The price paid per 1,000 ad impressions delivered; the basic unit for comparing media cost across channels.
In use: "CTV is running $65 CPM here versus $18 on paid social — that's why the channel mix leans social for volume."
Misuse: comparing CPM across channels as if it were the only relevant number; a higher-CPM channel can still be the better buy if it reaches an audience the cheaper channel can't.

**GRP (Gross Rating Point)** — Reach % multiplied by average frequency, used mainly in traditional (TV/OOH) media planning as a single delivery-weight number.
In use: "The TV buy is planned at 250 GRPs over four weeks, which nets out to roughly 5x frequency against 50% reach."
Misuse: treating GRPs as directly comparable across markets or demos without checking the underlying reach/frequency assumptions — the same GRP number can represent very different plans.

**Viewability** — The percentage of served impressions that were actually able to be seen (meeting a standard like 50% of pixels in view for 1+ second, per MRC guidelines), as distinct from impressions merely served.
In use: "Delivered impressions were 2.4M, but at 71% viewability the actually-viewable count is closer to 1.7M."
Misuse: treating "impressions served" and "impressions viewable" as the same number when evaluating whether a plan actually delivered.

**Attribution (last-click vs. multi-touch vs. incrementality)** — Last-click credits the final touchpoint before conversion; multi-touch distributes credit across the path; incrementality/holdout testing measures the actual lift a channel caused versus a control group that wasn't exposed.
In use: "Last-click says paid social drove 40% of conversions, but the holdout test shows only about a third of those would not have converted anyway."
Misuse: treating last-click or platform-self-reported attribution as ground truth rather than one directional, over-crediting signal among several.

**Frequency capping** — A setting that limits how many times a single person can be shown a given ad within a time window, used to stay inside the effective frequency range and avoid fatigue.
In use: "We capped this creative at 5 per week per user after CTR started dropping past that point in the last flight."
Misuse: setting a cap once and never revisiting it — the right cap depends on the specific creative and audience and should be checked against current performance data, not assumed from a past campaign.

**Programmatic / DSP (demand-side platform)** — Automated, auction-based media buying across a network of publishers/inventory, as distinct from buying directly from a single platform or publisher.
In use: "We're running the display and CTV portions through the DSP so we can apply the same frequency cap and audience segment across both."
Misuse: assuming programmatic buying is inherently cheaper or more efficient than direct buys — it depends on the audience, inventory quality, and fees layered into the DSP.

**Flight** — The scheduled start and end dates a campaign runs, used as the unit for planning frequency, budget pacing, and measurement windows.
In use: "The flight is 21 days to align with the intro-price window closing."
Misuse: treating the flight dates as flexible after the plan is built around them — frequency and budget pacing calculations assume the flight length; extending it without re-planning changes the actual delivered frequency.

**Brief** — The document specifying audience, objective, single core message, desired action, and mandatory constraints (brand, legal) that creative execution is built against.
In use: "The brief says one audience, one message: this is a renewal reminder, not a chance to also introduce the new tier."
Misuse: using the brief to specify the creative execution itself (exact visuals, copy, tone) rather than the audience/message/action it needs to hit — over-specifying the "how" undermines the point of briefing a creative team.

**Media mix modeling (MMM)** — A statistical approach using historical spend and outcome data (rather than user-level tracking) to estimate each channel's contribution to results, often used alongside or instead of platform attribution for higher-level budget allocation.
In use: "The MMM refresh this quarter shows CTV's contribution is being undercounted by last-click attribution by roughly 2x."
Misuse: treating MMM output as precise to the dollar rather than a directional, model-based estimate with its own assumptions and confidence range.

**Dayparting** — Scheduling ad delivery to specific times of day or days of week based on when the target audience is most likely to be attentive or convert.
In use: "We're dayparting the streaming buy to evenings and weekends, which is when this audience's app usage actually peaks."
Misuse: applying a generic dayparting assumption (e.g., "evenings are always best") without checking the specific audience's actual behavior data.

**Makegood** — Compensation a media vendor provides (extra impressions, spend credit) when they under-deliver against the guaranteed impressions or GRPs in a contract.
In use: "The publisher under-delivered by 8% on guaranteed impressions, so we're owed a makegood in the next flight."
Misuse: not tracking guaranteed-vs-delivered numbers closely enough to know a makegood is owed — vendors don't always proactively surface under-delivery.

**Share of voice (SOV)** — A brand's advertising presence (spend, impressions, or GRPs) in a category relative to total category advertising, used as a competitive-position signal.
In use: "Our SOV dropped to 18% this quarter as a competitor doubled their CTV spend — that's worth flagging before the next planning cycle."
Misuse: treating SOV as a direct proxy for market share; it's a leading indicator of competitive pressure, not a guaranteed outcome.
