# Red Flags

Smell tests for sampling, calibration, and lab-result work. Format per flag: what it usually means, the first question, the check to run.

## Soil test P or K jumps more than 30% from last cycle with no field-history change

- **Usually means:** a sampling pattern or depth inconsistency between cycles (different grid points, different core depth, or a lab method change — e.g., Mehlich-3 vs Bray-1), not a true fertility swing.
- **First question:** "Were this year's grid points, core depth, and lab method identical to last cycle's?"
- **Data to pull:** the GPS log or field map from both sampling events, and the lab's method code on both reports.

## Nozzle-to-nozzle catch CV over 10–15% during a 1/128-acre calibration check

- **Usually means:** one or more worn, partially clogged, or mismatched nozzle tips — the boom is not delivering uniformly across its width even if the average looks fine.
- **First question:** "Which specific nozzle(s) are the outliers, and are they visibly worn or a different tip size than the rest?"
- **Data to pull:** the individual per-nozzle catch values, not just the boom average; nozzle tip size/color code at each outlier position.

## All nozzles agree with each other but the mean catch is off the target GPA

- **Usually means:** a cause shared by the whole system — pressure regulator setting, a clogged master or nozzle-body strainer, pump wear, or a ground-speed sensor mismatch — not an individual nozzle problem.
- **First question:** "Is the boom pressure gauge reading upstream or downstream of a possible restriction, and does GPS-measured ground speed match the rate controller's displayed speed?"
- **Data to pull:** GPS speed log for the field pass, pressure gauge placement in the plumbing diagram, and strainer mesh condition (master and individual nozzle-body).

## A pest count sits just under the published economic threshold for two or more consecutive scouting visits

- **Usually means:** the population is on a growth trajectory that will cross the threshold before the next scheduled weekly visit, especially in warm/humid stretches that favor rapid reproduction.
- **First question:** "What's the week-over-week growth rate of the count, and does it project past threshold before the next scheduled visit?"
- **Data to pull:** the last 3 weeks of counts at the same sampling points, plus the current GDD accumulation against the pest's known development model.

## Tissue test shows a nutrient as sufficient while the plant shows visible deficiency symptoms

- **Usually means:** a sample handling or collection error — wrong leaf position sampled, sample sat too long before shipping and degraded, or the sample was mislabeled with another field's ID — more often than an actual lab error.
- **First question:** "Was the correct leaf position sampled for this crop's growth stage, and does the sample ID on the lab report match the field it came from?"
- **Data to pull:** the field collection log (leaf position, plant count, date/time bagged) and the lab intake log's sample ID cross-reference.

## Grain or soil moisture meter reads more than 0.5 percentage points off a known check sample

- **Usually means:** the meter has drifted out of calibration, most commonly from temperature swings between storage and field use or from a dirty sensor cell.
- **First question:** "When was this unit last checked against a certified reference sample, and under what temperature conditions?"
- **Data to pull:** the calibration log for the unit and the temperature at time of the failed check.

## Soil moisture sensor reading plateaus or barely moves during an active irrigation event

- **Usually means:** poor sensor-to-soil contact (an air gap from installation or soil settling), a sensor installed above the effective root zone, or channeling of water past the sensor along the installation slot.
- **First question:** "Did the reading move at all during or immediately after the irrigation set, and is the sensor at the depth specified for this crop's root zone?"
- **Data to pull:** the sensor's full time-series through the irrigation event, and the as-installed depth log.

## Adjacent grid points in the same field show soil pH differing by more than 0.5–1.0 units with no visible field-history reason

- **Usually means:** a genuine management-zone boundary (old field line, past liming pattern) worth mapping — or a sample mix-up between adjacent points.
- **First question:** "Is there a documented history reason for a boundary at exactly this point (old fence line, field merge, differential lime application)?"
- **Data to pull:** historical aerial imagery or field-merge records for the location; the field log's point-to-point sequence to rule out a bagging/labeling swap.

## A worker or scout re-enters a treated field before the label's restricted-entry interval (REI) has elapsed

- **Usually means:** the REI wasn't posted, communicated, or tracked at the field level — a compliance and safety failure, not a minor scheduling slip.
- **First question:** "What was the application date/time and label REI for this product, and is field-entry signage current and correct?"
- **Data to pull:** the pesticide application record (product, date/time, REI) and the field posting log required under the EPA Worker Protection Standard (40 CFR Part 170).

## A germination test result differs from the seed lot tag by more than 5 percentage points

- **Usually means:** sampling variance from a single small test rather than a genuinely mislabeled or degraded lot — especially likely on a single 400-seed test.
- **First question:** "Has this result been rerun on a fresh sample drawn from a different portion of the same lot before treating the tag as wrong?"
- **Data to pull:** the original test's replicate-level results (not just the averaged percentage) and a resample from a different container/location within the lot.
