# Vocabulary

Working vocabulary generalists reliably misuse. Format per term: definition, practitioner usage, common misuse.

## Economic threshold vs. economic injury level

- **Definition:** the economic injury level (EIL) is the pest density at which control cost exactly equals the damage prevented — the break-even point. The economic threshold (ET) is set below the EIL, at the density that triggers action in time for the control measure to actually take effect before damage reaches the EIL.
- **Practitioner usage:** "We flag at the ET, not the EIL, because if we wait for the EIL the population's already done the damage by the time the spray goes on."
- **Common misuse:** treating the two as interchangeable, or treating either as a fixed number rather than one that shifts with commodity price, control cost, and crop growth stage.

## Composite sample vs. grab sample

- **Definition:** a composite sample combines multiple cores or subsamples from a defined pattern into one representative sample; a grab sample is a single point taken and treated as representative of a larger area.
- **Practitioner usage:** "That's a grab sample from the truck-accessible corner, not a composite — it's not going into the grid average."
- **Common misuse:** calling a handful of convenience-accessible cores a "composite" when the pattern skipped the harder-to-reach parts of the field, quietly biasing the average.

## Management allowable depletion (MAD) vs. field capacity vs. wilting point

- **Definition:** field capacity is the soil water content after free drainage stops; wilting point is the water content below which most crops can no longer extract water; MAD is the fraction of the water available between those two points that's allowed to deplete before irrigation is triggered.
- **Practitioner usage:** "We're running a 35% MAD during pollination instead of the usual 50% — yield cost of stress is too high this stage to wait."
- **Common misuse:** treating field capacity as "as wet as the soil can get" (it's post-drainage, not saturation) or applying one MAD value to every crop stage instead of tightening it during yield-critical windows.

## Calibration vs. verification

- **Definition:** calibration adjusts an instrument to match a known standard; verification checks whether it's still reading correctly without adjusting it.
- **Practitioner usage:** "That was a verification check, not a calibration — it read within tolerance so nothing was adjusted."
- **Common misuse:** using "calibrated" to describe any check performed, even ones where the reading was fine and nothing was changed — which erases the useful information that the unit needed no correction.

## Chain of custody

- **Definition:** the documented, unbroken record of who collected a sample, when, how, and what happened to it until it reached the lab.
- **Practitioner usage:** "The tissue result doesn't match the symptom — first thing I checked was chain of custody, and sure enough the field IDs got swapped at bagging."
- **Common misuse:** treating it as paperwork overhead rather than the first diagnostic step when a result looks wrong.

## Growing degree days (GDD) / degree-day model

- **Definition:** an accumulated-heat unit used to predict insect or crop developmental stage, calculated from daily high/low temperatures against a species-specific base threshold.
- **Practitioner usage:** "We're at 450 GDD on the corn borer model — that puts first-generation moth flight right around now regardless of the calendar date."
- **Common misuse:** scouting or spraying on a fixed calendar date ("we always treat around June 1") in a year that ran unusually warm or cool, missing the pest's actual development window by a week or more.

## Restricted-entry interval (REI) vs. pre-harvest interval (PHI)

- **Definition:** REI is the time after a pesticide application during which entry to the treated area is restricted without PPE; PHI is the minimum time between the last application and harvest.
- **Practitioner usage:** "REI on this product is 24 hours — nobody scouts that block until tomorrow afternoon regardless of how urgent the pest count looks."
- **Common misuse:** conflating the two, or assuming a short PHI means a short REI (they're set independently and can differ by days).

## Coefficient of variation (CV), nozzle-to-nozzle

- **Definition:** the standard deviation of catch values across nozzles divided by their mean, expressed as a percentage — a measure of how uniformly the boom is delivering, independent of whether the average rate itself is correct.
- **Practitioner usage:** "CV's 4%, so the nozzles agree with each other — the shortfall is somewhere upstream of the nozzles, not at them."
- **Common misuse:** treating a low CV as proof the sprayer is calibrated correctly, when it only proves the nozzles agree with each other — the whole set can be uniformly wrong.

## Sufficiency range vs. critical value (tissue/soil testing)

- **Definition:** the sufficiency range is the band of nutrient concentration associated with no yield-limiting deficiency; the critical value is the specific concentration below which yield loss becomes likely for that crop and stage.
- **Practitioner usage:** "It's below the sufficiency range but still above the critical value for this stage — flag it, but it's not yet an emergency application."
- **Common misuse:** treating "below sufficiency" and "below critical" as the same urgency level, when the gap between them is exactly the room for a planned in-season correction instead of a rush application.

## Grid sampling vs. zone sampling

- **Definition:** grid sampling collects composite samples at fixed-interval points regardless of field features; zone sampling delineates management zones first (from yield maps, soil type, or elevation) and samples within each zone.
- **Practitioner usage:** "This field's soil types are too mixed for a flat grid to make sense — we're zone sampling off last year's yield map instead."
- **Common misuse:** defaulting to grid sampling everywhere out of habit, even on fields where a zone approach delineated from existing yield or soil data would need fewer samples and better represent the field's real variability.

## Tetrazolium (TZ) test vs. standard germination test

- **Definition:** the standard germination test grows seeds out under controlled conditions and counts normal seedlings; the TZ test is a biochemical stain that identifies living tissue without growing the seed out, distinguishing a dead seed from a dormant-but-viable one.
- **Practitioner usage:** "Germ came back low on this lot, but the TZ shows most of the non-germinators are dormant, not dead — the lot's viable, it just needs a stratification treatment before it'll germinate on test."
- **Common misuse:** reporting TZ viability and germination percentage as if they measure the same thing and are directly comparable — they answer different questions and a low germ score with a high TZ score is meaningful, not contradictory.

## Scouting vs. monitoring vs. survey

- **Definition:** scouting is field-level, in-season, action-oriented pest/crop assessment tied to a treatment decision; monitoring is systematic tracking over time (often with traps) to establish population trends; a survey is a broader, often one-time or seasonal assessment of pest presence/distribution across a region.
- **Practitioner usage:** "The trap counts are monitoring data for the regional flight model — that's separate from this week's in-field scouting numbers that feed the spray decision."
- **Common misuse:** using "scouting" for trend-tracking trap data that isn't tied to an immediate field decision, which blurs which dataset is actually driving this week's action.
