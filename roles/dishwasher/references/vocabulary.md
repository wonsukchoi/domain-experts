# Vocabulary

Terms generalists conflate that a steward keeps sharply separate. Each: definition, a sentence a practitioner would actually say, and the common misuse.

## Wash vs. rinse vs. sanitize

**Wash** removes visible soil and grease with detergent and hot water. **Rinse** removes wash-water and detergent residue with clean water. **Sanitize** reduces pathogens on an already-clean surface to a safe level, via heat or chemical, for a specified concentration and contact time. All three are required steps, in that order, not synonyms for "got the plate clean."

*In use:* "It's washed and rinsed, but it hasn't hit the sanitize step yet — it's not food-safe to plate on."

*Common misuse:* saying "it's washed" to mean "it's safe to use" — a washed plate with detergent residue and no sanitize step is neither clean of chemical nor pathogen-safe.

## Contact time

The minimum duration a sanitizer (chemical or hot water) must remain in contact with a surface at the required concentration or temperature to achieve the intended pathogen reduction — e.g., 7 seconds for chlorine, 30 seconds for quaternary ammonium and iodine, 30 seconds at 160°F plate-surface temp for hot water.

*In use:* "The strip read fine, but he pulled it out after five seconds — that's not thirty, so it's not sanitized."

*Common misuse:* treating "the item touched the sanitizer" as sufficient regardless of duration; concentration and time are both required, and a correct concentration with insufficient contact time still fails.

## Parts per million (ppm)

The unit sanitizer concentration is measured and specified in — e.g., quat at 200–400ppm, chlorine at 50–100ppm. Only a test strip (or in-line meter) reads this; nothing about smell, color, or "how strong it feels" substitutes for it.

*In use:* "Strip read 150ppm on the quat — that's under the 200 minimum, redose before the next load."

*Common misuse:* eyeballing concentration by smell or how much foam is present — quat solutions in particular can smell strong while under-concentrated.

## Air-dry vs. towel-dry

**Air-drying** is the required final step after sanitizing — a towel re-introduces contact with a surface (the cloth) that isn't itself sanitized, undoing the prior step. Food Code requires letting sanitized ware air-dry before stacking or storing.

*In use:* "Leave those on the rack, don't towel them — they just came off sanitize."

*Common misuse:* towel-drying to speed up service during a rush, which recontaminates ware that was correctly sanitized seconds earlier.

## Detergent vs. sanitizer

**Detergent** breaks down grease and food soil so it can be rinsed away — it has no required pathogen-kill standard. **Sanitizer** is a separate, EPA-registered chemical (or heat) applied after washing and rinsing, at a specified concentration and contact time, to reduce pathogens.

*In use:* "Dish soap gets it clean, but it's not doing anything to sanitize it — that's a separate step with its own chemical."

*Common misuse:* assuming a strong-smelling detergent or extra scrubbing substitutes for the sanitize step; grease removal and pathogen reduction are unrelated processes.

## High-temp vs. low-temp (chemical) dish machine

**High-temp machines** sanitize with hot water alone (final rinse ≥165–180°F depending on machine type) and are verified with heat-sensitive tape or a max-registering thermometer. **Low-temp (chemical) machines** sanitize with a chemical injected during the rinse cycle at a lower water temperature, verified with a test strip, not tape.

*In use:* "This is a low-temp unit — heat tape won't tell you anything here, pull a strip off the final rinse."

*Common misuse:* applying the wrong verification method to the wrong machine type — heat tape on a chemical machine, or assuming a chemical machine's water temp alone proves sanitization.

## Three-compartment sink

The manual wash/rinse/sanitize setup required when a dish machine is absent, down, or when an item is too large for the machine — three separate basins, each performing one of the three required steps, never collapsed into fewer basins.

*In use:* "Pots go to the three-comp tonight, the machine's on plates only."

*Common misuse:* running wash and rinse in the same basin to save time, which carries detergent residue into the sanitize step and can neutralize or dilute the sanitizer.

## Booster heater

A dedicated heating unit that raises the dish machine's final-rinse water above the building's standard hot-water temperature (typically 110–120°F) up to the 165–180°F+ required for sanitizing — a separate unit from the main water heater supplying the wash tank.

*In use:* "Wash water's fine at 150, it's the booster that's underperforming — that's why final rinse won't hold 165."

*Common misuse:* assuming a building's general hot-water system covers the sanitizing rinse requirement; a working main water heater does not mean the booster is functioning.

## Par level (clean ware)

The target stock of clean, ready-to-use plates, glassware, and flatware the floor needs on hand at any moment — distinct from food-inventory par, and often untracked even in kitchens that track food par carefully.

*In use:* "We're at half par on ten-inch plates going into the rush — pull ahead now instead of waiting for the floor to call it in."

*Common misuse:* tracking pace ("we're keeping up on average") instead of standing stock — a pit that processes fast enough on average can still run the floor dry during a burst if clean-ware par was never set as a number to hold.

## Log-reduction

The microbiological measure of how much a sanitizing step reduces pathogen counts (e.g., a "5-log reduction" means a 100,000-fold decrease) — the basis for why concentration and time are both specified rather than either alone.

*In use:* "The required log-reduction is why both the ppm and the thirty seconds matter — hit one without the other and you're not getting the reduction the standard assumes."

*Common misuse:* treating any exposure to sanitizer, at any concentration or duration, as equivalent to "sanitized" — the standard specifies a reduction level, not a pass/fail touch.
