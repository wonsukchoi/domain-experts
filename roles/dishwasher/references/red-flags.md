# Red flags

Smell tests a steward or kitchen manager catches by walking the pit for two minutes. Format for each: what it usually means, the first question to ask, and the data to pull before assuming the ware is safe.

## 1. Sanitizer test strip reads below spec (chlorine <50ppm, quat <200ppm, iodine <12.5ppm)

**Usually means:** the reservoir was topped off with water during the rush without a matching re-dose, or the chemical drum ran dry and the dispenser is pumping air. Concentration decays fastest under exactly the load that most needs it.

**First question:** "When was this sink last refilled, and was it re-dosed and re-tested at that time, or just topped off?"

**Data to pull:** the strip reading itself, the time of last full refill, dosing-pump calibration log if auto-dispensed, and how many loads have gone through since the last confirmed-in-spec reading.

## 2. High-temp machine final rinse gauge reads below 180°F (2-tank) or 165°F (single-temp stationary rack)

**Usually means:** a booster heater failing or undersized for the machine's demand, a scaled-up heating element, or incoming water pressure too low to hold rinse temp under back-to-back cycles.

**First question:** "Is this a one-cycle dip that recovers, or has it been holding low for the last several racks?"

**Data to pull:** gauge readings over the last hour if logged, heat-sensitive tape results on the most recent sample rack, maintenance history on the booster heater, incoming water pressure reading.

## 3. Heat-sensitive verification tape not changing color, or max-registering thermometer reads below 160°F

**Usually means:** the plate surface isn't reaching sanitizing temperature for the required contact time even if the tank gauges read fine — spray pattern blockage, overloaded rack, or a worn wash arm are common causes.

**First question:** "Was this rack loaded to standard density, or was it packed to squeeze in extra items during the rush?"

**Data to pull:** the specific rack's load photo or description if available, spray-arm condition, tank temp at the same moment, whether this is a one-off or a repeat failure.

## 4. Bus tubs stacked more than two deep at the bus station, or a cook repeatedly asking for a pan that isn't back yet

**Usually means:** the pit's queue has backed up past its holding capacity — either total volume exceeds combined machine+sink throughput for this cover count, or one queue (usually pots) has been given equal priority with the other and is starving it.

**First question:** "Is the machine or the sink the constrained resource right now, and what's routed to each?"

**Data to pull:** current routing split (what's going to machine vs. sink), covers/hour for the current rush window, staffing on the pit right now versus the pre-shift plan.

## 5. "It smells like chemical" or "the water looks clear" used in place of a test-strip reading

**Usually means:** a shortcut under time pressure — smell and clarity don't correlate reliably with concentration, especially for quat solutions, which can smell strong while under-concentrated after dilution with rinse carryover.

**First question:** "When's the last time this sink was actually strip-tested, not just checked by eye or nose?"

**Data to pull:** the most recent logged strip reading and its timestamp; if none exists this shift, that's the finding.

## 6. Breakage running roughly double the trailing weekly baseline

**Usually means:** rushed racking, bus tubs overfilled past a safe carry weight, or new/untrained staff handling ware without the pattern-sorting step — rarely "bad luck" at that magnitude.

**First question:** "Is this concentrated in one shift or one person, or spread evenly across the week?"

**Data to pull:** breakage log by shift and by staff member if tracked, bus-tub fill practice observed during the spike period, any recent staffing change on the pit.

## 7. Three-compartment sink water not changed for several hours during a long rush

**Usually means:** even a correctly dosed sanitize sink loses effective concentration as organic soil load builds in sinks 1 and 2 and carries over; a sink dosed once at 5pm and never refreshed by 9pm is not still delivering the same sanitization it was at 5pm.

**First question:** "How many loads or how many hours has this water been in service since the last full change?"

**Data to pull:** time of last full sink change, current strip reading versus the reading at setup, visible soil load in sinks 1 and 2.

## 8. Chemical dispenser not recalibrated after a new drum/concentrate swap

**Usually means:** dosing pumps are calibrated to a specific product's concentration; swapping to a different brand or dilution strength without recalibrating produces either an under-dosed or badly over-dosed sink, and an over-dosed sink is itself a chemical-exposure and rinse-residue problem.

**First question:** "Was the dispenser recalibrated when this drum was installed, or just refilled and assumed the same?"

**Data to pull:** product label/concentration on the current drum versus the one it replaced, dispenser output setting, a fresh strip reading taken right after the swap.

## 9. Oversized pots or sheet pans being run through the dish machine during a rush

**Usually means:** untrained or temp staff defaulting to arrival-order racking instead of routing rule, or genuine sink-capacity shortage forcing a workaround — either way, each oversized item run through the machine displaces a full rack's worth of plate capacity.

**First question:** "What's the routing rule tonight, and does everyone on the pit know it?"

**Data to pull:** the night's stated routing split, how many oversized items have gone through the machine this hour, current plate-capacity shortfall if the floor has called one in.
