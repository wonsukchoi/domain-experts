# School counseling working vocabulary

Terms a school counselor uses precisely and a generalist blurs together. Format: definition → how a practitioner says it → common misuse.

**Limited confidentiality** — The school counseling confidentiality framework where most disclosures stay private, but specific circumstances (harm to self/others, abuse, certain school policies) require breaking confidentiality to inform parents, administrators, or authorities — distinct from full clinical therapist-client privilege.
In use: "I told her upfront this is limited confidentiality, not like seeing a private therapist — that's why I have to loop her parents in on this specific concern."
Misuse: assuming school counseling confidentiality works like clinical privilege, where nearly everything stays fully private — the limits are broader and need to be disclosed to the student explicitly, not discovered when invoked.

**ASCA National Model** — The American School Counselor Association's framework organizing school counseling around three domains (academic, career, social/emotional) with recommended time allocations (majority to direct/indirect student services) and a recommended student-to-counselor ratio (250:1).
In use: "The ASCA model's 80% direct-service target is what I'm using to push back on being assigned full-time testing coordination."
Misuse: treating the ASCA model as a vague professional ideal rather than a specific, citable framework with concrete ratios and time-allocation targets useful in negotiating role scope with administration.

**Mandated reporting threshold** — The specific legal standard (commonly "reasonable suspicion" of abuse or neglect, defined by state statute) that triggers a legal obligation to report to child protective services or law enforcement, removing professional discretion once met.
In use: "This meets the reasonable suspicion threshold — I'm not weighing whether reporting will hurt my relationship with the family, the law doesn't leave that as a choice once the threshold's met."
Misuse: treating mandated reporting as a judgment call to be weighed against relationship or trust considerations — once the legal threshold is met, it's not discretionary.

**MTSS / RTI (Multi-Tiered System of Supports / Response to Intervention)** — A framework organizing interventions into tiers by intensity: universal (all students), targeted (at-risk groups), and intensive (highest-need individuals), used to match support level to actual need.
In use: "This student's presentation is Tier 2, not Tier 3 — targeted small-group support, not the full individual crisis-level protocol."
Misuse: applying a uniform response level to every student regardless of actual need severity, rather than matching intervention intensity to the tier the presentation actually indicates.

**Direct vs. indirect student services** — Direct services are face-to-face counselor-student interactions (individual/group counseling, classroom guidance lessons); indirect services are counselor activities on behalf of students without direct contact (consultation with teachers, referrals, data analysis). Both count toward the ASCA-recommended time allocation, distinct from non-counseling administrative duties.
In use: "Consulting with her teachers about accommodations counts as indirect service — it's still core counseling work, unlike lunch duty."
Misuse: assuming only face-to-face counseling counts as "real" counseling time, undercounting legitimate indirect service work, or conversely counting non-counseling administrative duties (testing coordination, discipline supervision) as if they were indirect services.

**Adjustment reaction (situational distress)** — A presentation of emotional/behavioral symptoms directly tied to a specific, identifiable life event or transition (divorce, relocation, loss), typically time-limited and distinct from a standalone diagnosable psychiatric condition.
In use: "This reads as an adjustment reaction to the divorce, not a standalone depressive disorder — that changes whether school-based support is appropriate or an outside referral is needed now."
Misuse: assuming any emotional distress following a life event is automatically "just an adjustment reaction" without screening for escalation — situational distress can still develop into or reveal a condition requiring outside clinical care, which ongoing monitoring is meant to catch.

**Four-year course plan** — An individualized academic planning document mapping a student's course sequence across their high school years toward specific college/career goals, distinct from single-semester scheduling.
In use: "The four-year plan shows she needs to stay in the advanced math track to keep her engineering-program options open — that's the context for this semester's placement decision."
Misuse: treating course scheduling as a semester-by-semester convenience decision without reference to the multi-year plan and its downstream implications for college/career readiness.

**Scope of practice (school counseling)** — The boundary of appropriate professional activity for a school counselor: short-term, solution-focused, school-based academic/career/social-emotional support, distinct from the scope of a licensed clinical mental health provider.
In use: "Ongoing weekly individual therapy for a diagnosable condition is outside our scope of practice — that's a referral, not something to take on internally."
Misuse: treating school counseling scope as flexible based on availability or student need alone — providing services beyond scope isn't more caring, it's both a professional overreach and often a worse outcome for the student than appropriate referral.

**Caseload ratio** — The number of students assigned to a single school counselor, benchmarked against the ASCA-recommended 250:1 ratio, used as a concrete metric in advocating for adequate staffing or duty reassignment.
In use: "At 425:1, I'm well above the recommended ratio — that's the number I'm bringing to this conversation about reassigning testing duties."
Misuse: describing caseload only in general terms ("I have too many students") rather than citing the specific ratio against the recognized benchmark, which carries less weight in an administrative conversation.

**Solution-focused brief counseling** — A short-term counseling approach emphasizing a student's existing strengths and concrete, achievable next steps rather than extended exploration of underlying causes, well-suited to the school counselor's scope and time constraints.
In use: "We're using a solution-focused approach here — a few sessions on concrete coping strategies for the transition, not open-ended exploratory therapy."
Misuse: assuming brief, solution-focused counseling means superficial or lesser care — it's a distinct, appropriately scoped clinical approach for the school setting, not an inferior substitute for "real" therapy.
