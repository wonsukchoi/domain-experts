# Red flags — signals a school counselor notices instantly

Smell tests with thresholds. Any one alone can be a manageable case; two or more together on the same student or role-scope pattern warrants immediate escalation or reassessment.

### A student discloses ongoing symptoms consistent with a diagnosable condition, and the plan is continued school-only weekly sessions with no outside referral
- **Usually means:** the school counselor may be operating outside appropriate scope, providing what amounts to unlicensed ongoing therapy rather than triage and referral to clinical care.
- **First question:** "Does this presentation meet the threshold for outside clinical referral per the scope-triage worksheet, and if so, why hasn't that referral been made?"
- **Data to pull:** scope-triage worksheet (see `references/playbook.md` §2), symptom duration/severity, current session plan.

### A counseling relationship began without the student being told the limits of confidentiality
- **Usually means:** a later required disclosure (safety risk, abuse) will land as a surprise betrayal rather than an expected part of a framework the student already understood.
- **First question:** "Was the limited confidentiality disclosure made explicitly before substantive conversation began, or only referenced after the fact?"
- **Data to pull:** intake/first-session documentation, limited confidentiality disclosure script (see `references/playbook.md` §1).

### A disclosure meets the mandated reporting threshold, and the report is delayed or reconsidered due to relationship/rapport concerns
- **Usually means:** the counselor may be treating a legal obligation as a discretionary judgment call, which the reasonable-suspicion threshold doesn't allow once met.
- **First question:** "Does this disclosure meet the specific state statute's reasonable-suspicion threshold, independent of how it might affect the relationship with the student or family?"
- **Data to pull:** disclosure content, applicable state mandated reporting statute and threshold.

### A student's course/track placement is being decided based on schedule availability rather than an individualized trajectory assessment
- **Usually means:** a decision with real multi-year academic/college consequences may be made as administrative convenience rather than deliberate guidance, disproportionately affecting students without another source of that advice.
- **First question:** "What's the specific academic/career trajectory reasoning behind this placement, separate from which sections currently have open seats?"
- **Data to pull:** student's academic history and stated goals, four-year course plan, comparable-student placement patterns.

### Non-counseling administrative duties are consuming a majority of the counselor's tracked time
- **Usually means:** direct and indirect student services — the actual counseling mission — are being crowded out, and informal complaint alone is unlikely to change the assignment without data-backed advocacy.
- **First question:** "What does the actual time-use breakdown show against the ASCA National Model's 80% direct/indirect service target?"
- **Data to pull:** caseload/time-use case worksheet (see `references/playbook.md` §3), current duty assignment list.

### A Tier 1 (universal) response is being applied to a student whose presentation indicates Tier 3 (intensive) need, or vice versa
- **Usually means:** intervention intensity is mismatched to actual need, either under-serving a high-need student or over-consuming limited capacity on a lower-need situation.
- **First question:** "What does the actual severity/risk assessment indicate about which tier this student needs, versus what's currently being provided?"
- **Data to pull:** tiered response matching table (see `references/playbook.md` §4), specific presenting symptoms and risk indicators.

### A significant academic decline (e.g., a large GPA drop within one grading period) is attributed only to "the student not trying" without exploring a situational cause
- **Usually means:** an underlying, often addressable situational factor (family transition, housing instability, undiagnosed learning difficulty) may be driving the decline and going unaddressed if the explanation stops at a motivation/effort framing.
- **First question:** "Has a triage conversation explored what changed in the student's life around the time the decline started, beyond assuming a motivation issue?"
- **Data to pull:** grade timeline, any recent family/life-event context, teacher observation patterns.

### A parent is informed of a disclosure the student made without a clear explanation of why the limited confidentiality threshold applied
- **Usually means:** the disclosure to the parent may be legally/ethically appropriate but handled in a way that damages the student's trust unnecessarily if the reasoning isn't communicated clearly.
- **First question:** "Has the student been told what's being shared with the parent and why, consistent with the limited confidentiality framework already established?"
- **Data to pull:** the specific disclosure and its mandated/policy basis, communication plan with the student about the parent notification.
