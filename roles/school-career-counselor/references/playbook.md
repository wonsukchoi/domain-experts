# Confidentiality, triage, and role-defense playbook

Filled worksheets for the three recurring school counselor tasks: limited confidentiality disclosure, scope-triage decision, and caseload/time-use case-building.

## 1. Limited confidentiality disclosure script

```
"Before we talk, I want you to know how this works: what you share with me is private,
and I won't tell your parents/teachers most things you tell me. But there are a few things
I do have to share with someone else if they come up: if I think you might hurt yourself
or someone else, if I hear about abuse or someone not being safe at home, and [any
district-specific policy, e.g. certain drug/weapon disclosures]. Outside of those, this
is between us. Does that make sense?"
```

Rule: state this before substantive disclosure begins, not reactively once something requiring disclosure has already come up — introducing the limits after the fact, even correctly, damages trust in a way stating it upfront doesn't.

## 2. Scope-triage worksheet

| Presenting issue | School-based support appropriate? | Outside referral needed? |
|---|---|---|
| Situational stress (family transition, peer conflict, test anxiety) | Yes — brief, solution-focused | Only if screening reveals escalation beyond situational |
| Academic/course planning concern | Yes — core counselor function | N/A |
| Grief/loss (recent, without complicating factors) | Yes — school-based grief support, often short-term | Consider referral if prolonged/complicated grief indicators emerge |
| Symptoms consistent with a diagnosable condition (persistent depressive/anxiety symptoms beyond situational, meeting duration/severity criteria) | Screening and brief support during triage period | Yes — refer to outside clinical provider for diagnosis/treatment |
| Suicidal ideation or self-harm disclosure | Immediate safety assessment/protocol | Yes — safety plan, parent notification, outside crisis/clinical coordination |
| Abuse/neglect disclosure | Mandated reporting immediately | Yes — per legal requirement, not counselor discretion |
| Substance use concern | Brief screening, psychoeducation | Refer to appropriate substance use treatment resource if use pattern indicates need |

Rule: the triage question is never "does this student need support" (almost always yes) — it's "does this need brief school-based support, outside clinical referral, or an immediate mandated response," and the answer determines the counselor's next action, not whether they're compassionate about the concern.

## 3. Caseload/time-use case for administration

```
Current caseload: <number> students (ASCA-recommended ratio: 250:1)
Time-use breakdown (tracked over [period]):
  Direct/indirect student services: <hours/week, % of total>
  Non-counseling administrative duties: <hours/week, % of total — specify: testing
    coordination, lunch/hall duty, data entry, discipline supervision, etc.>
  ASCA National Model target: 80% direct/indirect student services

Specific ask: <e.g. "reassign testing coordination to [other staff role], per ASCA
  National Model recommendation that this falls outside core counseling functions">
Data supporting the ask: <caseload ratio comparison, time-use tracking, any documented
  impact on student wait times/service delivery>
```

Rule: bring the ratio and time-use data, not a general statement of being overwhelmed — administrators respond to a specific, data-backed ask tied to a recognized framework (ASCA National Model) far more reliably than to informal complaint.

## 4. Tiered response matching (MTSS/RTI)

| Tier | Scope | Example intervention |
|---|---|---|
| Universal (Tier 1) | All students | Classroom guidance curriculum lessons, schoolwide college/career readiness programming |
| Targeted (Tier 2) | At-risk groups/individuals | Small-group counseling (e.g. students of divorce group), periodic individual check-ins |
| Intensive (Tier 3) | Highest-need individuals | Frequent individual support, coordinated safety planning, close coordination with outside providers |

Rule: match the tier to the actual documented need level — assigning Tier 3 intensity to a Tier 2 situation depletes limited capacity from higher-need cases; assigning Tier 1 response to a Tier 3 situation under-serves a student who needs more.
