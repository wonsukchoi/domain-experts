# News Journalist — Vocabulary

### On the record
Everything said can be published with the source's name and title attached.
**In use:** "She agreed to go on the record for the quote about the budget shortfall, but not for the personnel comments."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a source is on the record by default; the tier has to be agreed before the conversation, not asserted after the fact by the reporter.

### On background
Content can be used, but the source is described by role or category, not named.
**In use:** "A senior administration official, speaking on background, confirmed the timeline."
**Common misuse:** Treating "background" and "off the record" as interchangeable — background material is publishable with a vague attribution; off-the-record material is not publishable at all.

### Off the record
Information shared to inform the reporter's understanding but not usable in the story in any form.
**In use:** "That detail was off the record — I can't print it, but it tells me where to look next."
**Common misuse:** Publishing an off-the-record detail once it's confirmed elsewhere, without renegotiating with the original source — the information may be publishable now, but the original source's off-the-record grant doesn't expire just because a second source said the same thing.

### Deep background
Information usable as the reporter's own assertion, with no attribution at all, not even a vague description of the source.
**In use:** "That's deep background — I'll write it as fact, but there's no 'according to' anywhere near it."
**Common misuse:** Using deep background to launder a single, uncorroborated source's claim into a bare factual assertion — deep background changes the attribution, not the corroboration bar.

### Two-source rule
The newsroom standard that a factual claim about a specific event needs at least two independent sources (or one source plus a document) before publication.
**In use:** "We're holding the resignation story until we get a second source — right now it's just the one aide."
**Common misuse:** Counting two people who heard the same secondhand account from the same origin as two sources; independence, not headcount, is the requirement.

### Corroboration
Independent confirmation of a claim through a source, document, or record that did not originate from the same chain as the original claim.
**In use:** "The bid records corroborate the contract amount, but not the claim that it was steered."
**Common misuse:** Treating any second mention of a claim as corroboration, regardless of whether that second mention traces back to the same original source.

### Attribution
The stated source of a piece of information within the story itself, signaling to the reader how confident the outlet is in the claim.
**In use:** "Keep the cause-of-fire line attributed to the fire marshal until it's confirmed independently — don't let it read as our own conclusion."
**Common misuse:** Blurring attributed and unattributed claims into the same confident narrative voice, so the reader can't tell what the outlet has verified itself versus what it's relaying from a source.

### Nut graf
The paragraph, usually early in a story, that states why the story matters and what's newly true.
**In use:** "The lede has the scene, but the nut graf needs to land the actual news — that the contract went to a relative."
**Common misuse:** Burying the nut graf below several paragraphs of scene-setting, so a reader who stops early misses the actual news.

### Inverted pyramid
A story structure that front-loads the most important, newest information and orders subsequent paragraphs by descending importance.
**In use:** "This is breaking news — inverted pyramid, not a narrative lede."
**Common misuse:** Applying inverted-pyramid structure to a feature or investigative piece where the narrative arc, not front-loaded facts, is what earns the reader's continued attention.

### Embargo
An agreement not to publish information before a specified time, typically granted in exchange for advance access.
**In use:** "The report is embargoed until 6am Thursday — we have it early to prepare coverage, but can't run anything before then."
**Common misuse:** Treating an embargo break by another outlet as license to break it too — the reporter's own agreement with the source is independent of what a competitor does.

### Retraction
A formal withdrawal of a previously published story or claim, distinct from a correction, which fixes a specific error while the story stands.
**In use:** "This isn't a correction-level fix — the central claim is unsupported, so it needs a retraction."
**Common misuse:** Using "correction" language for an error serious enough to undermine the story's central claim, understating the severity to readers.

### Chain of custody (for documents)
The traceable history of who has had possession of a document from its origin to the reporter, used to assess authenticity.
**In use:** "We can't verify the chain of custody on this leaked memo — that doesn't mean it's fake, but it means we verify the content independently before running it."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a document's content is authentic because it "looks real," without any chain-of-custody or independent-detail verification.

### Independence (of sources)
The property that a source's account did not originate from, or get shaped by, another source already counted toward corroboration.
**In use:** "Check independence before you count that as source two — did she witness it herself, or is she repeating what she heard from the first source?"
**Common misuse:** Conflating "different person" with "independent" — a second person repeating the first person's account is not independent corroboration.

### Right of reply
The practice of offering the subject of an allegation a meaningful opportunity to respond before publication.
**In use:** "We reached out with 48 hours' notice — that's their right of reply documented, even though they declined to comment."
**Common misuse:** Sending a request with an unreasonably short window right before publication and treating that as having satisfied the obligation.
