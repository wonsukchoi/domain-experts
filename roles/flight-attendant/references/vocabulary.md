# Vocabulary — Flight Attendant

### Arming / disarming (a door)
Setting a door's evacuation-slide mode selector so the slide auto-deploys on opening (armed, used for flight) or does not (disarmed, used at the gate) — verified by a spoken cross-check, not by memory of the last action taken.
**In use:** "Doors to arrival, cross-check — Door 1 Left disarmed, confirmed."
**Common misuse:** Treating the physical selector movement as the complete procedure, when the cross-check confirmation from the crossing flight attendant is the half that actually catches a mismatch.

### 90-second evacuation demonstration
The Part 25.803/Appendix J certification test requiring a full passenger load to evacuate an aircraft within 90 seconds using only half the exits, in reduced lighting, with a representative (including less-mobile) passenger mix.
**In use:** "This exit's worst-case load just went from 54 to 72 passengers — that's the margin the 90-second test was built around, not a cushion above it."
**Common misuse:** Citing "90 seconds" as an operational promise about a real emergency rather than the specific worst-case parameters the number was demonstrated under.

### Sterile cockpit (cabin-side application)
The rule (14 CFR 121.542) restricting non-essential communication to the flight deck below 10,000 ft or during other critical phases of flight — applies to what the cabin sends forward, not only to flight-deck conversation.
**In use:** "That catering question waits till cruise — we're sterile below ten."
**Common misuse:** Assuming the rule only governs the pilots' own chatter, so a cabin call about a non-safety matter during climb or descent doesn't count as a violation.

### Silent review
A flight attendant's individual, unspoken mental rehearsal of exit operation, evacuation commands, and brace signals for the specific aircraft and seat configuration of the current flight, performed before each takeoff and landing.
**In use:** "Ran my silent review — Door 2 Right's the MEL item today, so I'm mentally routing that flow to 2 Left if it comes to it."
**Common misuse:** Treating it as a fixed memorized script that doesn't need to change with the day's actual aircraft, seating, or equipment status.

### Enhanced Emergency Medical Kit (EMK)
The expanded onboard medical kit (beyond basic first aid) required by 14 CFR 121.803/121.309, containing items such as epinephrine, antihistamine, nitroglycerin, atropine, and IV supplies, intended for use by qualified responders often under ground guidance.
**In use:** "Get the EMK to row 14, but hold on the epi until MedLink confirms — this isn't the clear-cut anaphylaxis case."
**Common misuse:** Treating "we have the EMK on board" as equivalent to being cleared to use every item in it without medical guidance.

### Ground-based medical consult (MedLink / STAT-MD)
A satellite or radio link to a ground-based emergency physician service, contracted by the airline, that guides in-flight medical response and helps decide whether a diversion is medically warranted.
**In use:** "MedLink wants vitals again in five minutes before they'll advise on the nitroglycerin."
**Common misuse:** Skipping the consult call because the flight attendant feels confident in a snap diagnosis, rather than treating consult as the default path except for unambiguous immediate life-threats.

### Unruly passenger / zero-tolerance policy
The FAA's enforcement posture (since 2021) treating interference with crew or unsafe passenger conduct with escalated civil penalties — up to $37,000 per violation — rather than warnings alone, alongside potential criminal referral.
**In use:** "This isn't a customer-service note, it's a zero-tolerance incident — the report goes to security and possibly the FAA."
**Common misuse:** Treating "zero tolerance" as meaning every incident skips straight to the harshest response, when the policy describes penalty severity, not skipping the documented escalation tiers.

### 49 U.S.C. § 46504 (interference with flight crew)
The federal criminal statute making it a crime to assault or intimidate a flight crewmember or attendant so as to interfere with their duties, independent of any FAA civil penalty.
**In use:** "Once he grabbed my arm, this stopped being an FAA fine question — that's a 46504 referral."
**Common misuse:** Assuming FAA civil penalties are the only consequence available, missing that a criminal referral is a separate and available track for physical interference.

### Type I / Type III exit (exit classification)
FAA exit-type classifications (Type I: floor-level door; Type III: smaller overwing exit with a minimum size and step-down/step-up limit) that determine passenger eligibility for exit-row seating and the exit's assumed evacuation throughput.
**In use:** "That's a Type III over the wing — the exit-row screening questions apply there just as much as at the Type I doors."
**Common misuse:** Assuming exit-row screening (mobility, language, willingness) only matters at the large floor-level doors, not the smaller overwing exits.

### Purser / lead flight attendant
The flight attendant assigned overall in-charge responsibility for the cabin crew on a given flight, the primary safety liaison to the captain, and typically the final internal escalation point before involving the flight deck or ground ops.
**In use:** "Take it to the purser before we call the flight deck — she needs to know regardless of whether we escalate further."
**Common misuse:** Treating the purser as a service-seniority title only, bypassing them on a safety call that's specifically their coordination responsibility.

### Girt bar
The mechanical bar connecting an evacuation slide/raft to the aircraft door sill, engaged when a door is armed so the slide deploys automatically on opening; a disengaged girt bar means no automatic slide even with the door marked "armed."
**In use:** "Confirm the girt bar's actually engaged, not just the selector — that's what the slide is physically attached to."
**Common misuse:** Assuming the mode-selector position alone guarantees slide deployment, without a physical/visual check that the girt bar is engaged.

### Exit-row screening
The pre-flight passenger qualification process (mobility, comprehension, willingness to assist) required for anyone seated in an exit row, since that passenger may need to operate the exit or assist others in an evacuation.
**In use:** "He said he can't lift the exit hatch — reseat him, that's a screening fail, not a preference."
**Common misuse:** Treating the screening as a formality answered by the passenger's self-report alone, without observing whether it's consistent with what's actually asked at boarding.
