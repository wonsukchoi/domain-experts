## 1. Skill/will/systems diagnosis worksheet (filled — see Worked example in SKILL.md for full narrative)

| Metric | Trend | Diagnosis implication |
|---|---|---|
| Close rate | Stable, 26-30% historical range (currently 28%) | Not a skill gap — the skill-sensitive metric hasn't moved |
| Opportunities created per rep/month | Dropped 14 → 9 (36%), starting 3 months ago | Volume/systems issue — coincides with CRM migration |
| Coinciding process change | CRM migration added 12 extra fields/opportunity (~15 min admin time/deal) | Root cause candidate — cuts directly into prospecting time |

**Diagnosis:** systems issue (CRM friction reducing prospecting time), not a skill gap — training on selling technique would not address this.

## 2. Cost/impact comparison (filled example)

| Option | Cost | Targets | Expected impact |
|---|---|---|---|
| Sales skills training program | $45,000 ($27,000 vendor + $18,000 opportunity cost) | Close rate (not actually declined) | Low — targets a metric that isn't the problem |
| CRM field simplification | $6,500 one-time | Opportunity volume (actually declined) | High — projected to recover ~4 of 5 lost opportunities/rep/month, ~$302,400/month in expected revenue |

**Rule applied:** the fix is selected based on which metric actually moved and why, not based on which intervention the L&D function is most equipped to build.

## 3. Kirkpatrick evaluation plan (filled example, for an approved training program)

| Level | Measure | Data source | Timing |
|---|---|---|---|
| Reaction | Post-session satisfaction score | Survey | Immediately after session |
| Learning | Knowledge/skill check (scenario-based assessment) | In-program assessment | End of session |
| Behavior | Manager-observed behavior change on defined criteria | Manager check-in, 30/60/90 days | 30, 60, 90 days post-training |
| Results | Business metric movement (e.g., defined KPI) | Business reporting system | 90 days post-training |

**Rule applied:** the program's success is evaluated at all four levels, not just reaction — behavior and results are what actually determine whether the program worked.

## 4. Reinforcement design template (filled example)

| Touchpoint | Timing | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| Initial workshop | Day 0 | Introduce concept, initial practice |
| Manager coaching conversation | Week 1 | Reinforce expectation, discuss application to real current work |
| Peer practice session | Week 3 | Spaced repetition, applied practice with feedback |
| Manager check-in | Week 6 | Assess behavior change, address barriers to application |
| Follow-up refresher (optional) | Week 12 | Address decay, reinforce for durability |

**Rule applied:** the program design includes reinforcement touchpoints spanning 12 weeks, not just the initial session — addressing the forgetting curve explicitly rather than assuming the single session is sufficient.
