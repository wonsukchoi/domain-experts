# Training and development working vocabulary

Terms a training manager uses precisely. Format: definition → how a practitioner says it → common misuse.

**Skill/will/systems diagnosis** — Triaging a performance gap as a genuine capability deficit (skill, coachable through training), a motivation/incentive/expectation problem (will, not fixed by training), or a broken process/systems issue (systems, not fixed by training) — only the first is actually addressed by a training intervention.
In use: "Close rate is stable and volume dropped right after the CRM migration — that's a systems diagnosis, not a skill one, so training isn't the fix."
Misuse: defaulting to a training program for any performance gap without first checking which of the three causes actually applies, wasting investment on an intervention that doesn't address the real cause.

**Kirkpatrick's four levels (reaction, learning, behavior, results)** — A training evaluation framework distinguishing participant satisfaction (reaction), knowledge/skill gained (learning), on-the-job behavior change (behavior), and business outcome impact (results) — most evaluation stops at the weakest level (reaction).
In use: "We have strong reaction scores, but we haven't measured behavior or results yet — that's the gap in our evaluation, not evidence the program worked."
Misuse: treating a positive satisfaction survey (reaction) as sufficient evidence the training worked, when it's the weakest and easiest-to-game level of the four.

**70-20-10 model** — A framework suggesting roughly 70% of workplace learning comes from on-the-job experience, 20% from social/coaching interactions, and 10% from formal training — a reminder that formal training is typically the smaller lever, not the primary one, for building capability.
In use: "We're investing more in manager coaching and stretch assignments this cycle, per 70-20-10 — formal training alone won't move the needle as much as those channels."
Misuse: over-investing in formal training programs while neglecting on-the-job application and coaching support, which the model suggests carry more of the actual learning weight.

**Forgetting curve** — The well-documented pattern of rapid knowledge decay after a single learning event without reinforcement, the basis for designing follow-up and application into training programs rather than relying on a one-time session.
In use: "Without the spaced follow-up sessions, most of what's covered in this workshop will be forgotten within a few weeks per the forgetting curve — that's why the reinforcement plan matters as much as the session content."
Misuse: designing a single, well-produced training session with no built-in reinforcement or follow-up, expecting durable behavior change from the initial exposure alone.

**Transfer (training transfer)** — Whether skills or knowledge learned in training actually get applied on the job, which depends as much on post-training workplace conditions (manager expectations, process support, opportunity to practice) as on the training content itself.
In use: "Transfer failed here not because the content was wrong, but because managers never actually expected or reinforced the new behavior back on the job."
Misuse: assuming well-designed training content alone guarantees transfer to on-the-job behavior, without checking whether the workplace afterward actually supports and expects the new behavior.

**Job aid** — A lightweight reference tool (checklist, quick-reference guide, decision tree) used as an alternative to formal training for infrequent or easily-referenced tasks, avoiding the overinvestment of building a full program for a narrow need.
In use: "This task happens twice a year — a one-page job aid solves it, we don't need a formal training module."
Misuse: building a full formal training program for a need that's infrequent or narrow enough that a good job aid or brief coaching conversation would address it more efficiently.

**Andragogy (adult learning theory)** — The theory that adult learners need relevance to real, immediate problems, active practice, and connection to existing experience, as distinct from a traditional passive, lecture-style classroom model optimized for information transfer.
In use: "We redesigned this from a lecture format to a scenario-based workshop with real case data — that's andragogy in practice, not just a content update."
Misuse: designing training for adults using a passive, information-transfer format optimized for content coverage rather than active practice and relevance, producing weak retention and application.

**Needs analysis (training)** — The diagnostic process conducted before designing a training intervention, confirming that training is actually the right response to a stated performance gap rather than assuming it based on the request alone.
In use: "The needs analysis showed this is a process issue, not a skill gap — we're recommending against the requested training program."
Misuse: skipping needs analysis and moving directly to program design because a stakeholder requested training, without confirming training is actually the correct intervention for the underlying cause.

**Spaced repetition** — Distributing learning and practice opportunities over time rather than concentrating them in a single intensive session, shown to produce more durable retention than a one-time exposure.
In use: "The program includes three follow-up practice sessions over six weeks, not just the initial two-day workshop — that's spaced repetition, and it's what actually drives retention."
Misuse: relying on a single intensive session's design quality to produce durable learning, without spacing reinforcement and practice opportunities over time.

**Business outcome measure (training)** — A defined, measurable business result (revenue, error rate, retention, safety incidents) that a training program is meant to move, established before the program is built, as distinct from a vague or absent success criterion.
In use: "The defined business outcome measure for this program is reducing onboarding time-to-productivity from 90 to 60 days — that's what we're evaluating against, not just session attendance."
Misuse: building a training program without a defined business outcome measure, making it impossible to evaluate whether the program actually achieved anything beyond delivering content.
