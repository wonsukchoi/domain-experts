# Vocabulary

Terms generalists reliably misuse. Format per term: definition, how a practitioner actually uses it, the common misuse.

### Church truck

A wheeled bier used to move a casket without hand-carrying it.
**In use:** "Truck it to the graveside path head, hand-carry only the last twenty feet where the turf starts."
**Common misuse:** treating it as optional equipment for "the heavy ones" rather than the default for any distance beyond a doorway threshold — it's the default, and hand-carry is the exception forced by stairs or terrain.

### Bearer vs. pallbearer

A bearer physically carries the casket; a pallbearer, strictly, carries or walks alongside holding the pall (the cloth covering the casket) — in modern US usage the terms are used interchangeably for whoever carries the casket, but the load-bearing distinction still matters for staffing.
**In use:** "We've got four family bearers — pair them with two staff on the front corners."
**Common misuse:** assuming any willing adult is a fungible bearer regardless of position; corner placement (steering, weight judgment) is not interchangeable with a middle or rear position.

### Committal

The graveside portion of the service where the casket or urn is lowered or placed at its final resting location, distinct from the funeral or memorial service itself.
**In use:** "Committal's at 11:30 — lowering device needs to be function-checked before the family arrives at 11:15."
**Common misuse:** conflating "the funeral" (which may happen at a chapel earlier) with "the committal" (which happens at the cemetery) — they're frequently scheduled separately and staffed differently.

### Cremated remains / cremains

The bone fragments (processed to a granular consistency) that remain after cremation — not ash from combustion in the fireplace sense.
**In use:** "Verify the disc on the container before it goes into the urn — cremains from different cases look identical."
**Common misuse:** treating visual similarity as identity confirmation; the ID tag/disc, not appearance, is the actual verification.

### Chain of custody

The unbroken, documented sequence of who had control of remains or personal effects at each transfer, with each transfer verified against paperwork.
**In use:** "Log the transfer with a name and a timestamp — an unlogged handoff is a broken chain even if nothing actually went wrong."
**Common misuse:** treating the log as paperwork to complete after the fact rather than the verification step itself; the check has to happen at the moment of transfer, not be reconstructed later from memory.

### General Price List (GPL)

The FTC Funeral Rule-mandated itemized price disclosure the funeral home presents before merchandise or service discussion — an arranger/director document, not something the attendant produces, but attendants should recognize it because family cost questions get routed to whoever owns that document.
**In use:** "That's a GPL question — I'll get the director, that's not something I can quote."
**Common misuse:** an attendant fielding a pricing question directly instead of routing it, which risks quoting something inconsistent with the actual signed GPL.

### Exposure Control Plan

The funeral home's written OSHA 1910.1030-required plan identifying tasks with bloodborne-pathogen exposure risk and the required controls (PPE, engineering controls, post-exposure procedure) for each.
**In use:** "Dressing and casketing are on the Exposure Control Plan's task list — gloves and a gown, every time, not just when it looks like it's needed."
**Common misuse:** treating PPE use as a personal judgment call based on how the task looks that day, rather than a task-triggered requirement regardless of appearance.

### Funeral escort vehicle / lead vehicle

The vehicle (staff or a licensed escort service) that leads a funeral procession and, in many states, holds a specific legal privilege at intersections that the rest of the procession does not independently have.
**In use:** "The escort has the right-of-way privilege at that light — the hearse and the rest of us are following through on their entry, not our own."
**Common misuse:** assuming every vehicle in the procession independently has the same right-of-way privilege as the lead vehicle; in most states the privilege attaches to the lead/escort vehicle's lawful entry, and the rest follow through under that entry, not under an independent right.

### Loaded weight

The combined weight of the casket and the decedent — the number that actually determines bearer staffing, as opposed to casket weight alone.
**In use:** "Casket's 190, decedent's estimated 210 — that's a 400 lb loaded weight, so we're at six bearers minimum."
**Common misuse:** staffing bearers off the empty casket weight from the merchandise order, which understates the actual carry load by whatever the decedent weighs.

### First call

The funeral home's initial transport of the decedent from the place of death (hospital, residence, hospice, medical examiner) to the funeral home — often the attendant's first direct task on a new case.
**In use:** "First call came in at 2 AM from the hospital — need a driver and a second person for the transfer."
**Common misuse:** treating first call as informal or low-stakes because it's early logistics; it's the first chain-of-custody point and the first documentation (time, location, condition) that follows the case through everything after it.

### Register book

The guest sign-in book at a viewing or service, later given to the family as a keepsake record of attendance.
**In use:** "Register book's open to page one at the entry — check it's not left closed or on the wrong page between services."
**Common misuse:** treating it as disposable event signage rather than a keepsake the family keeps; a book left messy, on the wrong page, or with a broken binding is a small but permanent-feeling error to the family.

### Vault / grave liner

A vault is a sealed, typically reinforced outer container the casket is placed inside before burial; a grave liner is a lighter, typically unsealed version that mainly prevents ground settling — not interchangeable terms even though both sit outside the casket in the ground.
**In use:** "Cemetery requires a liner at minimum, but the family purchased a sealed vault — confirm which one is staged at the graveside before the committal."
**Common misuse:** using "vault" and "liner" interchangeably when discussing what's required or purchased; cemetery requirements and family purchases can specify one and not the other, and staging the wrong one at graveside is a visible, hard-to-fix-in-the-moment error.
