# Fireground working vocabulary

Terms a company officer uses daily and precisely. Format: definition → how a practitioner says it → common misuse.

**Flashover** — The near-simultaneous ignition of all combustible materials and gases in a compartment once they reach ignition temperature, transitioning a room from a growing fire to full involvement in seconds.
In use: "Ceiling layer's black and rolling — we're close to flashover, hit it and back out if it doesn't darken down."
Misuse: assuming there's the same warning window legacy training taught (minutes). UL FSRI testing on rooms with modern synthetic furnishings shows flashover can occur in under 5 minutes versus roughly 17 minutes for a legacy natural-fiber room — treating today's fire like a 1970s training fire is how the warning window disappears.

**Backdraft** — An explosive ignition that occurs when oxygen is suddenly introduced into a superheated, oxygen-starved, fuel-rich compartment (unburned pyrolysis gases waiting on air).
In use: "Pressurized smoke, no visible flame, whistling at that gap — that's backdraft conditions, we vent remote before we touch that door."
Misuse: using it interchangeably with flashover. They're different mechanisms (oxygen introduction vs. heat accumulation) with different tactical responses — venting a suspected backdraft compartment from the wrong point is how you cause the explosion you were trying to avoid.

**Flow path** — The directional movement of heat and smoke from the fire (high pressure) toward one or more openings (lower pressure), carrying oxygen to the fire along the way.
In use: "Don't crack that window yet — you'll pull the flow path straight through the hallway we're searching."
Misuse: treating ventilation as simple relief for trapped heat and smoke rather than as adding fuel to an equation the fire is actively solving. Uncoordinated ventilation before water is applied increases heat release, not just visibility.

**RECEO-VS** — Tactical priority order: Rescue, Exposures, Confinement, Extinguishment, Overhaul, Ventilation, Salvage — the sequence in which a fire's problems are addressed by priority, not necessarily by clock order.
In use: "Rescue's our priority this arrival — attack and exposure protection wait for the second-due crew."
Misuse: treating "V" as literally last in time, meaning ventilation gets deferred until overhaul. In practice ventilation is coordinated throughout the incident, timed to attack readiness — deferring it to a literal last step is as wrong as doing it first without coordination.

**Two-in/two-out** — The OSHA 1910.134 minimum staffing rule for offensive interior structural firefighting: at least two members make entry together, with at least two members staged outside dedicated to rescue if needed.
In use: "We're two-in-two-out compliant once the second engine's crew is staged — until then we're on the known-rescue exception only."
Misuse: treating the known-viable-rescue exception (entering before the full four are staffed, for a confirmed life hazard) as a general license to skip the accountability structure rather than a narrow, time-boxed allowance.

**Rule of thirds (air management)** — A cylinder's air is split into three planning segments: one third to reach the objective, one third to perform the task, one third reserved to exit.
In use: "We're at the second-third mark — task needs to wrap in the next few minutes or we're turning around regardless."
Misuse: planning off the cylinder's rated time (e.g., 30 minutes) instead of the working-exertion yield, which is closer to half that under heat stress and elevated heart rate.

**LUNAR** — The mayday radio report format: Location, Unit, Name, Assignment, Resources needed.
In use: "Mayday, mayday, mayday — LUNAR: second floor Bravo bedroom, Engine 7, Diaz, was searching, need RIT and a hoseline."
Misuse: rushing through the report and skipping "resources needed," which is the field command needs most to actually deploy help — a mayday call without it just confirms trouble exists, without saying what fixes it.

**Offensive / defensive / transitional** — Offensive: interior attack and/or rescue. Defensive: exterior-only, structure not entered, crews withdrawn to a collapse zone. Transitional: a brief exterior knockdown to improve interior tenability before committing crews inside.
In use: "We're transitional — quick hit from the yard, then reassess for offensive once the interior cools."
Misuse: treating transitional as a standing strategy rather than a short bridge tactic, or treating a defensive call as a failure rather than the correct read of an unsavable structure.

**Size-up / 360** — The continuous assessment of construction, occupancy, life hazard, fire location/extent, and resources, ideally including a walk-around of all four sides before committing to a plan.
In use: "Got three sides, skipping Delta for the rescue — someone give me eyes on it once we're inside."
Misuse: skipping or truncating the walk-around under time pressure without explicitly naming what's being deferred and to whom — an unstated gap in size-up is what get discovered mid-operation instead of before committing.

**Collapse zone** — The exterior area kept clear of personnel during defensive operations, sized to the height (or 1.5× the height) of the wall most likely to fail.
In use: "Full collapse zone, one and a half times the wall height — nobody works the corner, corners fail with the wall."
Misuse: assuming a building corner is a safer position than the face of a wall; corners fail along with whichever wall goes, and are inside the same collapse radius.

**Tenability** — Whether an interior environment (heat, visibility, toxic gas concentration) can be survived by an unprotected occupant or safely worked by a protected crew for a meaningful duration.
In use: "Heat's still tenable at this level, but it's trending up fast — reassess in sixty seconds."
Misuse: equating "no visible flame" with "tenable." Smoke and gas conditions can be lethal to an occupant, or close to untenable for crew, well before flame is visible in a given space.

**VEIS (Vent-Enter-Isolate-Search)** — A targeted search tactic: vent a specific opening (usually a bedroom window), enter directly, isolate the room by closing its door, then search — bypassing a search from the main entry inward.
In use: "Taking VEIS on the Bravo bedroom for the reported victim — venting, entering, isolating, then searching."
Misuse: venting and entering without immediately isolating. Skipping the isolate step leaves the new opening connected to the rest of the structure's flow path exactly when a separate attack-side opening may also be forming.

**PAR (Personnel Accountability Report)** — A real-time roll call confirming every assigned member's status and location, taken at fixed intervals and immediately after any mayday or major event.
In use: "Command, PAR complete — all units accounted for."
Misuse: treating it as a formality read off a roster rather than a live confirmation triggered by every division change, task change, or mayday — a stale PAR is the reason a missing crew member isn't noticed for several minutes.

**Needed fire flow** — The estimated gpm required to overcome the heat being released by a given fire, roughly calculated from structure dimensions and adjusted for percentage of involvement.
In use: "Twenty-four hundred square feet, maybe fifteen percent involved — that's around a hundred twenty gpm, tank water gets us four minutes before we need the hydrant line."
Misuse: eyeballing tank capacity against "how big the fire looks" rather than running the flow calculation and checking it against actual tank duration at that flow rate.
