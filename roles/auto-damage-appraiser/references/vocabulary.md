### Total-loss formula (TLF)
A state-specific formula comparing repair cost (and, in some states, salvage value) against a percentage of a vehicle's actual cash value to determine whether it must be declared a total loss.
**In use:** "This state uses a 75% TLF threshold — repair cost has to hit $7,125 on a $9,500 ACV vehicle before it's a total loss."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a single national total-loss percentage applies everywhere; the threshold and formula structure vary by state.

### Actual cash value (ACV)
The determined market value of a vehicle immediately before a loss, based on comparable local sales adjusted for mileage, condition, and options — not a depreciation-schedule calculation from original purchase price.
**In use:** "ACV came back at $9,500 based on three comparable listings in the local market."
**Common misuse:** Calculating ACV as a flat percentage depreciation from MSRP or purchase price instead of pulling actual comparable sales data.

### Supplement
An additional cost added to an original repair estimate, typically discovered during teardown, requiring separate documentation and approval.
**In use:** "The supplement request for frame damage came in with teardown photos — approved."
**Common misuse:** Treating any supplement as automatic evidence of an inflated original estimate, or approving supplements without requiring the documentation that supports them.

### Teardown
The process of disassembling a damaged vehicle at the body shop to expose hidden damage not visible during the initial estimate.
**In use:** "We won't know the full structural damage until teardown."
**Common misuse:** Writing a final, non-negotiable estimate before teardown on damage severe enough that hidden damage is likely.

### OEM parts
Original Equipment Manufacturer parts — made by or for the vehicle's original manufacturer, as opposed to aftermarket or recycled parts.
**In use:** "State disclosure rules require OEM on this structural component given the vehicle's age."
**Common misuse:** Treating parts sourcing as a pure cost decision without checking state disclosure or restriction requirements tied to vehicle age or part type.

### Aftermarket parts
Replacement parts manufactured by a company other than the original vehicle manufacturer, typically lower cost but subject to disclosure or restriction rules in many states.
**In use:** "Aftermarket fender is fine here — it's cosmetic, not structural, and the state permits it on this vehicle."
**Common misuse:** Assuming aftermarket parts are permitted or prohibited uniformly, rather than checking the specific state rule for the specific part class and vehicle age.

### Recycled parts (LKQ — like-kind-and-quality)
Used original-manufacturer parts sourced from other vehicles, offered as a lower-cost alternative to new OEM parts.
**In use:** "We specified an LKQ door assembly to bring the estimate down, since it's cosmetic and in comparable condition."
**Common misuse:** Specifying recycled parts for safety-critical components where fit and material integrity matter more than cosmetic parts.

### Salvage value
The amount a damaged (typically total-loss) vehicle is worth for parts, scrap, or resale to a salvage buyer.
**In use:** "Salvage bid came back at $2,100 — that's what nets against the insured's payout."
**Common misuse:** Estimating salvage value as a fixed percentage of ACV instead of obtaining an actual bid or auction comparable for the specific damage profile.

### Diminished value
The loss in a vehicle's resale value that persists even after a proper repair, because a documented accident history reduces buyer willingness to pay full comparable price.
**In use:** "The repair was done correctly, but the insured is also claiming diminished value given the accident's now on the vehicle history report."
**Common misuse:** Treating diminished value as automatically included in the repair estimate rather than a separate claim category with its own evaluation method and, in some states, no first-party recognition at all.

### Betterment
A deduction applied when a repair replaces a worn or aged part with a new one, reflecting the value added beyond restoring the vehicle to its pre-loss condition.
**In use:** "Betterment applies to the worn tires being replaced as part of this repair — insured pays that difference."
**Common misuse:** Applying betterment broadly across a repair instead of only to the specific items where the replacement genuinely exceeds pre-loss condition.

### Book time
The standard labor-hour reference for a specific repair operation, provided by the estimating platform (CCC, Mitchell, Audatex) based on average shop conditions.
**In use:** "Book time for this panel replacement is 4.5 hours — the shop's requesting 6, so let's see what's different."
**Common misuse:** Treating book time as a fixed, non-negotiable number in either direction rather than a reference baseline that a documented vehicle condition can reasonably justify deviating from.
