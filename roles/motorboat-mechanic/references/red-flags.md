# Red flags

Smell tests for a lower-unit service, a corrosion/anode complaint, a fuel-system call, or a pre-delivery/winterization inspection.

### Gearcase oil comes out milky or frothy at a routine drain
- **Usually means:** a prop-shaft or driveshaft seal has failed and is admitting water into the gear lube; second most likely is a failed vent/fill plug O-ring on a boat that sat trailered nose-down.
- **First question:** "How long has the oil looked like this, and has the drive been run since — is there any metal in the drained oil?"
- **Data to pull:** drained-oil sample (color, metal content, volume vs. spec capacity), gearcase pressure/vacuum test result, prior service history on this seal.

### Anode consumed more than roughly 50–75% faster than its ABYC-calculated seasonal load
- **Usually means:** a stray DC current source (chafed wiring, a leaking bilge-pump circuit) or newly added unbonded underwater metal, not an undersized anode.
- **First question:** "What's the hull-potential reading vs. a reference electrode, and does it change when specific circuits are isolated?"
- **Data to pull:** hull-potential survey result, bonding-system continuity check, wiring inspection near through-hulls, wetted-metal-area recalculation if hardware was added since last season.

### Hull potential reads more positive than roughly −0.80V vs. Ag/AgCl (aluminum) or the equivalent under-protected threshold for the hull's metal
- **Usually means:** the anode system is genuinely under-protecting the hull — undersized anode, broken bonding continuity, or an anode past its usable life, not a stray-current issue.
- **First question:** "Is the anode physically intact and electrically connected to the bonding system, or has continuity failed somewhere in the circuit?"
- **Data to pull:** anode physical condition and remaining mass, bonding-wire continuity at each connection point, reference-electrode reading at multiple hull locations.

### Hull potential reads more negative than roughly −1.10V vs. Ag/AgCl
- **Usually means:** over-protection — commonly too much anode area or an impressed-current system miscalibrated — risking paint disbondment or, on aluminum, hydrogen embrittlement at welds over time.
- **First question:** "Is this an impressed-current cathodic protection system, and if so, what's it calibrated to?"
- **Data to pull:** anode/ICCP system specification and calibration log, paint condition survey, total installed anode area vs. calculated requirement.

### Fuel sample shows a visible water/ethanol layer separated from the gasoline in a clear vial
- **Usually means:** E10 phase separation from extended storage (commonly 60+ days) with sufficient water ingress — additives and stabilizers cannot reverse this once it's occurred.
- **First question:** "How long has this fuel been in the tank, and was stabilizer added before or after any water exposure?"
- **Data to pull:** fuel purchase/storage date if known, tank fill level over the storage period, water-test-vial result showing layer proportions.

### One cylinder's compression reads 15% or more below its neighbors on the same engine
- **Usually means:** a ring, reed valve (two-stroke), or head-gasket fault isolated to that cylinder — a uniformly low reading across all cylinders is more often altitude/temperature or a shared crankcase-seal issue.
- **First question:** "Is the low reading isolated to one cylinder, or is the whole engine reading uniformly low?"
- **Data to pull:** cylinder-by-cylinder compression figures, engine hours, prior compression history if on file for this engine.

### An electrical splice, connector, or component found in an enclosed gasoline engine compartment or fuel-tank space with no visible SAE J1171 ignition-protection marking
- **Usually means:** a prior repair used non-marine-rated automotive parts, or a low-voltage circuit was assumed exempt because of its amperage rather than its location.
- **First question:** "Is this component rated ignition-protected per SAE J1171, and if not, what's it doing in this space?"
- **Data to pull:** component manufacturer spec sheet or ignition-protection certification mark, compartment classification (gasoline engine space vs. isolated diesel/electrical space).

### Boat winterized with no documented confirmation that every raw-water passage was drained or filled with propylene glycol antifreeze
- **Usually means:** a rushed end-of-season sequence skipped a riser, elbow, or block passage — the failure (a cracked casting) won't show a symptom until spring start-up, months after the invoice closed.
- **First question:** "Which specific passages were confirmed drained or filled, and is that itemized on the winterization checklist?"
- **Data to pull:** the shop's winterization checklist for this engine, technician sign-off, freeze-plug/block-drain confirmation.

### Customer requests an oversized or non-OEM anode kit specifically because the current one "wears out fast"
- **Usually means:** an assumption that fast wear equals undersizing, without a stray-current or bonding check to confirm — the more likely cause in a fast-wear case is a current source beyond passive protection.
- **First question:** "Has a hull-potential and bonding-continuity survey been run before assuming this is a sizing problem?"
- **Data to pull:** anode consumption rate vs. calculated seasonal load, hull-potential reading, bonding-continuity test results.

### Trim/tilt cylinder or an outdrive shaft seal replaced twice or more within one season without a pressure or vacuum test locating the actual leak path
- **Usually means:** the first repair addressed a symptom (visible fluid loss) rather than a confirmed leak location — a repeat failure at the same point is evidence the diagnosis, not just the seal, was wrong.
- **First question:** "What pressure or vacuum test confirmed the leak location on the first repair, versus what was simply replaced?"
- **Data to pull:** prior repair order's test data (if any), pressure/vacuum test result on the current failure, seal part number and lot if a batch defect is suspected.

### Boat stored over a layup period longer than 30 days with a partial fuel tank and no stabilizer added
- **Usually means:** increased headspace accelerates moisture condensation into the fuel, shortening the runway to ethanol phase separation compared to a full tank.
- **First question:** "What was the fuel level at the start of layup, and was stabilizer added at that time or after the boat sat unattended?"
- **Data to pull:** layup start date and fuel level, stabilizer product and dosage used, tank inspection/fuel-vial test result at commissioning.
