# Vocabulary

Terms of art in coaching and scouting, with the misuse a generalist commonly makes.

### 20-80 scale
A scouting grading scale (normal distribution, mean 50, roughly 10-point standard deviation) applied per trait — 50 is an adequate starter at the next level, 60 is all-conference/all-league caliber, 70+ is elite or pro caliber.
**In use:** "His ball skills are a 65, but the tackling grades out at 42 — that's the whole evaluation right there."
**Common misuse:** Treating it as a 0-100 percentage scale, or clustering every prospect in the 55-65 band because nobody wants to commit to a real spread (grade inflation).

### Ceiling vs. floor
Ceiling is the best plausible developmental outcome; floor is the worst-case outcome you'd still accept for the roster slot in question.
**In use:** "For a redshirt slot I'll take the higher ceiling; for a two-deep need this fall I need the floor."
**Common misuse:** Grading every prospect on ceiling alone, which overrates traits (measurables) that predict upside but say nothing about immediate reliability.

### Acute:chronic workload ratio (ACWR)
The current week's training/competition load divided by the trailing 4-week average load; a proxy for whether a spike in workload is outrunning the athlete's conditioning base.
**In use:** "He's at 1.54 this week — pull him back to the sweet band before we practice him full again."
**Common misuse:** Tracking only team-level or season-total load instead of per-player rolling ratios, which hides exactly the individual spikes the metric exists to catch.

### Relative age effect (RAE)
The tendency for players born earlier in a selection-year cohort to be overrepresented in talent pools because early physical maturity is mistaken for skill at young ages.
**In use:** "He's the youngest kid in his class by ten months — discount the raw stat line accordingly before ranking him against the rest of the cohort."
**Common misuse:** Assuming RAE is only a youth-sport phenomenon; it persists into high-school and even college evaluation pools whenever selection has compounded across years.

### Periodization (macrocycle / mesocycle / microcycle)
The structured division of a training year (macrocycle) into blocks of several weeks (mesocycles) built around a training emphasis, further divided into individual weeks (microcycles).
**In use:** "This mesocycle is a strength-emphasis block; we'll taper volume the week before the conference opener."
**Common misuse:** Calling any weekly practice schedule "periodization" without an actual planned progression of volume/intensity across mesocycles — a plan that's the same every week isn't periodized, it's just a schedule.

### Taper
A planned, deliberate reduction in training volume (not intensity) in the days-to-weeks before a peak competition, intended to let accumulated fatigue dissipate while preserving fitness.
**In use:** "We taper live reps by about a third fight week, but keep tempo and intensity up so timing doesn't slip."
**Common misuse:** Confusing a taper with simply resting — cutting intensity along with volume, which lets conditioning slip right before the competition it was meant to protect.

### Live rep vs. scout-team rep
A live rep is a snap taken with the game plan and starting personnel at competitive intensity; a scout-team rep is a lower-intensity rep run against the upcoming opponent's schemes, typically by non-starters.
**In use:** "Swap him to scout-team reps Tuesday — he's over his weekly live-rep number."
**Common misuse:** Counting scout-team reps toward a starter's workload cap as if they carried the same physical cost as live reps against a starting defense.

### Graduated return-to-play protocol
A staged, symptom-gated progression (typically six stages) an athlete must clear, with a minimum 24 symptom-free hours per stage, before returning to competition after a head injury.
**In use:** "He's clean at Stage 3 as of this morning — earliest he can touch Stage 4 is tomorrow, not today."
**Common misuse:** Treating the protocol as a fixed number of calendar days rather than a symptom-gated process that resets to the prior stage on any symptom return, regardless of the game schedule.

### Composite ranking
An aggregated recruiting rank (e.g., a weighted average across multiple outside recruiting services) used as an external sanity check on a program's internal grade.
**In use:** "He's a composite 0.87 — below our internal grade, so let's double check what our staff is seeing that outside services aren't."
**Common misuse:** Treating a composite ranking as the grade itself rather than an outside cross-check; composite services grade for general traits, not a specific team's scheme fit.

### Point differential / Pythagorean win expectation
A team's scoring margin across a season, used to estimate the win total the team's actual performance level supports, independent of how close games have broken.
**In use:** "Our record says 8-2, but point differential says a 6-4 team — the record won't hold if the close-game bounces even out."
**Common misuse:** Reading win-loss record alone as the measure of a season's real improvement, ignoring how much of it came from close-game luck versus underlying performance.

### Two-deep / depth chart
The ranked list of players by position, typically to two (or three) deep, used to set starters and next-man-up substitutions.
**In use:** "Rebuild the two-deep off this week's grades before we walk into Wednesday's install."
**Common misuse:** Treating the depth chart as fixed once set in camp rather than a living document reviewed against current performance and matchup every week.

### Contact period / dead period / quiet period (recruiting calendar)
NCAA-defined windows that govern what in-person or off-campus recruiting contact is permitted with a prospect at a given point in the calendar.
**In use:** "We're in a quiet period — phone and written contact only, no campus visit until it opens back up."
**Common misuse:** Conflating "dead period" (no in-person contact at all, on or off campus) with "quiet period" (in-person contact allowed only on the program's own campus) — the two carry very different compliance risk if confused.

### Redshirt
A season in which a player practices and develops with the team but does not use a year of playing eligibility, typically by staying under a maximum-games threshold.
**In use:** "He's a ceiling play for a redshirt year — we're not counting on him for snaps this fall."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a redshirt year guarantees development; without a deliberate individual plan it's just a year on the roster, not a year of improvement.

### Transfer portal
The formal mechanism by which a player enters a pool signaling eligibility to transfer and be recruited by other programs, often mid-cycle rather than on the traditional annual signing calendar.
**In use:** "We lost a starter to the portal in March — the recruiting plan has to have a portal contingency, not just a high-school class."
**Common misuse:** Treating portal recruiting as identical to high-school recruiting evaluation; portal players carry a verifiable college-level performance file, which changes what evidence a grade should weight most.
