# Facilities/admin working vocabulary

Terms a facilities/administrative services lead uses daily and precisely. Format: definition → how a practitioner says it → common misuse.

**Total cost of ownership (TCO)** — The full cost of a vendor or asset over its useful life or contract term: price plus downtime, management overhead, and switching costs — not just the quoted rate.
In use: "Vendor A is 25% cheaper on paper, but TCO is higher once you price in their SLA-implied downtime."
Misuse: comparing only the line-item price at renewal, which is exactly the comparison TCO exists to correct.

**SLA (service level agreement)** — The contracted performance commitment (typically response time and resolution time) a vendor must meet, usually paired with a remedy (service credit) if missed.
In use: "The SLA is a 4-hour response, 24-hour resolution — anything past that triggers a service credit."
Misuse: treating the SLA as a target the vendor tries for rather than a contractual commitment with a defined remedy when missed.

**Service credit** — A contractually defined discount or refund owed to the customer when a vendor misses its SLA, distinct from a discretionary goodwill gesture.
In use: "Three SLA breaches this quarter — that's a $1,200 service credit we're owed, not a request."
Misuse: not tracking breaches against the clause, so credits go unclaimed by default rather than by choice.

**Termination for convenience vs. termination for cause** — Termination for convenience lets either party exit without alleging a breach, usually with a notice period; termination for cause requires a specific breach and often a cure period first.
In use: "We have termination for convenience with 30 days' notice — we don't need to prove they breached anything to walk."
Misuse: assuming all contracts allow exit without cause; many vendor agreements only permit termination for cause, which requires documenting a specific breach first.

**Right-sizing** — Adjusting provisioned capacity (space, seats, licenses) to match actual current utilization, in either direction.
In use: "Occupancy's been under 50% for two quarters — we're right-sizing the lease at the next break option."
Misuse: using it only to mean downsizing; right-sizing also means adding capacity when utilization is consistently over provisioned levels.

**Preventive vs. reactive maintenance** — Preventive maintenance is scheduled work performed before failure, based on time or usage intervals; reactive maintenance happens after a failure occurs.
In use: "We moved the HVAC to a preventive schedule after the second reactive emergency call this year cost more than two years of scheduled service."
Misuse: assuming preventive maintenance is always the right call regardless of asset age — near-end-of-life assets scheduled for replacement usually don't warrant further preventive investment.

**CMMS (computerized maintenance management system)** — Software that tracks assets, schedules preventive maintenance, and logs work orders and repair history.
In use: "Pull the CMMS history on that compressor — this is the third work order in six months."
Misuse: treating it as a ticketing tool only; its real value is the historical pattern across work orders that flags a recurring or worsening issue.

**Single point of failure (SPOF)** — Any component whose failure would take down a critical function with no fallback path.
In use: "Badge access runs through one server with no failover — that's a SPOF for building entry."
Misuse: labeling every non-redundant system a SPOF regardless of criticality; the term should be reserved for components whose failure would be catastrophic, not merely inconvenient.

**Redundancy vs. resilience** — Redundancy is a duplicate system ready to take over on failure; resilience is the broader capacity to absorb disruption and keep functioning, which can include redundancy but also processes, spare capacity, or manual workarounds.
In use: "We don't have redundant internet, but we're resilient — there's a documented failover to hotspot connectivity that keeps critical systems up."
Misuse: assuming resilience requires full redundancy everywhere; a documented manual workaround is often sufficient resilience for a lower-criticality system.

**Vendor consolidation** — Reducing the number of vendors serving a function, typically to gain pricing leverage or simplify management.
In use: "We consolidated from four cleaning vendors to one regional provider and picked up a 12% volume discount."
Misuse: treating consolidation as automatically good; it also concentrates risk and reduces negotiating leverage at the next renewal, which needs to be weighed against the savings.

**Occupancy cost per employee** — Total facilities cost (rent, utilities, services) divided by headcount, used to benchmark space efficiency over time or against comparable companies.
In use: "Occupancy cost per employee is up 18% year-over-year even though headcount grew — that's a utilization problem, not a rate problem."
Misuse: comparing occupancy cost per employee across companies without adjusting for market/city cost differences, which can swamp any real efficiency signal.

**Utilization rate** — The percentage of provisioned capacity (seats, space, licenses) actually in use, tracked against the original assumption behind the budget.
In use: "License utilization is 62% — we're paying for 240 seats and actively using 149."
Misuse: measuring utilization once at signing and never again; utilization drifts as headcount and work patterns change, and only a recurring check catches the drift.

**Evergreen (auto-renewal) clause** — A contract term that automatically renews the agreement for another term unless either party provides notice by a specified deadline.
In use: "The evergreen clause auto-renews us for another year unless we give 60 days' notice — that deadline is next month."
Misuse: not calendaring the notice deadline, which turns a decision that should be made deliberately into one made by default through inaction.

**Deferred maintenance** — Maintenance work identified but postponed, typically for budget reasons, with a cost that compounds the longer it's deferred.
In use: "That's $8K of deferred maintenance from last year that's now a $30K emergency repair."
Misuse: treating deferral as cost-neutral (just moving spend to next year) rather than cost-compounding, which is what it actually is for most physical-asset issues.
