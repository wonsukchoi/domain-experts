# Vocabulary

### Blotching
Uneven stain color absorption across a wood surface, caused by variation in wood porosity or density, distinct from uneven application technique.
**In use:** "That's blotching from the wood's own grain variation — a wash coat would have prevented it, not a steadier hand."
**Common misuse:** Attributing blotching to careless staining technique alone, when it's frequently a wood-porosity issue requiring pre-treatment rather than more careful brushing or wiping.

### Wash coat / wood conditioner
A diluted sealer applied before staining to partially seal wood pores and even out absorption on blotch-prone species.
**In use:** "This is pine — wash coat it before staining or expect blotchy color."
**Common misuse:** Skipping this pre-treatment step on species known to blotch, assuming careful stain application alone will produce even color.

### Flash-off time (finish application)
The time allowed for solvent evaporation between coats before the next coat is applied, distinct from full cure time.
**In use:** "Give it the full flash-off time before recoating — cutting it short traps solvent under the next layer."
**Common misuse:** Compressing flash-off time to speed up a finishing schedule, trapping solvent and risking poor intercoat adhesion or blistering.

### Grit progression (sanding)
The sequence of increasingly fine sandpaper grits used to prepare a wood surface, each step needing to fully remove the prior grit's scratch pattern before advancing.
**In use:** "Don't jump from 120 to 220 — that skips a step and leaves 120-grit scratches the finish won't hide."
**Common misuse:** Skipping a grit step to save time, leaving scratches that a clear finish won't hide and that become visible once the finish is cured.

### Full cure (vs. surface-dry)
The state at which a finish has completed its full chemical/physical hardening process, distinct from merely feeling dry to the touch shortly after application.
**In use:** "It's surface-dry, but full cure on this product takes another week before it's ready to rub out."
**Common misuse:** Treating a surface-dry finish as ready for rubbing out or handling, when full cure — often taking substantially longer — is required for those subsequent steps to succeed.

### Rubbing out / sheen leveling
The final abrasive process, using rubbing compounds or fine abrasives, applied to a fully cured finish to achieve a uniform, consistent sheen across the piece.
**In use:** "Rub it out once it's fully cured, not before — abrading it too early leaves swirl marks."
**Common misuse:** Attempting to rub out a finish before it's fully cured, producing swirl marks or an uneven result from abrading a still-soft film.

### Blushing (finish defect)
A white or cloudy haze appearing in a finish film, commonly caused by moisture trapped during application or cure under high-humidity conditions.
**In use:** "That blush is from the humidity today, not a bad batch of lacquer — adjust the schedule, not the product."
**Common misuse:** Assuming blushing indicates a defective finish product, when it's frequently a symptom of applying the product under humidity conditions outside its intended range without schedule adjustment.

### Grain raising
The roughening of wood fibers that occurs when water-based finishes or stains are applied, requiring an additional light sanding step after the first coat to achieve a smooth final surface.
**In use:** "First coat of the water-based finish is on — now do the grain-raising sand before the second coat."
**Common misuse:** Skipping the post-first-coat sanding step specific to water-based products, leaving a rougher final texture than an oil-based finish process would produce.

### Film build
The cumulative thickness of applied topcoat layers, built up coat by coat, which determines both the finish's protective properties and its final appearance and depth.
**In use:** "Film build's a bit thin for this application — one more coat before we move to rubbing out."
**Common misuse:** Judging finish adequacy by coat count alone without considering whether each coat actually achieved adequate, even film thickness, or assuming more coats always improve the result regardless of technique.

### Technical data sheet (TDS)
A finish manufacturer's document specifying application conditions, coat timing, cure time, and other product-specific parameters.
**In use:** "Checked the TDS for this new lacquer — its cure time is longer than what we used for the old product."
**Common misuse:** Applying a generic or remembered finishing schedule to a new or different finish product without checking its specific TDS for application and cure requirements that may differ from a previously used product.
