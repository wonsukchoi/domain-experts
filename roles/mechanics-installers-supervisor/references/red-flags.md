# Red flags

Smell tests a shop supervisor should catch inside the first pass of a month-end or weekly review, before a comeback pattern, a wage exposure, or a safety gap becomes expensive.

## 1. Shop-wide comeback rate above ~3% of repair orders

**Usually means:** either a systemic training gap across multiple technicians, or a bad parts lot that's hitting several job types at once — a shop-wide elevation, unlike a single technician's, usually isn't a technique problem for one person.

**First question:** "Break the comebacks down by technician and by job/part type — is this spread evenly or concentrated?"

**Data to pull:** comeback log segmented by technician and job category for the trailing 90 days; parts-return log for the same window; whether the rate moved after a supplier or parts-brand change.

## 2. One technician's efficiency above ~130% with no comeback-rate review attached

**Usually means:** either a genuinely strong technician, or comebacks are being generated that the efficiency number can't see — efficiency measures billed hours against clocked hours, not repair durability.

**First question:** "What's this technician's comeback rate by job category, not just their shop-wide average?"

**Data to pull:** technician-level comeback rate segmented by job type; whether any of their recent comebacks share a part number or failure mode.

## 3. A single technician's comeback rate on one job category above ~5%

**Usually means:** a technique gap specific to that repair, a rushed job under schedule pressure, or a tool/equipment calibration issue at their bay.

**First question:** "Pull the last five jobs of this type this technician did — same procedure, same tools, same result each time?"

**Data to pull:** the technician's certification record for that job category; the specific procedure spec (torque, tolerance) versus what was logged on the RO; bay/tool assignment history.

## 4. Comeback rate elevated across multiple technicians on the same part SKU

**Usually means:** a supplier batch or lot defect, not a shop-side quality problem — but it gets misdiagnosed as a training issue if nobody checks whether the elevation is technician-specific or SKU-specific.

**First question:** "Is every technician who installed this SKU seeing elevated comebacks, or just one?"

**Data to pull:** lot/batch numbers from the parts-return log; comeback rate on that SKU broken out by technician; whether the supplier has an open recall or quality bulletin on that part number.

## 5. Warranty-rate work rising as a share of total RO volume with no effective-labor-rate check

**Usually means:** the shop is filling capacity with lower-reimbursement warranty work without noticing the effective labor rate (total labor sales ÷ hours billed) compressing against the door rate.

**First question:** "What's our effective labor rate this month versus the door rate, and how much of the gap is warranty mix?"

**Data to pull:** RO mix by pay type (customer-pay, warranty, internal); effective labor rate trend over the trailing six months; warranty reimbursement schedule versus customer-pay rate for the same job categories.

## 6. Comeback repairs consistently closed with no root-cause code

**Usually means:** the shop is treating comebacks as a cost center to clear quickly rather than a data source, which guarantees the same pattern recurs because nobody's tracking it.

**First question:** "What's the root-cause field say on the last ten comeback ROs — or is it blank?"

**Data to pull:** comeback ROs with root-cause field populated versus blank over the trailing quarter; whether any technician or job type shows a disproportionate share of blank entries.

## 7. A technician's flat-rate pay for a period falls below the state minimum-wage floor for hours clocked

**Usually means:** a slow week, a heavy diagnostic/no-charge-comeback load, or a scheduling problem that's about to become a wage-and-hour exposure, not just a pay concern.

**First question:** "What did this technician actually clock this period, and does their flat-rate total divided by those hours clear the state floor?"

**Data to pull:** clocked hours versus flat-rate hours billed for the pay period; the specific state's piece-rate/flat-rate true-up and rest-break compensation rules; whether a true-up payment was issued.

## 8. Multiple technicians on one vehicle with a single lock applied

**Usually means:** a lockout/tagout program that exists on paper but isn't being followed at the bay — the most common near-miss precursor in a multi-technician shop.

**First question:** "Who applied their own lock, and who's working off someone else's?"

**Data to pull:** the shift's LOTO log against the day's job assignments; whether the shop's written program addresses group lockout at all.

## 9. Multi-point inspection sign-off rate at or near 100% with zero declined items

**Usually means:** the MPI is being checked off from habit rather than performed, or a technician is skipping it under schedule pressure to protect their efficiency number.

**First question:** "Pull this technician's last twenty MPIs — what's the declined-item rate compared to the shop average?"

**Data to pull:** MPI declined-item rate by technician; correlation between a technician's efficiency ranking and their MPI decline rate (an inverse relationship is the tell).

## 10. A technician assigned to a job category above their certification level

**Usually means:** a scheduling shortcut taken during a busy period, or a supervisor unaware the technician's ASE certification doesn't cover that repair category.

**First question:** "What's this technician's current certification level for this specific job category, and who approved the assignment?"

**Data to pull:** ASE certification records against job-category assignment for the trailing month; comeback rate on jobs assigned outside a technician's certified category.
