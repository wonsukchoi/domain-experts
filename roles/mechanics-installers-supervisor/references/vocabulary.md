# Vocabulary

Terms generalists conflate or use loosely that a shop supervisor keeps sharply separate.

## Technician efficiency vs. productivity

**Efficiency** is flat-rate hours billed ÷ clock hours worked on repair orders — how fast a technician completes billed work relative to the time they spent. **Productivity** is hours actually worked on repair orders ÷ total hours paid (including idle, training, or unassigned time) — how much of a technician's paid time is applied to billable work at all.

*In use:* "His efficiency is 130%, but productivity's only 78% — he's fast when he's on a job, he's just not on a job enough of the day."

*Common misuse:* using the two interchangeably, or citing only efficiency as "how good" a technician is — a technician can be highly efficient during the hours they work and still be unproductive because of scheduling gaps, waiting on parts, or excessive idle time.

## Comeback vs. warranty claim

A **comeback** is the shop's own redo of work it performed, at its own cost (or the technician's, per the shop's rework-pay policy) — a self-inflicted repeat repair. A **warranty claim** is a manufacturer-covered repair for a parts or factory defect, reimbursed by the OEM at its own schedule and rate, independent of who installed the part.

*In use:* "That's a comeback, not a warranty claim — we did the job wrong, the manufacturer didn't build the part wrong."

*Common misuse:* logging a comeback as "warranty" to avoid it counting against the shop's or technician's comeback rate, which erases the data needed to catch a technique problem.

## Effective labor rate (ELR) vs. door rate

The **door rate** is the labor rate quoted to a customer-pay job. The **effective labor rate** is total labor sales ÷ total hours billed across all pay types (customer-pay, warranty, internal) — it's always at or below the door rate once warranty and internal work, reimbursed lower, are mixed in.

*In use:* "Door rate's $165, but our effective labor rate is down to $138 this month — warranty mix is up to 30% of volume."

*Common misuse:* reporting the door rate as if it reflects actual shop labor revenue, which overstates real profitability when warranty or discounted internal work makes up a meaningful share of volume.

## Flat rate / flat-rate hour

A pre-set number of hours a specific repair is allowed to take, per a published labor guide (Mitchell1, ALLDATA) or the manufacturer's own time standard — used to bill the customer and, commonly, to pay the technician, regardless of the actual clock time the job took.

*In use:* "The water pump job books at 2.4 flat-rate hours — he did it in 1.8 clock hours, so that job alone put him at 133% efficiency."

*Common misuse:* assuming flat-rate hours and clock hours are the same thing, or that a technician "worked" the flat-rate hours rather than the actual clock time — the gap between the two is exactly what efficiency measures.

## Parts markup matrix vs. flat cost-plus

A **markup matrix** applies a sliding markup percentage to parts based on cost bands (e.g., higher percentage markup on cheap parts, lower percentage on expensive ones) to keep margin consistent across a wide price range. A flat **cost-plus** approach applies one markup percentage regardless of part cost, which erodes margin on cheap parts and overprices expensive ones relative to the matrix approach.

*In use:* "We're on the matrix — 50% on the $20–100 band, down to 20% above $500 — not a flat 35% across the board."

*Common misuse:* assuming a single "shop markup percentage" applies uniformly; most shops with a deliberate parts-pricing strategy use a banded matrix specifically because a flat rate misprices both ends of the cost range.

## Lockout/tagout (LOTO) vs. simply "powered off"

**Lockout/tagout** is the OSHA-defined procedure (29 CFR 1910.147) of physically isolating an energy source with a lock the worker controls and verifying a zero-energy state before servicing — not just switching a device off, which can be inadvertently re-energized by anyone with access to the switch or key.

*In use:* "Turning the key off isn't lockout — he needs his own lock on the isolating device before he's under that lift."

*Common misuse:* treating "the vehicle's off" or "the breaker's flipped" as equivalent to lockout; without a technician-controlled lock and a verification step, there's no protection against someone else re-energizing the source.

## Redo / rework pay

The reduced or zero flat-rate pay a technician receives for correcting their own comeback, as distinct from full flat-rate pay for new billable work — governed by the shop's written policy and bounded by state wage law on minimum-wage true-up for piece-rate pay.

*In use:* "Comeback redo pays at half his normal flat-rate equivalent, per policy — it's not free labor to the shop, but it's not full rate either."

*Common misuse:* assuming redo time is simply unpaid; in most states that risks a minimum-wage violation once clocked hours and total flat-rate pay for the period are reconciled.

## Core charge

A refundable deposit charged on a part with a reusable core (starters, alternators, some brake calipers) that the customer or shop gets back on returning the old unit — separate from the part's sale price and not part of the labor or markup calculation.

*In use:* "The alternator's $180 plus a $40 core charge — comes off the invoice once the old one's returned to the supplier."

*Common misuse:* folding the core charge into the parts markup calculation, which overstates the actual parts margin on that job.

## Diagnostic time vs. flat-rate repair time

**Diagnostic time** is billed separately (often at an hourly or half-hour-increment rate) for identifying the problem, before any flat-rate repair time applies to fixing it. Flat-rate repair time only starts once the diagnosis determines what repair is being performed.

*In use:* "Book 0.5 hours diagnostic before you touch the flat-rate repair time on the wiring job — that's not part of the flat rate."

*Common misuse:* absorbing diagnostic time into the repair's flat-rate hours, which understates true labor cost on complex driveability or electrical jobs where diagnosis can exceed the repair itself.

## RO aging

How long a repair order has sat open (parts on order, awaiting customer authorization, or awaiting technician assignment) without moving to completion — a shop-flow metric distinct from any single technician's efficiency or productivity.

*In use:* "We've got six ROs aged past five days waiting on backordered parts — that's tying up bay space that isn't showing up in anyone's efficiency number."

*Common misuse:* treating RO aging purely as a parts-department problem; a supervisor who doesn't track it can misread a bay-capacity shortage as a staffing or scheduling problem when it's actually parts-driven.
