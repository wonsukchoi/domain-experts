# Vocabulary

### Glass content
The percentage of a cured laminate's total weight that consists of reinforcing fiber (glass), as opposed to resin — the primary determinant of a hand-laid composite's actual strength.
**In use:** "Glass content came back at 34.5%, right in our 30-40% target band for this hand layup."
**Common misuse:** Assuming more resin and a thicker-looking laminate means a stronger part — a higher resin proportion actually lowers glass content, and since fiber carries the structural load, a resin-rich laminate is weaker per unit weight, not stronger.

### Wet-out
The state of a fiberglass ply being fully saturated with resin, with no dry (unsaturated) fiber remaining and no trapped air between fiber and resin.
**In use:** "That section isn't fully wet out — see the pale, dry-looking fiber under the surface sheen."
**Common misuse:** Judging wet-out by surface appearance alone (a glossy, wet-looking surface) without checking that saturation is complete through the ply's full thickness — a surface can look wetted while fiber underneath remains dry.

### Exotherm
The heat generated by a curing resin's chemical reaction, which increases with catalyst concentration and layup thickness, and which must not exceed levels that damage the laminate.
**In use:** "Thick section like this one, we're monitoring exotherm with a probe — too much heat here and we risk cracking."
**Common misuse:** Treating faster cure (driven by more catalyst or heat) as a straightforwardly good thing — excessive exotherm, especially in thick sections, can cause cracking, warping, or a runaway reaction, making cure speed a parameter to control, not maximize.

### Catalyst ratio (temperature-adjusted)
The proportion of catalyst/hardener mixed with resin, which manufacturers specify at a reference temperature and adjust (typically reduced at higher ambient temperature, increased at lower) to maintain a target working/gel time.
**In use:** "Ninety-five degrees in the shop today — we're dropping to 1.0% catalyst per the manufacturer's table, not our usual 1.5%."
**Common misuse:** Using a single "standard" catalyst ratio regardless of ambient conditions — the ratio's purpose is achieving a target gel time, and that target requires adjustment as temperature and humidity shift from the datasheet's reference condition.

### Ply schedule / layup schedule
The specified sequence of fiber layers, their orientation, and material type/weight for a composite part, functioning as a structural specification tied to the part's load path.
**In use:** "Ply schedule calls for 0°/90° alternating — that's not arbitrary, it's what gives this panel strength in both directions."
**Common misuse:** Treating ply orientation as a material-layout choice that can be adjusted for convenience or efficiency — orientation is chosen to match the part's actual load-bearing requirements, and deviating from it changes the part's real structural performance even if the finished part looks identical.

### Gel time / working time
The period after resin and catalyst are mixed during which the resin remains workable (can be applied and worked into fiber) before it begins to set and can no longer be manipulated.
**In use:** "Gel time's down to eight minutes in this heat — we need smaller batches or we'll lose material mid-application."
**Common misuse:** Treating a resin datasheet's stated gel time as fixed regardless of ambient conditions — actual working time shifts meaningfully with temperature and humidity away from the datasheet's stated reference condition.

### Post-cure
An additional controlled-temperature cure period (often in an oven) applied after initial gel/cure, used to bring a laminate to its full specified design strength beyond what ambient-temperature cure alone achieves.
**In use:** "It's tack-free and handleable, but it hasn't hit design strength until post-cure's complete."
**Common misuse:** Treating a laminate as fully cured and ready for service once it's tack-free and can be handled — tack-free and handleable happens well before full design strength is reached, and post-cure (where specified) is a separate, required step.

### Barcol hardness test
A portable hardness test used to verify a cured composite laminate has reached an adequate degree of cure, correlating hardness reading to cure completeness for a given resin system.
**In use:** "Barcol reading's at spec — that confirms this section is fully cured, not just surface-hard."
**Common misuse:** Relying on a visual or tactile "feels hard" assessment instead of an actual Barcol reading — surface hardness can develop before the laminate is cured through its full thickness, and the test is meant to catch that gap.

### Trapped air / void
Air pockets left between fiber layers or within resin during layup, which become permanent defects once the resin cures around them, acting as stress concentration points and moisture-intrusion paths.
**In use:** "Working the roller from center outward on every pass — that's how we keep air from getting trapped as we build up plies."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a small amount of trapped air is a cosmetic, inconsequential issue — even a small void concentrates stress and creates a path for moisture, and its consequence scales with the part's structural criticality, not just its visible size.

### Resin-rich
A laminate condition where the resin-to-glass ratio exceeds the target, meaning more resin was used than needed to wet out the fiber, resulting in excess weight and reduced structural performance relative to properly saturated fiber at the correct ratio.
**In use:** "This section's resin-rich — that's not extra protection, it's extra weight and a weaker glass-to-resin ratio than spec."
**Common misuse:** Treating a resin-rich laminate as "extra safe" because it has more resin — the fiber, not the resin, carries structural load, and a resin-rich condition actually reduces the laminate's strength-to-weight ratio relative to a correctly proportioned laminate.
