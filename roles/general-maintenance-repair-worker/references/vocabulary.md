# Vocabulary

Terms generalists blur that a practitioner keeps sharply separate — each with the definition, a sentence a practitioner would actually say, and the common misuse.

### Preventive vs. reactive vs. predictive maintenance

**Preventive (PM)** is scheduled work done on a fixed interval regardless of current condition (change the filter every 90 days). **Reactive** is repair after failure. **Predictive** is condition-triggered work based on a measured signal (vibration, temperature, run-hours) rather than a calendar or a failure.

*In use:* "We're not behind on PM, we're behind on *predictive* — the compressor's been running hot for two weeks and nobody pulled the trend before it tripped."

*Common misuse:* calling any non-emergency work "preventive," including work that was actually triggered by an early symptom (that's predictive) or work that was simply caught before it got worse (that's still reactive, just early reactive).

### Handyman exemption

The dollar threshold (commonly $300–$1,000, combined labor and material) below which a state allows unlicensed repair work, subject to carve-outs — it does not apply once a permit is required or the work touches gas, structural elements, or in most states, electrical/plumbing systems beyond simple device replacement.

*In use:* "We're at $420 on labor and material, under California's $500 line — but it needs a permit for the vent reroute, so the exemption doesn't save us here anyway."

*Common misuse:* treating the dollar number as the only test, or assuming a threshold learned in one state applies in another — these vary by state and by trade.

### Permit-triggering work

Work that requires a building/mechanical/electrical/plumbing permit from the local jurisdiction regardless of cost, which automatically voids any handyman exemption for that job.

*In use:* "Doesn't matter that it's under $500 — a water heater swap needs a permit here, so it's a licensed plumber's job either way."

*Common misuse:* assuming "small job" and "permit-exempt job" are the same category; a permit requirement is about the type of work, not its price.

### Habitability

The legal minimum a rental unit must meet to be considered fit to live in — heat, hot water, working plumbing, weatherproofing, functioning smoke/CO detectors — defined by state and local housing code, with its own response-time obligations independent of the property's internal SLA.

*In use:* "This no-heat ticket isn't just urgent by our tier system, it's a habitability violation past the 24-hour mark — that's a legal clock, not just ours."

*Common misuse:* treating habitability as a synonym for "important" rather than a specific statutory category with its own compliance deadlines that can expose the property to liability separate from the repair itself.

### Root cause vs. symptom fix (band-aid repair)

A **root-cause fix** resolves the underlying condition causing a fault; a **symptom fix** (band-aid) resolves the immediate presenting problem without addressing why it happened, so it recurs.

*In use:* "Swapping the GFCI is the band-aid — the root cause is the shower's exhaust fan pushing moisture straight into that outlet box."

*Common misuse:* closing a work order as "fixed" after a symptom fix without flagging it as temporary, which erases the recurrence pattern from the CMMS history and makes the third occurrence look unrelated to the first two.

### Lockout/tagout (LOTO)

The practice of physically isolating and tagging equipment or a circuit so it cannot be re-energized while it's being inspected or awaiting a licensed sub, distinct from just switching something off.

*In use:* "Breaker's locked out and tagged, not just flipped — nobody resets that pump until the electrician signs off."

*Common misuse:* treating "I turned it off" as equivalent to LOTO; without the physical lock and tag, anyone on-site can re-energize it, which is exactly the scenario LOTO exists to prevent.

### Work-order aging / backlog

**Aging** is how long an individual ticket has sat open; **backlog** is the aggregate volume of open tickets, usually expressed in tech-hours of estimated work still owed.

*In use:* "The ticket itself is only three days old, but the *backlog* is four weeks deep — that's why it's still in queue."

*Common misuse:* using "backlog" to describe one old ticket (that's aging) or citing backlog as a raw ticket count instead of tech-hours, which hides whether the volume is actually staffable.

### Scope creep (on a work order)

Performing more work than the ticket authorized or than falls inside the role's licensed boundary, usually because the tech is already on-site and "might as well."

*In use:* "The ticket was a faucet washer — replacing the whole shutoff valve because it looked old is scope creep, and if that valve's on a gas line assembly it's also a boundary problem."

*Common misuse:* treating scope creep as only a cost/authorization issue; when the extra work crosses a trade boundary, it's also a licensing exposure, not just an approvals one.

### Response SLA tiers (emergency / urgent / routine / cosmetic)

The response-time commitment attached to a hazard tier — emergency under an hour, urgent 24–48 hours, routine 3–5 business days, cosmetic within the next PM cycle — set by the ticket's classification, not by tenant escalation pressure.

*In use:* "It's tagged urgent because one AC zone is down in July, not because the tenant called twice — the tier doesn't change with call volume."

*Common misuse:* letting a tenant's persistence or a manager's mood re-tier a ticket instead of re-tiering only when new information changes the actual hazard classification.

### Deferred maintenance

Known needed maintenance that has been postponed rather than scheduled, distinct from a routine PM item simply waiting for its next scheduled date.

*In use:* "That roof flashing has been deferred maintenance for two winters — it's not on any PM schedule, it's a known gap someone chose not to fund."

*Common misuse:* using "deferred maintenance" to describe normal PM cadence ("the filter's due next month") rather than work that's known-needed and unfunded/unscheduled.

### Punch list

A finalized, itemized list of remaining defects or incomplete items tied to a specific project close-out (a renovation, a unit turn), distinct from the ongoing general work-order queue.

*In use:* "Unit 208's punch list from the turnover has three items left — that's separate from anything new coming through the regular queue."

*Common misuse:* using "punch list" for the everyday maintenance backlog; a punch list is scoped to one project's completion, not an ongoing operational queue.

### Trade-specific licensing threshold

The project value or scope point at which a given trade (electrical, plumbing, HVAC/refrigerant) legally requires a licensed practitioner, which is often lower — or entirely absent as a dollar concept — compared to the general handyman exemption.

*In use:* "The handyman exemption might cover $500 of general work, but there's no dollar exemption at all for opening a refrigerant circuit — that's federal, not state, and it's zero regardless of job size."

*Common misuse:* assuming one state dollar threshold covers every trade uniformly; gas and refrigerant work in particular often have no small-job exemption at any price.

### Bonding vs. grounding

**Grounding** connects electrical equipment to earth to provide a safe path for fault current. **Bonding** connects separate metal parts (like a pool's ladder, deck, and pump housing) together so they're at the same electrical potential, preventing shock from potential difference between touchable surfaces — a distinct requirement under NEC Article 680 for pools.

*In use:* "The pump's grounded fine — the trip is a bonding-grid continuity issue between the ladder and the deck rail, that's an electrician call under 680."

*Common misuse:* treating "grounded" as covering pool safety compliance; bonding is a separate, additional requirement that a general ground check won't catch.

### Wrench time / tech utilization

The share of a maintenance tech's paid hours actually spent on hands-on repair/PM work, as opposed to travel, waiting on parts, paperwork, or admin — used to judge whether a backlog is a staffing problem or an efficiency problem.

*In use:* "Wrench time's only around 55% this month — before we ask for more headcount, let's find out how much of that gap is parts delay versus routing."

*Common misuse:* citing low wrench time as proof of understaffing without first checking whether the loss is structural (parts lead time, building layout) rather than a headcount gap that more hires would actually fix.
