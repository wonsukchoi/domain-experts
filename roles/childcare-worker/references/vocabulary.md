# Childcare Worker — Vocabulary

### Active supervision
Continuous visual/auditory monitoring positioned to see and physically reach every child, not merely being present in the same room.
**In use:** "She was in the room, but she wasn't actively supervising — she was facing away, filling out forms."
**Common misuse:** Treated as synonymous with "an adult is present," ignoring positioning, sightline, and attentiveness.

### Ratio
The maximum number of children one staff member may supervise, set by age band and varying by state/jurisdiction.
**In use:** "We're at 1:4 for the infants, which is the governing ratio for the whole room right now."
**Common misuse:** Applied per age band independently instead of applying the youngest-present band's ratio to the full mixed-age group.

### Governing band
In a mixed-age group, the youngest age band present, whose ratio applies to the entire group's headcount.
**In use:** "Even though most of the room is preschoolers, the two infants make infant ratio the governing band."
**Common misuse:** Assumed the ratio for the majority age group applies, rather than the strictest (youngest) band present.

### Incident report vs. accident report
Some programs distinguish these terms by severity or by whether the event resulted from a program-level factor; some use them interchangeably. Either way, the operative test is mechanism and body area, not how the child is currently behaving.
**In use:** "Even though he stopped crying in a minute, it's a fall from height with a head involved — that's a report, not a mention at pickup."
**Common misuse:** Downgraded to a verbal mention because the child recovered quickly, rather than being decided by mechanism at the time of the incident.

### Mandated reporter
A legal designation (most childcare workers qualify in most US states) requiring reporting of suspected child abuse or neglect to the appropriate authority, with specific triggers and no discretion to "wait and see."
**In use:** "This is a mandated-reporting situation — I'm not deciding whether to escalate, I'm required to."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a judgment call about whether the situation seems "serious enough," rather than a legal trigger with defined criteria.

### Redirection
Guiding a child toward an acceptable alternative activity or behavior in the moment, rather than correcting or lecturing during the incident itself.
**In use:** "Redirect him to the sensory table now; the conversation about sharing happens later, calmly."
**Common misuse:** Confused with distraction alone — redirection specifically offers a related, acceptable alternative, not just a change of subject.

### Authorized pickup list
The program's documented list of individuals permitted to pick up a specific child, which staff must check against every release, not just for unfamiliar faces.
**In use:** "He's not on the authorized list — I need a same-day update from a parent before I can release her to him."
**Common misuse:** Applied only to strangers, skipped for someone staff recognizes informally (a family friend, a relative who's picked up before but isn't listed).

### Line-of-sight
The physical positioning requirement that a supervising adult can see (or, for some age bands, hear) every child under their care at all times.
**In use:** "Split the group so I keep the toddlers in line-of-sight while you take the preschoolers to the sink."
**Common misuse:** Treated as satisfied by being in the same building or general area, rather than actual unobstructed visual/auditory contact.

### Headcount reconciliation
The practice of recounting children physically present against the expected roster at every transition, not relying on the morning sign-in count.
**In use:** "Headcount reconciliation at the door before we go outside — I counted nine, roster says ten."
**Common misuse:** Done once at drop-off and assumed to hold for the rest of the day.

### Behavior guidance
Non-punitive strategies (environmental changes, redirection, consistent routines) for managing challenging behavior in young children, distinct from discipline framed as punishment.
**In use:** "Our behavior-guidance approach here is changing the environment first — fewer kids at that activity — before addressing the individual child."
**Common misuse:** Used interchangeably with "discipline," implying a punitive rather than environmental/developmental framing.
