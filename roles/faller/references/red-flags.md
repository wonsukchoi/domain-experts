# Red flags

Smell tests a faller catches on the size-up walk-around, before the saw starts on a given tree. Format for each: what it usually means, the first question to ask, and the data to pull before the first cut.

### Trunk lean and crown-weighted hang direction disagree by more than ~15°

**Usually means:** the crown carries enough asymmetric mass (storm damage, lopsided limb growth, broken top) that trunk angle alone is no longer a reliable predictor of fall direction — the tree will drift toward the hang-weight-corrected line, not the flagged stake.

**First question:** "Has anyone actually stood back and read the crown's mass distribution, or are we going off the trunk angle alone?"

**Data to pull:** measured trunk lean angle, estimated crown skew angle and mass-center height, the resultant direction calculation from `playbook.md` worksheet 1.

### Calculated forward lean above roughly 15% grade-equivalent

**Usually means:** the lean has moved past what a stacked wedge pair can reliably hold on a hand-felled tree — continuing to add wedges is working against a physical limit, not a technique gap.

**First question:** "Is this tree still in wedge-correctable range, or are we past the point where hand-felling is the right call?"

**Data to pull:** tree height, measured lean angle, forward lean distance and grade-equivalent percentage, availability of cable/tractor-line assist or mechanized equipment as an alternative.

### Visible tension-wood signs (fiber pull, bulged bark) with heavy forward lean

**Usually means:** elevated barber-chair risk — the trunk can split vertically ahead of the backcut reaching the hinge, especially in fast-growing Douglas-fir or southern pine under heavy forward lean.

**First question:** "Given this lean and this species, are we cutting an open-face notch with a bore cut, or defaulting to a straight backcut we haven't earned here?"

**Data to pull:** species, lean angle, notch type planned, whether a bore cut and trigger/strap sequence is in the cutting plan.

### Snag, broken top, or hung limb within the hazard tree's own height of the work area or escape route

**Usually means:** vibration from this or an adjacent cut can be enough to dislodge material that's been sitting loose through prior disturbance without falling — "it hasn't fallen yet" is not evidence it's stable.

**First question:** "Has this hazard tree been assessed and cleared by someone with the authority to drop or rig it, or are we just working around it?"

**Data to pull:** hazard tree's height and estimated distance from the work area and both escape routes; whether it's logged/flagged as a hazard tree; who has signed off on the exclusion zone.

### Two fallers working closer than two tree-lengths apart

**Usually means:** a felling-area spacing violation under OSHA 1910.266 — either faller's tree can reach the other's position if it goes off-plan.

**First question:** "What's the taller of the two trees' height, and is that the actual spacing between us right now?"

**Data to pull:** measured or paced distance between fallers, height of the taller tree in the area, whether the tree between them has been dropped first.

### Escape routes not physically cleared before the first cut

**Usually means:** the corridor that looks passable standing still — brush, slash, uneven ground — will not be passable at a run while watching the treetop instead of the ground, and this is the step that gets skipped under time or volume pressure.

**First question:** "Has anyone actually walked both escape routes today, or are we assuming they're still clear from the last tree?"

**Data to pull:** time since routes were last walked, visible obstructions (slash piles, stumps, uneven ground), whether routes are still 45° off the current (not original) backcut line.

### Decay, cavity, or ring shake exceeding roughly a third of the cross-section at the stump

**Usually means:** the tree's actual holding wood is less than the visible diameter suggests, which changes both the hinge-thickness math and the risk that the tree fails unpredictably rather than on the planned hinge.

**First question:** "How much of this cross-section do we think is sound, and does the hinge plan account for it or assume full diameter?"

**Data to pull:** visible decay/cavity extent at the stump, species-typical decay pattern, adjusted hinge dimensions if the sound-wood diameter is meaningfully smaller than the measured DBH.

### Tree hung up in a neighboring tree after the backcut

**Usually means:** the tree didn't reach the ground and is now storing energy against the neighboring tree — a hazard that kills people specifically because the instinct is to walk under it or cut the tree holding it up to "finish the job."

**First question:** "What's the plan to bring this down mechanically, and has everyone been told not to walk under it in the meantime?"

**Data to pull:** which tree is holding the hang, available winch/cant-hook/mechanized options on site, exclusion-zone boundary communicated to the rest of the crew.

### Sustained wind conditions during felling operations

**Usually means:** wind adds an unpredictable lateral force on top of the calculated lean/hang-weight direction, and can also be the trigger that drops an otherwise-stable hazard tree's loose material.

**First question:** "Is this still felling weather for the trees we've got queued, or does the plan change to limbing/bucking already-down wood until it settles?"

**Data to pull:** current sustained wind speed and gusts, crew's stated wind threshold for this unit, whether standing hazard trees are in the area.

### New or recently certified faller assigned to work alone on a tree with any of the above flags present

**Usually means:** the certification tier hasn't yet covered this specific hazard combination, and solo assignment removes the buddy-system check that catches a missed read before the first cut, not after.

**First question:** "Is this within this faller's current certification tier, or does it need a partner or a more experienced sign-off before the first cut?"

**Data to pull:** faller's certification tier and time in role, company/agency policy on solo work for this hazard class, availability of a partner or supervisor for this specific tree.
