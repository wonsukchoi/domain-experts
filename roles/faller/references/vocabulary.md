# Vocabulary

Terms generalists conflate that a faller keeps sharply separate — because the distinction changes where the tree actually goes, what's legal to do near it, or what a crew report is actually saying happened.

## Trunk lean vs. hang weight

**Trunk lean** is the stem's angle from vertical, read with a plumb bob or inclinometer. **Hang weight** is where the tree's actual mass — crown, top damage, one-sided limb growth — pulls the center of gravity, which can point meaningfully off the trunk's angle.

*Practitioner usage:* "Trunk's only leaning five degrees toward the flag, but the crown's lopsided enough that hang weight's going to put this thing thirty degrees left of where the stake says."

*Common misuse:* reading trunk lean alone and treating it as the fall direction — the crown is doing more work than the stem angle on any tree with an asymmetric or storm-damaged top.

## Open-face notch vs. conventional notch vs. Humboldt notch

**Conventional notch** is a 45° face cut, standard for straightforward lean. **Open-face notch** widens the angle to 70–90°, keeping the notch closed longer through the fall arc for more directional control — used on heavy lean or tension-wood species. **Humboldt notch** inverts the cut (bottom angled up from the stump) to save merchantable butt-log length in commercial timber; directional behavior is similar to conventional.

*Practitioner usage:* "This fir's got enough forward lean and tension wood that I'm not trusting a forty-five — open-face, ninety degrees, bore cut behind it."

*Common misuse:* treating notch angle as a cosmetic or habitual choice rather than a control decision tied to lean and species tension-wood behavior.

## Hinge vs. backcut vs. bore cut

**Hinge** is the uncut wood left between the notch and the backcut that steers the tree as it falls. **Backcut** is the cut made from the back of the tree toward the hinge, releasing the tree once the hinge is set. **Bore cut** (plunge cut) enters the trunk from the side, cuts the hinge to full width first, then works backward, leaving a trigger/strap that's cut last — used to set hinge dimensions precisely without the moment-of-failure risk a straight backcut carries on problem trees.

*Practitioner usage:* "Straight backcut's fine on this one — no tension wood, moderate lean. Bore cut's going in on the next one, that lean's heavy enough I want the hinge set before anything's released."

*Common misuse:* treating the backcut as the directional cut — by the time the backcut starts, the notch and hinge have already decided where the tree goes; the backcut only releases it.

## Trigger / strap

The narrow band of uncut wood left on the back of the trunk during a bore cut, holding the tree in place until it's deliberately cut last, once escape routes and crew clearance are confirmed.

*Practitioner usage:* "Hinge is set, trigger's about two inches — nobody moves until I've confirmed both escape routes are clear, then it comes out."

*Common misuse:* confusing the trigger with the hinge itself — the hinge steers the fall; the trigger is a timing device that controls exactly when the fall starts.

## Barber chair

A vertical splitting failure where the trunk tears upward ahead of the backcut reaching the hinge, typically on trees with heavy forward lean and tension wood — one of the most dangerous single failure modes in hand-felling because the split can kick the standing section of trunk back toward the faller.

*Practitioner usage:* "This one's a barber-chair candidate — heavy lean, tension wood in the fiber. Open-face and bore cut, not a straight backcut."

*Common misuse:* treating barber chair as a rare freak event rather than a predictable consequence of specific lean-plus-species conditions that the notch/cut choice is meant to manage.

## Widow-maker vs. snag vs. danger tree

**Widow-maker** is a specific loose or broken limb/top hung in a canopy, held only by other branches, that can fall with no warning. **Snag** is a standing dead tree, which may or may not carry a widow-maker. **Danger tree** (hazard tree) is the broader assessment category covering any standing tree — dead, damaged, or leaning unpredictably — that poses a falling-object or unpredictable-fall risk beyond a normal size-up.

*Practitioner usage:* "That snag's got a widow-maker hung about sixty feet up — it's a danger tree either way, but the loose top is the part that can come down without warning."

*Common misuse:* using the three terms interchangeably in a hazard report, which loses the distinction between "this whole tree could fall" and "this specific piece could drop with no warning" — the exclusion-zone response differs for each.

## Hung tree / hang-up

A felled tree that doesn't reach the ground, instead lodging against a standing neighbor and storing energy against it.

*Practitioner usage:* "We've got a hang-up on the ridge — don't cut the holding tree and don't walk under it, we're bringing it down with the winch."

*Common misuse:* treating a hung tree as "mostly down" and safe to walk under or finish by hand — it's storing energy exactly like a loaded spring and is one of the leading causes of struck-by fatalities in hand-felling.

## Escape route vs. exclusion zone

**Escape route** is the specific cleared path a faller retreats along after the trigger/backcut is released, sized and angled off the backcut line. **Exclusion zone** is the broader area kept clear of anyone else for the duration of the felling and any associated hazard (adjacent widow-maker, hung top).

*Practitioner usage:* "Escape routes are the two lanes off the backcut — exclusion zone is the whole quarter of the unit until this tree and that hung top on the neighbor are both down and checked."

*Common misuse:* treating the escape route as sufficient crew protection on its own — it protects the faller who's retreating, not bystanders or equipment elsewhere in the unit, which is what the exclusion zone is for.

## Two-tree-length rule

The OSHA 1910.266 requirement that fallers working the same area stay separated by at least two tree-lengths of the taller tree present, or that the tree between them be felled first.

*Practitioner usage:* "That tree's ninety feet — we need at least a hundred eighty between us, not just 'far enough that I can't see him.'"

*Common misuse:* judging spacing by sightline or habit rather than measuring or pacing it against the actual height of the tallest tree in the working area.

## Kickback zone

The upper quadrant of the chainsaw bar's tip, where contact with wood or another object can drive the bar back toward the operator faster than a chain brake or reaction time can reliably stop it.

*Practitioner usage:* "Watch your tip on that limb-over-limb cut — that's straight into the kickback zone if the bar rolls."

*Common misuse:* treating kickback as a defect of the saw or chain alone rather than a function of where and how the bar tip contacts material — reduced-kickback chain and a chain brake lower the risk but don't remove the operator's responsibility to keep the tip clear.
