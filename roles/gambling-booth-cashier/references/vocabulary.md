# Vocabulary

Working vocabulary a generalist reliably misuses. Format per term: definition, the sentence a practitioner would actually say, the common misuse.

### TITO (ticket-in, ticket-out)

The system where slot machines print a barcoded ticket for a cash-out instead of dispensing coins, and accept a ticket as a cash-equivalent to start play on another machine.

**In use:** "The ticket won't validate at the redemption unit — I'm not keying the printed amount, I'm calling a slot tech."

**Common misuse:** treating the printed dollar figure on the ticket as the source of truth. The barcode's record in the slot data system is authoritative; a ticket whose printed amount and barcode disagree is a fraud signature, not a rounding issue.

### Hand pay

A jackpot or win paid directly by a cashier or attendant in cash (or by voucher, in some properties) because it exceeds what the machine itself can pay out — either because it's above the machine's hopper capacity or above a reportable-win threshold requiring tax paperwork.

**In use:** "That's a $2,050 hand pay, over the W-2G line — I need two IDs before I release anything."

**Common misuse:** assuming any large win is automatically a hand pay. A win under the reportable threshold and under the hopper's physical capacity can pay straight from the machine; hand pay specifically means a human, not the machine, completes the payout.

### Fill / fill slip

The act (and the triplicate paperwork documenting it) of restocking a slot machine's hopper or bill acceptor with currency or tokens, counted and signed by two people before the machine reopens for play.

**In use:** "Machine's hopper is low — I need a fill slip and a second counter before I close this door."

**Common misuse:** treating a fill as a routine restock that can be documented after the fact. An unsigned or unwitnessed fill is exactly the gap that turns into an unexplainable variance days later, once nobody remembers the details.

### W-2G

The IRS form documenting a reportable gambling win, required at specific per-game thresholds (slots: wins of $2,000 or more for payouts after December 31, 2025, up from $1,200), completed with the patron's ID before or at the time of payout.

**In use:** "This hits the W-2G line — I need your ID and Social Security number before I can pay this out."

**Common misuse:** assuming the threshold is the same across all games. Slots, keno, and bingo each carry different reportable thresholds under the same form's instructions; the amount alone doesn't tell you whether a W-2G is required.

### MTL / ACL (Multiple Transaction Log / Action Control Log)

A property's internal log, kept below the federal $10,000 CTR line (commonly triggered at $2,500–$3,000), that records individual cash transactions so they can be aggregated by patron across windows and across a gaming day.

**In use:** "Log this one on the MTL — she already cashed out $1,100 at the cage earlier tonight, and this hand pay puts her over our internal line."

**Common misuse:** believing a single transaction under $10,000 never needs logging. The MTL exists precisely because no cashier or cage window sees a patron's full-day activity; the log is what makes aggregation possible at all.

### CTR (Currency Transaction Report)

The federal filing required under the Bank Secrecy Act (31 CFR Part 1021) when a patron's aggregated cash-in or cash-out exceeds $10,000 in a single gaming day.

**In use:** "Cage will file the CTR once compliance confirms the aggregate — my job downstream was making sure the MTL entry existed in the first place."

**Common misuse:** assuming the cashier or cage window personally files the CTR at the point of a large transaction. Filing is a compliance-department function done from aggregated logs; the floor role's job is making sure the underlying transaction data exists and is accurate.

### Structuring

Deliberately breaking a large cash transaction into smaller pieces, each below a reporting threshold, specifically to avoid triggering that report.

**In use:** "Three cash-outs of $2,400 each in twenty minutes from the same patron — that pattern gets flagged to compliance regardless of whether any single leg crossed the line."

**Common misuse:** thinking structuring only matters once the federal $10,000 CTR threshold is being dodged. A pattern of transactions engineered to stay under the property's own lower internal MTL threshold is still a structuring red flag worth escalating.

### Short pay

When a slot machine's hopper runs low and pays out less coin or cash than the win actually earned, requiring a cashier to manually make up the difference.

**In use:** "Meter shows a $40 win, hopper only paid $22 — I'm cutting a short-pay slip for the $18 difference before I hand her cash."

**Common misuse:** paying the difference in cash without documenting it as a formal short pay. That creates an unexplained cash-out with no slip behind it, indistinguishable from a shortage during a later audit.

### Imprest bank

A fixed-amount cash fund issued to a cashier at shift start that should return to that exact amount at shift end once every transaction is accounted for — the standard model for a booth's opening float.

**In use:** "My imprest is $2,500 — anything off that number at close has to trace to a slip, not just get written off."

**Common misuse:** treating the imprest total as a target to hit by any means (including quietly padding or shorting the count) rather than a number that should emerge naturally from correctly recorded transactions.

### Drop

The point at which cash and slips are removed from a machine or drawer and transferred to secured accounting/count-room custody, breaking the chain from the transaction to the vault.

**In use:** "Bill acceptor's full — that's a drop, not something I open myself; count room handles it under camera."

**Common misuse:** confusing "drop" (removing cash from the machine to a locked box) with "count" (the count-room process of tallying what's in the box) — they're sequential steps done by different people specifically so no single person controls both.

### Gaming day

The 24-hour accounting period a casino defines (often not midnight-to-midnight — commonly something like 6am to 6am) used consistently for aggregating a patron's transactions for CTR/MTL purposes.

**In use:** "Her cage cash-out was at 11:40pm and this hand pay's at 1:15am — same gaming day, so they aggregate."

**Common misuse:** assuming "same day" means the calendar date. A property's defined gaming day is what governs aggregation, and a transaction just after midnight often still falls in the prior gaming day.

### Cage vs. booth

The cage is the central, higher-security cash operation handling markers, credit, large reconciliations, and the CTR filing itself; a booth or change cart is a floor-level satellite station handling smaller, higher-volume transactions directly with slot players.

**In use:** "I can pay this hand pay from the booth bank, but a marker redemption goes to the cage — that's not something I'm set up to handle here."

**Common misuse:** treating "cashier" as one undifferentiated job. Booth cashiers and cage workers carry different authority levels, different transaction types, and different control requirements even within the same casino.
