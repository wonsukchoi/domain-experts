# Vocabulary

### Forging temperature window
The specified range of material temperature at the point of striking within which the material has adequate plasticity for die fill without risking grain growth, scale, or decarburization from excessive heat.
**In use:** "Window's 2,050 to 2,150 — pyrometer check before we strike confirms we're actually in it, not just that the furnace said so."
**Common misuse:** Treating furnace setpoint as equivalent to actual billet temperature at the moment of forging — heating time, billet size, and any delay after furnace removal can all cause the billet's actual temperature to differ from what the furnace was set to.

### Grain flow
The directional alignment of a metal's internal crystalline structure, shaped by the forging process to follow the part's contour, which is the source of a forging's structural strength advantage over a machined-from-bar-stock equivalent part.
**In use:** "This part's strength comes from grain flow following the beam's curve — machining that cuts across it would lose that advantage even if the dimensions come out the same."
**Common misuse:** Treating a forged blank as structurally equivalent to bar stock once it's roughly the right shape — the forging's strength benefit specifically depends on preserved grain flow, and finish machining that exposes grain ends at a load-bearing surface can create a weak point invisible in a standard dimensional inspection.

### Die fill
The degree to which forged material completely occupies and takes the shape of a die's impression, including fine details like ribs, corners, and recesses.
**In use:** "Underfill's isolated to this one rib — checking press energy at that station before assuming a temperature issue across the whole part."
**Common misuse:** Assuming any underfill defect traces to insufficient billet temperature — localized underfill in a specific die feature often points to press/hammer energy or force distribution at that location, a distinct cause from a bulk temperature problem.

### Flash line
The thin excess material squeezed out along a forging die's parting line during the forging process, whose thickness is a useful indicator of die condition and closure.
**In use:** "Flash line's running thicker than spec on this batch — checking die wear at the parting line before anything else."
**Common misuse:** Ignoring flash line thickness as a diagnostic signal — its thickness trend can reveal developing die wear before the wear is visually obvious on the die itself.

### Time-at-temperature
The duration a billet spends at or near forging temperature, a variable distinct from peak temperature that independently affects scale formation and decarburization.
**In use:** "Peak temp was fine, but this billet sat in the furnace an extra twenty minutes waiting on the line — that's more scale exposure than usual."
**Common misuse:** Treating peak temperature reached as the only relevant heat-exposure variable — two billets can reach the identical peak temperature while accumulating very different amounts of scale/decarburization if one spent significantly longer at or near that temperature.

### Decarburization (forging)
The loss of carbon content at a steel billet's surface layer from prolonged exposure to high temperature in an oxidizing furnace atmosphere, weakening the surface hardness/strength the material would otherwise have.
**In use:** "Extended furnace dwell time on this billet risks decarburization at the surface — that's a separate concern from just hitting the right peak temperature."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a billet that reaches the correct peak temperature is automatically free of decarburization risk — decarburization depends on time at elevated temperature as much as the peak value, making an extended or delayed heating cycle a real risk even at a nominally correct peak temperature.

### Press/hammer energy (or force)
The mechanical energy or force delivered by a forging press or hammer during a strike, which must be sufficient to fully fill the die impression, distinct from and independent of billet temperature.
**In use:** "Energy's set for the bulk of the part but might be short for this deep recess — that's a force distribution issue, not a heat problem."
**Common misuse:** Treating temperature as the only variable affecting die fill quality — even a billet at perfect temperature can produce underfill if the press/hammer doesn't deliver sufficient energy or force to that specific feature of the die.

### Macro-etch (grain flow inspection)
A destructive metallurgical inspection technique that chemically etches a sectioned sample to reveal its grain flow pattern, used to verify grain flow alignment for critical forged parts.
**In use:** "Pulling a sample for macro-etch to confirm grain flow follows the load path on this new part design before we finalize the machining plan."
**Common misuse:** Assuming grain flow alignment can be confirmed through a standard dimensional or visual inspection — grain flow is an internal structural characteristic that requires a specific destructive metallurgical technique to actually verify.

### Die wear (forging)
Gradual degradation of a forging die's impression accuracy from repeated high-force cycles, typically concentrated at high-stress areas like corners and parting lines.
**In use:** "Flash line's drifting thicker over the last thousand parts — that tracks with die wear at the parting line, worth scheduling rework before it gets worse."
**Common misuse:** Relying on visual inspection of the die itself to judge wear — wear can affect part dimensions and fill quality before it's visually apparent, making periodic finished-part measurement a necessary, separate check.

### Pyrometer
An instrument used to measure the temperature of a billet or material at the point of forging, providing direct verification distinct from and more reliable than trusting the furnace's setpoint display.
**In use:** "Pyrometer reads 1,940 on this one — that furnace setpoint of 2,100 doesn't reflect what actually happened after the delay."
**Common misuse:** Treating furnace setpoint temperature as a sufficient proxy for actual billet condition without direct pyrometer verification, especially after any delay or handling gap between furnace removal and forging.
