# Vocabulary

Terms generalists flatten together that a rigger keeps sharply separate — the value is in the misuse, not the definition.

## Vertical hitch vs. choker hitch vs. basket hitch

**Vertical hitch** rigs the sling straight from hook to load at its full tagged WLL. **Choker hitch** wraps the sling around the load and back through its own eye, rated lower than vertical and further derated by choke angle. **Basket hitch** cradles the load in the sling body with both ends at the hook, rated at roughly double the vertical WLL at 90° but derating with angle like any other bridle leg. Reading a sling tag without noting which hitch is rigged is reading the wrong number for the configuration in front of you.

**In use:** "That's a choker at 90°, not a basket — we're rigging to 74% of the choker rating from the angle chart, not the tagged vertical WLL."

**Common misuse:** applying the sling's vertical WLL to a choked or basketed configuration, which overstates the real capacity in one direction and understates it in the other depending on which hitch is actually rigged.

## D/d ratio

The ratio of the diameter of the pin, shackle bow, or spreader-bar trunnion a sling bears on (D) to the sling's own nominal body diameter (d). A low D/d ratio means the sling bends sharply around a small-diameter bearing surface, which bleeds real capacity through bending stress even though the tagged WLL never changes.

**In use:** "That shackle pin gives us a D/d of 2.6 to 1 — call it 87% efficiency, not the full 58,000-lb tag."

**Common misuse:** treating the sling's tagged WLL as the working capacity at any bearing point, without checking the actual pin or bow diameter it's reeved over.

## Critical lift vs. routine lift

A **critical lift** meets a defined trigger — over 75% of charted crane capacity at the working radius, multiple cranes, occupied space or critical assets in the load path, non-standard rigging geometry — and requires an engineered lift plan with its own sign-off chain. A **routine lift** meets none of those triggers and proceeds on standard rigging judgment and inspection. The line is the number, not how the crew feels about the load.

**In use:** "We're at 87% of chart on Crane A — that makes this a critical lift whether or not it looks like our usual vessel set."

**Common misuse:** deciding "critical" by how heavy or unusual a load *looks*, instead of checking it against the actual percent-of-capacity and configuration triggers.

## Tandem lift vs. single-crane multi-point lift

A **tandem lift** (or multi-crane lift) uses two or more separate cranes, each independently supporting part of the load and each checked against its own chart at its own radius. A **single-crane multi-point lift** uses one crane with a spreader bar or multi-leg bridle distributing the load across several pick points, all under one machine's single chart check. The load-share math looks similar, but a tandem lift adds crane-to-crane position tolerance and independent capacity checks that a single-crane multi-point lift doesn't need.

**In use:** "This is tandem, not multi-point off one hook — each crane gets its own contingency and its own chart check, not a shared spreader-bar capacity."

**Common misuse:** applying single-crane spreader-bar load-share logic to a tandem lift, which skips the added contingency and independent chart verification that two separate cranes require.

## Center of gravity vs. rigging point vs. resultant lift line

**Center of gravity (CG)** is where a load's weight balances, calculated from component weights and distances. **Rigging point** is where the sling or bridle physically attaches. **Resultant lift line** is the line through the crane hook, and for a level, controlled pick it must pass through the CG — which is why rigging points on an asymmetric load are chosen (or load-shared) to put the resultant line through the CG, not through the load's visual middle.

**In use:** "The CG's ten feet off the geometric middle because of the internals — we solve the load share to that number, not the fixed lug locations by themselves."

**Common misuse:** treating "between the pick points" as automatically meaning "through the CG," which is only true when the pick points happen to straddle the CG symmetrically.

## Exclusion zone vs. barricade

**Exclusion zone** is the calculated area — based on load dimensions and maximum working boom radius — where no personnel are permitted while a load is suspended. **Barricade** is the physical tape, fence, or cone line placed to enforce it. A barricade placed at a habitual distance rather than the calculated exclusion radius creates a false sense of enforcement over a zone that isn't actually the hazard area.

**In use:** "Barricade needs to go out to the calculated 93 feet, not the usual 20-foot tape line we use for smaller picks."

**Common misuse:** treating any barricade line as evidence the exclusion zone was correctly set, without checking whether its radius actually matches the lift plan's calculation.

## Spreader bar vs. equalizer beam vs. below-the-hook lifting device (BTH)

**Below-the-hook lifting device** is the general category — any rigging accessory attached below the crane hook that shares or redistributes load, rated and certified in its own right. **Spreader bar** holds pick points apart and under compression, converting sling angle to near-vertical legs to reduce angle-related load factor. **Equalizer beam** balances load between two or more pick points that aren't symmetric, using a pivot or trolley rather than fixed geometry. Both are BTH devices requiring their own documented rated capacity — neither is a passive accessory.

**In use:** "The spreader bar's rated for 40,000 lb at this configuration — check that against the actual per-leg load before we hook up, not just the sling ratings."

**Common misuse:** rigging a spreader bar or equalizer beam without checking its own certified capacity, treating it as neutral hardware rather than a load-bearing device with a rating that can be the actual limiting factor.

## Rejection criteria vs. "still serviceable"

**Rejection criteria** are the specific, countable conditions (broken-wire count, percent diameter wear, fabric cuts, elongation limit) that mandate removing a piece of rigging gear from service, per ASME B30.9 and the manufacturer. "**Still serviceable**" is a judgment call with no numeric basis — the two are not the same standard, and gear that "looks fine" can already meet a rejection criterion the inspector didn't count.

**In use:** "Count the broken wires in this lay before you call it serviceable — ten randomly distributed and it's out, no matter how it looks otherwise."

**Common misuse:** substituting a visual "looks okay" judgment for the actual numeric count or measurement the rejection criterion specifies.

## Shock loading vs. static load

**Static load** is the load applied smoothly, with the rigging already under tension before the crane takes the full weight. **Shock loading** happens when slack is taken up suddenly — a snatch lift, a load dropped and caught, sudden deceleration — applying a transient force well above the static weight, sometimes multiples of it, for a fraction of a second.

**In use:** "Take up slack slow and steady — snatching this off the trailer bed is a shock load the rigging wasn't calculated for."

**Common misuse:** treating a smooth-looking lift as automatically free of shock loading, when a jerky crane movement or sudden slack takeup can shock-load rigging that was sized correctly for the static weight alone.

## Load chart utilization

The load a crane is actually lifting expressed as a percentage of its charted (rated) capacity at the specific boom length, radius, and configuration used — not a generic tonnage rating for the machine. The same crane can be at 40% utilization at one radius and 90% at another with the identical load on the hook.

**In use:** "We're not '150-ton crane, 60-ton load' — at this radius the chart says 65 tons, so we're at 92% utilization, not 40%."

**Common misuse:** citing a crane's nameplate or maximum-configuration capacity as if it applied at the actual working radius, which can mask a lift that's far closer to the chart limit than the nameplate number suggests.
