# Vocabulary

Terms generalists conflate that certified health education specialists keep sharply separate. Each: definition, how a practitioner actually uses it in a sentence, and the common misuse.

### Health education vs. health promotion vs. health communication

**Health education** is the planned, sequenced set of learning experiences that builds knowledge, skill, and self-efficacy toward a specific behavior. **Health promotion** is the broader umbrella — education plus policy, environmental, and organizational change that makes the healthy choice easier. **Health communication** is the messaging and media layer that can serve either.

**In use:** "The flyer campaign is health communication, not health education — it can raise awareness, but it isn't going to build the self-management skills this population actually needs."

**Common misuse:** using the three interchangeably, or claiming "health promotion" credit for a single educational session with no environmental or policy component attached.

### CHES vs. MCHES

**CHES** (Certified Health Education Specialist) is the entry-level national certification from NCHEC, requiring a qualifying degree and passing the CHES exam covering the Areas of Responsibility. **MCHES** (Master Certified Health Education Specialist) requires 5+ years of documented practice at the advanced competency level plus a separate exam, and signals leadership/systems-level competency, not just program delivery.

**In use:** "This needs an MCHES sign-off — it's a multi-site policy initiative, not a single-program deliverable."

**Common misuse:** treating MCHES as "CHES plus more years," rather than a distinct competency tier (Areas 6–8: advocacy, leadership and management, and communication at a systems level).

### PRECEDE vs. PROCEED

**PRECEDE** (the diagnostic phases: social, epidemiological, behavioral/environmental, educational/ecological, administrative/policy) determines *what to do and why* before any program exists. **PROCEED** (implementation, process evaluation, impact evaluation, outcome evaluation) covers *running and evaluating* what PRECEDE designed.

**In use:** "We're still in PRECEDE — don't ask me for a curriculum yet, we haven't finished the behavioral diagnosis."

**Common misuse:** calling the whole model "PRECEDE" and skipping straight to implementation without completing the diagnostic phases the acronym is named for.

### Reach vs. Effectiveness (RE-AIM)

**Reach** is the number and representativeness of the target population who actually participate. **Effectiveness** is the outcome change among those who did. A program can have excellent Effectiveness and terrible Reach — meaning it works for the small, self-selected group who show up but says nothing about its impact on the population it was funded to serve.

**In use:** "Effectiveness looks great, but Reach is 32% and skewed older — we can't claim this solves the county's problem yet."

**Common misuse:** reporting Effectiveness alone as "the results," which silently drops Reach, Adoption, Implementation, and Maintenance from the picture.

### Formative vs. summative evaluation

**Formative evaluation** happens during development or early delivery, to catch and fix problems (pilot testing materials, mid-course participant feedback). **Summative evaluation** happens after a defined period, to judge whether stated objectives were met.

**In use:** "That's formative feedback — useful for revising session 3 now, not evidence for the annual outcome report."

**Common misuse:** treating mid-program anecdotal feedback as if it answers the summative question ("did we hit the objective"), or waiting until the summative report to discover a fixable delivery problem that formative checks would have caught.

### Health literacy vs. numeracy

**Health literacy** is the capacity to obtain, process, and understand basic health information to make decisions. **Health numeracy** is the specific subset involving numbers — interpreting a blood sugar reading, a dosage, or a risk percentage. A person can read fluently and still struggle with numeracy, or vice versa.

**In use:** "The handout's reading level is fine — the numeracy is the problem; nobody in the pilot could translate '30% risk' into a number of people out of 10."

**Common misuse:** running only a reading-level check on materials and assuming that covers comprehension of numeric risk information.

### Self-efficacy vs. knowledge

**Knowledge** is knowing facts about a health condition or behavior. **Self-efficacy** (Bandura) is the belief that one can actually perform the specific behavior in one's own circumstances. Interventions routinely raise knowledge without raising self-efficacy, and self-efficacy is the better predictor of behavior change.

**In use:** "Post-test knowledge is up 40 points, but the self-efficacy scale barely moved — that's why we're not seeing behavior change yet."

**Common misuse:** using a knowledge quiz as a proxy for whether the program will change behavior.

### Needs assessment vs. asset mapping

**Needs assessment** identifies gaps, risks, and deficits in a population. **Asset mapping** identifies existing strengths, resources, and networks (community organizations, informal leaders, existing infrastructure) that a program can build on. Planning from needs alone tends to produce deficit-framed, externally-imposed programs; pairing it with asset mapping produces ones the community will actually sustain.

**In use:** "The needs assessment says diabetes risk is high — the asset map says there's already a trusted promotora network we should be training, not bypassing."

**Common misuse:** doing a needs assessment and treating it as the complete planning input, without asking what the community already has working.

### Process objective vs. impact objective vs. outcome objective

**Process objectives** measure whether activities happened as planned (sessions delivered, materials distributed). **Impact objectives** measure intermediate change (knowledge, attitude, skill, behavior). **Outcome objectives** measure the longer-term health or quality-of-life result (disease rate, mortality). All three should appear in a logic model; a report with only process objectives has described activity, not effect.

**In use:** "We hit every process objective — six sessions delivered on schedule — but the impact objective on self-efficacy is what tells us if it mattered."

**Common misuse:** calling a fully-delivered curriculum a "successful program" based on process objectives alone.

### Fidelity vs. adaptation

**Fidelity** is delivering an evidence-based program as designed (content, dose, sequence). **Adaptation** is a deliberate, documented change to fit local context (language, literacy level, session length). Evidence-based programs tolerate some adaptation without losing effect, but undocumented, ad hoc adaptation (cutting content under time pressure) is a fidelity failure, not a customization.

**In use:** "Translating the materials to Spanish is adaptation; cutting session 4 to 30 minutes because we ran out of room time is a fidelity deviation we need to flag, not a rounding error."

**Common misuse:** using "we adapted it" to describe an undocumented content cut, blurring the line with a genuine fidelity failure.

### Health disparity vs. health inequity

**Health disparity** is a measurable difference in a health outcome between groups. **Health inequity** is a disparity judged to be avoidable, unfair, and rooted in unjust social conditions — not every disparity is labeled an inequity, but most disparities tracked in public health practice are.

**In use:** "It's a documented disparity — completers skew 15 points wealthier than the referred population — and given the transportation barrier we identified, that's an inequity, not just a statistic."

**Common misuse:** using the terms interchangeably in a report when the distinction (measurable difference vs. a judgment about avoidability and fairness) is exactly what a funder or policymaker needs to act on differently.
