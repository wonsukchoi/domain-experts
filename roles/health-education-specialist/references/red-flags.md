# Red flags

Smell tests a certified health education specialist catches in the first review of a program report. Format for each: what it usually means, the first question to ask, and the data to pull before anyone makes a decision.

### Enrollment or attendance reported with no completion rate

**Usually means:** completion is low and unflattering, and reach is being substituted for it in the report — a program can enroll 500 and still be failing if 32% finish.

**First question:** "What percentage of those who started actually completed the series, and how does that compare to the last four quarters?"

**Data to pull:** session-by-session attendance log; completion vs. enrollment ratio over time; whether the reporting template even has a completion field.

### Knowledge scores improve but no behavior or biometric measure was collected

**Usually means:** the program taught facts and stopped, without building the skill, self-efficacy, or environmental support needed to act on them — or the evaluation was designed around what's easy to measure (a quiz) rather than what matters.

**First question:** "What's the pre/post change in the actual behavior or biometric this program is meant to move — not the knowledge check?"

**Data to pull:** pre/post knowledge test alongside pre/post behavior self-report or biometric data (HbA1c, servings/week, quit attempts); the original SMART objective the program was funded against.

### Curriculum unchanged for 5+ years with no revalidation

**Usually means:** the program is running on inertia; the population, evidence base, or local risk-factor data it was built from may no longer match current conditions.

**First question:** "When was this curriculum last checked against current local needs-assessment data, not just re-ordered from the same publisher?"

**Data to pull:** date of last needs assessment; current vs. original-design demographic and risk-factor data for the target population.

### Dropout concentrated at one specific session or time slot

**Usually means:** a structural barrier (transportation, work schedule, childcare) rather than declining motivation — motivation-based attrition tends to be gradual and spread across sessions, not a single-session cliff.

**First question:** "Which session has the largest one-step drop, and what do exit surveys from that cohort say about timing and access?"

**Data to pull:** session-by-session attrition table; exit survey responses filtered to that session's dropouts; session time/format/location logs.

### Materials written above a 6th–8th grade reading level for a general adult audience

**Usually means:** materials were adapted from a clinical or grant-writing source without a health-literacy pass, and will underperform regardless of content quality.

**First question:** "What's the readability level of this handout, and has it been tested with teach-back on this specific audience?"

**Data to pull:** readability score; teach-back session notes or comprehension check results; translated-language version parity if the audience is multilingual.

### Needs assessment skipped or replaced by national/state data alone

**Usually means:** the program is solving the state's or the nation's problem, not necessarily this community's — local risk-factor prevalence, access barriers, and cultural context can diverge sharply from broader averages.

**First question:** "What local data — not state or national — specifically justifies this curriculum for this population?"

**Data to pull:** local surveillance or clinic data; any completed PRECEDE social/epidemiological diagnosis; comparison of local vs. state/national rates for the targeted condition.

### No demographic breakdown of completers versus the full referred population

**Usually means:** an equity gap is present and invisible — completers commonly skew toward those with more flexible schedules, transportation, and income, understating the barrier for the rest of the referred population.

**First question:** "How does the age, sex, income, or language distribution of completers compare to everyone referred?"

**Data to pull:** demographic fields on referral vs. completion records; any exclusion pattern by clinic site, insurance status, or preferred language.

### Funder or leadership asks only for reach, no outcome trend

**Usually means:** the program's actual effect on the target condition has never been reported, which is a renewal risk once a funder starts asking the harder question, and a sign the feedback loop into program redesign doesn't exist.

**First question:** "What outcome measure, tracked over time, would tell us if this program is actually working?"

**Data to pull:** prior year(s) of outcome data if it exists; the original grant's stated outcome objective; whether a RE-AIM Effectiveness line has ever been reported.

### Session notes contain individualized medical advice or informal diagnosis

**Usually means:** scope creep past the health-education/clinical boundary, usually because a participant asked a direct medical question and no provider was present to redirect it to.

**First question:** "Was this participant referred back to their provider for this specific question, and is that documented?"

**Data to pull:** session documentation templates; referral-back protocol and whether it's actually used; any supervising clinician sign-off requirement for the program.

### A single-session "one and done" workshop counted as a behavior-change intervention

**Usually means:** dose mismatch — most validated behavior-change curricula assume multiple contacts; a one-time session is closer to an awareness activity and shouldn't be evaluated (or funded) as if it produces the same effect size.

**First question:** "What's the evidence base's minimum effective dose for this behavior, and how many contacts does this format actually deliver?"

**Data to pull:** the source curriculum's recommended session count; this program's actual delivered dose; any outcome data specific to single-session delivery of this content.

### Objectives stated without a number or a date

**Usually means:** the program was designed around activities, not outcomes, and cannot be evaluated as pass/fail by anyone, including the person who wrote it.

**First question:** "If this objective were fully met, what specific number would be true, and by when?"

**Data to pull:** the written objectives as filed in the grant or program plan; whether a measurement method was specified alongside each one.

### Program budgeted and reported per participant enrolled, not per participant completed

**Usually means:** true cost-effectiveness is being understated or overstated depending on completion rate, and budget decisions are being made on the wrong denominator.

**First question:** "What's the cost per completer, not per enrollee, and how has that changed year over year?"

**Data to pull:** total program cost; enrollment and completion counts for the same period; any planned change to either that would shift the comparison.
