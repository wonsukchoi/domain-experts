# Playbook

Filled templates a health education specialist actually runs. Starting points to adapt to a specific population, not scripts to copy verbatim.

## 1. PRECEDE-PROCEED needs assessment (filled example — youth vaping cessation program)

Run PRECEDE phases before selecting a curriculum; skipping straight to "pick a program" is the single most common planning error.

| Phase | Question | Filled example |
|---|---|---|
| 1. Social diagnosis | What quality-of-life problem does the community report? | Parent/teacher surveys (n=340) cite vaping-related disciplinary incidents and school-nurse visits as top concern, ahead of vaping's long-term health framing. |
| 2. Epidemiological diagnosis | What health/behavioral data backs the concern? | District nurse logs: 62 vaping-related visits in one semester, up from 14 two years prior; state YRBS shows district e-cigarette use at 24% vs. state average 14%. |
| 3. Behavioral & environmental diagnosis | What behaviors and environmental conditions drive the problem? | Behavior: peer-influenced experimentation at ages 13–14. Environment: vape retailers within 0.5 mi of 3 of 4 middle schools; no school policy enforcement consistency across buildings. |
| 4. Educational & ecological diagnosis | What predisposing/enabling/reinforcing factors explain the behavior? | Predisposing: low perceived harm (68% of surveyed students rated vaping "not very risky"). Enabling: easy retail access, low cost per pod. Reinforcing: peer group norms, older-sibling access. |
| 5. Administrative & policy diagnosis | What resources/policy barriers exist for implementation? | $22,000 grant available; district policy allows curriculum time only in 8th-grade health class (45 min/week × 6 weeks). |

**Output artifact:** a one-page diagnosis brief citing the specific numbers above, handed to whoever approves curriculum selection — not a summary of "vaping is a problem," which any stakeholder already knows.

## 3. SMART objectives table (filled examples across settings)

| Weak (rejected on sight) | SMART rewrite |
|---|---|
| "Increase awareness of diabetes risk factors." | "By the end of the 6-session series, 80% of participants will correctly identify 3 of 5 modifiable diabetes risk factors on the post-test, up from a 40% pre-test baseline." |
| "Improve community health." | "Reduce the county's adult smoking rate from 19.4% (2024 BRFSS) to 17.0% by the 2027 BRFSS cycle." |
| "Get more people into the program." | "Increase DSME program completion (of those referred) from 32% to 45% within 12 months by removing the identified scheduling barrier." |
| "Teach kids about nutrition." | "70% of 4th-graders will correctly sort 8 of 10 foods into MyPlate categories on an end-of-unit assessment." |

Every objective needs: **who**, **how much**, **measured how**, **by when** — missing any one of the four means it isn't ready to plan against.

## 4. RE-AIM evaluation report template (filled — DSME program, see SKILL.md worked example)

| Dimension | Definition | This program, this year |
|---|---|---|
| Reach | % and representativeness of target population participating | 32% of referred patients completed (160/500); completers skew 8 years older and 15% more female than the full referred population — flag as an equity gap in the report, not just a completion number. |
| Effectiveness | Outcome change among those who completed | Mean HbA1c drop of 1.2 points among completers vs. 0.3 among partial completers (1–2 sessions attended). |
| Adoption | % of eligible sites/staff delivering the program as intended | 3 of 4 clinic partners actively referring; 1 partner referred zero patients this quarter — follow up before next report. |
| Implementation | Fidelity to the planned curriculum and dose | 5 of 6 sessions delivered on schedule at both sites; session 4 (meal planning) shortened to 30 of 60 planned minutes at one site — flag as a fidelity deviation. |
| Maintenance | Whether gains persist and the program continues past the funding cycle | 6-month follow-up HbA1c data collected on 58% of completers so far; program renewed for a second year at current funding level. |

A funder update that reports Effectiveness alone and omits Reach is the single most common way a leaky program looks healthier than it is.

## 5. Plain-language / teach-back materials checklist

- Reading level at or below approximately 6th grade (run through a standard readability check) unless there is direct evidence this audience reads higher.
- One idea per sentence; active voice; numbers presented both as a percentage and a natural frequency ("30%, or about 3 in 10 people").
- No unexplained acronyms on first use (HbA1c → "your average blood sugar over 3 months (HbA1c)").
- Every handout ends with 1–3 action items, not a summary of information.
- Session close uses **teach-back**, not "any questions?": ask the participant to explain the instruction back in their own words ("Show me how you'd explain this to your spouse tonight") and correct gaps in that explanation, not by repeating the original wording louder.

## 6. Attrition tracking table (filled example, template for any multi-session program)

| Session | Enrolled at start | Attended | Dropout this session | Cumulative completion % |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Orientation | 500 referred | 320 | — | 64% |
| Session 1 | 320 | 290 | 30 (9%) | 58% |
| Session 2 | 290 | 210 | 80 (28%) | 42% |
| Session 3 | 210 | 195 | 15 (7%) | 39% |
| Session 4 | 195 | 175 | 20 (10%) | 35% |
| Session 5 | 175 | 165 | 10 (6%) | 33% |
| Session 6 (completion) | 165 | 160 | 5 (3%) | 32% |

The session-2-to-3 drop (28% loss in one step, more than triple any other session) is where the barrier investigation starts — not at the top of the funnel.
