# Vocabulary

### Rig up / rig down
Assembling or disassembling equipment, hoses, and iron at a wellsite for a servicing operation.
**In use:** "We're rigged up and ready to pressure-test lines by 0900."
**Common misuse:** assumed to apply only to drilling rigs; it covers mobilizing any wellsite equipment — workover units, pump trucks, wireline units.

### Thief hatch
The access port on top of a storage tank used for manual gauging and sampling.
**In use:** "Don't lean over the thief hatch — stand upwind and use the wind."
**Common misuse:** treated as a harmless lid rather than the highest routine gas-exposure point on a tank battery.

### Flash gas
Vapor that separates out of liquid hydrocarbon when pressure drops, such as produced fluid entering a tank at atmospheric pressure.
**In use:** "That spike was flash gas off the hatch, not a leak in the line."
**Common misuse:** assumed to only occur at the wellhead, not understood as a routine byproduct of tank-level pressure drop.

### Hole watch / attendant
The person stationed outside a confined space during entry, in continuous communication with the entrant, authorized to order evacuation.
**In use:** "Hole watch has the retrieval line and the radio — nobody else touches either."
**Common misuse:** treated as an optional bystander role instead of a dedicated, permit-required duty that can't be combined with other work.

### LEL (Lower Explosive Limit)
The minimum concentration of a gas in air that will ignite, expressed as a percentage of that concentration — not a percentage of total air.
**In use:** "We're reading 14% LEL at the bottom of the pit — that's not safe to enter."
**Common misuse:** confused with percent oxygen, or treated as a linear danger scale instead of a threshold tied to a specific gas's ignition point.

### Cathodic protection (CP)
Corrosion control on buried pipe and tanks using impressed current or sacrificial anodes; ditch and trench work can disturb it.
**In use:** "Don't disconnect the CP lead without telling the corrosion tech first."
**Common misuse:** assumed unrelated to daily digging work rather than infrastructure that routine excavation can damage.

### Tong
A power or manual wrench used to make up or break out pipe connections — the primary pinch-point hazard on a rig-up crew.
**In use:** "Keep your hands off the tong dies while it's cycling."
**Common misuse:** treated as a hand tool rather than powered equipment with its own energy-isolation and hand-placement rules.

### Cellar
A below-grade excavation around a wellhead, often housing valves — a classic confined space due to heavier-than-air gas accumulation.
**In use:** "Test the cellar before anyone climbs down to work that valve."
**Common misuse:** assumed safe because it's outdoors and open-top, ignoring that it's still a low point where H2S and hydrocarbon vapor settle.

### Swab / flowback
Fluid recovered from a well after stimulation, often carrying entrained gas and pressure.
**In use:** "That flowback tank's still swabbing gas — keep the hatch closed."
**Common misuse:** treated as inert wastewater rather than a continuing gas-exposure source.

### Stand (of pipe)
Two or three joints of pipe racked together as the unit pipe is typically handled in.
**In use:** "Rack that stand in the fourth slot, not the third — it's already full."
**Common misuse:** confused with a single joint; a stand is heavier and longer than one joint.

### Berm
An earthen or built containment barrier around a tank or piece of equipment to catch spills.
**In use:** "Berm's got two inches of standing product in the low corner — call it in."
**Common misuse:** treated as permanent and maintenance-free rather than something inspected and kept clear of vegetation that can hide a leak.

### Competent person
An OSHA-defined role: someone capable of identifying existing and predictable hazards and authorized to take prompt corrective action, most commonly cited for excavation work.
**In use:** "Get the competent person to sign off on the trench before anyone goes in."
**Common misuse:** assumed to mean "whoever has the most years on the crew" rather than a specific trained and authorized designation carrying legal responsibility.

### Sweet vs. sour (gas/crude)
Sour indicates H2S present above a threshold set by the purchaser's sales spec or the site's safety program; sweet indicates it's absent or below that spec.
**In use:** "This is a sour lease — full H2S monitoring every shift, no exceptions."
**Common misuse:** treated as a description of smell rather than a hazard classification that changes the entire PPE and monitoring program for the site.

### Pumping unit / pumpjack
The surface reciprocating pump (walking beam and horsehead) that lifts fluid from a well — rotating and reciprocating equipment requiring lockout/tagout for maintenance.
**In use:** "Lock out the pumping unit at the motor disconnect before anyone's near the horsehead."
**Common misuse:** assumed safe to approach because the stroke looks slow — the counterweight and walking beam carry high force regardless of cycle speed.

### Line of fire
The path a load, a line under tension, or moving equipment would travel if something failed — a core rigging safety concept.
**In use:** "Get out of the line of fire before that comes tight."
**Common misuse:** understood only as "under a suspended load," missing tensioned wireline, snapping chains, and swinging pipe.

### Bleed off
Releasing residual pressure from a line or vessel through a bleed valve before breaking a connection.
**In use:** "Bleed it off and check a second gauge before you crack that union."
**Common misuse:** assumed complete once the primary gauge reads zero, without confirming at an independent second point.
