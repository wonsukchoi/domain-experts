# Fire Inspector/Investigator — Vocabulary

### Point of origin
The specific physical location where a fire began — a single, precise spot, not a general area.
**In use:** "The point of origin is the northwest corner of the stage platform, based on the deepest char depth and lowest burn indicators."
**Common misuse:** Confused with "area of origin" (the broader room/zone), which is a preliminary finding, not the final determination.

### Area of origin
The general room or zone identified as containing the point of origin, before pattern analysis narrows it further.
**In use:** "We've narrowed the area of origin to the stage and DJ booth; point-of-origin analysis is still in progress."
**Common misuse:** Treated as interchangeable with "point of origin" — reports that stop at "area of origin" haven't finished the analysis.

### Fire pattern
Any visible or measurable effect of fire and heat on materials and surfaces, used as evidence for origin and cause analysis.
**In use:** "The V-pattern on the east wall correlates with the char-depth mapping to confirm the stage area as area of origin."
**Common misuse:** Treated as self-explanatory evidence of cause (e.g., "arson") rather than data requiring a fire-dynamics explanation.

### V-pattern
A V-shaped burn pattern radiating upward and outward from a fire's point of origin, caused by the natural upward/outward spread of heat and flame.
**In use:** "The V-pattern's narrow apex points to a localized ignition source at floor level, not a spreading fire from an adjacent room."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to always point precisely to origin — ventilation and fuel-load variation can distort V-pattern geometry.

### Char depth
The measured depth of charring into a combustible material (typically wood), used as a relative indicator of fire duration/intensity at that location.
**In use:** "Char-depth measurements peaked at 0.8 inches near the stage wall, tapering to 0.2 inches at the room's perimeter."
**Common misuse:** Treated as an absolute clock for fire duration — char rate varies by wood species, ventilation, and fire intensity, so it's comparative within a scene, not a universal timer.

### Flashover
The near-simultaneous ignition of all combustible material in an enclosed space when radiant heat reaches the auto-ignition temperature of the room's contents.
**In use:** "Witness reports of the room being 'engulfed within minutes' are consistent with flashover, not a slow-developing accidental fire."
**Common misuse:** Conflated with a fire simply "spreading fast" — flashover is a specific, near-instantaneous full-room-involvement event, not gradual acceleration.

### Negative corpus
A prohibited investigative methodology (NFPA 921 §18.6.5) that concludes a fire was incendiary solely because no accidental cause could be identified.
**In use:** "That conclusion is negative corpus — they ruled out electrical and mechanical causes but never identified an actual ignition source for the arson finding."
**Common misuse:** Not recognized as a named, specifically prohibited methodology — often defended as "process of elimination," which sounds rigorous but isn't valid without a positively identified cause.

### Ignitable liquid residue (ILR)
Chemical residue from an ignitable liquid (e.g., gasoline, lighter fluid) detectable in fire debris via laboratory analysis.
**In use:** "ILR sampling from the origin area returned negative — no accelerant residue detected."
**Common misuse:** A positive ILR result is assumed to prove arson on its own — ignitable liquids are also present in ordinary building materials and consumer products, so ILR results must be interpreted alongside pattern and origin evidence.

### ASTM E1618
The standard test method (gas chromatography–mass spectrometry) for analyzing fire debris samples for ignitable liquid residue.
**In use:** "Samples were submitted to the lab for ASTM E1618 analysis before the scene was released."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to be a field test — it's a laboratory method requiring sample submission and turnaround time, not an on-scene instant read.

### Occupant load
The calculated maximum number of persons a space is designed to safely accommodate, based on floor area and a use-specific factor.
**In use:** "Occupant load for this assembly space calculates to 429 persons using the concentrated-use net factor."
**Common misuse:** Confused with the observed headcount at the time of an incident — occupant load is a code-derived design capacity, not a body count.

### Egress width
The total clear width of exit doors, corridors, and stairs available for occupants to evacuate a space, measured in inches.
**In use:** "Provided egress width falls 13.8 inches short of the code-required minimum for this occupant load."
**Common misuse:** Measured from architectural drawings or nominal door-frame width instead of field-measured clear width, which excludes hardware and stop encroachment.

### Ventilation-limited fire
A fire whose growth rate is constrained by available oxygen rather than fuel, common in modern sealed/well-insulated structures.
**In use:** "This was a ventilation-limited fire until the window failed and fresh air drove rapid flashover."
**Common misuse:** Assumed all fires behave the same regardless of building envelope — ventilation-limited dynamics can produce misleading pattern evidence if the investigator doesn't account for when ventilation openings occurred.

### Alligatoring
A checked, blistered charring pattern on burned wood surfaces resembling alligator skin.
**In use:** "The alligatoring pattern here is consistent with high, sustained heat — it doesn't by itself indicate an accelerant was used."
**Common misuse:** Historically read as reliable evidence of accelerant/rapid fire; NFPA 921 fire-dynamics testing established it occurs in ordinary high-heat fires regardless of cause.

### Reasonable degree of professional certainty
The confidence standard investigators state a conclusion is held to, distinct from absolute certainty or a lower "possible" standard.
**In use:** "The cause classification is accidental, to a reasonable degree of professional certainty based on the evidence tested."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a formality rather than a substantive statement — a report that can't support this standard should classify the cause as undetermined instead of hedging the language while still asserting a specific cause.
