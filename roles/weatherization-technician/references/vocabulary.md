# Weatherization Technician Vocabulary

### SIR (Savings-to-Investment Ratio)
The audit tool's ratio of a measure's present-value energy savings over its measure life to its installed cost; DOE WAP funds a measure only at SIR ≥ 1.0, ranked highest-first against the per-unit budget cap.
**In use:** "Storm windows came back at 0.6 — they're not making the cut regardless of how much budget headroom we've got left."
**Common misuse:** Treating SIR as a rough cost-effectiveness feeling rather than the audit tool's actual computed output, or funding a measure with SIR below 1.0 because the client wants it.

### CAZ (combustion appliance zone)
The space containing a fuel-burning appliance and the air-pressure boundary that determines whether it drafts properly or spills flue gas into the house.
**In use:** "Air sealing the basement rim joists is going to change the CAZ pressure around that water heater — we retest after, not just at intake."
**Common misuse:** Treating "the basement" and "the CAZ" as interchangeable, missing that the CAZ is specifically the pressure zone the appliance's draft depends on, which air sealing anywhere in that zone's boundary can change.

### Worst-case depressurization test
A combustion-safety test run with every exhaust fan, dryer, and air handler operating simultaneously and doors closed, to find the most negative pressure the CAZ can reach under realistic occupant behavior.
**In use:** "It passed with the exhaust fans off, but worst-case with the dryer and both bath fans running it dropped to -6 Pa — that's a fail."
**Common misuse:** Testing only under quiescent conditions (nothing running) and calling it a passing combustion-safety test.

### Orphaned water heater
An atmospherically-vented water heater left on a flue sized for a larger appliance (typically a furnace) after that furnace is replaced with sealed-combustion equipment, degrading the flue's draft for the water heater's smaller load alone.
**In use:** "The furnace went high-efficiency and direct-vent, so now we've got an orphaned water heater on an oversized flue — check its draft before you call this job done."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a shared flue is still correctly sized just because it worked before one of the two appliances was replaced.

### Priority list
The audit tool's SIR-ranked, budget-capped output that functions as the actual work order for a WAP-funded job, not a suggested list of options.
**In use:** "The priority list has air sealing and the thermostat ahead of the wall dense-pack — that's the order we install in, not whatever the client asks for first."
**Common misuse:** Treating the priority list as a menu the crew can reorder for scheduling convenience.

### Deferral
A program determination that a home cannot proceed to weatherization work until a specific health-or-safety hazard is tested or remediated, independent of any SIR calculation.
**In use:** "This one's deferred until the vermiculite gets lab-tested — we're not opening that attic on presumption."
**Common misuse:** Treating deferral as optional or negotiable when a client is eager to proceed, instead of a hard program requirement.

### NEAT / MHEA
DOE's National Energy Audit Tool (site-built homes) and Manufactured Home Energy Audit tool — the standard software (or a state-approved equivalent) that turns diagnostic inputs into an SIR-ranked measure list for WAP jobs.
**In use:** "Run the blower-door and combustion numbers through NEAT before we price anything — the measure list comes out of that, not a walkthrough guess."
**Common misuse:** Skipping the formal audit-tool run and pricing measures from experience alone on a program-funded job, which isn't how the funded scope gets determined.

### ASHRAE 62.2
The ventilation standard setting the minimum whole-house mechanical ventilation rate a dwelling needs once its infiltration can't be assumed to supply adequate fresh air — the check that follows a large post-weatherization CFM50 reduction.
**In use:** "We dropped this house 25% on CFM50 — that trips the 62.2 check, so run the required-CFM calculation before we close it out."
**Common misuse:** Treating a large blower-door reduction as an unqualified win without checking whether it crossed the point where mechanical ventilation becomes necessary.

### Backdraft / spillage
Backdraft is flue gas flowing in reverse down the vent into the house; spillage is flue gas escaping at the draft hood or relief opening instead of venting up the flue — both are combustion-safety test failures, not the same failure mode.
**In use:** "It's not backdrafting through the vent, but it's spilling right at the draft hood for the first 45 seconds — still a fail."
**Common misuse:** Using "backdraft" as a catch-all term for any combustion-safety failure, obscuring which specific test (draft, spillage, CAZ pressure) actually failed.

### Category I / II / III / IV appliance venting
The NFGC/UL classification of gas appliance venting by draft type (natural vs. induced/forced) and flue-gas condition (positive vs. negative pressure, condensing vs. non-condensing) — determines what venting material and CAZ dependency the appliance actually has.
**In use:** "That's a Category I atmospheric unit — it's the one that depends on the CAZ pressure; the Category IV furnace next to it is sealed-combustion and doesn't care."
**Common misuse:** Assuming every gas appliance in a mechanical room shares the same venting dependency and combustion-safety risk profile.

### Health-and-safety (H&S) measure
A repair or mitigation required by the funding program regardless of its SIR — funded from the same per-unit budget cap but not subject to the 1.0 cost-effectiveness threshold.
**In use:** "The water heater vent fix is H&S-mandatory — it comes out of the cap first, before we start funding SIR-ranked measures."
**Common misuse:** Applying the SIR ≥ 1.0 rule to a health-and-safety line item, or assuming H&S spending is unlimited and separate from the capped budget.

### Per-unit budget cap
The maximum dollar amount a funding program allows for a single dwelling's weatherization scope, covering both SIR-ranked measures and mandatory health-and-safety spending together.
**In use:** "We're $980 under the cap after H&S and the SIR-ranked list — that headroom doesn't roll over, it just goes unused unless another measure clears both SIR and the remaining budget."
**Common misuse:** Treating energy-savings measures and health-and-safety spending as separate budgets rather than one shared cap.

### Blower-door directed air sealing
Air sealing performed while the blower door runs continuously, so the technician can locate and confirm leaks by feel or by watching the CFM50 number drop in real time, rather than sealing from a visual inspection alone.
**In use:** "Keep the blower door running while we work the attic bypasses — we want to watch the CFM50 number move as we go, not guess and retest cold at the end."
**Common misuse:** Doing all the air sealing first and only running the blower door once, at the end, losing the ability to confirm which specific seal actually helped.

### Monitoring inspection
A program-required quality-assurance visit, separate from the technician's own final test, that verifies the job file's documentation and a sample of the installed measures against program standards before payment is released.
**In use:** "This file needs to survive the monitoring inspection — make sure the CAZ retest numbers are actually in there, not just the final blower-door reading."
**Common misuse:** Treating the technician's own final walkthrough as equivalent to the program's independent monitoring inspection.
