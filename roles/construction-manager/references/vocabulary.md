# Construction management working vocabulary

Terms a construction manager uses precisely. Format: definition → how a practitioner says it → common misuse.

**Critical path** — The sequence of dependent tasks with zero float (schedule slack) that determines the project's actual finish date; a delay anywhere on this specific sequence delays the whole project, while delays off it may not.
In use: "The framing repair is on the critical path — every day it slips is a day added to the finish date, no exceptions."
Misuse: treating every delayed task as equally urgent regardless of float; a task with 10 days of float slipping by 2 days has zero effect on the finish date, while a critical-path task slipping by 1 day does.

**Float (slack)** — The amount of time a task can be delayed without affecting the project's finish date. Critical-path tasks have zero float by definition; other tasks have some float depending on their position in the schedule network.
In use: "That inspection has 4 days of float — we can let it slide a couple of days without touching the finish date."
Misuse: assuming any task with a due date is time-critical; float tells you which delays actually matter to the finish date and which don't.

**Look-ahead schedule** — A rolling short-horizon schedule (commonly 2-3 weeks) reviewed regularly with subcontractors to catch near-term sequencing conflicts and material/inspection needs before they become active delays, distinct from the long-range master schedule.
In use: "The 3-week look-ahead flagged that drywall is scheduled to start before the electrical rough-in inspection is booked — we caught that two weeks early instead of at the wall."
Misuse: relying solely on the master schedule for coordination, missing near-term conflicts that only a shorter-horizon, more frequently updated view surfaces in time to act on.

**Contingency** — A budget and/or schedule reserve set aside for unexpected conditions or changes, sized to the project's actual uncertainty (higher for renovation/unknown-condition work, lower for well-characterized new construction).
In use: "This renovation carries 8% contingency given the unknown existing conditions; new construction on this same site would typically run 3-4%."
Misuse: applying a fixed generic contingency percentage regardless of the project's actual risk profile, or treating contingency draws as a failure to be hidden rather than a planned, trackable mechanism.

**RFI (Request for Information)** — A formal question from a contractor/subcontractor to the design team or owner about an ambiguity or missing detail in the plans, tracked with an open date and required response, since an unresolved RFI on the critical path stalls dependent work.
In use: "That RFI about the beam connection detail has been open 6 days and it's blocking steel erection — needs to be escalated today."
Misuse: treating an RFI as routine paperwork rather than tracking its age and its downstream schedule impact, letting a seemingly small open question quietly stall critical-path work.

**Change order** — A formally documented and approved modification to the project's scope, cost, or schedule, as distinct from work performed informally without documentation or owner sign-off.
In use: "The deteriorated framing repair is going through as a change order — $7,600 against contingency, documented and signed off before the crew proceeds."
Misuse: having a crew "just handle it" for an unexpected condition without a logged change order, leaving the cost and schedule impact untracked and undisclosed until it surfaces later, often in a worse form.

**Submittal** — A document (shop drawing, material sample, product data) a contractor submits to the design team/owner for approval before that material or component can be fabricated or installed, with its own lead time that needs to be planned backward from the need date.
In use: "The curtain wall submittal has a 3-week typical review turnaround — it needs to go in now if we don't want it to become the bottleneck for the exterior close-in date."
Misuse: submitting on a schedule driven by when the paperwork happens to be ready rather than backward-planned from the critical-path date the approved material is actually needed.

**Crashing (schedule crashing)** — Deliberately adding resources (overtime, additional crew, parallel work) to a critical-path task specifically to shorten its duration, evaluated against the cost of the added resources versus the value of the time recovered.
In use: "Crashing the framing repair with a weekend crew costs $4,200 but avoids $6,000 in delay costs — worth doing."
Misuse: crashing a task that isn't on the critical path, which spends money without moving the finish date at all — or failing to compare the crashing cost against the actual value of the time recovered.

**Punch list** — The list of remaining minor defects or incomplete items identified near project completion that need correction before final acceptance, distinct from major scope items still in progress.
In use: "We're down to 40 punch list items, mostly cosmetic — final walkthrough is scheduled once they're cleared."
Misuse: using "punch list" to describe substantial unfinished scope; it specifically refers to minor finishing items near the end of a substantially complete project.

**Liquidated damages** — A pre-agreed, contractually specified daily (or other period) monetary penalty owed to the owner if the contractor misses the contracted completion date, distinct from actual proven damages which don't need to be separately calculated.
In use: "The contract sets liquidated damages at $2,000/day past substantial completion — that's the number driving the decision to crash the schedule rather than absorb the delay."
Misuse: assuming liquidated damages require the owner to separately prove actual financial harm; the pre-agreed contractual figure applies regardless of the owner's actual demonstrated loss.
