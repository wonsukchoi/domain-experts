# Red flags — what a construction manager notices instantly

Smell tests with thresholds. Any single one can be innocent; two or three together are a pattern.

### Any proposed schedule acceleration that involves skipping, shortening, or working around a safety protocol
- **Usually means:** schedule pressure is being allowed to compete with safety as if they were comparable tradeoffs — they aren't, and this shouldn't reach a cost-benefit conversation at all.
- **First question:** "What's the safety protocol being shortened here, and is there any version of this acceleration that doesn't touch it?"
- **Data to pull:** the specific safety protocol in question, the schedule savings being sought, whether an alternative exists that doesn't compromise it.

### A schedule update or coordination effort focused on tasks with significant float while a critical-path task slips unaddressed
- **Usually means:** effort and urgency are misallocated — the tasks getting attention aren't the ones that actually determine the finish date.
- **First question:** "Is this task on the critical path? If not, why is it getting priority attention over one that is?"
- **Data to pull:** current CPM schedule with float values, recent coordination meeting agenda/focus areas.

### Contingency budget drawn below ~25-30% remaining before the project reaches its midpoint
- **Usually means:** the rate of unexpected conditions/changes is outpacing what was planned for — either the original contingency was undersized or the project is encountering more surprises than typical.
- **First question:** "At this burn rate, does remaining contingency cover the realistic remainder of the project, or are we on track to run out?"
- **Data to pull:** contingency draw history and remaining balance, percentage of project schedule/budget elapsed.

### A submittal or inspection request initiated less than its typical full lead time before the critical-path task that depends on it
- **Usually means:** an avoidable delay is being set up — the approval will likely become the actual bottleneck once the dependent task is ready to start.
- **First question:** "What's this submittal/inspection's typical turnaround, and does the initiation date leave enough runway before it's needed?"
- **Data to pull:** submittal/inspection log with request and typical approval turnaround times, critical-path need date for the dependent task.

### An RFI or field question sitting unresolved for more than a few days while blocking work that depends on its answer
- **Usually means:** a seemingly small open question is quietly stalling downstream critical-path work, with the delay compounding the longer it goes unanswered.
- **First question:** "What work is actually blocked on this RFI, and is that work on the critical path?"
- **Data to pull:** RFI log with open date and linked blocked tasks, whether those tasks are on or near the critical path.

### A discovered site condition or change handled quietly by the crew without a logged change order or prompt owner notification
- **Usually means:** the cost and schedule impact isn't being tracked or disclosed, risking a larger, harder-to-explain deviation surfacing later.
- **First question:** "Has this been logged as a change and communicated to the owner, or is the crew just working through it?"
- **Data to pull:** change order log, timeline between discovery and owner communication for recent site conditions.

### A subcontractor consistently arriving on-site before its predecessor trade's work has actually passed inspection or reached the needed completion point
- **Usually means:** sequencing coordination has broken down — trades are staging based on the calendar date rather than actual prerequisite completion, risking rework.
- **First question:** "Has the prior trade's work actually been inspected/completed, or is this trade just following the originally planned date?"
- **Data to pull:** inspection sign-off dates versus subcontractor mobilization dates for the sequence in question.

### A project schedule or budget with no explicit contingency line, or a contingency percentage that doesn't scale with the project's actual uncertainty (e.g., a renovation with unknown existing conditions carrying the same contingency as new construction on a well-characterized site)
- **Usually means:** the plan assumes a level of certainty the project doesn't actually have, setting up a predictable, not-actually-unexpected "surprise" later.
- **First question:** "What's driving this specific contingency percentage, and does it reflect this project's actual known-unknowns (renovation, unfamiliar site, etc.)?"
- **Data to pull:** contingency percentage and rationale, comparable past projects' actual change-order/contingency-draw rates by project type.
