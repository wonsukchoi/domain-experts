# Preschool Teacher — Vocabulary

### Zone of proximal development (ZPD)
The gap between what a child can do independently and what they can do with guidance from a more skilled adult or peer.
**In use:** "He can't cut a straight line alone yet, but he can with me holding the paper steady — that's exactly the skill to target right now."
**Common misuse:** Treated as "whatever's slightly hard for the child," rather than the specific, scaffoldable gap between independent and assisted performance — generalists apply it to any task the child struggles with, including ones far beyond reach where scaffolding won't help yet.

### Monitoring zone
The score range on a developmental screening tool between the "typical" cutoff and the "refer" cutoff — a signal to watch and re-check, not an immediate diagnosis or referral trigger.
**In use:** "Her fine-motor score landed in the monitoring zone, so we're re-screening in six weeks rather than referring right away."
**Common misuse:** Collapsed into either "fine" or "refer now," skipping the actual purpose of the zone, which is to flag a need for a second data point before acting.

### Developmentally appropriate practice (DAP)
NAEYC's framework for matching teaching methods to what's known about how children at a given age typically learn — not a synonym for "age-appropriate content."
**In use:** "Worksheets asking 3-year-olds to trace letters for 20 minutes isn't DAP — the attention span and fine-motor development aren't there yet."
**Common misuse:** Used loosely to mean "not too advanced for the age," when it specifically references a body of research-based practice standards, not general intuition about difficulty.

### Scaffolding
Temporary support (a prompt, a modeled step, a physical assist) that's deliberately withdrawn as the child gains independent competence.
**In use:** "I'm still holding the scissors with him for the first cut, but he's doing the rest alone now — pulling back the support gradually."
**Common misuse:** Treated as permanent help rather than support with a planned exit — generalists keep providing the same level of assistance indefinitely instead of reducing it as skill develops.

### Anecdotal record
A brief, factual, dated observation of a specific child behavior tied to a developmental domain, written at the time of observation.
**In use:** "I've got six anecdotal records this month showing the pointing-instead-of-naming pattern — that's what's backing the referral conversation."
**Common misuse:** Confused with a general daily log ("had a good day, ate well") — an anecdotal record used for developmental tracking has to be specific enough to support a pattern claim later.

### Redirection
Offering an alternative action or focus in the moment of an unwanted behavior, without a verbal lecture about why the behavior was wrong.
**In use:** "I redirected him to the sensory bin instead of correcting the block-throwing mid-tantrum — the conversation about why happens later, when he can actually hear it."
**Common misuse:** Used interchangeably with "distraction," missing that effective redirection offers a genuinely acceptable alternative outlet for the same underlying need, not just a different activity.

### Group size vs. ratio
Two separate licensing constraints: ratio is adults-per-child; group size is the maximum number of children who can be together regardless of how many adults are present.
**In use:** "We're in-ratio with two adults for 16 kids, but if we combine with the other group we'd hit 32, which blows the group-size cap even though the ratio math still works."
**Common misuse:** Treated as one constraint — a room can satisfy the ratio and still violate the group-size maximum, which changes different things (ratio affects supervision capacity, group size affects noise/stimulation load and manageability).

### Sensory processing
How a child's nervous system registers and responds to sensory input (touch, sound, movement) — over- or under-responsiveness can drive behavior that looks like defiance or inattention.
**In use:** "The meltdown happens every time the fire alarm testing runs — that's sensory, not behavioral."
**Common misuse:** Invoked as a catch-all explanation for any behavior without checking whether a clear environmental or communication trigger exists first — not every escalation is sensory-driven.

### Receptive vs. expressive language
Receptive language is what a child understands; expressive language is what they can produce. The two develop at different rates, and a gap is common, not automatically concerning.
**In use:** "His expressive vocabulary is behind, but his receptive skills are strong — he follows three-step directions fine, which changes how urgent this is."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to move together — a low expressive score alone is read as "the child doesn't understand," when receptive skill needs to be checked separately.

### Play-based learning
An instructional approach where developmental skills are taught through child-directed or lightly scaffolded play, rather than direct instruction, based on evidence that this age group builds skills most durably through play.
**In use:** "The counting isn't on a worksheet — it's built into the block-building prompt, where they have to count how many blocks tall before it tips."
**Common misuse:** Interpreted as "unstructured free time with no teaching happening," when deliberate play-based learning still has specific developmental targets embedded in the materials and prompts, just not delivered as direct instruction.

### IEP referral (early childhood)
The formal process of referring a child for evaluation for an Individualized Education Program, typically triggered by a documented, persistent delay in one or more domains.
**In use:** "Two consecutive screenings in the black zone on communication is enough to start the IEP referral conversation with the family."
**Common misuse:** Treated as something a teacher can start unilaterally without family involvement, or conversely as too big a step to raise after a single flagged screening — the actual trigger is a documented, persistent pattern, discussed with the family before formal referral.
