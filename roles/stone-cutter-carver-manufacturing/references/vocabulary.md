# Vocabulary

### Fissure
A natural crack or fracture plane within a stone slab, a potential structural weak point that must be assessed before cutting, distinct from ordinary color veining.
**In use:** "Mapped a fissure running diagonally through the back third of this slab — cutting the overhang section out of the front instead."
**Common misuse:** Treating any visible line in the stone as a fissure requiring rejection — many lines are cosmetic veining with no structural risk, and over-rejecting wastes usable material.

### Veining
The natural color/mineral pattern within stone, a cosmetic characteristic that must be matched across slabs in a multi-piece installation but does not itself indicate structural weakness.
**In use:** "Veining runs the same direction across both slabs — good match for the seam."
**Common misuse:** Confusing veining with a fissure — veining is a color/pattern feature, not necessarily a structural weak point, and the two require different assessment criteria.

### Flexural strength
A stone's resistance to bending/breaking under a load applied perpendicular to its face, the specific engineering property that determines a safe unsupported overhang span at a given thickness.
**In use:** "This granite's flexural strength rating supports a 10-inch unsupported overhang at 3cm thickness — the requested 12 inches exceeds that."
**Common misuse:** Assuming all granite or all "hard stone" shares the same flexural strength — it varies meaningfully by specific stone type and density, and the applicable rating must be checked for the actual stone being used.

### Slab matching
The practice of laying out and comparing actual intended slabs in their intended orientation to verify veining/color continuity across a seam before cutting.
**In use:** "Slab matching confirmed before cutting — vein pattern carries cleanly across both pieces at the seam."
**Common misuse:** Assuming slabs from the same quarry lot or stone type name will match without an actual side-by-side layout check — natural variation between slabs means matching must be verified visually, not assumed.

### Unsupported overhang
The portion of a stone slab (e.g., a countertop) extending beyond its base support with no additional bracing, subject to a maximum safe span determined by the stone's flexural strength and thickness.
**In use:** "12 inches requested, but the unsupported overhang limit for this stone at this thickness is 10 inches — adding a corbel bracket for the difference."
**Common misuse:** Treating "unsupported overhang" limits as a single industry-wide number — the safe limit depends on the specific stone type and actual cut thickness, not a generic rule applied regardless of material.

### Corbel/bracket support
Additional structural support added beneath a stone overhang to extend the safe span beyond the material's unsupported limit.
**In use:** "Added a corbel bracket at the 10-inch mark to safely support the additional 2 inches of overhang."
**Common misuse:** Treating bracket support as optional cosmetic reinforcement rather than a required engineering correction when an overhang exceeds the stone's verified unsupported limit.

### Thermal stress cracking
Cracking in stone caused by uneven heating during cutting, prevented by adequate coolant/water flow during diamond-blade cutting.
**In use:** "Increased coolant flow after noticing localized heat buildup — avoiding thermal stress cracking on this piece."
**Common misuse:** Assuming coolant only protects the blade — inadequate coolant flow also risks cracking the stone itself from cutting heat, a distinct and equally important function.

### Grain (stone)
The natural directional pattern in stone related to mineral structure, which can influence how cleanly the material cuts or fractures along a given orientation.
**In use:** "Checked grain direction before planning the cut on this piece to avoid an unnecessarily rough or fracture-prone edge."
**Common misuse:** Assuming grain direction is irrelevant to manufactured-scale stone cutting the way it might be dismissed for uniform materials — natural stone grain still affects cut quality and fracture risk.

### NDT (non-destructive testing)
Inspection methods, such as ultrasonic testing, used to detect internal flaws in stone without damaging the material, applied when visual inspection alone is inconclusive for a structural or large-scale piece.
**In use:** "Visual inspection was inconclusive on this large structural piece, so we ran an ultrasonic NDT check before finalizing the cutting plan."
**Common misuse:** Relying on NDT for every routine cut — it's typically reserved for larger structural pieces or cases where visual inspection alone can't resolve uncertainty, not a blanket requirement for all stone cutting.
