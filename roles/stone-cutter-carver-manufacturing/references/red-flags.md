# Red flags

### A slab cut without individually inspecting it for fissures or veins first
- **Usually means:** a structural weak point could sit right at a stress-bearing cut line, risking cracking after installation.
- **First question:** was this specific slab visually inspected for fissures/veins before the cutting plan was set?
- **Data to pull:** slab inspection record, planned cut lines relative to any identified fissures.

### An unsupported overhang cut without checking the specific stone's engineering rating at the actual cut thickness
- **Usually means:** the overhang may exceed the stone's safe span, risking cracking under normal use load.
- **First question:** does the requested overhang fall within this specific stone type's engineering max at this thickness, or was it assumed "granite is strong enough"?
- **Data to pull:** stone type flexural strength data, requested overhang dimension, cut thickness.

### Coolant/water flow not verified before or during diamond-blade cutting
- **Usually means:** risk of thermal stress cracking in the stone, blade damage, or both.
- **First question:** was adequate coolant delivery confirmed for both crack prevention and blade protection before cutting began?
- **Data to pull:** coolant system check record, cutting equipment log.

### Multiple slabs selected for one continuous installation without a side-by-side layout check
- **Usually means:** a visible veining/color mismatch could appear at the seam only after installation, when it can no longer be corrected.
- **First question:** were the actual intended slabs laid out in the actual intended orientation and checked for continuity before cutting?
- **Data to pull:** slab layout record, client sign-off on matched layout.

### A cutting plan proceeding without documenting fissure locations for handoff
- **Usually means:** the next worker on a multi-stage job may not know which areas to avoid, risking a cut through an identified weak point.
- **First question:** does the handoff record include the fissure map and cutting orientation rationale?
- **Data to pull:** handoff documentation, fissure assessment record.

### A load-bearing stone element's dimensions judged "substantial enough" by visual impression alone
- **Usually means:** the actual engineering margin for the intended load may not have been verified, risking failure under real use.
- **First question:** was cut thickness checked against the specific stone's engineering data for the intended span/load, or only against general appearance?
- **Data to pull:** stone engineering property reference, cut dimension record, intended load/span.

### A crack or failure occurring after installation without a clear root-cause check
- **Usually means:** the cause (fissure orientation, inadequate cooling, insufficient thickness for load) may recur on future jobs if not identified.
- **First question:** which of the three distinct causes — fissure orientation, cooling, or thickness/load mismatch — does the evidence point to?
- **Data to pull:** the failed piece's cutting/fissure record, coolant log, dimension-vs-load verification record.

### A slab rejected for minor natural variation without confirming it's actually structural
- **Usually means:** usable material may be discarded unnecessarily, and the rejection criteria itself may not be well-calibrated.
- **First question:** does the variation represent an actual structural weak point, or a cosmetic/non-structural natural characteristic?
- **Data to pull:** the specific defect's characteristics, comparison to known structural-risk patterns.
