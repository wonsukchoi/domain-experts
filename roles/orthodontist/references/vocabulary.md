# Orthodontist — Vocabulary

### Angle classification
A system classifying malocclusion by the anteroposterior relationship of the first permanent molars (Class I neutral, Class II distal, Class III mesial).
**In use:** "It's a Class II division 1 — distal molar relationship with flared, protrusive upper incisors."
**Common misuse:** Treated as if it describes the whole case; it only describes molar relationship, not skeletal pattern, crowding, or treatment need.

### Leeway space
The extra arch width freed when the smaller permanent premolars replace the larger primary canines and molars during the mixed-dentition transition.
**In use:** "We're not extracting yet — there's still 4mm of leeway space unaccounted for in this arch."
**Common misuse:** Ignored entirely in a raw crowding calculation, leading to overestimated extraction need in mixed-dentition patients.

### Bolton analysis
A tooth-size ratio comparing the combined mesiodistal width of the mandibular teeth to the maxillary teeth, used to predict whether a Class I occlusion is achievable given the existing tooth sizes.
**In use:** "The Bolton ratio is off by 2mm — we'll need interproximal reduction on the lower anteriors to get a Class I finish."
**Common misuse:** Confused with the overall space analysis; Bolton analysis addresses inter-arch tooth-size compatibility, not total arch-length discrepancy.

### CVM (cervical vertebral maturation) staging
A method of estimating skeletal growth status from the shape of the cervical vertebrae on a lateral cephalometric radiograph, used to time growth-modification treatment.
**In use:** "She's CVM stage 2 — we still have a good window for the functional appliance."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a fixed age-based rule rather than an individual skeletal-maturity assessment; chronological age and CVM stage frequently disagree.

### Growth modification
Orthopedic treatment (functional appliances, headgear) intended to redirect the growth pattern of the jaws while skeletal growth is still active.
**In use:** "This is growth modification, not just tooth movement — the goal is to change where the mandible ends up, not just where the teeth sit."
**Common misuse:** Applied to or expected from a patient past peak growth, where the same appliance produces dental compensation instead of skeletal change.

### Camouflage treatment
Orthodontic tooth movement that masks an underlying skeletal discrepancy through dental compensation, rather than correcting the skeletal relationship itself.
**In use:** "At her growth stage, this is camouflage — we're compensating for the skeletal Class III with tooth position, not correcting the jaw relationship."
**Common misuse:** Presented to the patient as equivalent to skeletal correction; camouflage has esthetic and functional limits a surgical correction doesn't.

### Anchorage
The resistance to unwanted tooth movement in teeth being used to move other teeth; a finite resource that must be planned, not assumed.
**In use:** "This is a high-anchorage-demand case — we're placing a TAD before starting canine retraction."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to be automatically sufficient from molar support alone, without an explicit plan, leading to anchorage loss mid-treatment.

### TAD (temporary anchorage device)
A small titanium screw placed in bone to serve as a fixed anchorage point for tooth movement, removed after treatment.
**In use:** "We'll use a TAD for intrusion here — there's no natural anchorage unit that can do this movement otherwise."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a universal fix rather than matched to the specific anchorage demand and movement type of the case.

### Interproximal reduction (IPR)
Removal of a small amount of enamel between adjacent teeth to create additional arch space without extraction.
**In use:** "We can gain about a millimeter with IPR across these four contacts — that closes the remaining discrepancy."
**Common misuse:** Treated as unlimited; IPR has an enamel-thickness ceiling (typically 0.2-0.5mm per contact) beyond which it risks sensitivity and structural compromise.

### Overjet vs. overbite
Overjet is the horizontal distance between the upper and lower incisor edges; overbite is the vertical overlap of the upper incisors over the lower.
**In use:** "Overjet is 7mm, overbite is within normal limits — this is a protrusion problem, not a deep-bite problem."
**Common misuse:** The two terms are used interchangeably by non-specialists, but they describe different planes of discrepancy and call for different mechanics.

### Retention
The phase, and lifelong maintenance, of holding teeth in their corrected position after active treatment ends.
**In use:** "Retention isn't a phase that ends — it's nights-only, indefinitely, because the teeth keep wanting to drift."
**Common misuse:** Treated by patients (and sometimes referring providers) as a temporary post-treatment formality rather than an indefinite commitment.

### Extraction pattern
The specific set of teeth planned for removal to resolve a space discrepancy, chosen to balance space needs, anchorage, and esthetics.
**In use:** "The extraction pattern here is both first premolars — that gives us the space and keeps anchorage symmetric."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to always mean "four premolars"; extraction pattern varies by case (single-tooth, asymmetric, second-premolar patterns) based on the specific discrepancy and anchorage needs.
