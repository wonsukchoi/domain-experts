# Vocabulary

### Fiscal constraint
The federal requirement (23 CFR 450) that an MTP/TIP's total project costs, stated in year-of-expenditure dollars, not exceed revenue reasonably expected to be available in those years.
**In use:** "The draft list isn't fiscally constrained until we inflate every project to its programmed year — right now it's just summed in today's dollars."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a one-time check against total available funding rather than a year-by-year, inflation-adjusted match between cost and revenue timing.

### Year-of-expenditure (YOE) dollars
The projected cost of a project in the actual future year it will be spent, after applying an inflation rate — as opposed to current-year (base-year) dollars.
**In use:** "That $180M is 2026 dollars; in YOE it's closer to $193M once we inflate to the 2028 programmed year."
**Common misuse:** Confused with "escalated" or "nominal" cost estimates that inflate for construction-cost growth generally but aren't tied to the specific programmed year used in the fiscal-constraint calculation.

### Four-step model
The standard travel demand modeling sequence: trip generation, trip distribution, mode choice, and trip assignment, run in that order to forecast link-level traffic volumes.
**In use:** "The mode-choice step is where transit ridership gets allocated before assignment loads the remaining trips onto the highway network."
**Common misuse:** Treated as producing a single validated "the" forecast, when it's a scenario-comparison tool whose absolute volumes need validation against counts before being quoted as a number.

### Screenline
An imaginary line crossing a set of parallel travel corridors (e.g., all river crossings), used to sum modeled volumes across those facilities and compare the total against a corresponding count of actual traffic — the standard model validation check.
**In use:** "Screenline validation on the river crossings came in at 9% deviation from counts, inside our 15% target."
**Common misuse:** Confused with checking a single link's volume against a single count station, which doesn't test the model's regional trip-distribution accuracy the way a multi-facility screenline does.

### Transportation Improvement Program (TIP)
The short-range (minimum 4-year), fiscally constrained list of specific projects an MPO commits to funding, drawn from the longer-range MTP.
**In use:** "That project's in the MTP's outer years but hasn't been programmed into the current TIP yet, so it's not eligible for obligation this cycle."
**Common misuse:** Used interchangeably with the MTP; the MTP is the long-range policy plan, the TIP is the near-term, project-specific funding commitment drawn from it.

### Air quality conformity
The Clean Air Act-driven requirement that, in nonattainment or maintenance areas, an MTP/TIP's projected regional emissions stay within an approved budget before regionally significant projects can proceed.
**In use:** "This interchange project is regionally significant, so it can't be added to the TIP until the conformity determination is updated."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to apply only to new highway capacity projects; transit and multimodal projects in a nonattainment area can also trigger or affect a conformity analysis depending on regional significance.

### Environmental justice (EJ) analysis
A comparison of the distribution of a plan's benefits and burdens between historically underserved populations and the rest of the region, required under Title VI and federal EJ guidance.
**In use:** "EJ tracts hold 28% of the region's population but are credited with only 18% of plan investment — that's a finding we have to address before adoption."
**Common misuse:** Reduced to producing a demographic map showing where EJ populations live, without computing an actual benefit-share or burden-share percentage against population share.

### Cost-effectiveness index (CEI)
FTA's Capital Investment Grant metric expressing a transit project's cost per hour of transportation-system user benefit, used to rate project cost-effectiveness for discretionary funding.
**In use:** "The BRT alignment change dropped our CEI enough to move us from a Medium to a Low cost-effectiveness rating."
**Common misuse:** Conflated with simple cost-per-rider or cost-per-mile figures, which don't capture the travel-time-savings basis the actual CEI calculation uses.

### Purpose and need
The NEPA-required statement of the problem a proposed transportation action is meant to solve, which defines and bounds the range of alternatives that must be considered.
**In use:** "We wrote the purpose and need around the safety and operational deficiency, not around 'build the interchange,' so the alternatives analysis can actually consider a roundabout."
**Common misuse:** Drafted narrowly enough to name a specific solution, which improperly forecloses the range of alternatives NEPA requires being considered.

### Fix-it-first
A prioritization default that funds state-of-good-repair (pavement, bridge condition) needs ahead of capacity expansion when revenue is constrained.
**In use:** "We're fix-it-first this cycle — the interchange gets pushed a phase because two structurally deficient bridges rank higher against the adopted criteria."
**Common misuse:** Applied as an absolute rule rather than a default that yields when a performance target (e.g., a safety or system-performance metric) is being missed and expansion is the corrective action.

### Regionally significant project
A transportation project serving a function beyond local access (added general-purpose travel lanes, new interchanges, major transit expansions) that must be individually identified in the conforming MTP/TIP in nonattainment/maintenance areas.
**In use:** "This is a local collector widening, not regionally significant, so it doesn't need its own conformity analysis."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to be determined by project cost alone; regional significance is about the project's function in the network, not its dollar size.

### Performance-based planning
The federally required practice (23 CFR 490) of setting numeric targets for safety, infrastructure condition, and system performance, and tying investment decisions to progress toward those targets.
**In use:** "Our PM2 bridge-condition target is 90% of deck area in good/fair condition by 2027 — we're at 87%, which is why the bridge program got bumped up this cycle."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a reporting exercise satisfied by publishing the targets, rather than an input that should visibly change project prioritization when a target is off track.

### Multimodal plan
A transportation plan or corridor study that allocates investment across driving, transit, walking, and biking based on the travel context of each corridor, not an equal split across modes everywhere.
**In use:** "The corridor's mostly rural with negligible pedestrian demand, so the multimodal element here is a paved shoulder, not a sidewalk network."
**Common misuse:** Interpreted as requiring every project to include every mode, rather than matching mode investment to the demand and context of the specific corridor.
