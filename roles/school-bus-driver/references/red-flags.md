# Red flags

Smell tests a transportation supervisor or safety reviewer catches early. Format for each: what it usually means, the first question to ask, and the data to pull.

## 1. Students released from the door in under 3 seconds of it opening

**Usually means:** the danger-zone mirror/visual scan is being skipped or compressed to protect route time — the full scan reliably takes closer to 5 seconds and the sequence should never be reordered to open the door and release simultaneously.

**First question:** "Walk me through the exact mirror sequence you ran before releasing students at the last three stops."

**Data to pull:** dashcam footage timestamped against door-open/release events; comparison of this driver's average door-open-to-release interval against the fleet average for the same stop type.

## 2. A specific stop location shows a repeating pattern of stop-arm violations

**Usually means:** either sightline/road-geometry issues (a curve, a hill crest, inadequate warning distance for the posted speed) making the stop hard for approaching traffic to react to in time, or a specific-driver pattern of activating warning lights later than the state-mandated distance.

**First question:** "How many violations have been logged at this stop in the last semester, and were they across multiple drivers or one?"

**Data to pull:** stop-arm violation log by location; sight-distance/speed data for the road segment; the driver's amber-light activation distance/timing at that stop versus the state-mandated minimum.

## 3. End-of-route child-check alarm or walk-through not logged on a given day

**Usually means:** the driver exited the bus without the physical rear walk-through — a check that exists specifically to catch a sleeping or hidden student the interior mirror can't reveal.

**First question:** "Pull the child-check log for this driver for the last two weeks — how many days are missing the confirmation entry?"

**Data to pull:** child-check system logs (alarm-reset timestamps) or the manual walk-through checklist; any parent/school report of a student found on a bus after route completion.

## 4. A driver's behavior-incident reports show escalating discipline language with no seating-chart change on file

**Usually means:** the driver is relying on verbal warnings alone rather than the one enforceable tool available without an administrator — a pattern that predicts either an unresolved ongoing conflict or an eventual physical incident.

**First question:** "Pull this driver's last five behavior incident reports — how many resulted in a documented seat reassignment?"

**Data to pull:** behavior incident reports; seating-chart revision history for the route; whether the transportation supervisor was radioed at level 3 (per the escalation ladder) for any of the incidents.

## 5. A crossing-control arm or stop-arm mechanism logged as defective on the pre-trip, but the bus ran the route anyway

**Usually means:** a spare-vehicle shortage or schedule pressure overrode the hold requirement — running a route with a non-functioning stop arm or crossing-control arm removes the one legal and physical safeguard for road-crossing students.

**First question:** "Who authorized this bus to run the route after the defect was logged, and was a substitute dispatched?"

**Data to pull:** pre-trip defect log timestamp versus pull-out time; substitute-vehicle dispatch log; any incident report for that route/date.

## 6. Dashcam or aide reports show students being signaled or allowed to cross behind the bus

**Usually means:** either a driver shortcut under time pressure or a stop positioned such that crossing in front isn't physically practical — both require a fix, since crossing behind the bus removes the driver's sightline entirely regardless of how carefully it's done.

**First question:** "Is this a one-time incident or does the stop's geometry make front-crossing impractical every day?"

**Data to pull:** dashcam footage from the rear/side camera; stop-location geometry (driveway width, parking, obstruction); whether the stop needs to be relocated rather than the driver retrained.

## 7. Railroad-crossing stop position inconsistent or not verifiable on video review

**Usually means:** the driver is estimating the 15–50 foot stop distance loosely, or treating a crossing with visible gates/signals as exempt from the full stop-look-listen procedure.

**First question:** "Show me the last five railroad-crossing passes on video — where does the bus actually stop relative to the nearest rail?"

**Data to pull:** dashcam/GPS timestamped footage at known crossings; whether the crossing qualifies for the official-flagger/exempt-crossing exception under the state code, or requires the full stop regardless.

## 8. A single student or student pair generates repeated bus-stop conduct complaints from parents

**Usually means:** an unresolved seating or supervision gap that the driver hasn't escalated through the transportation office — parent complaints at the stop (not aboard the bus) often precede an aboard-the-bus incident by weeks.

**First question:** "How many parent complaints have come in on this stop or these students in the last month, and what's been done in response?"

**Data to pull:** parent complaint log; behavior incident reports for the same students; whether the transportation office has looped in the school administrator for a joint intervention.

## 9. Amber warning lights activated less than the state-mandated distance/time before the stop, measured across multiple stops

**Usually means:** the driver is compressing the warning phase to save route time, which shortens the reaction window for approaching traffic below the margin the state code assumes.

**First question:** "Pull the telematics data for amber-light activation distance at this driver's last ten stops against the posted speed at each."

**Data to pull:** telematics/GPS speed and light-activation-event data; the applicable state pupil-transportation code's minimum activation distance by posted speed; comparison against fleet average.
