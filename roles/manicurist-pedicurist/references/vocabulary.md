# Manicurist / Pedicurist — Vocabulary

### Eponychium
The living skin at the base of the nail plate that seals the nail fold against contaminants — distinct from the cuticle.
**In use:** "I pushed the eponychium back gently but I'm not cutting into it, just trimming the dead cuticle on top."
**Common misuse:** Techs and clients say "cuticle" to mean the whole fold and ask to have it "cut clean," when the living eponychium underneath is what actually shouldn't be cut.

### Cuticle
The dead, thin layer of tissue attached to the nail plate's surface, distinct from the living eponychium beneath it.
**In use:** "Her cuticle was overgrown onto the plate, so I trimmed it back — didn't touch the fold itself."
**Common misuse:** Used interchangeably with eponychium, which leads to cutting living tissue under the label of routine cuticle work.

### Onycholysis
Separation of the nail plate from the nail bed, usually starting at the free edge.
**In use:** "There's onycholysis on the middle finger — I'm not applying product over that lifted section until it's checked."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to be a cosmetic gap that fresh product will "fix" by bonding over it, when the underlying cause (trauma, infection, psoriasis, thyroid) is what needs addressing first.

### Free edge
The portion of the nail plate that extends past the fingertip, unattached to the nail bed.
**In use:** "Shape the free edge to a soft square before applying tips."
**Common misuse:** Confused with the entire visible nail plate rather than just the unattached tip portion — relevant when describing exactly where a discoloration or lifting starts.

### MMA (methyl methacrylate)
A monomer once common in nail enhancements, now banned or restricted in most US states because it bonds too aggressively to the natural nail and doesn't break down safely with standard removal methods or techniques.
**In use:** "This set won't soak off after 20 minutes and it's pulling the plate when I test the edge — I think it's MMA, not EMA."
**Common misuse:** Treated by clients as simply "the strong, long-lasting kind," when the point of concern is that its over-adhesion makes safe removal difficult and it carries a higher allergic-sensitization rate.

### Soak-off gel vs. hard gel
Soak-off gel is formulated to break down in acetone within roughly 10–15 minutes; hard (builder) gel is not designed to soak off and is removed primarily by filing.
**In use:** "Check which product this is before you reach for the acetone — if it's hard gel, soaking alone won't take it off."
**Common misuse:** Both are called "gel" and assumed to share a removal method, leading to over-soaking hard gel (wasted time, dry skin) or mechanically filing soak-off gel unnecessarily (unneeded plate abrasion).

### Toxic trio
Shorthand for three ingredients historically common in nail polish — dibutyl phthalate (DBP), toluene, and formaldehyde — now commonly excluded from polish marketed as "3-free" or higher.
**In use:** "This polish line is 5-free, so the toxic trio plus two others are out, but I still work with the exhaust fan on."
**Common misuse:** "N-free" labeling is read by clients as "non-toxic," when it only means the listed ingredients are absent — other irritants can remain and ventilation still matters regardless of the label.

### Onychomycosis
A fungal infection of the nail plate, typically presenting as thickening, yellowing, and crumbling at the free edge.
**In use:** "That thickened yellow nail isn't just a ridge — I want to rule out onychomycosis before doing any enhancement on it."
**Common misuse:** Any discolored or thickened nail is assumed fungal and treated cosmetically (buffed, polished over) rather than referred, or conversely any discoloration is assumed fungal when it could be bacterial or traumatic.

### Paronychia
Infection of the skin fold surrounding the nail, presenting as redness, swelling, warmth, and sometimes pus.
**In use:** "That's not a hangnail, that's paronychia — I'm not doing cuticle work on that finger today."
**Common misuse:** Dismissed as an ordinary hangnail and serviced (pushed, trimmed) rather than left alone and referred, which can worsen the infection.

### Contact time (disinfectant)
The minimum continuous wet-surface time an EPA-registered disinfectant must sit on an implement to achieve its labeled kill claim, commonly 10 minutes for hospital-grade products used in nail salons.
**In use:** "These pushed 10 minutes in solution three minutes ago — they're not ready yet, grab the backup set."
**Common misuse:** Treated as the time spent wiping or the time implements sit in a tray at all, rather than the specific continuous-wet-contact duration the label requires.

### Overfiling / plate thinning
Removal of too much of the natural nail plate's surface through repeated or high-pressure filing/buffing, leaving the plate thinner and more flexible than its natural state.
**In use:** "I'm using the lowest grit that still works — this plate's already thin from overfiling at her last few fills."
**Common misuse:** Attributed only to e-file misuse, when hand-filing or buffing too frequently or too aggressively causes the same thinning.

### Credo blade / callus blade
A bladed foot-care tool used to mechanically remove thickened callus tissue; restricted or banned outright in some states due to injury risk.
**In use:** "I'm not using the credo blade on her — she mentioned reduced circulation, so it's file-only today."
**Common misuse:** Used routinely on any client with visible callus, without checking for circulation or diabetes status that raises the risk of a slow-healing nick.

### Melanonychia
A brown-to-black streak or band running the length of the nail plate.
**In use:** "That streak on her thumbnail is new since her last visit — I'm recommending she get it looked at before I do anything decorative on that nail."
**Common misuse:** Assumed decorative or a normal pigmentation variant because "some people just have dark nails," without checking whether it's new, changing, or isolated to a single digit — the pattern that raises melanoma concern.

### Lifting
Separation of applied product (gel, acrylic, dip) from the natural nail plate before the expected regrowth point.
**In use:** "This lifted after four days, not four weeks — that's a prep problem, not a bad batch."
**Common misuse:** Blamed on the product brand by default, when the far more common cause is oil/moisture left under the product or insufficient plate abrasion before application.
