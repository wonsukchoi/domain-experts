# Manicurist / Pedicurist — Playbook

## Removal-method decision (fallback order)

1. **Soak-off gel or well-cured EMA product, no resistance past 15 minutes** — standard acetone-and-foil soak-off; no mechanical assist needed beyond light buffing of residue.
2. **Hard/builder gel, or acrylic that softens but doesn't fully release by 15–20 minutes** — acetone soak to soften, then controlled hand-filing or e-file at low grit to remove the remaining bonded layer; never pry or peel.
3. **Product still hard and adhered past 20–25 minutes of soaking, or peels the natural nail's own surface layers when tested at the edge** — suspect MMA-based product; switch to e-file-led mechanical removal at the lowest grit that makes progress, never continued soaking or forced peeling.
4. **Any resistance case where the natural nail plate looks thin, flexible, or translucent after removal** — stop product application for that client's next visit; recommend a bare-nail recovery period (typically 2–4 weeks) before any new enhancement.

## Soak-off protocol (filled example)

1. Rough up the top coat/sealer layer lightly with a fine file (180–240 grit) so acetone can penetrate — do not file into the color or base layer.
2. Saturate a cotton pad with 100% acetone, place over the nail, wrap in foil.
3. Hold at 15 minutes for soak-off gel; check at 15 minutes for hard gel/acrylic and re-wrap in 5-minute increments if not fully released, to a 25-minute ceiling before switching to mechanical assist (see removal-method decision above).
4. Gently push off softened product with a cuticle pusher — if it resists more than light pressure, it isn't ready; re-wrap rather than scrape.
5. Buff residue lightly, condition the plate and surrounding skin (cuticle oil), and inspect the exposed nail plate before any new product goes on.

## Disinfection contact-time budget (filled example, one chair, one afternoon)

| Client | Service | Booked slot | Buffer to next | Disinfection need |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| A | Full-set acrylic fill | 1:00–2:30 (90 min) | 15 min | Full metal implement set — needs 10-min EPA-labeled wet contact before reuse |
| B | Gel manicure | 2:45–3:30 (45 min) | 15 min | Full set, or pre-disinfected backup set if turnover runs tight |
| C | Spa pedicure | 3:45–4:45 (60 min) | — | Footbath liner change + basin disinfection cycle (see below) |

Reconciliation: if Client A's service overruns the 90-minute slot (e.g., an unplanned partial soak-off adds 6 minutes, finishing at 2:36 instead of 2:30), the 15-minute buffer absorbs it but leaves only 9 minutes before Client B's 2:45 start — one minute short of the 10-minute disinfectant contact-time floor on the implements just used. The fix is a second, already-disinfected implement set staged for exactly this changeover, not shortening the wet-contact time on the first set or delaying Client B.

## Pedicure footbath protocol (per-client, filled example)

1. Between clients: empty basin fully, scrub with soap/water to remove visible debris and biofilm, then apply an EPA-registered hospital-grade disinfectant for its full labeled contact time (commonly 10 minutes) with the jets run if the unit has a recirculating line — jetted/whirlpool basins harbor biofilm in the plumbing, not just the visible bowl, which is why a wipe-only clean doesn't clear them.
2. If using a disposable liner system: change the liner every client, no exception, and still run the basin's own disinfection cycle beneath it per the manufacturer's schedule (commonly daily, not just at liner change).
3. Log date/time of each full basin disinfection cycle on a basin-maintenance sheet, separate from per-client liner changes — the log is what shows a state-board inspector the plumbing-level cleaning happened, not just the visible surface.
4. Screen/filter units: remove and clean the intake screen at the same cadence as the full disinfection cycle — debris trapped behind the screen is a documented source of resistant biofilm even when the basin itself tests clean.
