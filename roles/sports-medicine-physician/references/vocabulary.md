# Vocabulary

### Graduated Return to Sport (GRTS)
The 6-stage, minimum-24-hours-per-stage concussion recovery protocol from symptom-limited activity through full return to competition, with mandatory drop-back on symptom recurrence.
**In use:** "He's at stage 3 — sport-specific drills, no contact yet — earliest full clearance is four days out if every stage stays symptom-free."
**Common misuse:** Treating "feels fine at rest" as equivalent to completing the protocol, or allowing an athlete to skip a stage because a game is that week.

### Seattle criteria
The athlete-specific ECG interpretation standard distinguishing normal training-related adaptations from pathologic findings, reducing false-positive disqualifications compared to general-population ECG criteria.
**In use:** "Isolated voltage criteria for LVH reads normal under Seattle criteria — no workup needed if that's the only finding."
**Common misuse:** Interpreting an athlete's ECG with general-population criteria and disqualifying for a training-adaptation finding that isn't actually pathologic.

### RED-S (Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport)
A syndrome of impaired physiological function (menstrual dysfunction, low bone density, impaired metabolic rate, among others) caused by low energy availability relative to training demand — broader than the older "female athlete triad" and applies to male athletes too.
**In use:** "This isn't just a stress fracture — amenorrhea plus the fracture plus the training log is RED-S, and it needs a dietitian and a load reduction, not just a cast."
**Common misuse:** Treating menstrual dysfunction, low bone density, and a stress injury as coincidental separate referrals instead of one energy-availability syndrome.

### Second impact syndrome
Catastrophic, often fatal cerebral swelling that can occur when an athlete sustains a second head impact before fully recovering from a first concussion.
**In use:** "The reason there's no same-day return after a diagnosed concussion is second impact syndrome, not caution for its own sake."
**Common misuse:** Used loosely to mean "any concussion that happens twice," rather than the specific mechanism of a second impact during an unresolved recovery window.

### Exertional heat stroke
Hyperthermia with central nervous system dysfunction (altered mentation, seizure, coma) occurring during or after exertion, distinguished from heat exhaustion by the CNS involvement.
**In use:** "She's confused and hot after the race — that's heat stroke, not heat exhaustion, and the tub goes on now."
**Common misuse:** Waiting for oral or tympanic temperature confirmation (both unreliable in this setting) instead of using rectal thermometry and treating on presentation.

### Cool first, transport second
The exertional heat stroke management principle that on-site cold-water immersion takes priority over ambulance transport, because mortality tracks cumulative time above the critical core-temperature threshold, not time to hospital arrival.
**In use:** "Tub's already set up at the medical tent — we cool here, transport once she's under 102°F."
**Common misuse:** Defaulting to standard EMS "scoop and go" protocol, which is correct for most emergencies but delays the single intervention (cooling) that determines outcome here.

### Bone stress injury (Fredericson grading)
An MRI-graded spectrum from periosteal edema (grade 1) to a discrete fracture line (grade 4a/4b), used to set a graduated return-to-running timeline rather than treating all "stress fractures" as one entity.
**In use:** "It's a grade 2, not a grade 4 — different timeline and no orthopedic consult needed yet."
**Common misuse:** Calling every finding on this spectrum a "stress fracture," which conflates a 3-week recovery (grade 1) with a 12-week, surgical-consult case (grade 4b).

### Team physician conflict of interest
The structural tension between a team physician's employment or payment by a team, school, or league and the duty of care owed to the individual athlete, which requires active management rather than passive trust.
**In use:** "I'm documenting this clearance the same way I would in a meaningless preseason scrimmage, because the conflict doesn't go away just because it's the semifinal."
**Common misuse:** Assuming the conflict is resolved by good intentions or by everyone "wanting what's best for the kid," rather than treating it as a standing structural condition that needs explicit, documented handling on every close call.

### Sickle cell trait (exertional sickling risk)
A genetic carrier state (not sickle cell disease) associated with a real but manageable risk of exertional rhabdomyolysis and collapse under conditions of extreme exertion, heat, and dehydration.
**In use:** "Trait-positive doesn't mean disqualified — it means a graduated conditioning build and closer hydration monitoring, per NCAA screening guidance."
**Common misuse:** Treating a positive trait screen as grounds for blanket exclusion from sport, rather than a modifiable-risk conditioning issue.

### AHA 14-element preparticipation cardiovascular screen
The standardized 14-item personal-history, family-history, and physical-exam checklist used to flag athletes needing further cardiac workup before clearance.
**In use:** "Item 11 was positive — family history of a cardiomyopathy — so this one needs an ECG and a cardiology referral before he's cleared, checklist or not."
**Common misuse:** Administering the checklist as a formality and not escalating a positive item to actual ECG/echo/cardiology follow-up.

### VOMS (Vestibular-Ocular Motor Screening)
A structured concussion-assessment component testing smooth pursuit, saccades, convergence, and vestibulo-ocular reflex for symptom provocation, complementing symptom-checklist tools like SCAT6.
**In use:** "SCAT6 symptom score was borderline, but VOMS provoked clear dizziness on convergence testing — that's enough to hold him out."
**Common misuse:** Skipping VOMS and relying on the symptom checklist alone, which misses concussions that provoke symptoms only under specific vestibular-ocular load.

### Same-day return-to-play
Clearance to resume play within the same competition or practice session as an injury, appropriate for some musculoskeletal injuries but never appropriate after a diagnosed concussion.
**In use:** "Ankle's a grade 1 sprain with full functional range — same-day return is reasonable here, unlike the concussion two lockers down."
**Common misuse:** Applying the same-day-return mindset to concussion, where the graduated protocol's minimum multi-day timeline is not a discretionary judgment call.
