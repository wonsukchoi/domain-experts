# Payroll and Timekeeping Clerk — Vocabulary

### Regular rate of pay
The FLSA-defined hourly rate used to calculate overtime — total workweek compensation (including non-discretionary bonuses, shift differentials, and commissions) divided by total hours worked, not the posted base hourly wage.
**In use:** "We can't just pay overtime at $18 an hour this period — the production bonus changes the regular rate to $21.26."
**Common misuse:** treated as identical to the base hourly rate; generalists assume overtime is always base rate × 1.5.

### Non-discretionary bonus
A bonus the employer is contractually or customarily obligated to pay according to a pre-announced formula, schedule, or threshold — must be included in the regular rate of pay for overtime purposes.
**In use:** "The attendance bonus is non-discretionary because it's paid automatically whenever an employee hits 100% attendance for the month — that has to go into the OT calc."
**Common misuse:** classified as "discretionary" internally just because it's called a bonus, without checking whether the employer actually retains discretion over amount and timing.

### Disposable earnings
Gross pay minus legally required deductions only (taxes, FICA, mandatory retirement contributions) — the base against which garnishment percentage caps are applied.
**In use:** "The 25% garnishment cap applies to disposable earnings, not gross pay — so we back out taxes and FICA first, but not the voluntary 401(k) contribution."
**Common misuse:** calculated against gross pay (overstates the allowable withholding) or against net pay after voluntary deductions like health insurance (understates it).

### CCPA cap
The Consumer Credit Protection Act's limit on wage garnishment — generally the lesser of 25% of disposable earnings or the amount by which disposable earnings exceed 30 times the federal minimum wage; higher caps (up to 65%) apply to child-support orders with arrears.
**In use:** "This consumer garnishment can't take more than the CCPA cap, even though the creditor's order asks for 30%."
**Common misuse:** applied as a flat 25% to every type of garnishment, ignoring that support orders have a different, higher statutory cap.

### Garnishment priority
The legally mandated order in which multiple active garnishment orders for the same employee are satisfied — generally federal tax levies and child support before consumer-debt garnishments — independent of the order in which they were received.
**In use:** "The credit-card garnishment arrived first, but the child-support order still gets priority regardless of filing date."
**Common misuse:** orders processed strictly by arrival date, shorting a higher-priority legal obligation.

### Off-cycle payroll correction
A manual, out-of-schedule payment issued to correct an error discovered after a regular payroll run has already transmitted, as opposed to waiting to fix it in the next regular cycle.
**In use:** "This was an underpayment and the state requires correction within a few days of discovery, so it needs an off-cycle check, not a fix on the next cycle."
**Common misuse:** used as the default response to any error, when many overpayment corrections can legally wait for the next regular cycle with proper employee consent.

### Final pay
Wages owed to an employee upon termination, subject to state-specific (often faster than the normal pay-cycle) timing requirements that vary by whether the termination was voluntary or involuntary.
**In use:** "This is an involuntary termination in a state that requires final pay same-day, so it can't wait for the normal biweekly run."
**Common misuse:** processed on the standard pay-cycle schedule as if termination timing rules didn't exist, risking a state wage-and-hour violation.

### Timesheet exception
A flagged discrepancy on a timesheet — a missing punch, unapproved overtime, or a shift that doesn't match the posted schedule — that must be resolved before a payroll run locks.
**In use:** "There are three timesheet exceptions still open with two hours until the run locks — we need manager sign-off now."
**Common misuse:** treated as low-priority clerical noise rather than the cheapest point in the process to catch an error before it becomes a post-lock correction.

### Payroll register
The detailed report of every pay component (wages, bonuses, deductions, taxes, garnishments) for a completed payroll run, used to reconcile against the general ledger.
**In use:** "The payroll register total is $340 off from the GL payroll-expense account — let's find which component doesn't match."
**Common misuse:** treated as identical to the paycheck summary an employee sees, when it's the full internal accounting detail including employer-side items employees never see.

### Exempt vs. non-exempt
The FLSA classification determining whether an employee is entitled to overtime pay (non-exempt) or not (exempt), based on job duties and salary level tests — not job title.
**In use:** "Just because the title says 'manager' doesn't make this employee exempt — the duties test says otherwise, and they've been logging overtime every week."
**Common misuse:** classification assumed from job title or salaried-vs-hourly pay structure alone, without applying the actual duties and salary-level tests.

### Regular-rate recalculation trigger
The condition (a non-discretionary bonus, commission, or differential paid for a workweek containing overtime hours) that requires reopening and recalculating the overtime premium for that period.
**In use:** "The quarterly bonus just posted for a period with overtime in it — that's a recalculation trigger, not a one-off adjustment."
**Common misuse:** missed entirely because the bonus payment and the overtime hours were processed in what looked like unrelated payroll transactions.
