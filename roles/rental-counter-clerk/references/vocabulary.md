# Rental Counter Clerk — Vocabulary

### LDW (limited damage waiver)
An optional, priced product a renter buys to cap their liability for accidental damage to the rented item at a fixed per-incident deductible, commonly 10-15% of the rental subtotal.
**In use:** "The LDW is $112.50 on this rental — it caps your damage exposure at the $250 deductible instead of full repair cost."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a free courtesy the clerk can wave through on a verbal insurance claim; it's a coverage product with a documentation requirement (a COI) to decline validly.

### COI (certificate of insurance)
A document from the customer's insurer confirming coverage and naming the rental store as an additional insured, required on file before a damage waiver can be validly declined on higher-value equipment.
**In use:** "I can't decline the waiver without a COI on file — send it over before I release the machine."
**Common misuse:** Accepted as "they said they have insurance"; a verbal claim without the actual document doesn't satisfy the requirement.

### Authorization hold
A temporary reservation of funds on a customer's card that reduces available credit without actually charging the account; released after a clean return inspection.
**In use:** "That's a hold, not a charge — it'll drop off your card in 3 to 5 business days after we log the return."
**Common misuse:** Confused with an actual charge; customers (and sometimes clerks) treat the hold amount as money already spent.

### Deposit
An amount actually collected (not just held) up front, distinct from an authorization hold; more common on storage rentals and some vehicle classes than on equipment.
**In use:** "This is a deposit — it's collected today and applied to your first month, not released back to your card."
**Common misuse:** Used interchangeably with "hold"; the two have different accounting and different customer expectations about when funds move.

### Walk-around inspection
The joint pickup or return inspection where the clerk and customer document existing condition against a diagram or photo set, both parties signing or initialing.
**In use:** "Let's do the walk-around before you take it — I need your initials on the diagram."
**Common misuse:** Skipped or rushed as a formality; it's the sole evidence base for any damage dispute at return.

### Full-to-full (fuel/charge policy)
A return-condition term requiring the item to come back with the same fuel or charge level it left with, avoiding a per-unit refueling fee.
**In use:** "It's full-to-full — bring it back with a full tank or the refuel fee kicks in."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to be prorated by how much fuel is missing; most stores apply a flat fee regardless of the shortfall amount.

### Checked-in status
The RMS state confirming a returned item has actually been physically received and inspected, distinct from its scheduled return time.
**In use:** "It's not checked in yet — I can't promise it to the next customer until it is."
**Common misuse:** Treated as automatic once the scheduled return time passes; a late or not-yet-inspected return isn't checked in regardless of what the clock says.

### RMS (rental management system)
The software system of record for reservations, inventory status, contract terms, and condition photos (e.g., Point of Rental, Booqable, Wynne Systems).
**In use:** "Check the RMS for the actual contract terms before you quote a late fee from memory."
**Common misuse:** Treated as just a booking calendar; it's the authoritative record for contract terms and inspection history in a dispute.

### Damage diagram
The pickup/return condition-documentation form (paper or digital) showing existing marks on a schematic of the item, initialed by both parties.
**In use:** "That scratch is already marked on the diagram from pickup — no charge."
**Common misuse:** Filled out from memory after the fact rather than in front of the customer at the actual pickup or return moment.

### Per-incident deductible
The fixed dollar amount a customer owes toward a covered damage claim when the damage waiver is active, with the waiver covering the remainder.
**In use:** "With the waiver active, you're only on the hook for the $250 deductible, not the full $340 repair."
**Common misuse:** Confused with the waiver price itself; the deductible is what's owed on a claim, the waiver fee is what's paid up front regardless of whether damage occurs.

### Lien sale
The self-storage industry's statutory process for selling a delinquent tenant's stored property to recover unpaid rent, used in place of a court eviction process.
**In use:** "The account's past the cure period — start the lien-sale notice sequence."
**Common misuse:** Initiated informally without following the state-mandated notice and cure-period sequence, which invalidates the sale and exposes the store to liability.

### Utilization rate
The industry metric measuring what percentage of available rental-days an item or fleet actually generates revenue during, used at the manager level to decide fleet size and pricing.
**In use:** "That excavator's utilization is running low this quarter — it's a candidate to sell off, not re-stock."
**Common misuse:** Something a counter clerk is asked to optimize directly; it's a manager-level fleet decision the clerk's booking accuracy feeds into, not a metric the clerk chases at the counter.

### Cross-rental (substitute unit)
Offering a customer a different, typically comparable unit when their reserved item isn't actually available at pickup, sourced from store stock or a partner store.
**In use:** "Their reserved tiller isn't back yet — cross-rent them the backup unit so they're not standing here empty-handed."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a downgrade to apologize for; when comparable, it's a service recovery, not a lesser substitute.
