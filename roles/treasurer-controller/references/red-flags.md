# Red flags — what a treasurer/controller notices instantly

Smell tests with thresholds. Any single one can be innocent; two or three together are a pattern.

### Covenant headroom smaller than the metric's normal historical quarter-to-quarter variability
- **Usually means:** a forecast that technically clears the covenant threshold may still carry real breach risk if normal noise in the metric exceeds the current cushion.
- **First question:** "How does current headroom compare to this metric's typical quarter-to-quarter swing historically?"
- **Data to pull:** covenant metric trend over recent quarters, historical variance/swing size, current forecast headroom.

### A profitable income statement being cited as evidence the company's cash position is healthy
- **Usually means:** profitability and liquidity are different, faster-moving risk categories — a profitable company can still face a genuine cash crunch independently of its P&L.
- **First question:** "What does the rolling cash flow forecast actually show, separate from the profit and loss statement?"
- **Data to pull:** rolling 13-week cash flow forecast, current cash position and near-term obligations.

### Cash concentrated entirely in one bank/instrument beyond insured or safe limits, or entirely in the lowest-yield option with no liquidity ladder structure
- **Usually means:** either excess concentration risk or a quiet opportunity cost from idle cash — both mistakes that don't show up as an obvious line-item problem.
- **First question:** "Is cash structured across a deliberate liquidity ladder matched to actual forecasted need, or sitting in one place by default?"
- **Data to pull:** current cash/investment allocation by instrument and counterparty, investment policy statement (if one exists).

### A financial reporting characterization changed to accommodate operational pressure from a business unit or leader
- **Usually means:** reporting independence is being compromised, undermining the entire premise of the controller function regardless of how reasonable the pressure seems in the moment.
- **First question:** "Does this characterization reflect the actual financial substance, or has it been adjusted to please someone under pressure?"
- **Data to pull:** the specific item and characterization in question, the underlying financial substance versus the presented version.

### A lender/bank relationship engaged seriously for the first time only when a waiver or emergency facility is urgently needed
- **Usually means:** relationship capital wasn't built during stable periods, leaving far less flexibility exactly when it's needed most.
- **First question:** "When did we last proactively engage this lender outside of an active transactional need?"
- **Data to pull:** history of lender communication and relationship-review cadence, timing relative to the current need.

### External audit findings being discovered by the audit team rather than surfaced proactively by the company
- **Usually means:** an adversarial audit posture is producing a worse audit and less real assurance, since the value of an audit depends on transparency, not on making auditors dig for problems.
- **First question:** "Was this issue surfaced to auditors proactively, or did they find it independently?"
- **Data to pull:** the issue in question, timeline of when it was known internally versus when it was raised with auditors.

### A disclosure materiality decision made against a fixed percentage threshold with no check against what a reasonably informed board member or investor would want to know
- **Usually means:** a mechanical threshold can miss items that are qualitatively material (related-party, trend-changing, or governance-relevant) even if quantitatively small.
- **First question:** "Would a reasonably informed board member or investor want to know this, regardless of whether it clears a fixed percentage threshold?"
- **Data to pull:** the specific item under consideration, its quantitative size and any qualitative factors (related-party, trend, governance relevance).

### A treasury policy decision (investment of cash, hedging) made ad hoc rather than against a documented, pre-approved policy
- **Usually means:** decisions may be made reactively under pressure rather than against acceptable-risk parameters set in advance during a calmer period.
- **First question:** "Does a documented investment/hedging policy exist covering this decision, or is it being decided case by case?"
- **Data to pull:** the current investment policy statement (or its absence), the specific decision being made against it.
