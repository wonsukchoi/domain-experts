### ALARA (As Low As Reasonably Achievable)
The governing principle for balancing diagnostic image quality against acoustic-energy exposure — output power, exam duration, and mode are all managed together, not independently.
**In use:** "We're already at diagnostic quality, so let's drop the output and shorten the Doppler run instead of pushing for a slightly cleaner trace."
**Common misuse:** Treating it as "keep gain low" alone, while ignoring that TI/MI and total exam/Doppler time are the actual levers.

### Thermal Index (TI)
An estimate of potential tissue temperature rise from the current acoustic settings; reported separately for soft tissue (TIS), bone (TIB), and cranial bone (TIC) because bone absorbs differently.
**In use:** "TI's at 0.9 in spectral Doppler on this first-trimester scan — let's get the measurement and get out rather than optimize the trace further."
**Common misuse:** Assuming B-mode-only scanning is automatically low-risk in the same way regardless of which TI mode and duration are in play.

### Mechanical Index (MI)
An estimate of the likelihood of cavitation (mechanical bioeffects) from the acoustic pulse, distinct from thermal risk.
**In use:** "MI's fine for this abdominal exam, but I'd still keep contrast-mode time short."
**Common misuse:** Conflating MI with TI as if they measure the same risk — they describe different bioeffect mechanisms.

### Resistive Index (RI) / Pourcelot Index
A ratio of peak systolic minus end-diastolic velocity to peak systolic velocity, describing downstream vascular resistance; interpreted against organ- and context-specific thresholds, not a universal cutoff.
**In use:** "RI's 0.84 on the transplant kidney — that's above threshold, correlate with post-op day and creatinine before calling it rejection versus ATN."
**Common misuse:** Reporting the number without applying the organ-specific threshold and clinical timing that give it meaning.

### Graded compression
A technique of applying steady transducer pressure to displace overlying bowel gas and improve visualization (used for the appendix), distinct from venous compression testing.
**In use:** "Graded compression got the cecum out of the way — now I can actually measure the appendix."
**Common misuse:** Confusing it with the venous compressibility test, where the point is whether the vessel itself collapses, not displacing something in front of it.

### Aliasing
A color/spectral Doppler artifact where velocity exceeds the Nyquist limit set by the current pulse repetition frequency (PRF) and scale, producing wraparound rather than a true measurement of turbulence.
**In use:** "That's aliasing, not a stenosis — let's bump the scale and shift baseline before we measure again."
**Common misuse:** Reading wraparound as pathologic turbulence or an elevated velocity without first correcting the settings that produced it.

### Acoustic shadowing vs. enhancement
Shadowing is signal loss posterior to a strongly attenuating or reflecting structure (clean shadow = calculus/gas edge; dirty/dropout shadow = gas); enhancement is increased posterior signal behind a fluid-filled structure.
**In use:** "Clean posterior shadowing behind that focus — consistent with a stone, not just bowel gas."
**Common misuse:** Treating any dark area behind a structure as equivalent, when clean vs. dirty shadowing point to different causes.

### Nuchal translucency (NT)
The fluid-filled space at the back of the fetal neck, measured between CRL 45-84mm (roughly 11-14 weeks) as part of first-trimester aneuploidy risk screening.
**In use:** "CRL's at 78mm, still in window — NT measures 1.8mm, within normal range for gestational age."
**Common misuse:** Measuring or reporting NT outside the valid CRL window and treating it as an equally valid risk marker.

### Tardus parvus
A Doppler waveform pattern of prolonged systolic acceleration time and blunted peak, seen distal to a significant arterial stenosis.
**In use:** "Distal to the anastomosis we've got a tardus parvus waveform — that's consistent with proximal stenosis, not just a slow vessel."
**Common misuse:** Calling any generically dampened or rounded waveform "tardus parvus" without actually measuring acceleration time against threshold.

### Sonographic Murphy sign
Maximal patient-reported tenderness elicited by direct probe pressure over the sonographically visualized gallbladder — a major criterion for acute cholecystitis, but negative in up to 70% of true cases.
**In use:** "Sonographic Murphy's negative, but wall's thickened and there's pericholecystic fluid — that's still two major criteria."
**Common misuse:** Treating it as interchangeable with the clinical (manually palpated) Murphy sign, or treating a negative result as ruling out cholecystitis by itself.

### IOTA Simple Rules
A validated set of ultrasound features (B = benign, M = malignant) for triaging adnexal masses, with an explicit "inconclusive" category when both or neither feature set is present.
**In use:** "Both a B and an M feature are present here — that's inconclusive under Simple Rules, not a coin flip toward benign."
**Common misuse:** Forcing a binary benign/malignant call on a mass that the rules themselves classify as inconclusive.

### Nyquist limit
The maximum velocity a given pulse repetition frequency (PRF) and scale setting can measure without aliasing; velocities above it wrap around rather than displaying correctly.
**In use:** "We're past the Nyquist limit at this scale — raise the PRF before you trust that velocity number."
**Common misuse:** Diagnosing a stenosis from an aliased signal without first adjusting scale/PRF to confirm the true velocity.

### Real-time correlation
The diagnostic information available only during live scanning — compressibility, peristalsis, respiratory phasicity, torsion/detorsion — that a saved static image cannot fully reproduce.
**In use:** "It compressed fully in real time even though the saved clip makes it look borderline — that's why the worksheet note matters."
**Common misuse:** Relying solely on stored static images during a later review without accounting for the dynamic information that only existed during the live exam.

### Technically limited study
A documented statement that a specific structure or segment could not be fully assessed due to a specific, named obstacle (gas, body habitus, guarding) — distinct from a negative finding.
**In use:** "This is a technically limited assessment of the left calf segment, not a negative study for that segment."
**Common misuse:** Omitting the "technically limited" language and letting an incomplete assessment read as a clean negative in the final report.
