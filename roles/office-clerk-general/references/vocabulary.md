# Office Clerk, General — Vocabulary

### Hard deadline
A deadline with a real external cost to missing it — a courier cutoff, a same-day filing requirement, a promised callback.
**In use:** "The rush shipment is a hard deadline, the vendor reply isn't — that's what decides the order."
**Common misuse:** Treating every deadline someone states out loud as hard, when most stated deadlines are actually soft (preferred, not required).

### Soft deadline
A deadline with no real external cost to slipping a day — "sometime this week," "when you get a chance."
**In use:** "That's soft — due Friday means Friday, not today."
**Common misuse:** Assuming soft means unimportant; a soft-deadline task can still matter, it just has flexibility in timing.

### Triage
The act of sorting competing tasks by actual consequence-of-delay before starting any of them, rather than working in arrival order.
**In use:** "I triage the list every morning before I touch anything."
**Common misuse:** Using "triage" to mean simply "do the urgent-labeled stuff first" without checking whether the labels are accurate.

### Batching
Grouping same-type tasks together (all filing, then all data entry) instead of interleaving them by arrival time.
**In use:** "I batch the filing into one afternoon block instead of doing it between calls."
**Common misuse:** Batching so rigidly that a genuinely hard-deadline item waits for its "slot" instead of being handled immediately.

### Context-switching cost
The extra time lost re-orienting when jumping between unrelated task-types, beyond the tasks' own time estimates.
**In use:** "The context-switching cost is why three interrupted filing sessions take longer than one continuous one."
**Common misuse:** Treating it as an excuse to never multitask, when some interruptions (a ringing phone) aren't optional.

### Deferral
A deliberate, communicated decision to postpone a task to a specific later time, as opposed to simply not getting to it.
**In use:** "That's a deferral to tomorrow, not a drop — it's in tomorrow's list."
**Common misuse:** Using "deferred" retroactively to describe something that just didn't get done, with no plan for when it will.

### Capacity
The realistic number of task-minutes available in a period after fixed/hard commitments are subtracted.
**In use:** "After the hard items, I've got 315 minutes of capacity left for everything else."
**Common misuse:** Confusing capacity (time actually available) with the total workday length (time nominally available before subtracting fixed commitments).

### Escalation
Formally flagging a recurring capacity or process problem to a supervisor, rather than continuing to absorb it individually.
**In use:** "This is the second week filing's been deferred — that's an escalation, not just another busy day."
**Common misuse:** Waiting until the problem is severe (a missed hard deadline) to escalate, instead of flagging the pattern at the second occurrence.

### Handoff note
A written record at shift/day end of what was completed, what was deferred and to when, and any pattern worth flagging.
**In use:** "Check the handoff note — the vendor reply's deferred to today."
**Common misuse:** Writing a vague summary ("did what I could") instead of naming specific completed and deferred items.

### Consequence-of-delay
The actual cost of a task slipping, used to rank Soft items against each other once Hard items are scheduled.
**In use:** "Both are soft, but the audit summary has lower consequence-of-delay than the customer callback — defer the summary first."
**Common misuse:** Ranking by task size or personal preference instead of by what actually costs more if it waits.
