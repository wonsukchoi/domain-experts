### Hold point
A stage of construction (foundation, framing, rough-in, insulation, final) at which work must stop for inspection before the next phase begins, because the next phase will physically conceal what this inspection verifies.
**In use:** "Framing is a hold point — nothing gets insulated until it passes."
**Common misuse:** Treating hold points as scheduling checkpoints that can be combined or reordered for convenience, rather than as points chosen specifically because concealment makes later verification impossible.

### Red tag / stop-work order
A formal order, issued under the building official's delegated police-power authority, halting work on a permit until a cited violation is corrected and re-inspected.
**In use:** "The exposed structural connector issue gets a red tag, not a correction notice with a grace period."
**Common misuse:** Treating a stop-work order as a negotiable slowdown rather than a legally binding halt that persists until the specific cited item is reverified.

### Correction notice
The written record of a failed inspection finding, citing the specific code section, the measured or observed condition, and the required correction.
**In use:** "Correction notice cites IRC R310.2.1 — egress window net clear area 3.36 sq ft against a 5.7 sq ft minimum."
**Common misuse:** Writing a general description of the problem without the specific code section and measurement, producing a notice that reads as opinion and doesn't hold up on appeal.

### Egress (means of egress)
The code-required path and opening dimensions allowing an occupant to exit a space, most commonly a bedroom window meeting a minimum net clear opening area, height, and width.
**In use:** "Egress window fails on net clear area even though the width dimension passes."
**Common misuse:** Checking only one dimension (width or height) or only the overall opening size, rather than verifying width, height, and net clear area against all three sub-thresholds independently.

### Net clear opening
The actual usable open area of an egress window or door when operated to its full open position, as distinct from the rough or nominal opening size of the unit.
**In use:** "The window's rough opening looks fine, but net clear opening with the sash operated is what R310.2.1 measures."
**Common misuse:** Citing the manufacturer's nominal unit size instead of measuring the actual clear opening with the window operated as it would be during an escape.

### GFCI (ground-fault circuit interrupter)
A device that interrupts power when it detects current leaking to ground, required at specific locations (kitchen counters, bathrooms, garages, wet/damp areas) to prevent shock.
**In use:** "Kitchen countertop receptacles aren't GFCI-protected — that's a straight code violation, not a judgment call."
**Common misuse:** Confusing GFCI (ground-fault, shock protection in wet/damp locations) with AFCI (arc-fault, fire protection on branch circuits) — they protect against different failure modes and neither substitutes for the other.

### AFCI (arc-fault circuit interrupter)
A device that detects arcing conditions in a branch circuit (a fire-ignition risk) and interrupts power, required on specified branch circuits such as bedroom circuits.
**In use:** "Bedroom circuits need AFCI protection at the panel, separate from any GFCI requirement elsewhere in the house."
**Common misuse:** Treating AFCI and GFCI protection as interchangeable or assuming one device satisfies both requirements.

### Rough-in
The construction phase where plumbing, electrical, and mechanical systems are installed inside wall and ceiling cavities but not yet finished or covered, inspected before insulation and drywall.
**In use:** "Rough-in inspection is where you check pipe joints and wire protection before anything gets covered."
**Common misuse:** Treating rough-in as a preliminary or low-stakes check rather than the last point at which pipe joints, wire runs, and box fill can be verified non-destructively.

### Inspector of record
The specific certified inspector (or inspection authority) whose sign-off constitutes the jurisdiction's official record that a phase of work was verified compliant at the time of inspection.
**In use:** "As inspector of record on this permit, the framing approval carries my verification, not the contractor's assertion."
**Common misuse:** Treating the inspection stamp as a formality rather than as the specific legal record examined if the work later fails or is disputed.

### ICC certification
Credentialing issued by the International Code Council across disciplines (residential, commercial building, plumbing, electrical, combination) verifying an inspector's competency to enforce the adopted code family.
**In use:** "This jurisdiction requires an ICC Combination Inspector certification to sign off on both structural and electrical rough-in."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a single ICC certification covers all disciplines or all jurisdictions, rather than checking which specific discipline and which jurisdiction's adoption requirements apply.

### Local amendment
A jurisdiction-specific modification to the base ICC code edition (IRC/IBC) it has adopted, which can add, remove, or tighten a requirement relative to the unmodified model code text.
**In use:** "The 3-foot exclusion zone for smoke detectors near a bathroom door isn't in the base IRC text — it's this jurisdiction's local amendment."
**Common misuse:** Citing the unmodified model code section without checking whether the local jurisdiction has amended it, which can mean citing a requirement or threshold that isn't actually the one in force.

### Field change
A deviation from the stamped plan set discovered or approved during construction, requiring either engineer-of-record sign-off or a plan-review determination before it's accepted as compliant.
**In use:** "The header substitution is a field change — it needs the engineer of record's sign-off, not my approval."
**Common misuse:** An inspector approving a structural or code-intent deviation from stamped plans unilaterally in the field, which is outside inspection authority and belongs with plan review or the engineer of record.

### Certificate of Occupancy (CO)
The final document issued after the last hold-point inspection passes, certifying a structure is safe and compliant for its intended use and occupancy.
**In use:** "No CO issues until every open correction item from prior inspections is closed."
**Common misuse:** Treating the final inspection as a formality once earlier hold points have passed, rather than as the point where all previously open corrections must be verified closed.

### Life-safety item
A code requirement (egress, smoke/CO detection, GFCI/AFCI protection, fire separation) whose failure directly increases risk of injury or death, warranting zero-tolerance treatment distinct from cosmetic or convenience findings.
**In use:** "The egress shortfall is a life-safety item — it gets the same zero-tolerance treatment whether it's 5% or 40% under the minimum."
**Common misuse:** Scaling severity to the size of the numeric shortfall on a life-safety item the same way it would be scaled on a cosmetic item, when even a small shortfall on egress or detection carries outsized real-world consequence.
