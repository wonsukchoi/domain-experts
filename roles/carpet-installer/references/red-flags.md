# Carpet Installer Red Flags

### Slab is over 5 years old and no moisture test is scheduled before a glue-down bid
- **Usually means:** the GC or estimator assumed age equals dryness; second most likely is a below-grade or slab-on-grade pour with no vapor retarder under it.
- **First question:** "Has this slab ever had a calcium chloride or RH probe test, or is 'it's old' the only evidence it's dry?"
- **Data to pull:** original construction moisture-retarder spec, any prior flooring failure history for the space, current ambient RH/temp log for the room.

### Calcium chloride reading between 3 and 5 lbs MVER with a standard PSA specified
- **Usually means:** the adhesive is marginal for this slab and will pass inspection today but is a warranty risk within 12-18 months; second most likely is a test run before the space was conditioned to service temperature, understating the true reading.
- **First question:** "What's this specific adhesive's stated MVER limit, and how far under it is this reading really?"
- **Data to pull:** adhesive manufacturer's data sheet moisture limits, the test conditioning log (was the HVAC running 48 hours prior).

### Room is stretched with a knee kicker only and no power-stretcher marks on the tackless strip
- **Usually means:** the installer skipped the power stretcher to save time; second most likely is a tool availability problem on a small crew, not a technique choice.
- **First question:** "Walk me through the stretch sequence — where did the power stretcher set up on each wall?"
- **Data to pull:** photos of the stretch-in progress if available, crew labor hours logged against the job (a power-stretch sequence takes measurably longer than knee-kick-only).

### A seam runs directly across a doorway threshold or under a ceiling light fixture
- **Usually means:** the seam was placed by roll-width convenience rather than a traffic/light layout plan; second most likely is a late room-dimension change that wasn't re-planned.
- **First question:** "Was this seam location chosen, or is it where the roll ran out?"
- **Data to pull:** the original seam-layout sketch or lack of one, roll width and room dimensions to check if an alternate layout existed.

### Ripples or wrinkles appear within 3-6 months of a stretch-in install
- **Usually means:** insufficient stretch tension (knee-kicker-only or underpowered stretch) letting the backing relax under traffic; second most likely is a cushion/pad that's too soft or too thick for the traffic level, allowing movement under the carpet.
- **First question:** "Was this room power-stretched at install, and what's the pad specification underneath?"
- **Data to pull:** original install work order noting stretch method, pad thickness/density spec vs. what's actually installed.

### Seam peaking (raised edge) visible within days of a hot-melt tape seam
- **Usually means:** insufficient seam iron dwell time or pressure during rolling, letting the tape set before the seam was fully compressed; second most likely is incompatible backing types seamed with a standard tape.
- **First question:** "Was the seam rolled and re-checked while the tape was still workable, or only once at the end?"
- **Data to pull:** seam peaking severity rated on the 0-4 industry scale; anything above 2 (average/acceptable) merits a redo, not a touch-up.

### Dark grayish lines appear along baseboards or HVAC supply/return paths months after install
- **Usually means:** filtration soiling from airflow carrying particulate through a gap at the tackless strip or an unsealed transition — not a defect in the carpet or a cleaning failure; second most likely is a poorly sealed HVAC duct nearby.
- **First question:** "Is this along every airflow path in the room, or isolated to one seam or wall?"
- **Data to pull:** HVAC register locations relative to the affected lines, tackless strip and transition seal condition at those walls.

### Color break visible at a seam that wasn't there in the sample
- **Usually means:** two rolls from different dye lots or run numbers were seamed together; second most likely is one roll stored under different lighting/humidity conditions before install, temporarily shifting its appearance.
- **First question:** "Do the dye lot or run numbers on both rolls' labels match?"
- **Data to pull:** roll labels/invoice run numbers for every roll used in the room.

### Carpet tile edges curling or lifting 2-6 months after a glue-down install
- **Usually means:** adhesive failure from moisture the pre-install test either missed or was skipped for; second most likely is wrong adhesive trowel notch size leaving insufficient transfer.
- **First question:** "Was this substrate moisture-tested before adhesive selection, and what adhesive class was actually used?"
- **Data to pull:** original moisture test results and adhesive data sheet, trowel notch size used vs. adhesive manufacturer's spec.

### Bow or skew on a patterned roll noticeably exceeds the manufacturer's tag tolerance
- **Usually means:** a manufacturing defect in that specific roll, not an installer stretching error; second most likely is the roll being force-stretched on-site to try to fix a pattern misalignment, which won't hold.
- **First question:** "What tolerance does this roll's tag or the mill's spec sheet state, and how far out of it are we?"
- **Data to pull:** the roll's manufacturer tag/tolerance sheet, measured bow/skew at multiple points along the roll.

### Cushion (pad) seam lines up directly under a carpet seam
- **Usually means:** the pad was installed without planning seam offset against the carpet layout; second most likely is a pad substitution mid-job that wasn't re-sequenced.
- **First question:** "Was the pad seam laid out before or after the carpet seam plan was set?"
- **Data to pull:** pad layout sketch or lack of one, visual inspection for a ridge line at the carpet seam.
