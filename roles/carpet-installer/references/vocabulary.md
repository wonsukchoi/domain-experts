# Carpet Installer Vocabulary

### Power stretch
Stretching carpet to roughly 1-1.5% elongation with a power stretcher hooked between an anchor wall and the opposite wall, then hooking it onto tackless strip under that tension, per CRI 104/105.
**In use:** "This room's 18 ft wide, that's still a power-stretch job — set the tail block on the long wall and add a tube."
**Common misuse:** Generalists (and some crews under schedule pressure) treat "stretched" as anything pulled taut by a knee kicker; a knee kicker only positions the carpet onto the pins locally and doesn't deliver the wall-to-wall tension the standard requires.

### Knee kicker
A hand-operated tool struck with the knee to push carpet a short distance onto tackless strip pins — used for positioning and detail work, not for stretching a full room.
**In use:** "Knee-kick it into the closet corner, but the power stretcher's doing the rest of this run."
**Common misuse:** Using it as the sole stretching tool for an entire room to save setup time; it lacks the mechanical pull to hit CRI's elongation target and the slack shows up as ripples later.

### Calcium chloride test (ASTM F1869)
A moisture test that measures the concrete's moisture vapor emission rate (MVER) in lbs/1,000 ft²/24hr by weighing a calcium chloride dish sealed under a dome on the slab for 60-72 hours.
**In use:** "Ran four CaCl spots on the 1,800 — average came back 6.15, over the PSA's 3 lb limit."
**Common misuse:** Treating a single test location as representative of the whole floor; the protocol requires multiple locations scaled to square footage because moisture readings vary across a slab.

### RH probe test (ASTM F2170)
An in-situ relative humidity test using probes inserted to a specified slab depth (commonly 40%) and allowed to equilibrate before reading — measures the slab's internal moisture condition rather than surface emission.
**In use:** "CaCl came back borderline, so we drilled RH probes too — 82%, that's the number that actually rules out the urethane adhesive."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a CaCl and an RH test will always agree; they measure different things (surface emission vs. internal condition) and a slab can pass one while failing the other, so both matter when the adhesive spec cites both limits.

### Tackless strip (gripper)
A thin wood or composite strip with angled pins, nailed or glued around a room's perimeter, that the carpet's backing hooks onto during a stretch-in installation.
**In use:** "Strip's in at three-eighths off the wall all the way around — go ahead and start the stretch."
**Common misuse:** Placing it flush against the wall (zero gap) or with an oversized gap; either error prevents the carpet edge from tucking cleanly and shows as a visible line or a loose edge at the baseboard.

### Seam peaking
A raised or ridged seam edge that appears when the two carpet edges weren't fully compressed flat before the hot-melt tape or adhesive set.
**In use:** "That seam's peaking at a 3 on the scale — cut it out and reseam, a touch-up won't hold."
**Common misuse:** Treating any visible seam line as peaking; a well-made seam is still visible up close under raking light — peaking specifically means the edge is raised, not just seamed.

### Filtration soiling
Dark, grayish discoloration along baseboards or airflow paths caused by airborne particulate depositing as air is forced through a small gap — not a defect in the carpet fiber or a sign of poor cleaning.
**In use:** "That gray line under the return vent is filtration soiling, not a stain — check the transition seal, not the carpet warranty."
**Common misuse:** Diagnosing it as a cleaning failure or fiber defect and sending it back to the mill or the maintenance crew instead of checking the airflow gap causing it.

### Dye lot / run number
An identifier printed on a carpet roll's label marking which manufacturing batch produced it; carpet from different dye lots of the same style can show a visible color difference even though both are "correct."
**In use:** "Hold on before you cut — check the run numbers on both rolls match before we seam them."
**Common misuse:** Assuming two rolls of the same style number are automatically an exact color match; the style number identifies the product line, the dye lot identifies the actual batch and is what has to match for a seamless color transition.

### Bow and skew
Bow is a pattern's deviation into a curve across the roll width; skew is the pattern's deviation into a diagonal lean along the roll length. Both are manufacturing-tolerance measurements, stated per 12 ft of roll.
**In use:** "This roll's skewed almost 3 inches over 12 feet — that's outside the mill's tag tolerance, we should reject it, not fight it with the stretcher."
**Common misuse:** Believing a skilled installer can stretch a bowed or skewed pattern back into straight alignment; stretching adjusts tension, not the printed or tufted pattern geometry itself.

### Waterfall method (stairs)
A stair installation technique where carpet runs continuously over the nose and down the face of the riser without being cut and tucked at the back of the tread.
**In use:** "Standard risers, plush pile, customer's not picky — waterfall it, we'll be done in half the time of a French cap job."
**Common misuse:** Using it on patterned or high-end goods where the client expects a tailored look; waterfall reads as a budget finish compared to a mitered French cap on visible stairs.

### French cap (mitered) method
A stair installation technique where carpet is cut and mitered at the back of each tread and tacked individually, producing a tailored, precisely fitted look at roughly double the labor of waterfall.
**In use:** "Client wants the tailored look on the front stairs — that's a French cap job, budget the extra labor."
**Common misuse:** Applying it by default regardless of budget or goods type; it's a deliberate upgrade decision, not the "more correct" method for every stair job.

### Direct glue-down
An installation method where carpet or carpet tile is adhered directly to the substrate with adhesive, with no cushion/pad layer underneath.
**In use:** "This is a rolling-cart commercial space — direct glue-down carpet tile, no pad, so it doesn't shift under the carts."
**Common misuse:** Assuming glue-down always needs less substrate prep than stretch-in; glue-down is more moisture- and flatness-sensitive than stretch-in because the adhesive bond fails first when the slab isn't right, where a cushioned stretch-in floor tolerates more substrate variance.

### Cushion (pad) offset
The CRI 105 rule that cushion/pad seams should fall at a right angle to carpet seams, or be offset at least 6 inches to one side, so the two seam lines never coincide.
**In use:** "Lay the pad seams the other direction from how we're seaming the carpet — right angle, per spec."
**Common misuse:** Not planning pad layout at all and letting the pad seams land wherever the roll ends, which can stack a pad seam directly under a carpet seam and telegraph a ridge line through the finished floor.
