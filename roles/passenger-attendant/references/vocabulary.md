# Vocabulary — Passenger Attendant

### WC19 (ANSI/RESNA WC19)
The wheelchair tie-down and occupant restraint system (WTORS) standard governing four-point strap securement geometry and anchor-point load ratings (~2,500 lbf per anchor, tested to a 30 mph/20g frontal-impact-equivalent pull).
**In use:** "That's a WC19-compliant chair — four points, standard angles, standard anchor rating; nothing non-standard about the securement here."
**Common misuse:** Treating "WC19" as describing the wheelchair model itself rather than the securement/restraint system standard the chair and the vehicle's anchor points are both built to.

### Four-point-plus-belt securement
The complete tie-down sequence: two front straps (30–45° from the floor), two rear straps (30–60°), and a lap belt (plus shoulder belt where the configuration provides one) — treated as one indivisible safety unit, not four-plus-one separate optional steps.
**In use:** "Four-point-plus-belt or it's not secured — three straps and no belt isn't a partial credit situation."
**Common misuse:** Counting the four straps alone as "secured" and treating the belt as a courtesy add-on rather than the restraint component that keeps the rider in the chair under braking.

### Bridge plate
A short, level ramp-equivalent device bridging the gap between a level-boarding platform and the vehicle floor, used where platform and vehicle floor heights are matched closely enough that a full incline ramp isn't needed.
**In use:** "Level boarding here, so it's bridge plate, not the full ramp — saves the incline-alignment time but not the securement time."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a bridge plate makes the whole boarding-to-securement process faster, when it only shortens the deployment step, not the four-point-plus-belt securement itself.

### Extended-dwell request
A radio call from the attendant to the conductor/train operator asking that a stop's departure be held beyond its scheduled dwell time to finish a mobility-assistance boarding or detraining, made before the securement sequence is compressed to fit.
**In use:** "Requesting an extended dwell to 85 seconds for a four-point-plus-belt securement, currently in progress."
**Common misuse:** Making the request mid-securement or after rushing it, rather than estimating the time up front and calling before the sequence starts.

### 90-second assistance allowance
A commonly used agency policy threshold permitting an attendant to request a dwell extension without supervisor authorization, up to roughly 90 seconds of total assistance time; estimates or actuals beyond that trigger a supervisor/dispatch sign-off requirement.
**In use:** "Estimated 95 seconds on this one — that's over the 90-second allowance, so dispatch needs to sign off before we start."
**Common misuse:** Measuring the 90 seconds against the deficit versus scheduled dwell rather than against the total securement time itself — a long scheduled dwell doesn't raise the 90-second cap.

### Manifest reconciliation
The per-stop practice of checking the actual count of boarded/detrained mobility-assistance riders against the day's manifest, logging any discrepancy immediately rather than only at the end of the run.
**In use:** "Reconciled — two boarded, matches the manifest for this stop, one more expected further down the line."
**Common misuse:** Treating reconciliation as an end-of-shift paperwork task instead of a check performed at the specific stop where a discrepancy could occur.

### Complementary paratransit
The ADA-mandated (49 CFR §§37.121–37.139) shared-ride transportation service that transit agencies must provide to riders who can't use fixed-route service due to disability, operating parallel to (complementary to) the regular fixed-route system.
**In use:** "She's complementary paratransit certified, door-to-door — that's the service level on file, not curb-to-curb."
**Common misuse:** Using "paratransit" as a generic term for any accessible transit service, rather than the specific ADA-mandated complementary program with its own eligibility and service-level rules.

### Curb-to-curb vs. door-to-door
The two ADA paratransit service-level designations: curb-to-curb assistance ends at the vehicle and the curb/entrance; door-to-door extends assistance to the rider's actual door, both determined by the rider's eligibility certification, not attendant discretion on the day.
**In use:** "Manifest says curb-to-curb for this rider — I can't extend to the apartment door without a service-level change on file."
**Common misuse:** Deciding in the moment to provide (or withhold) door-to-door assistance based on how the request seems, rather than checking the certified service level first.

### On-time pickup window
The span of time (commonly up to 30 minutes after the scheduled pickup time under typical ADA complementary paratransit policy) within which a paratransit vehicle is considered on-time; running past it requires a documented dispatch call, not a unilateral decision to leave.
**In use:** "We're at 28 minutes past scheduled — call dispatch now, we're about to run out of window."
**Common misuse:** Treating the window as a soft target the attendant can extend informally rather than a service-contract figure that triggers a required dispatch report once exceeded.

### Non-clinical scope of care
The boundary of what a Basic First Aid/CPR/AED-trained attendant is authorized to do — direct pressure, positioning, CPR/AED use — as distinct from diagnosis or medication administration beyond an approved kit, which requires EMS or a ground/dispatch medical line.
**In use:** "That's outside my scope — I can keep her stable and call it in, but I'm not making a call on what this actually is."
**Common misuse:** Treating confidence in a guessed diagnosis as equivalent to being trained to make that diagnosis, and delaying an EMS call because the attendant feels sure of the cause.

### Good Samaritan protection
State-level statutory liability protection for a non-clinical responder who provides emergency assistance in good faith and within their trained scope; protection typically does not extend to actions outside that scope or to gross negligence.
**In use:** "Good Samaritan covers the CPR and the direct pressure — it doesn't cover me handing her medication that isn't in the kit."
**Common misuse:** Assuming Good Samaritan protection is a blanket shield for any well-intentioned action, rather than one conditioned on staying within trained, authorized scope.

### Areas of refuge / assisted-egress priority
Designated waiting locations or evacuation-sequence priority for mobility-limited passengers during a station or vehicle evacuation, referenced by station/vehicle emergency-egress planning (e.g., NFPA 130) so responders know where assisted riders are without searching.
**In use:** "In an evacuation, the manifest tells the crew exactly who's still in an area of refuge and needs assisted egress first."
**Common misuse:** Assuming an evacuation plan automatically accounts for mobility-limited riders without an accurate, currently-reconciled manifest feeding it.

### Trip manifest
The per-run document (paper or handheld) listing each mobility-assistance rider's identity, mobility-device type, certified service level, and expected boarding/detraining stop — the reference an attendant checks before assuming what assistance is owed.
**In use:** "Check the manifest before you assume door-to-door — it's the certified service level, not what the rider's asking for today."
**Common misuse:** Treating the manifest as a passenger list only, rather than the record that also carries the service-level and equipment details that determine what assistance is actually owed.
