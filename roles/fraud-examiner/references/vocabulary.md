### Predication
A documented, reasonable professional basis to believe fraud may have occurred, required before opening a formal fraud investigation.
**In use:** "Predication here is the specific tip naming the employee and vendor, corroborated by the independently surfaced financial pressure indicator."
**Common misuse:** Opening an investigation based on vague suspicion or a hunch without documenting the specific facts that establish a reasonable basis.

### Fraud triangle
The framework (pressure, opportunity, rationalization) explaining the three conditions typically present when occupational fraud occurs.
**In use:** "Opportunity is the leg we can actually control here — closing the segregation-of-duties gap addresses that directly."
**Common misuse:** Focusing prevention efforts on trying to detect or screen for pressure and rationalization, which are largely internal to the perpetrator and hard to observe, instead of closing the opportunity created by control weaknesses.

### Benford's Law
A statistical principle describing the expected frequency distribution of leading digits in naturally occurring numerical datasets (e.g., "1" appearing about 30.1% of the time, "9" about 4.6%), used to flag anomalies in financial data.
**In use:** "The leading digit '9' appears in 22% of this vendor's invoices versus an expected 4.6% — that's a strong anomaly worth investigating."
**Common misuse:** Treating a Benford's Law anomaly as proof of fraud on its own, rather than a lead requiring corroboration against source documents.

### Asset misappropriation
The most common category of occupational fraud (per ACFE statistics), involving theft or misuse of an organization's assets — typically the lowest median loss per case among the three main fraud categories.
**In use:** "This is a straightforward asset misappropriation case — diverted vendor payments."
**Common misuse:** Assuming asset misappropriation's high frequency means it deserves proportionally more investigative resources than financial statement fraud, which is rarer but typically far more costly per incident.

### Corruption (occupational fraud)
A category of occupational fraud involving misuse of influence in a business transaction for personal benefit, such as kickbacks, bribery, or undisclosed conflicts of interest.
**In use:** "This looks like corruption, not simple theft — the employee had an undisclosed ownership stake in the vendor."
**Common misuse:** Conflating corruption schemes (which involve influence and relationships) with straightforward asset misappropriation (which involves direct theft), when the investigative approach differs.

### Financial statement fraud
The rarest category of occupational fraud (per ACFE statistics) but typically the highest median loss per case, involving intentional misstatement of an organization's financial reports.
**In use:** "Financial statement fraud is rare, but the median loss when it happens dwarfs typical asset misappropriation cases."
**Common misuse:** Deprioritizing financial statement fraud risk because it occurs less frequently, without accounting for its disproportionately high loss severity.

### Segregation of duties
A control principle ensuring no single individual has end-to-end control over a process (e.g., creating a vendor and approving payments to it), reducing fraud opportunity.
**In use:** "The core control gap here is a segregation-of-duties failure — one person could both create the vendor and approve its invoices."
**Common misuse:** Treating segregation of duties as satisfied by a dollar-threshold-based dual approval alone, without checking whether the same person controls both master-record creation and approval below that threshold.

### Admission-seeking interview
The final interview in a fraud investigation, conducted with the primary suspect after evidence has been gathered and corroborated, aimed at obtaining an admission.
**In use:** "We're not ready for the admission-seeking interview yet — we still need to corroborate two more invoices."
**Common misuse:** Conducting the admission-seeking interview early, before documentary evidence and witness statements are gathered, risking a defensive response or destroyed evidence.

### Chain of custody
The documented record of who has handled, accessed, or transferred physical or electronic evidence throughout an investigation, used to establish its integrity if the matter proceeds to civil or criminal action.
**In use:** "Chain of custody needs to be documented from the moment these financial records are pulled, not after the fact."
**Common misuse:** Treating evidence handling informally during the early stages of an investigation, creating a documentation gap that weakens the evidence's admissibility later.

### Threshold-splitting (invoice splitting)
A fraud scheme pattern where transactions are deliberately structured just below a dollar amount that would trigger additional review or approval.
**In use:** "Repeated invoices at $9,800-$9,900 just under the $10,000 dual-approval threshold is a classic threshold-splitting pattern."
**Common misuse:** Dismissing a cluster of transactions just under a review threshold as coincidental rather than investigating it as a well-documented fraud red flag pattern.
