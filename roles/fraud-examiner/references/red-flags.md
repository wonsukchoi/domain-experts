### An investigation is opened based on a vague suspicion with no documented specific facts
- **Usually means:** Predication may be insufficient, creating legal exposure (defamation, wrongful termination) if the investigation proceeds without a documented, reasonable basis.
- **First question:** What specific, articulable facts support opening this investigation, and are they documented?
- **Data to pull:** The original tip, complaint, or anomaly that triggered the investigation, in writing.

### A digit-frequency or ratio anomaly is reported as a finding without source-document corroboration
- **Usually means:** The analytical result is a lead, not proof — it hasn't yet been traced to and confirmed against actual invoices, bank records, or other source documents.
- **First question:** Has this specific anomaly been traced to underlying source documents, or is it being presented as a standalone conclusion?
- **Data to pull:** Underlying transactions driving the anomaly, source documents for a sample of those transactions.

### The primary suspect was interviewed or confronted before witness interviews and documentary evidence gathering were complete
- **Usually means:** This risks destroyed records, a coordinated cover story among co-conspirators, or a defensive posture yielding no usable admission.
- **First question:** What evidence had been gathered and corroborated before this interview took place?
- **Data to pull:** Investigation timeline showing sequence of interviews and evidence-gathering steps.

### A control gap allows one person to both create/modify a master record (vendor, employee, customer) and approve transactions against it
- **Usually means:** A classic segregation-of-duties failure creating fraud opportunity, regardless of whether fraud has occurred yet.
- **First question:** Does any other person independently review or approve changes to this master record before transactions can be processed against it?
- **Data to pull:** System access logs and approval workflow configuration for the master record type in question.

### A cluster of transactions falls just below a dollar threshold that triggers additional review or approval
- **Usually means:** A well-documented red flag pattern (threshold-splitting) suggesting deliberate structuring to avoid oversight.
- **First question:** What percentage of this person's or vendor's transactions fall just under the threshold, compared to a typical/expected distribution?
- **Data to pull:** Transaction amount distribution for the person/vendor, comparison to expected distribution (e.g., via Benford's Law or peer comparison).

### An employee exhibits a documented lifestyle change (major purchases, debt resolution) inconsistent with known compensation
- **Usually means:** A classic behavioral red flag (living beyond apparent means) associated with occupational fraud, worth noting as context but not standalone proof.
- **First question:** Is there a plausible legitimate explanation (inheritance, spouse's income, prior savings), and has it been checked before treating this as investigative evidence?
- **Data to pull:** Any available context on the lifestyle change, without over-relying on this alone as evidence.

### Fraud prevention recommendations focus on employee screening or "character" assessment rather than control changes
- **Usually means:** This misdirects prevention effort toward the pressure/rationalization legs of the fraud triangle, which the organization can't reliably detect or control, instead of opportunity, which it can.
- **First question:** What specific control weakness (segregation of duties, approval gap, oversight lapse) enabled this scheme, and has remediation been proposed for that gap specifically?
- **Data to pull:** Root-cause control analysis for the scheme, proposed remediation's target (screening vs. control change).

### Investigative resources are allocated based on how frequently a scheme type occurs in general fraud statistics, not the specific situation's loss exposure
- **Usually means:** Rarer scheme types (like financial statement fraud) can carry substantially higher loss exposure than frequency alone would suggest, and resource allocation purely by frequency can under-invest in high-severity risks.
- **First question:** What is the estimated loss exposure for this specific role/scheme type, independent of how common that scheme category is generally?
- **Data to pull:** Role-specific risk assessment, potential loss magnitude estimate for the scheme type in this context.
