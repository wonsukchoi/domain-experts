# Nanny — Red Flags

### Paid entirely in cash with no written work agreement
- **Usually means:** The family is avoiding nanny-tax and FLSA obligations, or is genuinely unaware a household employer has any — either way, there's no reference point if hours, pay, or a firing dispute ever gets contested.
- **First question:** "Is this being run as W-2 with Schedule H, or are we treating it as off-the-books?"
- **Data to pull:** Any pay stub or payment record, the (likely nonexistent) work agreement.

### Overtime "averaged" across weeks instead of paid per week
- **Usually means:** The family is treating the nanny as salaried/exempt when a live-out nanny is nonexempt under the FLSA domestic-service rule — overtime is owed for any individual week over 40 hours, not smoothed against a lighter week later.
- **First question:** "What were the actual hours each individual week, not the average across the month?"
- **Data to pull:** Weekly timesheets or the handoff log's hour entries, week by week.

### Family repeatedly asks for care outside the written scope with no pay adjustment
- **Usually means:** Scope creep normalizing through inertia — each unaddressed ask becomes the baseline for the next one.
- **First question:** "When was the scope in the agreement last revisited in writing?"
- **Data to pull:** Original work agreement's duties section versus the actual task list over the past month.

### Medicine given based on a verbal instruction with no dose or time specified
- **Usually means:** No written medical-authorization protocol exists; the instruction covers one moment, not a standing rule.
- **First question:** "What exactly did they say — dose, medication, and time window?"
- **Data to pull:** Any text or email authorization on file, Exhibit A of the work agreement if one exists.

### Nanny driving children without a documented answer to whose insurance covers an accident
- **Usually means:** Driving authorization was assumed rather than confirmed in writing before the first drive.
- **First question:** "Is the nanny listed on the family's auto policy, or is she using her own vehicle and insurance?"
- **Data to pull:** Signed driving-authorization clause, insurance declarations page.

### Live-in nanny whose actual daily hours are never logged
- **Usually means:** An assumption that live-in status exempts the arrangement from any hour tracking at all, when it only removes federal overtime eligibility — not minimum wage, not state-law obligations, not the practical need for a record in a dispute.
- **First question:** "What time did the workday actually start and end this week, on average?"
- **Data to pull:** Informal texts or a log covering start/end times over the past two weeks.

### Two-weeks'-notice norm skipped on either side with no specific precipitating cause
- **Usually means:** An unaddressed grievance escalated to a sudden break instead of a conversation that should have happened earlier.
- **First question:** "What specifically happened this week that triggered an immediate exit rather than a conversation?"
- **Data to pull:** Recent handoff notes or message threads for a pattern building toward the break.

### Nanny told "don't bother writing incidents down, just tell us at pickup"
- **Usually means:** The family wants informal reporting, which exposes the nanny to liability if a dispute or an unrelated injury arises later with no record to point to.
- **First question:** "If the child showed up somewhere else with a mark and no written record existed, what would that look like?"
- **Data to pull:** Whatever informal notes or texts currently substitute for a written log.

### Screen time or discipline handled differently week to week with no written rule
- **Usually means:** No household policy was ever documented, so the nanny is defaulting to her own judgment inconsistently rather than a shared standard.
- **First question:** "What does the work agreement actually say about screen time and discipline?"
- **Data to pull:** The routine/discipline section of the work agreement, or its absence.

### Family classifies the nanny as a 1099 independent contractor
- **Usually means:** Near-certain misclassification under the IRS common-law employee test — a family that sets the schedule, location, and method of the work is the defining fact pattern of an employee relationship, not a contractor one.
- **First question:** "Who decides the hours and where the work happens — the nanny, or the family?"
- **Data to pull:** IRS Publication 926's common-law test criteria compared against the actual working arrangement.

### Nanny-share families split care unevenly with no rebalancing conversation
- **Usually means:** Informal drift in a shared arrangement — one family's kids consistently getting more attention or hours — that nobody has renegotiated since the original split.
- **First question:** "What's the actual weekly hour and attention split by family, not the split we agreed to at the start?"
- **Data to pull:** Handoff/activity log broken out by family over the past few weeks.

### A trial period is treated as one-sided (protecting the family only)
- **Usually means:** The reduced-notice trial window is being framed as a probation for the nanny, when it's meant to let either side exit cleanly if the fit isn't right.
- **First question:** "Is the trial period's reduced-notice term written to apply to both parties, or only one?"
- **Data to pull:** The trial-period clause in the work agreement.
