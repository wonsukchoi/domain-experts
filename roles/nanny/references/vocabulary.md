# Nanny — Vocabulary

### Nanny tax
The federal (and often state) payroll tax obligations a household employer owes once cash wages paid to a nanny cross the IRS's annual Schedule H filing threshold (a moving figure, recently in the $2,600-$2,800/year range).
**In use:** "We're past the nanny-tax threshold for the year, so payroll needs to start withholding FICA."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to be a tax the nanny pays herself out of her own return; it's primarily the family's withholding and remittance obligation, though it's also what generates the nanny's own Social Security and Medicare earnings record.

### FLSA domestic-service final rule
The Department of Labor's 2013 rule (effective 2015) extending federal minimum wage and overtime protections to most home-care and domestic workers, closing the prior "companionship exemption" that had excluded many of them.
**In use:** "She's live-out and nonexempt under the domestic-service rule, so overtime kicks in after 40 hours."
**Common misuse:** Assuming all nannies are automatically overtime-exempt because they work inside a private home — live-out nannies generally are not exempt; only live-in status changes that.

### Live-in exemption
The federal overtime exemption that applies to live-in domestic workers specifically — it removes federal overtime eligibility but not minimum-wage eligibility, and doesn't remove state-law overtime rules where they exist.
**In use:** "Because she lives in, we don't owe federal overtime, but this state's law still requires 1.5x after 40."
**Common misuse:** Treated as exempting the arrangement from hour tracking entirely, rather than just from the federal overtime calculation.

### Work agreement / nanny contract
The written document — commonly built from the International Nanny Association's template — specifying hours, pay, overtime treatment, PTO, duties, and driving/medical authorization.
**In use:** "Let's get the additional-child rate into the work agreement before she starts covering your nephew regularly."
**Common misuse:** A verbal understanding ("she's basically part of the family") is treated as equivalent to a written agreement, leaving nothing to point to when a dispute arises.

### Guaranteed hours
A contract clause paying the nanny for a set minimum number of hours per week even if the family doesn't need her for all of them — a vacation, a sick day, a canceled workday.
**In use:** "Her guarantee is 40 hours; if they travel without her, she's still paid for the full 40."
**Common misuse:** Confused with overtime — guaranteed hours protect against the family cancelling work, overtime compensates the nanny for doing extra work; they address opposite risks.

### Nanny-share
Two families jointly employing one nanny, splitting pay and typically care for both sets of children, often on alternating days or in a shared household.
**In use:** "The share rate is $28/hour split between the two families, plus a per-child differential above the base two kids."
**Common misuse:** Treated as equivalent to a group daycare setting; in a share, ratios, liability, and scheduling are negotiated directly between two separate employers, not set by a licensing body.

### Household employer
The legal classification of a family that directly hires and controls a nanny's schedule and method of work, triggering IRS Schedule H and state unemployment/workers'-compensation obligations — distinct from an agency or a genuine independent-contractor relationship.
**In use:** "As a household employer, they have to file Schedule H with their 1040 once they cross the wage threshold."
**Common misuse:** Families assume they can sidestep household-employer status simply by calling the nanny a contractor and issuing a 1099.

### Common-law employee test
The IRS's behavioral- and financial-control criteria for determining whether a worker is legally an employee or an independent contractor — for nannies, it nearly always resolves to employee.
**In use:** "Under the common-law test, they set her hours and provide the workplace, so she can't be classified 1099."
**Common misuse:** Believing that a signed 1099 agreement itself determines classification, rather than the actual facts of who controls the schedule, location, and method of the work.

### Additional-child differential
An agreed per-hour premium for caring for children beyond those named in the original work agreement — a sibling's friend, a relative's kids, a nanny-share partner's children.
**In use:** "The differential is $3 an hour per extra child, per the agreement, and it applies the moment a third child is added to the day."
**Common misuse:** Covering an extra child a few times as a favor is assumed to carry no future rate obligation, rather than being treated as the moment to add the clause to the agreement.

### Trial period
An initial window at the start of a placement — commonly two to four weeks — during which either party can end the arrangement with reduced notice, used to confirm fit before the full agreement's notice terms apply.
**In use:** "We're still inside the two-week trial, so let's finalize the ongoing rate once it converts."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a probationary period for the nanny only; it's meant to protect both sides' ability to exit cleanly if the fit is wrong.

### Mandated reporter (household context)
A legal designation requiring reporting of suspected child abuse or neglect — for nannies, coverage varies by state and by whether "private household employee" is a statutorily listed occupation, unlike licensed childcare workers, who are covered in nearly every state.
**In use:** "Check this state's mandated-reporter list specifically — private household nannies aren't automatically covered everywhere the way licensed childcare staff are."
**Common misuse:** Assumed to be identical nationwide, or identical to a licensed childcare worker's status, when it's state- and category-specific.

### Scope creep
The gradual expansion of duties beyond the written work agreement — extra children, housework, errands — without a corresponding adjustment to pay or hours.
**In use:** "Watching the neighbor's kids twice a week wasn't in the original scope — that's a conversation now, before it becomes the assumed default."
**Common misuse:** Left unaddressed until resentment builds enough to force a confrontation, instead of being raised as a routine, low-stakes adjustment the first time it happens.

### Two-weeks' notice norm
The International Nanny Association's placement-industry standard of two weeks' paid notice from either party when ending the arrangement, absent a safety-related cause for immediate termination.
**In use:** "Even though it's an at-will arrangement, the industry norm is two weeks' paid notice on both sides."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a legal requirement (it's an industry norm, not a statute) or as one-directional, obligating the nanny but not the family.
