# Vocabulary

Terms generalists use loosely that attendants and their managers keep sharply separate — the separation is usually the difference between a defensible incident report and an open-ended liability admission.

### Bailment

The legal relationship created when one party (the bailor, the patron) transfers physical possession of personal property to another (the bailee, the venue/attendant) with an expectation it will be returned, and the bailee accepts custody. A duty of reasonable care attaches the moment custody transfers — it does not require a contract or a fee.

**In use:** "The coat check created a bailment the second she handed over the coat and we gave her a stub — the self-service locker down the hall didn't, because she kept the padlock key."

**Common misuse:** assuming any interaction with a patron's property is a bailment, including self-service storage where the patron retained control of the lock — that's custody the patron never gave up.

### Claim check / ticket-token system

A paired, sequentially numbered ticket system where one half stays with the patron and the matching half is attached to the item, used to verify identity of ownership at retrieval without relying on memory or description.

**In use:** "Ticket 214 doesn't match the tag on that coat — hold it at the supervisor station until she can show the stub."

**Common misuse:** treating a verbal description ("it's the black wool one") as sufficient verification when the ticket system exists precisely to remove that judgment call.

### Innkeeper/hotel-keeper liability statute

State laws (most US states have some version) that cap a hospitality business's liability for guest property loss, typically distinguishing disclosed valuables (checked into a safe or high-value tag, often uncapped or capped much higher) from undisclosed ones (capped low, commonly $100–$300) — and typically requiring the cap to be posted to apply.

**In use:** "He never disclosed the watch at intake and our cap is posted at the counter — the exposure here is the statutory cap, not the appraised value."

**Common misuse:** assuming the cap applies automatically regardless of whether the required notice was actually posted, or assuming it protects against gross negligence (most statutes carve that out).

### Gross negligence vs. ordinary negligence

Ordinary negligence is a failure to exercise reasonable care (a lapse); gross negligence is a reckless or conscious disregard for a known risk (e.g., leaving a coat check unlocked and unattended overnight with keys inside). Liability caps under most innkeeper statutes protect against ordinary negligence claims but not gross negligence.

**In use:** "Forgetting to relock the cage after a break is ordinary negligence and the cap should hold; propping it open all night because it's 'easier' starts looking like gross negligence."

**Common misuse:** treating any staff mistake as automatically outside the liability cap — most operational slip-ups are ordinary negligence, which the cap is designed to cover.

### Par level

The target on-hand quantity of a consumable (towels, robes, linens) that keeps supply ahead of demand given the laundry or restocking cycle time, expressed as a formula (daily usage ÷ cycles per day) plus a buffer, not a fixed guess.

**In use:** "We're running to par-120 on bath towels, but that's the bare calculation with no buffer — bump it to 150 so a late laundry pickup doesn't take us to zero."

**Common misuse:** setting par to exactly the calculated baseline usage with no buffer, then treating the first late laundry cycle as an unforeseeable emergency instead of the expected case the buffer exists to absorb.

### Tip pooling vs. tip credit

Tip pooling is the practice of combining tips across a group of staff and redistributing by an agreed formula (often hours worked). Tip credit is a wage-law mechanism (in jurisdictions that allow it) where an employer counts a portion of tips toward meeting minimum wage, provided the combined base + tips reaches or exceeds the wage floor.

**In use:** "Pool comes to $62 a head this shift — at $16 base plus $12.40/hr in tips we're well clear of the wage floor, so the tip credit math checks out."

**Common misuse:** assuming tip pooling and tip credit are the same lever — a venue can pool tips without taking any tip credit, and the wage-floor math must still be verified independently per shift, not assumed from an average.

### Attended vs. self-service storage

Attended storage means staff take physical custody of the item (coat check, staffed valet lockers); self-service storage means the patron retains control of their own lock and key and staff never take possession. The distinction, not the venue type, determines whether a bailment exists.

**In use:** "Same locker room, two liability postures — the staffed spa lockers are attended, the gym floor lockers with member-owned locks are self-service."

**Common misuse:** assuming all lockers in a "staffed" locker room are attended just because an attendant is present in the room; presence without custody is not attendance in the legal sense.

### Chain of custody (lost & found)

The documented, unbroken record of who held a found item, where, and when, from the moment it's found to its final disposition (claimed, donated, or discarded) — the record that makes a lost & found defensible rather than a junk drawer.

**In use:** "Log it with today's date and a 30-day retention deadline before it goes in the cabinet — the log is what protects us if someone claims it was taken."

**Common misuse:** storing found items without a written log, relying on staff memory of "who found what when," which collapses the moment staff turn over.

### High-value (HV) tag/disclosure

A separate handling track offered at intake for patrons who disclose a valuable item, typically moving the item to a locked cabinet or safe with a cross-referenced log entry, sometimes for an added fee — the mechanism that shifts liability exposure above the standard statutory cap.

**In use:** "She disclosed the fur at check-in, so it went on the HV tag into the cage — that's a different liability posture than an undisclosed item in a regular coat."

**Common misuse:** treating the HV offer as optional customer service rather than the specific action that changes the venue's liability exposure — declining it (with the decline noted) is what keeps the standard cap in force.

### Key/lock control log

A running record of which house-issued lock or key is assigned to which locker number and to whom, used to track custody of attended lockers and to reset access between renters.

**In use:** "Pull the key control log before you reset locker 214 — we need to confirm which key was issued today before handing out a new one."

**Common misuse:** relying on a physical lock being present as proof of who has access, without checking the log for duplicate keys issued to a prior renter that were never collected.
