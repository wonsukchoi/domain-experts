# Vocabulary

Terms generalists reliably misuse or collapse into each other. Format per term: definition, how a practitioner actually uses it, the common misuse.

### Milieu (therapeutic milieu)

The structured social and physical environment of the unit itself — schedule, staff-patient interaction pattern, physical layout — treated as an active clinical intervention, not just the setting the real treatment happens in.

**In use:** "The milieu got loud and unstructured after the activity got cancelled — that's when the second incident started, not before it."

**Common misuse:** treating the milieu as background noise around "the real treatment" (medication, therapy sessions), rather than as a variable staff actively manage — the Safewards model's evidence is that milieu factors drive a large share of conflict independent of patient acuity.

### Restraint vs. seclusion

Restraint: any manual, physical, or mechanical method that restricts a patient's movement (holds, cuffs, belts). Seclusion: involuntary confinement alone in a room the patient cannot leave. They trigger the same regulatory clocks (face-to-face, order renewal) but are documented and reported as distinct interventions.

**In use:** "That was seclusion, not restraint — she was alone in the room with the door secured, nobody had hands on her."

**Common misuse:** using the terms interchangeably in documentation, which matters because facility policy and regulatory reporting track them separately, and mixing them up misrepresents what actually happened to reviewers.

### Chemical restraint vs. PRN medication

A PRN (as-needed) medication treats a clinical symptom (anxiety, agitation) on the patient's or team's judgment that it's needed. The same medication becomes a chemical restraint when it's given specifically to control behavior, not to treat a diagnosed condition, and does so in a way not required to treat the patient's medical/psychiatric condition.

**In use:** "Giving lorazepam because she's anxious and asking for it is a PRN. Giving it purely to make her stop yelling so the unit is quieter is a chemical restraint — same drug, different justification, different reporting requirement."

**Common misuse:** assuming any sedating medication given during an agitated moment is automatically "just a PRN" — the distinction is the documented clinical justification, not the drug or the moment.

### 1:1 (constant observation) vs. Q15 checks

1:1 means continuous, uninterrupted staff presence within sight and sound of one specific patient. Q15 means a documented check every 15 minutes, with staff not present in between.

**In use:** "She's ordered for 1:1, not Q15 — if you step away to help with the med pass, someone else has to physically take over first."

**Common misuse:** treating 1:1 as "check on them more often" rather than continuous presence — a 1:1 order that's actually being run as frequent Q5 checks is a silent downgrade of the clinically ordered observation level.

### Elopement

A patient leaving a unit or facility without authorization, distinct from a general "wandering" concern — it specifically implies departure from a secured or supervised area against the treatment plan.

**In use:** "She's flagged elopement risk after the last AMA attempt — door alarms and escort protocol apply, not just line-of-sight."

**Common misuse:** using "elopement" for any patient who's simply out of view for a moment inside the unit — it specifically means leaving the supervised area, which changes the response (security, facility lockdown protocol) from a routine location check.

### Akathisia vs. agitation

Akathisia is a specific, drug-induced (usually antipsychotic) inner sense of restlessness with an inability to stay still, often described by patients as a subjective, unbearable urge to move. Agitation is a broader behavioral state that can stem from many causes, including untreated psychosis, pain, or environment.

**In use:** "Ask her what it feels like before you chart 'agitated' — if it's 'I can't sit still, it's like bugs under my skin' onset a day after the dose increase, that's akathisia, and more antipsychotic makes it worse, not better."

**Common misuse:** charting any restlessness after a med change as generic "agitation" and requesting another PRN antipsychotic dose, which is the wrong direction of treatment for a drug-induced symptom.

### Neuroleptic malignant syndrome (NMS)

A rare, potentially fatal reaction to antipsychotic medication presenting with fever, muscle rigidity, autonomic instability, and altered mental status — a medical emergency, not a psychiatric symptom.

**In use:** "Temp's 101.8 and she's rigid, not just tense — that's an NMS workup, STAT vitals and MD notification now, not 'she's anxious, give her time.'"

**Common misuse:** attributing early NMS signs to anxiety or "just being difficult" because the patient is already on a psychiatric unit — the setting makes it easier, not harder, to misattribute a medical emergency to the underlying diagnosis.

### Brøset Violence Checklist (BVC) score

A structured 6-item, same-shift observation tool (confusion, irritability, boisterousness, verbal threats, physical threats, attacks on objects) scored 0/6 to 6/6, used to flag short-term violence risk.

**In use:** "Score's a 3 this shift, up from 1 last shift — that's not just 'having a bad day,' that's the pattern that gets escalated."

**Common misuse:** scoring it once at admission and never re-scoring, treating it as a static risk label rather than a same-shift, repeatable measurement meant to catch a trend.

### Least restrictive intervention

The principle that any intervention limiting a patient's freedom should be the least restrictive option that still manages the actual risk — a stepped hierarchy from verbal redirection through voluntary options to seclusion to mechanical restraint, in that order.

**In use:** "We went straight to seclusion because verbal de-escalation and the voluntary quiet-room offer were both tried and declined first — that's documented, so it's defensible as least restrictive."

**Common misuse:** treating "least restrictive" as a slogan rather than a documentation requirement — regulators and reviewers specifically look for evidence that lesser options were attempted and failed, not just asserted.

### Debriefing (post-incident)

A structured conversation with the patient and staff after a restraint, seclusion, or significant behavioral incident, aimed at identifying what preceded the event and what could interrupt it earlier next time — required by most facility policy and by NASMHPD's Six Core Strategies model.

**In use:** "The debrief flagged that the akathisia question wasn't asked until after the wall strike — next time that's step one at the first sign of restlessness."

**Common misuse:** treating the debrief as a formality documented after the fact ("pt calm, no further issues") instead of a specific extraction of the earliest interruptible point in the sequence.

### Face-to-face evaluation

The in-person assessment by a physician or a trained RN/PA, required within a specific window (commonly 1 hour) after a restraint or seclusion episode begins, to confirm the intervention is still clinically justified.

**In use:** "Face-to-face is due at 14:53 — page now, don't wait for the physician's next scheduled rounds."

**Common misuse:** conflating it with a phone/verbal order — the requirement is specifically an in-person evaluation of the patient, not just an order renewal by phone.

### Acuity (level)

A working classification of how intensive a patient's current care/observation needs are, distinct from diagnosis severity — a stable patient with a severe diagnosis can be low acuity, and a diagnostically mild patient in active crisis can be high acuity.

**In use:** "She's high acuity right now, not because the diagnosis is worse, but because she's 6 hours post-seclusion and still not to baseline."

**Common misuse:** using acuity and diagnosis severity interchangeably in handoff — acuity is about the current state and observation need, and it can change hour to hour independent of the underlying diagnosis.
