# Vocabulary

### Colorfastness
A dye's resistance to bleeding, fading, or transferring when exposed to water, solvent, or friction during cleaning.
**In use:** "Never seen this dye lot before — running a colorfastness test on the inside hem before it goes anywhere near the full wash."
**Common misuse:** Assuming colorfastness based on a garment's age or visible wear rather than testing it directly — an older, worn-looking garment can still bleed catastrophically if its dye was never properly set, and a newer one isn't automatically safe either.

### Tannin stain
A category of stain (wine, coffee, tea, fruit juice) caused by plant-based tannin compounds, typically requiring an acidic or specifically formulated tannin-remover treatment, distinct from protein or oil-based stains.
**In use:** "That's a tannin stain, not protein — different remover, different order in the sequence."
**Common misuse:** Treating all "food and drink" stains as one category — tannin, protein, and oil-based stains each require different chemistry, and misclassifying one as another risks an ineffective or damaging treatment.

### Protein stain
A stain from a biological source (blood, egg, dairy, sweat) containing proteins that denature and coagulate permanently when exposed to heat, requiring cold-water and enzyme-based treatment before any heat is applied.
**In use:** "Cold water and enzyme treatment first on that blood stain — hot water or a warm iron will set it permanently."
**Common misuse:** Applying hot water or heat to a protein stain expecting it to "loosen" the stain the way heat helps with some other stain types — for protein stains specifically, heat causes an irreversible chemical change that sets the stain rather than removing it.

### Spot/bleed test
A controlled test applying a cleaning agent or treatment to a small, inconspicuous area of a garment before committing the entire item to that process, checking for colorfastness or fiber reaction.
**In use:** "Spot test came back clean on the inside seam — cleared for full processing with this solvent."
**Common misuse:** Treating a spot test as optional for garments that "look fine" — the whole point of the test is to catch a risk that isn't visible from inspecting the garment's current condition.

### Wet cleaning
A professional cleaning process using water and specialized detergents/equipment designed to safely clean items labeled "dry clean only," as an alternative to solvent-based dry cleaning.
**In use:** "This one's dry-clean-only on the label, but we're wet-cleaning it — the fiber and construction actually tolerate it fine with the right equipment."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a "dry clean only" label rules out wet cleaning entirely — the label reflects one tested-safe method, not necessarily the only safe method, and professional wet cleaning can safely handle many garments labeled dry-clean-only.

### Solvent (dry cleaning)
The chemical medium (perchloroethylene, hydrocarbon, or other formulations) used in place of water to clean garments in a dry-cleaning machine, chosen based on fiber, finish, and construction compatibility.
**In use:** "This trim's adhesive won't hold up to perc — switching to a hydrocarbon solvent for this one."
**Common misuse:** Treating solvent choice as a facility-wide default rather than a per-garment decision — a solvent safe for a base fabric can still damage a trim, adhesive, or finish that isn't addressed by the fabric's general care instructions.

### Heat-setting (of a stain)
The permanent fixation of a stain into a fabric caused by applying heat (ironing, hot drying, hot pressing) before the stain has been fully removed.
**In use:** "Don't press that one yet — if any of that stain's still there, the heat will set it for good."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a stain that "looks gone" after treatment is safe to press — some stains (especially oil-based or incompletely treated protein stains) can be invisible while wet but still present enough to heat-set under pressing.

### Intake documentation
The record made when an item is received for processing, noting fiber content, existing damage, stains, and any risk factors, before any cleaning or treatment begins.
**In use:** "That fading was already there — it's in the intake photos from when the item came in."
**Common misuse:** Skipping intake documentation for a "simple" or routine item — a defect discovered post-processing with no intake record creates a liability dispute with no reference point to resolve it.

### Fiber-blend sensitivity
The principle that a treatment safe for a garment's dominant fiber can still damage a less prevalent fiber present in a blend.
**In use:** "It's mostly cotton, but the 40% silk content means we're diluting this treatment, not running it at full cotton strength."
**Common misuse:** Basing treatment strength on the dominant visible fiber alone — a smaller percentage of a more sensitive fiber in a blend can still be damaged by a treatment concentration that's perfectly safe for the majority fiber.

### Neutralization (between treatments)
Fully rinsing and chemically neutralizing a treatment before applying a second, chemically different treatment to the same or an adjacent area.
**In use:** "Rinsed and neutralized the tannin remover before starting on the ink stain — don't want those two chemistries meeting on the fabric."
**Common misuse:** Applying a second treatment directly after a first without confirming the first has been fully neutralized — residual chemistry from one treatment can react adversely with the next, or set the second stain rather than removing it.
