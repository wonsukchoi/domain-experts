# Vocabulary

Terms generalists conflate that practitioners keep sharply separate. Each: definition, how a practitioner actually uses it in a sentence, and the common misuse.

## Private/household cook vs. personal chef

A **private** or **household cook** is embedded in one household, usually daily and often live-in, cooking that family's actual meals across years. A **personal chef** more often serves multiple clients on a rotating schedule (batch-cooking a week of meals per client, then moving to the next), a business-services model closer to catering than employment.

**In use:** "I'm not taking personal-chef clients anymore — I placed as a household cook with one family, five days a week, and that's a different job."

**Common misuse:** using the titles interchangeably on a resume or job posting; agencies and families screen for one or the other because the schedule, exclusivity, and depth of relationship differ.

## Live-in vs. live-out

**Live-in** means residing on the property, usually with defined but often blurrier off-duty hours and housing as part of compensation. **Live-out** means commuting, with a harder daily start/end time and no housing benefit.

**In use:** "The live-in offer looks higher on paper, but once you back out housing value and count the actual hours I'm on call, the live-out role at a lower salary pays better per hour worked."

**Common misuse:** comparing live-in and live-out salary figures directly without adjusting for housing value and the real (not contracted) hours worked — live-in roles without an explicit off-duty clause routinely run far more hours than stated.

## Principal vs. estate manager vs. house manager

The **principal** is the employer — the family member(s) whose preferences ultimately govern the kitchen. The **estate manager** (larger households, often multiple properties) or **house manager** (single property) is the operational layer between staff and principal — budget approval, scheduling, hiring — and may never personally review a menu.

**In use:** "The estate manager signs off on the grocery budget; the principal is who I actually cook for and take taste direction from — don't confuse the two audiences in a memo."

**Common misuse:** sending cost or schedule requests to the principal when the estate manager is the actual approver, or assuming the estate manager's preferences reflect the principal's taste.

## Cross-contact vs. cross-contamination

**Cross-contact** is allergen transfer (a trace of peanut protein on a shared knife) — no growth of pathogens involved, but potentially life-threatening at trace levels for an allergic person. **Cross-contamination** is pathogen transfer (raw chicken juice onto a salad) — a food-safety issue, not an allergy issue, though the physical mechanism (shared surfaces) overlaps.

**In use:** "The nut-free board isn't about bacteria — it's cross-contact, and a rinse doesn't reliably remove allergen protein the way it removes a pathogen load."

**Common misuse:** treating a wash-and-reuse policy as sufficient for both — a properly sanitized surface can still carry enough allergen protein to trigger a reaction that a food-safety-only mindset wouldn't catch.

## NDA vs. non-disparagement clause

The **NDA** (confidentiality agreement) restricts disclosure of information observed in the household — health, finances, guests, security, routines. The **non-disparagement clause** separately restricts negative characterizations of the family, even true ones, after employment ends. Both are typically in the same contract but cover different conduct, and household staffing agencies commonly attach liquidated-damages figures (often in the tens of thousands to low hundreds of thousands of dollars) to breach of either.

**In use:** "Discussing a health diagnosis you overheard is an NDA breach; posting online that the family was difficult to work for is a non-disparagement breach — both are covered, and both carry the same liquidated-damages exposure."

**Common misuse:** assuming the NDA only covers information explicitly labeled as private, or assuming non-disparagement expires when employment ends — most standard household contracts extend it indefinitely.

## Dietary restriction vs. dietary preference

A **restriction** is medically or religiously required (allergy, celiac, kosher, halal) and carries safety or observance consequences for a miss. A **preference** is a taste or lifestyle choice (dislikes cilantro, doing intermittent fasting this month) with no safety consequence for a miss, only a satisfaction one.

**In use:** "The tree-nut allergy gets the dedicated equipment and the written action plan; the fasting window just gets a note in the profile — don't give them the same operational weight."

**Common misuse:** treating every stated dietary want with restriction-level rigor (wasteful, and dilutes attention from the ones that actually carry risk), or the reverse — treating a restriction as just another preference to accommodate "when convenient."

## Family food profile vs. menu

The **food profile** is the standing record of allergies, restrictions, preferences, and meal history for a specific household, built to outlast any one cook's tenure. The **menu** is the current week's or month's planned meals, derived from the profile.

**In use:** "The menu changes every week; the profile only changes when something about the family itself changes — a new diagnosis, a new preference, a new member."

**Common misuse:** keeping restriction and allergy information only inside weekly menu notes, which get discarded, instead of in the profile document a successor cook would actually inherit.

## Batch cooking vs. meal prep

**Batch cooking** produces large quantities of a few base components (a whole roasted protein, a big pot of grain) on a fixed day, portioned and frozen for later reheating or assembly. **Meal prep** more narrowly means assembling complete, ready-to-eat portions ahead of time, often daily or every few days, with less freezing.

**In use:** "We batch-cook the proteins and grains on Sunday, but I still meal-prep the actual plated dinner fresh each night so it doesn't taste reheated."

**Common misuse:** using "meal prep" to describe freezer-banked batch components, which sets a false expectation of daily-fresh assembly when the reality is a Sunday-cooked, Wednesday-reheated protein.

## On-call vs. on-duty

**On-duty** hours are contracted working time. **On-call** means available to be summoned outside those hours, typically at a different (or no) pay rate depending on the contract. Undefined live-in roles routinely default the cook to functionally always on-call.

**In use:** "My contract says on-duty is 7am–7pm; anything past that is on-call, and we agreed on-call gets a flat per-instance rate, not folded into salary."

**Common misuse:** accepting a live-in offer with no on-call terms defined, on the assumption that "family staff" hours are simply whatever the day requires — the single most common driver of early live-in-placement burnout.

## Elimination trial vs. permanent restriction

An **elimination trial** is a time-boxed removal of a food (often 2–6 weeks) to test a suspected sensitivity, ordered by a physician or dietitian, expected to end with either confirmation or reintroduction. A **permanent restriction** is a confirmed, ongoing requirement (diagnosed allergy, celiac disease) with no planned reintroduction.

**In use:** "The dairy elimination is a six-week trial ending May 1st — don't rebuild the whole profile around it as if it's permanent until the follow-up appointment confirms it."

**Common misuse:** treating every elimination trial as if it were a confirmed permanent restriction (over-engineering the kitchen for something that may end in six weeks), or the reverse — treating a confirmed diagnosis as a trial that might lapse on its own.
