# Red flags

Smell tests a placement agency, estate manager, or an experienced cook catches early — what each usually means, the first question to ask, and the data to pull before reacting.

### Grocery bill moves >15% month over month with no itemized explanation offered

**Usually means:** either an unflagged dietary or household change (new diagnosis, guest, seasonal ingredient), or drift — buying convenience/specialty items without tracking the cumulative cost.
**First question:** "Walk me through the delta by category — what's new, what's seasonal, what's just gotten more expensive?"
**Data to pull:** itemized weekly receipts by category for the last 8 weeks; the family food profile for any recent diagnosis or preference change entered in that window.

### The cook can't produce a written allergy/restriction list on request

**Usually means:** restrictions are being tracked in memory rather than in a document, which is fine until the cook is sick, on vacation, or replaced — then the next person is guessing with a life-threatening allergy on the table.
**First question:** "If you were out for two weeks tomorrow, what document would your replacement need, and does it exist right now?"
**Data to pull:** the family food profile document itself, its last-updated date, and whether it names sources (physician, dietitian) for each restriction.

### Same three meals repeating inside a 7-day window for a family without a stated preference for it

**Usually means:** the cook has stopped rotating for one of two reasons — burnout from an undefined schedule, or a batch-cook system built around convenience rather than the family's actual variety tolerance.
**First question:** "What's the current protein rotation cycle, and when did you last change it?"
**Data to pull:** the meals-served log for the last 30 days; the cook's stated on/off-duty hours versus what's actually being worked.

### Cook discusses details of the household (health, guests, finances, security) with other staff, vendors, or socially

**Usually means:** either the NDA/confidentiality terms were never made concrete to the cook beyond a signature, or a genuine boundary violation — the more common failure mode in placements that end abruptly and badly.
**First question:** "What specifically do you understand you can and can't discuss, and with whom?"
**Data to pull:** the signed confidentiality agreement; any documented prior incidents; the agency's standard non-disparagement and liquidated-damages clause language for comparison.

### No separate equipment or zone for an allergen the household has a diagnosed reaction to

**Usually means:** the kitchen was never actually reconfigured after the diagnosis — restrictions were added to the menu without changing the physical workflow that causes cross-contact.
**First question:** "Show me the dedicated board, pan, toaster, and storage shelf for this allergen — where are they?"
**Data to pull:** a kitchen walkthrough against the physician's written plan; any near-miss or reaction incidents logged since the diagnosis.

### Live-in cook has no written off-duty hours in the contract

**Usually means:** the role was scoped as "family staff" without defining boundaries, which is the leading driver of abrupt departures in live-in culinary placements — the cook burns out quietly for months, then leaves with little notice.
**First question:** "What are your contracted hours, on paper, and how often are you actually being asked to work outside them?"
**Data to pull:** the employment contract's schedule clause; a rough log of actual hours worked over the last month versus contracted hours.

### Estate manager or principal is hearing about a dietary change from a family member, not from the cook

**Usually means:** the cook isn't proactively updating the food profile or isn't looped into medical appointments/decisions that affect meals, and is reacting to secondhand information instead of driving the update.
**First question:** "When did you last update the food profile, and who told you about the most recent change?"
**Data to pull:** food profile revision history; timeline of the household's dietary event versus the cook's documented response.

### Cook proposes unsolicited changes to a family member's diet, weight, or parenting choices

**Usually means:** the closeness of a live-in or long-tenure relationship is being read as license to advise on things outside the cooking mandate — a common overcorrection once a cook has built trust with the family.
**First question:** "Was this raised by the family, or did you bring it up unprompted?"
**Data to pull:** none — this is a boundary conversation, not a data problem; if it recurs after being raised once, it's a placement risk.

### Menu costs are being cut right after a dietary-safety requirement was added

**Usually means:** budget pressure is being resolved by cutting corners on the newly required safety measure (dedicated equipment, specialty ingredients) rather than by renegotiating the overall budget — the wrong place to find savings when an allergy is involved.
**First question:** "Which specific line is being cut, and does it touch the allergen-safety setup?"
**Data to pull:** the grocery reconciliation table split by safety-required versus preference-driven spend; confirm nothing in the allergen-safety category has been reduced.
