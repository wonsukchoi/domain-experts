# Vocabulary

### Shrink rule / shrinkage allowance
A pattern-making ruler (or percentage) that accounts for a specific alloy's expected solidification shrinkage, used to build the pattern oversized by that percentage.
**In use:** "Use the 2% shrink rule for this steel casting — that's a different ruler than the aluminum job we ran yesterday."
**Common misuse:** Applying a generic shrinkage percentage regardless of the specific alloy being cast, when shrink rates vary meaningfully between alloys — steel, aluminum, and gray iron each have their own standard rule.

### Riser
A reservoir of molten metal attached to a casting that feeds it during solidification to prevent shrinkage porosity.
**In use:** "Riser modulus needs to beat the section's modulus, or it'll freeze first and leave porosity behind."
**Common misuse:** Sizing a riser by visual habit rather than a solidification-time calculation confirming it actually solidifies after the section it's meant to feed.

### Modulus method (solidification time)
A calculation, based on Chvorinov's rule, relating a section's volume-to-surface-area ratio to its solidification time, used to size risers so they solidify after the casting sections they feed.
**In use:** "Ran the modulus calc — riser's modulus comes out higher than the boss it's feeding, so it'll stay liquid longer, which is what we want."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a bigger riser is always safer, without checking that its modulus is actually higher — meaning slower-solidifying — than the section it's meant to feed.

### Gating system
The network of channels — sprue, runner, gate — through which molten metal fills a mold.
**In use:** "Gating's sized for iron's fill velocity — can't reuse this design for the aluminum job without recalculating."
**Common misuse:** Reusing a "standard" gating design across different alloys without checking that specific alloy's fill-velocity and turbulence tolerance.

### Casting yield
The ratio of good, sellable casting weight to total metal poured, including gates, risers, and scrap.
**In use:** "Scrap count looks better on the new tooling, but actual yield is barely different once you weigh in the bigger gating system."
**Common misuse:** Comparing processes by scrap count or piece count alone, rather than calculating actual yield, which captures the real cost picture more accurately.

### Green sand
A molding sand mixture — sand, clay binder, water — used unbaked ("green"), whose moisture content directly affects both moldability and gas-defect risk.
**In use:** "Green sand's moisture is off today — that's probably behind the pinholing, not the core binder."
**Common misuse:** Treating "green sand problems" as one single category, rather than separately checking moisture content, permeability, and compaction as distinct variables.

### Core
A sand or other material insert placed in a mold to create internal cavities or features that the mold's outer shape alone can't produce.
**In use:** "That internal passage comes from the core, not the mold cavity itself — check core binder gassing if it's the source of the defect."
**Common misuse:** Assuming core-related gas defects always trace back to the mold sand, rather than checking the core's own binder off-gassing separately.

### Permeability (sand)
A sand mixture's ability to let gas escape through it during pouring, a defect-prevention property distinct from its moisture content.
**In use:** "Moisture's within spec, but permeability's low from over-compaction — that's the real source of the gas defect."
**Common misuse:** Conflating permeability with moisture content, when a sand mix can have correct moisture but still poor permeability from grain size or compaction issues.

### Cold shut
A casting defect where two streams of metal meet and fail to fuse properly, often from metal that cooled too much before merging.
**In use:** "That's a cold shut, not porosity — points to a gating or pour-temperature fix, not the sand mix."
**Common misuse:** Treating any surface flaw as generically "a defect" rather than recognizing cold shut's specific cause — slow or interrupted fill — which points to a gating or pour-temperature correction, not a sand adjustment.

### Draft angle (pattern)
The slight taper built into a pattern's vertical surfaces to allow it to be withdrawn from the sand mold without damaging the cavity.
**In use:** "Add draft to those walls — a dead-vertical pattern is going to tear the mold every time it's pulled."
**Common misuse:** Designing a pattern with vertical, zero-draft walls assuming it will still pull cleanly, risking mold damage on every single withdrawal.
