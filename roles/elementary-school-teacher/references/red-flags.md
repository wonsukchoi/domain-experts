# Elementary School Teacher — Red Flags

### WCPM at or above seasonal benchmark but accuracy below 95%
- **Usually means:** fast-but-inaccurate reading profile — sight-word recognition or guessing is masking a decoding gap; second most likely is a passage-difficulty mismatch (text easier than the student's actual instructional level).
- **First question:** what band does the accuracy score fall in, and does the miscue pattern share a structural feature?
- **Data to pull:** the running record's miscue tags, not just the WCPM and error count.

### Self-correction rate below 20% despite meaning-disrupting errors
- **Usually means:** weak self-monitoring — the student isn't checking whether what they read makes sense; second most likely is the text is at frustration level and the student has disengaged from meaning-making entirely.
- **First question:** is the accuracy band instructional or frustration?
- **Data to pull:** accuracy percentage alongside the self-correction rate; don't evaluate self-correction rate in isolation.

### More than half of miscues share one structural feature
- **Usually means:** a specific, targetable decoding skill gap (a vowel pattern, affixes, a syllable type); second most likely is a text with an unusual concentration of one word type inflating the pattern artificially.
- **First question:** does the same pattern show up across more than one assessment, or only this one passage?
- **Data to pull:** miscue tags from at least two independent running records before committing to a tier placement.

### A flagged low score belongs to a student with an active accommodation
- **Usually means:** the accommodation wasn't delivered that day; second most likely is the accommodation is genuinely insufficient for the current task demand.
- **First question:** was the accommodation actually delivered, per the delivery log, on the day of this assessment?
- **Data to pull:** the accommodation-delivery log cross-referenced against the assessment date.

### A single low data point triggers a parent call or tier change
- **Usually means:** overreacting to noise instead of waiting for a trend; second most likely is a genuine one-off (illness, a bad day) being treated as a skill signal.
- **First question:** are there at least three consistent data points, or is this the first?
- **Data to pull:** the student's last 2–3 progress-monitoring checks on the same skill.

### A behavior incident escalates to a public correction on the first occurrence
- **Usually means:** the escalation ladder was skipped rather than followed in order; second most likely is a genuine safety issue that legitimately requires an immediate, visible response.
- **First question:** was this a safety issue, or could a private redirect have been tried first?
- **Data to pull:** the classroom's documented escalation-ladder steps and whether they were followed in sequence.

### A class-wide quiz or check shows a bimodal or split distribution
- **Usually means:** two subgroups need different responses (reteach vs. extension), not one blanket remediation; second most likely is a single confusing item dragging down an otherwise fine distribution.
- **First question:** does the split track a specific item or skill, or is it genuinely two ability clusters?
- **Data to pull:** item-level breakdown, not just the overall score distribution.

### Intervention minutes are being pulled from a core-instruction block
- **Usually means:** scheduling pressure led to solving one student's gap by creating a new one for the rest of the class; second most likely is a genuine lack of any other available block that week.
- **First question:** which non-core block could this have come from instead?
- **Data to pull:** the weekly schedule, checking whether center-rotation or independent time was available and unused.

### A student clears a benchmark check but grades or work samples tell a different story
- **Usually means:** the benchmark check and classroom work are measuring different skills, or the benchmark passage doesn't match the difficulty of daily classroom text; second most likely is a testing-condition anomaly (rushed, distracted, unusually motivated that day).
- **First question:** does the benchmark passage's difficulty match what the student handles independently in class?
- **Data to pull:** a work sample from the same week as the benchmark check.

### A parent reports a concern the classroom data doesn't show
- **Usually means:** the concern shows up in a context the classroom assessment doesn't capture (homework, a specific subject, home reading); second most likely is a genuine early signal the classroom data hasn't caught up to yet.
- **First question:** what specific context is the parent describing, and does the classroom have any data from that same context?
- **Data to pull:** any available data from the context the parent named, even informal.
