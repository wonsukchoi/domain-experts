# Elementary School Teacher — Vocabulary

### Running record
A real-time transcript of a student reading aloud, marking every error, self-correction, and behavior (repetition, pause) against the actual text, used to calculate accuracy and analyze miscues.
**In use:** "Her running record from this morning put her at 92% accuracy, instructional band."
**Common misuse:** Treated as interchangeable with a fluency timing (WCPM-only check) — a running record captures accuracy and miscue detail a timing alone does not.

### WCPM (words correct per minute)
A fluency rate measure: words read correctly divided by the time taken, expressed per minute.
**In use:** "He's at 74 WCPM against a winter benchmark of 86."
**Common misuse:** Used alone as a mastery signal — a student can hit benchmark WCPM while reading well below the accuracy threshold for independent-level text.

### Accuracy band
The classification of a reading accuracy percentage into independent (95–100%), instructional (90–94%), or frustration (below 90%) — determines whether a text is appropriate for independent reading, guided instruction, or is currently too hard.
**In use:** "That passage is frustration-band for him right now; we need something at his instructional level for the intervention."
**Common misuse:** Conflated with a grade-level label — a student can be reading grade-level text at frustration-level accuracy, which is a different fact than "reading below grade level."

### Miscue
Any deviation from the printed text during oral reading — a substitution, omission, insertion, or self-correction — analyzed for whether it preserves meaning and what structural feature it shares with other miscues.
**In use:** "Nine of her fourteen miscues were suffix drops on multisyllabic words — that's the pattern to target."
**Common misuse:** Counted only as an error tally without tagging the type — the count alone can't distinguish a decoding gap from a self-monitoring gap.

### Self-correction rate
The proportion of miscues a student catches and fixes without prompting, expressed as self-corrections ÷ (errors + self-corrections).
**In use:** "His self-correction rate is only 12%, well below the 30%+ we'd want to see — he's not checking whether what he read makes sense."
**Common misuse:** Evaluated without accuracy band context — a low self-correction rate on frustration-level text may just mean the text is too hard, not that self-monitoring itself is the deficit.

### Tier 1 / Tier 2 / Tier 3 (MTSS/RTI)
The multi-tiered system of supports framework: Tier 1 is core instruction for all students, Tier 2 is targeted small-group intervention for students showing a specific gap, Tier 3 is intensive, often individualized intervention, sometimes triggering a special-education referral.
**In use:** "She's Tier 2 for decoding, thirty minutes three times a week, center-rotation time."
**Common misuse:** Treated as a permanent label rather than a fluid, data-driven placement — students move between tiers as progress-monitoring data changes, not once and done.

### Accommodation vs. modification
An accommodation changes access to content (extended time, read-aloud, chunked text) without changing what is being measured; a modification changes the standard or expectation itself (fewer items, a lower reading level text).
**In use:** "Extended time is an accommodation — he's still expected to master the same standard, just with more time."
**Common misuse:** Used interchangeably — swapping in an easier passage "to help" a student with a reading accommodation is a modification, not an accommodation, and changes what the assessment is actually measuring.

### Formative vs. summative assessment
Formative assessment is low-stakes and diagnostic, meant to change the next instructional move; summative assessment measures mastery at the end of a unit or period and typically counts toward a grade.
**In use:** "That exit ticket is formative — I'm using it to decide tomorrow's small groups, not to grade it."
**Common misuse:** Grading formative checks at full weight, which converts them into low-stakes summatives and removes the incentive to use the data diagnostically.

### Progress monitoring
Repeated, brief assessment of a specific skill over time (typically weekly or biweekly during an intervention) used to determine whether a student is responding to the intervention.
**In use:** "We'll progress-monitor the affix-decoding skill every two weeks and adjust the intervention if the trend doesn't move."
**Common misuse:** Acting on a single progress-monitoring data point instead of waiting for a trend across three or more checks.

### Differentiation
Varying the instructional path, pacing, or scaffolding by which students reach the same learning target, without changing the target itself.
**In use:** "Same objective for the whole group — I'm differentiating the entry point, not the goal."
**Common misuse:** Confused with modification — quietly lowering what "meeting the standard" means for a struggling group is not differentiation, it's an unstated second standard.

### Self-contained classroom
A classroom structure (typical in elementary grades) where one teacher instructs the same group of students across most or all core subjects, as opposed to a departmentalized structure where students rotate between subject-specialist teachers.
**In use:** "In a self-contained classroom, the reading intervention has to come out of the daily schedule I control, not a separate specialist's block."
**Common misuse:** Assuming elementary scheduling flexibility works the same way secondary scheduling does — a self-contained teacher owns the whole day's tradeoffs, not just one subject's block.

### Structural analysis (decoding)
The strategy of breaking a multisyllabic word into meaningful parts — prefixes, suffixes, root words, syllables — to decode it, as distinct from whole-word sight recognition or phonics-only sound-by-sound decoding.
**In use:** "His miscues are almost all suffix drops — that's a structural-analysis gap, not a phonics gap."
**Common misuse:** Treated as the same skill as basic phonics — a student can have solid single-syllable phonics and still lack the structural-analysis strategy needed for longer words.

### PBIS (Positive Behavioral Interventions and Supports)
A tiered, proactive framework for teaching and reinforcing expected behavior, with a defined, least-restrictive-first escalation sequence for responding to behavior incidents.
**In use:** "Per our PBIS matrix, that's a Tier 1 redirect — proximity and a non-verbal cue before anything more."
**Common misuse:** Used only as a punishment/consequence system — PBIS is primarily about proactive teaching and reinforcement, with the escalation ladder as a fallback, not the main mechanism.

### IEP progress report
A formal, periodic report (tied to a student's Individualized Education Program) documenting measurable progress toward specific IEP goals, distinct from a general report card.
**In use:** "The IEP progress report needs the actual data points, not just 'making progress.'"
**Common misuse:** Filled out with vague qualitative language instead of the specific data trend the IEP goal specifies — it's a legal document, and vague language doesn't satisfy the reporting requirement.
