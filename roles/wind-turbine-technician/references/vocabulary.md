# Vocabulary

Terms generalists conflate that practitioners keep sharply separate. Each: definition, how a practitioner actually uses it in a sentence, and the common misuse to avoid.

## Fall arrest vs. fall restraint vs. work positioning

**Fall arrest** stops a fall already in progress, within a rated distance, using energy-absorbing components. **Fall restraint** physically prevents the worker from reaching a fall hazard at all — no fall occurs. **Work positioning** holds a worker in place to work hands-free at height, with fall arrest as a required backup, not a substitute.

*Practitioner usage:* "We're restraint-rigged on the platform edge, not arrest — the lanyard's short enough that he physically can't get to the edge."

*Common misuse:* calling any harness-and-lanyard setup "fall protection" without specifying which of the three it is — the rescue plan, anchor rating, and equipment spec are different for each.

## Self-rescue vs. assisted rescue vs. technical rescue

**Self-rescue** is the fallen worker descending or ascending back to safety themselves, immediately, using their own equipment. **Assisted rescue** is a trained crew member reaching and lowering/raising them using site equipment. **Technical rescue** is an external specialized team (fire/rescue with rope-rescue capability) responding — typically the slowest option and the one that most often exceeds the suspension-trauma window if it's the only plan on file.

*Practitioner usage:* "Our rescue plan is self-rescue primary, assisted-rescue backup — technical rescue is the plan only fails if both of those fail, and by then we're past the window that matters."

*Common misuse:* writing "call 911" as the rescue plan — technical rescue response time from an external service routinely exceeds the 5–15 minute suspension-trauma window, which is why it can't be the primary plan.

## Suspension trauma (orthostatic intolerance)

The physiological effect of hanging motionless and upright in a harness after a fall, where blood pools in the legs and venous return to the heart drops, which can progress to loss of consciousness — distinct from any injury caused by the fall itself.

*Practitioner usage:* "He's uninjured from the fall, but he's been hanging for eight minutes — get him down now, that's a suspension-trauma clock, not an injury assessment."

*Common misuse:* treating an uninjured suspended worker as low-priority because "nothing's broken" — the trauma risk is time-based and independent of impact injury.

## Torque vs. tension (preload)

**Torque** is the rotational force applied to a fastener. **Tension (preload)** is the actual clamping force generated in the joint — the thing that matters structurally. Torque is only a proxy for tension, mediated by a friction (K-factor) coefficient that changes with lubrication, surface condition, and reuse.

*Practitioner usage:* "Same torque wrench reading doesn't mean same tension if the K-factor's different — that's why we tension-control the blade-root bolts instead of trusting torque."

*Common misuse:* assuming a correct torque-wrench reading guarantees correct preload, ignoring that the same nominal torque can correspond to a 20%+ different tension depending on the friction condition.

## Wear-metal trending vs. single oil-sample reading

**Wear-metal trending** compares an element's ppm across multiple consecutive samples to judge the *rate of change*. A **single reading** only judges whether that one sample sits inside or outside an absolute band. A rapidly accelerating trend is a stronger and earlier failure signal than a single value crossing an absolute threshold.

*Practitioner usage:* "Fe's still under caution, but it doubled last quarter — that trend is the finding, not the absolute number."

*Common misuse:* clearing a sample because it's numerically "in range" without checking whether it represents a sudden jump from the prior sample.

## ISO 4406 cleanliness code

A three-number code (e.g., 18/16/13) reporting particle counts at three size thresholds per unit volume of oil — a fluid-cleanliness measure independent of the wear-metal (chemical) analysis run on the same sample.

*Practitioner usage:* "Wear metals look fine, but the ISO code jumped two steps — that's a filtration problem generating particulate, not necessarily a bearing problem yet."

*Common misuse:* treating cleanliness code and wear-metal ppm as the same measurement, or as redundant — they catch different failure modes (contamination/filtration vs. active component wear).

## Gear-mesh frequency / shaft-order harmonic

The vibration frequency generated by gear teeth meshing (gear-mesh frequency = shaft speed × tooth count) or by shaft rotation itself and its integer multiples (shaft-order harmonics). A vibration alarm landing precisely at one of these is far more likely to indicate a real mechanical fault than an alarm at an arbitrary frequency.

*Practitioner usage:* "The alarm's sitting right on the high-speed shaft's second gear-mesh harmonic — that's not noise, that's a tooth or bearing signature."

*Common misuse:* reporting "a vibration alarm fired" without checking where in the spectrum it sits, which is the detail that distinguishes a real fault from a sensor artifact.

## SCADA vs. condition-monitoring system (CMS)

**SCADA** (supervisory control and data acquisition) is the turbine's operational control and telemetry system — power output, wind speed, status codes, basic temperature/vibration tags. A dedicated **CMS** is a purpose-built vibration/oil/acoustic monitoring package, often higher sample-rate and more diagnostic than SCADA's basic tags, and not present on every turbine.

*Practitioner usage:* "SCADA flagged the temperature trend, but we don't have CMS on this unit, so the spectral detail has to come from a handheld reading."

*Common misuse:* assuming every turbine has full spectral vibration diagnostics available remotely — many fleets only have SCADA-level basic tags, and a handheld or borescope check is still required to confirm anything specific.

## Confined space vs. permit-required confined space

**Confined space** is any space large enough to enter, with limited entry/exit, not designed for continuous occupancy. **Permit-required confined space** adds at least one of: hazardous atmosphere, engulfment hazard, or a configuration that could trap/asphyxiate an entrant — triggering a formal entry-permit process, atmospheric testing, and attendant requirements.

*Practitioner usage:* "The hub's technically confined space either way, but with the atmosphere untested and only one egress path, we're treating it as permit-required."

*Common misuse:* relying on a manufacturer's non-classification of the nacelle/hub as evidence it isn't permit-required — many OEM documents predate current confined-space practice and don't address it at all.

## Major component exchange (MCE)

Industry term for replacing a large drivetrain or blade component (gearbox, generator, main bearing, full blade) in place, requiring a crane and typically the largest single cost/downtime event in a turbine's operating life outside of catastrophic failure.

*Practitioner usage:* "This is heading toward an MCE if we don't catch it at the bearing-only stage — book the crane lead time now, not after the borescope confirms it."

*Common misuse:* using "MCE" loosely for any repair requiring a crane, when the term specifically implies swapping a major drivetrain/blade component, not a minor lift.

## Predictive vs. preventive vs. corrective maintenance

**Preventive** maintenance runs on a fixed schedule regardless of condition (e.g., annual oil change). **Predictive** maintenance uses condition data (oil trend, vibration trend) to decide timing. **Corrective** maintenance responds to a fault that's already occurred.

*Practitioner usage:* "This was supposed to be a preventive quarterly sample, but the trend turned it into a predictive call — we're not waiting for the fixed interval."

*Common misuse:* calling a fixed-interval oil change or bolt check "predictive maintenance" just because it involves inspecting a component — predictive specifically means the *timing* is driven by condition data, not the calendar.

## Down-conductor / lightning protection system (LPS)

The conductive path (down-conductor) carrying a lightning strike's current from the blade receptors through the nacelle and tower to earth ground, engineered to a resistance spec so the current bypasses internal electronics and bearings rather than arcing through them.

*Practitioner usage:* "Down-conductor resistance is above spec after that strike — treat this as a possible internal arc path until it's verified, not just a normal post-strike inspection."

*Common misuse:* assuming a strike that didn't trip the turbine offline caused no damage — receptor and down-conductor degradation can be present with no immediate operational symptom.
