# Red flags

Smell tests an attendant catches before a fueling transaction goes wrong, or before a spill decision gets defended after the fact.

### Fuel weeping from a boat's tank vent hours after a "full" fill, with no fueling error logged

- **Usually means:** the tank was filled to 100% of rated capacity (or to the pump's automatic click-off) instead of the 90% convention, and thermal expansion pushed fuel out the vent line once the boat sat in the sun.
- **First question:** "What gallon figure did we stop at relative to this tank's rated capacity, and was that logged?"
- **Data to pull:** the fueling log's gallons-added figure against rated capacity, ambient temperature at time of fueling versus time of the weep report, prior weep reports for this vessel or slip.

### Visible sheen on the water near the dock, however small

- **Usually means:** fuel reached the water during or after a transfer — overflow, a loose fill cap, or a vent-line weep — and the size of the sheen has no bearing on whether it must be reported.
- **First question:** "Is there a sheen visible on the water right now, yes or no?"
- **Data to pull:** time of discovery, estimated source (transfer overflow vs. vent weep vs. bilge pump discharge), whether the National Response Center has been called, containment action taken.

### Customer or attendant treats a small on-water spill as "not worth calling in"

- **Usually means:** the size of the spill is being used as the reporting criterion instead of the sheen test, which has no minimum quantity threshold under 40 CFR 110.3.
- **First question:** "Did we confirm a sheen was actually visible, or are we deciding based on how many ounces spilled?"
- **Data to pull:** the sheen test's regulatory language, any prior incidents at this facility where a "small" spill was later cited, the facility's spill-response procedure sign-off log.

### Nozzle left unattended or clipped open during marine fueling

- **Usually means:** the attendant is treating a boat fueling like a self-service car fill-up — a habit that carries over from automotive work but has no acceptable failure mode on water.
- **First question:** "Was the nozzle hand-held and watched for the entire transfer, or clipped and left?"
- **Data to pull:** facility policy on attended fueling, any spill or near-miss reports tied to unattended transfers, staffing level at the dock during the shift in question.

### Bilge blower run skipped or cut short before engine start

- **Usually means:** the customer was in a hurry, or the attendant didn't treat the blower cycle as a hard gate — the same "it's probably fine" shortcut that shows up in any interlock-style safety step.
- **First question:** "Did the blower run the full stated minimum before the key was turned, or was it estimated?"
- **Data to pull:** the boat's placard-stated minimum blower time, actual elapsed time logged, any fuel-odor complaint or near-miss reports for this slip or vessel.

### Bonding clip seated on paint or gelcoat instead of bare metal at the fill fitting

- **Usually means:** the bonding step was performed as a formality rather than a functional connection — a clip that doesn't reach bare metal doesn't dissipate static charge no matter how it looks.
- **First question:** "Where exactly is the bonding clip making contact — bare metal, or a painted surface?"
- **Data to pull:** dispenser's bonding-continuity indicator (if equipped), photos or description of the clip's contact point, any static-related incident history at this dock.

### Tire inflated to the number on the sidewall rather than the door placard

- **Usually means:** the attendant is using the tire's maximum rating as if it were the manufacturer's recommended pressure — a generalist mistake that overinflates almost every passenger vehicle.
- **First question:** "Did we check the door-jamb placard PSI, or the number molded on the tire itself?"
- **Data to pull:** placard PSI versus sidewall max PSI for the vehicle in question, actual pressure set at the pump.

### Asymmetric tire pressure (one tire well below its paired tire) topped off without comment

- **Usually means:** a slow leak or valve-stem fault on the low tire, masked by simply reinflating it to spec without flagging the asymmetry to the customer.
- **First question:** "Was this tire low by itself, or were all four down evenly?"
- **Data to pull:** individual tire readings before top-off, any prior visits with the same tire low, whether the customer was told.

### Milky or metallic-flecked oil topped off without comment

- **Usually means:** coolant intrusion (milky) or bearing wear (metallic flecks) — a top-off that says nothing about the reading removes the customer's earliest warning of a developing engine problem.
- **First question:** "What did the oil actually look like on the dipstick before it was topped off?"
- **Data to pull:** dipstick color/consistency noted at time of service, vehicle mileage and any recent service history, whether the customer was informed.

### Spill-containment kit at the dock missing stock or past its usable condition

- **Usually means:** the kit hasn't been inventoried recently, and the facility is one spill away from discovering that at the worst possible moment.
- **First question:** "When was this kit last checked and restocked?"
- **Data to pull:** kit inspection log, absorbent capacity remaining, date of last restock versus facility's stated inspection interval.
