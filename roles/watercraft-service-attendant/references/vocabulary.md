# Vocabulary

Terms generalists flatten into loose synonyms that an attendant keeps sharply separate, because the distinction is what a safety step or a reporting obligation actually hinges on.

## Bonding vs. grounding

**Bonding** connects two conductive objects (nozzle and fill fitting, fill fitting and fuel tank) so they sit at the same electrical potential, preventing a static spark from arcing between them during fuel transfer. **Grounding** connects an object to earth potential, which is a different purpose (protecting against fault current or lightning) and does not by itself prevent the static buildup that occurs as fuel moves through a hose.

*In use:* "The nozzle's bonded to the fill fitting, metal to metal — that's what stops the static spark, not whatever grounding the boat's electrical system has."

*Common misuse:* assuming a boat's grounded electrical system also handles static bonding for fuel transfer — the two systems serve different purposes and one doesn't substitute for the other.

## Sheen test vs. reportable quantity (RQ)

The **sheen test** (40 CFR 110.3) is the Clean Water Act standard for oil discharges to water: any discharge causing a film, sheen, or discoloration on the surface must be reported, with no minimum volume. A **reportable quantity (RQ)** is a different concept used for many other hazardous substances under CERCLA, where a specific poundage threshold triggers the reporting duty. Applying an RQ-style volume threshold to a fuel spill on water is the wrong test.

*In use:* "There's no RQ math to do here — if there's a sheen on the water, it's reportable, period."

*Common misuse:* treating a small on-water fuel spill as non-reportable because "it's under the threshold," when the sheen test has no volume threshold at all.

## Attended fueling vs. self-service fueling

**Attended fueling** requires an operator holding the nozzle and watching the transfer for its entire duration — standard practice at marine fuel docks. **Self-service fueling** allows a customer to operate the pump unsupervised, common at automotive stations, often with a hold-open latch locking the nozzle handle open.

*In use:* "This is a marine dock — attended only, nozzle stays in my hand the whole time, no hold-open latch."

*Common misuse:* carrying automotive self-service habits (clipping the nozzle open, walking away) over to a marine transfer, where an unattended nozzle on water has no acceptable failure mode.

## Vapor density vs. flash point

**Vapor density** compares the weight of a vapor to an equal volume of air (gasoline vapor is roughly 3–4 times heavier than air), determining whether the vapor rises and disperses or sinks and pools. **Flash point** is the temperature at which a liquid gives off enough vapor to ignite given an ignition source — gasoline's flash point is well below any ambient marina temperature, meaning it's always producing ignitable vapor; the question is only where that vapor goes.

*In use:* "Flash point isn't the issue out here — it's always above it for gasoline. The issue is vapor density: it sinks into the bilge instead of blowing off like it would around a car."

*Common misuse:* citing flash point as the reason marine fueling is more dangerous than automotive fueling, when the actual distinguishing factor is where the (already-ignitable) vapor accumulates.

## Bilge blower vs. natural ventilation

A **bilge blower** is a powered fan required under 33 CFR 183.610 that actively exhausts vapor from a boat's enclosed engine compartment. **Natural ventilation** (cowl vents, ducting without a powered fan) relies on air movement from the boat's motion or ambient breeze and is not a substitute for the powered cycle before engine start.

*In use:* "The cowl vents help while underway, but before starting we still run the powered blower — natural ventilation alone doesn't clear a still, closed-up bilge."

*Common misuse:* assuming open cowl vents mean the compartment is already clear, skipping the powered blower cycle because "it's got vents."

## 90%-capacity fill vs. topping off

The **90%-capacity fill** convention stops a marine fuel tank short of its rated capacity to leave room for thermal expansion. **Topping off** — filling to the pump's automatic click-off or to visibly full — is standard and appropriate for a car's fuel tank, which vents differently and doesn't sit in direct sun for hours the way a boat does.

*In use:* "We stop at ninety percent on the boat, not the click-off — topping off like a car sets it up to weep fuel out the vent by afternoon."

*Common misuse:* applying automotive "fill it up" habits to a boat's tank, producing a vent-line weep hours later that looks like no error occurred at the pump.

## Placard PSI vs. sidewall PSI

**Placard PSI** is the vehicle manufacturer's recommended tire pressure, printed on the door jamb or glovebox and specific to that vehicle's weight and handling. **Sidewall PSI** is the tire manufacturer's maximum safe inflation pressure, molded into the tire itself and not tied to any specific vehicle.

*In use:* "Placard calls for 32 front, 30 rear — that's what we set to, not the 44 max stamped on the tire."

*Common misuse:* inflating a tire to its sidewall number as if that were the target pressure, which overinflates almost every passenger vehicle relative to the manufacturer's actual specification.

## Fill fitting vs. vent line

The **fill fitting** is the deck-mounted opening where the fuel nozzle is inserted, connected by a hose down to the tank. The **vent line** is a separate fitting that allows air (and, if the tank is overfilled or fuel expands, vapor or liquid) to escape as the tank fills — it is usually a smaller fitting near the hull side, not the fill fitting itself.

*In use:* "The sheen wasn't from the fill fitting overflowing — check the vent line on the hull side, that's usually where an expansion weep shows up."

*Common misuse:* inspecting only the fill fitting after a reported weep or odor, missing the vent line as the actual source.

## National Response Center (NRC) vs. local harbormaster/marina management

The **National Response Center** (1-800-424-8802) is the federal point of contact for reporting oil and hazardous-material discharges under the Clean Water Act — a legal reporting obligation, not a courtesy call. **Harbormaster or marina management** notification is an internal/local step that may also be appropriate but does not substitute for the federal report when a sheen has occurred.

*In use:* "Called the NRC first since there was a sheen, then let the harbormaster know separately — one doesn't cover the other."

*Common misuse:* notifying marina management about an on-water spill and treating that as sufficient, without making the separate federal NRC report the sheen test actually requires.

## Ethanol phase separation

A condition where ethanol-blended gasoline (E10 and similar) absorbs enough water — from humid marine air or tank condensation — that the ethanol-water mixture separates from the gasoline, dropping to the bottom of the tank as a corrosive, low-octane layer that can be drawn into the engine.

*In use:* "Fuel that's sat all winter in a partially-full tank is exactly where phase separation shows up — check for a cloudy or separated layer before assuming it's just old gas."

*Common misuse:* treating any old or discolored fuel complaint as generic "stale gas," when phase separation is a specific, water-driven chemical change more common in marine tanks than automotive ones due to humidity and longer dormancy periods.
