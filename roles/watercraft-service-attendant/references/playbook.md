# Playbook

Filled sequences an attendant runs, with the numbers that make them safe rather than merely fast. Adapt the specific capacities and timings to the boat's or vehicle's own placard/manual figures — these are worked examples, not universal constants.

## 1. Marine fueling: 90%-capacity fill calculation and bonding sequence

**Boat profile used for this example:** single gasoline outboard, rated main tank capacity 60 gallons, gauge reading 1/4 tank at arrival.

**Sequence:**

1. **Confirm the boat is secured to the dock and the engine is off** before rigging the nozzle — no fueling with an engine running.
2. **Seat the nozzle's bonding clip on bare metal at the fill fitting** (not on paint, gelcoat, or a hose clamp) and confirm continuity if the dispenser has a bonding-indicator light. This step comes before the pump handle is squeezed, not after.
3. **Calculate the stop point at 90% of rated capacity**, not the pump's automatic click-off:

   | Value | Calculation | Result |
   |---|---|---|
   | Rated capacity | given | 60 gal |
   | 90% stop point | 60 × 0.90 | 54 gal |
   | Current fuel | 60 × 0.25 | 15 gal |
   | Gallons to add | 54 − 15 | **39 gal** |

   Fueling to the pump's click-off instead of this number risks reaching 100% capacity with zero expansion headroom — the exact setup for a vent-line weep hours later with no attendant error involved.

4. **Fuel attended, nozzle in hand, watching the fill opening and the waterline continuously** — never clip, latch, or leave the nozzle unattended on a boat, even briefly.
5. **Stop at the calculated gallon figure, not by feel.** Confirm the pump's gallon counter against the planned stop point before removing the nozzle.
6. **Wipe any drips at the fill fitting immediately** with an absorbent pad kept at the dock, before they can run to the waterline.

## 2. Spill-response and NRC-notification decision (worksheet, filled example)

Run this the moment any fuel reaches the water, regardless of how small the amount looked.

```
EVENT: Overflow at deck fill fitting during transfer
ESTIMATED VOLUME: ~0.5 gal (visual estimate, hose-back-splash)
SHEEN VISIBLE ON WATER: YES
  -> If YES: reportable under CWA sheen test (40 CFR 110.3), regardless of volume.
  -> If NO (fuel fully contained on deck/dock, no water contact): not a federal
     reportable event; log as a contained spill, dispose of absorbent per facility
     hazmat procedure.
ACTION TAKEN:
  [x] Stopped fuel transfer immediately
  [x] Deployed absorbent boom/pads around visible sheen
  [x] Called National Response Center: 1-800-424-8802
  [ ] Notified marina management / harbormaster
TIME OF DISCOVERY: 1421
TIME OF NRC CALL: 1424
NRC REPORT NUMBER: [assigned on call]
SHEEN STILL VISIBLE AT: 1435 -> NO (dissipated/contained)
```

**Rule that never bends:** the "estimated volume" line never changes the YES/NO reporting decision. A visible sheen is reportable at any volume; the volume only matters for the incident narrative, not for whether the call gets made.

## 3. Bilge-blower pre-start timing

| Step | Action | Duration | Confirmed by |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Fueling complete, nozzle stowed, fill cap secured | — | Visual check |
| 2 | Blower switch engaged at helm | 0:00 start | Blower indicator light/sound |
| 3 | Minimum blower run before ignition | 4 min | Placard-stated minimum or manufacturer's manual |
| 4 | Sniff test at engine compartment hatch (if accessible) | at 4 min | No fuel odor detected |
| 5 | Engine start authorized | after step 3 and 4 clear | Attendant/owner confirmation |

**Rule that never bends:** step 5 does not fire on "it's probably fine" after a shorter wait. If the boat's own placard specifies a longer minimum than 4 minutes, the placard figure governs, not this table's default.

## 4. Automotive full-service check (fluid and tire sequence)

1. **Oil:** pull dipstick, wipe, reinsert fully, pull again — read against the full/add range printed on the stick, not a guess at "looks about right." Note color (milky = possible coolant intrusion; metallic flecks = possible bearing wear) before topping off; flag either to the customer rather than silently adding oil and moving on.
2. **Tire pressure:** check against the vehicle's door-jamb or glovebox placard PSI, never the number molded into the tire's sidewall (that number is the tire's maximum rating, not the vehicle's recommended pressure). Example: placard calls for 32 PSI front / 30 PSI rear; a reading of 24 PSI on one front tire only (not both) is an asymmetric drop worth flagging as a possible slow leak, not just a routine top-off.
3. **Washer fluid and visible fluid leaks:** top off washer fluid; scan under the vehicle at the pump island for any fresh drip spot before the customer pulls away — a spot under the engine bay is a different flag (oil/coolant) than one under the passenger cabin (A/C condensate, which is normal).
4. **Log any declined or flagged service** (customer declines a recommended tire inflation, oil noted but not changed) so the transaction record matches what was actually found versus actually done.
