# Vocabulary

### Core Competences (of Librarianship)
ALA's 2009 document enumerating the knowledge areas every graduate of an accredited program should hold, organized into numbered categories (foundations, organization of information, technology, reference, research, administration, and more).
**In use:** "Row 9, administration and management, has no required-course mapping — that's a self-study gap, not just a curriculum preference."
**Common misuse:** Treating it as interchangeable with a generic "learning outcomes" list rather than the specific numbered ALA document a COA reviewer checks against.

### Self-study
The evidence-based document a program submits to COA ahead of a comprehensive review, addressing each accreditation standard directly with data.
**In use:** "The self-study needs the disaggregated section data attached, not just the recommendation — reviewers can't verify a claim they can't see."
**Common misuse:** Drafting it as a promotional narrative about the program rather than a standard-by-standard evidence package.

### Comprehensive review / site visit
COA's roughly seven-year accreditation review cycle, including an on-site visit by a review team; an interim/mid-cycle report typically falls around year three or four.
**In use:** "We're two years out from comprehensive review — pilot the fix quietly this term before it needs to go in front of the full curriculum committee."
**Common misuse:** Treating every accreditation-related fix as equally urgent regardless of where the program sits in the seven-year cycle.

### iSchool
The informal branding many LIS programs adopted as members of the iSchools consortium, signaling a stated shift toward "information" broadly (data, UX, informatics) rather than libraries specifically.
**In use:** "Becoming an iSchool wasn't just a sign change — it added two required data-science credits and changed who we recruit."
**Common misuse:** Assuming the name change is cosmetic rather than a real curricular and hiring commitment documented in the program's mission statement.

### Practicum
A supervised field placement with documented learning objectives and instructor check-ins, distinct from an informal internship.
**In use:** "That site hasn't had a documented supervisor check-in in three terms — that's a practicum-oversight gap the accreditation standard actually asks about."
**Common misuse:** Using "practicum" and "internship" interchangeably in accreditation documentation, when the standard requires specific, dated supervision evidence the informal term doesn't imply.

### RDA (Resource Description and Access)
The content standard governing what descriptive data goes into a bibliographic record (replaced AACR2 in 2013); a *rule set*, not a data format.
**In use:** "The capstone has them build an RDA-compliant record set, then crosswalk it to BIBFRAME."
**Common misuse:** Conflating RDA (the content rules) with MARC (the encoding format the rules get expressed in) — they answer different questions and a syllabus can update one without the other.

### BIBFRAME
The Library of Congress's linked-data initiative intended to eventually succeed MARC as the encoding model for bibliographic data; still mid-transition, not yet the operational default in most integrated library systems.
**In use:** "We added a BIBFRAME sandbox exercise this term so students see where cataloging is headed, not just where it's been."
**Common misuse:** Assuming BIBFRAME is already in production use everywhere, when most library systems still run on MARC and BIBFRAME adoption is uneven.

### Tenure clock / probationary period
The fixed multi-year window (typically six years) before a mandatory up-or-out tenure decision, during which research, teaching, and service accumulate toward the dossier.
**In use:** "That committee role only counts if the chair confirms it in writing — don't assume it's tenure credit just because it's departmental service."
**Common misuse:** Assuming any form of service to the department or to ALA automatically counts equally toward tenure, when weighting varies by institution and must be explicitly confirmed.

### Application scholarship
Boyer's (1990) category for scholarship that applies existing knowledge to a real professional problem (a workflow redesign, a usability study), distinct from "discovery" scholarship's traditional empirical research contribution.
**In use:** "This UX study is application scholarship — it needs a methods-and-contribution framing in the dossier or the committee won't read it as research at all."
**Common misuse:** Assuming applied/practice work is automatically weighted the same as discovery research by a general P&T committee without any translation of its contribution.

### Adjunct / practitioner faculty
Part-time instructors, often working librarians, who deliver a large share — commonly the majority — of course sections in most LIS programs.
**In use:** "We can't count adjunct sections toward the faculty-sufficiency evidence — COA's Standard III is specifically about the full-time core."
**Common misuse:** Treating adjunct headcount as an interchangeable substitute for full-time faculty in accreditation faculty-sufficiency arguments.

### Learning outcomes mapping / curriculum map
The documented linkage between a stated program outcome (or Core Competence) and the specific course and assessed artifact that delivers it.
**In use:** "That outcome statement doesn't map to any graded artifact — it's aspirational language, not evidence."
**Common misuse:** Writing generic outcome statements that sound complete but don't verifiably tie to any specific assessed work a reviewer could inspect.

### Information literacy
A defined competency (per ACRL's Framework for Information Literacy for Higher Education) concerning evaluating, using, and creating information ethically within its social context — a specific named framework, not a synonym for general tech skill.
**In use:** "The information-literacy session is built around the ACRL Framework's frames, not a generic 'how to use a database' walkthrough."
**Common misuse:** Using "information literacy" and "digital literacy" interchangeably, collapsing a specific pedagogical framework into a vague skills label.

### Capstone (assessment)
A culminating assessment explicitly aligned to a competency rubric, used as accreditation-grade evidence of student learning — not simply the largest assignment in a course.
**In use:** "The midterm quiz isn't a capstone — swap it for the rubric-anchored record-building project before the next self-study cites this course as evidence."
**Common misuse:** Calling any big final project a "capstone" regardless of whether it's rubric-aligned to a stated competency the way accreditation evidence requires.

### ALISE (Association for Library and Information Science Education)
The research and education association for LIS faculty and programs, publisher of the annual ALISE Statistical Report — distinct from ALA, the practitioner/professional association.
**In use:** "Check the ALISE Statistical Report before assuming our faculty ratio is unusual — that's the peer-comparison data, not ALA's practitioner numbers."
**Common misuse:** Conflating ALISE (faculty/program research body) with ALA (the broader professional association, including the accrediting COA), which serve different audiences and publish different data.
