# Environmental Economist — Red Flags

### A contingent valuation survey where WTP doesn't rise with the quantity of the good (scope insensitivity)
- **Usually means:** respondents are answering "I support environmental protection in general" rather than pricing the specific good described — a signature failure of CV design, not a valid low estimate.
- **First question:** "Did you run a split-sample scope test — did a group asked about a larger version of the good state a materially higher WTP?"
- **Data to pull:** the scope-test sub-sample results; if none was run, the survey instrument itself to check whether the good's boundaries were described precisely enough to price.

### A hedonic price model on housing near an environmental hazard with no school-district or crime control
- **Usually means:** the estimated price discount attributed to the hazard is partly picking up an omitted variable that also correlates with proximity to industrial or low-amenity areas.
- **First question:** "What controls are in the model besides distance to the hazard — school quality, crime, housing age, other amenities?"
- **Data to pull:** the full regression specification and a robustness check with an alternate control set or a spatial fixed-effects model.

### A benefit-transfer value applied without an income or population adjustment
- **Usually means:** a WTP estimate from a study site with a different income level or affected population is being applied at face value, which typically misstates magnitude in either direction.
- **First question:** "How does the study site's median income and the affected population compare to the policy site — and was the value adjusted for the difference?"
- **Data to pull:** the original study's site characteristics and income level versus the current policy site's, and the adjustment factor (if any) applied.

### A regulatory impact analysis where one benefit category exceeds ~90% of total quantified benefit
- **Usually means:** the analysis's conclusion rests almost entirely on one contested input (commonly VSL-based avoided mortality), which is the input most likely to be challenged in review or litigation.
- **First question:** "What does the benefit-cost ratio look like with that category excluded or reported separately?"
- **Data to pull:** the benefit breakdown by category, and a version of the bottom line computed both with and without the dominant category.

### A net-positive aggregate benefit-cost result with no distributional or environmental-justice breakdown
- **Usually means:** the aggregate masks a specific community bearing net harm even where the national or regional total is positive.
- **First question:** "Who is nearest the source of the residual exposure or bears the compliance cost, and does that population differ from who receives the benefit?"
- **Data to pull:** exposure or benefit incidence by census tract or comparable geography, cross-referenced against demographic and income data for the affected area.

### A discount-rate choice that appears only after the analyst knows which rate produces the desired sign
- **Usually means:** the rate was selected to support a predetermined conclusion rather than reported per the governing standard.
- **First question:** "What does the result look like at both OMB Circular A-4 rates (3% and 7%), and were both computed before or after the recommendation was drafted?"
- **Data to pull:** the net-present-value table at both rates, with timestamps or draft history if available.

### A travel cost estimate for a site that's part of a multi-stop trip, with no apportionment
- **Usually means:** the model is attributing the full trip cost to one site, overstating that site's use value.
- **First question:** "Was this a single-purpose trip, and if not, how was cost apportioned across the stops?"
- **Data to pull:** the survey's trip-purpose and itinerary data; if multi-stop trips exceed roughly 20-30% of the sample, an apportionment or on-site multi-purpose correction should be applied.

### A natural resource damage claim with no habitat- or resource-equivalency baseline
- **Usually means:** the claim is valuing the injury without establishing what "made whole" (equivalent replacement scale and timing) actually requires, which is the standard CERCLA/OPA framework courts expect.
- **First question:** "What's the equivalency analysis — how many acre-years or service-years of restoration offset the injury, and over what recovery timeline?"
- **Data to pull:** the Habitat Equivalency Analysis or Resource Equivalency Analysis worksheet, including the assumed baseline (uninjured) condition and recovery trajectory.

### A value-of-statistical-life figure applied without a stated vintage or income-adjustment method
- **Usually means:** a VSL number is being reused from an older study or a different agency's guidance without updating for income growth, understating current benefits.
- **First question:** "What year and income basis is this VSL from, and has it been updated using the agency's current income-elasticity adjustment?"
- **Data to pull:** the VSL figure's source document and vintage, and the adjustment method (if any) applied to bring it current.
