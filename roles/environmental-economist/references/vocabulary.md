# Environmental Economist — Vocabulary

### Willingness-to-pay (WTP) vs. willingness-to-accept (WTA)
WTP is the maximum a household would pay to gain an environmental improvement; WTA is the minimum it would accept to permit an equivalent loss. Both measure the same good's value but from opposite reference points.
**In use:** "WTA for the wetland's loss came in at 4x the WTP for an equivalent gain — that gap is expected, not a modeling error."
**Common misuse:** treating WTP and WTA as interchangeable or averaging them, when the divergence (loss aversion, and WTP being capped by budget while WTA isn't) is itself informative and the two should be reported separately with the reason for the choice stated.

### Contingent valuation (CV)
A stated-preference survey method that asks respondents directly what they would pay for (or accept to forgo) a described environmental good, used when no market proxy exists.
**In use:** "We ran a CV survey with a dichotomous-choice format to value the endangered species' continued existence."
**Common misuse:** treating any survey that asks about environmental attitudes as a valid CV instrument — a defensible CV design specifies the good precisely, includes a payment vehicle, and tests for scope sensitivity.

### Hedonic pricing
A revealed-preference method that infers the implicit price of a non-market attribute (air quality, hazard proximity) from its effect on the price of a good that is traded, typically housing.
**In use:** "The hedonic model puts the price discount for Superfund-site proximity at roughly 8% of home value within a half-mile."
**Common misuse:** interpreting the coefficient as the full value of the attribute without checking for omitted-variable bias from correlated amenities the model didn't control for.

### Travel cost method
A revealed-preference method that infers a recreation site's use value from the cost visitors incur to travel there, using the cost-quantity relationship as a demand curve.
**In use:** "Travel cost puts consumer surplus at $42 per visitor-day for the lake access site."
**Common misuse:** applying it to multi-purpose trips without apportioning cost across stops, which inflates the estimated value of any single site on the itinerary.

### Benefit transfer
Applying a value estimated at one study site to a different policy site, used when time or budget doesn't allow primary valuation research.
**In use:** "We benefit-transferred the wetland value from the Chesapeake study, income-adjusted to the policy site's median household income."
**Common misuse:** transferring a value without checking that the study site and policy site match on ecosystem type, baseline condition, and population income — an unadjusted transfer is a placeholder, not a substitute estimate.

### Social cost of carbon (SCC)
The estimated economic damage caused by emitting one additional ton of CO2, used to monetize the climate benefit of an emissions-reducing policy.
**In use:** "At the current interagency SCC estimate, the rule's emissions reduction is worth roughly $51 per ton avoided."
**Common misuse:** treating the SCC as a single settled number rather than a discount-rate-dependent estimate — the figure varies several-fold depending on the discount rate and damage-function assumptions used to derive it, and that range should be disclosed.

### Discount rate (in regulatory analysis)
The rate used to convert future costs and benefits into present-value terms, reflecting both time preference and the opportunity cost of capital.
**In use:** "OMB guidance requires reporting net present value at both 3% and 7% — we lead with 3% but disclose both."
**Common misuse:** presenting a single discount rate as objectively correct rather than as a value-laden parameter that, for long-horizon policies, can determine the sign of the result on its own.

### Concentration-response function
A statistical relationship estimating the change in a health outcome (mortality, morbidity) per unit change in pollutant concentration, used to translate an emissions reduction into avoided health incidents.
**In use:** "The concentration-response function estimates 12 avoided deaths per year for the modeled 500-ton PM2.5 reduction."
**Common misuse:** applying a function estimated in one region or pollutant mix to a different population without checking whether baseline exposure and demographic composition are comparable.

### Value of a statistical life (VSL)
The dollar value society implicitly places on a small reduction in mortality risk, derived from studies of what people accept in extra wages or pay in extra costs for small risk changes — not the value of any identified individual's life.
**In use:** "At the agency's current VSL of $9.6M, the avoided-mortality benefit alone is $115M a year."
**Common misuse:** describing VSL as "what a life is worth," which invites both public misunderstanding and a false precision about a figure that is itself a statistical aggregate from risk-wage tradeoff studies.

### Habitat Equivalency Analysis (HEA)
A natural resource damage assessment method that scales required restoration to offset an injury by equating lost ecological services over time (from injury to recovery) with gained services from restoration, rather than assigning a dollar value to the resource itself.
**In use:** "HEA puts the required restoration at 40 acre-years of equivalent marsh habitat to offset the spill's injury."
**Common misuse:** skipping the discounting of both lost and gained service-years to a common present-value basis, which biases the equivalency scale.

### Environmental justice / distributional incidence
The analysis of who bears the cost (compliance, residual exposure) and who receives the benefit of an environmental policy, disaggregated by geography, income, or demographic group.
**In use:** "The distributional incidence check shows the nearest census tract bears disproportionate residual exposure despite receiving a majority of the modeled benefit."
**Common misuse:** treating a net-positive aggregate result as sufficient on its own, without checking whether the benefit and the burden fall on the same population.

### Existence value / non-use value
The value a person places on an environmental good's continued existence independent of any personal use of it (e.g., valuing an old-growth forest one will never visit).
**In use:** "Most of the WTP for the endangered species is non-use value — few respondents expected to ever see the animal in the wild."
**Common misuse:** assuming only use value is "real" and discounting or excluding non-use value from an analysis, when courts and agency guidance both recognize it as a legitimate benefit category.

### Pigouvian tax
A tax set equal to the marginal external cost of an activity, intended to make the private cost of the activity match its full social cost.
**In use:** "A Pigouvian tax on the emissions would internalize the externality at roughly the SCC per ton."
**Common misuse:** assuming any environmental tax is Pigouvian by design — a tax set for revenue or political feasibility rather than calibrated to marginal external cost doesn't achieve the efficiency result the term implies.
