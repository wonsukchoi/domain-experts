# Vocabulary

Terms generalists conflate or use loosely — practitioners keep them sharply separate because the distinction changes what gets triaged first.

### Bussing vs. pre-bussing

**Bussing** is the full clear-and-reset after a party leaves and the check is closed. **Pre-bussing** is clearing finished plates and glasses *while the party is still seated*, mid-meal, to keep the table usable and reduce the load at full bus time.

*In use:* "Pre-buss table 8's apps now — full bus can wait till they close out."

**Common misuse:** treating pre-bussing as optional tidying rather than a load-management tool; skipping it lets clutter accumulate and turns a fast full bus into a slow one.

### Turn time vs. dead time

**Turn time** is the total interval from one party being seated to the next party being seated at the same table — dining duration plus dead time. **Dead time** is only the portion between check-closed and next-seated: the part this role actually controls.

*In use:* "Turn time's fine, it's the dead time that's blown out — we're not slow to serve, we're slow to reset."

**Common misuse:** reporting "turn time is up" as if it's one number to fix, when the actionable half (dead time) and the uncontrollable half (dining duration) need different responses entirely.

### Reset (full vs. quick)

A **full reset** replaces linens (if used), rolls fresh silverware, and rebuilds the complete place setting — used for larger parties or visibly soiled linens. A **quick reset** wipes and re-lays existing settings without a linen change — appropriate for a fast 2-top turn where nothing was soiled.

*In use:* "Quick reset on 4, they were only there twenty minutes and nothing spilled."

**Common misuse:** defaulting to a full reset out of habit or "thoroughness," which roughly doubles reset time on tables that didn't need it.

### Par level vs. restock trigger

**Par level** is the target quantity a station should hold at the start of a shift or after a restock. **Restock trigger** is the point during service at which restocking should actually begin — always set *before* the item hits zero, commonly at the second-to-last unit or roughly half of par.

*In use:* "Par on limes is six dozen, but the trigger's at three — that's when we start cutting more, not when the tray's empty."

**Common misuse:** treating par as the point to restock (waiting until the bin is visibly empty), which guarantees a stockout call mid-rush since restocking itself takes time.

### 86'd

Means an item is completely out and unavailable to sell or use — originally kitchen slang, used at the bar for garnish, mixers, or specific bottles as well.

*In use:* "We're 86'd on limes till the next cut's ready, sub lemon on anything that calls for it."

**Common misuse:** using "86" loosely for "running low" — it specifically means zero stock, not approaching the restock trigger; conflating the two blurs the urgency signal.

### Camping

A party that lingers well past the meal's natural end — check paid or not — occupying a table the operation is counting on for its next turn.

*In use:* "Table 14's camping, twenty minutes past close-out with no check requested — flag it to the server, not a bussing problem yet."

**Common misuse:** treating a camping table as a bussing failure; it's a server/host issue (the party hasn't been prompted or the check hasn't been dropped), and an attendant hovering to bus it early reads as rushing the guest.

### Side-work

The recurring, scheduled maintenance tasks (stocking, sanitizing, restocking, prepping) assigned by shift phase — opening, running, closing — distinct from the reactive, moment-to-moment bussing and running work.

*In use:* "Running side-work's just the half-par bar restocks tonight, we're not touching linens till close."

**Common misuse:** treating side-work as a flexible to-do list that can be skipped wholesale when busy, rather than a triage-ordered set where safety/stock items are never cut and only cosmetic items flex.

### Sanitizer concentration (ppm)

The measured strength of a chemical sanitizing solution — chlorine, quaternary ammonium ("quat"), and iodine each have a different effective range (roughly 50–100 ppm for chlorine, 200–400 ppm for quat, 12.5–25 ppm for iodine) and are not interchangeable at the same dilution.

*In use:* "Bucket's reading 150 on the quat strip, that's under range — dump it and remake, don't just add more sanitizer to the same water."

**Common misuse:** assuming "more concentrated is safer"; over-concentration is also out of the approved range and can leave chemical residue, while under-concentration simply fails to sanitize while looking identical to a correct bucket.

### Well vs. speed rail vs. backup

The **well** is the bartender's working ice bin/mixer station built into the bar. The **speed rail** is the mounted row of house-pour spirit bottles within arm's reach. **Backup** is the reserve stock (extra bottles, kegs, garnish) held nearby but not in active use, restocked into the well/rail as it depletes.

*In use:* "Well's fine, but we're down to the last backup of the well vodka — get another from the cage before we're actually out."

**Common misuse:** using "backup" and "well" interchangeably when reporting a shortage — "we're out of vodka" could mean the bottle in the rail is empty (routine, swap from backup) or the backup is also gone (an actual stockout requiring an emergency restock).

### Dining duration vs. attendant-controlled dead time

**Dining duration** is how long a seated party actually takes to eat — driven by party size, course count, and pace, largely outside this role's control. **Dead time** is the bus/reset/reseat interval this role and the host stand directly control.

*In use:* "Dine time's up because it's a big-party night, that's not on us — dead time's what we're accountable for."

**Common misuse:** blaming or crediting bussing performance for a turn-time change that's actually a dining-duration shift (bigger parties, more courses ordered) rather than checking the POS timestamps to see which half actually moved.

### Cross-contamination (as it applies to this role specifically)

The transfer of pathogens or allergens between surfaces via a shared tool — for this role, concentrated in bus tubs, wiping cloths, and ice scoops that touch multiple tables or drinks in sequence, as opposed to a single dish or order.

*In use:* "Don't use the same rag on the raw bar surface and then table 6 — grab a fresh one from the bucket."

**Common misuse:** treating cross-contamination as purely a kitchen/food-prep concern; because this role's tools move across every table and the entire bar, a single lapse here has a wider blast radius than a single server or cook error.

### Table-ready signal

The explicit handoff (POS flag, host-stand light, verbal tag) marking a table as clean, reset, and available to seat — distinct from the table simply being physically clean.

*In use:* "Table's clean but don't seat it yet, I haven't flagged it ready — still checking the wobble on that chair."

**Common misuse:** assuming a visibly clean table is automatically available; without the explicit signal, the host has no reliable way to know it's ready, which is exactly the gap that produces "available soon" tables that are actually sitting idle.
