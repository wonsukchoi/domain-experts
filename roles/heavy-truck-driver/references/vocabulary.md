# Heavy Truck Driver — Vocabulary

### 14-hour window
The consecutive on-duty period, starting at the first duty-status change after a qualifying 10-hour break, inside which all driving must occur — it does not pause for breaks, loading, or fueling.
**In use:** "My window closes at seven, but that doesn't mean I've got driving hours until seven — check the 11-hour clock separately."
**Common misuse:** Treating the hours remaining in the window as equal to hours available to drive, when non-driving on-duty time earlier in the day already consumed window time without touching the driving clock.

### 11-hour driving limit
The maximum cumulative driving time allowed within a 14-hour window, after a qualifying 10 consecutive hours off duty.
**In use:** "I've only got 3.25 hours left on the driving clock — doesn't matter that the window's got six and a half."
**Common misuse:** Assuming this clock and the 14-hour window always run out at the same time; a day with long detention or loading time exhausts the window first without touching the driving clock, and a day of back-to-back driving exhausts the driving clock first.

### 30-minute break
The mandatory 30 consecutive minutes of non-driving time (off duty, sleeper berth, or other non-driving on-duty status) required before accumulating more than 8 cumulative hours of driving since the last qualifying break.
**In use:** "That fuel stop was twelve minutes — doesn't count, still owe the full break before the eighth hour."
**Common misuse:** Counting any stop, or a stop under 30 consecutive minutes, as satisfying the requirement.

### 34-hour restart
A voluntary 34 consecutive hours off duty that resets the 60-hour/7-day or 70-hour/8-day cumulative on-duty clock back to zero.
**In use:** "I'm at 66 of 70 — taking the restart tonight instead of squeezing one more short run in."
**Common misuse:** Using the restart reactively only after hitting the cumulative limit mid-route, instead of scheduling it proactively when the clock is still a few hours from the wall.

### Personal conveyance (PC)
An ELD duty-status code for movement of the truck for personal reasons while off dispatch, which does not count against driving-time limits.
**In use:** "Driving to the truck stop diner two miles up is PC — driving toward tomorrow's pickup point is not."
**Common misuse:** Logging miles that actually advance the driver toward the next dispatched load as personal conveyance to manufacture extra legal driving time.

### Yard move
An ELD duty-status code for movement within a facility's yard (not on public roads toward a destination), also excluded from driving-time limits.
**In use:** "Spotting the trailer in the yard is a yard move — once I hit the public road with it, that's driving time."
**Common misuse:** Extending the yard-move classification to movement that has already left the facility premises.

### Working load limit (WLL)
The maximum load a piece of tiedown hardware (chain, strap, binder) is rated to carry in securement service — distinct from and always lower than its breaking strength.
**In use:** "That chain's WLL tag says 5,400 pounds — that's the number that goes into the securement math, not what it'll break at."
**Common misuse:** Using breaking strength or an unrated visual estimate of "thick enough" instead of the hardware's actual WLL rating.

### Aggregate working load limit
The sum of the WLL ratings of all tiedowns used on an article, which federal rule requires to be at least half the article's weight.
**In use:** "Four chains at 5,400 each gets us 21,600 aggregate — that clears the 21,000 we need on a 42,000-pound coil."
**Common misuse:** Checking tiedown count against a habit or a customer's informal expectation instead of calculating aggregate WLL against actual cargo weight.

### Out-of-service (OOS)
A specific, published defect from the CVSA North American Standard Out-of-Service Criteria that legally bars a vehicle from being driven until repaired.
**In use:** "That's not a note-it-and-go item — it's OOS, the truck doesn't leave the yard until it's fixed."
**Common misuse:** Using "out of service" loosely to mean "needs repair soon" rather than "cannot legally be driven right now."

### ELD malfunction
A device-detected condition (power, timing, positioning, engine-synchronization, data-recording, or data-transfer compliance failure) under which the device can no longer reliably certify accurate records, distinct from a routine data diagnostic event.
**In use:** "That's a malfunction code, not just a diagnostic event — switching to paper logs for the rest of the shift."
**Common misuse:** Treating any flagged code as minor and continuing to log electronically without checking whether it's specifically classified as a malfunction requiring paper backup.

### Bridge formula
The federal calculation (based on axle spacing, not just total weight) that determines the legal maximum weight a given axle group can carry — a configuration can be illegal well under 80,000 lb gross, or legal at 80,000 lb, depending on axle spacing.
**In use:** "We're under 80,000 total, but the tandem spacing puts us over on the bridge formula — need to slide the axles back."
**Common misuse:** Treating 80,000 lb gross combination weight as the only weight limit that matters, ignoring axle-group spacing.

### Detention time
Time held at a shipper or receiver beyond the scheduled appointment or a reasonable loading/unloading window, typically compensable under carrier or broker detention pay policy after a threshold (commonly around 2 hours).
**In use:** "Gate-in was 9:05, didn't get loaded until 11:40 — that's over two hours of detention, logging it exactly for the claim."
**Common misuse:** Estimating detention time at day's end from memory instead of recording exact timestamps as they happen, which weakens or voids the pay claim.

### Short-haul exception
A provision allowing CDL drivers operating within a 150 air-mile radius of their work-reporting location to use time records instead of full ELD logs, while the 11-hour driving and 30-minute break rules still apply in full.
**In use:** "We're inside the 150 air-mile radius today, so it's a time record, not a full log — but I still owe my break at eight hours."
**Common misuse:** Assuming the exception removes HOS limits entirely rather than just the detailed-logging requirement.

### Adverse driving conditions exception
A provision extending the 11-hour driving limit and 14-hour window by up to 2 hours when unexpected conditions (weather, an accident-caused traffic backup) that weren't known at dispatch time slow the trip.
**In use:** "Logging the adverse-conditions extension now, at the point the fog hit — not after I've already run two hours long."
**Common misuse:** Invoking the exception retroactively to justify hours already driven, rather than noting it contemporaneously as the qualifying condition arises.

### PSP (Pre-Employment Screening Program)
An FMCSA-administered record of a commercial driver's crash and inspection/violation history, used by carriers in hiring decisions.
**In use:** "Two prior roadside HOS violations show up on the PSP report — that's a pattern, not a one-off."
**Common misuse:** Treating PSP as a carrier-level record like CSA BASIC scores, when it's specifically tied to the individual driver across employers.
