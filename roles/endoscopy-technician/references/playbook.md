# Endoscopy Reprocessing and Room Playbook

Operational defaults with concrete steps and thresholds. Facility infection-prevention policy and the specific scope model's manufacturer instructions for use (IFU) govern in any conflict — these are the technician-level defaults absent a documented override.

## 1. Point-of-use (POU) treatment and transport

The clock starts the moment the scope comes off the patient, not when the room is cleared.

1. Wipe the exterior and flush all channels with the manufacturer's enzymatic POU solution immediately at bedside — this step is not optional and not deferrable to the reprocessing room.
2. Log the POU completion time on the scope's traceability record. This timestamp is the reference point for every downstream delay decision.
3. Transport in a closed, leak-proof container to the reprocessing area.
4. **Check elapsed time against the manufacturer's validated window** (commonly 60 minutes; confirm the specific scope model's IFU — some manufacturers validate longer or shorter windows):
   - **Within window:** proceed to standard manual cleaning.
   - **Beyond window:** switch to the manufacturer's delayed-reprocessing procedure (typically an extended enzymatic soak before manual cleaning resumes) and note "delayed reprocessing protocol used" on the log — do not run the standard cycle and call it equivalent.

## 2. Manual cleaning and leak test sequence

1. Perform manual cleaning per IFU: full disassembly of removable parts, brushing of all accessible channels (including the elevator channel on duodenoscopes) until no visible debris returns on the brush, and flushing with the validated detergent/water volume for that scope model.
2. **Leak test before immersion in any disinfectant, every time, no exceptions:**
   - Attach the leak tester (manual or automated) per the scope's IFU pressure setting.
   - Automated tester: verify daily pressure calibration before first use of the day; run the test and read the result on the printout, not by ear.
   - Manual tester: inspect tubing and bulb for damage before each use; watch for bubbles in a controlled water bath for the full manufacturer-specified observation time (don't cut the wait short because the tester "looks fine").
   - **Pass:** proceed to immersion cleaning and high-level disinfection (HLD) or sterilization.
   - **Fail (bubbles, pressure drop, or a "fail" readout):** remove the scope from the workflow immediately, before it touches any disinfectant basin. Tag it for repair, document the failure (time, symptom, tester reading) on the scope's maintenance record, and do not attempt a "recheck and maybe it passes this time" without the repair vendor's sign-off first.

## 3. High-level disinfection / sterilization and MEC verification

1. Confirm the disinfectant/sterilant basin's activation date and reuse-life expiration (commonly 14 days for OPA-based products, but check the specific product label — reuse life is a labeled claim, not a universal number).
2. **Test MEC before every load, not just at shift start:**
   - Dip the test strip per manufacturer instructions, read at the specified time.
   - **Pass:** proceed with immersion for the full validated contact time and temperature.
   - **Fail or borderline:** pull the basin from service immediately. Trace every load run since the *last passing test* (not just the current load) using the AER cycle log or manual log. For each scope in that window: if still in unused storage, re-reprocess on a fresh, MEC-verified basin before release; if already checked out and used, flag to infection prevention for a look-back risk assessment — this decision is theirs, not a call the tech makes solo.
3. **AER cycle interruption:** if a cycle aborts partway (power loss, door seal fault, chemical delivery error), restart the full cycle from the beginning on that scope. A partial cycle has no validated disinfection-assurance data behind it, regardless of how far it got.
4. Log cycle parameters (time, temperature/concentration, cycle number), scope ID, and technician ID for every completed cycle — this is the record a look-back or audit will actually use.

## 4. Drying and storage

1. Dry all channels with pressure-regulated forced instrument air (or HEPA-filtered air at minimum) for a minimum of 10 minutes per channel before storage.
2. Hang the scope straight, uncoiled, in a ventilated storage cabinet — coiling or storing in a closed case traps residual moisture.
3. Track hang-time against the facility's validated storage-life limit (commonly 7–14 days per AAMI ST91-informed policy). If a scope has exceeded that limit unused, reprocess before the next use rather than relying on visual inspection alone.

## 5. Borescope inspection program

Run on a defined schedule (many programs start at monthly or quarterly per-scope, then adjust by findings rate) — not only when a scope "seems" damaged.

1. Insert the borescope through each working channel per the device's instructions, recording video/images for the log.
2. **Classify findings by severity, not by presence:**
   - Minor surface scratching with no debris retained after a repeat clean and no channel narrowing: log the finding, continue in rotation, flag for the next scheduled repair-vendor review.
   - Buckling, perforation, adhesive/lining breakdown, or debris/discoloration that persists after a repeat manual clean: remove from service immediately and route to the repair vendor — do not return to rotation pending a "we'll watch it" plan.
3. Track findings per scope over time; a scope with recurring findings across multiple inspections (not just one-off) is a candidate for retirement, not repeated repair.

## 6. Room setup and case turnover

1. Pull accessories against the case type: biopsy forceps, snares, injection needles, retrieval devices — default to single-use disposables for tissue-contact instruments unless the facility's reusable-instrument reprocessing protocol specifically validates that device.
2. Set up and function-check insufflator (CO2 supply and pressure), light source/processor, suction, and monitors before the patient enters — not during setup with the patient already positioned.
3. During the procedure, monitor and adjust equipment continuously; hand off accessories on cue and log any equipment substitution made mid-case.
4. At case end, begin POU treatment immediately (Section 1) while the next room's setup runs in parallel — turnover time is the reprocessing cycle time plus dry time, not an aspirational number.

## 7. Specimen handling for multi-site GI cases

GI procedures frequently generate several specimens per case from different anatomic locations (e.g., a colonoscopy with biopsies from ascending colon, transverse colon, and rectum).

1. Each specimen gets its own labeled container the moment it's off the scope — never a shared container for "we'll sort it out after."
2. Label reads back to the physician/RN for confirmation: patient identifier + anatomic site + specimen number, matched against the procedure note's numbering.
3. Chain of custody logged: collection time, site, container number, and time to pathology — deferring this "until things slow down" is exactly where a site-to-container mismatch happens.
