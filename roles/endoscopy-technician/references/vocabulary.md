# Vocabulary

Working vocabulary that generalists (and some new hires) reliably misuse. Format per term: definition, how a practitioner actually uses it, the common misuse.

### Minimum effective concentration (MEC)

- **Definition:** the labeled concentration threshold a reusable high-level disinfectant or sterilant must remain at or above to still be effective; tested with a chemical indicator strip before each use, not just at activation.
- **Practitioner usage:** "Strip read below MEC at 13:40 — every load since the 06:15 pass is unverified, not just this one."
- **Common misuse:** treating a failed MEC test as only invalidating the current load, when it actually calls into question every cycle run since the last passing test, since concentration degrades continuously rather than dropping at a single detectable instant.

### Point-of-use (POU) treatment

- **Definition:** the immediate bedside wipe-down and channel flush performed the moment a scope comes off the patient, before transport to the reprocessing area.
- **Practitioner usage:** "POU's logged at 10:02 — that's the clock everything else measures against."
- **Common misuse:** treating POU as a courtesy pre-clean that can wait until the room is cleared; the delay window that determines whether standard or delayed reprocessing applies starts at this timestamp, not at transport.

### High-level disinfection (HLD) vs. sterilization

- **Definition:** HLD kills all microorganisms except large numbers of bacterial spores; sterilization kills everything including spores. Most flexible GI endoscopes are HLD-processed; duodenoscopes and some specialty scopes increasingly move to sterilization or single-use design per updated guidance.
- **Practitioner usage:** "This is an HLD scope, not a sterilized one — that's why the reuse-life tracking on the basin matters so much."
- **Common misuse:** using "sterile" loosely to describe any reprocessed scope; an HLD-processed scope is not sterile by definition, and describing it that way in documentation misstates its actual disinfection assurance level.

### Automated endoscope reprocessor (AER)

- **Definition:** a machine that runs a scope through a validated high-level disinfection or sterilization cycle with programmed time, temperature, and chemical parameters, after manual cleaning.
- **Practitioner usage:** "AER cycle aborted at minute 6 — full restart, not a resume."
- **Common misuse:** assuming an AER's automation removes the need for manual pre-cleaning or leak testing beforehand; the AER only processes what manual cleaning has already prepared and what leak testing has already cleared for immersion.

### Leak test

- **Definition:** a pressure-based check, performed before immersion cleaning, for a breach in the scope's internal channels or external sheath.
- **Practitioner usage:** "Leak test failed — pulling it before it goes anywhere near the basin."
- **Common misuse:** running the leak test after immersion cleaning has already started, or treating a "probably fine" quick check as equivalent to the full manufacturer-specified observation time.

### Borescope

- **Definition:** a rigid or flexible high-resolution inspection scope used to visually examine the interior of an endoscope's working channels, which are otherwise invisible to external inspection.
- **Practitioner usage:** "Borescope's showing debris in the biopsy channel after a repeat clean — that one's going to repair, not back into rotation."
- **Common misuse:** treating any borescope finding as an automatic remove-from-service event; near-universal minor scratching means the actual decision runs on severity (lumen breach, retained debris after re-clean) not on presence of any wear at all.

### Reuse life

- **Definition:** the manufacturer-labeled maximum duration (often 14 days for many OPA-based products, but label-specific) that an activated, reusable high-level disinfectant remains valid for use, independent of whether it's still passing MEC tests.
- **Practitioner usage:** "Basin's on day 12 of a 14-day reuse life — MEC's still passing, but we're tracking the expiration date too."
- **Common misuse:** assuming a passing MEC test alone extends usability past the labeled reuse-life date; the MEC test and the reuse-life clock are two separate limits, and either one hitting its threshold ends the basin's service, whichever comes first.

### Hang time / storage life

- **Definition:** the validated maximum duration a reprocessed, dried endoscope can sit in storage before it must be reprocessed again prior to use, commonly 7–14 days depending on facility policy and packaging.
- **Practitioner usage:** "That scope's been hanging 9 days — past our limit, reprocess before it goes on the cart."
- **Common misuse:** relying on a visual check ("it still looks fine") instead of the actual elapsed-time log; storage-related risk isn't visible on inspection.

### Elevator channel (duodenoscope)

- **Definition:** the mechanism at the distal tip of a duodenoscope that angles accessories (like a sphincterotome) toward the target during ERCP; a recessed component that's difficult to clean and was central to the 2013–2015 duodenoscope-associated CRE outbreaks.
- **Practitioner usage:** "Elevator channel gets a dedicated brush pass every time — it's the piece that's easiest to under-clean."
- **Common misuse:** treating the elevator channel as just another surface covered by the general channel-brushing step; its recessed geometry needs a distinct, deliberate cleaning action, not an assumption that the standard brush pass reaches it equally.

### Biofilm

- **Definition:** a matrix of microorganisms adhering to a surface (here, endoscope channel walls) that resists standard cleaning once established, forming during any delay or gap in the reprocessing chain.
- **Practitioner usage:** "That's exactly the delay window biofilm needs to start forming — we're not treating it as a standard cycle."
- **Common misuse:** assuming biofilm risk is only about total elapsed time; a single POU delay past the validated window can start biofilm formation regardless of how well the eventual reprocessing cycle runs afterward.

### Moderate sedation vs. deep sedation

- **Definition:** moderate sedation preserves purposeful response to verbal/tactile stimulation with intact airway reflexes; deep sedation means the patient is not easily aroused and may need airway support — a clinical distinction, not a dosing label.
- **Practitioner usage:** "We're targeting moderate, but if response drops off, monitoring escalates to deep-sedation protocol, not just 'a bit more sedated.'"
- **Common misuse:** treating the two as a smooth continuum requiring the same monitoring throughout; capnography's evidence-supported use is for deep sedation, not routinely mandated at moderate sedation levels.

### Traceability log

- **Definition:** the record tying a specific scope, a specific patient/case, a specific reprocessing cycle, and the technician who ran it together — the record any contamination look-back actually depends on.
- **Practitioner usage:** "Pull the traceability log for GS-114 — I need every case it's touched since the last MEC pass."
- **Common misuse:** treating the log as a compliance formality filled in after the fact from memory; if the cycle wasn't logged in real time, it's functionally unproven for look-back purposes regardless of whether it actually ran correctly.

### Single-use vs. reusable accessory

- **Definition:** biopsy forceps, snares, and similar tissue-contact devices labeled either for one use and disposal or for a validated reprocessing cycle between uses; the label, not habit or cost pressure, determines which applies.
- **Practitioner usage:** "This forceps is single-use labeled — it doesn't go through reprocessing no matter how clean it looks after one case."
- **Common misuse:** reprocessing a single-use-labeled device because it looks reusable or to save cost; the label reflects validated design and material limits, not just a suggestion.
