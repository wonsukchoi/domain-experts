# Vocabulary

### Classification (custody level)
The process of assigning an incarcerated individual a security/housing designation based on risk factors — offense severity, behavioral history, gang affiliation, mental health status — that determines where and with whom they're housed.
**In use:** "That housing assignment doesn't match his classification level — this needs a review before it's finalized, not after."
**Common misuse:** Treating classification as a one-time intake decision rather than a status that should update on defined trigger events throughout an incarceration, not just at fixed periodic reviews.

### Use of force continuum
A tiered framework specifying the range of response options — from verbal commands to physical restraint to more severe intervention — matched to the level of resistance encountered, used both to guide staff response and to evaluate incidents after the fact.
**In use:** "The report needs to show where on the continuum de-escalation was attempted before physical intervention began."
**Common misuse:** Treating the continuum as a simple escalation ladder to justify whatever force was used, rather than a standard against which each incident is independently evaluated for whether a lower tier could have resolved it.

### Restrictive housing / administrative segregation
Housing an individual separately from the general population, typically for extended periods in a single cell with limited outside contact — used for disciplinary, protective, or administrative reasons, and subject to well-documented mental health risk with extended use.
**In use:** "This placement is past its 15-day review point — that's an automatic escalation to the review board, not a routine continuation."
**Common misuse:** Using restrictive housing as a general population-management tool for convenience (overcrowding, staffing shortages) rather than the last-resort, individually justified measure policy requires.

### PREA (Prison Rape Elimination Act) compliance
The set of mandatory federal standards (in the U.S. context) governing sexual-assault prevention, detection, and response in correctional facilities — including cross-gender supervision limits, mandatory reporting timelines, and audit requirements.
**In use:** "This incident's reporting timeline is a PREA compliance question first — the investigation follows, but the clock started at disclosure."
**Common misuse:** Treating PREA compliance as satisfied by the absence of substantiated incidents, when audits specifically examine the documentation and process trail regardless of outcome.

### Deliberate indifference
The legal standard (from *Farmer v. Brennan*, 1994) governing Eighth Amendment failure-to-protect claims — liability attaches when officials knew of and disregarded an excessive risk to inmate health or safety, not merely when harm occurred.
**In use:** "This staffing gap was documented and unaddressed for three months — that's exactly the fact pattern a deliberate indifference claim is built on."
**Common misuse:** Assuming any bad outcome creates liability, when the actual standard turns on whether a known, documented risk was disregarded — which is precisely why documentation practice functions as legal risk management.

### Staffing ratio / critical minimum
The ratio of staff to incarcerated individuals on a given shift or unit; the critical minimum is the facility-specific ratio below which incident rates rise sharply rather than gradually.
**In use:** "We're not treating this as a routine schedule gap — it's below our documented critical minimum, which means it needs a risk-acceptance memo."
**Common misuse:** Using a generic industry staffing benchmark instead of the facility's own historical incident data, which can miss where that specific facility's actual inflection point sits.

### After-action review
A structured post-incident review process examining systemic contributing factors — staffing, classification status, physical plant — separate from and prior to any individual conduct review.
**In use:** "Individual conduct gets its own track — the after-action review looks at what in the system let this happen, independent of that."
**Common misuse:** Collapsing the after-action review into the individual disciplinary process, which produces blame-assignment instead of the systemic pattern-finding the review is actually for.

### Rated capacity
The population level a facility is designed and licensed to safely house, based on physical plant, staffing model, and program space — distinct from the actual current population, which can exceed it.
**In use:** "We're at 106% of rated capacity — that's not a number to normalize, it's an active risk factor requiring a mitigation plan."
**Common misuse:** Treating rated capacity as a soft target that can be routinely exceeded without consequence, rather than a safety-relevant threshold tied to the facility's actual design and staffing assumptions.

### Security threat group (STG)
A formally identified group (often gang-affiliated) within the incarcerated population whose members require specific housing and classification consideration due to known conflict patterns or organizational structure.
**In use:** "This inmate's STG affiliation changed last week — that's a mandatory reclassification trigger, not something that waits for the next scheduled review."
**Common misuse:** Relying on outdated STG affiliation data at intake without a process for updating it as affiliations shift during incarceration.

### Duty of care
The legal and ethical obligation a facility holds toward the people in its custody — covering safety, medical care, and mental health needs — that exists specifically because incarceration removes an individual's ability to provide for their own safety and care.
**In use:** "Duty of care doesn't stop at physical safety — an overdue mental health review is a duty-of-care gap too."
**Common misuse:** Framing duty of care narrowly as physical protection from other inmates, when it extends to medical, mental health, and conditions-of-confinement obligations as well.

### Classification review
The scheduled or event-triggered reassessment of an individual's custody classification, determining whether their current housing assignment still matches their risk profile.
**In use:** "That review was due at 7 days and completed at 11 — the gap is documented as a contributing factor in the incident review."
**Common misuse:** Relying solely on the fixed periodic review calendar without triggering earlier review on defined disqualifying events, which is where most classification failures actually originate.

### Direct supervision model
A facility design and staffing philosophy where officers are stationed directly within housing units among the population (rather than in separate control booths), intended to increase interaction and enable earlier de-escalation.
**In use:** "Direct supervision only works if the staffing ratio actually supports real interaction — understaffed direct supervision is worse than a well-staffed indirect model."
**Common misuse:** Assuming the direct supervision model itself guarantees better outcomes regardless of staffing level, when its safety benefit depends entirely on officers having enough capacity to actually engage rather than just being physically present.
