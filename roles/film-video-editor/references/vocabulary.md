# Film and Video Editor — Vocabulary

### Rough cut
The first full assembly of a scene or film using selected takes, before pacing or runtime has been optimized.
**In use:** "The rough cut is at 42 minutes — we haven't touched pacing yet, this is just every beat covered once."
**Common misuse:** Treating the rough cut's runtime as a meaningful signal of the final length; it isn't, since nothing has been cut for pace yet.

### Fine cut
A cut that has been through at least one pacing/structure pass and is close to final length and rhythm, but not yet locked.
**In use:** "We're in fine cut now — the acts are in the right order, we're just trimming for rhythm."
**Common misuse:** Confusing "fine cut" with "final cut" — a fine cut can still receive structural notes, a locked cut should not.

### Picture lock
The point at which the edited image sequence is frozen and handed to sound and color finishing; any change after this point is a formal reopen, not a routine edit.
**In use:** "We're locked as of Friday — anything after that goes through a change order, not a quick note."
**Common misuse:** Treating "locked" as informal or negotiable, leading to silent post-lock changes that invalidate sound and color work already in progress.

### L-cut / J-cut
An edit where the audio from one shot begins before (J-cut) or continues after (L-cut) the picture cut to the next shot, used to smooth dialogue-driven transitions.
**In use:** "Put an L-cut on her line so we hear the reply before we cut to his reaction."
**Common misuse:** Using hard cuts by default on every dialogue exchange, which produces a choppier, less naturalistic rhythm than L/J-cuts would.

### Coverage
The range of camera angles and takes shot for a given scene, providing the editor options for how to assemble it.
**In use:** "We didn't get coverage on her reaction in that take, so we can't cut to her here."
**Common misuse:** Treating more coverage as unconditionally better — excessive coverage without a selection plan increases decision paralysis without improving the final cut.

### Selects
The subset of takes an editor has chosen as usable candidates for the final cut, organized before assembly begins.
**In use:** "Pull selects from the B-camera on that scene — the A-camera take has a boom shadow."
**Common misuse:** Skipping the selects pass and cutting directly from raw dailies, which slows every subsequent revision because there's no organized shortlist to work from.

### EDL (Edit Decision List)
A text-based list of every cut point, source clip, and timecode in a sequence, used to conform an offline edit onto full-resolution media for finishing.
**In use:** "Export the EDL before you send it to color — they need it to conform against the original camera files."
**Common misuse:** Assuming a rendered video file alone is sufficient for handoff; finishing houses need the EDL/AAF/XML to relink to full-resolution source media, not just a flattened export.

### Conform
The process of relinking an offline edit's EDL/XML/AAF to full-resolution source media for finishing, or verifying that relink is frame-accurate.
**In use:** "Run a conform check before you send the XML out — we had a dropped-frame gap last time."
**Common misuse:** Treating conform as a purely mechanical, error-free step; frame-rate mismatches and gaps are common and must be checked, not assumed.

### Temp music / temp love
Placeholder music used during editing to sell a scene's rhythm before the final score exists; "temp love" is the risk of the cut becoming dependent on that placeholder's specific tempo and feel.
**In use:** "We're temp-loved on this cue — the composer needs to hear it before final scoring starts, or the new score won't fit the cut."
**Common misuse:** Assuming any music will work once the "real" score arrives, ignoring that the cut's rhythm may have been unconsciously built around the temp track's specific tempo.

### 180-degree rule (line of action)
A continuity convention keeping the camera on one side of an imaginary line between subjects across a scene, preserving consistent left/right screen direction.
**In use:** "We crossed the line in that setup — we'll need a cutaway to bridge it or the eyeline will jump."
**Common misuse:** Treating the rule as absolute rather than a continuity convention that can be deliberately broken for a disorientation effect, provided the break is intentional and bridged.

### Rule of Six (Murch)
Walter Murch's ranked priority list for cut decisions when criteria conflict: emotion, story, rhythm, eye-trace, 2D screen plane, 3D spatial continuity — in that descending order of importance.
**In use:** "The cut breaks continuity slightly there, but it serves the emotional beat — Rule of Six says emotion wins."
**Common misuse:** Applying the ranking as a rigid formula rather than a tiebreaker; context can invert the priority (a procedural documentary scene may reasonably rank story above emotion).

### Match cut
A cut that uses visual, compositional, or motion continuity between two shots to make the transition feel seamless or thematically linked.
**In use:** "Match the swing of her arm in shot A to the swing of the door in shot B — it'll read as one continuous motion."
**Common misuse:** Forcing a match cut where the underlying content doesn't actually connect, producing a transition that reads as a coincidence rather than a deliberate craft choice.

### Insert / cutaway
A brief shot — often a close-up or a reaction — cut into a scene to bridge a continuity gap, add information, or control pacing.
**In use:** "Cut to the insert of the clock before we come back — it buys us the time-jump without a hard cut."
**Common misuse:** Using cutaways reflexively to cover every editing problem, which can read as evasive rather than purposeful if overused.

### Beat
A single unit of narrative or emotional change within a scene — the smallest structural building block an editor works with when diagnosing pacing.
**In use:** "That scene has three beats but we're only landing two of them — the reversal beat is getting lost."
**Common misuse:** Confusing "beat" with "shot" — a single beat can span multiple shots, or multiple beats can occur within one continuous shot.
