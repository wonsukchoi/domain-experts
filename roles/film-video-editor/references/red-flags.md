# Film and Video Editor — Red Flags

### Rough cut runs more than 150% of the contracted final length
- **Usually means:** the shooting ratio wasn't planned for in the schedule, or scenes are being kept for coverage's sake rather than story need.
- **First question:** which acts are carrying the excess — is it spread evenly or concentrated in one or two scenes?
- **Data to pull:** act-by-act runtime breakdown compared against the original script page count or outline.

### Preview/test-audience attention drops more than 15% mid-act
- **Usually means:** a pacing sag, not necessarily a content problem — the material may be right but overlong or misplaced.
- **First question:** is the scene too long, or is it in the wrong position in the sequence?
- **Data to pull:** preview screening drop-off timestamps or engagement-tracking data, mapped against the scene boundaries.

### Three or more "just tighten it" notes arrive with no runtime target attached
- **Usually means:** the note-giver hasn't diagnosed the actual problem, or is avoiding a harder structural conversation.
- **First question:** what specific beat isn't landing, or what is the target runtime?
- **Data to pull:** the full revision-round notes log to check whether this note has repeated without resolution.

### A temp music track has been locked into the cut for more than two weeks before score discussion begins
- **Usually means:** temp-love risk — the cut's rhythm has become dependent on a track that will not exist in the final mix.
- **First question:** has the composer heard the temp track against the current cut yet?
- **Data to pull:** temp-music timeline markers compared against the composer's brief/kickoff date.

### Coverage-to-used ratio exceeds roughly 8:1 with no reduction plan in place
- **Usually means:** over-coverage has become a crutch, and selection is stalling into decision paralysis.
- **First question:** which takes are actually distinguishable from each other in performance or composition?
- **Data to pull:** bin organization and the current selects reel.

### An eyeline or screen-direction mismatch appears across a cut with no cutaway bridging it
- **Usually means:** the 180-degree line was crossed during shooting without coverage available to bridge the crossing in the edit.
- **First question:** is there a neutral insert or reaction shot available to smooth the transition?
- **Data to pull:** the shot list's camera-position notes for that setup.

### Picture is reopened after "final lock" more than once on the same project
- **Usually means:** the lock wasn't actually final — either scope crept in through later notes, or a stakeholder wasn't included in the lock approval.
- **First question:** who signed off on the lock, and did they see the reopened cut before other departments were told?
- **Data to pull:** the lock sign-off record or approval email.

### A single scene survives three or more edit passes with no runtime change
- **Usually means:** the editor and director disagree about the scene's function but haven't said so directly — indecision standing in for an unresolved disagreement.
- **First question:** what is this scene actually for — plot, character development, or tone?
- **Data to pull:** the outline or beat sheet the scene is supposed to map to.

### Sound or color finishing flags timeline gaps or a frame-rate mismatch on handoff
- **Usually means:** the EDL/XML/AAF export wasn't conform-checked before delivery, putting the finishing schedule at risk.
- **First question:** was a conform check run by re-importing the export before sending it out?
- **Data to pull:** the EDL/AAF export log and the finishing house's conform report.

### A client requests both "make it shorter" and "add scene X back" in the same revision round
- **Usually means:** the runtime target and the completeness expectation were never reconciled by the client themselves.
- **First question:** which constraint is the hard one — the runtime, or the content inclusion?
- **Data to pull:** the original delivery spec's contracted runtime.
