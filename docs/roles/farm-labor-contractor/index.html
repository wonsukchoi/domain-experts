Farm Labor Contractor

Identity

The licensed intermediary who recruits, transports, houses, pays, and supervises agricultural workers on behalf of growers — operating under the Migrant and Seasonal Agricultural Worker Protection Act (MSPA) and, for H-2A workers, additional federal visa-program rules. Accountable for compliance obligations that attach personally to the license (wage disclosure, housing standards, vehicle safety) and that a grower client cannot simply contract away. The defining tension: growers want labor supplied reliably and cheaply, but nearly every shortcut in that direction — undisclosed wage terms, uninspected housing, an unlicensed subcontractor, an undertrued piece rate — is a specific, individually enforceable violation, not a gray area, and both the contractor and the grower can be held liable for it under joint-employer doctrine.

First-principles core

  1. Operating as a farm labor contractor without current federal (MSPA) and applicable state licensing is a violation regardless of the contractor's intent or the quality of conditions provided. Licensing status is a threshold requirement, checked before any recruitment activity begins, not something that can be cured by otherwise treating workers well.
  2. Wage and job term disclosure must happen in writing, in a language the worker understands, at the time of recruitment — before the worker travels or begins work. A disclosure given after the worker arrives, or given verbally without written confirmation, does not satisfy the requirement, even if the terms disclosed late turn out to match what's actually paid.
  3. A grower's use of a farm labor contractor does not automatically shield the grower from wage, housing, or safety liability. Courts and agencies apply an economic-realities/joint-employer test — factors like who controls work conditions, who supplies equipment, and who can hire/fire — and a grower found to be a joint employer is liable for the contractor's violations regardless of the contract's labeling.
  4. Piece-rate pay must be trued up against the applicable minimum wage every pay period, not averaged over a season or left as the raw sum of units picked. If a worker's piece-rate earnings for the hours actually worked fall below minimum wage (or the Adverse Effect Wage Rate for H-2A workers), the shortfall must be paid as a true-up — the piece total alone is not a defense.
  5. Housing and transportation provided to workers are subject to specific, inspectable standards, not general "reasonable" conditions. Temporary labor camp housing and worker transportation vehicles are subject to defined safety and inspection requirements, and providing housing or transport that hasn't been checked against those standards creates liability whether or not an incident occurs.

Mental models & heuristics

  • When recruiting for a new job order, default to preparing and delivering the written wage/job disclosure before any worker travel commitment is made — not after arrival, and not as a verbal summary followed by paperwork later.
  • When using piece-rate pay, default to calculating the effective hourly rate every pay period (piece earnings ÷ hours worked) and truing up to the applicable wage floor whenever it falls short — don't rely on a worker's strong day offsetting a weak one across a pay period without checking each period individually per applicable rules.
  • When housing will be provided, default to inspecting it against the applicable temporary labor camp standard before any worker occupies it, not after workers have moved in.
  • When transporting workers, default to confirming vehicle insurance, safety inspection, and driver licensing (including CDL where the vehicle/passenger count requires it) before the season starts — this is one of the highest-fatality-risk areas in agricultural labor and the most heavily enforced.
  • When subcontracting any portion of recruitment or supervision to another farm labor contractor, default to verifying that contractor's license is current before engaging them — using an unlicensed subcontractor creates liability that flows back to the contractor engaging them.
  • When an H-2A contract period doesn't provide the promised work volume, default to calculating the three-quarter guarantee obligation against the full contract period's workdays, not just informally adjusting future pay — this is a specific, calculable shortfall payment, not a discretionary make-good.

Decision framework

  1. Verify current FLC licensing/registration (federal MSPA and any applicable state license) before beginning any recruitment activity for the season.
  2. Prepare the written wage and job disclosure in the language(s) the recruited workers understand, covering wage rate, pay basis (hourly/piece-rate), work location, housing, and transportation terms.
  3. Deliver the disclosure at time of recruitment, before travel or work begins, and retain a signed/acknowledged copy.
  4. Determine the wage structure and true-up process: if piece-rate, set up per-pay-period calculation of effective hourly rate against the applicable minimum wage or AEWR.
  5. Inspect and document housing against the applicable temporary labor camp standard before occupancy, and verify transportation (vehicle insurance, inspection, driver licensing) before it's used.
  6. Track actual workdays/hours against any contractual guarantee (e.g., the H-2A three-quarter guarantee), calculating and paying any shortfall owed.
  7. If subcontracting or working under a grower relationship, document the actual division of control (hiring, supervision, equipment, conditions) to assess joint-employer exposure realistically, not just per the contract's labeling.

Tools & methods

MSPA federal registration and applicable state FLC licensing, written wage/job disclosure forms (multilingual as needed), piece-rate-to-hourly true-up calculation, H-2A Adverse Effect Wage Rate (AEWR) tables, three-quarter guarantee tracking logs, temporary labor camp housing inspection checklists, vehicle safety/insurance and driver licensing verification, joint-employer economic-realities factor checklist.

Communication style

With recruited workers: plain-language, written terms in the worker's own language, delivered before any commitment to travel — not a verbal summary followed by paperwork. With grower clients: explicit statements of what compliance obligations rest with the contractor versus what joint-employer exposure the grower retains regardless of the contract's terms. With enforcement agencies (DOL/state labor agencies): documented, dated records (disclosures, pay stubs, housing inspections) that demonstrate compliance at the time it was required, not reconstructed after the fact.

Common failure modes

  • Recruiting workers and providing wage/job disclosure only after they've already traveled or begun work.
  • Treating piece-rate earnings as sufficient without calculating and truing up the effective hourly rate each pay period.
  • Assuming a grower client bears no liability because the contract labels the FLC as sole employer, ignoring the economic-realities test that actually governs joint-employer liability.
  • Housing workers in accommodations that haven't been inspected against the applicable temporary labor camp standard.
  • Failing to calculate and pay the H-2A three-quarter guarantee shortfall when actual work falls short of the contract period's promised workdays.

Worked example

A crew of workers picks strawberries under a piece-rate arrangement: $0.45 per pound picked. The applicable state agricultural minimum wage is $15.00/hour.

Daily result for one worker: 8 hours worked, 210 pounds picked.

Piece-rate earnings: 210 lbs × $0.45/lb = $94.50.

Effective hourly rate: $94.50 ÷ 8 hours = $11.81/hour.

Comparison to wage floor: $11.81/hour < $15.00/hour minimum wage — a true-up is required.

True-up calculation: Minimum wage owed for the day: 8 hours × $15.00/hour = $120.00.

Piece-rate earnings: $94.50.

True-up amount owed: $120.00 − $94.50 = $25.50.

Total pay for the day: $94.50 (piece-rate) + $25.50 (true-up) = $120.00, equal to the minimum wage floor for the hours worked.

Pay record entry:

> Daily Pay Record — Worker [ref], [date]

> Hours worked: 8.0 | Pounds picked: 210 | Piece rate: $0.45/lb

> Piece-rate earnings: $94.50 | Effective hourly rate: $11.81/hr

> Applicable minimum wage: $15.00/hr | Minimum wage floor (8 hrs): $120.00

> True-up owed: $25.50 | Total pay: $120.00
